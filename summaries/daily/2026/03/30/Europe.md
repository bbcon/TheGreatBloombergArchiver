European equity markets showed limited movement, with the Stoxx Europe 600 broadly flat on Monday. The more consequential European developments this week concern the intersection of energy policy, monetary policy, and domestic politics rather than markets per se. UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves is scheduled to address G7 partners, urging them to coordinate on boosting domestic energy supplies and warning that the oil and gas price surge is directly benefiting Vladimir Putin — a framing that seeks to link the Middle East crisis to the ongoing Russia-Ukraine dynamic.

On monetary policy, the ECB, Bank of England, and Swiss National Bank all held rates at their most recent meetings, each acting unanimously in response to geopolitical uncertainty. ECB Governing Council member Francois Villeroy de Galhau signaled the central bank stands ready to act against rising inflation expectations should they become unanchored. Euro-area CPI data is due Tuesday, alongside German inflation figures on Monday, which may show a notable jump reflecting fuel costs. The ECB's Cipollone is scheduled to speak Wednesday.

The UK faces a separate set of pressures. London's youth unemployment has reached a rate of approximately one in four 16-to-24 year-olds actively seeking work, the highest in the country. Graduate job postings in London fell from roughly 13,000 in 2019 to just over 2,000 at the start of 2026, according to Adzuna — a decline attributed to AI-driven hiring retrenchment compounded by broader economic uncertainty. The trend poses a political risk for Keir Starmer's Labour government ahead of municipal elections in just over a month. In Paris, French authorities detained two additional suspects linked to a foiled bomb plot near a Bank of America office, an incident officials believe is connected to the Middle East conflict.
