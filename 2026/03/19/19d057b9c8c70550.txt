Oil Surges With Gas as Attacks on Gulf Facilities Widen War Risks

<https://sli.bloomberg.com/click?s=1149114&li=17343409&m=11b724c487f61753eb1a7a4a5f3ef261&p=03192026&lctg=6689f0ec-2ca1-4687-9972-bdcb0c2dfbdf&stpe=default>
If the price of oil is a metric by which the war in the Middle East is being 
measured, right now it’s advantage Iran.

Brent crude jumped 10% to above $118 a barrel on Thursday, taking its gain 
since the conflict began to 63%. European gas futures roseas much as 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/european-gas-jumps-35-after-world-s-top-lng-plant-hit-by-iran?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 35% to more than double their pre-war level.

The cause of the most recent surge is the risk <> of escalating tit-for-tat 
attacks on key energy facilities in the region, which would further squeeze 
production of oil and gas.

Qatar’s Ras Laffan Industrial City — home to the world’s largest liquefied 
natural gas export plant — suffered “extensive damage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/qatar-reports-extensive-damage-at-site-of-ras-laffan-lng-plant?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
” after Iran struck the site in retaliation for Israel’s attack on Iran’s giant 
South Pars gas field. A gas facility in Abu Dhabi was shut after being hit by 
falling debris from an intercepted strike and two oil refineries in Kuwait were 
set ablaze by drone attacks. Saudi Arabia was assessing damage after a drone 
fell on a refinery near the Red Sea.

Tehran warned that energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia and United Arab 
Emirates had become “legitimate targets” due to the Israeli attack on its gas 
field, which is a core part of Iran’s energy infrastructure.

In response, Donald Trump said in a social media post that the US wasn’t 
involved in the South Pars attack, adding that Israelwould refrain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/iran-warns-gulf-nations-of-major-response-after-gas-field-strike?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 from further strikes on the site. He said any additional attacks by Iran on 
Qatar’s LNG facilities would prompt the US to “massively blow up the entirety” 
of the South Pars field.

Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian said attacks on the country’s energy 
infrastructure would “yield nothing” for the US, Israel and their supporters. 
“This will complicate the situation & could have uncontrollable consequences, 
the scope of which could engulf the entire world.”

With the Strait of Hormuz still largely impassable, and the risk of a widening 
in hostilities increasing, the outlook for oil — and the global economy — is 
growing more troubled.

“The market is still underestimating and not fully pricing the risk of how 
quickly this could escalate into direct hits on wider Gulf energy 
infrastructure,” said Haris Khurshid, chief investment officer at Karobaar 
Capital in Chicago. “If this escalates into direct hits then $120 won’t be the 
ceiling, it’ll be the starting point. To see $140 to $160 won’t be crazy at 
all,” he added, referring to Brent prices.—Richard Frost

Who’s Who in Iran? Guide to the Islamic Republic’s New Leaders

Read more 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/who-s-who-in-iran-guide-to-the-islamic-republic-s-power-players>
China’s onshore stocks are top assets to own during the Iran war, underpinned 
by their relative insulation from the oil shock, according to a veteran 
strategist at Jefferies.

“The best equity market to own in the world during such a period is the 
Chinese mainland market,” Christopher Wood, the firm’s global head of equity 
strategy, said in a Bloomberg TV interview. “China is light years ahead of the 
US in terms of energy, and energy is probably the key sector globally.”

China is the least affected by negative developments from the war as it has 
“almost unlimited access to cheap power” thanks to a decent supply of oil 
reserves and advances in renewable energy and battery storage, Wood said. He 
also cited potential buying by state funds known as the national team and 
easing factory deflation as tailwinds.
