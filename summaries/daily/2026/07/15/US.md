• US CPI fell in June — headline down month-on-month for the first time in six years, with core CPI flat, surprising nearly all economists and coming in well below consensus expectations.
• Fed Chair Warsh declined to declare "mission accomplished" in Congressional testimony, signaling inflation remains too elevated despite the benign print.
• Major Wall Street banks posted record or near-record trading revenues in Q2, with JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs leading; IBM suffered its largest single-day decline in history, falling more than 25%.

**Inflation**
June CPI delivered the most broadly encouraging inflation print in years. Headline prices fell month-on-month for the first time since 2020, while core CPI — excluding food and energy — was flat, a reading described by Oxford Economics as reflecting the absence of all three forces the Fed was most alert to: tariff pass-through, AI-driven pricing, and oil. The Fed's supercore services measure declined, the Atlanta Fed sticky-price gauge fell to its lowest since the pandemic, and the Cleveland Fed trimmed mean dipped below 3%. Labor-intensive services linked to immigrant workers remained an exception, with home healthcare costs up 10.7% year-on-year.

**Monetary Policy**
Despite the encouraging data, Fed Chair Warsh used his House Financial Services Committee testimony to push back firmly against premature optimism. He declined to signal rate cuts and notably framed a "family fight" among policymakers over the "extent and timing" of any future tightening — his closest approach yet to acknowledging a rate hike remains on the table. Futures pricing for a July hike collapsed following the CPI release, marking the second-largest post-CPI repricing of Fed meeting odds since 2008. Capital Economics maintained that the AI investment boom and rebounding consumer demand will keep core inflation above target, and that a rate hike remains a question of when, not if.

**Markets and Earnings**
The S&P 500 gained 0.38%, a muted response reflecting residual uncertainty over oil and the Hormuz situation. Q2 earnings season opened strongly: JPMorgan reported its highest-ever quarterly profit, while Goldman Sachs posted a record $7.42 billion in quarterly trading revenue. IBM fell more than 25% — the largest single-day decline in its history — after disappointing results attributed to customers redirecting spending toward chips and servers.
