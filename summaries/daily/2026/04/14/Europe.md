Financial markets in Europe reacted positively to the prospect of resumed US-Iran talks, with the Stoxx Europe 600 gaining 0.9%. The more consequential European story of the day, however, was political: Viktor Orbán's landslide defeat in Hungary's parliamentary elections, which markets had increasingly priced in as polls pointed toward opposition leader Péter Magyar. The Hungarian forint rose to a four-year high — surpassing its level at the time of Orbán's 2022 re-election — reflecting currency traders' relief at the prospect of restored access to frozen EU funds and a more conventional economic policy framework.

The market judgment was on economic rather than political grounds: Hungary's growth has lagged peers Poland, Czechia, and Slovakia through the Orbán years, and forex markets consistently penalized the self-imposed isolation from EU financing. Magyar has indicated he intends a swift transition of power, supports eventual euro adoption, and wants to reintegrate Hungary into the EU's institutional framework. Orbán called a Fidesz party congress for April 28 to review the defeat.

Elsewhere in Europe, the Iran conflict is generating tangible economic fallout. LVMH reported that sales at its fashion and leather goods division — home to Louis Vuitton — fell in the first quarter, with the war's dampening effect on demand cited alongside broader consumer caution. HSBC's CEO told Bloomberg Television that Middle East uncertainty is beginning to dent client confidence. The Bank of England's Andrew Bailey warned that private credit funds face heightened risks from one-off loss events that could threaten broader confidence in the sector. Italy's Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni called Trump's remarks about Pope Leo XIV "unacceptable," signaling a growing rift with Washington that reflects wider European discomfort over the Iran conflict's management.

ECB President Christine Lagarde was due to speak at the IMF Spring Meetings, with markets attentive to any shift in guidance given the competing pressures of elevated energy-driven inflation and slowing regional growth.
