For all the opportunities offered by artificial intelligence, from streamlined 
healthcare to humanoid robots, some big questions over its downsides won’t go 
away.

Mounting evidence of entry-level roles being lost to AI 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/half-of-uk-executives-think-ai-will-mean-fewer-jobs?cmpid=BBD050826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4266>
. Rising concerns over data privacy and automated warfare. Widespread 
plagiarism of artistic content that is rendered into AI slop.

White House-generated images of President Donald Trump as Jesus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/trump-deletes-social-media-post-seemingly-depicting-him-as-jesus?cmpid=BBD050826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4266>
 are probably not the best advertisement for AI’s promise.

Then there’s the phenomenal energy needs of the data centers that are being 
rolled out to house the necessary computing power.
A Microsoft data center in Aldie, Virginia.Photographer: Lexi 
Critchett/Bloomberg
In one of the most striking developments, Three Mile Island, the site of the 
most famous US nuclear accident, iscoming back online <> to power chatbots and 
other AI applications — now rechristened as the Crane Clean Energy Center.

Microsoft, meanwhile, sees its ambitious renewable-energy targets as a hurdle 
to its planned data-center buildout, so is weighing whether todelay or abandon 
them 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-06/microsoft-considers-dropping-100-100-0-clean-energy-target?cmpid=BBD050826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4266>
 altogether, sources say.

The amount of power consumed by US data centers alone is likely to more than 
double to 106 gigawatts through 2035, according to BloombergNEF, where a 
gigawatt can feed around 750,000 homes.

This massive demand is already worrying governments desperate not to fall 
behind in the AI race, but just as anxious to keep voters onside.

In Australia, the state of New South Wales — whose capital is Sydney — is 
conducting an investigation into the impact of new data centers on power, water 
and other resources.

Britain’s AI minister hit out at OpenAI 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/uk-ai-minister-hits-back-at-openai-for-pausing-stargate-project?cmpid=BBD050826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4266>
 for halting a UK data-center project and blaming the decision in part on 
energy costs.

Kenyan President William Ruto has suggested that the energy for a proposed 
facility exceeded the nation’s available resources.

It’s said that advocates of artificial intelligence are losing the PR battle. 

The prospect of blackouts and higher bills doesn’t help AI’s cause. — Alan 
Crawford <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AOTqYzZDKuY/alan-crawford>

And Finally

The Venice Biennale opens to the public tomorrow with more than two dozen 
national pavilions and a cacophony of controversy. Israel has been one focus, 
with hundreds of artists and cultural leaders calling for the country to be 
excluded. Russia is another, reopening its pavilion for the first time since 
its 2022 invasion of Ukraine, prompting Finland to scale back its participation 
and the EU to pull more than $2 million in funding. The Biennale is meant to 
showcase the cream of the art world, but this year it’s insteaddisplaying the 
world’s political fractures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-08/middle-east-conflicts-russia-ukraine-war-dominate-2026-venice-art-biennale?cmpid=BBD050826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4266>
.
