Climate unfriendly skies

By Ben Elgin <https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AP8O5gRPysw/ben-elgin>

Delta Air Lines Inc. quietly scrubbed a pair of key environmental targets from 
its sustainability web page .

The Atlanta-based carrier deleted its pledge 
<https://web.archive.org/web/20260403043617/https://www.delta.com/us/en/about-delta/sustainability>
 to use sustainable aviation fuel (SAF) for 10% of its jet fuel by 2030. It 
also rephrased its quest to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050 as an 
тАЬaspiration,тАЭ rather than a тАЬgoal.тАЭ

A Delta spokesperson said the carrier still sees SAF as one of the most 
important ways to decarbonize flight, but its slow development threatens the 
industryтАЩs climate ambitions. тАЬWhile we have successfully increased use of SAF 
every year, we recognize that the technology has not advanced as rapidly as the 
industry requires,тАЭ said the spokesperson.

Delta generated about 60 million tons of heat-trapping emissions in 2024. 
ThatтАЩs equivalent to the entire climate footprint of Ireland or Hungary. Like 
most airlines, it has long touted sustainable jet fuel тАФ made from feedstocks 
like animal fat or used cooking oil тАФ as a critical lever to cut its emissions.

Delta first pledged five years ago to get 10% of its jet fuel from these 
alternative sources by 2030. тАЬTravelers should not have to choose between 
seeing the world and saving the world,тАЭ Chief Executive Officer Ed Bastian said 
at the time.

Many other carriers have made similar commitments. Progress, however, has been 
glacial. The growth in cleaner jet fuel is expected to slow substantially this 
year, according to the International Air Transport Association. The trade group 
expects SAF will account for 0.8% of global airline fuel in 2026.

DeltaтАЩs overall SAF usage jumped more than six-fold in the past two years to 
23 million gallons, but in 2025 it still accounted for only about 0.5% of total 
jet fuel used by the carrier.
