**French Fiscal and Political Pressure**

The French government's public finances alert committee convened Tuesday against a deteriorating fiscal backdrop. Central government balances through May have worsened year-on-year, with risks compounding from local governments and social security. OAT spreads over Bunds remain elevated relative to pre-2024 levels, reflecting a persistent political risk premium. A budget must be presented to parliament in October, a process that will be contentious given the three-way parliamentary impasse. Le Pen's confirmation Wednesday that she will run in 2027 — her appeal to the highest court delaying sentence enforcement — keeps the policy discontinuity risk alive, though markets appear to have been gradually pricing elevated political risk rather than awaiting a discrete catalyst.

**German Economy**

Factory orders data released Monday offered a cautiously encouraging signal, rebounding after months of suppressed activity during the Strait of Hormuz closure. German industrial production rose for a second consecutive month, and export data showed an unexpected rise in May for the fourth consecutive month, driven by a sharp rebound in US-bound shipments. The sequential improvement is meaningful but partial: the Hormuz re-escalation midweek reintroduces energy supply uncertainty that could interrupt the recovery. Chancellor Merz is seeking to align positive data momentum with structural policy action ahead of the autumn legislative calendar.

**Industrial Restructuring and Corporate Developments**

Volkswagen's supervisory board meeting Thursday on a plan to eliminate up to 100,000 positions and close four factories represents one of the most consequential corporate decisions in European industry this year, reflecting EV transition pressures, softening Chinese demand, and an uncompetitive cost base. Separately, EasyJet — weakened by jet fuel inflation during the Iran war — attracted a competing Apollo Global Management offer on Friday, sending shares 14% higher as a bidding war with Castlelake emerged. Ireland's tech sector employment fell nearly 11% YoY in Q1, with Meta cutting approximately 20% of its Irish workforce as AI-driven restructuring accelerates, creating tension with the tax-incentive model that attracted US multinationals.
