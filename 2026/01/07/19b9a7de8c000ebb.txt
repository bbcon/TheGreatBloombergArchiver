For years, Venezuela was all but off-limits. Now, Wall Street is rushing in.


As ousted Venezuelan leader Nicolás Maduro pleaded not guilty 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-05/maduro-appears-in-us-courtroom-to-face-narco-terrorism-charges>
 this week to US charges in a narco-terrorism case against him, the rewards 
were evident. In public markets, bondholders grabbed a one-day gain of some $4 
billion and saw hope for a restructuring that would unlock more profit. And for 
private capital firms and energy investors, Donald Trump was dangling an even 
bigger prize, pledging the US wouldspend billions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-03/trump-says-us-to-run-venezuela-in-interim-after-maduro-capture>
 to fix Venezuela’s failing oil infrastructure. 


 The promise revealed how Wall Street and private capital will be intertwined 
with Trump’s aggressive gambit to reshape the South American country. In the 
business world, Maduro’s capture marked a turning point for a market long seen 
as too risky — with dealmakers and traders rushing to assess whether 
Venezuela’s ample natural resourcesreally are coming into reach 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-07/wall-street-eyes-venezuela-investments-oil-assets-after-maduro-ouster>
.
