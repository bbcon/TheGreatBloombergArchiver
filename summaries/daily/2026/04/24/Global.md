The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with oil rising for a fifth consecutive day as no meaningful progress toward US-Iran de-escalation was apparent. A US-sanctioned supertanker, the Yuri, carrying 2 million barrels of Iranian crude from Kharg Island, was tracked sailing toward the strait on Friday, apparently testing Washington's resolve to maintain its naval blockade. Goldman Sachs estimated Persian Gulf oil output is running 14.5 million barrels per day below pre-war levels this month — roughly 57% below pre-conflict volumes — and assessed that any restoration would likely require several months following a full reopening of the strait and an absence of renewed strikes.

President Trump announced a three-week extension of the Israel-Lebanon ceasefire, which creates some space for diplomatic activity but falls well short of a broader resolution. Iranian negotiators, according to officials familiar with the matter, have grown less willing to engage in direct talks, citing Trump's social media posts as intended to humiliate Tehran's leadership rather than facilitate a deal. Pimco has privately extended more than $10 billion in loans to Gulf state-linked borrowers since the war began, a signal of the scale of wartime capital reallocation. Energy trader Pierre Andurand's largest hedge fund fell approximately 52% in the first half of April after bullish oil bets placed at the conflict's outset reversed.

On the defense and technology dimensions of the broader geopolitical shift, Chinese defense contractors made their largest-ever showing at the Defence Services Asia exhibition in Kuala Lumpur, with 192 firms attending and drone and counter-drone technologies prominently featured. The event coincided with Japan lifting most restrictions on lethal weapons exports for the first time since World War II. The US unveiled measures targeting the practice of AI model distillation by Chinese developers. Xi Jinping's government also moved to exclude Taiwan's president from African airspace by pressuring island nations to revoke overflight permits. The US-China summit, tentatively planned for mid-May, is framed by Beijing as occurring on terms increasingly favorable to China, given Washington's perceived missteps in both the Iran conflict and the trade confrontation.
