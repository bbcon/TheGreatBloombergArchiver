Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Rakesh Sharma looks at how Indian oil refiners have emerged as 
unlikely beneficiaries from the war in the Middle East.

The timing of the Iran war could hardly have been worse for India. 

Refiners in the country, one of the major economies most dependent on energy 
imports, had beenpulling back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/indian-imports-of-russian-oil-seen-halving-on-white-house-order?cmpid=BBBXT031026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 from buying Russian oil since December in the face of intensifying US 
pressure. They’d turned instead to replacement barrels from Saudi Arabia and 
Iraq.

So when the US and Israel attacked Iran in late February, and the Strait of 
Hormuz became effectively blocked as the war expanded, the alarm bells were 
ringing in New Delhi.

After several days of mounting panic, the Trump administration rode to the 
rescue: Its announcement last Thursday that it was giving India aone-month 
waiver 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/us-issues-license-to-allow-some-russian-oil-sales-to-india-mme5qix4?cmpid=BBBXT031026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 to resume Russian oil purchases brought an almost palpable sense of relief.
US President Donald Trump has delivered a much-needed reprieve to Indian Prime 
Minister Narendra Modi by allowing the country to resume buying Russian oil.
Photographer: Francis Chung/Politico/Bloomberg
Since then, Indian refiners haven’t wasted any time taking advantage of the 
reprieve, scooping up every available shipment of Urals crude in the spot 
market, according to traders.

The fact that tankers carrying more than 20 million barrels 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/millions-of-barrels-of-russian-oil-are-already-close-to-india?cmpid=BBBXT031026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 of unsold Russian oil were aready at sea, within a week’s sailing time from 
Indian ports, offered the country a quick fix to the supply crunch.

The upshot is that Washington has shielded the country in Asia that has 
perhaps the most vulnerable oil supply chain to a Middle East war.

Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s government has not pressed private refiners to 
curb exports of fuel, and prices at the pumps have been left unchanged. The 
country also doesn’t look like it will need to tap its strategic reserves in 
the near future. Unlike in neighboring Pakistan or Bangladesh, there are nolong 
lines snaking 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/asia-races-to-contain-energy-crunch-as-war-drives-oil-past-100?cmpid=BBBXT031026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 out of fuel stations.

The war has exposed other energy pain points in India, most notably in 
liquefied petroleum gas 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/india-households-face-a-looming-fuel-crunch-as-iran-crisis-drags?cmpid=BBBXT031026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
, popular as a cooking fuel, and liquefied natural gas used in industry and 
power generation.

But for crude oil supply at least, New Delhi looks to be in one of the more 
comfortable positions in Asia.

—Rakesh Sharma, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Growing LNG deliveries from West Africa provide Europe with greater supply 
diversification, allowing the region to accelerate its phase-out of Russian 
imports without relying solely on US fuel, according to BloombergNEF. New 
projects from the Republic of Congo and Nigeria are becoming essential to 
maintain Europe’s energy security.
