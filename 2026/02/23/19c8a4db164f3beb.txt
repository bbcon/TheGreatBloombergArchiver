 Blue Owl’s red flags 



Blue Owl Capital’s decision last week to permanently shut the gates on one of 
its funds israttling the $1.8 trillion private credit market 
<https://bloom.bg/4raBs7f>. 

Investors have been pulling money from such funds because of jitters over the 
disruptive power of AI and lending standards more broadly. And it’s evoking 
comparisons to the run-up to the global financial crisis.

“The red flags we are seeing in private credit today are strikingly familiar 
to those of 2007,” said Orlando Gemes of Fourier Asset Management. He pointed 
to worsening lender protections and convoluted liquidity terms.

Private credit has in recent years transformed from a niche lending market to 
a linchpin of global finance. It now back everything from data center 
operations to multibillion dollar buyouts ofsoftware makers 
<https://bloom.bg/4raBtbj> and healthcare companies <https://bloom.bg/4c9BGYk>.

The timing is especially awkward because Wall Street is rushing to bring 
private assets to the masses. Trying toconvince 401(k) retirement plans 
<https://bloom.bg/4axu80n> to add such funds gets tougher when investors are 
blocked from exiting. —Silas Brown <https://bloom.bg/42L7k9W>, Olivia Fishlow 
<https://bloom.bg/44OhabF>, Davide Scigliuzzo <https://bloom.bg/40DZ48W>, and 
Leonard Kehnscherper <https://bloom.bg/4c3JYkq>

 Bitcoin blues 



It wouldn’t have seemed possible a year ago. But Bitcoin is caught in one of 
its deepest struggles yet, with no obvious way out <https://bloom.bg/4cGgKrY>.

The world’s largest cryptocurrency has plunged more than 40% from its peak, 
and the usual playbook isn’t working —dip buyers have vanished 
<https://bloom.bg/4l9HnYR>, and the forces that would normally fuel a rebound 
are now working against it.

The strange part: none of this is happening because the system failed Bitcoin. 
Washington has never been more accommodating. Institutional adoption has never 
been deeper. Wall Street has never been more bought in. Bitcoin boosters got 
everything they wanted — and it wasn’t enough.

That means the defining struggle of this crypto era isn’t about price. It’s 
about purpose.And this selloff is forcing a question Bitcoin hasn’t needed to 
answer when prices were rising: if it isn’t the best hedge, the best payment 
rail or the best speculation — what, exactly, is it for?

“The central story of Bitcoin was ‘number go up’ and we don’t have that 
anymore,” said Owen Lamont at Acadian Asset Management. “We have number go 
down. That is not a good story.” —Isabelle Lee <https://bloom.bg/4gRZU8q> and 
Vildana Hajric <https://bloom.bg/3DSeO0i>
