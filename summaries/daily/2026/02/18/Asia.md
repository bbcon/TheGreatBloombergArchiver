Japan dominated the Asia newsflow, with Tokyo announcing it will invest up to $36 billion in US oil, gas, and critical minerals projects — the first concrete tranche of the $550 billion framework agreed with the Trump administration. The most prominent component is a large natural gas facility in Ohio. The announcement arrives weeks before Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, formally re-elected today following her strong electoral showing earlier this month, is set to visit Washington. The investment serves multiple strategic functions: it provides visible progress on the trade deal, reinforces energy security ties, and may help Tokyo navigate the tariff environment. The Japan-US trade deficit, notably, has remained largely static in nominal terms for three decades — partly because Japanese manufacturers shifted production to America, a model that Trump officials are now reportedly exploring with Chinese automakers. The Ford-China joint venture concept, in which US firms would hold controlling stakes, represents a meaningfully different structure from either the Japan precedent or existing Chinese investments elsewhere in the world such as BYD's Hungary facility, and faces material political headwinds at the state level even if Washington proves receptive.

Indonesia presents a contrasting picture within the region. MSCI has placed the country's emerging-market classification under review, and FTSE Russell has followed suit, citing governance deterioration under President Prabowo Subianto. The rupiah has weakened to levels last seen nearly three decades ago, Moody's cut the credit outlook to negative, and market capitalization fell nearly $80 billion from peak levels following the abrupt dismissal of the heads of the Financial Services Authority and the Indonesian Stock Exchange. The structural concerns are layered: Prabowo's replacement of technocrats with loyalists, the appointment of a family member as Bank Indonesia deputy governor, and a flagship school lunch program costing an estimated $28 billion annually that strains an already narrow fiscal base. Central bank rate cuts — warranted by benign inflation — remain politically constrained by currency weakness, illustrating the compounding nature of the credibility erosion.
