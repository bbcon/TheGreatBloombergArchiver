# European Daily Briefing — February 4, 2026

**Novo Nordisk shock dominates corporate tape.** Shares of the Danish pharmaceutical giant plunged as much as 20% in Copenhagen after the company forecast a steep decline in sales, providing concrete evidence of an intensifying price war in the obesity drug market. The guidance suggests competitive dynamics in GLP-1s are deteriorating faster than the sell-side had modeled, with implications for the broader European healthcare complex and for Denmark's equity index, where Novo carries outsized weight. The scale of the move — among the largest single-day declines in the company's history — points to a meaningful reset of consensus assumptions around pricing power in the category.

**UBS delivers shareholder return signal.** UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat, reinforcing the constructive narrative around large-cap European banks. The capital return underscores management's confidence in integration progress following the Credit Suisse absorption, though investors will want to assess underlying revenue quality and the trajectory of cost synergies in subsequent disclosures.

**Disinflation continues to run ahead of the ECB.** Euro-area inflation sank further below the ECB's 2% target, sharpening the policy debate as the Governing Council weighs its next move. The undershoot complicates the recent communication that easing is largely complete, and markets will likely lean toward pricing additional accommodation if the trend persists into the spring projection round. The data sit uneasily alongside firmer activity signals elsewhere, and the Council's reaction function around target undershoots — versus overshoots in 2022-23 — remains an open question.

**Energy diversification and Equinor.** Chancellor Friedrich Merz is courting Middle Eastern suppliers to diversify German energy sources and reduce reliance on US LNG, a notable strategic pivot given recent transatlantic frictions. Separately, Equinor scaled back its buyback program to as much as $1.5 billion this year from $5 billion in 2025 after Q4 profit missed estimates on lower oil and gas prices — a reminder that European energy majors' capital return capacity remains highly sensitive to commodity prints.
