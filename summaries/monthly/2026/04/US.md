• S&P 500 fell approximately 5–7.8% in March, its worst monthly performance in roughly a year, before staging a remarkable recovery in April that carried the index above 7,000 for the first time
• The Fed held rates unchanged at Powell's final meeting as chair, producing the most divided FOMC vote since 1992; Kevin Warsh confirmed as successor with markets pricing zero cuts in 2026
• Energy shock is transmitting rapidly into domestic prices — gasoline crossed $4 per gallon, retail diesel rose nearly 50%, and University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to an all-time low of 47.6
• First-quarter earnings beat rate of approximately 80% with a 12% aggregate surprise, though the semiconductor sector alone accounts for roughly 43% of upward earnings revisions
• Supreme Court invalidation of IEEPA tariff authority and a Court of International Trade order mandating $160 billion in refunds to importers represent a substantive legal constraint on executive trade policy

**Equity Markets**

US equity markets traced a pattern of extreme volatility across April that, viewed in aggregate, produced a striking recovery from late-March lows. The S&P 500 entered the month roughly 8% below its pre-war peak, with the index displaying a consistent intraweek behavioral signature: cumulative gains over the first three sessions of each week repeatedly erased on Thursdays and Fridays as geopolitical anxiety reasserted itself, with the index posting net losses of approximately 9% on those two days alone since hostilities began in late February. The mid-month ceasefire announcement on April 8 catalyzed the S&P 500's best single session in nearly a year, with the index subsequently extending gains to close above 7,000 by the third week — a 12.33% advance over just 14 trading days, a pace of appreciation recorded only four times this century. The closest historical parallel analysts drew was March 2000 in terms of earnings multiple and timing, a comparison that warrants serious attention. Fewer than half of S&P 500 members traded above their 50-day moving averages at the index's record close, and volume ran 11% below the six-month average, suggesting the rally was driven in significant part by short-covering among investors conditioned by 2025's Liberation Day episode not to hold short positions through stress events. Energy names and health insurers diverged sharply — Exxon Mobil fell 5.8% mid-month on the ceasefire, while Humana gained 11% and UnitedHealth rose 7% on a constructive Medicare Advantage payment proposal. The Bloomberg US Corporate High Yield Index returned to pre-war levels by late April, closing at a record high, a signal of credit market normalization that sits in some tension with the macro backdrop.

**Monetary Policy and the Fed Transition**

The Federal Reserve's policy stance and leadership transition together constituted the dominant domestic institutional story of the month. Powell's final FOMC meeting as chair produced the most divided vote since October 1992, with four members dissenting — three regional presidents objecting to any residual easing bias in forward guidance, and Stephen Miran dissenting separately. Rates were left unchanged but the statement's tone was unambiguously hawkish. Two-year Treasury yields rose 11 basis points on the decision day, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, and 30-year yields reached 5% for the first time this year by month-end. Futures markets moved to price zero easing in 2026, with approximately 50% odds assigned to a rate increase by April 2027 — a stark reversal from the two cuts that had been fully priced before hostilities began. Kevin Warsh's confirmation hearing produced substantive signals: a preference for narrower mandates, a smaller balance sheet, and a pivot toward trimmed-mean inflation gauges — the Dallas Fed's trimmed PCE currently reads 2.33% versus 3.0% for core PCE, a 70-basis-point gap that would materially reframe perceived distance from the 2% target if adopted as the policy benchmark. Powell's decision to remain on the board as a governor, amid a deeply divided committee, will likely constrain Warsh's ability to shift policy direction quickly. Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized the arrangement as a violation of Fed norms; the parallel to Marriner Eccles somewhat complicates that framing but does not resolve the underlying tension.

**Corporate Earnings and Technology**

The first-quarter earnings season opened constructively against a difficult macro backdrop. With slightly over a third of S&P 500 companies reported by late April, the beat rate stood at approximately 80% — above the historical 77% average — with an aggregate earnings surprise near 12%, well above the typical 7% premium. The AI-semiconductor complex provided the season's clearest upside, with TSMC reporting a 58% profit surge and raising full-year revenue growth guidance above 30%, Texas Instruments surging 12% on data-center-driven demand, SK Hynix delivering a fivefold quarterly profit increase, and Samsung Electronics reporting a 755% year-on-year profit increase. The Philadelphia Semiconductor index extended what appeared to be an 18-session winning streak, the longest in the index's history. Results outside the AI complex were more mixed: Netflix fell approximately 10% on a miss and co-founder Reed Hastings's departure, ServiceNow fell 13% after cutting its margin outlook citing Middle East-related deal delays, and Alcoa disappointed despite elevated aluminum prices. The hyperscaler earnings released in the final week of April delivered a nuanced verdict — Alphabet surged more than 7% and added $421 billion in market capitalization in a single session, the second-largest one-day gain on record for any stock, while Meta fell roughly 7% after raising 2026 capital expenditure guidance by $10 billion to potentially above $145 billion. The combined capex envelope for the four major hyperscalers now approaches $725 billion, a figure that sustains rather than extends AI enthusiasm. SpaceX began investor meetings for an IPO that could value the company above $2 trillion.

**Consumer Stress and Political Economy**

Beneath equity market resilience, consumer conditions deteriorated measurably through the month. The University of Michigan's preliminary April sentiment index fell to 47.6, the weakest in the survey's history dating to 1978, surpassing troughs from the 1980 hostage crisis and the Covid pandemic, with year-ahead inflation expectations rising a full percentage point to 4.8%. Mortgage applications fell more than 10% through March, and the household savings rate has declined to 4.5% from 6.4% in January 2024. Buy-now-pay-later provider Klarna lost more than 60% of its value since its September IPO, with usage increasingly concentrated in essentials such as groceries. Corporate equities now represent over a quarter of household net worth — a record in eight decades of Fed data — making equity market performance a potentially more potent consumer spending channel than pump prices, an observation that cuts both ways. Electricity costs are emerging as a defining issue ahead of November midterms. On fiscal policy, Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed tariff rates may be restored to pre-Supreme Court levels by July, and the administration invoked the Defense Production Act to direct federal funds toward energy infrastructure including coal, LNG, and petroleum. Fitch warned that the US debt burden remains far above other AA-rated sovereigns, while key congressional Republicans signaled potential resistance to a proposed 44% Pentagon budget increase — a rare act of intra-party defiance that may signal softening executive influence ahead of midterms.
