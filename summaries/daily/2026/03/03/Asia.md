Asian equity markets bore the sharpest losses of any region on Tuesday, with MSCI's Asia-Pacific benchmark declining approximately 3% as the Iran conflict entered its fourth day with no visible path to de-escalation. The standout move was in South Korea, where the Kospi fell 7.2% as markets reopened following a long weekend — a severe repricing that nevertheless leaves the index up 37% year-to-date, a performance driven largely by semiconductor exporters Samsung and SK Hynix benefiting from AI-driven demand. The magnitude of Tuesday's decline may partly reflect catch-up selling, but it underscores the vulnerability of tech-heavy markets to a sharp reversal in risk appetite.

China's response to the conflict has been notably calibrated. Beijing issued its most direct call to date for all parties to ensure safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz, a waterway through which nearly half of China's crude imports transited as recently as December. Chinese solar companies — beneficiaries of renewed energy-security sentiment — were among the few gainers in the region, with some inverter manufacturers rising more than 10% on the week as investors reconsidered the strategic value of domestically produced clean energy. On the diplomatic front, US and Chinese trade negotiators are reportedly scheduled to meet in Paris in mid-March, with Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, USTR Jamieson Greer, and Vice Premier He Lifeng expected to discuss the framework for a potential Trump-Xi summit. The meeting's continuation despite the Iran crisis appears to signal that both sides view the bilateral economic agenda as distinct from the geopolitical environment.

Separately, Australia's Reserve Bank Governor Michele Bullock signaled that the central bank's patient approach to inflation has limits and that a rate hike remains possible this month — a reminder that the Iran-driven energy shock compounds pre-existing inflation management challenges across the region.
