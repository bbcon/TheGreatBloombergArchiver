Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Yongchang Chin discusses the lasting impact of the Iran war on the 
Persian Gulf’s oil market.

The Strait of Hormuz may be poised to reopen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/us-at-odds-with-allies-over-how-easy-it-is-to-reopen-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT061626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5572>
, but the experience of the last few months will likely permanently change the 
dynamics of the Middle Eastern oil trade.

First and foremost, the region’s risk profile has been irrevocably altered. 
Prior to the war, traders and analysts had only modeled the closure of the 
waterway as a theoretical outcome.

But the conflict has shown how easily Iran can cripple the global oil market. 
This is a particular risk because there’s still likely to be plenty of issues 
to resolve between Washington and Tehran following the initial deal to be 
signed on Friday.

As a result, Asian oil refiners and shipowners could approach the region 
differently by seeking some cargo discounts to mitigate risk from the 
chokepoint. Tanker operators may charge a premium to account for costlier 
insurance and the possibility of being stuck inside again.
The Eneos Endeavor supertanker approaches an oil refinery in Yokohama, Japan, 
after making a rare, dark transit through the Strait of Hormuz in May.
Photographer: Akio Kon/Bloomberg
Buyers in Asia, which take the bulk of Persian Gulf crude, will also want to 
be better prepared to cope with any future disruptions. The war has forced them 
to hurriedly diversify their sources of supply, and, while they will snap up 
Middle Eastern oil as it becomes available again, they’ll be loathe to go back 
to a situation where they rely too heavily on the region.

Japan is a case in point. It built its oil industry almost entirely around 
Middle Eastern supply, with around 90% of its crude coming from the Persian 
Gulf before the war. It now buys a consistent baseload of US oil each month, 
even if it costs more.

On the supply side, the conflict has incentivized Saudi Arabia and the United 
Arab Emirates to accelerate plans to bypass Hormuz. The UAE is considering 
building an additional pipeline to Fujairah, which sits outside the strait on 
its eastern coast, while the Saudis may maintain anincreased level of throughput
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-22/strait-of-hormuz-trump-threatens-iran-but-saudi-arabia-finds-pipeline-bypass?cmpid=BBBXT061626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5572>
 via their pipeline to the Red Sea.

The Persian Gulf will remain the base of the global oil industry, but the 
risks of being too dependent on the region have become plain for everyone to 
see.

—Yongchang Chin, Bloomberg News
