Trump’s Africa Minerals Push Faces Long Road to Match Dominant China
Trying to climb China’s mountain.

Amid a week in which it went to war with Iran and sought to cement ties 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/trump-woos-venezuela-with-potential-deals-as-relations-reset?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
 with Venezuela after having renditioned its president, the Trump 
administration managed to find time to slap sanctions on the army of a tiny 
East African nation: Rwanda.

The move looks to be part of a broader initiative 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/us-sanctions-rwandan-army-for-backing-m23-rebels-in-congo?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
 to seek stability in the Democratic Republic of Congo, the eastern region of 
which is under the control of rebels backed by its neighbor and rival. The 
decision also spoke to Washington’s underlying interest in securing critical 
minerals and marks a major shift from past decades when the US paid limited 
attention to Africa — at least when compared with China, its top geopolitical 
rival.

Until recently, efforts to build supply lines between the world’s biggest 
economy and nations possessing its biggest mineral deposits have been limited 
and piecemeal. But since Donald Trump returned to the White House last year, 
the push to increase US access to crucial metals such as copper hasgone into 
overdrive 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-09/trump-to-host-african-leaders-offering-major-mining-ventures?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
.

The new strategy favors country-level deals rather than small-scale 
agreements, like the 2023 move by the Biden administration to put $50 million 
toward a South African rare earths project. As always, follow-through and 
execution will be needed to translate vision into reality. But over the longer 
haul, the US-China competition could see major waves of investment into the 
African continent.

Congo is the world’s second-biggest copper producer and largest source of 
cobalt. The nation also is in dire need of a security guarantor as it struggles 
to recover that large swath of territory from Rwanda-backed rebels.

While it’s an open question whether Trump would send troops to join a conflict 
that’s lingered forthree decades 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-18/why-trump-s-congo-rwanda-peace-deal-is-so-fragile?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
, he did put down a marker by sanctioning Rwanda’s army and some of its top 
officials over their rebel support. It’s a strong signal to Rwandan President 
Paul Kagame, who despite remaining in power through a series of controversial 
elections, has done his best to court Western approval.

Trump’s sanctions have been complemented by moves by US companies. Last month, 
a consortium including New York-based Orion Resource Partners and the US 
government’s Development Finance Corp. reached a multibillion-dollar deal to 
buy 40% of Glencore’s majority stakes in two Congolese copper-cobalt mines.

Around the same time, Virtus Minerals — headed by veterans of the US military 
and intelligence services — made a deal to acquire another Congolese copper and 
cobalt producer. The US also continues to champion a Biden-era initiative to 
help fund theLobito Corridor <bbg://news/stories/SW92AWT1UM0W>, a rail line 
that’s being revamped to move minerals from deposits in Congo and Zambia to 
Angola’s Atlantic coast, closer to the US than the east and southern African 
ports used by the miners.

And this new American attention isn’t only focused on Congo. The Trump 
administration has discussed mineral deals with a range of African countries, 
including Zimbabwe. The so-calledLiberty Corridor 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-12-19/guinea-scores-in-geopolitical-race-for-african-minerals?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
 is a rail line and port that would allow a US company backed by billionaire 
mining tycoon Robert Friedland to export iron ore to America from the mountains 
of Guinea, by way of Liberia.

US interest in both the Lobito and Liberty corridors began 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2024-lobito-corridor-rail-ev-mining/?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
 during President Joe Biden’s presidency. Under Trump, there appears to be a 
greater impetus to get things done more quickly. Still, his administration is 
likely to find there are big mountains to climb.
China’s CMOC Group’s Tenke Fungurume mine in the Democratic Republic of Congo.
Photographer: Emmet Livingstone/AFP/Getty Images
China has a stranglehold on many of the continent’s resources — controlling a 
vast swath ofCongo’s copper 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/congo-copper-exports-rose-10-last-year-as-china-drives-output?cmpid=BBD030726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260307>
 and cobalt production and Zimbabwe’s lithium industry. In 2024, Congo did $28 
billion of trade with China, dwarfing the US’s $1.6 billion. Companies from the 
Asian country accounted for 80% of Congo’s copper output last year.

Across sub-Saharan Africa, China’s annual trade flow of about $200 billion is 
four times that of the US. Supply chains running from mines in Africa to 
refineries and factories in China give Beijing a commanding lead. Whether it’s 
lithium in Zimbabwe, bauxite in Guinea or copper and cobalt in Congo, Chinese 
firms dominate.

High-profile deals and accords are one thing. But to shift the balance after 
years of assiduous investment and relationship building by China, the US will 
need to step up its game for many years — if not decades — to come.—Antony 
Sguazzin <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ABwnnzA9x6k/antony-sguazzin> and 
William Clowes <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ATTnI1unX8M/william-clowes>
