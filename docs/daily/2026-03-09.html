Daily Brief

Monday, March 09, 2026

US

The dominant story for US markets on March 9 is the cascading economic fallout from the US-Israeli war against Iran, now in its tenth day, compounded by a dismal February employment report that preceded the conflict's intensification. Brent crude surged as high as $119.50 per barrel intraday before partially retracing after reports of a prospective G7 coordinated strategic reserve release, but remained firmly above $100. S&P 500 futures declined 0.9%, a relatively contained move compared with broader global equity markets, though approximately $6 trillion in global market capitalization has been erased since hostilities commenced. The February payrolls report, released Friday, delivered an unambiguous negative surprise: private sector payrolls recorded their first decline in five years, while the unemployment rate rose. Under normal circumstances, such data would reinforce expectations for Federal Reserve easing and satisfy political pressure from the Trump administration to cut rates. Instead, the implied fed funds rate in overnight index swap markets has risen over the past week, as oil-driven inflation expectations overwhelm the labor market signal. Oxford Economics maintains its baseline of Fed inaction until June, with two cuts thereafter, but acknowledges the war materially clouds the path. The February CPI report due Wednesday is expected to show benign core readings of approximately 0.2% month-on-month — a pre-war snapshot that will offer limited forward guidance given oil's near-30% surge in March alone. The stagflationary tension is the central macro challenge: deteriorating labor markets argue for accommodation while an energy price shock argues against it. ING's Carsten Brzeski characterized this as a second supply-side inflation shock arriving while tariff pass-through remains unresolved. Consumer balance sheets were already showing stress — New York Fed data on credit card delinquencies indicated elevated household financial pressure — before gasoline prices reached their highest level in more than 18 months. President Trump dismissed the oil price surge as "a very small price to pay" for safety and peace, though midterm elections in November create political sensitivity around an affordability squeeze that the administration had previously sought to contain. On the corporate side, Hims & Hers Health surged 49% after Novo Nordisk agreed to distribute its weight-loss drugs on the telehealth platform, ending a prolonged commercial dispute. Live Nation is reportedly close to settling its DOJ antitrust case on terms that would preserve the Ticketmaster business. Airline stocks fell more than 3% across American, Delta, and United on fuel cost exposure.

Asia

Asian equity markets absorbed the sharpest session losses of any region on Monday. The MSCI Asia-Pacific index fell 3.7%, extending its weekly decline to nearly 10%. Japan's Nikkei 225 dropped 5.2%, with the index now in correction territory from its recent highs, as the oil shock interacts with a structurally weak yen to amplify Japan's import cost burden — raising the risk of stagflation in an economy where activity was already sluggish. South Korea's Kospi fell 6%, with Seoul reporting that US military transport aircraft, including C-17 and C-5 variants, had flown out of Osan Air Base in recent days amid discussions around potential redeployment of American assets as the Iran conflict broadens. Gauges in Pakistan and Vietnam fell more than 6%. The macro stress on Asia's energy-importing economies is acute. Fitch Ratings identified India and Pakistan as particularly exposed to deteriorating credit metrics from sustained high oil prices. India imports approximately 90% of its oil needs; a widening trade deficit and currency pressure compound an already fragile external position. South Korean President Lee Jae Myung called for fuel price caps, while the Philippines ordered government offices to operate four days weekly to reduce energy consumption and initiated talks with suppliers in the Americas, Australia, and Africa. Taiwan set a weekly cap on oil price increases. Vietnam faces potential airfare increases of up to 70% as jet fuel costs have tripled. China presents a more buffered picture. As the largest single buyer of Hormuz-transiting oil, China holds substantial strategic petroleum reserves that analysts believe can absorb near-term supply disruption. Separately, Chinese consumer price growth accelerated to its fastest pace in over three years. On the diplomatic front, Foreign Minister Wang Yi described 2026 as a potentially defining year for US-China relations, signaling Beijing's intent to insulate the bilateral relationship from the Iran conflict despite maintaining an upgraded partnership with Tehran. China simultaneously warned Japan of unspecified consequences after Tokyo allowed a visit by Taiwan's Premier Cho Jung-tai — a reminder that regional geopolitical tensions remain layered beneath the Iran-dominated headlines. Taiwan's dollar fell 0.8% on the session.

Europe

European markets are confronting a dual shock: soaring energy prices threatening to reignite inflation, and a deteriorating growth backdrop that was already fragile. Interest rate markets moved sharply; swaps now imply two full 25 basis point ECB rate hikes in 2026, compared with one priced as recently as Friday. The first hike is fully priced by June. For the Bank of England, money markets shifted from pricing cuts to a 70% probability of a hike by year-end, as estimates from ING and RSM UK suggest UK inflation could rise to more than double the 2% target if current energy price levels persist. The ECB and BOE face a genuinely uncomfortable position: easing into an energy-driven inflation shock risks credibility loss, while tightening risks amplifying a growth slowdown. German economic data released Monday added to the difficult backdrop. Both industrial production and factory orders declined at the start of the year, tempering expectations for a cyclical recovery in Europe's largest economy. Germany's chemical industry faces a structural crisis that predates the Iran conflict — higher energy costs following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, rising carbon prices, new Chinese capacity, and softening demand have already forced plant closures — but the war materially intensifies pressure. European chemical producers are estimated to pay approximately €3 billion more annually in natural gas costs if the post-war price surge persists. The Leuna chemical park in eastern Germany, exposed through its dependence on energy and raw material imports, has become a test case. In politics, Chancellor Friedrich Merz suffered a notable setback as his CDU lost the Baden-Württemberg regional election to the ruling Greens — a state the CDU had been projected to reclaim after 15 years of Green governance. The result compounds Merz's domestic political difficulties and reflects broader voter unease. In the UK, Prime Minister Starmer pledged support for households facing energy cost pressures. German bund futures slid to an almost 15-year low, with Italian fiscal credibility also identified as at risk from a prolonged Middle East conflict by Scope Ratings.

Global

The defining development of March 9 is Iran's selection of Mojtaba Khamenei — the 56-year-old son of the assassinated Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, with deep ties to the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps — as the new supreme leader. The appointment, ratified by Iran's Assembly of Experts, signals continuity of hardline governance rather than any movement toward negotiated resolution. Prediction markets place a 48% probability on no ceasefire occurring before the end of April. President Trump described himself as "not happy" with the selection and is reportedly weighing deployment of special forces to seize Iran's near-bomb-grade uranium, amid US official concern that enriched stockpiles may have been relocated since UN inspectors last verified their position nine months ago. Tehran has stated it can sustain high-intensity conflict for at least six months. Brent crude surged intraday to $119.50 — the largest intraday move in percentage terms since April 2020 — before retreating to near $104-105 after the Financial Times reported G7 finance ministers would convene to discuss a coordinated strategic petroleum reserve release. OECD nations collectively hold over 1.2 billion barrels in emergency reserves; the announcement provided meaningful short-term relief, though Citigroup estimates the Hormuz closure is removing approximately 16 million barrels per day of crude and refined products from global markets — roughly 16% of world supply — a disruption that dwarfs the 2022 Russian shock. Saudi Arabia has begun reducing output as Hormuz closure fills storage tanks; Iraq, the UAE, and Kuwait have similarly curtailed production. QatarEnergy has delayed a major LNG expansion project to at least 2027 following drone attacks on the Ras Laffan facility. Beyond energy, the war is disrupting approximately one-fifth of global air freight, compressing Asia-Europe container traffic, and creating acute stress in fertilizer markets. Iran accounts for approximately 5% and 11% of global urea and ammonia trade respectively; urea prices have risen 25% since hostilities began, reaching levels last seen during Russia's Ukraine invasion. Crop prices are rising in parallel, threatening a global food inflation impulse that will materialize with a lag. The IMF has urged policymakers to prepare for a prolonged conflict scenario. European natural gas futures jumped as much as 30% on Monday. Aluminum briefly hit a near four-year high, and coal prices reached their highest level in over a year as gas substitution effects propagated through power markets.
