Europe's market reaction to the ceasefire was broadly positive, with equity futures and bond prices both rallying as WTI crude fell approximately 16% on the day. European natural gas and diesel prices briefly slumped around 20%, reflecting the intensity of the war premium that had built up over the prior six weeks. The relief, however, is tempered by genuine uncertainty about how quickly energy flows through the Strait of Hormuz will normalize, and European industry remains more directly exposed to the supply shock than its US counterpart.

UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer traveled to the Gulf Wednesday for meetings with regional leaders, seeking to leverage Britain's role in organizing multilateral diplomatic efforts around Hormuz — the UK chaired a meeting of approximately 35 nations to coordinate pressure on Iran, notably in the absence of the US. Starmer has also used his government's early divergence from Washington's position on the war as a domestic political asset ahead of local elections, criticizing both Reform UK's Nigel Farage and Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch for their initial support of the US-Israeli campaign. Britain faces its own economic pressures: real wages are stagnating, consumer prices are elevated, unemployment appears set to rise, and the Bank of England's rate is expected to remain well above the fed funds rate by year-end as UK inflation stays comparatively sticky.

In Germany, measures to alleviate surging fuel costs have centered on regulatory adjustments rather than fiscal outlays. European inflation dynamics differ somewhat from the US: the euro zone's five-year/five-year inflation breakeven has risen but remains below 2%, though the gap has widened relative to the US since the war began. France moved to fast-track emergency agricultural legislation to address a deteriorating food trade balance, while EDF pledged €240 million to accelerate heat pump and electric truck adoption as subdued electricity demand and continued renewable buildout have depressed power prices. Moody's cut the outlook on Blue Owl Capital's flagship fund to negative, adding to broader signals of stress in private capital markets.
