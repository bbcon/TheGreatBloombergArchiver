Bessent Shifts on Rates

After repeatedly indicating for the better part of a year that conditions 
called for the Federal Reserve tolower rates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-30/bessent-says-current-yields-mean-no-sense-in-long-debt-ramp-up?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has a new message: It’s all right for the US 
central bank to hold off.

Twice in two days, Bessent said he would understand it if the Fed doesn’t 
proceed with reducing borrowing costs as it awaits more information about the 
impact the Iran war is having on the US.

“If they need, for their own sanity, to wait on interest-rate cuts, I 
understand it,” Bessent said on CNBC Wednesday. The day before, he told 
reporters during a roundtable that “if they want to wait for some clarity, I 
understand that,” Politicoreported 
<https://www.politico.com/news/2026/04/14/bessent-fed-chair-interest-rates-iran-inflation-warsh-00870941>
.

The immediate backdrop, of course, is the surge in energy prices that’s come 
from the Iran war. Last week’s CPIinflation report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-10/us-cpi-surges-0-9-in-largest-monthly-jump-since-2022-on-gas?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 showed the biggest monthly jump in the headline gauge since 2022. While the 
core CPI suggested there’s little to worry about, the other main monthly price 
measure — the PCE — has lately been pointing to signs of continuingprice 
pressures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/us-consumer-spending-barely-rose-as-inflation-lingered-pre-war?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
, adding to the case for patience on the part of US monetary policymakers.

Bessent said on CNBC that once energy costs come back down, “that means 
they’re just going to be able to do more” with regard to rate cuts.
Scott BessentPhotographer: Mattie Neretin/Bloomberg
Beyond the data, though, there’s another bit of context for the Treasury 
chief’s comments: Time is ticking down toward the end of Jerome Powell’s 
official term as Fed chair, and — assuming the Trump administration can somehow 
get pasta roadblock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/us-prosecutors-made-unannounced-visit-to-federal-reserve-in-dc?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 to his Senate confirmation — the beginning of Kevin Warsh’s turn at the helm.

Should Warsh be in place by the time of the June Fed policy meeting, that may 
be a little early for a majority of the rate-setting Federal Open Market 
Committee to support the idea of a resumption of rate cuts. While oil prices 
are currently off the highs reached in recent weeks, they remain substantially 
above those back in February. Bessent himself on Wednesday indicated it might 
take some time forgasoline prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/bessent-optimistic-gas-will-drop-to-3-per-gallon-over-summer?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 to come down.

And if a majority of the FOMC isn’t quite ready to back rate cuts, the 
question for Warsh would be, would he plug for a reduction anyway — as 
President Donald Trump has long called for — and get outvoted? That would be an 
event the Fed hasn’t seen in decades.

When Paul Volcker got outvoted in 1986, he considered resigning <> as Fed 
chair. Befitting his fame for vanquishing the high inflation of the 1970s, his 
dissent was against lowering rates. He did that having been in charge of the 
Fed for almost seven years. In Warsh’s case, it would be on his first vote in 
the job, potentially raising questions about his sway at the institution at the 
very start of his tenure.

Bessent on Tuesday told reporters that Warsh could “lead the next cycle” at 
the Fed. Does that mean Warsh will be given some space from the Trump 
administration for the data to come round and help him marshal an FOMC majority 
behind rate cuts? Bessent hasn’t spelled that all out.

But Warsh himself has the opportunity of laying out his views on a potential 
rate-cut timeline when he appears for hisconfirmation hearing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/warsh-hearing-for-fed-chair-set-for-next-week-scott-says?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 at the Senate Banking Committee, scheduled for April 21. Stay tuned.

Need-to-Know Research

Fifty-four countries with populations totaling 3.4 billion people spend more 
on debt servicing than on health or education — that eye-opening statistic 
offers a window into a potential area of fragility in the global economy that’s 
worth tracking more closely, according to UN Trade and Development (UNCTAD 
<https://unctad.org/topic/debt-and-finance/borrowers-platform>).

The agency launched a web portal to help policymakers boost their capacity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-04-12/imf-world-bank-to-meet-in-dc-this-week-as-iran-shock-looms?cmpid=BBD041626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 to address public debt challenges, improve sustainability measures and 
increase transparency. External debt hit $11.7 trillion in 2024 — roughly the 
GDP of Japan, Germany and the UK — while debt servicing costs increased to 
about $920 billion, according to the research tool.

“People deserve better outcomes. They’re not asking for charity,” said Rebeca 
Grynspan, UNCTAD’s secretary-general. “They want a level playing field where 
finance enables development rather than constraining it.”
