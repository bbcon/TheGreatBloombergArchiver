Warning to the World

Central banks across much of the developed world have spent the past year or 
so dissecting weak productivity and soft output. Now, one of them has been 
forced to take action as a result.

Australia’s rate hike this month wasn’t a response to runaway growth. Instead, 
it followed adowngrade <bbg://news/stories/T9UYTNT96OSH> to productivity and 
output forecasts, alongside an upward revision to the neutral rate — the level 
consistent with stable inflation — which policymakers now judge to be higher 
than previously thought.

In effect, officials concluded the economy’s non-inflationary capacity is more 
limited than assumed. The question now is whether Australia is an outlier or a 
forerunner, after becoming thefirst major monetary authority 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-03/australia-raises-key-interest-rate-to-combat-inflation-pressure?cmpid=BBD021726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 to tighten in 2026.

If lagging productivity constrains the ability of economies to generate 
moderate growth without sparking an inflation outbreak, the Reserve Bank of 
Australia’s caution may prove prescient — an early recognition that the neutral 
rate is higher than previously believed.

In Australia’s case, swelling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/australia-s-fiscal-bonanza-now-rivals-mining-boom-westpac-warns?cmpid=BBD021726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 government spending and an economy heavily reliant on migration have boosted 
demand without delivering a commensurate lift in productivity. A recent report 
from Westpac showed government outlays now account for a record 35% of gross 
domestic product, around 7 percentage points higher than a decade ago, a surge 
that rivals the 2000s mining boom.

“A larger and more persistent public footprint implies tighter near term 
capacity, a marginally negative, though uncertain in net terms, impact on trend 
productivity growth, and a significant reallocation of labor and capital across 
industries,” Westpac’s Pat Bustamante wrote in thereport 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/australia-s-fiscal-bonanza-now-rivals-mining-boom-westpac-warns?cmpid=BBD021726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
.

That mix leaves inflation more sensitive to steady, rather than spectacular, 
growth of somewhere around 2%.Minutes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/rba-says-material-shift-in-economic-outlook-drove-rate-hike?cmpid=BBD021726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 of the RBA’s February meeting released Tuesday showed the decision to raise 
rates was based in-part on policymakers belief that some of the recent 
inflation pressures would stick around because the economy is running closer to 
its limits.

Central banks from the Europe to New Zealand have acknowledged weak 
productivity and softer potential growth, but rate decisions have remained 
framed primarily around demand dynamics and the pace of disinflation.

The contrast is even sharper with the US, where productivity outcomes are 
better than most other developed economies. There, markets and economists are 
sticking to bets onfurther rate cuts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/dollar-gains-as-traders-question-pricing-for-three-fed-rate-cuts?cmpid=BBD021726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 even as the economy and employment remain buoyant.

Grilled by parliamentarians after the unpopular rate hike, RBA officials have 
pointed to the supply limits and resulting weaker potential growth. Former Bank 
of England official and now RBA Deputy Governor Andrew Hauserlast week 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/rba-s-hauser-warns-inflation-is-too-high-can-t-let-it-persist?cmpid=BBD021726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 said it’s an open question whether Australia is a “first mover” confronting 
pressures others will face or merely “a complete idiosyncratic case”?

If productivity constraints broaden across advanced economies, the implication 
is uncomfortable: Neutral rates may be higher than markets assume, and policy 
could remain tighter even in the absence of exuberant growth.
