The Federal Reserve's April meeting, Jerome Powell's last as chair, produced the most fractious policy vote since October 1992, with four FOMC members dissenting — three regional presidents (Beth Hammack, Neel Kashkari, and Lorie Logan) objecting to any residual easing bias in the forward guidance, and Stephen Miran dissenting on separate grounds. Rates were left unchanged, as expected. The episode signals that incoming chair Kevin Warsh will inherit a committee disinclined to accommodate the rate cuts President Trump has sought: futures markets no longer price any easing in 2026, and traders now assign roughly 50% odds to a rate increase by April 2027. Two-year Treasury yields jumped 11 basis points on the day, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, while 30-year yields reached 5% for the first time this year.

Adding to the institutional complexity, Powell announced he will remain on the Fed board as a governor under Warsh until the Justice Department's investigation into the Fed's building renovation is "well and truly over." Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized the decision as a "violation of all Federal Reserve norms," though the parallel to Marriner Eccles — who remained as governor after leaving the chair — somewhat undercuts that framing. Powell's stated intention is to maintain a low profile and support the incoming chair, but the presence of a former chair with two years remaining on his board term, amid a deeply divided committee, will complicate Warsh's ability to quickly shift policy direction.

On the corporate side, Wednesday's mega-cap earnings delivered a differentiated verdict. Alphabet surged more than 7% after reporting revenues and profits ahead of estimates, with its custom TPU chip architecture seen as a credible challenge to Nvidia's dominance in AI inference workloads. Meta fell roughly 7% in after-hours trade despite strong advertising revenue of $56.3 billion, after raising its 2026 capex guidance by $10 billion to potentially above $145 billion — a level investors deemed excessive relative to near-term returns. Amazon's cloud unit AWS posted 28% revenue growth, its fastest in over three years, and was received more favorably. Microsoft's Azure growth was viewed as underwhelming, with shares slipping modestly. The combined 2026 capex envelope for the four hyperscalers now approaches $725 billion, sustaining but not expanding AI-related enthusiasm.
