After peace talks between the US and Iran floundered last weekend, leaders 
from all corners of the globe rushed to speak to one man: Xi Jinping.

China’s president had his busiest week in nearly two years 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/xi-welcomes-slew-of-world-leaders-as-trump-fights-with-allies?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
, excluding major summits, as he welcomed at least five high-profile officials.
Xi Jinping meets with Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez in Beijing on April 
14.Photographer: Haruna Furuhashi/Pool/Getty Images
In some of his first public comments on the war, Xi lamented the “crumbling” 
world order 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/china-s-xi-urges-closer-spain-ties-to-counter-global-disarray?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 in a meeting with Spain’s prime minister, proposed a four-point peace plan for 
the Middle East with Abu Dhabi’s crown prince, and vowed to deepen ties with 
Moscow in a meeting with Russia’s foreign minister.

The rush of diplomatic traffic to Beijing underscores a quiet but growing 
expectation among international players that Xi shouldplay a more active role 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/china-s-wang-yi-urges-talks-to-sustain-fragile-iran-us-ceasefire?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 in calming a world edging deeper into conflict.

His potential as a stabilizing force was thrown into sharp relief by President 
Donald Trump, who spent the weekberating one-time allies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/bessent-and-uk-s-reeves-at-odds-over-iran-war-ahead-of-meeting?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 in the UK and Italy, not to mention the American pope.

But Xi’s diplomatic efforts have so far fallen short of the more concrete 
initiatives pressing ahead elsewhere. France and the UK areconvening talks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/france-and-germany-split-on-europe-s-role-in-securing-hormuz?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 on opening up the Strait of Hormuz to shipping, while Japan is offering a$10 
billion financial package 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/japan-may-extend-10-billion-to-southeast-asia-to-help-buy-oil?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 to help Southeast Asian nations hit by soaring oil prices.

China’s position is more complex.

Beijing wants to be seen as a credible force for peace — building on past wins 
like brokering a détente between Saudi Arabia and Iran in 2023 — yet remains 
reluctant tofully step into volatile conflicts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/spain-s-sanchez-calls-for-chinese-diplomacy-to-solve-iran-war?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
.

Tellingly, state media pushed back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-11/china-s-role-in-iran-ceasefire-is-overstated-cctv-account-says?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 against claims that China nudged Tehran to the negotiating table.

As Trump talks up a deal to end the Iran war, the question for China isn’t 
just whether it can convene power — but whether it’s willing to use it. —
Rebecca Choong Wilkins 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AT8YdRfOdJk/rebecca-choong-wilkins>

Orbanism

Chart of the Day

Israel’s compensation fund, a tax-agency department providing financial relief 
to civilians and businesses for property damage inflicted during wars, has 
deployed dozens of teams across the nation to assess damage atsites impacted by 
missiles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/iran-war-how-israel-assesses-damage-from-missile-and-rocket-attacks?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 and rockets fired from Iran and by Hezbollah, its Lebanon-based proxy. Since 
the Hamas attacks in October 2023, the fund says it has handled about 1 million 
claims and made roughly 30 billion shekels ($10 billion) in payments — equal to 
about 1.5% of Israel’s GDP.

And Finally

Tankers and cargo ships are facing 3 1/2-day waits to enter the Panama Canal 
as the Iran war sparks a surge in traffic, prompting one vessel to plunk downan 
extra $4 million 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/panama-canal-traffic-jam-spurs-4-million-line-jumping-payment?cmpid=BBD041726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 to jump to the front of the line. The queue at the 50-mile (80-kilometer) 
waterway — the longest since a historic 2023-2024 drought that slashed vessel 
passages — has grown as the near-shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz strangled 
oil, natural gas, fertilizer and chemical shipments from Persian Gulf nations.
