Europe’s Depleted Natural Gas Tanks Spur Energy Security Worries
Depleted inventories spell another challenging summer for the continent’s gas 
markets.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Priscila Azevedo Rocha looks at Europe’s depleted gas stockpiles.

When energy traders arrived in Essen, Germany this week for the annual E-world 
trade fair and conference, many were sounding the alarm over the continent’s 
depleted natural gas inventories.

There was an element of déjà vu — similar concerns dominated the event a year 
go 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-13/gas-traders-leave-essen-hungry-for-more-on-german-storage-plans?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
 — but the situation has worsened. With six weeks to go until the end of the 
heating season, Europe’s storage tanks are set to emerge from this winter even 
more depleted than in 2025.

Natural gas inventories play a vital role during winter, meeting about a third 
of demand. When consumption surges during a cold snap, fuel stored in tanks 
supplements the regular daily flow from pipelines and liquefied natural gas 
terminals. It’s normal for storage to decline in winter and be refilled in 
summer.

Yet there’s little financial incentive right now for buyers to purchase fuel 
to inject into storage sites during the warmer months. Summer gas prices are 
still slightly higher than those for next winter, setting the stage for another 
challenging injection season.

“It is clear that the storages that are quite low are out of the money,” Helle 
Ostergaard Kristiansen, senior vice president for gas and power at Equinor ASA, 
said in an interview.

Discussions across the conference halls centered around the need for state 
intervention, with some traders even advocating for gas tanks to be treated as 
a matter ofnational security 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/germany-to-push-for-end-of-gas-storage-targets-after-2027-expiry?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
.

In Germany, home of the region’s biggest storage sites where inventories are 
now only a quarter full, a debate about the government’s role isunder way 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/german-conservatives-want-stronger-state-role-in-gas-storage?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
. But there’s a fear that these discussions might be too little, too late 
because any legislation required to foster changes would take months to pass.

There’s a risk that more storage sites could close if no state support comes 
in time. Germany’s Uniper SE has alreadysubmitted a request 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-02/uniper-seeks-permission-to-close-key-german-gas-storage-site?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
 to the regulator to shut down its loss-making facility, Breitbrunn, a move 
followed by Bayernugs GmbH’s site in Wolfersberg.

If winter conditions remain around average up to the end of April, Europe will 
need approximately 130 more LNG cargoes than last year to fill the storage gap 
this summer, saidMarco Saalfrank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/20863656>, head of merchant trading 
at Axpo.

The bloc’s LNG imports have already surged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/gas-hungry-europe-to-get-rare-lng-shipment-reloaded-from-china?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
 this year and moreproduction 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-08/global-lng-market-faces-looming-supply-glut-after-years-of-scarcity?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
 is expected to come online, even as some key projectsface delays 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/qatar-pushes-start-of-its-massive-lng-expansion-to-end-2026?cmpid=BBBXT021226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260212>
. For now, the supply balance remains tight and forecasts for a colder end to 
winter could drive up consumption.

It all adds up to another costly and challenging storage-injection season for 
Europe in the months ahead.

—Priscila Azevedo Rocha, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Jet fuel demand is likely to surge even as oil consumption peaks early in the 
next decade. BloombergNEF estimates use of jet fuel is likely to rise 56% from 
2024 levels by the middle of the century to 10.8 million barrels a day. Crude 
demand by contrast is set to peak in 2032 at 104 million barrels a day.
