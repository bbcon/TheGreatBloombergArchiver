A famous diagram <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Maslow%27s_hierarchy_of_needs> 
shows human needs as a pyramid, with the most important ones like food and 
shelter at its foundation. Jensen Huang has now come up with something similar 
to “Maslow’s hierarchy,” but for AI. And it all rests on electricity.

In a Davos presentation last month, the Nvidia Corp. boss unveiled his vision 
of the artificial intelligence buildout in the form of a “five-layer cake 
<https://blogs.nvidia.com/blog/davos-wef-blackrock-ceo-larry-fink-jensen-huang/>
.” Near the top is where economic value is created, via the models that drive 
AI and the applications that make it work for businesses. But the base layer – 
the sponge in the cake — is energy.

Right now that means electricity, with power-hungry data centers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-12/data-centers-will-need-3-trillion-through-2030-moody-s-says?cmpid=BBD021426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260214>
 springing up around the world to drive the new technology. Those centers are 
effectively competing with humans, who of course rely on electric power to heat 
and cool their homes and increasingly to get around in cars or trains. The 
obvious conclusion is, we’re going to need a lot more of it.

That’s also the key finding of the new annual report on power production and 
markets by the International Energy Agency – which heralds the dawning of an “
Age of Electricity <https://www.iea.org/reports/electricity-2026>.” In the 
coming five years, the IEA predicts global demand for power is set to grow 
roughly 50% faster than it did during the previous decade – and more than twice 
as fast as energy demand overall.

It’s also set to outpace gross domestic product, marking “a fundamental shift 
in the longstanding relationship between electricity demand and economic 
activity.” Governments around the world have only begun to grasp this 
realization, spurred in part by voter concerns. It all augurs major shifts in 
policies, and potentiallyelectoral politics 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-26/us-power-electricity-is-becoming-a-big-deal-in-politics?cmpid=BBD021426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260214>
.

Put simply, economies are becoming more electrified. AI is a big part of the 
story, but not all of it. Electric cars and buses are a fast-growing share of 
the global fleet. Countries like the UK that have environmental targets are 
encouraging its use in home-heating.China <> — which is adding generating 
capacity at a breakneck pace — is the pioneer when it comes to electric-powered 
industrial machines.

All of this creates opportunities to lower both carbon emissions and costs – 
but also huge challenges for governments, which in most countries play at least 
a significant regulatory role in power production and distribution. Sometimes 
they outright control it.

Those economic challenges can involve both international politics and the 
domestic kind. In Europe, both have been on display of late.

Germany appears to be regretting its environmentalist-driven shift away from 
nuclear power in recent decades. Amid growing concern about the competitiveness 
of the country’s once-powerhouse industries, Chancellor Friedrich Merz this 
week sounded amore pragmatic note 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/merz-says-eu-should-be-open-to-revising-carbon-market?cmpid=BBD021426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260214>
 on climate politics. He said the European Union should be ready to rework its 
flagship carbon-market plan if it begins to weigh on business.

Elevated power prices are seen as a key reason why Germany has been losing its 
industrial edge. One contributing factor was Russia’s full-scale invasion of 
Ukraine, and the resulting Europe-wide shunning of cheap Russian gas. A key 
replacement has been American LNG. But since the US is no longer the most 
reliable partner in European eyes, there’s a risk that one kind of geopolitical 
leverage has merely been exchanged for another.

In this respect, if no other, Europe’s status as an also-ran in the AI race 
has some kind of silver lining. Had it been keeping pace with the US and China 
in the data-center buildout, instead offalling well behind 
<https://think.ing.com/articles/data-centre-divide-why-europes-shortfall-threatens-future-economic-growth/>
, there would be even more strain on its power system.

Over in the US, meanwhile, there’s a different kind of electrical problem.

President Donald Trump has made AI a priority, insisting the US will win the 
race against China to develop the strategically crucial technology. And in some 
areas of Huang’s five-layer cake (besides energy it includes semiconductors, 
cloud infrastructure, AI models and applications) American competitiveness is 
strong.

But at the bottom of the cake, China definitely has an advantage. It’s 
electricity output build-out is going much faster than America’s. Onetime-Trump 
lieutenant Elon Musk has been among thoseworrying aloud 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/china-ramps-up-energy-boom-flagged-by-musk-as-key-to-ai-race?cmpid=BBD021426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260214>
 about it.

The US also faces political constraints. The cost of living is shaping up as a 
key issue in November’s midterm elections, as voters struggle withsurging 
electricity bills 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-07/us-affordability-americans-enjoy-cheaper-gas-but-utility-bills-are-climbing?cmpid=BBD021426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260214>
. The data center boom is one reason they’re soaring.

And that’s not likely to change anytime soon, according to a study this week 
by Goldman Sachs Group Inc. economists Manuel Abecasis and Hongcen Wei. They 
predict data centers will account for 40% of new power demand over the next 
five years, and expect electricity-price inflation to hold around 6% through 
2027 – likely high enough to cause headaches for Trump and the GOP.

Trump has sought to demonstrate he’s on top of the problem 
<https://www.politico.com/news/2026/02/09/trump-administration-eyes-data-center-agreements-amid-energy-price-spikes-00772024>
, with an initiative to ensure that rich tech companies building the data 
centers are also investing to produce the electricity they’ll need. And that 
may prove a key measure: the Goldman economists warned that, if non-AI 
customers end up bearing a bigger share of those power capex costs, electricity 
inflation will be even higher.

To put it in terms of Huang’s cake: those looking forward to eating the upper 
layers had better help pay for the flour.—Ben Holland 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AExYCI7r_48/ben-holland>
