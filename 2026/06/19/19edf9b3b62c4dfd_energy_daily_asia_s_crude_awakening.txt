Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Oil Trading Team Leader Serene Cheong explains why the biggest buyers of 
Middle Eastern crude might have reservations about a sudden surge in supply.

With oil exports expected to ramp up following a reopening of the Strait of 
Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/us-ends-hormuz-blockade-downplays-tolls-as-negotiations-restart?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
, starved buyers in Asia could suddenly face a different challenge: too much 
crude, too soon.

Traffic through the strait jumped yesterday, giving a preview of what might be 
to come. Although transitslater thinned 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-19/hormuz-traffic-thins-friday-as-shipowners-err-on-side-of-safety?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
, around 40 supertankers, laden with almost 80 million barrels of oil, await the
go-ahead to exit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-19/supertankers-with-80-million-barrels-of-oil-ready-to-pass-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
 the Persian Gulf.

Most will be destined for Asia. The continent is home to the biggest long-term 
buyers of crude from the Middle East, where producers are pressing customers to
take their cargoes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-19/abu-dhabi-tells-buyers-to-load-oil-shipments-from-inside-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
 or face penalties.

These vessels, however, were loaded just before the war began in late February 
and in the weeks that followed. They were meant to arrive from March. When they 
didn’t, refiners were forced to either replace lost barrels with costly 
alternatives or cut operating rates.

Now, with the flotilla poised to sail eastward at a moment’s notice, Asian 
buyers could be about to find themselves with too much prompt supply.

Those with large storage facilities — Japan, China and South Korea — will see 
an opportunity toreplenish their stockpiles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-10/china-taps-commercial-oil-stockpiles-to-help-weather-gulf-shock?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
. But others, such as India, may be forced to cancel or resell cargoes; 
otherwise, they’ll face hefty demurrage fees for keeping oil-laden ships 
waiting offshore.

In addition to these tankers, Gulf producers such as Iraq and Kuwait 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/iraq-takes-steps-to-boost-oil-exports-when-hormuz-reopens?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
, which were unable to sell new cargoes for months, will be keen for customers 
to book more vessels as soon as possible.

Loadings will free up some terminal storage space, allowing these suppliers to 
crank up production 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/kuwait-starts-oil-output-hike-to-top-2-million-barrels-soon?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
 and rebuild lost revenue streams.

Buyers, on the other hand, are less excited. It won’t be cheap to secure an 
empty tanker 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/empty-tankers-entering-gulf-via-hormuz-is-key-for-energy-market?cmpid=BBBXT061926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260619&utm_content=5733>
 in the region, while barrels sourced from the Gulf are still expensive, taking 
their cue from high official prices and benchmarks. That’s a double whammy for 
refiners who see ample, affordable supplies in the spot market.

So while buyers may be relieved to see the oil market take a first step back 
to its prewar status, there are plenty of challenges ahead before normality can 
be restored.

—Serene Cheong, Bloomberg News
