Christine Lagarde is reportedly considering stepping down ahead of the official conclusion of her ECB term in October 2027, a move that would preserve outgoing French President Emmanuel Macron's ability to influence the choice of successor before a potential hard-right electoral outcome in France next year. The report has not been confirmed but has accelerated a succession debate that carries both policy and political dimensions. The hawkish Bundesbank-aligned governor Isabel Schnabel stated she intends to serve out her term, while Spain is actively campaigning for its own candidate. Dutch governor Klaas Knot appears the most plausible consensus choice. The process threatens to expose the fault lines — hawks versus doves, national versus supranational interests — that European institutions routinely struggle to manage.

The euro's recent strength provides some context for Lagarde's potential legacy. In real effective terms, the currency has reached its strongest level in over a decade, reflecting both the weak dollar environment and a partial recovery of French sovereign spreads. The OAT-Bund spread has retraced much of its post-2024 snap-election widening, suggesting that financial markets have become more comfortable with France's political situation than the underlying fiscal dynamics strictly warrant.

Airbus shares fell 6.7% in Paris after the company disclosed that engine supplier Pratt & Whitney has failed to deliver engines in sufficient quantities, forcing a reduction in aircraft output. The development is a material setback for a company managing record order backlogs, and shifts some accountability to the supply chain rather than demand. Nestlé gained 4.5% in Zurich after new CEO Philipp Navratil signaled the turnaround is on track and guided for 3-4% organic revenue growth in 2026. Novo Nordisk's approximately 5,000 job cuts — among the largest layoffs in Danish history — are beginning to weigh on consumer sentiment in Denmark ahead of a general election due before October.
