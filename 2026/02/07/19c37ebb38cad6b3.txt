Globalization Is Alive and Well in the Middle East

These could hardly be considered the best of times for global trade, with 
globalization practically a dirty word in some circles and protectionism on the 
rise. Yet one region of the planet is seeing an outright boom: the Middle East.

Led by members of the Gulf Cooperation Council, which includes the United Arab 
Emirates and Saudi Arabia, the Middle East saw its trade climb at an annual 
rate of 15% from 2021-24, according to a tally by Standard Chartered 
economists. By outpacing the 9% global pace, the region’s market share 
increased. More recent figures show trade continues to climb.

The growth reflects deepening ties with Asia especially, and with Africa as it 
emerges as a notable source of imports. The Asia-Middle East trade corridor 
will see commerce reach $1.5 trillion by 2030, up from $905 billion in 2024, 
Bader Al Sarraf, Carla Slim and Ethan Lester at Standard Chartered wrote in a 
note this week. Trade with Africa is expected to roughly double, to $260 
billion.

A combination of rising consumer demand plus the region’s increasing role as 
an intercontinental logistics hub — an analogue to the function its airports 
have long served in international passenger travel — is helping transform how 
the rest of the world views the Middle East: No longer just as a source of 
energy imports.

One product in particular illustrates the Middle East’s emergence as a trade 
bloc to pay attention to:Chinese vehicles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-17/china-s-involution-problem-how-price-wars-overcapacity-are-fueling-deflation?cmpid=BBD020726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260207>
. The United Arab Emirates, with a population of about 11 million people, 
imported about 560,000 passenger vehicles from China in 2025, Chinese official 
figures show.

That made the UAE the third-largest destination after Mexico, population 132 
million, and Russia, home to 145 million people and where imports of new cars 
from major exportersother than China are banned.

While Chinese cars are ubiquitous on roads in the Emirates, not all those 
imports were aimed for sale there — pointing to the UAE’s status as a transit 
hub given its strategic location between Asia, Africa and Europe, low trade 
barriers and developed infrastructure.

“Some are just first destinations, before they go on to the broader Middle 
East region,” said Bloomberg Intelligence analyst Joanna Chen. Saudi Arabia 
ranks among the top 10 importers of Chinese cars.

To handle rising volumes, the UAE is expanding logistical ties with Asia and 
Africa. Dubai-based DP World, one of the world’s largest seaport operators, 
manages a global network of economic zones and maritime services. The company’s 
$2.5 billion plan for 2025 sawprojects 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-29/dp-world-vies-for-deal-to-run-montreal-port-championed-by-carney?cmpid=BBD020726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260207>
 launched across India, Africa, South America and Europe.

That should help DP World continue to outpace the global trend. Between 2020 
and 2025, it saw 5.6% annual gains versus a world port volume total of 4.3%, 
according to Eleanor Hadland, a senior associate in maritime research at the 
consultancy Drewry. Much of that expansion has come from outside the Middle 
East. The five-year average growth rate was “strongest in the Asia Pacific and 
India region” at 7.5%, Hadland said.

Another sign of the UAE’s growing influence as a global trade hub is the 
competition emerging at home. DP World now faces a serious rival just an hour’s 
drive away, at Abu Dhabi’s Khalifa Port. Owned by ADQ, one of the region’s 
largest sovereign wealth funds, AD Ports hasgrown 
<bbg://news/stories/Q2ATU5T0G1KW> rapidly through joint-venture terminal 
operations with global shipping groups, including MSC from Europe, China’s 
Cosco Shipping and, mostrecently <bbg://news/stories/T5X6VYGQ7L0O>, France’s 
CMA CGM.

Meantime, Middle East trade growth with Africa is being propelled by precious 
metals — again with the UAE at the center. Imports of gold, diamonds and other 
precious materials doubled in 2024 compared with 2020, according to Standard 
Chartered. In 2025 the UAEranked <bbg://news/stories/T95W96KIJH8J> first 
globally in per-capita consumer gold purchases.

But the region has broad visions of deepening ties 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-07-08/the-uae-in-africa-power-influence-and-conflict?cmpid=BBD020726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260207>
 with its continental neighbor. Investors from the Middle East have been 
promising African nations billions of dollars in investments in renewable 
energy, logistics, technology, real estate and agriculture, eclipsing the likes 
of China and European countries in terms of pledged foreign direct investment.

More broadly, the Gulf’s increasing investment in its own economies is set to 
pull in more imports from many other regions of the world — though Saudi Arabia 
has recentlyreined in 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-28/saudi-arabia-enters-new-era-of-restraint-on-megaproject-spend?cmpid=BBD020726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260207>
 some of its grandest plans. Government-led digital transformation programs 
propelled “a notable increase in imports of servers and telecom hardware” in 
the first half of 2025, according to the World Trade Organization.

So in some parts of the world, globalization appears alive and well. —Andrey 
Biryukov <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASGDlI8qi6I/andrey-biryukov>
