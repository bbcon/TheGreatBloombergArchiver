The Other K-Pop

The global AI community breathed a sigh of relief when the world’s biggest 
supplier of memory chips averted a potentially disruptive labor dispute. That 
was quickly followed by gasps at headlines about Samsung Electronics’ 
semiconductor workers standing to win$340,000 bonuses 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/samsung-chip-workers-to-get-average-340-000-bonus-in-ai-boom?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
.

Peers at fellow South Korean chipmaker SK Hynix. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-23/-900-000-korean-chip-sector-bonuses-show-k-shaped-economy-risks?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
 are doing even better. The eye-popping compensation figures speak to the 
northeast Asian nation’s pivotal role in the artificial intelligence supply 
chain. But they also threaten to worsen a K-shaped dynamic, posing a challenge 
to policymakers — with potential lessons for governments round the world.

While just 1.5% of South Korea’s workforce is directly linked to the 
semiconductor industry, the outsize compensation figures for chip workers 
suggest a supercycle that’s starting to spill from corporate earnings to a 
segment of the household population.

“Korea’s chip-bonus surge is no longer just a firm-specific story — it is 
becoming a macroeconomic transmission mechanism, channeling corporate profits 
into household liquidity and feeding asset-price, wage and inflation 
pressures,” says Hyosung Kwon at Bloomberg Economics.

 * For Kwon’s full note on the Bloomberg terminal, click here <>. 
Wage growth could pick up more broadly if profit-sharing demands spread across 
the economy, Kwon says. A number of other unions have started to make such 
calls.

Economists have catalogued such sectoral income booms before, especially in 
resource-rich nations. Norway famously channeled oil surpluses into a giant 
wealth fund. With private-sector tech titans driving the earnings in this case 
— rather than state-regulated energy giants — the policy landscape is somewhat 
more complicated, says Louis Kuijs at S&P Global Ratings.

“Setting aside and saving a part of the windfall revenues using a rules-based 
approach would make sense,” says Kuijs, the firm’s chief Asia-Pacific economist.

Presidential policy chief Kim Yong-beom suggested in a Facebook post this 
month that excess tax revenues from the AI boom could someday be used for a “
citizen dividend 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/meet-the-man-who-set-off-debate-over-citizen-dividend-from-ai?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
.” And South Korea’s newcentral bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/bok-holds-rates-steady-amid-uncertain-outlook-for-policy?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
 head Shin Hyun Song discussed the trickle down effect from the chip bonanza on 
Thursday, noting bonuses such as those being paid to Samsung’s chip workers 
will generate income tax revenue that “ultimately benefits the public as a 
whole.”

“There are spillover effects through demand, but especially through fiscal 
channels, where the benefits ultimately return to the broader public,” he said.

South Korea possibly has yet to see the full impact of the cash getting 
generated by the country’s tech industry, with previous analysis suggesting 
that much of its current-account surplus is beingplowed into 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/circular-ai-boom-goes-global-as-asia-windfall-funds-hyperscalers?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
 US assets.

But economists warn that pressures are building. Frederic Neumann at HSBC says 
that “impressive headline growth in places like Korea and Taiwan is masking 
growing economic challenges in sectors not directly benefiting from booming 
demand for AI hardware.”

“This has led to a two-speed economy, further exacerbating the rise in income 
inequality seen during the K-shaped recovery coming out of the pandemic,” says 
Neumann, the bank’s chief Asia economist.

Need-to-Know Research

While US inflation has picked up strongly since the start of the Iran conflict 
— data Thursday will show what the Fed’spreferred gauge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-23/more-war-driven-inflation-seen-in-fed-s-favored-gauge?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
 did in March — the good thing for monetary policymakers is that the bond 
market has effectively moved to counter that impulse, according to Bloomberg 
Economics.

“The bond market may have done much of the heavy lifting” for the Fed, as 
10-year Treasury yields, which help setmortgage rates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/us-mortgage-rates-climb-to-highest-level-since-august?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
, are up roughly half a percentage point since Feb. 27,Anna Wong 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/AVZEA8jmBvs/anna-wong?cmpid=BBD052826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260528&utm_content=4877>
, chief US economist, wrote in a note. The Bloomberg Economics Global SHOK 
model indicates that’s tightened financial conditions by the equivalent of 75 
basis points of Fed hikes, she said.

Wong said the bulk of the rise in US yields is due to the so-called term 
premium, which is the extra yield investors are thought to demand for 
longer-dated securities rather than simply buying bills and rolling them over. 
“That distinction matters for the macro impact” she wrote. A higher term 
premium “tightens financial conditions directly” as it doesn’t imply higher 
growth or inflation expectations. “In that sense, it acts as a substitute for 
Fed tightening.”
