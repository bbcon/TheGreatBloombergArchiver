Winners, Losers Emerge as Trump’s Tariffs Take Hold
As US Trade Representative lauds a wheat shipment to Bangladesh, exports to 
China are floundering

For close observers of the upheaval US tariffs are causing, Tuesday provided 
views of both the benefits and drawbacks of President Donald Trump’s trade 
policies — one from the Port of Los Angeles and another from the White House 
lawn.

US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer provided the perspective from 
Washington, hailing the revival of an overseas customer for America’s farmers.

“We recently started wheat shipments to Bangladesh — hasn’t happened since 
2018,” Greertold CNBC 
<https://www.cnbc.com/video/2026/02/17/u-s-trade-rep-jamieson-greer-president-trumps-tariffs-policy-has-been-incredibly-successful.html>
, referring to the US’s 67th-largest export market last year and one of a 
handful of countries to havecompleted 
<https://www.cfr.org/articles/tracking-trumps-trade-deals?utm_source=substack&utm_medium=email>
 a trade deal recently with the Trump administration. “All these countries are 
bringing down their tariffs and trade barriers.”

Hours later on the opposite coast, Port of LA Executive Director Gene Seroka 
had a very different read from the nation’s busiest maritime trade gateway, 
which owes its top title to decades of booming transpacific commerce.

Read More: Port of LA Imports Slump as Trump Tariffs Disrupt Trade Flows 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/port-of-la-imports-slump-as-trump-tariffs-disrupt-trade-flows?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>

During a press briefing, he said container exports through the facility 
dropped 8% in January from a year earlier, posting the lowest monthly figure in 
almost three years.

“Exports to China look dismal,” said Seroka, 
<https://portoflosangeles.org/references/2026-news-releases/news_021726_jan_cargo>
 referring to the US’s third-largest export buyer and a country with whom Trump 
very much would like to forge a lasting deal. “Overall they’ve dropped 
considerably across the country.”

‘No Improvement’

Seroka said soybeans shipped from the Port of LA to China were down 80% last 
year, “with no improvement seen in either November or December” — after talks 
between the US and China resolved some short-term differences and started a
one-year truce 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-24/trump-tariffs-usmca-global-trade-poised-for-rocky-2026?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
.

In the meantime, competitors in South America grabbed that business.

“Argentina, Brazil have picked up a lot of the contracts for China on 
soybeans,” Seroka said. “These are agreements that will last three, six, 12 
months in duration, so it will be yet another cycle before the US soybean 
exporter has a chance to bid and get into the game.”

With such mixed results, the Trump administration is eager to show the upside 
of tariffs, as consumer sentimentdroops 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/us-consumer-sentiment-unexpectedly-rises-to-a-six-month-high?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
, lawmakersgrow nervous 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/us-house-defies-trump-and-votes-to-end-his-canada-tariffs?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 and the Supreme Courtprepares to rule 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/supreme-court-schedules-opinions-on-feb-20-as-tariff-case-looms?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 on the legality of most of the president’s import taxes.

Japan’s Investments

On Tuesday, the White House announced 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/trump-announces-first-japan-investments-under-trade-deal?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 that Japan plans to invest as much as $36 billion in US oil, gas and critical 
mineral projects, thefirst tranche 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-18/softbank-toshiba-among-firms-interested-in-us-japan-gas-project?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 of its $550 billion commitment under the trade framework it struck with Trump.

Read More: Japan’s Exports Rise Most in Three Years as AI Underpins Chips 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/japan-s-exports-rise-most-in-three-years-on-china-shipment-surge?cmpid=BBD021826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>

The news came just in time to quell some criticism from observers who’ve noted 
sluggish progress on tariffs and investment pledges.

In his “Most Favoured Nation” newsletter 
<https://mostfavourednation.substack.com/> Monday, trade expert Sam Lowe of 
Flint Global in London included Japan on a list of countries that appeared to 
have adopted a playbook of “strategic under-implementation.”

Here’s how he summed up the approach by economies including Indonesia, the UK 
and the European Union:
“Agree to just enough to secure tariff relief, and quietly defer the bits that 
hurt,” Lowe wrote. “In the short term, it may even be rational: agree now, 
defer later, and hope enforcement never materializes. However, in the medium to 
long term, it rests on the idea that Trump will one day go, and US trade policy 
will return to normal. I’m not sure this is a good assumption.”
