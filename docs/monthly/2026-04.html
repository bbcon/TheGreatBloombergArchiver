Monthly Brief

April 2026

US

  • S&P 500 fell approximately 5–7.8% in March, its worst monthly performance in roughly a year, before staging a remarkable recovery in April that carried the index above 7,000 for the first time
  • The Fed held rates unchanged at Powell's final meeting as chair, producing the most divided FOMC vote since 1992; Kevin Warsh confirmed as successor with markets pricing zero cuts in 2026
  • Energy shock is transmitting rapidly into domestic prices — gasoline crossed $4 per gallon, retail diesel rose nearly 50%, and University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to an all-time low of 47.6
  • First-quarter earnings beat rate of approximately 80% with a 12% aggregate surprise, though the semiconductor sector alone accounts for roughly 43% of upward earnings revisions
  • Supreme Court invalidation of IEEPA tariff authority and a Court of International Trade order mandating $160 billion in refunds to importers represent a substantive legal constraint on executive trade policy

Equity Markets

US equity markets traced a pattern of extreme volatility across April that, viewed in aggregate, produced a striking recovery from late-March lows. The S&P 500 entered the month roughly 8% below its pre-war peak, with the index displaying a consistent intraweek behavioral signature: cumulative gains over the first three sessions of each week repeatedly erased on Thursdays and Fridays as geopolitical anxiety reasserted itself, with the index posting net losses of approximately 9% on those two days alone since hostilities began in late February. The mid-month ceasefire announcement on April 8 catalyzed the S&P 500's best single session in nearly a year, with the index subsequently extending gains to close above 7,000 by the third week — a 12.33% advance over just 14 trading days, a pace of appreciation recorded only four times this century. The closest historical parallel analysts drew was March 2000 in terms of earnings multiple and timing, a comparison that warrants serious attention. Fewer than half of S&P 500 members traded above their 50-day moving averages at the index's record close, and volume ran 11% below the six-month average, suggesting the rally was driven in significant part by short-covering among investors conditioned by 2025's Liberation Day episode not to hold short positions through stress events. Energy names and health insurers diverged sharply — Exxon Mobil fell 5.8% mid-month on the ceasefire, while Humana gained 11% and UnitedHealth rose 7% on a constructive Medicare Advantage payment proposal. The Bloomberg US Corporate High Yield Index returned to pre-war levels by late April, closing at a record high, a signal of credit market normalization that sits in some tension with the macro backdrop.

Monetary Policy and the Fed Transition

The Federal Reserve's policy stance and leadership transition together constituted the dominant domestic institutional story of the month. Powell's final FOMC meeting as chair produced the most divided vote since October 1992, with four members dissenting — three regional presidents objecting to any residual easing bias in forward guidance, and Stephen Miran dissenting separately. Rates were left unchanged but the statement's tone was unambiguously hawkish. Two-year Treasury yields rose 11 basis points on the decision day, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, and 30-year yields reached 5% for the first time this year by month-end. Futures markets moved to price zero easing in 2026, with approximately 50% odds assigned to a rate increase by April 2027 — a stark reversal from the two cuts that had been fully priced before hostilities began. Kevin Warsh's confirmation hearing produced substantive signals: a preference for narrower mandates, a smaller balance sheet, and a pivot toward trimmed-mean inflation gauges — the Dallas Fed's trimmed PCE currently reads 2.33% versus 3.0% for core PCE, a 70-basis-point gap that would materially reframe perceived distance from the 2% target if adopted as the policy benchmark. Powell's decision to remain on the board as a governor, amid a deeply divided committee, will likely constrain Warsh's ability to shift policy direction quickly. Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized the arrangement as a violation of Fed norms; the parallel to Marriner Eccles somewhat complicates that framing but does not resolve the underlying tension.

Corporate Earnings and Technology

The first-quarter earnings season opened constructively against a difficult macro backdrop. With slightly over a third of S&P 500 companies reported by late April, the beat rate stood at approximately 80% — above the historical 77% average — with an aggregate earnings surprise near 12%, well above the typical 7% premium. The AI-semiconductor complex provided the season's clearest upside, with TSMC reporting a 58% profit surge and raising full-year revenue growth guidance above 30%, Texas Instruments surging 12% on data-center-driven demand, SK Hynix delivering a fivefold quarterly profit increase, and Samsung Electronics reporting a 755% year-on-year profit increase. The Philadelphia Semiconductor index extended what appeared to be an 18-session winning streak, the longest in the index's history. Results outside the AI complex were more mixed: Netflix fell approximately 10% on a miss and co-founder Reed Hastings's departure, ServiceNow fell 13% after cutting its margin outlook citing Middle East-related deal delays, and Alcoa disappointed despite elevated aluminum prices. The hyperscaler earnings released in the final week of April delivered a nuanced verdict — Alphabet surged more than 7% and added $421 billion in market capitalization in a single session, the second-largest one-day gain on record for any stock, while Meta fell roughly 7% after raising 2026 capital expenditure guidance by $10 billion to potentially above $145 billion. The combined capex envelope for the four major hyperscalers now approaches $725 billion, a figure that sustains rather than extends AI enthusiasm. SpaceX began investor meetings for an IPO that could value the company above $2 trillion.

Consumer Stress and Political Economy

Beneath equity market resilience, consumer conditions deteriorated measurably through the month. The University of Michigan's preliminary April sentiment index fell to 47.6, the weakest in the survey's history dating to 1978, surpassing troughs from the 1980 hostage crisis and the Covid pandemic, with year-ahead inflation expectations rising a full percentage point to 4.8%. Mortgage applications fell more than 10% through March, and the household savings rate has declined to 4.5% from 6.4% in January 2024. Buy-now-pay-later provider Klarna lost more than 60% of its value since its September IPO, with usage increasingly concentrated in essentials such as groceries. Corporate equities now represent over a quarter of household net worth — a record in eight decades of Fed data — making equity market performance a potentially more potent consumer spending channel than pump prices, an observation that cuts both ways. Electricity costs are emerging as a defining issue ahead of November midterms. On fiscal policy, Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed tariff rates may be restored to pre-Supreme Court levels by July, and the administration invoked the Defense Production Act to direct federal funds toward energy infrastructure including coal, LNG, and petroleum. Fitch warned that the US debt burden remains far above other AA-rated sovereigns, while key congressional Republicans signaled potential resistance to a proposed 44% Pentagon budget increase — a rare act of intra-party defiance that may signal softening executive influence ahead of midterms. ---

Asia

  • TSMC reported a 58% quarterly profit surge and raised full-year revenue growth guidance above 30%; Taiwan's equity market overtook the UK to become the seventh largest globally at $4.14 trillion
  • Bank of Japan delivered its most divided policy vote under Governor Ueda, holding at 0.75% on a 6-3 split; Japan intervened directly in currency markets in the final week of April after dollar-yen approached 160
  • South Korea's Kospi rose approximately 60% over the period, with Samsung reporting a 755% profit increase and SK Hynix quadrupling from August lows, driven by AI memory chip demand

China: Growth Composition and Export Resilience

China's economic performance over April was stronger in headline terms than the underlying structure suggests. First-quarter GDP growth of 5.0% was the strongest in three quarters and beat consensus, but the composition warrants scrutiny: industrial output expanded 5.7% in March while retail sales rose only 1.7%, missing forecasts. Factory-gate prices exited deflation in March, with PPI rising 0.5% year-on-year after a 0.9% decline in February, partially attributable to the global energy surge, though Capital Economics cautioned the turn may not persist. Urban private-sector wage growth had fallen below 1% by end-2025, against a pre-pandemic rate of approximately 8%, and the latest Five-Year Plan contains no specific numerical consumption targets — reinforcing the assessment that domestic demand will remain structurally subdued and China's already-record trade surplus is likely to persist or widen. EV and hybrid exports surged 140% year-on-year in March to a record 349,000 units, and BYD raised its 2026 export target to 1.5 million units from 1.3 million as elevated global fuel prices accelerated EV adoption, even as BYD's domestic sales fell more than 40% — a bifurcation that illustrates the divergence between China's export competitiveness and subdued internal demand. Lithium battery shipments rose 34% year-on-year in March, electric vehicles 53%, and solar cells 80%. The Canton Fair in late April provided a more sobering ground-level view: Middle Eastern buyers — who had increasingly substituted for absent American customers since the pandemic — were conspicuously absent due to the conflict and flight disruptions, with exporters describing 2026 as potentially the worst year in memory, facing the sequential blows of tariffs and war simultaneously.

China: Energy Exposure, Strategic Positioning, and Governance

The Hormuz disruption exposed meaningful vulnerabilities in China's energy supply chain. Iran is a critical crude supplier, and Saudi Arabia's shipments to Chinese customers were set to halve in May — to approximately 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April — as Aramco raised official selling prices to a record. China's independent teapot refiners, accounting for roughly a fifth of national capacity and heavily reliant on discounted Iranian crude, held floating stockpiles of approximately 38 million barrels, providing a buffer of perhaps two months. Beijing responded by releasing strategic reserves, granting additional crude import quotas, and pivoting to US ethane imports expected to hit a record 800,000 tons in April. Yuan-denominated crude oil transactions reached a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS on Thursday of the third week, and China's lithium battery exports rose 50% year-on-year in the first quarter as buyers sought alternatives to oil-dependent energy. Xi Jinping's most diplomatically active week in nearly two years — receiving delegations from Spain, Abu Dhabi, Vietnam, and Russia, and convening a four-point Middle East peace proposal — positioned Beijing as a stabilizing force, building on the 2023 Saudi-Iran rapprochement, though China's concrete contributions to conflict resolution remained limited. Beijing's formal order to unwind Meta's $2 billion acquisition of agentic AI startup Manus, with co-founders barred from leaving China, signaled that the government views AI talent and capability outflows as a strategic vulnerability — a posture that risks reinforcing perceptions of elevated political risk in the technology sector at a moment Beijing is simultaneously attempting to attract international capital back to the private economy. Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable. The Politburo pledged to counter external shocks and enhance energy security.

Taiwan and Korea: AI Supercycle Outperformance

Taiwan and South Korea emerged as the clearest regional beneficiaries of AI-driven semiconductor demand, providing a counterweight to the energy-cost headwinds affecting the rest of Asia-Pacific. TSMC's market capitalization reached $1.7 trillion, Taiwan's total equity market surpassed the UK to become the seventh largest globally at $4.14 trillion, and the Taiex ended April with a year-to-date gain of approximately 25%, recording a record close in the third week. Taiwan's export orders jumped 66% in March, the fastest pace in 16 years. South Korea's Kospi rose approximately 60% from its conflict-period lows, with Samsung and SK Hynix together now representing 42% of Kospi capitalization, up from 28% two years ago — a concentration that amplifies both upside and tail risk. South Korean chip exports surged 183% year-on-year in the first 20 days of April, with overall exports up 49%. BlackRock flagged Korean equities as a preferred position. Labor tensions at Samsung are building, with thousands of employees rallying over wages and the union threatening an 18-day strike beginning May 21 — a risk that warrants monitoring given Samsung and SK Hynix's combined weight in both corporate earnings and index composition. DeepSeek unveiled V4 Flash and V4 Pro preview versions in late April, with advances in coding benchmarks, reasoning, and a one-million-token context window, with Tencent and Alibaba in preliminary discussions to participate in DeepSeek's maiden financing round.

Japan: Normalization Under Political Pressure

Japan's monetary policy trajectory grew more complicated across the month. The Bank of Japan's 6-3 vote to hold at 0.75% — the most divided outcome under Ueda's tenure — reflected genuine uncertainty about whether the conflict's inflationary impact warranted immediate action or whether its growth-dampening effects counseled patience. The yen weakened toward 160 per dollar by the final week before authorities intervened directly for the first time since 2024, with the intraday move reaching 155.5 before partially retracing. Rabobank cautioned that intervention offers only temporary stabilization given Japan's structural dependence on imported energy. Spring Shunto negotiations produced a third consecutive year of approximately 5% wage increases, and five-year inflation breakevens reached 2.7% — yet the yen's persistent weakness creates a visible tension between inflation data and market pricing. Prime Minister Takaichi's political pressure on the BOJ's normalization path — through dovish board appointments and public criticism of trade ministers who linked rate hikes to yen strength — and Japan's first overseas arms export framework since World War II, including a warship deal with Australia, together signal a government navigating simultaneously between economic vulnerability and strategic ambition. Japan also participated for the first time in major naval exercises with the US and the Philippines, even as the BOJ leaned toward holding at its next meeting, citing war-related uncertainty.

India, Southeast Asia, and Regional Macro

India remained among the most acutely exposed regional economies. Foreign investors sold $17.8 billion in equities over 23 consecutive sessions, a record outflow pace. The RBI banned key offshore derivatives and forced the unwind of tens of billions in speculative positions — a measure that delivered the rupee's largest single-day gain in over a decade but raised concerns about deterring the foreign investors India has sought to attract, a tension that remains unresolved. State Bank of India disclosed approximately $5 billion in positions against the rupee caught by the crackdown, with estimated losses of around $32 million from forced unwinds. Every one of the world's 50 hottest cities in the final week of April was located in India, with temperatures exceeding 40°C across densely populated states; McKinsey Global Institute estimates heat-related productivity losses could reach $250 billion, with monsoon relief not expected until June. India signed a free trade agreement with New Zealand in late April, continuing a sequence of deals following agreements with the EU and UK, though structural constraints limit the pace of liberalization. ASEAN manufacturing PMIs fell to their weakest since September, with Vietnam, Indonesia, and the broader bloc bearing asymmetric impact from both the energy shock and rerouted trade flows. Singapore became the first Asian central bank to tighten monetary settings in response to rising energy-driven inflation, a move that may signal broader regional policy divergence if crude remains elevated. ---

Europe

  • European equities fell 7.6% in March — the worst monthly performance in nearly four years — before a brief ceasefire rally mid-April that subsequently gave back gains as optimism faded
  • Viktor Orbán's 16-year tenure ended Sunday of the third week when Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured a supermajority in Hungary, removing a persistent obstruction to EU cohesion on Ukraine and Russia sanctions
  • ECB and Bank of England both held rates in the final week of April, but market pricing shifted dramatically to three ECB hikes in 2026 — a reversal from February's expectation of unchanged rates throughout the year
  • Norway's driest winter in decades — with snow reserves at their lowest in two decades — compounded the imported energy shock, with Norwegian electricity exports to the UK falling approximately 50% year-on-year
  • UK Prime Minister Starmer faces growing calls for resignation over the Mandelson vetting controversy, with Labour projected to lose approximately 1,850 seats in early May local elections

Macro Conditions and the Stagflationary Trap

Europe's economic position deteriorated visibly across April, converging toward a configuration that several analysts characterized as stagflationary: energy-driven inflation building precisely as growth momentum fades. Euro-area inflation jumped to 2.5% in March — the steepest monthly acceleration since Russia's Ukraine invasion in 2022 — though the reading came in below consensus at 2.6%, and critically, core and services inflation softened relative to February, suggesting the energy pass-through remains the primary driver rather than a broadening of underlying pressure. Preliminary April PMI data showed the eurozone economy unexpectedly contracting for the first time since late 2024, driven by a steep services decline as war-driven energy costs and eroding consumer confidence fed through. Germany's government halved its 2026 growth forecast to 0.5%, the ZEW investor expectations index collapsed to -17.2 in April from -0.5 in March — its lowest level in more than three years and well below consensus — and the Bundesbank estimated only slight first-quarter expansion. The Ifo Institute's business expectations index fell to its lowest since 2023. Germany's leading economic institutes warned that potential growth may approach zero in the medium term. The Ifo president characterized the country's €500 billion infrastructure stimulus fund as potentially the primary buffer preventing outright contraction, a sobering assessment of how quickly the cyclical picture has shifted since Merz took office. TS Lombard raised the risk of a "Trichet 2.0" scenario in which the ECB tightens into a weakening economy.

Energy Supply Stress

Europe's energy position deteriorated on multiple fronts simultaneously during the month. Diesel futures for the ICE Gasoil benchmark broke above $200 per barrel in the second week, the highest since 2022, as Hormuz disruptions compressed refined product flows. Tankers that loaded diesel in UK waters diverted cargoes as far as Australia while vessels originally heading toward Europe turned toward Africa as Asian buyers outbid European counterparts. Jet fuel in Europe surged to all-time highs equivalent to over $215 per barrel at one point, and Brent briefly surpassed $126 per barrel late in the month following reports of fresh US military planning against Iran. European natural gas futures spiked nearly 18% on a Monday open in the final week before partially recovering. Compounding the imported shock, Norway emerged from its driest winter in decades, with snow reserves at their lowest in two decades and an estimated 25 terawatt-hour energy deficit equivalent to nearly a fifth of the country's total hydropower output last year. Norwegian electricity exports to the UK fell approximately 50% year-on-year and to Germany by around 40%, while Nordic prices surged to more than four times 2025 levels in parts of the region. UK household energy costs tracked roughly £500 higher for a typical household. The EU Emissions Trading System adjustment proposals, EU resident guidance to work from home and reduce driving, and EDF's €240 million commitment to accelerate heat pump and electric truck adoption together reflect a policy response that is reactive rather than structural.

Central Banks: Holding Under Pressure

The ECB and Bank of England held rates at their late-April meetings, in line with consensus, but the decisions represent a holding pattern rather than a settled direction. Lagarde disclosed that policymakers debated an immediate increase but concluded they had "insufficient information" to act — language that implies a narrow path to avoiding a June move if Hormuz disruption persists. Bundesbank President Nagel separately indicated he favors a June hike. At the Bank of England, Chief Economist Pill voted for an immediate increase, and Governor Bailey warned that waiting for second-round effects to materialize before acting would mean responding "too late." Market pricing shifted dramatically across the month: traders moved to price a June hike as the base case for both institutions, with three ECB increases priced for 2026 — a dramatic reversal from February's expectation of unchanged rates. UK gilt markets came under particular strain, with mortgage rates above 5% in March according to RICS data and homebuyer demand falling to its lowest since August 2023. The structural rigidity of the UK — regulated energy pricing and inflation-linked wages — leaves it comparatively exposed relative to euro-area peers. The White House's Kevin Hassett publicly characterized potential ECB or Fed hikes as "policy errors," an unusual intervention in the affairs of independent central banks that drew little immediate policy response.

Political Developments: Hungary and the UK

Hungary's Sunday election in the third week of April produced the month's most consequential European political development. Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured a supermajority, ending Viktor Orbán's 16-year tenure and removing a persistent obstruction to EU cohesion — Orbán's Hungary had held up approximately €90 billion in EU support for Ukraine and exercised veto power to fracture sanctions consensus. The forint surged to a four-year high following the result, and German Chancellor Merz moved quickly to press for disbursement of the €90 billion EU loan for Ukraine. Magyar has pledged to reorient Hungary toward Brussels, supports eventual euro adoption, and has signaled a swift transition of power. That said, Hungary's dependence on Russian energy and existing commercial ties to Asian battery manufacturers will constrain the pace of reorientation, and the economy has consistently underperformed regional peers Poland and the Czech Republic. The EU also finalized its €90 billion loan to Ukraine and Europe-wide Russian LNG import ban took effect — poorly timed given current energy market stress, though strategically consistent with the bloc's longer-run posture. Bulgaria's presidential election produced a victory for former President Radev, who opposes Russian sanctions and military aid to Ukraine, adding a complicating element to European political cohesion that partially offsets the Hungarian development. In the UK, Starmer survived a parliamentary vote on the Mandelson vetting controversy but faces local elections in early May where Labour is projected to lose approximately 1,850 seats — a performance that would raise credible questions about his political durability. The Mandelson episode consumed considerable political bandwidth at precisely the moment the government needed to project competence on energy, defense, and diplomatic coordination.

Corporate Earnings

European corporate earnings reflected the bifurcation between sectors exposed to energy-cost shock and those riding AI-driven structural demand. ASML raised its full-year sales forecast to €36–40 billion from €34–39 billion, driven by AI-related semiconductor demand, with its CEO noting supply cannot meet demand for the foreseeable future — a constructive signal for European technology manufacturing. UBS reported first-quarter net income of $3.04 billion, beating all estimates with revenue rising nearly 30% on equities and foreign exchange strength. BP reported adjusted net income of $3.2 billion, more than double the year-earlier figure. Adidas gained 8.3% in Frankfurt on strong apparel momentum. SAP gained 7.3% on strong cloud revenue. By contrast, Hermès fell as much as 14% on its worst single day on record after first-quarter sales disappointed, LVMH reported fashion and leather goods sales declining in the first quarter, Alstom fell 36% after withdrawing its free cash flow guidance, and Lufthansa cancelled approximately 20,000 short-haul flights over the next six months in response to the jet fuel surge. S&P cut credit ratings for Belgium and Slovakia and placed Finland on negative watch, reflecting fiscal deterioration partly linked to elevated energy costs. Universal Music surged 11% after Bill Ackman proposed a deal valuing the company at approximately $65 billion. The UniCredit-Commerzbank standoff continued without resolution despite Orcel having accumulated just under 30% of Commerzbank and tabling a €35 billion offer. Greece received an MSCI upgrade to developed-market status effective May 2027. ---

Global

  • Brent crude rose more than 40% from pre-conflict levels to briefly surpass $126 per barrel late in April, before the two-week ceasefire announced April 8 produced a single-day decline of as much as 16% — among the largest ever recorded — that subsequently partially reversed
  • The US-Iran ceasefire entered its second week by month-end but remained structurally fragile, with the Strait of Hormuz effectively impassable, only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker completing an exit with cargo since the blockade's formal imposition
  • The IMF cut its global growth forecast to approximately 2.9% for 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — with a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 per barrel; global goods trade volume growth is projected at 2.8%, below world GDP growth of 3.1%
  • The UAE's shock exit from OPEC after 60 years added a structural dimension to energy market dynamics, removing meaningful spare capacity from the cartel and potentially signaling a durable regional realignment
  • Clean power surpassed coal's share of global electricity generation in 2025 for the first time since 1919, with renewables at 34% against coal's 33%, as the oil shock simultaneously accelerated EV adoption globally

The Strait of Hormuz: From Shock to Structural Disruption

The Strait of Hormuz remained the defining variable across all asset classes and regional economies throughout April, and the month's evidence shifted the probabilistic assessment toward a protracted closure rather than a near-term diplomatic resolution. Under normal conditions, approximately 135 vessels transit daily; that figure fell to fewer than six per day on average at the conflict's peak, with only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker completing an exit with crude cargo since the US naval blockade's formal imposition in the final week. Brent crude broke above $107 per barrel following Trump's late-March address and rose more than 40% since hostilities began approximately five weeks before April opened. The ceasefire announced April 8 — brokered with Pakistan serving as the key intermediary — produced a striking single-day market reaction: Brent fell as much as 16% to approximately $91.70 a barrel and WTI tumbled nearly 19%, among the largest single-day crude declines on record, comparable only to the Covid demand collapse in 2020 and the allied entry into Kuwait in January 1991. By mid-week following the announcement, WTI had partially recovered toward $98–99 as Tehran alleged ceasefire violations and Israeli strikes in Lebanon intensified. By late April, Brent had surged to briefly above $126 per barrel following reports that President Trump was scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options and US Central Command was preparing fresh strike plans. Trump confirmed the naval blockade of Iranian ports will not be lifted without a nuclear agreement, removing near-term ambiguity about conditions for reopening. Polymarket assigns only approximately a one-in-five probability on tanker traffic returning to normal by end-April, and Gulf Arab and European officials believe a durable agreement is more likely six months away than within weeks. The forward curve tells a more nuanced story — oil for delivery later in the year trades closer to $80 — suggesting markets do not view the disruption as permanent, but the timeline for any resolution remains genuinely uncertain.

Diplomatic Dynamics: Active but Unresolved

The diplomatic architecture around the conflict evolved substantially across the month without producing resolution. Pakistan's emergence as the key mediating channel — leveraging ties with Saudi Arabia, Iran, the US, and China simultaneously — reflects a meaningful realignment of diplomatic influence. China's role in persuading Iran to accept the initial ceasefire was credited by Iranian officials to a last-minute Beijing push, illustrating the growing influence Beijing derives from its $270 billion financial footprint across the Gulf. That footprint simultaneously constrains how openly China can back Iran, creating a nuanced position: quietly facilitating de-escalation while avoiding overt alignment with either side. Iran submitted a peace proposal through Pakistani mediation calling for reopening the strait in exchange for lifting the US port blockade, with nuclear negotiations deferred to a separate track; Washington confirmed the proposal is under active consideration. The gap between the parties' stated positions remains wide: the US claims Iran has agreed to surrender enriched uranium and end its nuclear program, while Iranian officials deny both. Iranian negotiators reportedly grew less willing to engage through the month, citing Trump's social media posts as intended to humiliate rather than facilitate, a dynamic that does not augur well for formal agreement. The contrast with the 2015 nuclear negotiations is instructive — those talks involved career diplomats, multilateral frameworks, and exhaustive document review; the current process is managed by figures whose primary professional backgrounds lie outside diplomacy. The anticipated Trump-Xi summit in mid-May, confirmed as on track by USTR Greer, adds complexity: Beijing's calibrated posture is under close scrutiny from Washington and regional interlocutors, and Xi's call to Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman urging restoration of Strait transit in the final week — Beijing's most explicit public statement of the month — signals that China's acute vulnerability as a major oil importer is beginning to override its preference for passive beneficiary status.

Energy Markets: Volatility, Second-Order Effects, and Structural Consequences

The energy shock transmitted into adjacent commodity and logistics markets in consequential ways throughout the month. Aluminum reached four-year highs above $3,600 per ton in the final week, with JPMorgan forecasting prices could approach $4,000 per ton, characterizing the market as entering a structural supply "black hole" following Iranian strikes on Gulf smelters. Fertilizer prices — urea and ammonia particularly — rose sharply, with nearly half of global urea supply originating in the Persian Gulf, raising food security concerns across sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia; a Farm Bureau survey found more than 70% of US farmers unable to afford sufficient fertilizer at prevailing prices. PGMEA, a photoresist chemical derived from naphtha and critical to semiconductor fabrication, emerged as an underappreciated supply chain casualty, with Shin-Etsu Chemical withholding its full-year guidance citing naphtha-derived constraints. Container throughput at UAE ports outside the strait surged dramatically — daily truck volumes at Khor Fakkan rose from roughly 100 pre-conflict to approximately 6,000 — while Jebel Ali's container vessel arrivals fell from 12 per day to approximately one. The Port of Los Angeles chief estimated full shipping normalization will take approximately four and a half months even after the Strait reopens. The NY Fed's Global Supply Chain Pressure Index rose in March to its highest since January 2023. Oxford Economics estimates global goods trade will expand just 2.5% in 2026, roughly half the 4.9% pace in 2025, with the oil shock potentially subtracting 0.6–0.9% from world trade volumes. The Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index rose meaningfully since hostilities began, with wheat and rice moving higher, and the copper-to-gold ratio touched four-decade lows — a level historically associated with the onset of commodity supercycles.

Geopolitical Architecture Under Strain

The conflict is accelerating structural shifts that may outlast the hostilities themselves. Secretary of State Rubio declared NATO's response "very disappointing," calling out allies for denying base access — remarks that may presage a formal reassessment of the US-NATO relationship. Not a single NATO member or Gulf ally directly joined the US-Israeli campaign, and French President Macron publicly called on medium powers to assert greater independence from Washington. The US formally requested that European NATO allies submit concrete plans to help secure the Strait of Hormuz, shifting from rhetorical commitment to operational demand. European allies explored deeper integration with France's nuclear umbrella; public support for nuclear deterrence rose in South Korea and Japan; and the Trump administration reportedly considered resuming nuclear testing and potentially sharing enrichment technology with Saudi Arabia. King Charles III's address to a joint session of Congress — making unusually pointed appeals to reject isolationism and support NATO commitments — represented a visible diplomatic moment that British officials privately acknowledged is unlikely to alter Trump's underlying disposition. Foreign central bank holdings at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York fell approximately $82 billion since late February to $2.7 trillion, the lowest since 2012, as Gulf producers liquidated Treasuries to defend currencies and fund wartime expenditures — a structural erosion of the petrodollar recycling mechanism that warrants monitoring over a longer horizon. The UAE's shock exit from OPEC and potential reconsideration of participation in other regional bodies, if sustained, would represent a meaningful shift in Gulf political architecture with implications for energy markets well beyond the current crisis.

The Energy Transition Paradox

The crisis is simultaneously acting as a catalyst for energy transition in ways that create a genuine paradox: the same shock that benefits fossil fuel producers is accelerating the structural shift away from them. Clean power surpassed coal's share of global electricity generation in 2025 for the first time since 1919, with renewables at 34% against coal's 33%. Battery installations are expected to jump roughly a third this year. European consumers purchased 206,200 electric vehicles in March, a 44% year-on-year increase; South Korean EV sales more than doubled; and Chinese EV and hybrid exports reached a record, rising 140% from the prior year. UK EV registrations hit a record 86,120 units in March. LNG's credibility as a long-term energy
