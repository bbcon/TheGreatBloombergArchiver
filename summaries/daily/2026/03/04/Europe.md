European equity markets offered a partial counterpoint to the global selloff, with the Stoxx Europe 600 rising 1.3% on Wednesday, partly on reports of indirect Iran-US contact toward a ceasefire. However, the underlying macro picture deteriorated sharply. European natural gas prices surged more than 50% on the week — their highest level since Russia's invasion of Ukraine — reflecting the continent's substantial reliance on Qatari LNG at a time when the Ras Laffan facility has been shuttered by drone attack and Hormuz transits remain effectively halted. Bloomberg Economics estimates that sustained high energy prices could push Europe to the brink of recession.

The week's most politically consequential European story involved Spain. President Trump threatened to "cut off all trade" with Spain after Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez denied Washington access to Spanish military bases for its Iran bombing campaign. Sánchez declined to yield, stating Spain would not "be complicit in something harmful to the world." Spanish equities fell to their lowest in nearly three months. Trump's trade threat faces legal complications given that bilateral trade is governed by EU-level agreements, and Banco Santander executive chair Ana Botín sought to publicly downplay the rift. UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves stated Britain would not sacrifice its principles for favorable US trading terms, declining to endorse the American-Israeli operation. The Office for Budget Responsibility separately indicated that meeting Labour's commitment to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035 would require approximately £40 billion in additional outlays. In corporate news, Adidas fell 7.6% in Frankfurt after its profit forecast missed expectations due to US tariffs and currency headwinds, while Novo Nordisk rose 3.5% after US regulators moved against copycat GLP-1 drugs.
