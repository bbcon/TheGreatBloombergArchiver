Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Grant Smith looks at the prospects for the oil market following the 
IEA’s announcement that it will release a record volume from emergency 
stockpiles.

To calm markets during the 2008 global crisis, US Treasury Secretary Hank 
Paulson <https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/1489668> signaled he had a “
bazooka <bbg://news/stories/K42U4Q3PWT1D>” of financial resources to deploy.

This week, major economies confronting an unprecedented oil shock posed by the 
Iran conflict brandished some of their own firepower.

The International Energy Agency 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/iea-confirms-huge-release-of-emergency-oil-stockpiles?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 — whose members include the US, Germany and Japan — said Wednesday it would 
release a record 400 million barrels from emergency reserves to steady lurching 
crude markets.

The intervention far exceeds previous moves to tap stockpiles, such as during 
the 2011 Libyan civil war and Russia’s invasion of Ukraine four years ago. 
Expectations of the IEA’s announcement helped ease prices from a high of almost 
$120 a barrel reached Monday.

Yet the scale of the current disruption is — as the IEA noted Thursday 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/iran-war-is-causing-biggest-ever-oil-market-disruption-iea-says?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 — the biggest ever seen.

With tanker traffic halted through the Strait of Hormuz, the 20 million 
barrels of crude oil and refined products that normally flow through the 
corridor each day have been slashed by 90%, the agency estimates. Gulf nations 
have been forced to shutter 10 million barrels a day of production.
Oil tankers cruise out of the Strait of Hormuz off the shores of Oman, before 
the Iran war broke out. The critical waterway remains effectively closed as the 
conflict drags on.Photographer: MARWAN NAAMANI/AFP via Getty Images
On Thursday, attacks on two oil tankers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/oman-evacuates-vessels-from-oil-terminal-in-precautionary-move?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 in Iraqi waters and onparts of Dubai 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/iran-escalates-attacks-on-shipping-and-dubai-sending-oil-higher?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 showed that — despite US President Donald Trump’s indications that the war
could soon end 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-03-10/oil-falls-after-trump-suggests-war-will-end-very-soon-video?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 — the conflict is far from abating.

Furthermore, while the magnitude of the IEA stockpile release is formidable, 
key details haven’t yet been specified: At what rate will the inventories flow, 
and what will be the composition of crude oil versus refined products?

Based on how fast reserves have been drawn down in previous crises, several 
oil traders, banks and consultants are doubtful the process can be swift enough 
to plug the gap.

With so much uncertainty, Brent futures — which had eased back toward $81 a 
barrel earlier this week — have resumed their march higher, brieflysurpassing 
$100 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-12?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 again in London on Thursday.

As IEA Executive Director Fatih Birol 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/6020647> acknowledged, the most 
meaningful hope of restoring stability to world oil markets is to reopen the 
Strait of Hormuz.

Big as the IEA’s “bazooka” may be, such a task is beyond its power. 

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

When Qatar closed its giant Ras Laffan liquefied natural gas complex, which 
produces roughly 20% of the world’s LNG, gas prices surged in Europe and Asia 
to their highest levels since at least early 2023. In North America, prices 
haven’t moved nearly as much because of plentiful and efficient production 
across various gas basins. US companies selling exportable gas at soaring 
international prices are now“in the catbird seat,” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/iran-war-boosts-us-natural-gas-export-sector-disrupts-qatar-lng?cmpid=BBBXT031226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 says Ira Joseph of the Center on Global Energy Policy at Columbia University. 
“If you’re an LNG producer and can manage to stay online, this is your moment 
to make more money in a year than you were expecting to make in a decade.”

BNEF today

in January,BloombergNEF <> data show. The Asia Pacific region saw the greatest

financials.
