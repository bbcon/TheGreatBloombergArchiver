The most consequential cross-regional dynamic remains the state of US-Iran relations and its implications for oil markets. US and Iranian officials concluded a further round of nuclear talks in Geneva on Thursday, agreeing to reconvene as soon as next week. The diplomatic channel remains open, but the context is extraordinary: Iran's nuclear facilities were struck by the US last June, a second US carrier strike group is reportedly already in Israeli waters, and Tehran is simultaneously preparing for a potential war while attempting to negotiate. Oil markets appear to price a deal as the less likely outcome, with Brent futures around $70 per barrel — up roughly 19% year-to-date — reflecting a meaningful geopolitical risk premium.

OPEC+ is set to meet Sunday, and delegates indicate the group will likely agree to resume the output increases it suspended in Q1. The group's ability to consider this option reflects a combination of factors: supply disruptions in Kazakhstan, North America, and Russia have trimmed the theoretical glut, and Chinese strategic reserve purchases have absorbed additional barrels. Whether the supply overhang is as large as the IEA and JPMorgan project remains genuinely uncertain, with several US shale executives arguing the surplus will arrive later — or not at all.

Pakistan declared "open war" with Afghanistan after cross-border strikes killed over 130 people, adding a further geopolitical fault line in a region already under strain. China meanwhile removed nine military lawmakers from its National People's Congress, shrinking the military delegation to its smallest size since at least 1974, in an apparent continuation of Xi Jinping's defense establishment purge.

On energy transition, US renewable power generation hit a record 1,162 terawatt-hours in 2025 — a 10% year-on-year increase representing 26% of total US electricity output — despite the Trump administration's sustained policy headwinds for the sector. The economics of wind and solar continue to drive deployment independent of subsidy status, with Wood Mackenzie projecting renewables will account for nearly one in three US electrons by 2030.
