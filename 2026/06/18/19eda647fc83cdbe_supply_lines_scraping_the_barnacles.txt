Hundreds of commercial ships stuck in the Persian Gulf have attracted the 
worldтАЩs attention for nearly four months of fighting and ceasefires. TheyтАЩve 
also picked up a bunch of aquatic hitchhikers.

Barnacles, the cousins of crabs, survive by cementing themselves to stationary 
objects like the metal hulls of ships that provide stable bases to feed. With 
so many vessels awaiting the reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, thereтАЩs a new, 
if niche, gold rush playing out as a US-Iranpeace deal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/trump-blows-through-his-iran-red-lines-in-justifying-peace-deal?cmpid=BBD061826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5493>
 takes effect.

As marooned cargo carriers prepare to leave the Gulf in coming weeks with what 
they hope will be safe passage, the demand for divers paid to scrape hulls free 
of crustaceans is soaring, as BloombergтАЩs Stella Macklerreports this week 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/wanted-war-zone-divers-to-scrape-barnacles-from-ships-sailing-strait-of-hormuz?cmpid=BBD061826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5493>
.

Port operators frown on heavily barnacled vessels because of the threat of 
destructive, invasive species nestled in their midst, and the International 
Maritime Organization hasguidelines 
<https://www.imo.org/en/ourwork/environment/pages/biofouling.aspx> against 
so-called biofouling. Shipowners dislike barnacles because they cause drag and 
reduce fuel efficiency.

$8,000 Per Ship

Hence the sudden rise in demand for crews to service scores of vessels that 
have been idling in the bathwater-warm shallows of the Gulf since the war 
erupted in late February.

Like equity markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/asian-stocks-bonds-to-fall-on-hawkish-fed-signals-markets-wrap?cmpid=BBD061826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5493>
 thatтАЩve rallied as Mideast shipping snarls look close to ending, the bartering 
over barnacle busting is also booming.

Fees to scrub a single vessel are running about $8,000, a chief surveyor at a 
shipping service company told Bloomberg. Just days ago, when Trump announced the
peace agreement 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/us-hails-deal-that-defers-war-goals-and-gives-iran-relief?cmpid=BBD061826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5493>
, crews were fetching $5,000 for such work.
