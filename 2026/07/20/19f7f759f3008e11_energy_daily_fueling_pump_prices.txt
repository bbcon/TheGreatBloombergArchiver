Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, oil 
reporter Grant Smith takes stock of the latest attacks on shipping in the 
Middle East as US pump prices climb.

The latest chaos in the Strait of Hormuz couldn’t come at a worse moment for 
global fuel markets.

Visible oil tanker traffic through the chokepoint ground to a halt 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/oil-tanker-appears-to-halt-in-hormuz-after-iran-targets-vessels?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 again today after Iran targeted a number of vessels, further squeezing an 
already-diminished flow of cargoes from the Persian Gulf.

A critical lifeline of shuttle runs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/iran-s-tanker-attacks-squeeze-the-hormuz-oil-shuttling-trade?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
, which have discreetly hauled cargoes during the strait’s disruption, was 
already in jeopardy after ships linked to the United Arab Emirates — a leading 
proponent of the shuttle trade — were struck last week.

The most recent wave of attacks lands just as fuel markets tighten.

When the war between the US and Iran broke out in February, the shock was 
partly cushioned by a global supply surplus, plentiful emergency stockpiles 
held by consumer governments, and a seasonal lull in fuel consumption.

Those saving graces no longer apply.

With the US summer driving season in full swing, gasoline pump prices have 
surged past$4 a gallon 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/us-average-gasoline-prices-rise-above-4-a-gallon-again?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 for the first time in a month. Truck drivers and farmers are already having to 
contend withdiesel costs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-16/us-drivers-are-again-paying-more-than-5-a-gallon-for-diesel?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 north of $5, while refining-margin measures known as crack spreads have hit 
records.
Trucks drive along Interstate 5 on June 3, 2026, in Castaic, California.
Photographer: Justin Sullivan/Getty Images North America
Oil erased an earlier gain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-19/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-july-20?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 today following a report that mediators have proposed a 10-day cessation of 
strikes. But the situation remains highly volatile, and futures are coming off 
the back of the biggest weekly jump since April.

The recent escalation also coincided with a flareup in another war, one that 
has ramifications for energy supply, too.

Ukrainian strikes on Russian refineries have slashed processing rates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/russian-refinery-runs-plunge-to-lowest-in-more-than-two-decades?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 to the lowest in more than two decades, analysts estimate. Attacks have also 
forced the keyCaspian Pipeline Consortium 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/kazakh-black-sea-oil-terminal-halted-again-after-tanker-attack?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 terminal on the Black Sea to suspend oil loadings.

As far as Europe is concerned, the diesel squeeze will likely only get worse, 
shrinking inventories “to multiyear lows toward year-end,”Morgan Stanley 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/diesel-squeeze-in-europe-is-set-to-deepen-morgan-stanley-says?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 warned at the weekend.

Finally, the shock absorbers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-18/oil-s-battered-shock-absorbers-risk-price-spike-as-war-returns?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 that consumer governments used to weather the initial phase of the Iran war 
are now looking a little worn.

Following an unprecedented stock release announced in March, the US Strategic 
Petroleum Reserve 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/us-oil-reserve-hits-43-year-low-as-trump-aims-to-tame-gas-prices?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 has been depleted to its lowest since 1983. While authorities in developed 
nations insist they have enough reserves to intervene again, there are doubts 
over how much they can plausibly deliver.

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

Chinese imports of seaborne gas jumped in June as the world’s biggest buyer 
prepared for a spike in summer demand, even as war in the Middle East curbs 
global supply. Deliveries ofliquefied natural gas 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/china-s-lng-imports-rise-a-second-month-as-demand-recovers?cmpid=BBBXT072026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6697>
 rose 8.3% year-on-year, a second consecutive month of increases, customs data 
show. Buyers are under pressure to refill inventories after disruptions in the 
Strait of Hormuz sent prices soaring and dramatically suppressed shipments.

BNEF today

After months of growth, sustainable debt sales cooled in June with issuance at 
$201 billion, according to BloombergNEF. The contraction was present in all 
regions, and most significant among supranational issuance. Only 
non-social-securitized issuance in the Americas experienced month-to-month 
growth.
