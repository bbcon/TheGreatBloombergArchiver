**UK Political Crisis: Deterioration and Partial Stabilization**

The United Kingdom's political trajectory was the most volatile in the region through February. Prime Minister Keir Starmer entered the month already under pressure from within his parliamentary party over his association with Peter Mandelson, whose appointment as US ambassador has been complicated by documented links to Jeffrey Epstein. Parliament voted to compel disclosure of related documents in the opening weeks. The crisis intensified in mid-month when Starmer's chief of staff and communications head resigned and Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar publicly called for Starmer to stand down — a statement that drove gilt yields higher, though the market reaction remained an order of magnitude below the 2022 Truss episode, with sterling holding up and the curve avoiding dislocation. By mid-month Starmer had secured temporary reprieve through cabinet backing, and prediction markets priced his departure by end-June rather than imminently. The successor question may prove more consequential for fixed income than the current leadership itself: the most plausible candidates sit to Starmer's left and are perceived as more fiscally expansive. Macro data compounded the pressure: fourth-quarter GDP barely expanded, UK unemployment rose to its highest level since the pandemic, and wage growth eased — collectively prompting traders to fully price half a point of Bank of England rate cuts by year-end, with March increasingly discussed as a live meeting. Against this, the UK recorded its largest budget surplus on record late in the month, boosted by surging capital gains tax receipts — a brief fiscal reprieve that does not resolve the underlying structural constraints Chancellor Reeves faces.

**ECB and Eurozone: Divergent Signals**

The ECB held rates as expected through February, and Governing Council member Stournaras downplayed concerns about the euro's recent appreciation. Euro-area inflation continued to run below the ECB's 2% target, sharpening the policy debate; if the undershoot persists into the spring projection round, additional accommodation may be priced more aggressively. This sits in tension with firmer activity signals: German factory orders rose at their fastest pace in two years mid-month, and the euro-area flash PMI surprised to the upside late in the month, with manufacturers recording their best performance since 2022. Q4 negotiated wages rose 3%, a figure the Governing Council will monitor carefully against residual services-price pressures. The Stoxx Europe 600 touched an all-time high of 630 in the final week of the month before settling near 628, though strategists are flagging caution: eight consecutive months of gains and valuations at five-year highs leave limited room for further multiple expansion. The most significant European institutional development was the reported likelihood that ECB President Lagarde will depart ahead of her October 2027 mandate expiry, with timing calibrated around the French presidential election. An ECB spokesperson preserved optionality, but the succession debate — with Dutch governor Knot appearing the most plausible consensus choice — has begun to register in rate markets.

**Corporate Developments: Stress and Selective Strength**

The month produced some of the sharpest single-name moves in recent European corporate history. Novo Nordisk plunged as much as 20% early in the month — among the largest single-day declines in the company's history — after forecasting a steep sales decline, providing concrete evidence that competitive dynamics in GLP-1 obesity drugs are deteriorating faster than the sell-side modeled; approximately 5,000 job cuts announced late in the month represent among the largest layoffs in Danish history. Stellantis fell as much as 24% in Milan — a record intraday decline — after announcing €22 billion in charges tied to unwinding its EV strategy. BP suspended its $750 million quarterly buyback and extended declines as much as 7.7% the following session after offering no timeline for restoration. Dassault Systèmes fell approximately 20% in Paris, the latest European software name caught in the AI-displacement selloff. Nuveen agreed to acquire Schroders for $13.5 billion in cash at a roughly 29% premium, ending more than two centuries of independence for the UK's largest standalone asset manager. On the upside, Kering rose 10% on a smaller-than-expected Gucci sales decline, Moncler rallied 13% on results described as significantly ahead of estimates, and UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat.

**Geopolitical and Energy Risks**

Gas inventories are on track to exit winter more depleted than in 2025, with German storage roughly a quarter full and little financial incentive via summer-winter price spreads to incentivize refilling. Chancellor Merz courted Middle Eastern energy suppliers to diversify German dependence on US LNG — a notable strategic pivot — and simultaneously indicated openness to revising or delaying the EU's carbon market, driving the steepest drop in EU carbon prices since 2022. French customs data showed Q4 2025 US tariffs weighing materially on exports: spirits shipments fell 47%, wine 39%, and perfumes and cosmetics 25%. At the Munich Security Conference late in the month, European officials were reportedly reassessing the continent's nuclear deterrent posture following what attendees described as a sobering recalibration of US security commitments — a geopolitical undercurrent whose market implications remain, at this stage, difficult to quantify.
