• The FOMC held the fed funds rate unchanged at 3.50–3.75%, as expected, but new Chair Kevin Warsh delivered a decisively hawkish debut: half of the 18 submitting Fed members project at least one rate hike by year-end, with the average projected end-2026 rate rising from 3.24% to 3.83%.
• Two-year Treasury yields surged 13 basis points — the largest single-day move in over a year — to 4.18%, while fed funds futures shifted sharply toward pricing a hike as soon as September.
• Intel shares surged approximately 9% in premarket trading after Trump indicated the chipmaker would work with Apple to produce semiconductors domestically; Apple also disclosed plans to raise product prices to offset rising memory and storage chip costs.

**Monetary Policy**
Kevin Warsh's inaugural FOMC press conference reinforced the Fed's inflation-fighting credibility rather than signaling accommodation. The committee's statement was cut from 341 to 130 words, stripped of forward guidance and any reference to the maximum employment mandate, with the sole concluding line being "The Committee will deliver price stability." Warsh declined to submit his own dot plot — an unusual move suggesting he intends to de-emphasize that communication tool — but the projections of his 18 colleagues moved meaningfully hawkish, with half anticipating rate increases before year-end. Goldman Sachs vice chairman and former Dallas Fed president Rob Kaplan said the Fed may need to act as soon as September if inflation remains elevated. Warsh also announced five task forces covering communications, the balance sheet, alternative data, AI and productivity, and the inflation framework, signaling structural regime change ahead — though outcomes likely won't be known until year-end.

**Markets**
The hawkish Fed signal produced a sharp tightening of financial conditions. The 10-year TIPS yield rose to its highest level since May 2025, and the dollar recorded its strongest single-day gain of the year, an outcome that complicates the administration's stated preference for a weaker currency. Equity markets initially slumped on the rate-hike repricing but S&P 500 futures recovered roughly 0.9% the following morning, supported by the US-Iran interim peace deal and the associated decline in oil prices. Credit spreads, which remain near historic lows, face increasing scrutiny as the prospect of further monetary tightening prompts investors to reassess rich valuations.
