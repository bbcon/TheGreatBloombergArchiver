**Brazil: Bilateral Friction and Fiscal Risk**

The US designation of Brazil's two largest criminal organizations as terrorist entities is the most consequential LatAm development of the period. The move gives US law enforcement expanded tools to pursue international financial flows linked to PCC and CV, but carries significant diplomatic cost: Lula has consistently resisted framing Brazilian organized crime through a counterterrorism lens, and any perception of US interference in domestic security matters is politically toxic in Brasília. The relationship between Trump and Lula was already strained by trade policy and ideological divergence; this designation adds a new source of bilateral friction at a moment when Brazil is deepening commodity export relationships with China. The macro implications are indirect but real — political turbulence in Brazil historically widens sovereign spreads and weakens the real, though the magnitude of any market reaction depends on whether the designation produces measurable policy escalation from either side. The fiscal backdrop complicates the picture further: Bloomberg Economics characterizes Lula's pre-election stimulus as a deliberate effort to boost activity ahead of the vote. While the near-term demand impulse may support growth data, the strategy risks widening Brazil's already elevated fiscal deficit and adding to inflationary pressures at a point when the Banco Central do Brasil has been working to anchor expectations. The tension between electoral fiscal expansion and monetary credibility is a recurring dynamic in Brazilian political economy, and markets will likely price additional risk premium into Brazilian rates if the stimulus trajectory extends beyond what primary balance targets can accommodate.

**Venezuela and Regional Political Economy**

Machado's signal of willingness to negotiate on a path to presidential elections represents a meaningful shift in tone, suggesting Washington's pressure campaign may be producing marginal movement. The context is an economy in severe distress: inflation running near 600% has produced majority public support for full dollarization — a measure that would eliminate the inflation tax but remove the government's ability to monetize deficits, a constraint Venezuela's fiscal structure has historically been unable to absorb without substantial external support. The broader regional picture is shaped by the commodity cycle: industrial metals' best monthly performance since January provides genuine terms-of-trade relief for Chile, Peru, and Colombia, offering some offset to the tighter global financial conditions implied by higher US rates and the Hormuz-driven energy shock. China's commercial outreach in the Western Hemisphere is simultaneously broadening — Beijing's rapprochement with Canada, including reduced EV tariffs exchanged for lower agricultural levies, is a reminder that Latin American producers of agricultural commodities and resources face evolving competition dynamics in key export markets. Colombia's approaching presidential election, with a leftist candidate polling as frontrunner, adds to a cluster of EM political risk events that Oxford Economics' quarterly survey identified as contributing to the sharpest deterioration in emerging-market conditions since late 2022.
