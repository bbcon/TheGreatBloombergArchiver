China is emerging as a relative outperformer amid the Middle East crisis, though the picture is nuanced. The export-oriented economy expanded in March for the first time in 2026, Chinese equities have declined approximately 5.5% since the US-Israel assault on Iran began in late February — meaningfully less than the roughly 10% drawdowns recorded in South Korea, Japan, and India over the same period. This relative resilience appears to reflect the structural investments Beijing has made in renewable energy, diversified export markets, and strategic oil reserves, all of which provide a degree of insulation from the crude price shock that peers lack. BYD reported overseas sales of 120,083 vehicles in March, a three-month high, as elevated fuel prices accelerate EV adoption globally; the company has raised its 2026 export target to 1.5 million units from 1.3 million. In a strategic corporate development, TCL Electronics announced it will acquire a 51% stake in a new joint venture absorbing Sony's home entertainment business for approximately $475 million, a move that would position the combined entity above Samsung in global TV market share at roughly 16.7%. The deal exemplifies Chinese manufacturers' strategy of acquiring premium Western brand equity to penetrate higher-margin consumer segments in Europe and the US. Nevertheless, cracks are appearing in China's resilience narrative. A decline in shipping container volumes out of Chinese ports is a leading indicator worth watching. Smaller manufacturers reliant on oil-derived inputs are facing margin pressure, and the manufacturing PMI for smaller exporters slowed in March. Beijing's unusual activism in peace negotiations — including a five-point joint proposal with Pakistan calling for safeguarding Strait of Hormuz shipping — reflects the economic stakes for a country that sourced roughly a third of its growth from exports last year.
