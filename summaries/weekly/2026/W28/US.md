• FOMC minutes (Wednesday) delivered a hawkish surprise: "a few" members saw a case for an immediate rate hike at Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair, with "almost all" indicating policy firming would be warranted if inflation remained elevated.
• The May trade deficit widened to its largest since March 2025, with imports rising 3.3% and exports falling 3.2%, missing expectations and raising downside risk to Q2 GDP.
• Manufacturing employment stood 38,000 below year-ago levels as of Monday, undermining the tariff-driven industrial revival narrative.
• Trump declared the Iran ceasefire "over" on Wednesday, triggering fresh US strikes on more than 80 Iranian sites and a sharp repricing across rates, energy, and equities.
• Five of the six largest US banks are due to report Tuesday of the following week, with consensus anticipating near-record equity trading revenues amid months of sustained market volatility.

**Manufacturing and Trade Policy**

The gap between the administration's tariff revival narrative and underlying factory data widened further this week. The May trade deficit — the broadest available — showed export weakness concentrated in nonmonetary gold, likely reflecting the unwind of tariff-driven stockpiling flows from Switzerland; stripping out gold, goods exports still declined 2.4%. Bloomberg Economics flagged downside risk to Q2 GDP from the widening gap. On the policy front, the US Trade Representative opened a three-day hearing on forced-labor tariff investigations covering 60 countries, with 10%–12.5% additional duties flagged. The existing 10% global tariff baseline expires July 24th, creating the next major trade policy inflection point. Total extra tariffs paid by US businesses and consumers since March 2025 have reached $317 billion.

**Monetary Policy**

The June FOMC minutes were the week's most consequential domestic policy development. "Most participants" identified scenarios — AI-driven demand, Middle East conflict, tariff effects — in which inflation would remain elevated and "some policy firming would likely be warranted." The directional signal is unambiguous: the next rate move is now more likely up than down. The transmission mechanism reinforcing that posture is refined products: the gasoline crack spread sat near $57 per barrel, close to its highest since June 2022, with diesel crack spreads also materially elevated. The 10-year Treasury yield rose from 4.46% Monday to 4.58% by Thursday, partially retracing after the ceasefire collapse.

**Markets and Corporate News**

The AI trade experienced its first meaningful sentiment inflection of the cycle. Samsung's record quarterly operating profit — beating consensus by roughly 6% — triggered a sell-the-news response in Seoul that cascaded into US premarket trading, with Micron down 5.1%, Western Digital off 5.6%, and Seagate declining 4.4% on Tuesday. Morgan Stanley argued the divergence between chipmakers and hyperscalers was unsustainable and characterized the semiconductor correction as a healthy rebalancing. Amazon's $25 billion bond offering on Wednesday attracted demand of only 1.6 times deal size, well below the year's four-times average, suggesting appetite for hyperscaler credit may be cooling. The KBW Bank Index has risen 13% year-to-date, outpacing the S&P 500's 10% gain, ahead of major bank earnings. Vertex Pharmaceuticals agreed Tuesday to acquire Crinetics Pharmaceuticals for $10 billion in cash, its largest deal on record, to expand into endocrinology.
