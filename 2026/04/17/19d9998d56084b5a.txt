<https://bloomberg.com/authors/AOUGMYRGHl8/allen-k-wan> at the Canton Fair in 
Guangzhou.

The specter of the Iran war hung heavy like the banners inside the gigantic 
exhibition halls of the country’s oldest and biggest trade show this week.

The bi-annual fair in the southern city offered an ominous glimpse of how the 
US-Israel war against Iran isdimming the outlook 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/china-s-growth-rebound-tops-forecasts-with-iran-war-impact-muted?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 for an economy that remains dependent on manufacturing and exports for growth. 
Some exporters said orders are drying up due to fewer visitors from the key 
Middle East market. One businessman who did make it — Egyptian Abdul Rahman El 
Sharqawy — said he’d struggled to find flights as the war has led to 
skyrocketing fares.
Attendees at the Canton Fair in Guangzhou on April 15.Photographer: Qilai 
Shen/Bloomberg
In recent years, fewer visitors have been coming from the US and Europe — 
first due to the pandemic and then because of US tariffs — but they’d been 
replaced by customers from the Middle East and Africa.

It’s a geographical diversification that’s helped companies like Guangdong 
Baiyin Cleaning Co., which depends on exports for more than half of its 
business, ride out US President Donald Trump’s tariffs. But not this year.

“We have fewer Middle East buyers due to the conflict,” said Huang Gui Feng, 
area manager for the maker of scrubber machines and other cleaning equipment, 
who added that her company has been coming to the fair for over two decades and 
fears this year may be the worst.

“At first, it was the tariffs and now we have to deal with the war,” she said. 
“There is nothing we can do about it.”

But perhaps President Xi Jinping can. Beijing has been more active on the 
diplomatic front in recent weeks, aiming to end a conflict that has squeezed 
its Middle East oil supplies and risks a deeper global economic slowdown that 
would harm China’s export-led growth momentum.

While export growth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/china-s-export-growth-slowed-sharply-in-first-month-of-iran-war?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 slowed sharply in March, economic growth as a whole rebounded more than 
expected in the first quarter, with GDP expanding 5% from a year ago.Watch this 
video 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-04-16/china-s-economy-revs-up-despite-iran-war-video?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 on how the economy is revving up.

High-tech exports surged nearly 30% 
<http://www.customs.gov.cn/customs/2026-04/14/article_2026041410445544739.html> 
in the first quarter from a year ago, almost double the pace of overall 
exports. That offers hope that exports may stay resilient in the months ahead. 
Economists polled by Bloomberg forecast China’s overseas shipments to rise 4% 
this year after gaining5.5% <> last year.

In his first public statements on the war since the conflict began more than a
month ago, Xi this week told visiting Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez 
that the international order is“crumbling into disarray”  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/china-s-xi-urges-closer-spain-ties-to-counter-global-disarray?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
and vowed to play a constructive role in the Middle East.

The Chinese president — who’s casting the nation as a beacon of stability in 
an increasingly volatile world — will have a chance to discuss his views on the 
global order in roughly a month from now ata summit meeting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/trump-hormuz-blockade-risks-showdown-with-china-before-xi-summit?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 with Trump.

While the US-China detente is holding up, there were signs of strain this week 
with Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent blasting China as an unreliable partner 
to the world by “hoarding” oil and Trump seeking assurances from Xi in aletter 
exchange 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/trump-says-he-exchanged-letters-with-xi-over-iran-war?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 that Beijing wasn’t providing weapons to Iran.

Peace can’t come soon enough for Liao Kewen, CEO of drone-maker Guangzhou 
Chengzhi Intelligent Machinery Technology Co., who has been seeing his 
shipments to its Middle East customers held hostage by the conflict.

A delivery his firm sent out in February to the Middle East is still sitting 
in Indonesia, he said, adding “that’s why during this period our Middle East 
clients are just negotiating but won’t place an order.”

Under the Hood

At Tesla’s gigafactory on the outskirts of Shanghai — its most productive 
plant — robotic arms weld and fit car parts like high precision surgeons, 
helping to churn out an electric vehicle every30 seconds 
<https://sichuan.scol.com.cn/ggxw/202511/83149962.html>. 

Bloomberg reporters got a glimpse of the facility during a rare tour organized 
by the government this week. The Elon Musk-led company’s Shanghai operations 
are typically media shy and the last time that Bloomberg had access to the site 
was before the pandemic.
Workers inspect a Tesla Inc. Model Y electric vehicle on a production line at 
the company’s Gigafactory plant in Shanghai.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
The plant, first opened in 2019, pioneered many of the innovations that other 
Chinese electric vehicle makers came to adopt. Among the most notable is the 
so-called gigacasting technology, which uses a giant die-casting machine to 
press the car chassis into shape, streamlining a process that previously 
comprised dozens of parts into one aluminum alloy piece.

But compared to some of the newer EV factories we’ve visited, the Tesla Model 
3 production line in Shanghai has started to show some wear and tear.

Smudges of dirt and grease were seen on some of the machinery. Although 
there’s a high degree of automation, about 95% in welding and painting lines, 
there were fewer automated guided vehicles transporting inventory than what 
we’ve seen in other facilities such as premium EV brand Zeekr’s Intelligent 
Factory or the Super Factory of Huawei partner Seres.

The fact that the oldest production lines are only six years old but are 
already showing their age compared to local rivals demonstrates just how fast 
the country’s EV industry is developing and how rapidly fortunes can change.

In 2020, the first year of operating the Shanghai factory, Tesla became the 
top selling EV brand in China, beating out BYD. The Model 3 sedan and 
subsequent Model Y sport utility vehicle sold consumers on EV’s range and 
intelligent features. That meant local manufacturers had to improve to catch 
up, with BYD last year overtaking Tesla as the world’slargest 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-01/byd-sells-4-6-million-vehicles-in-2025-meets-revised-sales-goal?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 seller of EVs.

Now, Tesla and China’s EV brands are seeing a boost in demand from the Iran 
oil shock. Tesla’s Shanghai factory shipments rose 9% in March compared to the 
same time last year, boosted by exports. And while the Model 3 production line 
in Shanghai may be looking a little dated, there’s now a gleaming new Megapack 
factory next to it, Tesla’s first battery plant outside of the US.

Musk is shifting Tesla’s focus to robotics, robotaxis and energy storage 
systems, betting rapid growth in AI will drive demand. Although the company 
says there’s no plans at this stage to start making robots at the Shanghai 
site, Tesla’s China head Allen Wang said he was confident of the factory’s 
potential.

“Once we’re in the age of humanoid robotics, the Shanghai Gigafactory, like 
other Tesla Gigafactories, will take on an very important role,” Wang said.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

China’s social media world is turning sour on Lululemon.

The yoga-wear maker is getting roasted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/lululemon-sparks-china-social-media-debate-on-texas-pfas-probe?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 on platforms such as Xiaohongshu and Weibo amid concerns over the safety of 
its products following aTexas investigation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/lululemon-slips-as-texas-announces-probe-of-forever-chemicals?cmpid=BBD041726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 into its clothing for the presence of so-called “forever chemicals.”

“I’ve paid 1,000 yuan for yoga pants,” said one Weibo user. “Are they slowly 
poisoning me?”
Clothing on display at the Lululemon flagship store in Shanghai.Photographer: 
Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
Lululemon China has pushed back, saying all its products sold in mainland 
China are free of polyfluoroalkyl substances, or PFAS. These substances are 
difficult to degrade and long-term exposure mayincrease the risk 
<https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC8878656/> of liver and kidney damage 
as well as certain types of cancer, according to various studies.

Even if the company is ultimately cleared, the episode highlights how quickly 
sentiment can shift in China’s consumer market. Lululemon has been one of the 
few Western brands to sustain strong momentum in the country since the 
pandemic. While North America has slowed, Lululemon posted about 30% sales 
expansion in China in the fiscal year ended in February.

On Xiaohongshu, reactions have been polarized. Some users questioned whether 
they should continue supporting the brand, while others struck a more 
opportunistic tone, hoping the controversy would lead to discounts if resellers 
are forced to cut prices or ease chronic stock shortages of popular items.

“I’m still alive and kicking after all those food additives,” one Xiaohongshu 
user wrote. “Why would I be scared of a piece of clothing worn outside?”
