A Trade Whodunnit Shows How US-China Decoupling Is a Myth
A deeper look at trade data reveals two nations with strong links

Hi, this is James Mayger in Beijing, where this week we solved a mystery of 
pretty epic proportions.

Call it The Case of the Missing Tariffs.

Consider this big clue: US data show that last year shipments from China 
tumbled 30%. Scholars and politicians in the US have pointed to that figure as 
evidence that tariffs wereforcing America to decouple 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-25/us-china-tension-fuels-decoupling-in-tech-research-study-shows?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
, at least in trade, from a geopolitical rivalsome deem an existential threat 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/pentagon-spends-more-on-surveillance-of-china-s-subs-satellites?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

Not so fast.

When we dug into the data we discovered that Chinese exports were still pretty 
healthy in 2025.

The disparity between the two sets of data reveals a staggering problem — one 
that argues very strongly that the decoupling talk is wildly overblown. Namely, 
there is a whopping $112 billion gap between what China said it was exporting 
to the US and what America said actually arrived.

That means up to a quarter of what Asia’s top economy shipped to American 
shores last year slipped pastthe tariff man 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/latest/trump-tariffs>. Read more about the trade 
whodunnit here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-24/china-s-112-billion-cargo-gap-shows-record-us-tariff-evasion?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

There are a number of likely culprits for the gap but fraud looks to be one of 
the main explanations. American importers are getting bombarded with WhatsApp 
messages and emails promising a deal that seems too good to be legal: move 
goods from China to the US while avoidingPresident Donald Trump’s levies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/us-to-keep-china-tariffs-steady-avoid-escalation-greer-says?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.
The Port of Long Beach in California, one of the US’s busiest container 
terminals.Photographer: Kyle Grillot/Bloomberg
Companies that take up the deal pose an especially acute problem for honest 
businesses. Take Michael Kersey, president of American Lawn Mower Co. His firm 
began outsourcing production to China two decades ago and has paid duties of as 
much as 45% to bring goods into the US over the past year.

That outlay puts him at a decided pricing disadvantage against competitors who 
duck the duties. “Tariff cheating is much, much worse than tariffs for us,” 
Kersey said.

US authorities know about the suspected tariff evasion tactics but have a hard 
time keeping up with the cheats. Shell companies can offer a business a deep 
shipping discount, undervalue the arriving cargo on customs declarations and 
then disappear with their ill-gotten gains.

Rinse and repeat, and stay well ahead of law enforcement indefinitely.

“If you want to commit fraud, this is how you would do it,” said Carrie Owens, 
who recently left the Department of Homeland Security’s intelligence office and 
previously led the Enforcement Operations Division at US Customs and Border 
Protection.

“You would take on the liability, put it onto a shell company, who then you 
can run away from very easily and start a new one up,” she said.

The fraud may also be undercutting one of Trump’s main reasons for the tariffs 
in the first place:reviving manufacturing back home 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-09/trump-s-tariffs-aren-t-helping-bring-back-struggling-us-manufacturing?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

US firms will be loath to open any new plants if they can’t compete with 
foreign companies paying low tariffs — possibly none at all — to get their 
goods into the American market.

This problem also complicates Washington’s efforts to use the import taxes as 
a tool of economic policy, and it’s not likely to go away anytime soon. A 
number of different tariffs remain on Chinese goods even after the Supreme Court
ruled Trump can’t use a 1977 emergency law to impose the duties 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/trump-s-global-tariffs-struck-down-by-us-supreme-court?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

World Traveler

And this is Allen Wan, in Beijing, fresh off welcoming in the Year of the Fire 
Horse 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/lunar-new-year-2026-10-luxury-gifts-for-the-year-of-the-horse?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 by visiting some hot spots in China and Japan.

In Harbin — a city in northeastern China that’s known for its 
Russian-influenced architecture — I saw firsthand how domestic travelers 
enjoyingthe long Lunar New Year holiday 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-20/chinese-new-year-2026-what-lunar-holiday-tells-us-about-china-s-dining-habits?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 were out in big numbers, though maybe not with big pockets.

They swamped the tourist streets, restaurants, and the famous ice and snow 
festival 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2019-01-06/harbin-ice-festival-in-china-video?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
. Such exuberance provided a bullish yet qualified signal for consumption in 
China.

In fact, total domestic tourism spending over the break, which at nine days 
was one day longer than usual, climbed to a record. The country recorded 596 
million local tourist trips during the period, 95 million more than a year 
earlier. Domestic travel and hotel stays, retail sales and overseas visits all 
jumped.

While the figures are impressive at first glance, it would be premature to 
call this a turning point considering consumer confidence, property sales and 
other key indicators remain sluggish.

Read more about what the holiday says about China’s economy here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/china-holiday-travel-booms-in-sign-of-consumer-sentiment-rebound?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 and aboutShanghai easing real estate rules here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/shanghai-eases-homebuying-rules-in-sign-of-china-property-relief?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.
Shoppers at a bazaar in Shanghai just before the Lunar New Year holiday.
Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
More Chinese were traveling but, thing is, they weren’t spending more than in 
the past. Per-trip outlay was 1,348 yuan, a touch less than a year ago. 
Government measures to spur consumption — from extending the holiday to 
offering subsidies or cash rewards for purchases — seemed to help but whether 
the momentum can pick up depends on whether more policy support is on the way.

Businesses badly need that backing. For a tourist hotspot as popular as 
Harbin, plane tickets and top hotels were surprisingly cheap, the result of 
several years ofintense price competition 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/china-bans-below-cost-car-sales-to-end-prolonged-price-war?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 anddeflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-china-deflation-cost/?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 that has been exacerbated by travel apps offering tons of discounts.

That hints at the long way the government has to go to get people spending 
again and break the deflationary cycle.

My travels in Japan showed me something different but just as interesting: how 
the Beijing-Tokyo tensions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/china-puts-export-curb-on-japan-firms-including-mitsubishi-heavy?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 are discouraging Chinese travel. That frictionhas been full throttle since 
November 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-09/xi-faces-dilemma-over-taiwan-after-takaichi-s-big-win-in-japan?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 last year, when Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi suggested Tokyo could deploy its 
military if China uses force to try and seize Taiwan.

Beijing has rolled out a series of retaliatory measures in response, including 
discouraging its people from traveling to Japan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-25/why-china-is-reducing-flights-upping-economic-retaliation-against-japan?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 and reducing the number of flights.

In January, arrivals from China shrank 61% from a year earlier, according to 
the Japan National Tourism Organization. That followed a 45% decline in 
December.

At the famous Kenrokuen Garden in Kanazawa 
<https://visitkanazawa.jp/en/attractions/detail_10106.html>, a staffer told me 
that there were fewer tourists from China this holiday.

That made sense when I checked flights to the city. There were fewer options 
from China than in the past, and the available flights were either super 
expensive for a direct shot or required long layovers. (Chinese travelers did 
jet off to Thailand, a sign thatworries about scam centers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/cambodia-claims-its-crackdown-has-halved-online-scam-activity?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 are receding. Read more aboutpeople hitting the Land of Smiles here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/avoiding-japan-chinese-tourists-fuel-rebound-in-thai-tourism?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.)

The scenes in both Harbin and Kanazawa made it clear that Japan wasn’t a 
terribly enticing destination for Chinese people, not when they had cheaper 
options right back home.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

If the recently concluded Milano Cortina Winter games 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/latest/2026-winter-olympics> handed out a medal for 
most criticism dodged, a top contender would definitely beUS-born freestyle 
skier Eileen Gu or Gu Ailing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2022-01-27/eileen-gu-china-s-best-hope-for-winter-olympics-gold-is-being-very-carefulhttps://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2022-01-27/eileen-gu-china-s-best-hope-for-winter-olympics-gold-is-being-very-careful?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 as she’s known in China.

The fact that she represented America’s biggest geopolitical rival — 
successfully to boot, winning a gold and two silvers — galled certain US 
politicians such as J.D. Vance.

The American vice president told Fox News he hoped athletes who grew up in the 
US, benefited from the education system and enjoyed its freedoms would want to 
compete for the country.

Gu responded by telling USA Today that, well, first Vance was “sweet” but that 
while a lot of athletes competed for different countries, some people “just 
hate China.”
Eileen Gu, who won three medals including a gold at the Milano Cortina games.
Photographer: Hector Vivas/Getty Images
“I admire Gu Ailing very much, not only because of the gold medal but also 
because of her ability to resist online violence,” wrote a Chinese Weibo user 
with 260,000 followers whose name translates to Jade Shore.

Many other Americans competed (and medaled) for other countries during the 
games, though few get as much attention as Gu. That might be because few are as 
talented as a model who studies international relations and some quantum 
physics at Stanford. (Maybe that latter subject comes in handy when she’s 
defying the laws of gravity in theBig Air 
<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=dB8nrfLBvGU>.)
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
In China, Gu is mostly seen as a national hero and role model among young 
people,complimented for her beauty, brains and guts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-02-15/eileen-gu-wins-silver-after-fall-but-she-s-still-golden-in-china?cmpid=BBD022726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
. It helps that her face is all over shopping malls due to her gigs with top 
fashion brands.

Still, some on Weibo had their own criticism of Gu, saying that competing for 
the Asian nation was only a cash grab. They pointed to the millions of dollars 
she’d get from her Chinese supporters and endorsements.

“No one can resist the temptation of huge amounts of money,” another Weibo 
user wrote.

Well, it’s real tough making everyone happy, isn’t it?
