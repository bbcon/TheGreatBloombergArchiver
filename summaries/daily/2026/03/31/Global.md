The defining story of March 2026 is the US-Iran conflict and its cascading effects on global energy markets. After a month of fighting, the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly 135 vessels transited daily under normal conditions — is seeing fewer than six vessels per day on average. Iran's most consequential strategic achievement has not been on the battlefield but in its grip over this chokepoint. An Iranian drone struck a fully laden Kuwaiti oil tanker at anchor near Dubai on Tuesday, one of the most significant vessel attacks of the conflict, temporarily pushing WTI toward $107. WTI has risen more than 50% in March, the largest monthly gain since May 2020, and closed above $100 for the first time since hostilities began.

Despite this, benchmark crude futures have not fully priced the physical disruption — jet fuel in Europe has surged to all-time highs equivalent to over $215 per barrel, while crude remains in the low triple digits. The divergence reflects record IEA emergency reserve releases, a well-supplied market entering the conflict, and trader reluctance to hold leveraged positions into weekends when major strikes have occurred. Energy consultancy FGE NexantECA warns that if the near-closure of Hormuz persists for six to eight weeks, Brent could reach $150–$200.

A separate threat is materializing via the Houthis in Yemen. Tehran is urging the group to renew attacks on Red Sea shipping; Bloomberg Intelligence estimates oil could surge to $140 per barrel if Houthi strikes resume targeting crude flows bypassing Hormuz. That would constitute what analysts describe as the largest energy disruption in history, compounding an already severe shock. Fuel shortages are spreading to Sub-Saharan Africa — Kenya, Ethiopia, and Zambia are reporting station outages, with prices in some markets set to rise as much as 50%.

Geopolitically, Secretary of State Rubio declared NATO's response "very disappointing," calling out allies for denying base access — a statement that may presage a formal reassessment of the US-NATO relationship once the conflict concludes. The WTO's 14th ministerial conference ended without agreement on renewing the e-commerce tariff moratorium, prompting the US Trade Representative to declare the organization's role in global trade policy "limited" and pivot toward bilateral and plurilateral agreements.
