In a topsy turvy world, China is proving stolidly predictable: High-profile 
purges. Exports blowing out. Weak domestic spending a persistent drag on the 
economy.

Former Politburo member Ma Xingrui was expelled 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/china-expels-former-politburo-member-over-graft-and-sex-charges?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6517>
 from the ruling Communist Party on corruption charges, state broadcaster CCTV 
said Tuesday. Ma, a former party secretary of Xinjiang, was put under 
investigation in April for “severe violations” of unspecified laws and party 
discipline.

His alleged offenses include accepting bribes, helping family members buy 
property at a discount and trading political favors for sex, the broadcaster 
said. The purge is the latest in an escalating anti-graft campaign that’s seen 
former Politburo member He Weidong expelledfrom the Party 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-17/china-ousts-top-military-official-as-xi-widens-corruption-purge?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6517>
 in October and Zhang Youxia — Xi Jinping’s former top general —placed under 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-26/xi-s-purge-of-top-general-spurs-questions-on-taiwan-succession?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6517>
 investigation in January.

Exports, meanwhile, surged 27% in June, up from 19.4% in May and widening the 
trade surplus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/china-s-exports-imports-soar-faster-than-forecast-amid-ai-rush?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6517>
 to $125.6 billion — the second-biggest ever, official data Tuesday showed. 
Exports were buoyed by demand for hardware amid the AI boom, whileshipments of 
cars <bbg://news/stories/TI5EOCT9NJLT> surged to more than a million for the 
first time.

Such explosive growth will help the economy, but subdued consumer spending 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-16/china-s-retail-sales-fall-for-first-time-since-covid-in-new-worry-for-economy?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6517>
, a continuing slump in the housing market and weak investment are stilllikely 
to weigh 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/china-s-growth-seen-weakening-to-bottom-of-official-target-range?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6517>
 on growth. Economists see the government on Wednesday reporting a 4.5% gain in 
gross domestic product for the second quarter, according to the median forecast 
in a Bloomberg survey.

That would be down from the 5% year-on-year increase in the first three months 
of the year, and leave growth skirting the bottom of Beijing’s 2026 target 
range of 4.5% to 5%.

Not great, but no surprise either.
