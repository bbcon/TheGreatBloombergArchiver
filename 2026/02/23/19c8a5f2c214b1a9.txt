Trump’s ‘Draconian’ Toolkit

Opponents of tariffs cheered on Friday when the US Supreme Court nixed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/trump-tariffs-ruled-illegal-by-supreme-court-what-to-know?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 Trump’s ability to impose, effectively at will, any import duties he wanted. 
But as the old adage goes, be careful what you wish for.

The so-called IEEPA tariffs had offered what Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent 
said hours after the ruling was a clean, “instantaneous” tool of leverage. And 
Trump used them, or the threat of them, for everything from the gaping trade 
deficit to the fentanyl crisis to seeking greater US swayover Greenland 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-19/why-does-trump-want-greenland-could-he-really-take-over-the-danish-territory?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
.

Removing that particular tool is hardly likely to stop Trump from unveiling a 
shocking threat in order to force a negotiation. As Wall Street investor 
Anthony Scaramucci — who briefly worked as White House communications director 
in Trump’s first administration —explained at Davos 
<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=Ag3DEDmshaE> last month, Trump is “a reality 
television producer.”

“So, you create conflict, you create drama, some level of histrionics, and 
then you have a climactic situation where it goes away,” Scaramucci said in a 
Sky News interview. “Get everybody upset. Watch it on television, get a giggle 
to himself, and then work it out.”
Anthony ScaramucciPhotographer: Stefan Wermuth/Bloomberg
IEEPA was a tool for creating that drama. Now, Trump has already turned to a 
temporary alternative 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/trump-pegs-new-tariffs-to-a-payments-crisis-experts-doubt-exists?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
, a15% levy for 150 days 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/trump-says-he-will-increase-global-tariffs-to-15-from-10?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
, to buy time as the administration prepares ahost of other 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/trump-s-tariffs-ruled-illegal-by-supreme-court-what-are-his-options-now?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 tailored duties for products and trading partners. But none of those offer the 
kind of leverage Trump has relied on to either pressure other nations into 
talks on a given issue or to punish them for perceived slights (as with a 
Canadiantelevision ad 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-01/carney-says-he-apologized-to-trump-over-tariff-advertisement?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 last year).

Bessent highlighted on Fox News Friday that the Supreme Court ruling had 
provided authorization for a “draconian alternative.” That would be “a complete 
embargo” — cutting a nation off from selling any products in the US.

Other leverage options are apparent from Trump’s record, such as using 
regulation. In January, hethreatened to decertify 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/trump-says-us-to-decertify-levy-tariff-on-planes-from-canada?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 all new aircraft made in Canada. In 2024, running for his return to the White 
House, Trumpraised questions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-02-11/us-calls-trump-s-russia-remark-on-nato-allies-appalling?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 about whether the US would come to the aid of a NATO member invaded by Russia.

All sorts of tools can be imagined, including sowing doubts about the American 
nuclear umbrella for key allies. Or thenuclear equivalent 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-11-22/forget-tariffs-capital-controls-are-game-changer-trump-risk?cmpid=BBD022326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 in finance: Capital controls and access to the US dollar.

Which may help explain why major trading partners

 aren’t rushing to repudiateagreements

 reached on the basis of IEEPA tariffs. China said that it’sclosely monitoring

Need-to-Know Research

The general narrative about Kevin Warsh, Trump’s planned nominee to become the 
next Federal Reserve chair, is that he was a hawk on monetary policy following 
the financial crisis, but turned dovish after the president took office at the 
start of last year. Now there’s some numerical backup to that assessment.

Bloomberg Economics analyzed the former Fed board member’s public comments 
going back to 2009 through its Fedspeak Natural Language Processingmodel <>. 
The exercise “confirms a striking evolution in his thinking,” economistNick 
Hallmark <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWY998hQvVs/nick-hallmark> wrote in 
a note last week.

Over the period from 2009 to 2011, when he left the board, Warsh “regularly 
showed up as more hawkish than the broader Fedspeak index,” Hallmark said. But 
since 2025, “his statements score as substantially more dovish” than current 
policymakers. “Whether that reflects changed economic circumstances, a 
philosophical shift, or smart messaging in an intense race will be one of the 
most consequential questions for monetary policy in the years ahead.”
