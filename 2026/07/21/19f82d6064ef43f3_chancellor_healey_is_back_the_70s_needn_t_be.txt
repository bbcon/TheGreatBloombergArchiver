Chancellor Healey Is Back

Meet Britain’s new boss, slightly different from the old boss 
<https://bloom.bg/40mIsDg>. Andy Burnham has arrived in 10 Downing Street after 
a remarkably swift and bloodless ascent. He is the best communicator to occupy 
the top job in British politics since Tony Blair (with the possible exception 
of the divisive Boris Johnson), and must now try to end a damaging streak of 
short premierships.

That will mean persuading voters to give him and his Labour Party another 
chance in the general election due three years hence. And in the shorter term, 
that will require getting the bond market to cut him some slack, without making 
concessions thatundermine his appeal to the electorate 
<https://bloom.bg/4vR0SsU>. It’s not going to be easy. 

The experience of Liz Truss, felled only seven weeks after becoming prime 
minister by a gilts market revolt against unfunded tax cuts, provides crucial 
discipline <https://bloom.bg/4gIWTup>. And gilts investors did indeed greet 
Burnham with a rise of 8.1 basis points in the 10-year gilt yield, taking it 
back above 5%. That’s higher than it reached under Truss:

Other than a brief spike when Labour’s terrible local elections in May opened 
the risk of a chaotic premiership contest, this is as high as long gilt yields 
have been since 2008. Burnham should consider himself warned. It’s part of an 
inexorable decline in confidence in the UK’s fiscal management. At the start of 
this century, gilts used to trade at much the same yield as German bunds; now, 
the gap is close to two percentage points. That’s a problem for any prime 
minister who wants to spend money:

To prove to the bond market that he was listening, Burnham announced John 
Healey as the new chancellor of the exchequer. Speculation had centered on Ed 
Miliband, who was made foreign secretary, or Shabana Mahmood, home secretary. 
Polymarket’s odds accurately capture that this came as a total blindside:

Healey shares a surname with Denis Healey <https://bloom.bg/3TeFEY7>, Labour’s 
chancellor for five years inthe 1970s <https://bloom.bg/4ntKpIr> (they aren’t 
related). This enabled critics to bring back incidents like Britain’s need to 
borrow from the IMF in 1976 <https://bloom.bg/4lOM8pd>. However, the symbolism 
of appointing him seems aimed at reassurance that the UK is going to stay 
within limits.

John Healey served another former chancellor, Gordon Brown, as a Treasury 
minister in the 1990s and 2000s. Most recently, he was Keir Starmer’s defense 
secretary, and inflicted terrible political damage on him by resigning over 
what he thought was an inadequate budget for the UK’s attempt to rearm. 
Presumably, he was assured that he could hike the defense budget before he took 
his new job.
No relation: John Healey in Downing Street. Photographer: Chris J. 
Ratcliffe/Bloomberg 
Funding stronger defense and keeping bond markets onside presumably means less 
money for the many other projects that would be popular with voters. Burnham is 
going to need a lot of political skill to make this work.

But the bottom line for markets is clear. Burnham knows he needs to respect 
them. Like Tony Blair in 1997, who won years of leeway by making the Bank of 
England independent in his first week in office, this is a surgical strike 
designed to win investors’ trust and ensure that Britain’s discount doesn’t 
deepen further. John Healey doesn’t want to be Denis. Burnham is doing his 
utmost not to be Liz Truss.

Big Mo Takes a Beating

The main US indexes are still sitting on comfortable gains for the year, close 
to all-time highs. But the movement of the plates immediately below the surface 
is dramatic.

This month, the biggest US semiconductor groups are all down more than 20%. 
That’s often labeled a bear market, which is absurd as they’re still ahead more 
than 60% in 12 months. But it certainly qualifies as a dramatic reverse:

It was not hard to see this coming. On July 1 <https://bloom.bg/4vzmw4t>, 
Points of Return celebrated a remarkable first half with a bulletin from 
Hindsight Capital, our imaginary hedge fund that can foresee the future. At 
that point, semiconductors’ extreme outperformance of software looked 
unsustainable. It was easy to imagine that with hindsight, it would seem that a 
crash was always inevitable. This is the chart we published three weeks ago:

And this is how that same chart looks now:

There is nothing inherently unhealthy about this. Anyone could see that the 
chips trade looked overdone by midyear. That something like this would happen 
before long was a good bet, but timing these things is impossible. As it turned 
out, the reversal started just as the month turned — and it was good to get it 
over with swiftly.

What could be an issue, however, is the impact this has on active investors’ 
favorite factor — momentum. The process of trusting the signals the market is 
sending, buying the stocks that have been winning and selling or shorting those 
that have been lagging, is the backbone of many successful investment 
strategies. It works most of the time, but investors have to put up with 
occasional drastic reversals, like the one now underway. And they need to be 
prepared to manage risks.
SK Hynix has had a volatile ride since its blockbuster US listing on July 10. 
Photographer: Michael Nagle/Bloomberg 
Travis Prentice of Informed Momentum <https://bloom.bg/45g2jpC>, a 
California-based fund manager, describes the approach as “taking the steps 
upwards and being prepared to take the elevator down.” The reverse came at the 
end of a quarter when the Russell indexes had just rebalanced, so it remains 
unclear whether this is the beginning of a new trend or a “natural correction 
to let off steam.”

Momentum strategies tend to do particularly well compared with value — the 
other most popular investment style, which involves buying stocks that look 
cheap compared to their fundamentals — when the overall market is also doing 
well. Over time, momentum stocks’ ratio to value stocks tends to correlate 
closely with stocks’ ratio to bonds. The market started a huge rally at the end 
of March as hopes of a ceasefire in the Gulf took hold. But by June, stocks 
were pausing while the momentum trade blazed ahead. That was a big hint that 
something unsustainable was afoot:

Also, surprisingly, it turns out that there is more to it than the incredible 
hopes put in semiconductors. Bloomberg’s Factors To Watch function shows that 
China saw just as big a momentum surge as the US, and has taken an even nastier 
July reverse. This is now Big Mo’s biggest setback since the pandemic:

If there’s something more unhealthy about this, it concerns the involvement of 
retail investors. Democratizing trading is always invoked as a worthy and lofty 
goal, and in principle it sounds great. If poorer people get to profit from the 
gains in capital markets, so much the better for everyone.

The problem is that in practice, democratizing markets in fact involves luring 
in the little guys just in time to take the fall. The appearance of leveraged 
single-stock exchange-traded funds to trade big semi manufacturers has been 
particularly alarming. Such vehicles are only of use for short-term speculation 
and are aimed at the retail market — hedge funds already have access to 
instruments to make these trades without paying someone to put together an ETF 
for them.

When Mom and Pop investors are involved, they tend to boost trading volumes, 
which boomed last quarter and made buckets of money for Wall Street banks. 
Their presence tends to exacerbate momentum, and with it volatility. That 
explains why China and other Asian markets have seen even more of a reverse 
this month. As Societe Generale SA’s Andrew Lapthorne illustrates, Asian 
trading volumes have rallied in recent years, even as activity in Europe is 
long-term decline:

To quote Lapthorne:

Europe looks almost sleepy by comparison. Trading activity is only a fraction 
of that seen elsewhere, which may explain why momentum strategies have been far 
more resilient there. If you’re looking for shelter from the collapse in 
retail-driven momentum trades, Europe currently looks like one of the safer 
harbors.

Beyond the risk of pain for individuals, a reverse on this scale could mean 
trouble for quant managers. Momentum has now delivered weaker returns under 
Trump 2.0 than a strategy of buying the most heavily shorted stocks while 
shorting the least:

The big semiconductor groups are the least shorted stocks in the US, so this 
is not surprising. But losses for trend-followers plus reverses for 
short-sellers imply bad times for quant hedge funds.

Earnings season could provide a catalyst in either direction. If the 
semiconductor story of huge AI-driven demand and tight supply survives intact, 
there could be an opportunity fora new momentum trade <https://bloom.bg/4vKIyRP>
. It’s healthy that Big Mo has been reined in. But it’s worth watching to 
ensure that the reversal doesn’t gather speed of its own, and start to inflict 
serious losses on quants and on Mom and Pop.



 Survival Tips 



Farewell to Kevin Keegan <https://bloom.bg/4vMh925>, who has passed away at 
75. England’s soccer captain and later its manager, winner of two Ballons d’Or, 
he was possibly the first true English superstar, setting a template that David 
Beckham would to turn into global fame and billions of dollars. Keegan had a 
knack for saving his greatest for the big occasions like the1974 FA Cup final 
<https://bloom.bg/4fpteE3> or the 1977 European Cup final 
<https://bloom.bg/4fhBENK>, and he had a moderately OK hit single 
<https://bloom.bg/45dMCPP>.

As manager of a great <https://bloom.bg/45dtyRE> Newcastle 
<https://bloom.bg/4pwaWFO> team in the 1990s, he fell victim to the mind games 
of Manchester United’s Alex Ferguson and gave afamous interview 
<https://bloom.bg/4gJYjov>, saying he would “love it” if his team could beat 
Man U (they didn’t). The closest American equivalent might be Jim Mora being 
asked aboutthe playoffs <https://bloom.bg/45dsaOX>. Keegan maybe had too much 
passion, but he shared it, and it was exciting. Rest in Peace, Kevin.
