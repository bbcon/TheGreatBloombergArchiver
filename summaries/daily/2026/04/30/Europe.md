The eurozone economy unexpectedly decelerated at the start of 2026, with first-quarter GDP growth falling short of consensus estimates as surging energy costs — a direct consequence of the Strait of Hormuz blockade — weigh on industrial output and household purchasing power. The configuration increasingly resembles a stagflationary dynamic: energy-driven inflation pressures are building precisely as growth momentum fades. Germany, Italy, and Spain individually outperformed expectations, but the aggregate picture remains concerning, and the trajectory into the second quarter depends heavily on whether oil prices stabilize or continue to rise.

The European Central Bank and the Bank of England both held rates steady at their respective April meetings, in line with consensus expectations. The policy stance represents a holding pattern rather than a clear directional signal: both institutions face a genuine dilemma between responding to accelerating energy inflation and cushioning economies that are more directly exposed to the Middle East conflict than the United States. Market pricing has shifted materially over recent months — traders now expect the ECB to begin hiking rates in June, with three increases priced in for the year, a dramatic reversal from February's expectations of unchanged rates throughout 2026. Whether that pricing holds will depend on how durable the oil price surge proves to be, and whether second-round inflation effects materialize in wage and services data.

UK domestic politics present a separate source of uncertainty. Keir Starmer's Labour government is projected to lose approximately 1,850 seats in next week's local elections, according to expert analysis, a performance that would raise credible questions about the Prime Minister's political durability. Standard Chartered, BNP Paribas, and Société Générale each reported first-quarter results that exceeded expectations, offering some evidence that European financial institutions are navigating the energy shock more robustly than their equity valuations — which have underperformed the S&P 500 by roughly 9 percentage points since the Iran conflict began — might suggest.
