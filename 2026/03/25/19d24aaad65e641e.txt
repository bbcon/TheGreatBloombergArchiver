No Good Options

Armed with only the bluntest of instruments, central banks have just two 
options when it comes to responding to inflation pressures stemming from the 
war in Iran: look through them or act quickly to snuff them out.

Over the past week, comments from central bank chiefs in Australia and New 
Zealand have pointed to divergent approaches.
Anna BremanPhotographer: Mark Coote/Bloomberg
In a speech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/breman-signals-rbnz-won-t-rush-to-raise-rates-on-iran-war?cmpid=BBD032526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 in Auckland on Tuesday, RBNZ Governor Anna Breman said:
“A short-lived disruption and a temporary increase in petrol prices can — and 
should — be looked through from a monetary policy perspective if it is unlikely 
to have an impact on medium-term inflation outcomes.” Michele Bullock
Photographer: Brendon Thorne/Bloomberg
A week earlier, after raising interest rates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/australia-delivers-first-back-to-back-rate-hikes-since-mid-2023?cmpid=BBD032526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 for a second straight meeting, RBA Governor Michele Bullock had this to say:
“This hit with fuel prices and this additional rise in mortgage rates is going 
to be hard for some people. I do understand that. But it will be much worse if 
inflation gets built into the fibres and then we will see the costs of 
everything going up and that will be a much worse outcome.”
The contrasting approach may come down to the scars of their predecessors.

Then-RBNZ chief Adrian Orr acted swiftly as Covid-supply shocks intersected 
with Russia’s 2022 invasion of Ukraine to spike inflation, with unemployment 
surging as a result. By contrast, then-RBA chief Philip Lowe sought to preserve 
job gains, leaving the bank with a longer inflation battle on its hands.

This time around, it seems Breman is taking the softly-softly approach while 
Bullock is more firmly fixated on the inflation side of her mandate.

A lot comes down to the starting point. Surging government spending has 
already driven prices higher in Australia, withheadline inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/australia-s-inflation-was-elevated-before-war-driven-oil-shock?cmpid=BBD032526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 running at 3.7% in February, even before higher oil, gas, fertilizer and other 
costs filter through supply chains. In New Zealand, aprice gauge <> compiled by 
Bloomberg Economics estimates inflation was a cooler 3.2% in February, giving 
Breman a better chance of keeping expectations anchored somewhere around target.

It’s a debate playing out in the world’s largest economy too.

As US Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell said earlier this month, standard 
economics says that “you look through energy shocks — but that’s always been 
dependent on inflation expectations remaining well-anchored.”

In the US case, there’s a bit of an issue, because this oil shock comes in the 
wake of the Fed having well overshot its 2% inflation target for five straight 
years. The latest problem: dealing with the impact of President Donald Trump’s 
import levies.

US policymakers aren’t even going to think about “whether we look through the 
energy inflation” until that tariff box has been checked, Powell said March 18. 
Whenever the oil question “does arise, it will be one to approach not lightly,” 
given recent history, he said.

Need-to-Know Research

China’s policymakers regularly explain that their nation’s giant trade surplus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-14/china-ends-2025-with-1-2-trillion-trade-surplus-as-exports-soar?cmpid=BBD032526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 reflects the natural competitiveness of Chinese companies.Fresh analysis 
<https://www.federalreserve.gov/econres/notes/feds-notes/chinas-trade-dominance-and-the-role-of-industrial-policies-20260323.html>
 from Fed economists, however, suggests industrial policy plays a role.

The team including François de Soyres used data from the recently established 
New Industrial Policy Observatory 
<https://globaltradealert.org/reports/new-industrial-policy-observatory-nipo> 
(NIPO) database, a record of state measures since 2017. This covers not just 
subsidies but things like procurement policies and credit provision. “Evidence 
points to a systematic link between industrial policy interventions and China’s 
trade outcomes,” the Fed economists wrote.
Source: François de Soyres, Ece Fisgin, Mike Liu and Eva Van Leemput
By using a wide range of instruments, policymakers can affect trade via 
multiple channels, they said — such as lowering financing and production costs, 
reducing risk for new entrants and helping scale up quickly. “Of roughly 200 
sectors in our dataset, the top 15 in terms of policy support contributed 76% 
of the increase in the aggregate trade surplus over this period,” they said.
