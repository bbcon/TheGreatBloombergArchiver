**The Strait and the Strategic Impasse**
The US-Iran conflict, now one month old, remains the organizing fact of global markets. Trump's weekend ultimatum — threatening to bomb Iranian power plants unless the Strait of Hormuz was reopened within forty-eight hours — was effectively rolled back Monday after markets sold off sharply, with the deadline extended by five days and subsequently by a further ten, to April 6, following what the administration described as diplomatic progress. Iran publicly rejected those characterizations and continued striking Gulf states and Israel throughout the week. Former MI6 chief Alex Young assessed that the US "lost the initiative to Iran about two weeks ago." The structural difficulty is that even a ceasefire would not immediately resolve the Strait question: a CBS report indicated approximately a dozen Iranian mines are now present in the waterway, raising questions about the timeline and logistics of any reopening. Iran's reported draft legislation to impose passage fees on commercial vessels transiting the strait — and its reported practice of permitting passage for vessels paying in yuan — represents a form of de facto toll extraction and monetary leverage that persists regardless of near-term political outcomes. Prediction markets place the probability of a ceasefire by end of April at no better than fifty-fifty.

**Energy Markets and the Commodity Transmission**
The supply disruption is, by the IEA's assessment, comparable in magnitude to the combined effect of the 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis. Dallas Fed modeling suggests a Strait shutdown lasting through June would reduce global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points next quarter. Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — the world's largest — has sustained damage estimated to require years to fully repair, affecting both near-term supply and long-run investment calculus. Iran stopped natural gas exports to Turkey following an Israeli strike on the South Pars gas field. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have reportedly taken steps toward joining the conflict on the US-Israeli side, including Riyadh granting US military access to King Fahd Air Base. Saudi tanker freight rates from Yanbu — the Red Sea alternative routing — have eased somewhat as capacity arrives but remain well above pre-conflict levels. The Brent-WTI spread has widened to levels exceeding those seen during the Ukraine invasion, reflecting the US's relative insulation as a major domestic producer. Urea, the world's most widely used nitrogen fertilizer, has reached its highest level in more than three years, with the risk of a feedback loop from energy into food prices real and precedented in the 2008 rice crisis. A suspected sea-drone attack on a crude tanker in the Black Sea added a further dimension to energy market risk. Iran appears to be a net financial beneficiary of the disruption — as the only major exporter currently able to utilize the strait freely, Tehran has reportedly earned hundreds of millions in incremental oil revenue since hostilities began.

**Gold, the Dollar, and Financial Markets**
Gold's behavior continues to challenge conventional safe-haven narratives. The metal fell 10.5% in its worst weekly decline since 1983 before recovering modestly on ceasefire headlines — the selloff appearing to reflect a confluence of overbought positioning entering the conflict, dollar strength as an alternative haven, and the shift in rate expectations undermining the debasement trade thesis. Turkey sold approximately $8 billion — around sixty tons — of gold reserves this month to defend its currency, a pace larger than concurrent ETF outflows; should other energy-importing central banks follow, the narrative of central banks as consistent net buyers that has underpinned gold's more-than-doubling since 2022 would face meaningful revision. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on track for its largest monthly gain since July, an approximately 2% advance that complicates the global inflation picture by raising local-currency oil and food costs for importing nations. JPMorgan strategists turned bullish on the dollar for the first time in a year, with speculative positioning flipping from the most net-short in five years to net-long. The correlation between gold and real yields that anchored its valuation framework through the post-GFC era appears to have broken down; whether this represents a structural regime change or a temporary dislocation remains genuinely uncertain.

**Geopolitical Realignment and the Energy Transition**
The conflict is functioning as a stress test for the post-Cold War architecture simultaneously across multiple dimensions. Nations that had pivoted away from China and Russia toward deeper US alignment are reassessing that strategy under the combined pressures of trade tariffs, energy disruption, and reduced security commitments — a dynamic visible from Manila to Ankara. European Commission Executive Vice-President Ribera argued explicitly this week that fossil fuel market disruption would prove "a very relevant driver of the green economy," and early data suggest the conflict is accelerating EV adoption, rooftop solar uptake, and electrification of cooking across several markets. Pakistan stands to save an estimated $6.3 billion this year by substituting solar for oil and gas at elevated prices — a structural response the war may be advancing beyond what the energy transition alone would have achieved. Satellite data show elevated flaring at impacted Gulf facilities, adding an underappreciated emissions dimension to the conflict's footprint. The EU-Australia free trade agreement, concluded this week in Sydney with European Commission President von der Leyen taking an implicit swipe at US tariff policy and Chinese critical minerals restrictions, reflects Europe's broader effort to diversify strategic partnerships amid simultaneous pressure from multiple directions. The honest assessment is that the range of outcomes remains wide, and current market pricing may not fully reflect the left tail of the distribution.
