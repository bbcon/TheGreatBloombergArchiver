US

  • US public transit ridership rising sharply as gasoline prices average approximately $5.70/gallon nationally; no major macro data releases identified for this session.
  • LA Metro weekday ridership up ~8% January–May; Boston rail +13%, Chicago +11%, BART +11%, Caltrain +21% year-over-year in April.
  • Elevated energy costs continue to reshape consumer behavior, with the New York Fed documenting reduced gasoline consumption among low- and moderate-income households.

Energy-Driven Demand Shift

The sustained elevation in gasoline prices — a direct transmission channel from the US-Iran conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruption — is producing measurable behavioral responses in US consumer transportation patterns. California prices exceeding $6/gallon for regular fuel are particularly acute, with San Diego light rail ridership jumping nearly 18% in February–April. The New York Fed's May report corroborates the directional shift, finding lower-income households substituting away from gasoline, potentially through carpooling or transit. Academic research suggests each 10% sustained increase in gas prices generates up to a 1.2% rise in transit demand, implying the current shock — well above that threshold — carries meaningful ridership implications.

Structural Versus Cyclical Caution

Whether this represents durable modal shift or a temporary price-induced response remains genuinely uncertain. Post-2008 and post-2022 oil shocks saw drivers revert to prior habits once prices normalized. This cycle differs at the margin — return-to-office mandates and proliferating alternatives (EVs, delivery, remote work) complicate attribution. With the Iran conflict at a stalemate, elevated pump prices appear likely to persist through 2026, extending the window for behavioral entrenchment, though analysts stop short of declaring a structural inflection. ---

LatAm

  • Venezuela's oil production has surged 45% in three months to approximately 1.2 million barrels per day following the January removal of Nicolás Maduro.
  • Exxon Mobil and ConocoPhillips are in active talks to re-enter Venezuela; Chevron has committed to raising output 50% by end-2027.
  • Rystad Energy estimates Venezuela could add over 900,000 barrels per day by 2035 if current price levels are sustained, requiring $100–180 billion in capital investment over a decade.

Production Recovery and Investment Appetite

Venezuela's oil trajectory has shifted materially since January. Acting President Delcy Rodríguez moved rapidly to reform oil and gas laws, signal royalty flexibility, and engage the Trump administration — which responded with visits from cabinet-level officials including energy, interior, and intelligence leadership. The combination of a receptive political environment in Caracas and crude prices well above $90/barrel — itself a product of the Hormuz disruption — has transformed the investment calculus for international majors who had previously written off the country. Operating costs on existing fields of approximately $8/barrel imply substantial margins at current prices.

Capital Requirements and Historical Risk

The scale of required investment is formidable. Returning to Venezuela's 3.7 million barrel-per-day peak would require an estimated $100–180 billion over a decade. More immediately, the country's history of nationalization — Exxon has had assets seized twice — remains the dominant constraint on capital commitment. Exxon's CEO publicly called Venezuela "uninvestable" as recently as January. That posture appears to be softening, though the durability of the current political and legal framework is untested. The premium on supply diversification created by the Hormuz closure may be the decisive variable accelerating re-engagement. ---

Global

  • The Strait of Hormuz disruption — cutting approximately 10–15% of global crude supply since late February — has been characterized by the IEA as the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market; crude remains above $90/barrel.
  • Elevated energy prices are accelerating structural responses across multiple sectors: renewable energy adoption, EV uptake, US public transit ridership, and a fundamental reassessment of supply chain geography for hydrocarbons.
  • US-Iran conflict remains at a stalemate with peace talks ongoing but unresolved; timeline for Hormuz normalization is uncertain.

Supply Disruption and Diversification Premium

The effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz from late February has functioned as a stress test for global energy architecture. With roughly 20% of world oil and gas transiting the strait, the supply shock has been both immediate — in price — and structural, in forcing consumers and producers to reassess concentration risk. The most visible beneficiary has been Venezuela, where the diversification premium is translating directly into major re-engagement by international oil companies. Rystad Energy's scenario analysis suggests Venezuelan output could exceed 2 million barrels per day by 2035 under sustained high prices, a non-trivial offset to Persian Gulf exposure.

Downstream Behavioral and Infrastructure Effects

Beyond commodity markets, the energy shock is propagating through consumer behavior, infrastructure utilization, and power markets. Elevated gasoline prices in the US are driving measurable transit ridership gains. Separately, the AI data center buildout is creating a distinct but concurrent pressure on electricity grids — with Nvidia-driven compute demand potentially outpacing available power generation capacity within months, according to industry commentary. These two demand vectors — transportation energy substitution and AI power consumption — represent divergent but simultaneous structural forces reshaping energy markets independent of the near-term Hormuz resolution.
