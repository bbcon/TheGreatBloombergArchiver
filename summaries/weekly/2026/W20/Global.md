**The Iran Conflict and Hormuz**
The Strait of Hormuz closure — now in its eleventh week — remains the dominant cross-cutting variable in global markets. Monday opened with Trump rejecting Iran's latest peace proposal, sending Brent crude up as much as 4.6% toward $106 before settling above $103. By Tuesday, with WTI near $98.93 and Trump characterizing the ceasefire as on "massive life support," the diplomatic outlook deteriorated further. Satellite imagery confirmed that Iranian crude exports from the Kharg Island terminal had halted for several days — the first sign of a prolonged stoppage since the conflict began. BloombergNEF estimates disruption across 27 Middle Eastern oil fields has reached 9 million barrels per day. A Japanese supertanker appeared to make a covert Hormuz transit, two India-bound LNG cargoes crossed under elevated-risk conditions, and Qatar directed vessels near Ras Laffan to disable their AIS transponders — at least nine tankers went dark from Monday. The asymmetric warfare dimension remains central: Iran's deployment of midget submarines has sustained the stalemate despite significant US naval superiority. Friday's Brent price of approximately $109 per barrel represents a rise of more than 60% since the conflict began in late February. The UAE announced plans to double its Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027, a structural adaptation to what some planners are now treating as a prolonged-disruption scenario.

**Oil Market Buffers and Their Limits**
Morgan Stanley and Mercuria both characterized the short-term buffers preventing a more severe price spike as unsustainable. US crude and product exports are running above 14 million barrels per day with emergency stockpile drawdowns at record rates; simultaneously, Chinese state refiners have reduced crude purchases by drawing on commercial inventories. Neither dynamic can persist indefinitely. The IEA's assessment that global inventories are falling at a record pace and will continue declining for months implies continued upward price pressure absent resolution. The US EIA now projects global oil demand growth of only 200,000 barrels per day in 2026, against a pre-war forecast of 1.2 million; the IEA projects outright demand contraction of 420,000 barrels per day. Hapag-Lloyd reported approximately $250 million in additional monthly operating costs since February 28, though its CEO noted that Asian export volumes outside the Persian Gulf have been "surprisingly" resilient. The jet fuel market is under particular strain, with US jet fuel exports surging to a record 455,000 barrels per day as airlines in Europe and Asia scrambled for supply. OPEC+ is targeting completion of quota restoration by end-September, though the relief that provides depends substantially on Hormuz conditions.

**Trade Flows and Energy Infrastructure Adaptation**
The Hormuz disruption is reshaping physical trade routes in ways that may prove semi-permanent. Iran has boosted rail freight to China via Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan, with train frequency rising from roughly one per week to one every three to four days and container rates along the route climbing 40% above typical levels. China's clean technology manufacturers are explicitly leveraging the energy shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third, with Chinese firms actively targeting Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia. The commercial logic is clear — sustained high fossil fuel prices improve the economics of electrification across every geography simultaneously. At the same time, Beijing's new supply chain coercion regulations — allowing China to retaliate against foreign governments or firms that assist in actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial security — are calibrated to deter the sourcing diversification that Washington and Brussels have been encouraging. Rhodium Group warns of a self-reinforcing dynamic in which China's rising manufacturing share erodes competitor margins, reducing Western firms' capacity to reinvest in R&D, while private capital increasingly avoids sectors with entrenched state-backed Chinese competition.

**Global Bond Markets and Inflation Propagation**
The synchronized selloff in sovereign bond markets across the US, UK, eurozone, and Japan this week reflects a common inflation driver propagating through different institutional contexts. US 10-year yields closed Friday at 4.54%, UK 10-year gilts at 5.14%, and Japan's 20-year rate at its highest level since 1996. Producer price indices in the US, China, and Japan all surprised to the upside this week, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. Investors captured 5% yields on 30-year US Treasuries for the first time in nearly two decades — a notable level for long-duration pricing globally. The broader consequences of a world in which all major central banks face simultaneous inflationary pressure — limiting their ability to buffer growth slowdowns with easing — remain to be fully absorbed in equity valuations, credit spreads, and emerging market capital flows. Asian central banks are already responding: the Philippines, India, and Indonesia are burning FX reserves to defend weakening currencies, while the Bank of Japan is signaling an accelerated tightening path. The coherence of losses across global bond indices this year represents a meaningful change from the post-GFC environment of divergent monetary cycles, and its full implications for asset allocation have not yet resolved.
