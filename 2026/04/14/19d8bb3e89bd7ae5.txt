Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
China-based reporter Dan Murtaugh describes how the country’s teapot refiners 
are withstanding the current energy crisis.

Almost seven weeks since the outbreak of the Iran war threw the global energy 
market into turmoil, China’s independent refiners are battered but not broken.

The so-called teapots — which account for about a fifth of China’s refining 
capacity — have in recent years become reliant on discounted crude from Iran, 
Russia and Venezuela. That’s allowed them to survive on wafer-thin margins 
competing against their bigger, deeper-pocketed state-owned rivals.

But that strategy has unraveled.

First, US intervention in Venezuela narrowed the teapots’ supply options. 
Then, Washington issued sanction waivers for Iranian and Russian oil, erasing 
discounts that were once as high as $10 a barrel.

Now, the US blockade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/hormuz-tracker-ship-transit-slowed-sharply-ahead-of-us-blockade?cmpid=BBBXT041426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 of the Strait of Hormuz threatens to completely halt Iranian crude exports, 
nearly all of which used to go to Chinese private refiners.


Oil storage tanks near Ningbo, China.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
To make matters worse, Beijing is leaning on the sector to maintain fuel 
supply. Authorities have granted extra import quotas and ordered them to keep 
output steady, even if it means operating at a loss.

For now, floating stockpiles offer a temporary buffer. About 38 million 
barrels of Iranian oil aresitting in tankers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/iran-oil-hoard-at-sea-shields-china-s-refiners-from-us-blockade?cmpid=BBBXT041426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 in Asian waters, much of it within easy reach of Shandong province, where most 
of the teapots are. That will give them a couple of months’ breathing space.

China has a multifaceted approach to limit its exposure to the Middle East 
crisis. For years it’s been shifting away from oil and gas toward electricity, 
which can be generated from domestically sourced coal and renewables. It also 
hasmassive reserves 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/china-s-energy-security-push-pays-off-as-war-roils-asia-refiners?cmpid=BBBXT041426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 of crude, some of which it’s now allowing refiners to draw down.

That’s enabled China’s leaders to play the long game diplomatically, calling 
for peace and stability in the Middle East without having to push for 
short-term solutions to blunt economic pain.

But if the US blockade of Iranian ports drags on and alternative supplies 
remain expensive, that cushion will deflate.

For China, teapot refiners are the canary in the coal mine for this crisis. It 
bears watching whether they can keep flapping their wings.

—Dan Murtaugh, Bloomberg News
