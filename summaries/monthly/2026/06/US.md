• May CPI topped 4.0% YoY in W24 — a three-year high — while May PPI rose 6.5% YoY in W25, the fastest in over three years, both above consensus; core PCE is projected by Goldman Sachs at 3.2% YoY by December.
• May nonfarm payrolls printed 170,000 in W24, beating expectations for the third consecutive month; Goldman Sachs dropped its 2026 rate-cut forecast entirely, with consensus now placing the Fed on hold well into mid-2027.

**Labor Market and Real Economy**
The May payroll print of 170,000 in W24 — the third consecutive month of solid job creation — effectively removed residual recession risk and negated the Sahm Rule signal that had briefly emerged. The labor market is resilient but internally inconsistent: average hourly earnings are growing at 3.4% year-over-year, below prevailing inflation, while real personal income has declined 0.6% over the past year — a pace Goldman Sachs associates with recession conditions. NFIB small-business optimism slipped below expectations in W25, erasing all post-election gains, with capital expenditure falling to its lowest since 2009. The divergence between large-cap resilience and small-business stress is widening. Factory output was little changed in May after April's upwardly revised 0.7% gain, the first meaningful deceleration after four consecutive months of expansion, suggesting war-related supply disruptions are beginning to weigh on manufacturing activity.

**Monetary Policy**
The period's defining macro story is the structural repricing of US monetary policy. Two-year Treasury yields surged 13 basis points on FOMC day — the largest single-day move in over a year — as fed funds futures shifted from pricing cuts to pricing a meaningful probability of hikes. Warsh's debut FOMC statement was cut from 341 to 130 words, stripped of all forward guidance and the maximum employment mandate; the sole concluding line read "The Committee will deliver price stability." His announcement of five internal task forces — covering communications, the balance sheet, alternative data, AI and productivity, and the inflation framework — signals a structural regime change whose outcomes will not materialise until year-end. May PPI of 6.5% YoY confirms that cost pressures remain acute despite Hormuz de-escalation, with lagged pass-through in natural gas, jet fuel surcharges, and fertilizers still working through the system.

**Equity and Capital Markets**
The AI capital cycle reached exceptional scale during the period. The S&P 500 technology sector outperformed the ex-tech index by 33% over the prior three months — the strongest relative performance on record — though the trade began fracturing visibly in W26. Memory and infrastructure names surged following Micron's results while the Nasdaq 100 fell more than 1% Friday after Apple raised device prices citing chip shortages. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index recorded its largest single-day decline since early June during Tuesday's global tech selloff before recovering sharply on Micron's guidance. Goldman partner Bobby Molavi flagged co-correlation risk explicitly, noting that active, passive, hedge fund, and quant strategies are "effectively getting longer AI by the day" — a dynamic that "feels great on the way up, but can be extremely dangerous on the unwind." The Shiller CAPE has exceeded its September 2000 peak; BofA's Savita Subramanian set a year-end S&P 500 target of 7,100, implying roughly 6% downside, citing 17 of 20 valuation metrics as statistically expensive. Memory names are up over 200% year-to-date while the Magnificent Seven cohort is negative for 2026 — a structural divergence within the AI trade that warrants close monitoring.
