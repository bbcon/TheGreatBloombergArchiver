Faced with new uncertainties and surcharges related to the Mideast conflict, 
the global trade community is asking container carriers to keep pushing forward 
on a pandemic-era effort toward digitalization and away from paper documents.

“The next 12 months represent a decisive window,” said a letter to a group of 
CEOs whose companies move about 75% of global container trade. “Without 
accelerated adoption, inefficiencies will persist, eroding customer confidence 
and increasing costs.”

The April 8 letter asks carriers to implement clear standards for things like 
booking cargo and arrival notices, and was signed by dozens of trade groups, 
seaports and private companies including Tesla, Anheuser-Busch InBev and IKEA 
Supply, the Port of Los Angeles and the National Retail Federation.

The outreach builds on a 2024 letter that asked carriers to implement a common 
way to communicate vessel schedule information to customers, and another letter 
sent last yearasked for transparency <https://dcsa.org/standard-conformance> 
around which companies were using which standards.

Since then, carriers have largely implemented commercial schedule standards 
and published a conformance tracker, and there’s been progress with other 
initiatives, too.

“A couple of the carriers went to online platforms,” said Gabriel Rodriguez 
<https://www.linkedin.com/in/gabrielrodriguez1/>, president of A Customs 
Brokerage in Florida, who led the April 8 letter. “You log in, see your 
container, pay the bill, and the container’s released.”

What used to take eight hours can now take 30 seconds, he said.

Electronic Bills of Lading

Keen to move away from paper documents and the inefficiencies that come with 
incompatible IT, nine out of 10 of the largest ocean carriers formed the 
Digital Container Shipping Association in 2019. The need for the upgrade came 
into focus just a year later, when the pandemic seized global supply chains, 
causing widespread port congestion, with stacks of containers and chassis 
stranded in the wrong places.

The Covid logistics turmoil brought momentum to digitalization effort, and in 
2023 the DCSA members pledged to issue all bills of lading — which acts as 
receipt, contract of carriage and title to the goods being shipped — 
electronically by 2030.

Since then, DCSA has published a glossary 
<https://dcsa.org/standards/shipping-glossary> of hundreds of shipping terms, 
as well as a popular Track & Trace standard that allows stakeholders to see 
shipping data in real time across different platforms.

More to Do

Digital adoption is inconsistent across carriers, so their customers are 
calling on CEOs to lead the digitalization effort through the whole supply 
chain.

Exporters, for example, face a confusing matrix of dates and document 
deadlines they need to meet in order to get their container loaded onto a 
vessel.

“Standardizing terminal-managed milestones and digital processes will minimize 
disruptions, reduce storage costs, and enable better planning for all 
stakeholders,” said the April 8 letter.

For imports, Rodriguez said conflicting or inaccurate arrival information 
<https://dcsa.org/standards/arrival-notice> about shipments often results in 
delays and extra fees. All of it means chasing information with a flurry of 
emails and phone calls.

“In today’s age, we’re still having to do manual stuff,” Rodriguez said. “I 
feel like I’m working with Fred Flintstone.”
