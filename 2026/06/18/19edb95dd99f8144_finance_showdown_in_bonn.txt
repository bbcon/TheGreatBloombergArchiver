By Laura Millan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASzdyi2b6bE/laura-millan?cmpid=BBD061826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5669>

United Nations-sponsored talks in the German city of Bonn headed into overtime 
on Thursday as representatives struggled with a perennial problem: how rich 
countries should pay to help poorer nations cut emissions, adapt to climate 
change and cope with more extreme weather.

тАЬWeтАЩve heard a familiar tendency towards you-first-ism: groups refusing to

The lack of progress on climate finance at Bonn risks delaying the flow of 
funds to developing countries at a time when other sources of climate 
investment and development aid are shrinking. Many rich nations have shifted 
their focus to defense spending and blunting the economic fallout of the Iran 
war at home.

The UN discussions will pick up at the COP31 climate summit in Antalya, 
Turkey, in November. Finance issues are expected to be the main sticking points 
there, said Rachel Cleetus, a senior policy director at the nonprofit Union of 
Concerned Scientists.

тАЬThis is going be the issue that takes us to the end,тАЭ she said. тАЬCountries 
are completely divided.тАЭ

Conversations were further soured by attacks on climate research produced by 
the UN-backed Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change.

Negotiators from fossil fuel-producing nations pushed back on references to

keep global warming to 1.5C from pre-industrial levels, the more ambitious
