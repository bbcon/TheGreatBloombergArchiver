Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, oil 
reporter Grant Smith takes stock of the interim peace agreement between the US 
and Iran that emerged over the weekend.

Oil traders might be tempted to feel the latest diplomatic breakthrough to end 
the war in Iran echoes others they’ve seen before.

Washington and Tehran said Sunday they reached an interim agreement 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-14/pakistan-says-us-and-iran-have-reached-a-peace-deal?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
 to reopen the vital Strait of Hormuz, and US President Donald Trump announced 
that flows will resume when the two sides sign their accord in Switzerland on 
June 19.

Crude markets are giving the pact the benefit of the doubt, with Brent futures 
slumping more than 8% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-14/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-june-15?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
 over the past two sessions to trade near $83 a barrel. If the deal holds, the 
world economy may have dodged the calamity feared when war broke out more than 
three months ago.

Yet there have been false dawns before. Video montages circulate online of the 
president making similar assertions dozens of times since the conflict began.

In mid-April, a flurry of stranded tankers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-18/greek-indian-tankers-u-turn-before-hormuz-amid-reopening-doubt?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
 exited the strait after Washington and Tehran said it had reopened, only for 
the flow tostop suddenly 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/gunfire-u-turns-and-threats-mark-a-chaotic-weekend-in-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
 when the apparent agreement broke down.
US President Donald Trump in the White House on May 27, days after saying a 
peace deal with Iran was “largely negotiated.”Photographer: Win McNamee/Getty 
Images North America
Shipowners such as Japanese giants Mitsui OSK Lines Ltd. and Nippon Yusen KK 
are reacting to this weekend’s news with caution, insisting theyneed more 
clarity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/shipowners-seek-clarity-on-hormuz-deal-as-600-vessels-eye-exit?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
 before sending vessels through the waterway.

Critical details have yet to be spelled out. Trump insists that tankers will 
be able to cross unencumbered by the financial tolls Tehran has levied. Iran 
has been more ambiguous, signaling free transit only for a 60-day period.

There are also serious questions about logistics, with shipowners’ group Bimco 
warning that the threat of mines remains a concern. Safety could depend on the 
progress of a multinational mine-clearing mission led bythe UK and France 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-04/uk-and-france-finalize-postwar-hormuz-mine-clearing-mission?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
.

It’s not even clear whether there’ll be any system to ensure that trapped 
ships can depart in an orderly flow, or a free-for-all.

Yet with all the wariness, there are grounds for optimism, too. Even before 
the interim agreement emerged, oil producers had beensneaking more barrels 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-11/hormuz-oil-sneakouts-rise-50-as-iran-and-us-vie-for-control?cmpid=BBBXT061526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5504>
 out of the strait, bolstering flows by 50% this month. As a final deal gets 
closer, their confidence to ship more should increase.

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Steel producers are aiming for a fivefold increase in green steel output by 
2030, based on projects tracked by BloombergNEF. Electric arc furnaces, which 
were largely used in the past to recycle steel, are emerging as the key 
technology for green steel production in the near term as alternative methods 
that rely on hydrogen and carbon capture remain more expensive.
