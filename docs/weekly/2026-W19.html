Weekly Brief

May 04 – May 10, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 sustained record highs throughout the week, with the index approaching 7,400 by Wednesday, even as WTI crude gyrated between $100 and $112 per barrel.
  • AI capital expenditure — information-processing equipment up 64% in real terms in Q1 — remained the dominant force in US business investment, with S&P 500 buyback authorizations at a record $665 billion for the first four months of the year.
  • The K-shaped energy shock deepened: gas prices exceeded $4.50 per gallon by Thursday, with New York Fed research showing households earning under $40,000 cut real gasoline consumption 7% even as nominal spending rose 12%.
  • A federal trade court declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful on Thursday, compounding earlier Supreme Court setbacks, while the administration gave the EU until July 4 to ratify a trade deal or face higher duties.
  • The April nonfarm payrolls report, expected to show approximately 62,000–65,000 new jobs, was due Friday — the labor market not yet registering the energy shock's full pass-through.

Equity Markets

US equity markets sustained record highs across the week in a manner that continued to unsettle strategists even as it rewarded momentum investors. The S&P 500 approached 7,400 by Wednesday, and Goldman Sachs strategists flagged "yellow flags" in the advance's composition: factor analysis showed quality stocks — characterized by stable earnings and strong balance sheets — were consistent underperformers since Liberation Day, while momentum and liquidity factors dominated. The rally appeared driven more by abundant liquidity and AI-related animal spirits than fundamental re-rating, leaving the index potentially vulnerable to any deterioration in the macro or geopolitical backdrop. Roughly 80% of S&P 500 companies reporting thus far had beaten estimates, providing genuine earnings support, but the index's resilience in the face of renewed Iran-related tensions and oil price volatility was notable. Corporate buybacks provided a structural floor, with full-year authorized repurchases projected at $1.55 trillion — potentially eclipsing last year's record.

AI and Corporate Earnings

The AI investment cycle remained the week's dominant corporate theme. AMD surged approximately 17% in premarket trading on Wednesday after raising its sales forecast, and Super Micro Computer gained 19% on a strong report. Alphabet raised nearly $17 billion in debt on Wednesday to fund data-center buildout. Nvidia, by contrast, fell 9% over six sessions and was the Philadelphia semiconductor index's worst performer year-to-date, as customers including Alphabet, Amazon, and Intel developed credible competing chip architectures — though evidence of material market share loss remained limited. Amazon's launch of Amazon Supply Chain Services on Tuesday, packaging its freight, trucking, air, ocean, and last-mile capabilities into a single commercial offering, sent FedEx, UPS, and Expeditors shares lower, though analysts characterized the near-term competitive threat as incremental. Fortinet surged 15% in premarket trading on Thursday after raising its revenue outlook, while Snap fell sharply on advertising revenue weakness attributed directly to the Middle East conflict.

Fiscal and Consumer Stress

The fiscal backdrop grew more complex. Jeffrey Gundlach disclosed on Thursday he was repositioning portfolios for an extreme sovereign-debt scenario — placing the probability of a US debt restructuring below 30% but rising — joining Apollo's Marc Rowan and Bridgewater's Ray Dalio in issuing unusually direct warnings about fiscal trajectories. The CBO projects federal debt reaching 108% of GDP by 2030. Former Treasury Secretary Mnuchin stated plainly there is no "break-the-glass" solution to the US debt problem. The Trump administration began processing refunds for $166 billion in tariffs declared unlawful by the Supreme Court — a development with meaningful fiscal implications that received limited market attention. At the household level, Spirit Airlines ceased operations during the week, illustrating the energy shock's asymmetric impact on businesses serving lower-income consumers. Kraft Heinz's CEO warned that lower-income shoppers are "literally running out of money at the end of the month." Bank of America estimates the full-year energy "gas tax" amounts to roughly 0.5% of GDP, approximately equal to the projected boost from either the One Big Beautiful Bill or AI capital expenditure — a structural offset that complicates the administration's growth narrative.

Monetary Policy and the Fed

The Federal Reserve's path toward rate cuts appeared increasingly constrained throughout the week. Two-year inflation swaps crossed 3% on Tuesday — their highest since 2022 — driven by Middle East-linked energy inflation. Fed Governor Collins aligned herself with last week's policy dissent on easing language, suggesting internal divisions at the FOMC had not fully resolved. The 30-year Treasury yield briefly breached 5%, though analysts at Evercore ISI argued a durable Hormuz deal bringing oil lower would render rate cuts delayed rather than derailed. The self-reinforcing dynamic between the administration's military engagement in the Gulf and its stated desire for lower interest rates represents a tension that deserves close attention. New research published during the week suggested the politically motivated dismissal of Bureau of Labor Statistics chief Erika McEntarfer correlated with approximately $20 billion in foregone industrial investment — a figure 25 times the BLS annual budget — as executives grew less willing to commit capital amid eroding confidence in government data. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi gained 14% for the week, with Samsung Electronics crossing a $1 trillion market capitalization on Wednesday and SK Hynix rising 30%, as AI-driven semiconductor demand re-rated the country's equity market to what Goldman Sachs described as the strongest forecast earnings growth for any Asian market since 1999's post-crisis recovery.
  • Japan's Ministry of Finance deployed an estimated $30 billion in foreign exchange intervention during the week to defend the yen near ¥160, producing the currency's largest single-day gain since 2022 — though analysts remained skeptical of durability absent a meaningful shift in Bank of Japan policy.
  • China directed its largest banks to halt new lending to five US-sanctioned refiners while simultaneously ordering private companies to disregard US secondary sanctions, a calculated attempt to signal defiance while protecting dollar-system access ahead of the Trump-Xi Beijing summit scheduled for May 14–15.
  • Indonesia's economy expanded 5.61% year-on-year in Q1 — a three-year high — but its currency set a record low as oil-dependent emerging markets bore the heaviest burden from the Hormuz disruption.
  • The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a third consecutive rate hike in an 8-1 vote, unwinding all of last year's easing, leading developed-market peers in the hawkish response to the energy shock.

Semiconductors and the AI Super-Cycle

Asian equity markets opened the week at fresh record highs, with South Korea's Kospi and Taiwan's benchmark surging 5.1% and 4.6% respectively on Monday as AI-linked semiconductor names led the advance. The dynamic accelerated materially on Wednesday when Samsung Electronics crossed a $1 trillion market capitalization — only the second Asian company to achieve that threshold — with the stock rallying 14–16% as markets repriced memory's structural role in AI infrastructure. SK Hynix rose 30% over the week. Goldman Sachs raised its Kospi target for the fourth time this year, projecting an additional 20% gain and citing forecast earnings growth of 300% for 2026. Morgan Stanley's chief Asia economist characterized the broader dynamic as the beginning of a multi-year industrial super-cycle, arguing that AI infrastructure, energy transition, and defense spending will broaden from semiconductors into wider capital expenditure and ultimately consumption across the region, with Taiwan, South Korea, China, and Japan identified as primary beneficiaries.

China: Sanctions, Energy, and Summit Positioning

China's behavior on US sanctions represented one of the week's most consequential geopolitical-financial developments. Beijing's weekend directive ordering private companies to disregard US secondary sanctions targeting Iranian crude purchases was an explicit act of defiance — a meaningful departure from its historically cautious approach of quiet compliance. By Thursday, however, regulators had separately directed the country's largest banks to halt new lending to five sanctioned refiners while leaving existing loans intact. The resulting posture is deliberately calibrated: project defiance in the policy domain, protect dollar-system access at the institutional level. Wang Yi hosted Iran's foreign minister on Wednesday and urged Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, reflecting Beijing's dependence on Middle Eastern oil — approximately two-fifths of its imports — and its desire to position Xi Jinping as a peacemaker ahead of the May 14–15 Trump summit. JPMorgan and Citigroup being sued in Chinese courts over frozen payments tied to a sanctioned firm illustrates the narrowing legal corridor available to global institutions navigating competing jurisdictions.

Japan and Southeast Asia

Japan's monetary and diplomatic postures both evolved significantly during the week. The Ministry of Finance deployed an estimated $30 billion in foreign exchange intervention, pulling the yen from above ¥160 toward ¥155–157 per dollar, but analysts broadly characterized the effort as increasingly ineffective level-targeting absent a Bank of Japan commitment to near-term rate hikes. Vanguard remained bearish on the yen, arguing that elevated oil prices are structurally eroding Japan's terms of trade. On the geopolitical front, Prime Minister Takaichi visited Vietnam and Australia, and Defense Minister Koizumi deployed to the Philippines on Tuesday to observe Japanese troops in joint Balikatan exercises — including a ship-sinking drill in the South China Sea — marking the first such deployment since World War II. Toyota on Thursday issued a notably cautious earnings outlook, projecting ¥3 trillion in operating income for fiscal 2027 against a consensus of ¥4.6 trillion, citing elevated raw material costs from the Iran conflict, and fell as much as 3.5%. In Southeast Asia, the Philippines faced its most severe economic stress since the pandemic, with growth slowing and consumer spending at its weakest in over a decade. Indonesia tightened foreign currency purchase limits to $25,000 from $50,000 to support the rupiah. ---

Europe

  • UK 30-year gilt yields reached 5.78% on Wednesday — the highest since 1998 — reflecting a combination of the UK's twin fiscal and current account deficits, persistent inflation, and political uncertainty.
  • Reform UK secured a net gain of 275 seats in Thursday's English local elections, while Labour lost approximately 204 — Labour losing roughly half the seats it was defending — raising questions about Prime Minister Starmer's authority without triggering an immediate leadership challenge.
  • UniCredit reported a record quarterly profit of €3.22 billion on Tuesday, up 16% year-on-year and well ahead of consensus, while HSBC fell 5.9% in London after missing estimates due to an unexpected UK fraud charge and Middle East provisions.
  • Chancellor Merz's government faces questions about coalition longevity one year into office, and Trump's announcement of additional US troop withdrawals from Germany in response to Merz's public criticism of US Iran policy underscored how transatlantic tensions constrain European policy space.
  • The EU reiterated its demand that Washington adopt the main elements of their trade deal by July and respect a 15% tariff ceiling, as US Ambassador Puzder warned 25% tariffs on European vehicles could arrive "relatively soon."

Political Fragmentation and UK Gilt Stress

The week's defining political development in Europe was the scale of Reform UK's gains in Thursday's English local elections. With counts approaching completion, Reform had secured a net gain of 275 seats while Labour's tally fell by 204 — Labour losing roughly half the seats it was defending — and Nigel Farage's party simultaneously displaced Conservative support in traditional strongholds and Green support among dissatisfied progressives. Prime Minister Starmer ruled out stepping aside despite the results, but the outcome suggests meaningful fragmentation of Britain's traditional two-party structure. Market attention focused specifically on whether pressure on Starmer could push potential successor candidates toward looser fiscal commitments — a scenario gilt markets would view unfavorably. UK 30-year gilt yields reached 5.78% on Wednesday, the highest since 1998, with the spread over US Treasuries having widened beyond levels seen at the peak of the Liz Truss episode in 2022. Bank of England officials joined private forecasters in questioning the reliability of recent UK economic data, adding further complexity to the rate-setting path. Overnight index swaps implied significantly more BoE tightening than was priced before the Iran conflict.

Transatlantic Friction and Continental Politics

Europe's relationship with Washington deteriorated on multiple fronts. Trump's announcement of a 25% tariff on European automobiles and the disclosure of US troop withdrawals from Germany beyond the initially disclosed 5,000 — both appearing connected to Merz's public criticism of US Iran policy — illustrated the linkage between geopolitical alignment and economic leverage that European governments are struggling to navigate. The EU reiterated retaliatory options should auto tariffs proceed, while US Ambassador Puzder warned tariffs could arrive "relatively soon." France's Macron nominated Emmanuel Moulin to head the Banque de France and take a seat on the ECB's Governing Council on Wednesday — a personnel decision with implications for future ECB deliberations. Romanian politics added continental uncertainty, with a no-confidence vote against Prime Minister Bolojan backed by an unusual far-right and Social Democrat coalition, potentially deepening a fiscal crisis around the EU's widest budget deficit. Meanwhile, China's five-year plan, emphasizing quality upgrading and frontier technologies, generated what Germany's machinery association VDMA described as unprecedented corporate anxiety about European export competitiveness in sectors including industrial machinery and precision equipment.

Corporate Earnings and Energy Exposure

European corporate results this week illustrated the Hormuz disruption's uneven sectoral impact. Shell reported stronger-than-expected first-quarter earnings as elevated energy prices and increased trading volatility more than offset production declines. UniCredit's record quarterly result of €3.22 billion contrasted with HSBC's miss, the latter weighed by an unexpected UK fraud charge and Middle East conflict provisions — shares fell 5.9% in London on Tuesday. Novo Nordisk surged approximately 9% on Wednesday after its new Wegovy pill drove quarterly sales growth. Infineon's revenue outlook beat expectations on AI-driven power chip demand. BMW's earnings declined, reflecting intense pricing competition in China. The European Commission's stagflationary characterization of the continent's predicament — higher energy costs meeting sluggish growth — accurately captures the ECB's constrained position, where the inflation impulse from oil prices argues against the accommodation that weak growth would otherwise warrant. In Switzerland, lawmakers advanced a legislative process that could impose up to $20 billion in additional capital requirements on UBS, with significant implications for the institution's capital allocation and broader European banking sentiment. ---

Global

  • WTI crude fell nearly 10% on Wednesday after Trump suspended "Project Freedom" and Axios reported a one-page US-Iran memorandum of understanding framework, though the commodity remained above $99 per barrel and December 2026 futures stayed near $77 — suggesting markets do not yet price a near-term, sustained reopening of the Strait.
  • US forces struck Iranian missile and drone launch sites near the Strait of Hormuz on Thursday after Iran attacked three American warships, introducing fresh uncertainty about whether the diplomatic framework reported earlier in the week remained viable.
  • China's defiance of US secondary sanctions, Iran's collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin, and Gulf sovereign wealth funds reportedly reassessing US asset allocations collectively represent observable — if still modest — stress points in dollar-denominated financial architecture.
  • The New York Fed's global supply chain pressure index reached its highest reading since July 2022 in April, and global LNG exports fell to their lowest level in nearly two years during the same month.
  • The Trump-Xi Beijing summit, scheduled for May 14–15 with trade, Taiwan, and Iran all on the agenda, will be the first visit by a sitting US president to China since 2017.

The Hormuz Crisis: Escalation, Diplomacy, and Oil Markets

The Strait of Hormuz remained the central fault line in global markets for the week's entirety, with the diplomatic and military situation oscillating in ways that produced sharp intraday moves across oil, equities, and fixed income. The week opened with President Trump's "Project Freedom" initiative — a plan to guide neutral vessels through the waterway — generating confusion among shipping executives given an absence of operational detail and the US Navy's stated non-involvement. On Wednesday, Trump suspended the operation after Iran struck an oil terminal at the UAE's Fujairah port and US forces repelled multiple drone, missile, and fast-attack boat strikes — Axios subsequently reported the two sides were working toward a one-page memorandum of understanding involving a Hormuz reopening in exchange for a moratorium on uranium enrichment and lifting of some sanctions. WTI fell nearly 10% on Wednesday. By Thursday, however, US forces struck Iranian launch sites after Iran attacked three American warships, reintroducing genuine uncertainty about the framework's durability. Brent remained above $99 per barrel at week's end, and prediction markets assigned less than 50% odds to a return to normal Hormuz traffic by end of June, down from roughly 90% two weeks earlier. The cumulative supply loss since the conflict began is estimated to be approaching one billion barrels.

Energy Markets: Supply Response and Structural Adaptation

The US has emerged as the world's largest crude exporter, shipping over 250 million barrels in nine weeks, with domestic inventories drawing down rapidly in ways that raise questions about the sustainability of that role. Diamondback Energy's CEO signaled an "inflection point" for the Permian Basin on Wednesday, with expectations of up to 30 additional rigs; Exxon announced plans for a 12% production increase. OPEC+ agreed on a modest symbolic increase of 188,000 barrels per day for June, largely a signaling exercise following the UAE's surprise exit from the bloc. Abu Dhabi's ADNOC simultaneously announced $55 billion in accelerated project awards. US gasoline prices topped $4.50 per gallon for the first time since July 2022. In parallel, structural adaptation is accelerating: Chinese truck operators are reportedly advancing EV adoption timelines in response to surging diesel costs; green bond issuance in emerging markets rose 53% year-on-year to $24 billion in Q1 as energy-import-dependent economies seek to accelerate renewables deployment; and DP World introduced end-to-end war risk insurance for cargo traversing the region, an acknowledgment that perceived risk will persist even if a deal materializes.

Dollar Architecture and Geopolitical Realignment

The accumulation of de-dollarization stress points became harder to dismiss as episodic by week's end. Iran's collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin represents a de minimis but symbolically notable bypass of dollar payment infrastructure. China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US secondary sanctions directly challenges the enforcement architecture that gives dollar primacy its coercive utility. Gulf sovereign wealth funds are reportedly reassessing US asset allocation as American security guarantees prove less reliable. Central banks across emerging markets are increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves. Project mBridge — the BIS-coordinated cross-border settlement system developed with China, Hong Kong, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Thailand — remains nascent in transaction volume relative to SWIFT-based flows, but elevated geopolitical tensions create conditions under which adoption could accelerate. At the Milken Institute conference in Los Angeles, EQT's Per Franzén, overseeing roughly €270 billion in private-market assets, noted clients are actively reassessing dollar-denominated overweights. The more salient risk, as multiple conference participants noted, is not displacement by a rival currency but erosion through sustained US policy inconsistency — a self-inflicted vulnerability rather than an externally imposed one. Whether these strains prove transitory or structural remains genuinely uncertain, and the dollar retains substantial network advantages that competing systems have not yet overcome.

Climate, AI, and the Energy Transition

A widening tension between AI infrastructure expansion and corporate climate commitments crystallized more visibly this week. Microsoft was reported to be weighing abandonment of its 2030 hourly clean-energy matching target — a significant retreat from pre-ChatGPT commitments — as data center power demand and natural gas's appeal grow. Across major hyperscalers, reported carbon emissions have risen 23–64% versus pre-ChatGPT baselines. BloombergNEF projects US data center power consumption will exceed 106 gigawatts by 2035, more than double current levels. New York Governor Hochul moved on Thursday to soften the state's landmark climate law, pushing the emissions-enforcement deadline to 2028, citing energy affordability concerns — reflecting a broader pattern across US states where Iran war-induced fuel price increases are weakening political support for near-term climate mandates. The Hormuz disruption is simultaneously reinforcing the strategic rationale for domestic clean energy: energy-import-dependent economies from Australia to India to Southeast Asia are accelerating renewables investment, illustrating how supply security and decarbonization incentives have — at least temporarily — realigned.
