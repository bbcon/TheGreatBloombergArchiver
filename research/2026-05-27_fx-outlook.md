# Currency Developments: USD Reversal, Divergent EM Outcomes, and the Oil Offset Myth
**Prepared:** 2026-05-27
**Purpose:** Strategic context for airline industry partner briefing

---

## 1. The USD: From Structural Decline to Crisis Haven Reversal

The dollar's trajectory in 2025–26 has followed a two-phase pattern that is critical to understanding non-USD airlines' current cost position.

**Phase 1 — Structural USD weakness (2025 through early 2026):** The Bloomberg Dollar Index declined approximately 6% over the year prior to the Hormuz crisis. The drivers were well-understood: narrowing rate differentials as the Fed cut three times, twin fiscal and current account deficits, and a deliberate rotation away from concentrated US asset exposure — non-resident EM portfolio inflows approached $100 billion in January 2026 alone, the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades. The institutional consensus entering 2026 was "Sell America, Buy World": dollar depreciation was the modal call across more than 60 major asset allocators. This phase was a tailwind for non-USD airlines — their revenues in local currency converted more favorably against USD-denominated fuel costs.

**Phase 2 — Crisis-driven reversal (late February 2026 onward):** The Hormuz closure reversed this entirely within days. Sovereign bonds sold off alongside equities, leaving the dollar as the only functioning haven vehicle. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index rose approximately 2% in March alone — its largest monthly gain since July 2025 — reversing four months of depreciation. JPMorgan strategists turned dollar-bullish for the first time in a year; speculative positioning flipped from the most net-short in five years to net-long within weeks. The DXY broke above its 200-day moving average.

**The current configuration (May 2026):** The dollar remains elevated against most currencies on a combination of genuine haven demand, the Fed's higher-for-longer repricing, and the US's relative energy self-sufficiency advantage as the world's largest crude producer. De-dollarization stress points are accumulating — Iran collecting Hormuz tolls in Bitcoin, Gulf sovereign wealth funds reducing US asset allocations, central banks lifting gold reserves — but these remain marginal relative to the dollar's structural network advantages. The 2025 dollar-weakness trend has not resumed.

## 2. Currency Developments by Pair

### Japanese Yen (JPY/USD) — Structurally Weak, Intervention-Capped

The yen is the clearest underperformer among the currencies in focus. After strengthening briefly to ¥153 following the Takaichi election result in early 2026, it weakened progressively: to ~156 in February, ~159.5 in March, approaching 160 in April before direct BoJ/MoF intervention capped the move at approximately 155.5. The structural driver is straightforward: the BoJ held at 0.75% while every other major central bank pivoted hawkish — the rate differential compression that would support the yen is not arriving. Japanese super-long JGB yields hit post-1999 records in mid-May as PM Takaichi's fiscal expansion added term premium, creating the uncomfortable combination of a weak yen and rising long rates without the policy rate response that would conventionally accompany it. The BoJ is caught: hiking into a growth slowdown risks amplifying the damage; holding invites further yen weakness and imported inflation. Intervention provides temporary stabilization but not a trend reversal.

**Airline implication:** Japanese carriers (JAL, ANA) face the worst of all worlds — USD jet fuel near all-time highs AND a materially weaker yen to pay for it. Every 5-yen weakening in USD/JPY adds approximately ¥2–3 billion per year to a major Japanese carrier's fuel bill at current oil prices.

---

### Euro (EUR/USD) — Hawkish Pivot Constrained by Stagflation

The euro's position is more nuanced. It was a beneficiary of the early-2026 "Sell America" rotation, and the ECB's pivot to a tightening bias (three hikes now priced for 2026) provides some rate-differential support. However, the eurozone economy is now contracting (composite PMI at 47.5 in May — sharpest contraction in 2.5 years) and the ECB faces the "Trichet 2.0" risk of tightening into weakness. EUR/USD has not recovered to pre-crisis levels as the dollar's haven premium partially offsets the ECB's hawkish turn. The EU-US trade deal at 15% caps (vs. 25% auto tariff threat) limits the downside but formalizes a structurally worse trade baseline, with eurozone exports to the US running 37% below year-ago levels in March.

**Airline implication:** European carriers (Lufthansa, Air France-KLM) face elevated USD fuel costs in EUR terms. Partial offset from ECB rate support, but growth contraction in core markets compresses demand side simultaneously.

---

### British Pound (GBP/USD) — Depressed by Political Risk and Rate Divergence

Sterling is conspicuously weak. UK M&A activity has surged more than 250% year-to-date as overseas acquirers exploit "depressed sterling valuations" — a phrase from current Bloomberg coverage that encapsulates the currency's position. The drivers are cumulative: political instability (Starmer leadership challenge from Burnham, Labour projected to lose 1,850 seats in local elections), fiscal constraints limiting the Chancellor's room to cushion energy costs, and a rate divergence from the eurozone — UK CPI has fallen to its lowest in over a year (diverging downward from EU), prompting traders to pare BoE hike expectations relative to the ECB. Hedge funds extended short sterling positions against the euro in February; option markets built fresh bearish GBP positions in May around the Burnham leadership risk. The structural rigidity of the UK — regulated energy pricing, inflation-linked wage contracts — makes second-round inflation effects more likely even as headline CPI softens.

**Airline implication:** IAG (British Airways) and Ryanair report revenues partly in GBP/EUR but bear USD fuel costs. GBP weakness is a direct margin headwind. IAG's balance sheet also carries meaningful USD and EUR debt, so currency moves affect financing costs asymmetrically.

---

### Malaysian Ringgit (MYR/USD) — Oil Exporter Partial Insulation

Malaysia is structurally differentiated from most of the ASEAN bloc as a net petroleum exporter (Petronas). While most Asian currencies fell sharply versus the dollar in the Hormuz shock — the Korean won hit its lowest since 2009, the Indian rupee and Philippine peso reached record lows, the Thai baht fell 1.8% intraday at peak — Malaysia's current account benefit from elevated oil prices provides partial insulation. Malaysia's exports were rising at their fastest pace in over three years entering the crisis (January 2026 data), and higher oil prices lift Petronas-linked government revenue and the current account balance. However, ASEAN manufacturing PMIs broadly fell to their weakest since September, and MYR remains exposed to general EM risk-off dollar demand and to US-China trade war disruption of Malaysia's semiconductor supply chain role.

**Airline implication:** AirAsia and Malaysia Airlines are relatively better positioned than peers in oil-importing ASEAN economies. The MYR's relative resilience (vs. IDR, INR, PHP) means their USD fuel cost in local currency terms has risen less sharply. This is a genuine, if partial, natural hedge not available to airlines in oil-importing countries.

---

### Brazilian Real (BRL/USD) — Commodity Windfall vs. EM Risk-Off

Brazil, like Malaysia, is a net commodity exporter (oil, soybeans, iron ore), placing it on the more favored side of the EM divergence. The Bloomberg emerging-market coverage explicitly noted "oil-exporting EMs such as Brazil and Colombia stand to benefit from elevated prices, while net importers including Mexico, Chile, and much of Asia face headwinds." Brazilian soybean and agricultural exporters are also displacing US exporters in key Asian markets as tariff rerouting creates new trade flows. Brazil's bond yields rose in sympathy with the global move (joining a pattern noted across Canada, Brazil, and the UK), but the BRL's underlying current account support from higher commodity prices is meaningfully more positive than for EM oil importers. Fiscal dynamics and political risk remain BRL-specific vulnerabilities in any risk-off episode.

**Airline implication:** Brazilian carriers (LATAM, Gol, Azul) face a more favorable currency backdrop than Asian or European peers relative to their USD fuel costs. Higher oil supporting BRL partially offsets the fuel cost increase in local currency terms — a natural hedge that is a structural feature of Brazil's export mix.

## 3. The Oil Price Offset Question

**Does currency depreciation offset rising oil costs for non-USD airlines? Only in normal times — and this is not a normal time.**

In standard market conditions, there is a well-documented negative correlation between the dollar and oil: when oil rises, the dollar tends to weaken (petrodollar recycling, commodity-currency flows, risk-on rotation), reducing the local-currency cost of fuel for non-USD airlines. This was broadly operative through 2025 — the dollar fell ~6% while oil was rangebound, providing a structural tailwind.

The Hormuz shock has broken this correlation in the worst possible way for non-USD airlines:

| Factor | Normal environment | Hormuz crisis environment |
|--------|--------------------|--------------------------|
| Oil price direction | Variable | Sharply higher (+60-80%) |
| USD direction | Typically weaker when oil rises | **Stronger** (haven demand) |
| Net impact on non-USD fuel cost | Partially offset | **Amplified — double negative** |

The reason is structural: geopolitical crises generate simultaneous safe-haven USD demand and energy price surges — the same shock drives both in the same direction. The petrodollar offset mechanism that normally caps non-USD airlines' fuel cost exposure has inverted.

**The partial exceptions** are oil-exporting countries (Malaysia, Brazil, Norway) where the currency has commodity-income support that partially resists dollar strength. MYR and BRL carry this property. JPY, GBP, EUR, and most EM importing currencies do not.

**Outlook:** The offsetting benefit will only return sustainably if:
1. Oil prices normalize (Hormuz reopens, forward curve reverts toward $80), allowing risk appetite to recover and the dollar to soften, **or**
2. The Fed pivots back toward cuts (currently not priced), narrowing the rate differential

Neither is near-term. Until then, non-USD airlines face a structurally more expensive fuel environment than either the dollar or oil price alone would imply.

## 4. Airline Implications — Bullets

- **Double negative for most non-USD airlines:** USD strength coinciding with the oil price surge means the historical FX offset has inverted. Airlines in Japan, Europe, and oil-importing Asia bear the full brunt of higher USD jet fuel with no local-currency relief.

- **JPY airlines most exposed:** USD/JPY approaching 160 and BoJ unable to hike compounds the fuel cost shock. Every 5-yen shift is material at current jet fuel prices. JAL and ANA have flagged fuel cost as their primary earnings risk for H1 2026.

- **Sterling creates specific IAG/Ryanair headwinds:** Depressed sterling against both USD (fuel costs) and EUR (intra-European revenues) is a margin squeeze with no near-term catalyst for reversal given political risk overhang.

- **Natural hedges exist for MYR and BRL airlines:** AirAsia and Brazilian carriers benefit from commodity-exporter currencies that have partially resisted dollar strength. This is a structural advantage worth noting — their fuel cost in local terms has risen, but meaningfully less than for JPY or GBP carriers.

- **Hedging programs repriced sharply:** Airlines that had locked in fuel hedges pre-crisis at sub-$130/barrel jet fuel are now sitting on unrealized gains — but those contracts expire. New hedges entered at $180–215/barrel lock in elevated costs and do not benefit from any FX offset at current USD levels.

- **Revenue currency mismatch matters:** Long-haul international carriers that collect significant revenue in strong currencies (USD-ticketed routes, or routes where tickets are priced in USD) are partially insulated. Pure domestic and short-haul regional carriers with 100% local-currency revenue face the full FX-amplified fuel cost exposure.

- **EM route exposure carries currency risk:** Airlines with significant exposure to oil-importing EM markets (India, Philippines, Indonesia, Thailand) face a compounding pressure — weaker destination currencies reduce yield in USD terms precisely when fuel costs are highest.

- **The offset may return, but only after resolution:** Historical precedent (1990-91 Gulf War, 2022 Russia/Ukraine) shows that once energy supply disruption resolves, the USD weakens, oil corrects, and the traditional negative correlation reasserts — providing a material tailwind for non-USD airlines in the recovery phase. Planning for that scenario in hedging and fleet strategy is as important as managing current exposure.
