US Supply Chains Are Straining Like It’s 2022 Again

US supply chains are flashing the clearest signs of tightness since the 
one-two punch of Covid and the Russia-Ukraine war disrupted global shipping 
almost four years ago.

The Logistics Manager’s Index rose in March to 65.7, the third straight 
monthly increase and the highest level since May 2022, according to a report 
released Tuesday. Costs for transportation, warehousing and inventories all 
increased, according themonthly gauge 
<https://www.the-lmi.com/march-2026-logistics-managers-index.html> released 
from Colorado State University in Fort Collins.

Read More: US Truck Rates at Highest Since 2022 Add to Inflation Pressures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-06/us-truck-rates-at-highest-since-2022-adds-to-inflation-pressures?cmpid=BBD040726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>

Among the propellants: a surge in the price of freight transportation combined 
with another drop in transport capacity, leading to the widest gap between the 
two readings since November 2021, the report showed.

Retail diesel prices <https://gasprices.aaa.com/> in the US have soared almost 
50% since the Iran war began Feb. 28, reaching an average of $5.62 a gallon and 
nearing the peak of $5.82 set in June 2022, according to AAA data.

The latest LMI draws parallels between the international energy crisis 
following Russia’s full-scale invasion in 2022, which restricted about 10% of 
global oil supplies, and the US-Israeliwar in Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-06/trump-says-reopening-hormuz-must-be-part-of-iran-ceasefire-deal?cmpid=BBD040726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
, which is currently blocking about twice that share because of shipping 
restrictions through theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-06/hormuz-traffic-rises-to-highest-in-weeks-as-more-transits-agreed?cmpid=BBD040726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
.

But this time is different in an important way that’s worrisome for the 
economy’s ability to absorb the shock — inventories are leaner, the report said.

Lean Inventories

“Freight activity was more easily able to slow down in 2022 because 
inventories were high, consumer demand dropped, and there was less product to 
move,” the LMI said. “With the current leanness of inventories, firms will not 
have large stores of goods and components to draw on, which may necessitate 
some continued movement in freight.”

What’s more, “the leanness extends to fleet sizes as well, which have 
right-sized considerably since 2022, meaning there will be less excess capacity 
this time around.”

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-06/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-7?cmpid=BBD040726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>

The response from trucking companies is to raise the weekly per-mile fuel 
surcharge paid by shippers to its highest since 2022, according to 
Truckstop.com data.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-06/us-truck-rates-at-highest-since-2022-adds-to-inflation-pressures>
Trucking employment has tumbled since peaking during the pandemic reopening 
rush and is now the lowest since September 2020. A crackdown on certain foreign 
holders of commercial driver’s licenses due to safety concerns is contributing 
to thetighter market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-12-10/trump-immigration-rules-remove-thousands-of-truckers-disrupting-us-freight?cmpid=BBD040726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
 for truckers, said Jason Miller, a supply chain professor at Michigan State 
University.

“The transportation market seems to be booming and inventories are lean,” 
according to today’s LMI report. “However, the possibility for stockouts due to 
that leanness, as well as possible demand destruction due to increasing costs, 
are realistic possibilities.”

Supply strains are mounting outside the US, too. The Federal Reserve Bank of 
New York’s Global Supply Chain Pressure Indexrose last month 
<https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/policy/gscpi#/interactive> to a reading of 
0.68, the highest since January 2023.
