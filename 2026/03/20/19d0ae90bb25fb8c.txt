Rate Alert

Trump’s capacity to unnerve global central bankers appears as untrammeled as 
ever, if recent events are anything to go by.

That’s the emerging picture from the past week, which featured interest-rate 
decisions across all of the Group of Seven, and eight of the world’s 10 
most-traded currency jurisdictions.

The impact of Trump’s war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/asia-turns-to-coal-as-iran-war-rapidly-shrinks-supplies-of-gas?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 against Iran on energy supply, with the associated threaten of another 
inflation shock, may have been all the Reserve Bank Australia needed to trigger 
another hike in borrowing costs, in asplit decision 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/australia-delivers-first-back-to-back-rate-hikes-since-mid-2023?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
. While acknowledging the double blow for households already facing higher pump 
prices, Governor Michele Bullockargued 
<https://www.rba.gov.au/speeches/2026/mc-gov-2026-03-17.html> that letting 
quicker price increases to get “built into the fibers” of the economy would be 
worse.

The Bank of Japan kept the possibility of a hike in April 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/boj-holds-rates-steady-as-iran-conflict-clouds-inflation-outlook?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 on the table and cited the “the situation in the Middle East” as a concern.

Over in Europe, Iceland pivoted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/iceland-reverses-course-with-quarter-point-rate-hike-to-7-5?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 to hike, while heightened alertness elsewhere underscored that business as 
usual is over.

At the Swiss National Bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/snb-keeps-up-increased-resolve-on-franc-as-rate-held-at-zero?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, which has probably stepped up interventions against haven flows, officials 
restated an increased willingness to sell the franc.

Bank of England policymakers declared that they’re “ready to act 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/boe-stands-ready-to-act-as-it-unanimously-votes-to-hold-rates?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
” if needed as they voted unanimously to stay on hold.

“That of course is a change 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/bank-of-england-hits-reverse-as-white-house-drives-uk-rate-moves?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
,” Governor Andrew Bailey said in an interview. “We were, over the course of 
the coming meetings, expecting some scope to cut.”

Over in Frankfurt, the European Central Bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/ecb-holds-rates-steady-as-officials-measure-cost-of-iran-war?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 stopped describing policy as being in a “good place.” Officials might have to 
raise ratesin April 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/ecb-s-makhlouf-says-april-hike-possible-if-data-signal-need?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, people with knowledge of the mattersaid 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/ecb-officials-see-possibility-of-rate-hike-at-april-meeting?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
. Economistsare shifting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/economists-pivot-to-see-ecb-hikes-while-diverging-on-first-move?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 to followmarket bets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/traders-fully-price-three-quarter-point-ecb-rate-hikes-this-year?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 on hikes, while split on whether the ECB might wait until June.

“The medium-term inflation outlook could deteriorate and inflation 
expectations could rise on a sustained basis, meaning that a more restrictive 
monetary-policy stance would probably be necessary,” Bundesbank President 
Joachim Nageltold Bloomberg 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/ecb-would-need-april-hike-if-price-outlook-sours-nagel-says?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
.

The irony is that the central banks that appear more sanguine are those closer 
to the heart of decision making on the war. The Bank of Canada pledged to “look 
through 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/bank-of-canada-holds-at-2-25-looks-through-near-term-oil-risk?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
” the immediate inflation impact.

For his part, Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell gave no suggestion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-19/powell-pushes-back-against-trump-on-rates-and-legal-investigation?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 that hikes are on the table, and largely just set the whole war question aside 
as too early to address. But he also emphasized that officials will need to see 
the impact of tariffs on inflation disappear by mid-year. Futures now show no 
rate cut for 2026, after previously fully pricing in two reductions.

Need-to-Know Research

A number of forecasts have by now come out on the inflationary impact of the 
Iran war. Fed policymakers, for example, justjacked up <> their median estimate 
for the year-end headline rate to 2.7% from 2.4% as of three months ago. 
Bloomberg Economics is offering a nowcast.

The models show that, for March the consumer price increase has climbed by 
about 0.4 percentage point on average across a selection of advanced economies,
Andrej Sokol <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVr1W3uGmwA/andrej-sokol> at 
Bloomberg Economics wrote in a note earlier this week. The models “provide 
daily estimates of the next inflation print based on past data and up-to-date, 
high-frequency information from commodity, energy and financial markets,” he 
wrote.

“The models are overwhelmingly expecting the March inflation readings to print 
higher than was predicted before the conflict in Iran began, even though there 
have been some downward influences from other sources of inflation news,” Sokol 
said. (Among the downward pressures: a tumble inindustrial metals 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/copper-gives-up-2026-gains-as-iran-war-roils-metals-markets?cmpid=BBD032026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, on concern about the negative impact to growth.)
