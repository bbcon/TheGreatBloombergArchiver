Ailing Tiger Cubs

In Southeast Asia, politics is again scratching away at optimism toward some 
of the region’s so-called tiger cubs.

Thailand, Indonesia and Philippines — with a combined population of about 470 
million that tops both the US and European Union — were meant to be among the 
next batch of economies that would follow north Asian peers like South Korea up 
the development ladder. But not for the first time, economic optimism is giving 
way to growing angst as politics gets in the way.

In Thailand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-05/thais-keep-voting-for-change-and-getting-the-same-old-economy?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
, marked now by two decades of underperformance, voters this weekend go to the 
polls in what’s shaping up as a three-cornered contest that pits the reformist 
People’s Party against the more establishment leaning Pheu Thai and Prime 
Minister Anutin Charnvirakul’s Bhumjaithai.

The People’s Party, led by 38-year-old Natthaphong Ruengpanyawut, has garnered 
the most interest, with bold policy proposals that argues monopolies are a 
central driver of rising living costs and eroding competitiveness. But the 
electoral math is tricky, meaning even if they win most seats in the 500-member 
lower house, they may lose out to a coalition of Pheu Thai and Bhumjaithai.

And the reality is that whoever wins government will inherit an economy with 
little fiscal or monetary room. So while all major parties promise to boost 
growth to between 3% to 5%, economists are skeptical that’s feasible given an 
aging population, soaring household debt and widening income inequality.

In Indonesia, it’s not so much a lack of growth that’s the problem, but what’s 
fueling the expansion. Fourth-quartergrowth surged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-05/indonesia-grows-5-39-in-fourth-quarter-beating-forecasts?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 to a three-year high of 5.39% from a year earlier, but it was driven by an 
additional $1.8 billion in cash handouts in the period, fueling concerns over 
swelling budget deficits.

Moody’s Ratings this week lowered Indonesia’s credit outlook 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-05/moody-s-downgrades-indonesia-rating-outlook-to-negative?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 to negative, adding togrowing warnings 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/indonesia-finance-chief-pushes-back-at-moody-s-outlook-cut?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 about policy uncertainty and weakening governance under President Prabowo 
Subianto’s administration.

It was yet more bad news after the benchmark stock index suffered its worst 
two-day rout since 1998 last week after index compiler MSCI Inc. warned that 
the emerging market could be downgraded to frontier status because of a lack of 
liquidity and transparency in the stock market.

Prabowo’s reign has been tumultuous, unsettling businesses 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-06/indonesia-s-prabowo-stokes-investor-concerns-amid-land-seizures-stocks-meltdown?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 and markets. His administration has seized mines and plantations, arrested 
professionals who worked for the government on corruption charges, restructured 
the nation’s powerful state-owned enterprises and rolled out costly social 
welfare programs.

It’s a combination that’s proving to be a turnoff for foreign investors.

And over in the Philippines, an ongoing corruption scandal is weighing on 
investments, households and government spending. The economy there slowed to 
the weakest pace in nearly 15 years outside of the pandemic in the closing 
quarter of last year. Investments slumped 10.9%.

Revelations last July that public funds meant for flood-control projects had 
been misused have led to a sharp slowdown in what had been one of Asia’s 
fastest-growing economies. While protests have died down, the fallout 
continues, with the government cutting its 2026 growth target by a percentage 
point to 5%-6%.

President Ferdinand Marcos Jr., who revealed irregularities in public spending 
and insisted he will root out graft, is now facing impeachment complaints in 
connection with the scandals. He denies wrongdoing and his allies in Congress 
are expected to dismiss the efforts.

One cub is defying the growing pains. Vietnam is riding a trade boom <> as 
it’s increasingly seen as an alternative to China for global manufacturers. It 
posted economic growth of about 8% last year, buoyed also by surging tourism.

And it may not be a coincidence that the political situation in the communist 
nation is solidifying, with leaderTo Lam 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-23/vietnam-s-communist-party-appoints-to-lam-leader-for-5-year-term?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 last month securing more authority after a party congress, clearing the path 
for his “new growth model” policies.

Need-to-Know Research

Whether this Sunday’s Super Bowl can surpass last year’s record TV audience 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-11/fox-attracts-record-high-126-million-viewers-for-the-super-bowl?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 remains to be seen. What’s certain is that this year’s match-up featuring the 
New England Patriots facing off against the Seattle Seahawks in Santa Clara, 
California, will provide a boost to the local economy.

Spending on attending sport and entertainment events surged after the pandemic 
as consumers craved live experiences. And watching the Super Bowl in person 
doesn’t come cheap: A week or so before the Feb. 8 game, ticket prices at the 
host Levi’s Stadium were the second-highest ever with an average ofabout $8,230 
each 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/super-bowl-ticket-prices-are-second-highest-ever-at-8-230-each?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
, according to reseller TickPick.

On a typical home game day at that stadium in the San Francisco Bay Area 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/super-bowl-2026-san-francisco-proves-it-s-more-than-a-tech-hub?cmpid=BBD020626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
, spending in surrounding zip codes rose by about 70% in the past nine years, 
based on internal debit- and credit-card data from Bank of America. And looking 
across the US, a Super Bowl brought in an extra median boost of 7% on top of 
the usual game day during that period, Bank of America Institute researchers 
David Tinsley and Taylor Bowley said in areport 
<https://institute.bankofamerica.com/economic-insights/football-economics-local-spending-impact.html>
 this week.
Bank of America Institute
Last time the Levi’s stadium hosted the Super Bowl, in 2016, the area 
attracted an estimated1.1 million visitors 
<https://levisstadium.com/2016/02/super-bowl-50-sets-records-across-the-board/>
, boosting hotel and sales tax revenues.

Unsurprisingly, the biggest increases in spending are in food, booze, parking 
and lodging. During the 2024 Super Bowl in Las Vegas, spending in bars and 
liquor stores rose more than 40%, according to Bank of America Institute.
