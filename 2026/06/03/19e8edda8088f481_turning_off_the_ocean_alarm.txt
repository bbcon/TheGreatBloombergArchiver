Data adrift

By Leslie Kaufman 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUZ8izNmriE/leslie-kaufman?cmpid=BBD060326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5178>
 andEric Roston 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQdgGkZhizQ/eric-roston?cmpid=BBD060326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5178>

The Trump administration’s decision 
<https://oceanobservatories.org/2026/05/announcement-on-ooi-descoping/> to 
dismantle most of a federal ocean-observing network will leave scientists 
without an irreplaceable source of data used to understand how climate change 
is affecting crucial currents and marine ecosystems and increasing coastal 
flooding.
A scientist conducting research in Cape Cod Bay, Massachusetts, in 2024. 
Photographer: Joseph Prezioso/AFP/Getty Images
“No other system has the comprehensiveness,” said Craig McLean, the former 
acting chief scientist at the National Oceanic and Atmospheric Administration 
and now a senior fellow at the Ocean Foundation. Other monitoring networks 
provide valuable information but represent only “bits and pieces,” he said, of 
what is collectively measured.

The network of sensors, moorings and autonomous instruments known as the Ocean 
Observatories Initiative will be stripped of almost all in-water 
infrastructure, according to plans announced in late May by the National 
Science Foundation. Federal officials will send boats to remove the equipment 
placed in most the Atlantic and Pacific installations.

Experts say there’s no alternative that can fully replace the network. The Argo
 <https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Argo_(oceanography)> float network, supported 
by dozens of nations, the UK-ledRAPID <https://rapid.ac.uk/> array and 
observatories operated by Canada, Europe and Japan also collect ocean data, but 
those and other systems are generally viewed as complementary to rather than 
interchangeable with OOI.

OOI provides a unique level of detail, according to Chris Robbins, associate 
director of scientific initiatives at the Ocean Conservancy.

“Argo is somewhat analogous to taking someone’s vitals, in that it provides a 
snapshot of one’s health,” he said. “OOI provides a deeper level of diagnosis 
that would be gained through imaging and blood panels.”

Disaster economy

By Olivia Raimonde 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUdOpjWq8Dk/olivia-raimonde?cmpid=BBD060326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5178>

Extreme weather will likely spur more than $20 trillion in global spending 
over the next decade, boosting sales and earnings for reinsurers and companies 
involved in energy efficiency and climate security, according to analysts at
Bloomberg Intelligence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/0006612D:US?cmpid=BBD060326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5178>
.

Returns for a group of 275 companies focused on environmental adaptation and 
mitigation beat the broader market by almost 32 percentage points in the year 
through April 19, Bloomberg Intelligence analysts Andrew John Stevenson and 
Eric Kane wrote ina report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/terminal/TFZQ52KGZAIP?cmpid=BBD060326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5178>
 published Tuesday. Spending on climate adaptation has helped drive gains for 
companies includingBWX Technologies Inc. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/BWXT:US?cmpid=BBD060326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5178>
, RenaissanceRe Holdings Ltd., Woodward Inc. and Dycom Industries Inc., they 
wrote.
A downed tree and blocked-in vehicle after hurricanes Helene and Milton in St. 
Pete Beach, Florida, in 2024.Bloomberg
While several sectors are poised to benefit from climate-related spending, 
other parts of the economy are likely to bear the brunt of climate-change 
costs. Bloomberg Intelligence identified municipalities and consumers as among 
the most exposed. The research also warned that federal support for disaster 
recovery could fall to levels that jeopardize rainy-day funds in 26 states, 
potentially putting pressure on state and local government credit ratings.
