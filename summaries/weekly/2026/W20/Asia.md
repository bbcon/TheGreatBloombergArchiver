**Trump-Xi Summit**
The week's geopolitical centrepiece was the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing on Thursday and Friday, the first US presidential visit to China in nearly a decade. The delegation's composition was itself a signal: Jensen Huang of Nvidia joined Tim Cook, Elon Musk, Kelly Ortberg, and Goldman Sachs' David Solomon, thrusting AI chip access into the heart of the agenda alongside trade, Taiwan, and Iran. Xi greeted Trump with a full state welcome at the Great Hall of the People, and both leaders struck cooperative public tones. China renewed import licenses for hundreds of US beef plants as an early gesture, and Treasury Secretary Bessent outlined a potential board of investment allowing Chinese capital into non-sensitive US industries, alongside possible tariff relief on roughly $30 billion of non-critical trade. Pre-summit working-level talks between Bessent and Vice Premier He Lifeng had begun in Seoul on Wednesday. The most pointed moment came when Xi warned that mishandling Taiwan could push the bilateral relationship into a "highly dangerous situation" — language published by Chinese state media while the two leaders were still in the room. On Iran, both sides expressed shared interest in reopening Hormuz but produced no operational mechanism. Trump subsequently confirmed that China has not approved purchases of Nvidia H200 chips and intends to develop domestic alternatives — a reminder that the semiconductor competition underlying the summit cordiality remains unresolved.

**South Korean Market Dislocation**
South Korean equities experienced two distinct episodes of volatility this week. On Monday, the Kospi shed more than $300 billion in market capitalization within 97 minutes — falling as much as 5.1% — after presidential policy chief Kim Yong-beom floated a "citizen dividend" funded by AI-profit taxes via a Facebook post, before recovering partially following a clarification. By Friday, the index fell a further 6.1% in its steepest single-day decline since March. The underlying tension is genuine: Samsung Electronics posted a 755% profit increase in Q1 and its labor union is threatening an 18-day strike from May 21 demanding 15% of operating profit for chip-division employees. The convergence of redistribution politics, labor unrest, and speculative positioning — retail borrowing is at record levels — around AI-driven windfall profits appears likely to persist. Goldman Sachs economists noted that AI-chip export booms in South Korea and Taiwan are on track to push both economies' current-account surpluses to record levels, likely pressuring their central banks toward rate increases later this year.

**China: Inflation, Credit Quality, and Strategic Posture**
China's April inflation data marked a meaningful inflection: PPI rising to 2.8% year-on-year — its fastest since July 2022 — alongside a 1.2% CPI reading prompted analysts to declare a formal exit from the deflationary spiral that had weighed on domestic pricing for the better part of two years. The drivers are partly imported, however, with elevated energy and raw material costs from the Hormuz closure contributing materially; Chinese crude imports fell 20% year-on-year in April as state refiners drew on commercial inventories. More structurally concerning, independent analysts estimate true non-performing loan ratios in the Chinese banking system at approximately 10% of total credit — against an official reading of 1.5% — implying roughly $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt. Regulatory forbearance has so far contained systemic risk but is visibly slowing loan growth and trapping capital in unproductive enterprises. China's April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above the 8% consensus, with technology the primary driver — even as 10-year domestic bond yields remain below 2%, now trading below Japanese government bond yields, a striking inversion that reflects the dichotomy between external competitiveness and subdued domestic demand.

**Japan, India, and Regional Currency Stress**
Japan's 20-year JGB yields touched their highest level since 1997 this week, driven by energy-import inflation feeding domestic price pressures. A Bank of Japan board member called for tightening "as early as possible," and Finance Minister Katayama confirmed close coordination with Bessent on foreign exchange policy, with Bessent characterizing excess FX volatility as undesirable — a tacit endorsement of recent intervention. Japanese household spending fell for a fourth consecutive month, illustrating the demand compression accompanying the cost-of-living shock. Corporate goods prices rose at the fastest pace in 12 years. In India, Prime Minister Modi doubled gold and silver import tariffs to approximately 15%, pairing earlier appeals for citizens to curb bullion purchases with formal policy tightening to defend the rupee and shore up foreign exchange reserves. The Philippines, India, and Indonesia are all burning FX reserves to defend weakening currencies as oil import costs mount, with shrinking import cover pushing policymakers toward rate hikes. Thailand convened an emergency meeting between the prime minister and leading business figures to address converging crises of inflation, unemployment, and energy costs.
