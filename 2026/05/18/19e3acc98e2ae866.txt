Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Dan Murtaugh looks at how the Iran war has shifted the balance in 
gas-pipeline talks between Russia and China.

For the first time in years, Russian President Vladimir Putin will visit 
Beijing with the realistic prospect of making progress on his countryтАЩs 
flagship gas pipeline project.

Unfortunately for the Kremlin, the decision on when тАФ and how тАФ to proceed 
with thePower of Siberia 2 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-02/russia-china-sign-deal-for-power-of-siberia-2-gas-pipeline-ifx?cmpid=BBBXT051826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4587>
 still rests with Beijing.

The Iran war has helped PutinтАЩs case. China has worried about energy security 
for decades and remains the worldтАЩs top oil importer. The events of the last 
three months have shown that obsession is justified.

The closure of the Strait of Hormuz has cut off 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/how-iran-s-qatar-lng-attack-hormuz-closure-are-driving-up-oil-and-gas-prices?cmpid=BBBXT051826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4587>
 nearly a fifth of global liquefied natural gas, and ChinaтАЩs imports have 
plummeted. Even when the strait reopens, newfound questions around control of 
the passage have eroded the reliability of the Persian Gulf as a supplier.

A direct, steel-and-concrete land link to Russia suddenly looks more 
attractive.
A drilling rig on GazpromтАЩs Chayandinskoye field, which supplies the Power of

This gives Putin more leverage than heтАЩs had in a long time, and some of that 
is already visible. While announcements around the project over the years 
tended come from Russia and Gazprom PJSC, China has now folded progress on the 
pipeline into its latest five-year plan, which runs through 2030.

But that doesnтАЩt mean Russia has a clear run at an agreement, with key issues 
still up for negotiation.

China wants to buy gas at low prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/russia-sees-china-buying-gas-at-discount-to-europe-through-2029?cmpid=BBBXT051826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4587>
 and only when needed тАФ meaning the pipeline may actually be half-empty at 
times. Russia wants guaranteed sales to help defray construction costs.

Why does China have the edge, even with all the disruption in global gas 
markets?

Firstly, its need for gas is limited thanks to domestic coal reserves and a 
fast-growing clean energy sector. Drillers like Cnooc Ltd. have also given it 
more confidence that domestic supply can keep rising. Finally, it also has 
other potential sources of imports, such asCentral Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-24/central-asia-gains-as-war-shakes-energy-markets-and-trade-routes?cmpid=BBBXT051826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4587>
.

Russia has fewer options. Moscow has all but lost Europe as an export market 
and has no other buyers at this scale.

With the Middle East conflict still unresolved, itтАЩs clear Putin will be 
underlining the issue of energy security during his visit this week. ChinaтАЩs 
President Xi Jinping, however, may take another lesson from the war: the need 
to avoid relying on a single source of supply.
