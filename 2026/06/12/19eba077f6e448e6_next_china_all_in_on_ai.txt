Hi, this is Gao Yuan in Singapore.

When the Chinese government makes a plan, it likes a grand one.

Think rapidly building a bullet train network or a city near Beijing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-01-09/xi-s-empty-dream-city-of-xiongan-shows-limits-of-his-power-even-in-china?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 because the capital was getting too crowded. Last year it started work ona 
$167 billion hydropower project in Tibet 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-16/why-china-s-167-billion-mega-dam-project-in-tibet-is-so-controversial?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 that few people knew was even being planned.

This year may be remembered for when China decided that AI could also mean 
“all in.” Bloomberg News has reported that Beijing is considering spending a 
whopping 2 trillion yuan ($295 billion) over the next five years to builda 
network of data centers to generate AI computing power 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/china-prepares-295-billion-plan-to-fund-nationwide-ai-buildout?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 for users across the country.

Extra money will go toward a smart electric grid to power those facilities, 
bringing total spending to about ... 5 trillion yuan.

That’s the policymaker equivalent of a card player coolly pushing all their 
chips forward.

The blueprint for the data center nexus apparently remains in the early stages 
and the details could change, but it shows the Chinese leader’s determination 
to do two things: use tech development to drive the economy and compete in AI 
with the US like no other country in the world.

The lion’s share of the funding will come from sovereign debt and state 
investment funds, underscoring Beijing’s determination to continue its spending 
spree in industries it deems strategicdespite rising government debt 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/china-s-3-trillion-of-hidden-bad-debt-prolongs-economic-pain?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
.

We also saw some major reminders this week of that rivalry. For one, the US 
accused some major Chinese companies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-08/pentagon-accuses-alibaba-baidu-byd-of-aiding-china-s-military?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 of supporting the country’s military. It has now singled out tech giants 
including Alibaba, Baidu and Tencent, a move that could restrict their access 
to certain US research funding, among other consequences.

Additionally, officials in Taiwan are reportedly mulling tougher controls on 
AI chip sales to China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/taiwan-mulls-curbs-on-ai-chip-exports-to-china-to-align-with-us?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
, potentially further aligning them with Washington’s measures.

If those curbs come to pass, they’d be among the most far-reaching steps from 
the administration of President Lai Ching-te to protect the tech and national 
security interests of Taiwan — which makes 90% of the world’s most advanced 
chips.

To counter these kinds of restrictions, Xi would like nothing better than to 
make China self-sufficient in all things tech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-23/china-deepens-bid-for-tech-self-reliance-in-new-development-plan?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
. And he can claim some success so far. His nation has become a world leader in 
EVs and green tech — industries that arebecoming more prominent 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-10/china-s-green-exporters-seize-on-new-markets-after-iran-war-fuel-shock?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 as the Iran war disrupts energy markets.

While the data center plan is big and doesn’t include the money that some 
private firms are spending, it pales when compared to the more than $700 
billion that US leaders like Meta and Microsoft earmark for this year alone.

Hence, the pressing need to keep up with the US.

To do that, China will count on certain key companies — ones the government 
likes to designate “champions.” A vast spectrum of other firms and suppliers 
will be called upon for almost all the work and components required in the 
expansive system.

The data facilities are likely to be built by state-backed contractors using 
locally sourced materials. Chips and switches will probably come from companies 
such as Huawei and Hygon. Optical fiber from Yangtze Optical Fibre and Cable. 
Cooling systems from Envicool. Network operations from China Mobile. The list 
goes on.

What about foreign suppliers like Nvidia or Amazon Web Services? For starters, 
China is not getting their top products because ofthose US restrictions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-09/a-guide-to-the-nvidia-chips-at-the-center-of-us-china-ai-rivalry?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
. The best AI chip Nvidia is allowed to sell is the H200 — a years-old GPU 
that’s being eclipsed by the Blackwell and Rubin series.

Also, using foreign tech wouldn’t help resolve a deep concern among 
policymakers that China could fall behind the US in the race to dominate 
strategic technologies. That’s because Washington is also restricting sales of 
the top-line Nvidia GPUs,ASML’s chipmaking machines and much more 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-02/us-lawmakers-propose-crackdown-on-sales-of-chip-tools-to-china?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
.

The good news for China is that over the past three decades, it has built 
perhaps the world’s most complex tech supply-chain ecosystem, one capable of 
producing not necessarily the best, but the most comprehensive range of 
components for everything from electric vehicles to data centers.

Now, with the grand data center plan, China will push that advantage to the 
extreme.

A Second Act?

... and hello, this is Allen Wan.

Lately investors have been asking if Labubu’s parent company has a comeback in 
store for its fans 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/labubu-maker-pop-mart-rejects-one-hit-wonder-tag-as-global-sales-slow?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 or whether it’ll be relegated to the dustbin of toy history like the Beanie 
Babies.

That was what I went to find out during a recent trip to the Beijing 
headquarters of Pop Mart, whichrode a boom last year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-18/labubus-might-be-the-perfect-avatar-for-2025?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 when the snaggle-toothed monster fueled a sales frenzy at home and abroad. 
Since hitting a record high in August, Pop Mart shares have slumped by almost 
half on concern Labubu was a fad and that the company cannot duplicate its 
success.
Labubu dolls want you to know they are here to stay.Photographer: Qilai 
Shen/Bloomberg
But it won’t be for a lack of trying.

Besides aggressively expanding overseas and partnering with artists around the 
world to design new characters, the toymaker is diversifying into accessories 
and household appliances, expanding a theme park and planning a Hollywood movie.

There are some signs Pop Mart’s plans may be working. While Labubu still tops 
the charts globally,other characters like Twinkle Twinkle 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/labubu-mania-is-over-pop-mart-bets-it-can-survive-with-new-toys?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
 and Skullpanda have become just as popular, depending on the region.

Sales of non-Labubu items accounted for about half of total revenue in the US 
last year, Chief Operating Officer Si De told my colleague and me. And in 
places like Japan, South Korea and Southeast Asia, non-Labubu characters make 
up the majority of its sales, he said.

Morgan Stanley expects sales growth of 13% this year but others are more 
pessimistic. Deutsche Bank predicts a 2% drop.

“We believe there is just hype surrounding Labubu and it has already entered a 
sharp downcycle in overseas markets,” said Deutsche Bank consumer analyst Sammi 
Xu.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

People in China were downright aghast this week after a man was accused of 
abusing and killing dogs he adopted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-11/china-erupts-in-fury-after-man-accused-of-killing-adopted-dogs?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
, prompting a small protest that lasted days, a police probe, the suspect’s 
detention and renewed calls for national animal protection legislation.

The case in the southwestern city of Chongqing entered the public spotlight 
last week, when neighbors filmed a man apparently abusing a dog on his balcony 
late at night, according to local media reports.

Animal welfare volunteers went to confront him and found a severely injured 
puppy in a hallway. The animal had broken bones, a severed tail and teeth 
ground flat.
Rescued dogs in Shanghai. Pet ownership has taken off as China grows wealthier.
Photographer: Hector Retamal/AFP/Getty Images
The incident triggered an outpouring of anger online. More than 100 people 
also gathered outside the man’s building to demand action from authorities. 
Police dispersed the crowd and detained some demonstrators on Tuesday. That was 
repeated on Wednesday.

By Thursday, authorities put up fencing around the residential compound, along 
with a notice saying construction was underway.

The Chongqing police said that the 39-year-old was placed under 
“administrative detention,” which can last up to 20 days. Underscoring how much 
the story has resonated with the Chinese public, a hashtag about the statement 
was viewed some 65 million times on Weibo.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
Many people said that punishment was far from enough.

“Administrative detention doesn’t leave a criminal record, so it’s just a slap 
on the wrist for him,” one person wrote on Weibo, attracting more than 3,000 
likes. “This isn’t a punishment — it actually just protects his peace and quiet 
for a few days!”

The man’s neighbors said they had seen him abusing dogs in the past and called 
the police, but said authorities did not take action. That’s probably because 
China doesn’t have a national law barring animal abuse even aspet ownership 
soars 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-03-11/china-s-birth-rate-is-falling-but-pets-are-reaping-the-benefits?cmpid=BBD061226_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5376>
.

For more than a decade, animal welfare advocates and legal experts have 
clamored for a law to no avail. Delegates to the rubber-stamp legislature have 
submitted proposals over the years but nothing has come of them.

“Without an anti-animal cruelty law, things like this will keep happening — 
it’s a scar on a civilized society,” one Weibo user wrote.
