**ECB Policy and Inflation Risks**

European coverage was thin across the week, with source material comprising primarily global outlook compilations rather than dedicated regional reporting. The most relevant strand for euro-area investors from the sell-side consensus is the prevailing view that inflation will prove stickier than current pricing implies, introducing meaningful two-way risk around the front end of the EUR curve. The ECB entered 2026 with markets pricing a continued, if shallow, easing path; if disinflation stalls amid AI-related capex impulses and fiscal support, Bund yields may struggle to rally despite softer growth signals, and peripheral spreads could face episodic widening should terminal rate expectations drift higher. The degree to which euro-area inflation dynamics decouple from the US — given Europe's weaker underlying demand and more advanced wage normalization — remains an open and important question, one that the January flash CPI releases will begin to illuminate. No material ECB Governing Council communications were captured across the period.

**Currency Headwinds and Equity Positioning**

The widely-held call for continued dollar weakness is mechanically supportive of EUR/USD and, by extension, a structural headwind for euro-area exporters already contending with subdued external demand and persistent competitiveness concerns in autos and chemicals. A stronger euro would assist the ECB's disinflation objective at the margin, potentially complicating the stickier-inflation thesis for the bloc specifically — an internal tension worth monitoring. For European equities, the constructive global risk backdrop predicated on AI capex has historically benefited the region's cyclicals and financials, though the index composition remains considerably less exposed to the AI theme than US benchmarks, limiting the direct upside from that particular driver.

**Venezuela: Energy and Sanctions Dimensions**

The potential rehabilitation of Venezuelan crude production carries specific relevance for European energy names. Repsol and Eni both retain legacy exposures to Venezuelan joint ventures and would be among the more directly affected European names should sanctions architecture be dismantled in an orderly fashion. The sequencing and legal treatment of prior claims — including expropriation arbitration awards and claims on defaulted PDVSA paper held by European investors — remain unresolved. More broadly, a Trump administration willing to commit US fiscal resources to overseas energy infrastructure signals an activist external economic posture that may carry consequences for transatlantic trade alignment and sanctions coordination in coming months. Whether Brussels aligns with or diverges from Washington on the eventual Venezuela sanctions framework is worth monitoring. UK-specific political and gilt market developments were not represented in this week's source material.
