# US Daily Briefing — May 18, 2026

**Bond Rout Reshapes Rate Expectations.** US Treasuries extended last week's selloff as Brent pushed above $111/barrel amid the unresolved Iran conflict and continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz. The 10-year yield held at 4.60% in early trade, while the long end remained the focal point of stress — Federated Hermes' Karen Manna framed the move as a market "reckoning with renewed inflation," with 30-year yields approaching levels not seen in two decades. Rate-cut pricing has shifted materially: traders that earlier in the year were positioned for Fed easing in 2026 are now beginning to discount the tail possibility of a hike. DoubleLine's Jeffrey Gundlach said a June cut is off the table, and Ed Yardeni publicly urged the Fed to abandon its easing bias to avoid losing control of the long end. The FOMC minutes due Wednesday will be parsed carefully for any acknowledgment of the inflation impulse from energy.

**Equities Test the AI Thesis Against Higher Yields.** S&P 500 futures declined 0.6% overnight following Friday's drop of more than 1%. The episode appears so far to resemble a wobble rather than a regime change, though Morgan Stanley's Michael Wilson — who recently raised his S&P target — cautioned that sustained yield volatility could produce the first meaningful correction since the late-March lows. The week's earnings calendar is consumer-heavy (Home Depot, Lowe's, Walmart) and culminates in Nvidia on Wednesday, a key test of whether AI-driven earnings momentum can offset the discount-rate headwind.

**Corporate and Deal Flow.** NextEra is reportedly in talks to acquire Dominion Energy at roughly $66 billion, which would rank as the largest power-sector transaction on record — notable given accelerating data-center load growth. UnitedHealth fell 5.6% premarket after Berkshire disclosed it had exited the position; Berkshire built a $2.6 billion Delta stake and added Macy's. Regeneron dropped 12% on a failed late-stage melanoma trial. SpaceX's S-1 may surface as soon as Wednesday, setting up a potential June roadshow at a valuation reportedly approaching $2 trillion.

**Policy Backdrop.** The post-summit US-China framework remains thin on specifics beyond a $17 billion annual agricultural commitment through 2028. With Section 232-style overcapacity and forced-labor investigations queued to replace the tariffs struck down in February, a fresh round of duties is likely this summer — potentially colliding with gasoline prices that have risen toward $4.50 and consumer affordability concerns ahead of the midterms.
