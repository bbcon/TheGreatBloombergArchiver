Feels Like We Only Go Backwards <https://bloom.bg/47aNOnT> 

Life can only be understood backwards, but it must be lived forwards 
<https://bloom.bg/4gLDfhv>. It’s hard to disagree with this somewhat 
exasperating aphorism from the Danish existentialist philosopher Søren 
Kierkegaard <https://bloom.bg/4yqlwlR>, but the implications can be 
dispiriting. That’s particularly so at present. Understood backwards, it should 
have been obvious to me, as an Englishman born a few weeks after our only World 
Cup victory in 1966, that England was going to offer up unfathomable 
capitulation in their semifinal — which they did. In less important matters, 
there has been a deluge of financial data that freeze the frame at June 30, 
when the war in Iran was winding down and traffic through the Strait of Hormuz 
was steadily returning to normal. Does it really help us comprehend the world 
in which we must live forwards?

Understanding life backwards, the only way that’s possible, we can say with 
some confidence that two weeks ago everything was looking awesome 
<https://bloom.bg/4gGOQOF> (and not just for England as they won an epic 
encounter with Mexico in the Azteca Stadium). Understanding it forward is 
altogether harder (though it does look like a Spain triumph lies ahead), 
because the year’s midpoint coincided almost perfectly with a turn in the oil 
price. Until we know whether that was merely a correction before a range-bound 
period, or the start of another big rise, it’s very much harder to make sense 
of what lies ahead:

As pointed out earlier this week, the arrow of causation between the oil price 
and the supply blockage through theStrait of Hormuz <https://bloom.bg/4gHtAZ9> 
points both ways. If the price rises much higher, the chances are that efforts 
to find a workable compromise will be redoubled, while the sharp drop into the 
end of June emboldened Iran, and the US, to return to hostilities.

As it stands, however, prediction markets — which have always seemed more 
bearish about the Mideast situation than the financial markets linked to it — 
have grown sharply more pessimistic about the chance that normal traffic can 
return even by the end of this year. Kalshi’s contract suggests that’s now 
slightly less than a 50/50 shot, and the odds have fallen sharply this month:

There are plenty of reasons to be alarmed by the growth of prediction markets, 
which can be seen as a covert way to bring gambling and sports betting to a 
wider audience. But the ability to break down perceived risks, and how they 
change over time, is very useful.

How much should the turn in the Middle East color the June data, which showed 
inflation moderating (and doing so broadly, beyond oil) while the labor market 
cooled off? The question is particularly tricky when applied to the latest 
numbers, onproducer prices <https://bloom.bg/4fh3rhz>. The headline shows a 
sharp improvement in June, after what had been a menacing spike:

But this owed a lot to energy. Stripping that out, the trend in all the other 
components of the index was clearly upward (which wasn’t true of consumer 
prices). With energy costs rising again, the question of whether businesses 
must eventually pass higher prices on to consumers must return to the agenda:

There’s a further issue when it comes to sentiment surveys. Bank of America 
has just published its latest poll ofglobal fund managers 
<https://bloom.bg/4fg9NO6>, who were interviewed between July 2 and July 9 as 
the Middle East situation began to veer out of control. At that point, they 
were their most bullish about prospects for the world economy since the 
post-pandemic inflation surge. A majority now think there will be neither a 
“soft” nor a “hard” landing, and that the economy can continue powering on 
without a significant dip:

That strong optimism for growth was expressed in very bullish positions in the 
stock market. Their holdings of cash dropped below 4% to reach a level that 
BofA usually regards as a “sell” signal. It did this, remember, just as 
international events conspired to provide their own excuse to sell:

That was plainly driven in large part by optimism on the oil price. Fund 
managers got this bullish on the assumption that crude would finish the year 
somewhere between $70 and $80:

This was in line with market pricing until today — Brent crude for delivery in 
time for next January is now trading at a few pennies over $80. The events of 
the last few days certainly don’t negate the positive views people held last 
month, but they clearly suggest that the optimism wasn’t fully grounded. If the 
Middle East situation were to deteriorate significantly with destruction of oil 
infrastructure (still unlikely but possible), then there’ll be a need for a new 
view.

Understanding life backwards, which is all we can do, the odds are that the 
primal desire on both sides for basic survival will limit escalation from here, 
and disaster will be avoided, but thatoil prices will remain elevated 
<https://bloom.bg/4ynBeyb> — $80 Brent for January is 33% higher than it was 
trading at the start of this year.

Reasons for optimism come from the ongoing shock to demand from the build-out 
of artificial intelligence. It is reasonable to fear that the trade in 
semiconductors has become overcrowded and could become a bubble. That’s the 
almost universal belief of the fund managers in BofA’s survey, more than 80% of 
whom say that semiconductors are the world’s most crowded trade:

This makes it the most crowded “most crowded” trade ever, as there has never 
been such strong agreement before. Consider it good news, as the world’s most 
powerful money managers are aware of the risk. There’s also been some 
correction this month with MSCI’s index of semiconductors falling while the 
hyperscalers — their customers attempting to build out data centers to offer AI 
models — have recovered a little. But thetrade remains blatantly crowded 
<https://bloom.bg/3T5B8v1>:

Sighs of relief are greeting news that inflation abated last month, due partly 
to falling oil prices. That means lower bond yields and easier monetary 
conditions, which can be self-fulfilling positive prophesies. Wall Street 
banks’ results have also confirmed that the strength of the semiconductor 
trade, and the turbulence caused by the oil shock, contributed to amazing 
market conditions.

But if those conditions are only easing on the back of faulty assumptions 
based on backward data, they could contribute to more of a future take-off in 
inflation. The “no landing” hypothesis, which most fund managers now expect, 
generally ends with inflation and higher rates — and it’s also generally those 
higher rates that are needed to end equity bull markets. But that’s still a 
matter of months in the future.

As Kierkegaard might have warned, if (and it remains a big if) the 
backward-looking assumptions of June 30 prove to be wrong, then that would have 
major implications for an array of financial markets. None would be more 
affected than the dollar.

Dollar’s Cameo

The dollar is a traditional safe haven for times of geopolitical uncertainty. 
That’s helped it this year as the Iran war drove a recovery. Now comes a test 
of its staying power as the structural forces weighing on the greenback look 
set to reassert themselves.

While the Federal Reserve’s minutes from last month confirmed a hawkish tilt, 
subsequent inflation data suggest expectations of a more aggressive policy 
shift may have beenoverdone <https://bloom.bg/4w6oUAX>. A single month’s data 
print isn’t enough to indicate a trend, but it calls the dollar’s revival into 
fresh question.

Further, the Treasury market is behaving as though concerns about Fed 
tightening were overstated. There’s a tight relationship between relative bond 
yields and currency performance. The narrowing advantage of US Treasury yields 
over the rest of the world points to a dollar that is already losing momentum. 
Further downside is likely if expectations of easing solidify:

This doesn’t diminish the inflationary risks posed by the renewed hostilities 
in the Middle East, but respondents to the BofA survey seem to see little 
further upside for thedollar <https://bloom.bg/4fHhgGn>, even as geopolitical 
risks remain very much alive. A strong majority think the dollar is overvalued 
— which if anything implies an expectation that this latest escalation will be 
short-lived:

BCA Research’s Artem Sakhbiev argues that a sustained breakout in oil prices 
could still provide near-term support and put a floor under the currency, even 
if the broader uptrend has run its course:

Tactically, seasonality tends to be quite negative for the dollar in the third 
quarter, and if we see some steepening in the yield curve... all of those could 
be near-term headwinds for the dollar. That’s why we are more comfortable being 
on the sidelines.

A strong second-quarter earnings season could provide a tailwind. With US 
banks off to a solid start, HSBC’s Max Kettner argues that the reporting season 
may prove even more consequential for US assets than the latest inflation data. 
How much of that strength ultimately feeds through to the dollar is less clear.

—Richard Abbey
