Nearly one month into US President Donald Trump’s war on Iran, there’s one 
person whose silence stands out: Xi Jinping.

In contrast to most other Group of 20 leaders, China’s commander-in-chief 
hasn’t commented on the conflict, keeping quiet aspanic elsewhere sets in 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/south-korea-shifts-to-crisis-mode-as-iran-oil-shock-intensifies?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
. South Korea has set up an emergency economic task force, thePhilippines warned
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/philippines-says-grounding-planes-is-a-distinct-possibility?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 about grounding planes and Japan is beginning its largest oil release from 
emergency stockpiles to date.

In Beijing, it’s largely business as normal. 
Xi at the opening session of the National People’s Congress in the Great Hall 
of the People in Beijing on March 5.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
That’s because while the US doubles down on fossil fuels, drilling more crude 
than ever last year and moving to shut down offshore windfarms, Xi’s been
ramping up renewables 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-28/china-s-four-year-energy-spree-has-eclipsed-entire-us-power-grid?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
. Around half of newcar sales are now electric 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-25/the-extraordinary-rise-of-electric-cars-in-developing-countries?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 in the world’s No. 2 economy, keeping down pressure from pump prices. And 
China still has coal in its back pocket.

In the short term, the war is working out well for Xi: US sanctions China has 
long railed against as “hegemonic” are coming off Russian and even Iranian oil, 
American military power has been humbled by drones and US allies have snubbed 
Trump’s call for help.

Look further out, and things get trickier.

Rising oil prices will hurt the factories underpinning China’s lopsided economy
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/ai-demand-is-shielding-china-s-booming-trade-from-iran-war-shock?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
. Chinese firms have a trail of investment snaking across the Middle East that 
Beijing doesn’t want to see blown up, literally.

With rumblings of US-Iran talks in Pakistan, Chinese diplomats are hitting the 
phones, telling Iran to “seize every opportunity for peace” — showing they’re 
keen for an end to the volatility.

For now, though, Xi is sitting pretty. And with his calendar clear of a Trump 
summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-says-he-s-rescheduling-xi-meeting-as-iran-war-rages-on?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 next month, he’s got time to play with.

As the Chinese proverb says: Never interrupt your enemy when he’s making a 
mistake. — Jenni Marsh 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVX1HMBXAFs/jenni-marsh>

Chart of the Day

Poor countries may lose 10 times more people to deaths from high temperatures 
than rich ones, a Climate Impact Lab analysis shows. The report projects that 
by 2050, vulnerable countries will see an increase inheat-related fatalities 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/future-heat-danger-differs-starkly-for-rich-and-poor-countries?cmpid=BBD032526_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
 equivalent to current mortality from common diseases, and 16 of the 20 
countries facing the most new deaths are lower income.
