Financial conditions in the United States remain taut ahead of President Trump's 8 p.m. Eastern Time deadline for Iran to agree to a ceasefire and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, with S&P 500 futures oscillating in a narrow range—erasing overnight losses before settling near flat—as traders weigh the possibility of resolution against the risk of further escalation. Oil's 50% surge since the conflict began in late February is now transmitting clearly into the domestic economy: retail diesel prices have risen nearly 50% to $5.62 per gallon, gasoline has crossed $4 per gallon for the first time since 2022, and the Logistics Manager's Index climbed to 65.7 in March—its highest reading since May 2022—as transportation costs surge and inventories remain lean relative to the prior shock cycle. Bloomberg Economics projects March CPI will show the largest month-over-month headline increase since June 2022, penciling in a 0.9% gain, while core PCE for February—due Thursday—is seen rising 0.4%, keeping the year-over-year rate above 3%. The parallels with 2022 are becoming difficult to dismiss, though Bloomberg Economics does not currently project a recession absent a significant further widening in credit spreads.

In markets, health insurers rallied sharply after CMS proposed a 2.5% increase in Medicare Advantage payment rates for 2027, with Humana gaining 11% and UnitedHealth rising 7%. Broadcom advanced 3.6% in premarket trading following a chip development agreement with Google. Private credit continues to draw attention after JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon's annual shareholder letter reiterated concerns about weakening credit standards across leveraged lending, while stopping short of declaring systemic risk—though Blue Owl Capital shares have now retraced to their 2020 IPO price. SpaceX has begun investor meetings for a potential IPO that could value the company above $2 trillion. The Supreme Court's earlier ruling invalidating emergency-powers tariffs and a subsequent Court of International Trade order mandating $160 billion in refunds to importers represent a meaningful fiscal and legal development that has received less attention than geopolitical headlines.
