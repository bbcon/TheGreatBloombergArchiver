Coaxing rain

By Kyle Stock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ARKz8x17rNs/kyle-stock?cmpid=BBD061326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5523>

Dairy cows tend to eat a lot of grass and alfalfa. But those crops need water 
and there isnтАЩt much of that in Gill, Colorado, these days.

On a recent morning, after the driest winter on record, the little farm town 
just east of the Rocky Mountains was as tan and crispy as a potato chip, 
roiling with tumbleweed and fire ants.
Rain Enhancement Technologies Chief Technical Officer Scott Morris walks up to 
the companyтАЩs ionization rainfall generation device on May 13, 2026, in Gill, 
Colorado.Photographer: Michael Ciaglo
Scott Morris, chief technical officer of a startup called Rain Enhancement 
Technologies Inc., was there to try to change that, at least a bit. The affable 
Australian stood next to a gangly contraption, a shock of metal in the sea of 
brown. Two poles 26 feet high held a 39-foot beam spooled with stainless steel 
wire. Morris tapped his phone and the contraption started to faintly hum like a 
cloud of mosquitos. An electric charge тАФ supplied by a small solar array тАФ 
imperceptibly chewed up the metal wire and sent charged particles into the sky, 
like electrons from a battery terminal. In a moist cloud, the particles attract 
water droplets like iron filings to a magnet. When enough moisture gloms on, 
the whole package falls out of the sky as a raindrop.

Happy cows. Happy cow farmers тАФ at least thatтАЩs the theory.

As millions of businesses and residents in the American West ration water 
after the driest winter on record, some strange ideas are bubbling up, novel 
but largely unproven technologies designed to coax a bit more rain out of 
anemic and unforgiving skies. Cloud seeding, a decades-old idea, is havinga 
renaissance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-31/colorado-and-utah-ski-resorts-turn-to-snowmaking-drones-amid-drought?cmpid=BBD061326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5523>
 from entrepreneurs using drones to sprinkle chemicals into snowstorms. Rain 
Enhancement is trying to sell a similar strategy in warm weather. It is 
standing up its infrastructure for free as proof of concept, hoping surrounding 
farmers and water managers will sign on.

The company still needs clouds тАФ some level of moisture тАФ but it is promising 
its systems will yield at least 10% more rain than would fall naturally.

Naples, Florida-based Rain Enhancement developed its contraption in the 
deserts of Oman. Now, it has two US installations, the machine in Colorado and 
another just outside of Moab, Utah, though just $10,500 in revenue to date.

This summer, the valley around Moab is expected to receive about half of the 
snowmelt it typically harvests, according to Kevin Clyde, a board member of the 
Grand County Water Conservation District, which serves about 10,000 residents. 
Still, Clyde has been watching the skies around the Rain Enhancement site since 
it switched on in November and he sees the long jags of rain like clockwork. 
Streams that are typically dry this time of year are gushing.

As the UK moves from a record-hot May into an unseasonably wet June, the risk

The trend тАФ often a quick fix to create space to park cars or to make garden

. The more asphalt and the less spongy vegetation and natural ground, the

Flood ReтАЩs garden at the Chelsea Flower Show demonstrated ways to prevent

The conventional wisdom is that тАЬlower maintenance is better,тАЭ says Kelly

As a model for the ideal UK garden in a small space, Flood Re displayed a

Homes surrounded by floodwater in Wraysbury, west of London, in 2024.

Gardens make up almost 5% of BritainтАЩs total land area, rising to 41% of land 
in London, according to the Royal Horticultural Society, a charity. But instead

In England, the Climate Change Committee estimates that the total area defined

2022; thatтАЩs roughly equivalent to the size of Singapore. In cities across the 
UK, permeable land fell from 63% to 54% over the two decades through 2022.

As Britain loses much of its natural sponginess, climate change has made

heat adds an estimated 7% of additional moisture to the atmosphere, which 
scientists say is contributing to heavier summer downpours.

Meanwhile, Britain is warming faster than the global average. And 
тАЬsurface-water flooding is the fastest-growing climate risk at the moment,тАЭ
