And just like that, US President Donald Trump’s whole trade policy — which has 
dominated global discourse since he returned to the White House more than a 
year ago — is in disarray.

The clear loser from the US Supreme Court’s decision to declare Trump’s 
preferred mechanism for applying tariffs was illegal is Trump himself. Stripped 
of the sweeping authority he had claimed under emergency powers, the tools he 
has left are less agile and subject to fresh legal challenges.

Winners include rivals China, India and Brazil, which will now see lower 
tariff rates <https://bloom.bg/3OWKHdt> on shipments to the US as Trump’s 
competitive penalties evaporate. Average levies on goods from China will fall 
to 24% from 32%, according to Morgan Stanley, while for Asia overall the 
weighted average tariff rate will drop to 17% from 20%.

Although Trump has since announced plans for a 15% global rate, the revision 
creates a level playing field. That’s painful for economieslike the UK 
<https://bloom.bg/4qPKpCA> and Australia which had negotiated lower levies of 
10% under the original framework. The British Chambers of Commerce estimates 
the increase will boost the cost on UK exports to the US by as much as £3 
billion ($4 billion) and will impact 40,000 British companies.

The ruling also means Trump will hold a weaker hand <https://bloom.bg/4aHzRiL> 
as he heads to the negotiating table with Chinese President Xi Jinping during a 
visit to Beijing next month.

While US officials are insisting that previously agreed deals with trading 
partnersremain in place <https://bloom.bg/3OmPrZX> — with US Trade 
Representative Jamieson Greer calling them “good deals” — not every country may 
share that view right now. The European Parliament’s trade chief said he’ll 
propose freezing the EU’s ratification of a trade deal with the US until the 
Trump administration clarifies its policy. Indian officials cited similar 
reasons for New Delhipostponing talks <https://bloom.bg/4qPVdAw> in the US this 
week on finalizing an interim trade deal.

What happens from here isn’t clear. What is certain is Trump has lost his most 
potent instrument for extracting global concessions in the name of trade.
—Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



Trump’s new global tariffs are pegged on seemingly thin ice. To roll out a 
tariff of 10% — which Trump later raised to 15% — to replace those the Supreme 
court invalidated in its landmark ruling on Friday, Trump invoked Section 122 
of the Trade Act of 1974.

The statute allows US presidents to impose duties for up to 150 days “in 
situations of fundamental international payments problems.” Those include 
“large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits” and an “imminent 
and significant depreciation of the dollar.”

The potential problem <https://bloom.bg/3MpIxT7> for Trump and his 
administration is that many economists don’t see the US teetering on any such 
precipice. That means his latest import taxes seem likely to lead to yetanother 
legal challenge <https://bloom.bg/4qKiTWZ> and more uncertainty 
<https://bloom.bg/4aLu1NA> for trading partners, companies, consumers and 
investors.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent said the new tariffs would be temporary, 
ensure that revenues continue to flow to the Treasury and eventually be 
replaced by ones under separate authorities.

The uncertainty bled into financial markets. S&P 500 futures dropped 
<https://bloom.bg/4ruTwcR> 0.6% and Bitcoin briefly slid below $65,000 
<https://bloom.bg/46R8amy>. A Bloomberg gauge of the greenback strength 
retreated <https://bloom.bg/4tNAKPw> as much as 0.3%, extending Friday’s 
decline amid thin trading with Japan and mainland China markets shut for a 
holiday. Traders sought refuge in the Swiss franc and yen, whilegold headed 
<https://bloom.bg/4qMN0xf> for the highest level this month. Chinese stocks 
rallied in Hong Kong <https://bloom.bg/4b3aXvj> due to the lower tariffs.




Private equity’s struggles continue. The industry returned fewer profits 
<https://bloom.bg/4b55sw8> to investors for a fourth straight year after 
sitting on $3.8 trillion of unsold assets and struggling to raise money for new 
funds.

Distributions as a percentage of net asset value remained at 14% last year — 
the second-lowest level since the depths of the 2008 financial crisis, 
according to a new report from Bain. The duration of the rut is more severe 
than back then.

Private equity investors have become more discerning and are looking to commit 
capital to vehicles that deliver net internal rates of return exceeding 20%, 
according to the report.




A powerful winter storm sweeping the US East Coast has shut down the vast 
majority of flights <https://bloom.bg/40uKDnL> out of the region’s largest 
airports, including in the New York area and in Boston.

More than 5,300 flights in, out or within the US were scrapped at the start of 
the week, according to FlightAware as of 2 a.m. New York time. The impact on 
the region’s airports are at least as severe as the late-January winter storms.




South Korea’s export-driven economy is expected to post “significantly higher” 
growth this year compared to 2025, supported by a chip boom and a 
stronger-than-anticipated global backdrop, the central bank said.

The Bank of Korea told parliament that the semiconductor market is exhibiting 
a more robust cycle <https://bloom.bg/4tOTONv> than previous upswings and is 
likely to remain stronger than usual at least through this year. The nation’s
exports jumped <https://bloom.bg/3OVzfPi> 47% in the first 20 days of February, 
when adjusted for working day differences, official data showed Monday.




Mexican authorities killed a top drug lord during a raid in the western state 
of Jalisco, triggering retaliatory clashes across the country.

Nemesio Rubén Oseguera Cervantes, the leader of a cartel known as “El Mencho,” 
was slain <https://bloom.bg/4aK8YL3> in a federal operation against the group 
on Sunday morning, Mexico’s government said. Oseguera rose to prominence after 
helping found the Jalisco Cartel around 2009 and making it one of the main 
groups that traffics drugs to the US. After the raid, criminal groups burned 
cars and trucks to block roads in different areas of Jalisco, and there were 
also clashes between gangs and the authorities. Trumphas pressured 
<https://bloom.bg/3MZAgW1> Mexico to halt the flow of drugs.
A Mexican federal agent stands guard near a bus set on fire by gangs in 
Zapopan, Jalisco state, on Sunday.Photographer: Ulises Ruiz/AFP 



The European Central Bank is delving into the risks faced by banks from the AI 
industry, amidheightened concern <bbg://news/stories/TAQXIOT96OSL> over hidden 
credit exposures and financial-sector disruption.

The Frankfurt-based central bank is asking a small number of individual 
lenders for more details on their lending to areas including data centers, 
people familiar with the effort told Bloomberg. In parallel, the regulator is 
running targeted workshops to identify how banks are using generative AI. 
Increased attention by the ECB to AI risks underlines how global regulators are 
aware of the potential for the technology to upend the banking sector.

 For Your Commute 



Long before he turned South Korea into the world’s hottest stock market, Lee 
Jae Myung was just a newbie, 30-something day trader bleeding cash month after 
month.

All these decades later, Lee, now the nation’s president, is still miffed 
about those losses.

It’s not that he thinks he was any good at day trading. Too reckless, he says. 
No, what bothers him — according to interviews with over half a dozen people 
close to Lee, many of whom requested anonymity to discuss private conversations 
— is the gnawing sensation that those losses were amplified by the unfair deals 
that controlling shareholders cut to enrich themselves at the expense of 
ordinary investors.

That lingering anger prompted him <https://bloom.bg/3Otf9vM> to impose 
sweeping financial reforms since he took power last June, including rules that 
level the playing field for all shareholders and strengthen boardroom 
accountability. Those measures in turn helped ignite the biggest equity rally 
globally.
