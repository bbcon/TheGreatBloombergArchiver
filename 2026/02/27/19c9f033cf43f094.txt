Trumponomics in Japan

Championing tax cuts. Hauling the central bank chief in for a heart-to-heart 
on the economy. Extolling the benefits of a weak currency. Nominating dovish 
candidates to serve on the monetary-policy committee.

That all may sound like US President Donald Trump. But that’s a summary of 
Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s economic record over the past several 
weeks. And it’s stoking concern that policymakers in Tokyo may be baking in
inflationary pressures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-26/tokyo-cpi-cools-below-boj-s-target-for-first-time-since-2024?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 for the world’s fourth-largest economy.

One school of thought held that Takaichi, after securing the biggest electoral 
victory in Japan’s postwar history earlier this month, might ease off somewhat 
on an economic stimulus agenda, given her newfoundpolitical security 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/takaichi-hails-japan-mandate-wants-to-build-trust-with-markets?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

But her instincts were on display Wednesday, when she nominated two 
reflationist academics 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/takaichi-s-government-picks-two-academics-to-join-boj-board?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 to fill forthcoming openings on the Bank of Japan board. The move underscored 
concerns that the prime minister favors BOJ restraint in raising interest 
rates. The yen and Japanese bonds dropped on the news.

Japan’s inflation has averaged above the BOJ’s 2% target the past four 
calendar years now — a trend that’s becoming embedded as companies get used to 
regular price rises after a decades-long “deflationary mindset.” That pattern 
has even seen Haruhiko Kuroda — the champion of mega monetary stimulus when he 
ran the BOJ from 2013 to 2023 — call for more rate hikes.

“Japan needs to move toward tighter fiscal and monetary policy,” Kuroda said 
in a Reutersinterview 
<https://www.reuters.com/world/asia-pacific/ex-boj-chief-kuroda-calls-rate-hikes-tighter-fiscal-policy-2026-02-25/>
. He also cautioned that the yen’s recent levels could be “somewhat too weak.”

Rather than tighten policy, Takaichi has pledged to repeatedly “hit the button 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-19/japan-s-takaichi-to-tout-plans-aimed-at-stoking-private-spending?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
” on economic growth, and her campaign platform featured a temporary sales-tax 
cut on food along with stronger spending on national security. And on the 
campaign trail, Takaichi made waves saying at one point that the weak yen was “
a huge opportunity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-01/japan-s-takaichi-clarifies-weak-yen-remarks-from-election-rally?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
” for some industries.

But she’s also promised 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-19/japan-s-takaichi-to-tout-plans-aimed-at-stoking-private-spending?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 not to pursue “reckless” measures that undermined market confidence, while 
current BOJ Governor Kazuo Ueda said Takaichihadn’t given him 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/boj-s-ueda-says-takaichi-had-no-specific-request-at-meeting?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 specific requests during their meeting last week at the prime minister’s 
office.

Time will tell if the prime minister becomes vocal in opposing more rate 
hikes, and how she proceeds on fiscal policy. For now, her dovish brace of 
nominations this week “makes it clear that she intends to try to contain the 
pace of BOJ moves,” Evercore ISI analysts led by Krishna Guha wrote in a note.

Need-to-Know Research

One of the many causes of the inflation surge that followed Covid was a 
shortage of semiconductors. Today, something of an echo may be appearing thanks 
to the AI build-out, which has driven up demand forhigh bandwidth memory 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-15/rampant-ai-demand-for-memory-is-fueling-a-growing-chip-crisis?cmpid=BBD022726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 — in turn, propelling prices of memory chips.

“Bloomberg Economics estimates that if memory prices stay elevated and firms 
pass on a substantial share of the increase, that could add an upward impulse 
of about 0.2 percentage point to inflation in the US, euro area and UK this 
year,” Hyosung Kwon and Michael Deng wrote in a note this week.

“If memory chip shortages spread to autos, the impact could be fractionally 
larger,” the Bloomberg Economics duo wrote. Or, if companies absorb some of the 
cost shock, the uplift to CPI might be just 0.1 percentage point. But under the 
baseline expectation, the inflation bump “could reduce the scope for rate cuts” 
in the US and UK, and reinforce the European Central Bank’s caution.
