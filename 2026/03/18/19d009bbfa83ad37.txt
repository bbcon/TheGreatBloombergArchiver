What, Me Worry?

In a year when even a bag of lettuce 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/hold-the-salad-as-lettuce-and-tomato-prices-surge-in-february?cmpid=BBD031826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 feels like a luxury, the last thing Americans needed was a surge in gasoline 
prices on the back of an unpopular war. The White House’s top economist had a 
palliative message Tuesday: gas prices will come down, the economy is fine, the 
administration has aplan for disruptions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/war-spurs-us-backup-fertilizer-effort-as-planting-season-begins?cmpid=BBD031826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

But to some, National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett appeared to set 
aside household concerns altogether. An extended Iran war “would hurt 
consumers, and we’d have to think about what we’d have to do about that. But 
that’s really the last of our concerns right now,” he said on CNBC. And that 
seems at odds with his boss, who’s called for an immediate interest-rate cut.

Fed policymakers meeting Wednesday, for their part, are hardly likely to 
ignore the immediate inflation implications of the cost shock reverberating 
through the economy. Investors and economists alike have rolled back 
expectations for further interest-rate cuts this year, and will be looking to 
see if Fed officialsdo the same 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/fed-to-hold-interest-rates-steady-as-iran-war-scrambles-the-economic-outlook?cmpid=BBD031826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

Hassett bases his confidence on the war ending soon, although President Donald 
Trump himself has framed that point as occurring only “when Ifeel it in my bones
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/trump-leaves-allies-and-foes-guessing-on-his-endgame-for-iran?cmpid=BBD031826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.” Gut feelings aren’t generally the foundation for Fedrate decisions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/fed-officials-to-hold-rates-weigh-oil-shock-decision-day-guide?cmpid=BBD031826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

For the central bank, the inescapable reality is that the US and Israeli war 
with Iran has flung a big, oily wrench into the economic outlook.

The cost of filling up the family vehicle plays an outsized role in the US 
national mood — especially when those enormous, glowing gas signs on street 
corners are broadcasting the rising prices. Prices at the pump have climbed by 
roughly 27% to $3.79 a gallon since the conflict started, while global oil 
prices as of the start of this week had jumped almost 38%.

That wipes away the declines that Trump touted last year, and risks 
compounding persistentvoter anxieties 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/democrats-seize-on-iran-driven-gas-prices-in-appeals-to-voters?cmpid=BBD031826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 about affordability ahead of November midterm elections that will determine 
whether Republicans keep control of Congress.

For all Hassett’s confidence, Trump suggested some concern when he called on 
the Fed to hold anemergency meeting 
<https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116217763545289031> to slash 
interest rates – a move only seen during crises.

Need-to-Know Research

Ahead of the new economic projections from Fed policymakers Wednesday, a group 
of former central bankers warned of faster inflation and higher unemployment 
this year, and mainly expected no change in interest rates.

The survey 
<https://econ.duke.edu/news/new-survey-finds-former-federal-reserve-insiders-projecting-higher-inflation-unemployment>
, which included 28 former policymakers and staff members, showed a median 
forecast for the Fed’s preferred PCE price gauge of a 3% rise for this year. 
That’s above the Fed’s official 2% target and Fed policymakers’ 2.4% median 
prediction backin December <>. The survey, conducted by ex-reporter Jon 
Hilsenrath, now a visiting scholar at Duke University, also showed a median 
projection for unemployment of 4.6% at year-end, from 4.4% now.
Construction on the Marriner S. Eccles Federal Reserve building in Washington.

“Most respondents said the Fed would likely need to hold policy steady this 
year,” the survey showed. “Thirteen respondents said appropriate policy in 2026 
would likely be no change in rates, while six people said it would be 
appropriate to raise rates and seven said it would be appropriate to reduce 
rates.”
