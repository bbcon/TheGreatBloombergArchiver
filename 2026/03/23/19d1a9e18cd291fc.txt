Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the economy. Today, Simon 
Casey, managing editor for energy and commodities in the Americas, looks at 
what’s in store at CERAWeek as the Iran war continues to roil the global oil 
market.

Remember the energy trilemma 
<https://www.worldenergy.org/transition-toolkit/world-energy-trilemma-framework>
?

It was only few years back that the challenge of trying to balance security, 
affordability and sustainability seemed like a relevant way of thinking about 
energy in the 21st century.

You’re unlikely to find many of the 10,000 or so delegates in Houston this 
week looking at things in quite that way now.

For Dan Yergin <https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/1718179>, the 
tireless emcee and lead interviewer over the grueling five-day schedule of
CERAWeek by S&P Global <https://www.ceraweek.com/en>, everything changed in 
2022, thrusting geopolitics center-stage.

The “first act” was the Russian invasion of Ukraine, Yergin said last week. 
“Now, we’re in act two of the return of energy security.”
Dan Yergin pictured in May 2022.Photographer: Bloomberg
If the Ukraine war was a wake-up call for Europe, then the Iran conflict and 
the near-closure of the Strait of Hormuz is a global alert, flashing bright 
red. Thesupply disruption 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/iran-strikes-gulf-targets-as-trump-s-hormuz-deadline-approaches?cmpid=BBBXT032326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
 and surging energy prices are being felt practically everywhere, and even with 
US President Donald Trump’slatest about-turn 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/trump-backs-off-from-iran-threat-as-ceasefire-talks-get-underway?cmpid=BBBXT032326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
, there’s potential for things to get worse.

Needless to say, Iran will dominate the agenda in Houston. It has already 
prompted some last-minute changes.

On Sunday, it emerged that Saudi Aramco Chief Executive Officer Amin Nasser 
canceled plans to attend 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-22/aramco-ceo-withdraws-from-houston-event-to-deal-with-iran-war?cmpid=BBBXT032326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
 as a keynote speaker so he can focus on what’s happening at home. Other 
companies have dropped media appearances for their executives.

Yet Iran is by no means the only topic of conversation at CERAWeek.

For one thing, there’s the potential revival of Venezuela’s oil industry 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-22/venezuela-to-debate-oil-bill-allowing-private-sales-arbitration?cmpid=BBBXT032326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
. Back in January, it seemed likethat might dominate the conference. 

Then there’s the question of how to power the AI boom 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/half-of-americans-at-high-risk-of-power-shortfalls-amid-ai-boom?cmpid=BBBXT032326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
, something that appeared to be the biggest single challenge in energy before 
the Hormuz crisis began.

Plus, much of CERAWeek is given over to discussions on clean power and 
emerging technologies, including geothermal and nuclear fusion.

But as worthy as those things are, they’ll be overshadowed by the Middle East. 
As events of the past three weeks show, security trumps all other concerns.

—Simon Casey, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

The spread between gas contracts for the coming winter and summer 2027 at 
Europe’s Amsterdam hub rose by €2.51 a megawatt-hour week-on-week. It hit a 
record on March 19 after an Iranian missile strike damaged two liquefied 
natural gas trains in Qatar. That’ll reduce LNG production and affect both 
seasons, even if undamaged trains return to full output by next winter, 
according toBloombergNEF <>.
