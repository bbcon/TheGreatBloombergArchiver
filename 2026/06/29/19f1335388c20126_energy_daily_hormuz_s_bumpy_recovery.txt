Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asian oil team leader Serene Cheong looks at the status of shipping flows from 
the Persian Gulf after the weekend’s hostilities.

The swift resumption of oil flows out of the Persian Gulf has faltered after 
twoIranian attacks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-27/ship-struck-in-hormuz-as-naval-authorities-raise-threat-level?cmpid=BBBXT062926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=6015>
 on vessels in the Strait of Hormuz.

The brief hostilities — now halted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-28/us-strikes-iran-again-as-tit-for-tat-attacks-test-ceasefire?cmpid=BBBXT062926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=6015>
 — haven’t returned the waterway to the near-closure seen before the US-Iran 
peace deal. Visiblecommercial traffic persisted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-29/hormuz-traffic-drops-off-as-vessel-attacks-raise-fresh-concerns?cmpid=BBBXT062926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=6015>
 over the weekend at a reduced level, with some shipowners still making 
transits while openly broadcasting their location.

But confidence has been dented. Several vessels that recently abandoned 
crossings, including two laden very large crude carriers and an inbound 
Qatar-owned gas tanker, have not made fresh attempts.

Tensions rose in recent days as Washington and Tehran made new interpretations 
of their peace agreement, each of whichput them at odds 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-24/iran-us-make-conflicting-claims-about-key-issues-video?cmpid=BBBXT062926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=6015>
 with the other side.

The White House has claimed that the memorandum of understanding only allows 
Tehran to use unfrozen funds for the purchase of American agricultural goods. 
Iran is asserting much tighter control on traffic through the strait than the 
US has said it will tolerate.

The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps said on Sunday that “traffic control 
arrangements in the Strait of Hormuz are with Iran, and from now on, violating 
ships will be dealt with more strongly than before,” Press TV reported ina post 
on X <http://x.com/PressTV/status/2071031535650677158>.

Israeli attacks on Lebanon have also continued, contrary to another clause of 
the agreement.
A sign displays the prices of gasoline and diesel at a Chevron fuel station in 
Bay Harbor Island, Florida.Photographer: Zak Bennett/Bloomberg
Yet both sides also have very good reasons to avoid backsliding into a full 
closure.

For President Donald Trump, the price of gasoline can’t fall fast enough ahead 
of theUS midterm elections <>. For Iran, a resumption of the US Navy’s blockade 
of its ports would cut off a crucial source of revenue.

A fresh test of shipowners’ willingness to transit Hormuz may come soon. 

On Monday morning, the tanker Umm Al Ashtan, which loaded a liquefied natural 
gas shipment in the United Arab Emirates over the weekend, was heading to the 
western entrance of the strait, according to ship-tracking data compiled by 
Bloomberg.

Global markets will be watching closely. While few people expected the 
reopening of Hormuz to be smooth, just how much pain the bumps will cause 
remains uncertain.

—Serene Cheong, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Oil refining capacity is set to grow by 1.1 million barrels a day this year, 
according to BloombergNEF’s asset-level analysis, reversing a decline of 
690,000 barrels a day in 2025. Much of those gains are due in the fourth 
quarter when projects in India and China come online.
