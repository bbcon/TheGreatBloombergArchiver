The Strait of Hormuz remains the dominant fault line in global markets. Both the US and Iran are maintaining effective blockades of the waterway, with Washington conditioning any easing on receipt of a new Iranian proposal and Tehran refusing to negotiate while the US siege remains in place. Traffic through the strait ground to a halt after Iran fired on commercial ships and seized at least two vessels. The US military separately intercepted two Iranian oil supertankers attempting to evade its blockade. Brent crude rose above $104 per barrel, its fourth consecutive daily gain, and WTI was trading near $94. European natural gas futures also climbed. Persian Gulf and Asian sovereigns have sought currency swap lines with the US Treasury, reflecting the dollar's reinforced role in global trade under stress conditions.

Oil market analysts continue to debate how long strategic petroleum reserve releases and demand destruction can cushion the disruption, with a consensus that prolonged Hormuz closure would escalate supply consequences materially. Shell reported its Rotterdam refinery is operating at maximum throughput to produce jet fuel, as European airlines warn of a looming summer supply crunch.

The energy shock is producing a discernible second-order effect: acceleration of clean energy policy and investment. At the Petersberg Climate Dialogue in Berlin — the opening event in the road to COP31 in Turkey this November — delegates from nearly 40 countries framed the crisis as a catalyst for reducing fossil fuel dependence. Australia's climate minister articulated the strategic logic with characteristic directness, contrasting the 150 million kilometers solar energy travels from the sun with the 150 kilometers of the Strait of Hormuz. China's EV and battery exports more than doubled year-on-year in March, in part reflecting this demand shift.

On trade, approximately $300 billion in goods subject to US tariffs are estimated to be circumventing levies annually via transshipment through Southeast Asia and Mexico, according to Altana data showing a 76% year-on-year surge in suspect transactions in 2025. The pattern is sharpening USMCA renegotiation tensions, with USTR Greer signaling potential enforcement action against Canada over retaliatory trade measures.
