European equity markets recovered on Tuesday, with the Stoxx Europe 600 gaining 2.21% as oil prices retreated and Trump's remarks on a potential near-term end to the Iran conflict improved sentiment. The rebound followed a sharp session Monday when Brent briefly approached $120 during European trading hours, prompting a pause in primary bond issuance as borrowers stood down from planned debt sales.

European credit markets remain under visible strain. The iTraxx Europe investment-grade index reached its highest level since May, while the Crossover index of sub-investment-grade credits crossed 300 basis points for the first time since June. Bank of America analysts describe liquidity as "broadly supportive" but note a rising share of respondents reporting deteriorating conditions. An analysis by Goldman Sachs shows that nearly 150 European companies have already ceded control to direct-lending funds after failing to service their debts — a measure of how private credit stress is materializing on the continent. With corporate issuance delayed last week and again Monday, a pipeline of borrowers is waiting for a sufficiently stable window to access markets.

UK consumer sentiment fell to a four-month low in March according to Barclays data, reversing gains made at the start of the year as Middle East developments weighed on household confidence, even as British Retail Consortium figures showed modest retail sales growth in February. On a longer-duration European policy question, EU Commission President Ursula von der Leyen made pointed remarks calling nuclear energy abandonment a "strategic mistake," a thinly veiled reference to Germany's reactor closures, as energy security anxiety intensifies across the bloc.

Marcus Ashworth's analysis highlights that global deleveraging since the Iran war began has been particularly damaging for the euro, with markets now pricing the possibility of ECB rate tightening as early as July — a notable shift in expectations that reflects how quickly the inflationary oil shock is reshaping European monetary policy assumptions. Whether that repricing is durable depends heavily on how long supply disruptions persist.
