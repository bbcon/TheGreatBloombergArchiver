Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, oil 
reporter Grant Smith takes stock of the latest attacks in the Strait of Hormuz 
following a string of assaults on commercial shipping.

For the past few months, tanker shuttle runs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-08/aramco-adnoc-sneak-oil-through-hormuz-as-iran-menaces-strait?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 have constituted a lifeline in getting Persian Gulf oil to the world. That 
escape route just suffered a blow.

Two vessels linked to the United Arab Emirates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/two-uae-tankers-attacked-bringing-fresh-risk-to-hormuz-flows?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 were hit by projectiles while crossing the Strait of Hormuz today as the 
interim peace deal between the US and Iran effectively collapsed. The UAE’s 
Defense Ministry said they were targeted by Iranian cruise missiles.

The ships — the Al Bahyah and Mombasa B — are part of a fleet of shuttle 
tankers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-05/the-supertanker-tycoon-making-millions-on-hormuz-shuttle-runs?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 employed by the UAE and some of its neighbors to keep exports flowing since 
Iran declared Hormuz closed at the start of the war in February.

After collecting cargoes at Emirati ports such as Das Island or Zirku, the 
vessels sail through the chokepoint with their transponders switched off to 
avoid detection.

Once through, they typically transfer their cargoes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-25/adnoc-s-tankers-are-slipping-oil-gas-and-fuel-through-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 to clients’ tankers in safer waters off Fujairah or Sohar, or travel on to 
India’s west coast.
Oil tankers and other vessels anchored around Oman’s Qaboos port on June 22, 
2026.Photographer: Elke Scholiers/Getty Images Europe
Their stealthy trade helped prevent oil prices rocketing toward the 
$200-a-barrel levels feared at the outset of the war, and enabled the UAE to 
boost production to a record above4 million barrels a day 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/uae-s-oil-output-hit-record-high-in-june-iea-says?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 last month.

The latest attack exposes a particular vulnerability of the wartime oil supply 
chain, as the Mombasa B was on hire under a time-charter arrangement from 
tanker owner Sinokor Group.

The firm has been a critical mover 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-14/iran-war-supercharges-secretive-korean-tycoon-s-big-tanker-bet?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 of Emirati cargoes — in the process making millions for Ga-Hyun Chung, the 
intensely private Korean shipping tycoon at its center.

If Sinokor’s willingness to shoulder the risk of ferrying those barrels frays, 
then a key pillar in moving Middle East crude to international buyers frays too.

In the short term, world markets have been cushioned by a burst of supply from 
the Gulf and depressed buying from China. That’s leftAsia awash in crude 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/some-china-refiners-to-skip-saudi-term-oil-cargoes-next-month?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
, and compelled Saudi Arabia to offer price discounts for the first time in six 
years.

But Brent futures have already soared back above $85 a barrel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-july-14?cmpid=BBBXT071426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6526>
 and Middle East markets are strengthening. A hit to the crucial network of 
shuttlers stands to bolster that rally.

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News
