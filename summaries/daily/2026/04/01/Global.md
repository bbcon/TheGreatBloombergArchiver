The prospective end-state of the US-Iran conflict is generating as much market anxiety as its continuation. Trump's Tuesday comments that the US would exit within two to three weeks, without necessarily reopening the Strait of Hormuz, represent a significant strategic shift. If Iran retains effective control over the Strait as a condition of any peace settlement, it acquires a structural chokehold over global energy flows that no prior adversary has held — functioning, as one analyst noted, less like an oil producer and more like an involuntary central bank for global energy pricing. Brent crude, which reflects non-US supply dynamics, barely moved in response to Iran's peace overture, even as WTI fell sharply, illustrating the divergent exposure. The market's deep contango — futures pricing implies oil will revert significantly from current spot levels — reflects a belief that the supply shock is transitory, but that assumption may prove fragile if the Strait remains restricted post-conflict.

Polymarket probabilities now assign a 60% chance that the war concludes in April and only 20% that it extends beyond June, a sharp shift following Pezeshkian's remarks. However, the conditions Iran is understood to require for any settlement — including guarantees of regime survival and continued Hormuz influence — may prove difficult to formalize. Meanwhile, attacks continued across the region even as diplomatic signals multiplied, and US troop deployments to the area are ongoing.

Asia continues to scramble for alternative crude sources, with Russian oil seeing increased demand from war-affected regional buyers. Myanmar, operating under a military junta and experiencing civil war, is among the hardest-hit economies as gasoline supplies disappear. Oil futures curve dynamics and the asymmetric regional impact of the Hormuz closure are increasingly defining the macro divergence between the US and the rest of the world. The Russia-Ukraine conflict is also re-intensifying as spring conditions return to the battlefield, with diplomatic attention consumed by the Gulf crisis reducing pressure on Moscow. The Trump-Xi summit, postponed from an earlier date, is now targeted for mid-May; US Trade Representative Greer characterized the bilateral relationship as stable for the near term.
