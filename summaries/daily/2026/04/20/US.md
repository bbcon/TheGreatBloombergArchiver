US equity futures pulled back modestly on Monday, with S&P 500 futures off approximately 0.5% as a turbulent weekend in the Strait of Hormuz undercut the assumptions underpinning the market's recent historic rally. The S&P 500 had risen 12.33% in just 14 trading days following Iran's initial signals of willingness to negotiate, a pace of appreciation seen only four times this century — and the first such episode to bring the index to an all-time high rather than merely recovering from deeply oversold levels. Whether Monday marks the start of a reversal or another false alarm, as last Monday proved, remains to be seen.

The more consequential near-term catalyst for markets may arrive Tuesday, when Kevin Warsh appears before the Senate Banking Committee for his Fed chair confirmation hearing. Bond markets have been partially buoyed by the expectation that Warsh would favor looser monetary policy; any signal that he intends to look through the war-driven energy shock would likely reinforce wagers on rate cuts and support the short end of the Treasury curve. Any hawkish pivot on inflation, however, could trigger a reversal. Separately, Republican Senator Thom Tillis has pledged to block Warsh's confirmation until the Justice Department concludes its investigation into Jerome Powell, creating the possibility that Powell remains in his post beyond his May 15 term expiry.

On the corporate front, American Airlines explicitly rejected merger interest from United Airlines, dampening speculation of a transformative combination in the industry. QXO, the Brad Jacobs vehicle, announced a roughly $17 billion acquisition of insulation company TopBuild. Energy Secretary Chris Wright contradicted Treasury Secretary Bessent by stating US gasoline prices are unlikely to fall below $3 per gallon until next year, a notable divergence within the administration that may complicate the political narrative around energy relief. The SaaS sector remains under structural pressure, with the S&P Composite 1500 Software index having shed approximately 35% over the prior six months amid AI disruption fears, though some analysts argue the selloff overstates the near-term risk to incumbent business models.
