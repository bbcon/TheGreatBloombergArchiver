Taxing Times

Doubts over the future of Prime Minister Keir Starmer 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-22/britain-s-economy-turns-sour-just-as-starmer-had-a-reason-to-brag?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
 and jittery markets mean that hopefuls to replace him are touting policies 
that won’t spur borrowing.

That means more taxes. In particular, as we explain here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-25/starmer-leadership-strife-reignites-britain-s-wealth-tax-debate?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
, contenders to succeed the premier are talking about how to get rich voters to 
pay more.

Andy Burnham 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-22/burnham-vows-to-change-uk-economy-in-official-campaign-launch?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
, the betting markets’ favorite, believes the country has “overtaxed labor and 
undertaxed wealth.” He wants higher taxes on land.

His potential rival, Wes Streeting, is considered a more centrist option. But 
last week he called for a “wealth tax that works” and pledged to raise the rate 
on capital gains to at least that applied to income from working.

That idea isn’t new. Two opposition parties, the Liberal Democrats and the 
Green Party have previously supported such a policy.

In a perfect world, the change could raise as much as £10 billion ($13.4 
billion) a year while removing distortions and loopholes in the system.

But taxing wealth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-05-26/starmer-crisis-is-britain-ready-for-burnham-chancellor-ed-miliband?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
 is rarely so simple, and the problem with capital gains levies is that they 
depend on people choosing to cash.

Streeting believes the tax could raise £12 billion, citing research from the 
Centre for the Analysis of Taxation. In 2024, the LibDems estimated their own 
plan, including an allowance for inflation, would net just £5.2 billion.

The UK tax authority believes the opposite is true. It reckons a 10 percentage 
point increase in CGT rates would cost more than £6 billion across three years, 
as people chose not to cash in gains — so no tax would be liable.

The wealthy could choose to sit on investment and property gains in the hope 
that a more conservative government returns to power at the next election in 
2029 and reverses the policy.

The controversy points to the difficulty any UK government has in repairing 
Britain’sprecarious public finances 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-05-25/andy-burnham-is-being-underestimated-by-the-bond-markets?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
. National debt is approaching 100% of output, and worried investor keep on 
sending gilt yields higher. Meanwhile the UK tax burden is already at a post 
war high. So watch this space.

Need-to-Know Research

US inflation gauges are on the verge of seeing the end of the impact of Trump 
administration tariff hikes, according to Morgan Stanley economists, helping to 
damp price gains as the year wears on.

“The rate of increase in goods prices should decelerate in the coming months 
as pass-through is completed,” economists led by Michael Gapen wrote in a 
recent note. “Inflation payback from an end to tariff pass-through could mean a 
70-basis-point reduction in the year-on-yearrate of inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-22/economists-boost-us-inflation-forecast-push-out-fed-cut-on-war?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
 if these goods components move back to their pre-Liberation Day trend,” they 
wrote, referring to the April 2, 2026, tariff hikes.

Nodding to concerns that higher oil prices and AI-related demand will push US 
inflation up, the team said those pressures “would need to be stronger” than 
the disinflationary dynamic from the end of tariff hikes to keep price 
increases going at the current pace. Also, they see low probability ofnew 
tariff hikes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/trump-tariffs-revival-trade-investigations-launched-in-china-eu?cmpid=BBD052626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4781>
 ahead of the November congressional elections, given voters’ affordability 
worries.

The bank sees core PCE inflation at 2.8% year-on-year in the fourth quarter of 
this year — down from a 3.2% annual rate in March — and 2.3% in Q4 2027.
