• A gauge of Asian technology stocks fell 6%, the steepest drop since March 9, as AI-related selling spread across the region.

• Thailand's central bank governor signaled plans to curb buy-now-pay-later credit growth, citing risks to an economy already carrying one of Asia's highest household debt burdens.

**Equity Markets**
The AI trade, which has been the primary driver of Asian equity performance in 2026, went into sharp reverse. The Kospi's 10% decline was the most consequential move, given South Korea's role as a bellwether for global semiconductor demand. Samsung and SK Hynix — the two largest memory chip producers globally — each fell more than 12%, raising questions about whether the hyperscaler AI spending cycle that underpinned their re-rating is approaching a plateau. Separately, an SK Hynix ETF listed in Hong Kong saw assets swell to $17 billion, reflecting the scale of retail positioning now at risk. Chinese equities in Hong Kong entered bear market territory.

**Iran Oil and Regional Dynamics**
Iran's receipt of a 60-day US sanctions waiver set off an immediate scramble to re-engage Asian buyers. Sellers contacted Indian, Japanese, and South Korean refiners ahead of the formal license grant, though obstacles remain: EU and UK restrictions complicate insurance, dark-fleet vessels face port access issues, and Trump could revoke the waivers at any time. China's private refining sector — historically the dominant buyer of Iranian crude — is operating at a nine-year low utilization rate, limiting the immediate demand uplift for Tehran.
