Nvidia's earnings release dominated pre-market attention, with the chipmaker delivering a revenue forecast of approximately $78 billion that beat consensus expectations yet failed to ignite a sustained rally. Shares slid as much as 1.5% in after-hours trading before recovering marginally, a reaction that encapsulates the increasingly demanding bar set by AI-adjacent equities. The muted response extends a pattern visible across the software sector: Salesforce fell roughly 4% after its full-year outlook was judged underwhelming, while C3.ai tumbled 24% following a revenue guidance cut, and Trade Desk dropped 17% in pre-market on a weak forecast. The common thread is investor anxiety that AI infrastructure spending may crowd out legacy software revenues rather than lifting all boats — a tension that remains unresolved.

The broader S&P 500 has been range-bound for four months. Options market structure is now reflecting elevated caution, with the put-call skew on the index rising to a two-year high last week and normalized two-month skew approaching the upper end of its five-year range. Some strategists interpret extreme positioning as a contrarian setup, though the fundamental picture — tariff uncertainty, the aftermath of the Supreme Court ruling on global levies, and a Fed leadership transition — provides ample reason for the hedging.

On monetary policy, discussions at the National Association for Business Economics conference centered almost entirely on AI's labor market implications. Fed Governor Lisa Cook offered a sober assessment, suggesting the economy may be approaching the most significant reorganization of work in generations, and that the Fed may lack the usual tools to offset AI-driven unemployment since elevated joblessness would not necessarily signal conventional economic slack. Richmond Fed President Tom Barkin echoed similar caution, noting that benchmark rate policy cannot resolve the structural adjustments AI will impose. Separately, Deutsche Bank analysts sketched potential reforms under incoming Fed Chair nominee Kevin Warsh, including revisions to the dot plot framework and a possible shift toward rolling 12- and 24-month rate horizons rather than year-end projections.
