The defining development across global markets this week is the continued effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz, now entering its third week, with no credible near-term resolution in sight. Brent crude is above $105 per barrel, up more than 40% since the conflict began. The IEA has characterized the disruption as the largest oil supply shock in recorded history by volume, though longer-dated futures — Brent for five years hence remains below $70 — suggest markets still price the closure as temporary. That view may be increasingly difficult to sustain.

The game theory has shifted against a quick resolution. Prediction markets now put the probability of a ceasefire by May 31 below 50%. Iran appears committed to maintaining the Strait's closure as its primary leverage instrument, and Trump's public demands that NATO allies and China contribute naval assets to reopen the waterway have so far been rebuffed: Australia ruled out deploying vessels, Japan's defense minister said there are no plans to send warships, and China — whose carriers appear to still be transiting selectively — has condemned the war without committing to intervention. Trump threatened to delay his end-of-month summit with Xi Jinping if Beijing does not assist.

The macro consequences are broadening. Goldman Sachs economists assessed that broader supply-chain disruption comparable to 2021-22 is unlikely, as non-energy Gulf trade represents only 1% of global commerce. However, the energy shock alone is sufficient to materially complicate central bank deliberations across 18 policy meetings this week, covering approximately two-thirds of the global economy. The week's central bank calendar is historically dense: Australia on Tuesday, the Fed and Brazil on Wednesday, the ECB, Bank of Japan, Bank of England, and several others on Thursday. All are expected to hold, but the tone will be notably more cautious. Tungsten prices have more than doubled this year amid Chinese export controls and rising military demand, illustrating how the conflict is amplifying pre-existing commodity tensions.
