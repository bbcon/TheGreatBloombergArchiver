**US Daily Briefing — January 7, 2026**

Coverage in today's flow is thin on core US macro and rates developments, with the day's most actionable item centering on Venezuela and its implications for US capital markets, energy, and foreign policy.

**Venezuela Reopens to US Capital.** The removal of Nicolás Maduro, who pleaded not guilty this week to US narco-terrorism charges, has triggered a rapid repricing of Venezuelan exposure across US-accessible markets. Sovereign and PDVSA bondholders captured roughly $4 billion in mark-to-market gains in a single session, with the move predicated on the prospect of an eventual debt restructuring rather than near-term cash flows. President Trump signaled that the US would commit billions to rehabilitating Venezuela's degraded oil infrastructure, a stance that appears designed to channel private capital and US energy majors into reconstruction. The combination of distressed-debt opportunities, upstream investment, and services contracts has drawn renewed engagement from Wall Street desks and private capital sponsors that had treated the credit as effectively untouchable since the 2017 sanctions regime tightened.

**Implications and Caveats.** For US energy markets, a credible path to restoring Venezuelan heavy crude output — even on a multi-year horizon — could weigh on the long end of the oil curve and benefit Gulf Coast refiners configured for heavy grades. For credit, the rally compresses the distressed-sovereign opportunity set but opens a potentially sizable restructuring mandate pipeline. Execution risk remains substantial: the political transition is not consolidated, sanctions relief mechanics have not been formalized, and the scale of required capex on Venezuelan fields is large relative to realistic near-term production recovery. Whether private capital can deploy at the pace Washington is signaling remains to be seen.
