Keir Starmer's political position deteriorated further on Tuesday as former top official Olly Robbins testified to a public inquiry that Downing Street was "dismissive" of concerns raised during Peter Mandelson's vetting process ahead of his appointment as UK ambassador to Washington. Mandelson's well-documented ties to convicted sex offender Jeffrey Epstein were at the center of the controversy, and Starmer's decision to dismiss the Foreign Office's permanent secretary has triggered what amounts to an open revolt by senior civil servants — a damaging and distracting conflict at a moment when global crises demand coherent diplomatic capacity. Starmer's credibility appears to be eroding incrementally rather than acutely, but the trajectory is concerning for a government that entered office promising institutional restoration.

On the macroeconomic front, UK inflation accelerated in March, with the surge in energy costs tied to the Hormuz closure feeding through to consumer prices. Research published this week suggests the Iran conflict could erase most of Chancellor Rachel Reeves's fiscal buffer, compounding an already narrow margin. In Germany, the Bundesbank estimated slight first-quarter GDP expansion, but the ZEW investor expectations index collapsed to -17.2 in April from -0.5 in March — its lowest level in more than three years and well below the consensus estimate of -5.8 — signaling that business confidence is deteriorating rapidly as the war weighs on Europe's industrial core. European bank lending conditions are also reportedly tightening under war-dented demand, adding to medium-term growth concerns.

Hungary's incoming leader Péter Magyar has made euro adoption a top priority, which would represent the eurozone's largest single expansion since Greece joined in 2001. The ambition is real, but the timeline is long: Viktor Orbán's 16-year legacy of sluggish growth leaves the budget deficit well outside Maastricht parameters, and the convergence process typically spans several years under the best of conditions.
