**Iran Conflict and Energy Markets**
The week's dominant global theme was the tension between renewed military escalation and nascent diplomacy. US strikes on Iranian targets on consecutive days mid-week marked an effective breakdown of the April ceasefire, yet crude markets absorbed the news with notable composure — Brent remained below $100 throughout a conflict now exceeding 100 days. Three factors appear to explain the relative stability: China drawing on commercial reserves rather than competing aggressively for spot cargoes, rising Hormuz transit signals suggesting limited flows, and market confidence that the conflict will not escalate to full-scale regional war. That confidence is an assumption, not an established fact, and constitutes the principal tail risk to the global macro outlook. Iranian crude exports to China have fallen from approximately 1.8 million barrels per day in February to around 160,000 barrels per day in May — acute fiscal pressure that may be accelerating Tehran's willingness to negotiate.
