After I passed through security to enter the Blue House presidential complex 
in Seoul more than a decade ago, a high-definition television screen caught my 
eye. It was broadcasting a live image of a South Korean flag flying over an 
islet in the Sea of Japan. And it spoke to a fierce dispute at the time with 
Japan.

Then-President Lee Myung Bak had steadily increased 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2012-08-10/south-korea-s-lee-to-visit-islands-at-center-of-japan-dispute?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 his nation’s presence on the contested Dokdo islands (known as Takeshima in 
Japan), sparking Tokyo’s ire. Economic and financial ties weredamaged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2012-10-09/japan-south-korea-currency-swap-to-lapse-amid-tensions-1-?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
. In August 2012, the tensions became farcical when Lee sent back to Tokyo a 
letter from then-Prime Minister Yoshihiko Noda, and Japan’s Foreign Ministry 
simplyrefused to accept <> the returned item — leaving it in limbo presumably 
forever after.

How things have changed. Current South Korean President Lee Jae Myung touts “
future-oriented cooperation <bbg://news/stories/T4XWXMGP9VCW>,” after having 
accused Japan earlier in his career of showing too little contrition for its 
occupation of Korea, which ended 81 years ago. It used to be that bilateral 
relations were particularly vulnerable when a right-wing politician was in 
charge of Japan. But the current prime minister, Sanae Takaichi, fits that 
description, and that hasn’t stopped anewfound bonhomie 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-13/japan-s-takaichi-courts-south-korea-with-china-rift-deepening?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 between the two nations.

Lee and Takaichi “have proven to be pragmatists rather than ideologues, 
prioritizing national interest over historical grievances,” says Jinwan Park, a 
nonresident fellow at the European Centre for North Korean Studies at the 
University of Vienna.

What’s helped bring Japan and South Korea together — Asia’s No. 2 and No. 4 
economies, respectively, and both longstanding US allies — is a complex 
combination of forces emanating from both Beijing and Washington. As outlined 
below, it’s one important example of “middle powers” increasingly seeking 
solidarity in a new era of profound uncertainty.

The legacy of Japan’s 1910-45 occupation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-04-17/trump-needs-them-to-be-friends-but-japan-and-south-korea-feud?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 of the Korean Peninsula — during which Korean women were put in Japanese 
military brothels and men were forced to work in factories or mines — hasn’t 
disappeared. Tokyo’s stance has long been that past reparations and expressions 
of regret, along with official agreements, ought to allow the neighbors to move 
on.

Actions on both sides in recent decades fed residual resentment. Add to that 
tensions over the contested Dokdo/Takeshima Islands, and the irritants proved 
serious enough, until recently, to hamper economic and diplomatic ties. In 
2019, during US President Donald Trump’s first term, bilateral trade between 
Japan and South Korea wasplunging 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-09-17/south-korea-removes-japan-from-trade-whitelist-as-feud-deepens?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
.

But during the subsequent US administration, President Joe Biden sought to 
reconcile the two nations. Each hosts American military bases and tens of 
thousands of US personnel, serving as essential security anchors for Washington 
in the western Pacific. Biden hosted his counterparts at alandmark summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-19/five-takeaways-from-the-us-japan-and-south-korea-summit?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 in August 2023 at the Camp David presidential retreat in Maryland.

What’s notable is that the bilateral rapprochement has survived leadership 
changes in both Seoul and Tokyo — not to mention Washington. Lee, who became 
South Korea’s president last June, quickly described the two countries as 
inseparable, and said the Dokdo/Takeshima issue could be compartmentalized. 
“While fighting with our right hand, we hold our left hands. So I think we need 
aflexible yet reasonable 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-03/lee-vows-to-focus-on-reviving-south-korea-s-sputtering-economy?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 approach,” Lee said last July.

One byproduct of the Camp David initiative was the establishment of the 
US-ROK-Japan Next-Gen Study Group, which is designed to bring together emerging 
leaders from the three nations to regularly discuss issues from defense to 
economic security. This year’s gathering isin Honolulu 
<https://kr.usembassy.gov/030626-2026-young-trilateral-leaders-summit/>.
Joe Biden, center, greets Fumio Kishida, right, and Yoon Suk Yeol, left,

Rintaro Nishimura, one of the co-founders of the group, representing Japan, 
says China “has been a major underlying factor for Seoul and Tokyo’s interest 
in improving relations.”

While President Xi Jinping’s government has regularly sought its own 
trilateral relationship 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-05-27/china-japan-and-south-korea-start-first-summit-talks-since-2019?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 with Japan and South Korea, it’s a tough ask given both countries have been 
targets of Beijing’s economic coercion. Indeed, Japanese companies are 
currently operating withlimited access 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/china-puts-export-curb-on-japan-firms-including-mitsubishi-heavy?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 to Chinese rare earths and other key items.

Nishimura, a senior associate at strategic advisory firm the Asia Group, says 
the growing China-Russia-North Korea military partnership also “poses 
challenges for both countries — a threat on the Korean Peninsula for Seoul and 
an increasingly hostile neighborhood for Japan.”

Park, another co-founder of the group, says, “Faced with growing threats from 
North Korea and China, both leaders have calculated that cooperation in 
security, economy and technology is a strategic imperative that outweighs 
domestic populist gains from stoking historical or territorial tensions.”

A further key source of pressure: Trump, who has slapped sanctions on both 
nations and pressured them to pledge giant USinvestment commitments 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/how-tariff-threats-got-japan-south-korea-to-promise-billions-in-us-investments?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
: $550 billion in Japan’s case and $350 billion in South Korea’s. He’s also 
raised questions about the American security commitments to both, highlighting 
their cost. He’s even pressed them to help with the war with Iran.

“The Trump administration has unquestionably pushed Japan and Korea together 
by casting aside alliance unity for a transactional relationship that knows no 
boundaries between friends or foe,” says Nishimura. Park adds: “A more 
‘passive’ or unpredictable United States in the Indo-Pacific has necessitated a 
more ‘active’ Korea-Japan partnership.”

Both anticipate a steady deepening in economic and security relations between 
South Korea and Japan. Just last week, their finance ministers agreed to boost 
economic cooperation in areas such as their bilateral currency swap 
arrangement. That safety line for financial liquidity, currently at $10 
billion, had been one of the casualties of past enmity.

The new tone of pragmatism between the two nations “is mirrored in public 
sentiment,” Park notes. One recent survey showed Koreans’ favorability ratings 
of Japan surpassed 56% —a record high <https://www.mk.co.kr/news/world/11956915>
. Amajority of Japanese 
<https://japannews.yomiuri.co.jp/politics/politics-government/20250625-265822/> 
also have positive sentiments toward South Korea. That suggests “leaders no 
longer feel domestic pressure to adopt antagonistic stances,” Park says.

And in the context of Trump and Xi each upending previous norms 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2023-12-23/how-china-and-cold-war-ii-are-upending-the-international-order?cmpid=BBD032126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260321>
 in international relations, it’s also good to have a friend next door. —Chris 
Anstey <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AF7uNtxCFaA/chris-anstey>
