Japan’s Wages

A South Korean central banker once told me that it’s important to keep tabs on 
what happens inJapan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-02/former-boj-chief-economist-sees-likelihood-of-april-rate-hike?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 because “it’s a living museum of our future” — the first major advanced 
economy to go through demographic decline.

With that in mind, Japan’s shift in recent years from deflation to inflation 
is worthy of examination. A big part of that, of course, is large-scale 
depreciation of the yen, which pushed up prices of things Japan imports, 
especially energy.

But that’s not the whole story. A key part of Japan’s “deflationary mindset 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2014-10-31/kuroda-jolts-markets-with-assault-on-deflation-mindset?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
” was trenchant wage declines. From the 1990s to early 2010s, the thinking was 
firms had to just keep tightening their belts to reflect poor opportunities for 
growth. Falling wages then made growth prospects all the worse, in a vicious 
cycle.

As Japan’s population started to contract, some economists hypothesized 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2014-03-06/japan-s-muted-wages-belie-start-of-hottest-job-market-since-70s?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 that, at some point, things would flip. Wages would have to rise because 
that’s what it would take for employers to attract the dwindling number of job 
seekers.

Last November, Federal Reserve Governor Stephen Miran even proposed 
<https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=tKwBb2NwXzo> that Japan showed a two-stage 
process to declining demographics. In stage one, “the demand side dominated,” 
he said. In other words, a smaller and older Japanese population meant spending 
declines and thus price declines.

“Now, potentially they may have turned the corner and the inflation process in 
Japan seems to have changed,” he said. This is when a negative labor-supply 
shock would “push wages higher and be inflationary.”

Wages now are indeed gaining pace. The latest release showed the biggest 
base-pay increase inmore than 33 years 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/japan-s-real-wages-advance-for-first-time-in-13-months?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
. Last month, Japan’s main labor union group secured an average wage rise of 
more than 5% for athird straight year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/japan-s-key-labor-union-wins-wage-hike-topping-5-for-third-year?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 in spring negotiations known as the Shunto.

It would be easy to conclude this is mainly a function of workers having 
greater bargaining power in a nation whose population just contractedalmost 
900,000 <https://www3.nhk.or.jp/nhkworld/en/news/20260226_13/> last year. But 
there’s more to it than that. Despite there being fewer Japanese, there are 
actually more in the labor force, which has climbed by over 1 million the past 
three years.

Total employment is rising, not falling, thanks in part to the legacy of the 
late Prime Minister Shinzo Abe, who pushed to raisewomen’s participation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2018-09-19/what-is-womenomics-and-is-it-working-for-japan-quicktake?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 rates andloosen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-02-26/japan-has-a-new-guest-worker-program-just-don-t-call-it-an-immigration-policy?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 Japan’s notoriously tight strictures on immigration. (The foreign resident 
population climbedalmost 10% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-27/japan-s-chinese-population-continues-to-grow-amid-sour-ties?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 last year.)

Along with more women and foreign workers, employers have been embracing older 
staff staying on forlonger before retirement 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/japan-s-biggest-bank-plans-to-keep-its-aging-workers-for-longer?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
, which also lifts total pay because those workers tend to earn more. It all 
means Japan’s wage increases contain at least some element of dynamism — 
showing there’s much for others to learn in this “living museum.”

Need-to-Know Research

At the start of the year, wrote about a big switch 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-12/as-trump-aims-to-lure-investment-back-to-america-china-is-stepping-up-spending?cmpid=BBD040226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260402>
 in global capital flows as the US seeks to lure investment and China becomes a 
more important source of capital for economies across the globe. A new report 
from Oxford Economics dived into 2025 data to highlight some interesting trends.

Last year, the top destination for China ODI was no longer Asia, but Latin 
America. It found that Chinese FDI into communications infrastructure surged to 
make up 41% of the total, again overwhelmingly benefitting Latin America.

“In 2025, Chinese firms appear to have rapidly pivoted, reallocating capital 
both geographically and into tech infrastructure amid tariff uncertainty and 
AI-driven investment opportunities,” economists Adam Ahmad Samdin and Jun Hao 
Ng wrote. “The speed and scale of this shift suggest a structural break, rather 
than a purely cyclical response, with few precedents over the past two decades.”
