A Fright in the Bond Markets <https://bloom.bg/3RkhRFg> 

It’s a while since we’ve been here, and it’s not a good place to be. The yield 
on 30-year Treasury bonds rose Tuesday to 5.19% for the first time since June 
2007:

A similar headline appeared on a piece I wrote with my colleague Michael 
Mackenzie about that 2007 landmark for the Financial Times, our employer at the 
time, and it makes uncomfortably relevant reading today. The concern was that 
rates at these levels might burst higher, bringing down the then-booming stock 
market and puttingunbearable pressure on credit markets 
<https://bloom.bg/4fwShH5>. As I argued <https://bloom.bg/3RQHrlj>:

Money just seems too cheap. Bond yields are not high by historical standards, 
but the suddenness of their move might dislodge the financing that underpins 
stocks. If that scenario turns out to be true — and it is still too early to 
say that it will — the bond selloff might presage the end of an era for stocks.

I hedged by saying that it was too early to tell, but that bond selloff did 
indeed signal the end of an era for equities. Within weeks, subprime credit was 
selling, two Bear Stearns hedge funds collapsed, and the long spiral into what 
we now call the Global Financial Crisis had begun.

Frankly, the question today is exactly the same. The earnings now produced by 
US companies are impressive (just like banks’ earnings in 2007), but will 
financial conditions let this continue?

Now as then, equities are roaring despite the warnings from bonds. The latest 
Bank of America survey <https://bloom.bg/4f2CZtD> of global fund managers shows 
that they had increased theirequity allocations <https://bloom.bg/3PDu8UC> last 
month by the most in history:

Now as then, they’re buying stocks despite concerns about the economy. 
Remarkably, most fund managers are worried about stagflation in the next 12 
months. This question asked them which of four economic conditions was most 
likely:

I couldn’t find the BofA survey for June 2007, but Gemini offered a summary 
that it “captured a period of peak economic optimism and robust risk appetite, 
right before the initial tremors of the subprime mortgage crisis… [It] 
reflected a global economy in its late-cycle expansion where institutional 
investors heavily favored equities over cash and fixed income.”

It’s concerning that corporate balance sheets were “perceived as healthy” on 
the eve of an epochal credit crisis. Then as now, there was optimism about 
corporate profits. 

There are differences. The Fed was on hold at the top of a tightening cycle 
then; it’s now on a temporary hold during a loosening cycle, but appears likely 
to have to hike before it cuts. Government debt has risen in round numbers to 
120% of gross domestic product from 60% then, making higher interest rates much 
more of a problem for Uncle Sam. And while 19 years ago the Iraq war had become 
a miserable fact of life, investors are still assimilating what is going on in 
Iran; it’s shifted expectations for oil prices and financial conditions sharply 
upward:

And stocks were cheaper the last time bond yields were this high. At that 
point, the cyclically adjusted price/earnings multiple, as calculated by Robert 
Shiller of Yale University, was 27.4; it’s now 39.6. It’s only ever been higher 
than now for the top 20 months of the internet bubble, which burst in March 
2000.

The combination of equities like 2000 and bonds like 2007 is unnerving. The 
big difference is that this time, there is a genuine force powering positively 
through the economy — the artificial intelligence revolution. Nothing remotely 
comparable was afoot in 2007.

If AI delivers as hoped, people could live to think that stocks were fairly 
valued in May 2026. But much is expected of AI. Which brings us to Nvidia…

It’s Nvidia’s Time

An epic earnings season is set for its great operatic encore. Nvidia Corp. 
steps up after trading on Wednesday with the ultimate numbers on the durability 
of the gravity-defying AI trade. Thanks to thehyperscalers’ splurge on AI 
datacenters <https://bloom.bg/4dAMgGG>, the world’s biggest chipmaker has 
consistently posted healthy margins. That trajectory won’t change as long as 
mega caps keep building. But it’s difficult to tell how much longer Nvidia’s 
reign can last:

Wednesday’s earnings should offer a glimpse into the steam left in its engine, 
which cannot run in perpetuity. Analysts project revenue to surge 79% to $78.9 
billion, with datacenter revenues representing roughly 90% of that growth.

Where to next? Restoring broad access to the Chinese market might cement 
Nvidia’s dominance. Its chief executive, Jensen Huang, accompanied President 
Donald Trump to China last week, and saysaccess to Beijing is within reach 
<https://bloom.bg/4eYeFcb>. That would build on Trump’s December decision 
<https://bloom.bg/4dq8aMI> to allow the sale of H200 chips to Chinese firms 
<https://bloom.bg/4eWzIvR>:

This doesn’t mean that China is a low-hanging fruit. Jay Woods of Freedom 
Capital Markets notes that restoring access to China is far more complicated. 
Despite recent reports that about 10 Chinese firms were granted access to buy 
H200 chips, no deals have been reached so far:

On top of that, the deal structure itself creates confusion. There is a 25% 
revenue cut and a requirement that chips pass through a US lab, which could 
delay or complicate transactions. This could give the Chinese more reason to 
justify blocking any deals.

Further, US-imposed restrictions have fast-tracked domestic ingenuity, with 
Huawei and others poised to fill the gap. The Chinese company expectsAI chip 
revenue to climb dramatically <https://bloom.bg/3PMO9s5> with its latest Ascend 
model entering mass production. This may mean China’s domestic competitors can 
move on without Nvidia, Woods cautions.

Beyond that, there’s broad confidence that Nvidia can continue its remarkable 
outperformance since ChatGPT jumpstarted the AI trade four years ago. It has 
beaten earnings expectations in 18 of its last 20 reports and exceeded revenue 
estimates in 19, suggesting a high but not insurmountable bar. Clearing it will 
depend on hyperscalers’ outlays:

Synovus Trust’s Daniel Morgan notes that capex projections have recently 
reached $812 billion and $968 billion for 2026 and 2027, led by higher 
forecasts from Microsoft Corp. and Meta Platforms Inc. Among the top four 
players alone, the splurge has already been revised up 89% year-to-date. This 
unrestrained outlay benefits Nvidia because of its exceptional network effects, 
with its chips still regarded as peerless. It’s not clear that this guarantees 
leadership in the semiconductor business for the long term.

HSBC’s Frank Lee explains that over the past five years, all major Nvidia 
stock price movements have been led by a combination of its evolving AI product 
roadmap along with consistent “beat and raise” results. This leadership is not 
without blemish.

Lee points out that since last year’s buzz around sovereign AI 
<https://bloom.bg/4uWkF9U> and neoclouds <https://bloom.bg/4dBqFhB>, no new 
narrative has emerged. Nvidia’s shares have underperformed thePhiladelphia 
Stock Exchange Semiconductor Index <https://bloom.bg/4eRLtUd> (SOX) over the 
last six months despite having twoGPU technology conferences 
<https://bloom.bg/4uXhyyH>, and twice publishing financial results that beat 
estimates and raised expectations. The AI trade has broadened from GPU makers 
to SOX index companies in networking, memory chips and power management:

The other key risk is to watch whether hyperscalers’ use of proprietary chips 
<https://bloom.bg/4u4WOV3> can dent Nvidia’s forecasts. For Lee, absent another 
compelling narrative, earnings momentum and Nvidia’s upcoming Vera Rubin and 
Rubin Ultra roadmap might no longer work as catalysts for a significant rise in 
the share price.

—Richard Abbey


Capex

The Nvidia excitement highlights a strange development: Investors have decided 
they like companies makingcapital expenditures <https://bloom.bg/4tL8Wdw>. It 
wasn’t always thus. Generally, shareholders prefer firms to pay cash to them 
through dividends or buybacks, to ensure they don’t waste it on vain expansion 
projects.

This is the established theory of shareholder value, and it’s borne out 
empirically. Companies whose capex most exceeds the amount they write down in 
depreciation have, over history, badly underperformed the overall US index, as 
illustrated with Bloomberg data by StoneX Capital’s Vincent Deluard:

The cumulative underperformance by the 10% of companies who do the most capex 
over the last 20 years comes to 2.2% per year. Deluard adds that while S&P 500 
profit margins rose 1.7 percentage points in the past two years to 14%, a key 
factor in the current bullishness, “free cash flow margins, which incorporate 
capital expenditures, reverted to their pre-Covid average of 9.5%.” Those 
investments had better pay off.

Even now, the BofA survey shows that fund managers would prefer companies to 
return cash, or batten down their balance sheets. In the decade after the GFC, 
they wanted them to invest to boost growth that was sorely lacking — but these 
days, a plurality would prefer to see executives tending to their balance 
sheets:

While equity investors like capex, bond investors understandably don’t. 
Pimco’s Lotfi Karoui <https://bloom.bg/42G6VEt> looked at the option-adjusted 
spread of hyperscalers’ bonds compared to Treasuries, and compared it to 
spreads for other investment-grade companies. Hyperscalers traded at spreads 
some 15% tighter than the rest last year, but now contend with spreads 25% 
wider — a big shift suggesting credit market disquiet.

Channeling the Nobelist Robert Merton’s insight that shareholders are “long a 
call on the company’s assets” while “bondholders are short a put on those same 
assets,” Karoui argues:

A leveraged bet on AI infrastructure with uncertain and potentially volatile 
payoffs tends to raise the value of the equity call even as it makes the 
bondholders’ implicit put more risky. In Merton terms, asset values may be 
increased, but so is the strike price of the put. For shareholders, the upside 
justifies the gamble. For bondholders, the downside is real and the upside 
belongs to someone else.

Bondholders should be respected — by the entire global equity market, and 
specifically by the hyperscalers currently spending unimaginable sums on Nvidia 
chips.
