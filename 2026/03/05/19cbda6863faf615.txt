 Euro vulnerability 



The war in the Middle East is reviving an old vulnerability in Europe — when 
energy gets expensive,the euro tends to pay the price <https://bloom.bg/40JILrp>
. The common currency has dropped about 2% against the dollar since the US and 
Israel attacked Iran.

Europe’s dependence on imported energy means higher commodity prices deliver a 
hit to the region’s growth and purchasing power. It’s this dynamic that helped 
drag the euro below parity during the 2022 energy crisis.

The euro slump is just one of the many ways the war has upended markets. Such 
moves in currencies are perhaps the purest barometers of investors’ shifting 
assessment of risks from the conflict.

Take the Canadian dollar, which is the flip side of the euro trade. The loonie 
has become a top pick in the $9.5-trillion-a-day foreign exchange market this 
week thanks to Canada’s status as world’s fourth-largest crude producer.

And then there’s the US dollar, which is headed for its biggest weekly gain in 
more than a year, as measured by a Bloomberg index that tracks the greenback 
against a basket of 10 currencies.

The US also is a powerhouse oil producer, better able to weather a surge in 
crude prices. As we noted yesterday, that’sprompting investors to give up 
<https://bloom.bg/4lkGCfp> on the “Sell America, Buy Asia” trade that had 
dominated this year.

“Capital doesn’t wait for certainty — it’s already rotating, and the dollar’s 
strength this week tells you everything about where the smart money is 
heading,” said Hebe Chen, senior market analyst at Vantage Global Prime. —Phil 
Serafino, Vassilis Karamanis, Winnie Hsu and Miles J. Herszenhorn
