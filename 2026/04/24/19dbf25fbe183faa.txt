Ennui in Energy

Economists have largely expected US growth to prove resilient this year in the 
face of the energy crisis triggered by the American-Israeli war with Iran. 
After all, the US producesmore energy 
<https://www.eia.gov/energyexplained/us-energy-facts/imports-and-exports.php> 
than it consumes, and the oil-price surge should help propel that sectorтАЩs 
expansion.

An unusual survey 
<https://www.dallasfed.org/research/surveys/des/2026/2601/2601update#tab-questions>
 released by the Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas on Thursday raises some 
questions, however.

In the run-up to the US central bankтАЩs policy meeting next week, the Dallas 
Fed asked energy firms in its oil-rich district about the Iran war, as a 
follow-up to its regular quarterly poll. A key takeaway was that a minority of 
respondents anticipate a ramping up of hiring and production тАФ even though the 
vast majority expect the Strait of Hormuz to remain closedat least until August 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-23/shale-bosses-see-chaos-from-iran-war-in-anonymous-fed-survey?cmpid=BBD042426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
.

Some 59% of the 120 oil and gas companies see payrolls at year-end being the 
same as they were at the end of 2025, with only 4% projecting a significant 
increase and 28% a slight one. Considering that non-energy sectors of the 
economy face the risk of layoffs if energy costs remain high, those results may 
sow some angst about the broader US job market.

Most also see limited gains in US oil output this year, with 43% expecting a 
rise of less than 250,000 barrels a day (a figure that works out to a 1.8% 
increase from last weekтАЩs 13.6 million production figure) and 30% seeing no 
boost at all.

So, how come no wave of enthusiasm about high oil prices providing a big 
opportunity for expansion? Some of theanonymously recorded comments 
<https://www.dallasfed.org/research/surveys/des/2026/2601/2601update#tab-comments>
 speak to that.

тАЬThe geopolitical events are too chaotic to provide any degree of certainty to 
commodity pricing,тАЭ one respondent said. Another offered: тАЬEven after nearly a 
month of oil above $90 per barrel, rig counts declined, signaling little 
confidence that prices will hold.тАЭ

And one executive summed it up as: тАЬWith all of the chaos, predicting anything 
in the energy sector is very difficult.тАЭ

US economic growth potential picked up notably after Covid, propelled by an 
acceleration in productivity, and the coming few years should see the pace 
sustained by AI, according to Goldman Sachs.

тАЬWe expect US potential growth to average around 2.3% over 2026тАУ2028,тАЭ Goldman 
economist Pierfrancesco Mei wrote in a note this week. тАЬA significant boost 
from AIтАЭ will helpproductivity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/us-productivity-growth-exceeds-projections-after-upward-revision?cmpid=BBD042426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
 rise 2% annually, he wrote. American productivity outperformance also explains 
why the US GDP potential surpasses that of other major developed nations, Mei 
wrote.

Labor-force growth will contribute 0.3 percentage point to annual gains in 
potential GDP, Goldman calculates. Of that, immigration will account for less 
than 0.1 percentage point тАФ down from roughly 1 percentage point during the 
2022-24 surge of foreign migrants, and also lower than the 0.3 percentage point 
contribution over the 2010-19 period, the bank says. An aging of the US 
population will also depress labor force expansion in coming years, Mei wrote.
