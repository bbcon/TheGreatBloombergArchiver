The defining macro tension of the moment is the simultaneity of an acute energy supply shock and unresolved geopolitical uncertainty. Brent crude traded near $108 a barrel on March 26 and is on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990, with the near-total shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of global oil supply transits — driving what the International Chamber of Commerce described as potentially "the worst industrial crisis in living memory." BloombergNEF's base case sees Brent averaging $106.70 in April; a delayed Hormuz reopening until mid-June would push that average to $154.40. Iran, meanwhile, appears to be a net financial beneficiary: as the only major exporter currently able to utilize the strait freely, Tehran has reportedly earned hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental oil revenue since hostilities began, effectively converting the disruption into strategic and fiscal leverage.

Diplomatically, the gap between Washington and Tehran remains wide. The White House describes Iran as seeking an exit ramp; Iran publicly rejects US overtures and is drafting legislation to impose passage fees on vessels transiting the strait. Former MI6 chief Alex Young assessed that the US "lost the initiative to Iran about two weeks ago." Prediction markets place the probability of a ceasefire by end of April at no better than 50-50. A suspected sea-drone attack on a crude tanker in the Black Sea added a further dimension to energy market risk.

The OECD, the first major institution to formally revise its forecasts, left its 2026 global growth projection unchanged at 2.9% while trimming 2027 by 0.1 percentage point to 3.0%, noting the conflict arrived just as AI investment, tariff normalization, and accommodative policy were generating genuine upside. G20 average inflation is now expected at 4.0% versus 2.8% in December. Central banks are pivoting away from rate cuts, and the OECD warned that "monetary policy adjustments may be needed if price pressures broaden." The structural response from consumers — accelerating EV adoption, rooftop solar, and electrification of cooking in India — may prove durable, though it is unlikely to offset the near-term coal reversion across energy-stressed Asia.
