**Tariff Architecture in Transition**

The defining US macro development across March was a wholesale restructuring of the trade policy framework. The Supreme Court's invalidation of IEEPA-based tariffs mid-month, followed by the administration's rapid pivot to a 10% global levy under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, left an estimated $170 billion in contested duties the subject of more than 1,500 refund lawsuits filed by mid-month. Legal scholars broadly questioned whether the Section 122 threshold — requiring evidence of "fundamental international payments problems" — is met, though courts are unlikely to move within the 150-day expiry window. Treasury Secretary Bessent's characterization of refund claims as "corporate welfare" signaled the administration's combative posture. Separately, a fresh Section 301 investigation targeting over a dozen economies including China, the EU, Mexico, India, and Taiwan was filed late in the month, with a public hearing set for May 5. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimated the IEEPA invalidation reduces government revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036, with the replacement levy recouping approximately $925 billion.

**Iran Conflict: Domestic Consequences**

Saturday evening strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities at the end of the first week reframed every subsequent US macro variable. The US is a net energy exporter, providing relative insulation from Hormuz disruption that proved acutely damaging to Asian and European peers — a structural divergence Goldman Sachs chairman David Solomon described as "benign" and somewhat surprising. That insulation is partial, however. Average gasoline prices rose approximately 27% from hostilities' onset, reaching roughly $3.70 per gallon by the final week of the month — approaching levels that historical precedent associates with electoral consequences — while diesel surpassed $5 per gallon for the first time since December 2022. SMBC Nikko estimated the gasoline price increase could reduce the household savings rate by approximately 60 basis points to roughly 3.9%. The Pentagon disclosed the first six days of operations cost over $11.3 billion, and a supplemental funding request of $200 billion was submitted to Congress in the final week, with economists projecting the 2026 federal deficit could approach $2 trillion against Bessent's original 3%-of-GDP target.

**Federal Reserve: Stagflation Bind Deepens**

The Federal Reserve's policy path became increasingly constrained as March progressed. At the March 19 FOMC meeting, the committee voted 11-1 to hold at 3.5%–3.75%. Chair Powell acknowledged "meaningful" internal movement toward fewer cuts and explicitly noted that colleagues had raised the possibility that the next move could be a hike — a statement that produced the S&P 500's largest Fed-day decline since 2024. Core PCE tracked approximately 3.1% annualized in January, with Barclays, Morgan Stanley, and Bank of America projecting core PCE goods prices advancing at least 0.8% in February alone. Core PPI rose to a three-year high, and the ISM prices-paid component for February surged to its highest since 2022, surprising to the upside by the widest margin on record. By month-end, futures markets had repriced from two cuts anticipated in January to zero, with brief periods in the final week pricing a non-trivial probability of a hike at the April meeting. Goldman Sachs pushed its first cut forecast to September and raised its 12-month recession probability to 30%.

**Institutional Uncertainty at the Fed**

The succession question added a further layer of complexity. Kevin Warsh, the reported frontrunner for Fed chair, remained unconfirmed after a meeting with Senator Thom Tillis — who is blocking the confirmation vote pending resolution of a Justice Department investigation into Fed building renovation practices — produced no commitment. Powell confirmed he would remain pro tempore if Warsh is not confirmed by his May term expiry and stated he will not vacate his board seat until the probe concludes. Senior Justice Department officials supported a US attorney's challenge to Powell's authority, with the White House declining to oppose the effort — a development that raised market concern about central bank independence at an acutely sensitive juncture.

**Equity and Credit Markets**

US equities declined across the month, with the S&P 500 posting its fifth consecutive weekly loss by the final week — the longest such streak since 2022 — and down roughly 3% in March through the final Friday. The Magnificent Seven gauge approached correction territory, falling nearly 10% from its peak. AI disruption anxiety remained a persistent sector headwind: IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years early in the month following Anthropic's COBOL-modernization announcement, Salesforce fell roughly 4% on a weak outlook, C3.ai tumbled 24% on a revenue cut, and Trade Desk dropped 17%. Nvidia's Thursday earnings beat in the first week — a roughly $78 billion revenue forecast — produced a muted after-hours response, reflecting an increasingly demanding bar. Private credit stress intensified throughout the month: Apollo moved to cap redemptions from its $25 billion Debt Solutions vehicle after clients sought to withdraw 11.2%, JPMorgan restricted lending to certain private credit funds, and Fitch reported a trailing 12-month private credit default rate of 5.8% in January, the highest since tracking began. Unusual oil futures trading in the two minutes preceding a late-month Trump Truth Social post — approximately 6 million barrels against a five-day average of 700,000 — drew regulatory scrutiny. Bank of America's final-week fund manager survey recorded the largest jump in cash allocations since March 2020, reaching 4.3%.

**Macro Backdrop and Consumer**

The macro context entering March was already mixed: 2025 GDP growth confirmed at 2.2%, the slowest since the post-COVID expansion, with core inflation ending 2025 at 3%. February payrolls, released at the start of the second week, recorded the first private-sector decline in five years, while February CPI printed a benign 2.5% year-on-year core — relief that proved short-lived given PCE dynamics. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to a three-month low in the preliminary March reading, January consumer spending was essentially flat, and KB Home lowered full-year guidance citing the conflict as a contributing factor. Goldman Sachs projected unemployment rising to 4.6% by year-end from 4.4% in February, and the OECD projected US inflation reaching 4.2% in 2026. What remains unresolved at month-end is whether the energy shock proves transitory — contingent on Hormuz reopening — or persistent enough to transform the current stagflation tension into a genuine contraction, a question the Fed cannot yet answer and on which the range of plausible outcomes remains unusually wide.
