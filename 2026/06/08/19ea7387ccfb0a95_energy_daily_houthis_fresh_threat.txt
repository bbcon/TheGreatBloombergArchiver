Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, Oil 
Europe Team Leader Alaric Nightingale examines the Houthis’ fresh threat to 
shipping in response to the war in Iran.

Yemen’s Houthis today declared a “complete ban” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-08/houthis-to-impose-complete-ban-on-israeli-ships-in-the-red-sea?cmpid=BBBXT060826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5253>
 on Israeli shipping in the Red Sea.

The announcement is not in and of itself critical. What matters is the 
militants’ definition of what constitutes an Israeli vessel, and whether the 
warning is the start of a wider danger.

The Red Sea has become a key route for Saudi oil exports since the war in Iran 
shut theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/why-strait-of-hormuz-blockade-is-at-heart-of-us-iran-peace-deal-stalemate?cmpid=BBBXT060826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5253>
. But traffic out of the waterway, transiting the Bab el-Mandeb passage into 
the Gulf of Aden, remains far below levels seen in 2023, before the Houthis hit 
out at commercial shipping in protest at Israel’s war on Gaza.

Back then, the Iran-backed militants began by targeting Israeli-linked 
vessels, butescalated their campaign 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-06-24/houthi-attacks-on-commercial-shipping-ramp-up-and-get-deadlier?cmpid=BBBXT060826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5253>
 to include US and UK ships when those two nations joined in attacks on the 
group.

Given the reduced volume of transits, it’s unclear how many ships the 
militants will be able to identify as Israeli. They may, again, widen the 
target list to include American and British vessels, but that’s not outlined in 
their current plan.

It’s also not clear whether an end to Iran’s current military operation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-08/iran-and-israel-exchange-missile-attacks-imperiling-peace-talks?cmpid=BBBXT060826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5253>
 against Israel, as reported by the semi-official Fars news agency today, will 
change the group’s calculus.

For now, the risk profile of shipping in the area hasn’t materially shifted as 
a result of the Houthis’ warning, according to shipowners’ trade groupBimco 
<https://www.bimco.org/>.

It’s also worth noting that, while the Houthis have long been seen as an 
Iranian proxy, they haven’t targeted a single commercial vessel since conflict 
broke out in the Middle East at the end of February. Indeed, the group hasn’t 
launched any attack on shipping since last September, according to 
maritime-security firm Ambrey.

Until the group’s intentions become clearer, risk analysts advise caution.

While the announcement doesn’t appear to alter the shipping situation much, 
“current and historic Israeli ownership are at risk,” said Martin Kelly, head 
of advisory at consultants EOS Risk Group. “The Houthis say they will meet 
escalation with escalation.”

—Alaric Nightingale, Bloomberg News

China’s liquefied natural gas 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-08/china-ramping-up-lng-buying-in-anticipation-of-summer-heat?cmpid=BBBXT060826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5253>

BNEF today

Chinese companies have developed a lead in battery technologies that US and 
European rivals probably won’t be able to match for decades to come, according 
toBloombergNEF <https://about.bnef.com/>. Producers such as Contemporary 
Amperex Technology Co. Ltd. have slashed manufacturing costs sharply below 
overseas rivals, highlighting a dilemma for policymakers: Raise barriers such 
as tariffs and local-content rules, or allow cheaper Chinese products to flood 
the market to lower costs, but risk snuffing out competitors.
