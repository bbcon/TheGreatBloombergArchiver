Tuesday, July 21, 2026

US

  • No major US macro data releases today; the key market development is a momentum factor reversal in semiconductors, with the biggest chip names down more than 20% this month despite the S&P 500 remaining near all-time highs up roughly 18% from late March.
  • The Trump administration announced a fresh 50% tariff on select Canadian goods — including milk, beer, hockey equipment, and plywood — invoking the 1930 Smoot-Hawley Trade Act, effective in 30 days.
  • A federal judge temporarily blocked the $110 billion Paramount-Warner Bros. Discovery merger, ruling it "likely" violates antitrust law.
  • US gasoline prices crossed $4 per gallon, the first time in months, as Middle East hostilities intensify.

Monetary Policy

Federal Reserve policy remains genuinely unsettled. A surge in oil prices and a more hawkish tone from the Fed under Chairman Kevin Warsh had briefly priced in a July rate increase, but two benign inflation prints last week shifted expectations materially. Money market funds have responded by shortening weighted average maturity to 40 days from 45 days in mid-May — a tactical defensive repositioning that reflects the absence of a clear policy signal rather than a defined directional bet.

Markets and Momentum

US equity markets appear stable at the index level, with S&P 500 futures up 0.5% and Nasdaq 100 futures gaining 1.3%. Beneath the surface, however, the momentum factor has experienced its largest drawdown since the pandemic. The biggest semiconductor names have declined more than 20% in July alone. The confluence of retail participation via leveraged single-stock ETFs and post-Russell rebalancing may have amplified the reversal. Earnings season — with GM, Northrop Grumman, 3M, and others reporting today — may provide the next directional catalyst. ---

Asia

  • China's Q2 GDP came in at 4.3% YoY, missing the official 4.5%–5.0% target range, prompting the State Council to pledge full-year goal attainment through maximized fiscal deployment.
  • China's AI tech gauge fell 25% from its June 30 high before state-backed buyers drove a partial recovery; the Star 50 ETF attracted $2 billion in inflows Monday alone after the index dropped a record 17% last week.
  • South Korea's Kospi rose 3.6% and the CSI 300 gained 3.1%, led by a chipmaker rebound.
  • TSMC has initiated discussions with clients on price increases of 5%–10% for 2027, covering both advanced and mature semiconductors.

China Macro and Policy

The growth miss — 4.3% YoY against a 4.5%–5.0% official range — has forced Beijing into explicit damage control. The State Council, chaired by Premier Li Qiang, pledged to maximize fiscal efficiency, suggesting a potential loosening of the de facto austerity stance that had seen the cumulative budget deficit narrow in May for the first time in over two years. Electronics and IT contributed 1.4 percentage points to last quarter's growth, underscoring the degree to which the AI hardware boom is buffering broader domestic weakness in fixed asset investment and consumer spending.

Markets and State Intervention

Beijing's response to the AI stock selloff illustrates a difficult balancing act: regulators, state-backed investors, and asset managers intervened broadly to stabilize prices, yet doing so risks reinforcing the speculative dynamics authorities had explicitly warned against just last month. The Star 50 Index, the most AI-heavy gauge, appears the primary target of support. The broader question of whether intervention merely delays price discovery remains unresolved. ---

Europe

  • UK 10-year gilt yields rose 8.1 basis points on the day, breaching 5% — the highest level since 2008 — as markets assessed incoming Prime Minister Andy Burnham's fiscal credibility.
  • Andy Burnham became UK prime minister, appointing former defense secretary John Healey as chancellor in a move designed to signal fiscal restraint.
  • UK labor market data showed conditions steadying ahead of the Bank of England's next decision.

UK Political Transition

Andy Burnham's arrival at 10 Downing Street — Britain's seventh prime minister in a decade — was accompanied by an immediate bond market test. The 8.1 basis point rise in 10-year gilt yields, taking them above 5% and above levels seen during the Truss crisis, signals that investors are withholding full confidence pending concrete fiscal signals. The appointment of John Healey as chancellor appears deliberately calibrated to convey restraint: Healey is viewed by markets as a fiscally orthodox choice, though his resignation from Starmer's cabinet over inadequate defense spending suggests upward pressure on the military budget remains a live constraint.

Fiscal Outlook

Burnham's first announced policy — scrapping a 5% tax on energy bills from October — reflects political imperatives around cost-of-living relief. The tension between this and gilt market demands for consolidation is not yet resolved. The spread between UK gilt yields and German bunds has widened to nearly two percentage points, compared with near-parity at the start of the century, a structural credibility discount that constrains whatever fiscal room Burnham might claim. ---

Global

  • The US and Iran exchanged strikes for a 10th consecutive day; the Strait of Hormuz was reported completely clear of transiting vessels on Tuesday, an extraordinary disruption to one of the world's most critical energy chokepoints.
  • Goldman Sachs estimates Brent could rally above $120/barrel in Q4 if Hormuz disruptions persist, though its base case remains $80; Brent traded at approximately $89.76.
  • The US announced 50% tariffs on select Canadian goods under the 1930 Smoot-Hawley Trade Act, escalating bilateral trade tensions.

Energy and Hormuz

The operational shutdown of Hormuz transits represents a material escalation. Persian Gulf flows have reportedly fallen below 45% of pre-war levels, according to Goldman Sachs, which has pushed oil prices meaningfully higher. US gasoline has crossed $4 per gallon domestically. Asian LNG buyers are bearing acute secondary effects: Pakistan's state buyer paid approximately $21.88 per million BTU for a late-July shipment, the highest price since 2022. Houthi threats to impose a maritime blockade on Saudi Arabia via the Red Sea add a second potential chokepoint. The interaction of Hormuz and Red Sea disruptions simultaneously would represent a genuine structural shock to global energy supply chains.

Trade and Geopolitics

The US invocation of the 1930 Smoot-Hawley Act against Canada — targeting consumer goods while exempting energy, potash, and critical minerals — signals that tariff escalation remains an active tool of US bilateral negotiation rather than a resolved dynamic. Bloomberg Economics expects Washington and Ottawa to work toward partial resolution before the August 19 effective date, but the directional trend toward more aggressive US trade posture appears durable. China's AI advances — Moonshot's Kimi K3 release and CXMT's planned DRAM IPO — meanwhile raise the prospect of a semiconductor market disruption analogous in structure to prior China Shock episodes, with implications for Korean and Taiwanese chip valuations.
