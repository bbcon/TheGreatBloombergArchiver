The dominant cross-cutting theme this week was AI sovereignty — the question of whether nations outside the US and China can meaningfully insulate themselves from dependency on a small number of platforms and infrastructure providers. India's AI summit crystallized this concern, with delegates from across Asia, Africa, Europe, and Latin America confronting a common structural reality: the most consequential AI systems are controlled by US and Chinese entities, and access to them can, in principle, be withdrawn. The 2024 UniSuper incident — in which a Google cloud misconfiguration cut Australian pension fund members off from their accounts — served as a reference point for how technology dependency can manifest in practice.

Rishi Sunak, now a Goldman Sachs adviser, offered a framework for sovereign resilience: secure a niche position in the AI supply chain, diversify providers to avoid vendor lock-in, and build switching capabilities through partnerships. These are practical, if difficult, prescriptions. India's approach — combining domestic small language models, shared compute, semiconductor ambitions, and capital attraction — reflects a more state-directed variant of the same logic.

On climate and energy, the week produced a clear illustration of divergence between the US federal trajectory and the rest of the world. While the Trump administration rolled back EPA emissions standards and advanced plans for a large new gas plant, former US officials were in Mumbai urging developing nations to accelerate green transitions without waiting for Washington. Hillary Clinton's framing — that the Global South must lead because US policy change is "a few years off" — reflects a broader recalibration among climate advocates who are repositioning emerging economies as the primary site of ambition. Whether India can fulfill that role while simultaneously planning new coal capacity through mid-century remains an unresolved tension that no summit rhetoric has yet bridged.
