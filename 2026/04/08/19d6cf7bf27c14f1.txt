Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Alex Longley looks at what it might take to restart oil shipping out 
of the Persian Gulf after a US-Iran ceasefire agreement.

Energy markets have reacted to the two-week ceasefire between the US and Iran 
as the first sign that the worst of the Middle East energy supply shockcould be 
over 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-8?cmpid=BBBXT040826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260408>
. In reality, it will take time to fix.

Brent crude at one point headed for its biggest drop in six years today. 
Natural gas and diesel prices in Europe both briefly slumped around 20%.

But there was little immediate change in traffic through the Strait of Hormuz 
тАФ the number of vessels transiting the critical waterway remains dramatically 
lower than prewar levels. In the oil market, physical barrels have been trading 
far higher than futures as traders price in extreme scarcity.

The worldтАЩs shipowners are awaiting clarity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/hormuz-stays-blocked-for-now-as-hundreds-of-ships-seek-escape?cmpid=BBBXT040826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260408>
 on the situation.

While several expressed optimism that ships would be able to escape the 
Persian Gulf, they added it was unclear what steps they would need to take to 
do so. They also said it would require support from the owners of cargo on 
board.

Industry bodies have also stressed the uncertainty. Bimco, which represents 
almost two-thirds of the worldтАЩs seaborne freight capacity, said its members 
are still awaiting technical details of how transits will work. A group that 
represents London insurers said it doubted trade will return to normal soon.

ThatтАЩs just for ships trying to get out of Hormuz. There are even bigger 
questions about whether owners will be willing to re-enter and pick up new 
cargoes.

тАЬUnless the two-week window is quickly lengthened, I doubt there will be a 
large influx of ships into the Persian Gulf,тАЭ BimcoтАЩs chief shipping analyst 
Niels Rasmussen said. тАЬPartly because many ships have already sailed to other 
regions and partly because they do not want to risk being trapped after the 
two-week window closes.тАЭ

ItтАЩs possible many of these issues will be ironed out in the coming hours and 
days. President Donald Trump said on his Truth Social platform that the US will 
help with the traffic buildup, and also that his country is discussing 
sanctions and tariff relief with Iran.

But this morning also brought reminders of the risk of the ceasefire breaking 
down after both Kuwait and the UAE reportedfresh missile attacks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/us-and-iran-agree-to-ceasefire-easing-fears-of-energy-crisis?cmpid=BBBXT040826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260408>
.

With all that uncertainty, itтАЩs unlikely Hormuz will go back to its prewar 
levels of traffic any time soon. That means oil and gas markets will also face a
slow return to normal
