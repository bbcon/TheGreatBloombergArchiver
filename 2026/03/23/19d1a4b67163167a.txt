It’s getting close to zero hour for Donald Trump’s ultimatum to Tehran to 
reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Will the US president follow through 
<https://bloom.bg/4bHTXtI> with his threat to destroy Iran’s power plants, 
triggering ever widening tit-for-tat reprisals? It’s impossible to know, but 
investors are bracing for the war — and its globe-wide consequences — to worsen.

Stocks sank <https://bloom.bg/4dEBIbb> on Monday, with MSCI’s Asia Pacific 
gauge falling 3.7% to almost erase its gain for the year. South Korea’s 
benchmark tumbled 6.5%. Hong Kong’s Hang Seng Index dropped 3.5%. S&P 500 
futures are down 0.9%. A gauge of European equities slumped toward the lowest 
since September.

Shelter is hard to find outside of the US dollar. Government bonds fell as 
tradersstepped up bets <https://bloom.bg/3NEaaYU> that surging energy costs 
will force central banks to increase borrowing costs. More than $2.5 trillion 
has been wiped <https://bloom.bg/4uMZp7l> from the value of global bonds this 
month.

Asian currencies extended their dizzying descent. The South Korean won 
weakened to the lowest level since 2009, while the Indian rupee and Philippine 
peso dropped to a record low. Australia’s dollar tumbled 1.3%.
Smoke and flames rise at the site of airstrikes on an oil depot in Tehran on 
March 7.Photographer: SASAN/AFP 
Gold slid <https://bloom.bg/4uLDfT4> as much as 9% to about $4,100 an ounce, 
taking its decline from the January peak to 27%. Bitcoin traded below $68,000. 
The cryptocurrencyhas lost <https://bloom.bg/4bLqMWN> roughly 20% since the 
Middle East conflict began at the end of February. Brent was at $113 a barrel.

“It is pandemonium,” said Ed Al-Hussainy, a portfolio manager at Columbia 
Threadneedle Investments in New York.

There is a growing sense <https://bloom.bg/4d4JVWa> that the forces unleashed 
by the war — runaway energy prices, fuel shortages, rising inflation — will be 
hard to put back into the bottle, regardless of what comes next.

If Trump does  go ahead <https://bloom.bg/4c0jKyD> with his threat to demolish 
the power stations in a country of 92 million, Tehran has vowed to retaliate by 
shutting the waterway indefinitely, as well as targeting US and Israeli-linked 
infrastructure across the region, including power and desalination plants. 
Trump gave the Islamic Republic 48 hours in a Truth Social post sent at 7:44 
p.m. New York time on Saturday.

It’s hard to see a path to an end of hostilities. For Asia, that means 
increasing pain in terms of the hit to economies, financial markets, and 
livelihoods.—Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



The disruptions to energy supplies are equivalent to the two major oil crises 
in the 1970s and the 2022 natural gas crisis after Russia invaded Ukraine “all 
put together,” International Energy Agency Executive Director Fatih Birol said.

More than 40 energy assets across nine countries in the Middle East have been 
“severely or very severely” damaged by the war <https://bloom.bg/4d9Sor7>, 
Birol said at Australia’s National Press Club in the capital, Canberra. Asia is 
at the forefront of this crisis, because of its heavy reliance on crude from 
the region, he said.

“Not only oil and gas, but some of the vital arteries of the global economy — 
such as petrochemicals, such as fertilizers, such as sulfur, such as helium — 
their trade is all interrupted, which will have serious consequences for the 
global economy,” Birol said.
Fatih Birol at the National Press Club in Canberra on Monday. Photographer: 
Rohan Thomson/Bloomberg 



China’s state-owned refiners are said to have begun exploring purchases of 
Iranian crude, after Washington allowed the sale of some oil already loaded 
onto tankers.

Representatives of National Iranian Oil Co. and traders who act as 
intermediaries have also been quietlysounding out <https://bloom.bg/4dBSdon> 
potential buyers among these and other Asian refiners, people familiar with the 
matter said. Iran was once a key supplier to major Asian importers, including 
South Korea and Japan, before US restrictions tightened. China is the single 
most important buyer of Tehran’s crude.

Meanwhile, Qatar Airways has sent 20 of its largest aircraft to a site used 
for long-term storage in Spain, the Financial Timesreported 
<https://bloom.bg/3NVEI8L>. Many of the planes are A380, A350 and Boeing 787 
planes, which usually carry passengers to its Doha hub to transfer to onward 
flights, it said.




An Air Canada Express plane with 76 people aboard collided with a fire truck 
<https://bloom.bg/4dnnxHJ> shortly after landing at New York’s LaGuardia 
Airport late Sunday, killing the two pilots.

The pilot and co-pilot died in the collision, the Port Authority of New York 
and New Jersey said early Monday. The US National Transportation Safety Board 
is sending a team to investigate, it said in a post on X. The airport will stay 
shut until at least 2 p.m. local time.
An Air Canada Express aircraft on the runway at LaGuardia Airport in New York 
on March 23.Photographer: ANGELA WEISS/AFP 



Asian governments are readying measures to deal with fuel shortages. 

Myanmar’s military government is stepping up fuel rationing by implementing a 
system of barcodes and QR codes to determine how much customers can buy. 
Customers will be allowed to buy fuel once or twice a week depending on the 
size of their vehicle engines, the Ministry of Energy said. A shortage of jet 
fuel has led several airlines in Myanmar to temporarily halt domestic routes, 
Eleven Myanmar reported.

Pakistan ordered fans of its top cricket tournament to stay home 
<https://bloom.bg/4bAWDe9> and watch matches on television to conserve fuel. 
The Pakistan Super League, the nation’s biggest sporting event that takes place 
from March 26 to May 3, has shifted to a watch-from-home model, the organizer 
said following consultation with the government. It also streamlined the 
hosting venues from six to two, with all matches now being staged in Lahore and 
Karachi.




Wall Street is lifting projections for China’s inflation this year and pushing 
back <https://bloom.bg/4lKQvmE> predictions for the country’s next 
interest-rate cut due to the conflict in Iran.

Bank of America, Citigroup and Goldman Sachs all expect a faster increase in 
consumer and producer prices this year than predicted previously. The spike in 
energy costs is also making the odds of monetary easing much lower in the 
months ahead, with economists at BofA removing their call for two rate 
decreases totaling 20 basis points this year.




Singapore-listed companies are packed with executive directors that are either 
substantial shareholders or related to them, and firms aren’t disclosing enough 
about how pay is decided.

About three in four executive directors are substantial shareholders or are 
family members, according to a study by the National University of Singapore 
Business School’s Centre for Investor Protection. These directors tend to be 
better compensated, raising the question of potentiallyexcessive remuneration 
<https://bloom.bg/4lK9EVX> for these companies, the authors led by Mak Yuen 
Teen said.




Hybe’s shares plunged as much as 15% after a heavily-promoted comeback concert 
by K-pop megastars BTS drewa smaller crowd <https://bloom.bg/4lHYZLk> than 
authorities initially expected.

Stock in BTS’s agency recorded its biggest intraday decline since June 2022. 
The group’s event at Gwanghwamun square attracted 104,000 fans versus the 
260,000 initially estimated by police, according to Chosun Ilbo. Stringent 
crowd control measures may have played a part. The concert was live-streamed by 
Netflix, which should release viewership figures later this week.

 For Your Commute 



A Chinese-made Leapmotor C10 was traveling down Germany’s fast-moving Autobahn 
last year when the driver-assistance system braked sharply and jolted it to the 
side, as if boxed in by scooters in a crowded China megacity.

Leapmotor International’s Germany head, Martin Resch, was at the wheel and 
emailed engineers in Hangzhou to report the problem before going into a 
meeting. By the time he got out, a software update had been beamed to the 
electric vehicle, smoothing its behavior. A similar fix at a European carmaker 
would have taken weeks.

Executives call it China Speed, and it’s the global auto industry’s new 
benchmark.

For decades, carmakers measured themselves against German engineering, Detroit 
scale or Japanese reliability. Now executives from Michigan to Wolfsburg are 
confronting a new yardstick — one defined by rapid-fire development cycles, 
software-first design and relentless cost compression born of China’s EV boom. 
Read the full story <https://bloom.bg/4d4ts4i> about how European and US 
carmakers are being forced to adopt Chinese platforms and engineering practices 
to stay competitive.
