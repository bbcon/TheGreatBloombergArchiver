тАЬWe believe our revised targets reflect a pragmatic response to external

An increasing number of companies across industries rowed back on climate

many US climate targets, spurring firms including tech giants and carmakers to 
dilute efforts to reduce harmful emissions.

What Americans think about climate now

By Eric Roston 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQdgGkZhizQ/eric-roston?cmpid=BBD052926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4992>

Climate change is in the background this US election year as high prices, 
conflict in the Middle East, AI and other issues take center stage. But that 
doesnтАЩt mean the public has forgotten about it.

More than half of Americans regard climate change as a very or moderately big 
problem, and only a quarter of them think the world is equipped to address the 
challenge, according tonew survey results from the Pew Research Center 
<https://www.pewresearch.org/science/2026/05/28/americans-are-increasingly-pessimistic-about-avoiding-the-worst-effects-of-climate-change/>
.

Since the groupтАЩs previous poll on the topic in 2022, Democrats and people who 
lean to the left have moved toward pessimism: 69% of them said the US and other 
countries will not do enough to address climate risks тАФ an 18-percentage-point 
leap in four years.

The poll, which surveyed 3,524 adults in mid-March, found a stark partisan 
divide. Only a fifth of Republicans and right-leaning respondents said climate 
change is delivering harm to people, and most called it a small problem.

Among those respondents who said warming is already hurting people, more than 
two-thirds agreed that big companies тАФ especially the energy industry тАФ and the 
federal government are positioned to address the problem. Just a fifth said 
they expected that new technologies will have solved the problem in three 
decades.
