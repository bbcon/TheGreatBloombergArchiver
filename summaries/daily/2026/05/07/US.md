US equity markets closed at record highs on Wednesday and extended those gains in overnight futures, with the S&P 500 approaching 7,400 as investors increasingly look through the energy shock toward a potential US-Iran deal. The market's resilience is notable: having largely decoupled from oil's gyrations in recent weeks, equities are drawing support from robust high-tech investment and an earnings season that continues to outperform expectations — roughly 80% of S&P 500 companies reporting thus far have beaten estimates. Wall Street bonuses are projected to rise for a third consecutive year as volatility-driven trading revenues and a recovery in dealmaking reinforce the sector's performance.

The more contested debate in fixed income markets centers on whether 30-year Treasury yields can sustain a push through 5%. The long bond briefly breached that level this week, driven by persistent fears that Middle East-linked energy inflation will delay Federal Reserve easing further. Market pricing remains relatively hawkish by some estimates, though analysts at Evercore ISI argue a durable deal bringing oil lower would render rate cuts delayed rather than derailed. Fed Governor Collins this week aligned herself with last week's policy dissent on easing language, suggesting internal divisions at the FOMC have not fully resolved.

The K-shaped nature of the oil shock is increasingly well-documented. New York Fed research finds that households earning under $40,000 annually reduced real gasoline consumption by 7% even as nominal spending rose 12%, while higher-income cohorts barely adjusted consumption at all. This dynamic complicates the Fed's task: the inflation impulse is real but its incidence is uneven, limiting the demand-destruction signal from aggregate data.

On corporate news, Fortinet surged 15% in premarket trading after raising its revenue outlook, while Snap fell sharply on advertising revenue weakness attributed directly to the Middle East conflict. The Trump administration began processing refunds for $166 billion in tariffs declared unlawful by the Supreme Court, a development with meaningful fiscal implications that has received relatively limited market attention amid the broader geopolitical noise.
