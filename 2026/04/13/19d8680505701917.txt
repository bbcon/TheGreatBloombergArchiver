The economist Carmen Reinhart has made a career out of studying the causes of 
economic crises and the responses to them. Her most famousbook 
<https://www.amazon.co.uk/This-Time-Different-Centuries-Financial/dp/0691152640>
, published in 2009 with co-author Ken Rogoff, dove into what they called 
“eight centuries of financial folly” across 66 countries and five continents 
and identified predictable patterns. Its title –This Time is Different – mocked 
the speculators, politicians and pundits pushing the idea that any moment in 
history can defy economic gravity.

So it’s worth paying attention when Reinhart says she’s worried that the world 
today is the least prepared it’s been in decades to respond to a meltdown. 
Almost two decades and a pandemic on from the 2007-08 global financial crisis, 
the capacity to combat an economic downturn has been steadily eroded, the 
Harvard Kennedy School economist argues, even as the global economy has grown 
more fragile.

We don’t know yet how bad the damage to the global economy from the Iran war 
will be. But we do know it’s not going to help growth. And that there’s plenty 
of volatility ahead.

The IMF on Tuesday will be downgrading its global growth forecast 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-11/world-finance-chiefs-head-to-imf-with-a-sense-of-deja-vu?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
 for this year. Bloomberg Economics expects growth to slip to 2.9% this year 
from 3.4% in 2025, though a more prolonged conflict could take that down to as 
low as 2.2%.

But it’s worth asking if those forecasts may slip yet again. The idea that the 
conflict set off by the US and Israel’s Feb. 28 attacks on Iran is nearing an 
end game certainly feels vulnerable.

In a matter of days we have gone from hoping a cease fire would yield a 
permanent end to hostilities and a reopening of the Strait of Hormuz to 
President Donald Trump announcing a naval embargo of the world’s most important 
energy corridor as peace talks collapsed. Put another way, as of late Sunday at 
least, Trump is vowing toblock the chokepoint 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-12/uk-won-t-take-part-in-trump-s-planned-blockade-of-hormuz-strait?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
 he was seeking to unclog when the weekend began.

‘Bad News’

As our colleagues at Bloomberg Economics wrote Sunday <>: “All of this is bad 
news for oil prices and global markets.” And thus the global economy. Crude oil 
benchmarks soared on Monday and global bond yields rose on renewedinflation 
concerns 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/global-bonds-slide-as-failure-of-talks-adds-to-inflation-fears?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
.

But it’s the context that worries some eminent economists like Reinhart who 
fret about the world’s ability to respond to a new crisis because of three 
things:

 * The world now has a $348 trillion debt mountain looming over it that limits 
the ability of governments to roll outfiscal stimulus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/germany-agrees-on-measures-to-cushion-jump-in-energy-prices?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
 in the event of a crisis. Good times have come and gone and few countries have 
taken the opportunity to pay down their pandemic debts.
 * Central banks are still battling persistent inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-02/imf-says-fed-has-little-scope-for-interest-rate-cuts-this-year?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
 and worries of more to come from higher energy prices that were reinforced 
with US consumer price data Friday. That means they will face a difficult 
choice in a crisis: Raise rates to combat inflation or cut them to support 
growth?
 * The world is more fragmented than it was before the pandemic or any other 
recent crisis. And that means the idea of a US-led order and an era of 
coordinated responses to crises seem like history.
Given all that, you shouldn’t expect this week’s IMF/World Bank meetings to 
yield a big splashy global response to the economic effects of the war. You 
should instead expect the two institutions to publicly and repeatedly stress 
the resources they have available forcountries needing help 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-30/g-7-finance-and-energy-officials-meet-on-impact-of-iran-war?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
. Prepare also to hear lots of calls for governments to keep their 
interventions at home “targeted” and “limited” to aiding the most vulnerable.

But, as the cliche now goes, we live in a brave new world 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/oil-may-rise-to-150-if-us-goes-ahead-with-blockade-onyx-says?cmpid=BBD041326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260413>
 in which things move and change fast. Friday the world was contemplating peace 
and the possibility of economic calm. This Monday morning it’s again not really 
clear what lies ahead. The only certainty is that if things get worse 
economically from here, responding is likely to be harder than it was.
