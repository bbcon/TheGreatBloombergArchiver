Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Will Mathis looks at the pitfalls of Europe’s burgeoning solar 
industry.

Is it possible to have too much green power?

Europe has been building solar farms at an astonishing clip in recent years, 
but it’s starting toconfront the limits 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/europe-solar-power-wasted-as-electricity-grids-fail-to-handle-surge?cmpid=BBBXT051426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4478>
 of that boom.

At peak production hours on sunny days, there’s so much electricity being 
generated thatgrids can’t cope 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-europe-solar-power-boom/?cmpid=BBBXT051426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4478>
. And that means solar panels are increasingly being shut off.

In the coming months, about 40 terawatt-hours of electricity — enough to power 
Greater London for a year — could go to waste, according to researchers at 
BloombergNEF.

It’s an uncomfortable moment to be frittering away energy.

Europe is feeling the effects of a tighter global gas market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-24/iran-war-to-keep-gas-market-tight-for-two-more-years-iea-says?cmpid=BBBXT051426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4478>
 following the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, and higher prices are likely to 
curb gas-fired generation, according to Wood Mackenzie Ltd.

Politicians across the region have been pushing for increased reliance on 
homegrown energy, but an abundance of solar is useless if grids can’t take it.

For European consumers, it’s bad news.

They’ve already forked out to fund the billions of euros of subsidies that 
underpinned solar’s growth. Now that the technology is cheap and commercially 
competitive, they’re getting hit with the cost of compensating solar plants 
when they’re ordered to turn off.

This kind of confounding situation is emblematic of the energy transition’s 
growing pains.

Installing lots of new solar and wind is easy when those technologies account 
for just a small fraction of the overall grid. But with Europe working on a
wholesale phaseout of fossil fuels 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/france-unveils-fossil-fuel-exit-plan-at-colombia-climate-summit?cmpid=BBBXT051426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4478>
, the ballooning share of renewables presents a raft of new problems.

The solution will involve an extensive build-out of batteries — an expansion 
that’salready in the works 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-04-19/cheap-batteries-are-taking-over-the-world-s-power-grids?cmpid=BBBXT051426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4478>
. But Europe will also have to rein in subsidies further, channeling them more 
selectively to projects that really need them.

Indeed, the market has already taken a cue. After a decade of successive 
annual records in new solar capacity, additions this year are actually set to 
fall.

—Will Mathis, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

As US President Donald Trump and his Chinese counterpart Xi Jinping meet in 
Beijing, energy markets are watching for any breakthrough in a dispute that has 
reshaped global fuel flows: China’s tariffs on US oil and gas. Shortly after 
Trump announced sweeping tariffs last year, Beijing retaliated with levies on 
US energy. Now, the Middle East crisis may be shifting the calculus. Take 
liquefied natural gas: China has been forced to slash its imports following 
disruptions to Qatari supply. That couldcreate an opening 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/iran-war-gives-xi-the-chance-to-rekindle-gas-sales-with-trump?cmpid=BBBXT051426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4478>
 for US fuel.

BNEF today

Colorado River runoff is flashing a warning for the US Southwest. Lake 
Powell’s April-July inflow is forecast at just 13% of the average, leaving less 
water for reservoirs that support cities, farms and power markets from Arizona 
to California. Weaker hydro could lift gas-fired generation just as cooling 
demand builds, while dry soils and early melt raise wildfire risks for power 
lines, rail, mines and agriculture, according toBloombergNEF <>.
