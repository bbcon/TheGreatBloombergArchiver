The defining development of the period remains the Iran war, now approaching its third week, with no credible path to de-escalation visible. Brent crude held above $100 per barrel, with Iran's new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei explicitly vowing to keep the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed and threatening to open additional fronts if US-Israeli strikes continue. Approximately a fifth of global oil and natural gas flows through the strait; Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, and the UAE have all been forced to curtail production. Trump responded with a stark social media threat, language that markets interpreted as signaling no near-term easing of hostilities.

The strategic picture is deteriorating in ways beyond the energy market. Russia is providing Iran with satellite imagery and drone-targeting intelligence, according to multiple sources, complicating the US campaign while simultaneously benefiting from the Strait's closure. With the discount on Russian Urals crude nearly eliminated relative to Brent, Russia stands to capture a windfall comparable to its 2022 energy revenues. The US has responded by issuing a second authorization to ease Russian oil sanctions, allowing buyers to take cargoes already at sea — a decision that has drawn particular resentment among European allies.

The traditional 60/40 portfolio hedge is breaking down as bonds and equities move in tandem, driven by stagflation concerns. Government bond yields have risen across the US, UK, Germany, Canada, and Australia by more than 25 basis points since the conflict began, eroding bonds' conventional cushioning role. Chinese assets, gold, the dollar, and buffer ETFs are emerging as the practical havens of the moment.

US shale producers have not yet publicly committed to expanding output despite WTI touching its highest level since August 2022, though early signals — rising rig counts and government production forecasts revised upward for 2027 — suggest the industry's capital discipline may be tested. The White House has limited tools to suppress prices, and Trump, who campaigned on low energy costs, may move to pressure producers directly if prices remain elevated.
