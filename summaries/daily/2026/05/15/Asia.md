The dominant story across Asian markets on Friday was a broad and sharp risk-off move, with a gauge of Asian equities falling 2.2%, the steepest single-day decline since March. South Korea's Kospi led the selloff, dropping 6.1%, as the market's heavy concentration in semiconductor names — the very stocks that have driven its world-beating rally in recent months — left it acutely exposed to a repricing of rate expectations. The move appeared to reflect exhaustion following an exceptionally rapid run-up rather than a fundamental deterioration in earnings, though the distinction may offer limited comfort in the near term.

The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing concluded after roughly 36 hours of meetings, pageantry and a state dinner at Zhongnanhai. The headline deliverable was a Chinese commitment to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft — a meaningful symbolic win, though below the upper-end expectation of 500 jets — and an agreement to maintain the trade truce reached in South Korea last autumn under a newly coined framework of "constructive strategic stability." Agricultural purchase commitments are anticipated but not yet finalised. On Iran, both sides expressed convergence in wanting the conflict to end and the Strait of Hormuz reopened, though no actionable mechanism emerged. The most pointed moment came when Xi warned that mishandling Taiwan could push bilateral relations into a "highly dangerous situation" — his bluntest language on the subject to date. A delayed $14 billion US arms package for Taiwan now represents the most immediate test of whether the summit's goodwill proves durable.

Beneath the summit headlines, China's credit quality issue warrants attention. Estimates from independent analysts suggest true non-performing loan ratios may be closer to 10% — versus the official 1.5% — implying as much as $3 trillion in loans that should technically be classified as past due. Regulatory forbearance has so far contained systemic risk, but the policy traps capital in unproductive firms and is visibly slowing overall loan growth. Japan's corporate goods prices rose by the most in 12 years, adding to pressure on the Bank of Japan to tighten. India raised retail fuel prices for the first time in four years, absorbing a fraction of the roughly 50% increase in Brent crude since the Iran conflict began. Thailand convened an emergency meeting between the prime minister and leading business figures to address what officials described as converging crises of inflation, unemployment and energy costs.
