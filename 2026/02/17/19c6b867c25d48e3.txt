The US Is Beefing Up Its Nuclear Power Supply Chain
New US reactors will need a lot more fuel if they eventually get built.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities powering the global economy. Today, 
reporter Will Wade discusses the buildout of the US nuclear-fuel supply chain.

The much-hyped resurgence in US nuclear power is notable for one thing: the 
lack of actual new reactors.

Despite splashy announcements, like November’s agreement for Japan to fund a 
$80 billion plan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-19/us-to-own-reactors-stemming-from-japan-s-550-billion-pledge?cmpid=BBBXT021726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 to build as many as 10 big reactors in the US, no new commercial-scale 
facilities are actually under construction (meanwhile in China, work has 
started on 10new sites 
<https://world-nuclear.org/nuclear-reactor-database/summary/China> since the 
beginning of 2025).

Even if reactors were being built in the US right now, it’s unclear how they 
would be fueled.

Almost all of the uranium going into the current US nuclear fleet is imported 
<https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=64444>, and there’s only 
enough enrichment capacity tosupply about one-third 
<https://world-nuclear.org/information-library/country-profiles/countries-t-z/usa-nuclear-fuel-cycle?utm_source=chatgpt.com>
 of domestic reactors.

If next-generation atomic reactors eventually get built, they’ll need a new 
type of more potent fuel called high-assay low-enriched uranium, or HALEU, for 
which there’s just a single demonstration production line in the US that makes 
small volumes.

“The core of the issue is insufficient capacity,” says Amir Vexler, the chief 
executive officer of enrichment company Centrus Energy Corp. “We need a lot of 
everything.”
Canisters of enriched uranium at the Urenco USA enrichment facility near 
Eunice, New Mexico.Photographer: Mark Felix/Bloomberg
However, efforts are under way to expand the nation’s nuclear supply chain, at 
all four stages of the fuel cycle.

First, several uranium miners, including Ur-Energy Inc., are planning to boost 
US output <bbg://news/stories/TA94R8MB2SJU>. Next, when it comes to converting 
that uranium into gas, Solstice Advanced Materials Inc. — the only US 
conversion company — said last week it plans toincrease capacity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-10/uranium-company-increases-output-to-meet-nuclear-power-demand?cmpid=BBBXT021726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
.

The third stage, enriching the gas, will get a boost from Centrus. It hired 
construction giant Fluor Corp. for a multibillion-dollar facility in Ohio, and 
was one of three companies that eachreceived $900 million 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-05/centrus-leu-nuclear-fuel-makers-get-2-7-billion-from-us-as-power-use-soars?cmpid=BBBXT021726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 in January from the Department of Energy to expand US enrichment capacity.

The fourth and final stage in the chain, fuel fabrication, is also seeing 
progress. X-Energy Reactor Co., an advanced nuclear company backed by 
Amazon.com Inc., received US approval for a new plant just last week.

It’s the first such license in more than five decades — another sign of how 
activity is picking up in the US nuclear sector, even if major new reactors 
remain years away.

—Will Wade, Bloomberg News

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/india-s-solar-manufacturing-excesses-turn-a-boom-into-a-glut?cmpid=BBBXT021726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 since 2020, according to BloombergNEF. The scale of that surge prompted the 
government late last year to urge banks lending to the sector to exercise 
caution. Manufacturers, meanwhile, are beginning to pull back from lower-value 
output.
