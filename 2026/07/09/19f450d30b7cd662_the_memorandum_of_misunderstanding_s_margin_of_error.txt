Can’t Afford a Break

The umpteenth renewal <https://bloom.bg/4aDxPRO> of hostilities between 
Washington and Tehran has sent oil surging by the most since April. At the time 
of writing, Brent crude is trading at $78.02, up 11.6% for the week. But 
overall, markets’ reaction feelsjaded <https://bloom.bg/4wBKoFN>. This is not a 
major turning point, and the latest events aren’t so much shocking as 
distressing for economies that had hoped that the truce laid out in last month’s
Memorandum of Understanding <https://bloom.bg/4wzrEGQ> would hold. It seems to 
have been more of Memorandum of Misunderstanding <https://bloom.bg/44o05UT>.

Broadly, events in the Middle East matter to markets only to the extent that 
they affect the oil price. The deeply controversial situation in Gaza, for 
example, has barely had any financial impact as it doesn’t call oil supply into 
question. In the Iran conflict, traders asked two binary questions:

 * Will Iran successfully close the Strait of Hormuz? and 
 * Will the war escalate into a ground conflict, involving permanent damage to 
oil production facilities throughout the region?
The market sold off aggressively in March when it turned out that the answer 
to the first question was “yes.” The turnaround at the end of that month was 
prompted by clear evidence that the US was determined not to escalate into a 
ground conflict (and so the answer to the second question was “no”). That’s 
been followed in recent weeks by what now looks like an overreaction to the 
Memorandum of Understanding as a virtual peace treaty, rather than the pause it 
barely was, and its likely effect on the Strait.

The risk of an escalation involving ground troops seems as slim as ever. 
President Donald Trump said in Ankara that this would be a“waste of time.” 
<https://bloom.bg/44XrDAw> His reluctance to risk American lives keeps the 
conflict’s overall risks manageable for markets, while ensuring that volatility 
for those most affected will persist, as the US is not prepared to deter Iran 
from flexing its muscles in the Gulf.
Iranians honor the slain Ayatollah Ali Khamanei on July 4. Photographer: Majid 
Saeedi/Getty Images Europe 
That entails a non-disaster scenario in which oil prices stabilize 
significantly higher than at prewar levels. What has been most surprising in 
the last few weeks has been the scale of their fall, even as movement through 
the Strait remains limited. Marko Papic of BCA Research suggests that this 
follows a pattern coming after major geopolitical shocks of recent years:

A similar dynamic occurred in 2025 with tariffs and in 2022 with the Russian 
invasion of Ukraine. As soon as a geopolitical risk peaks, as soon as the 
second derivative rolls over, markets adjust with shocking speed. Very little 
risk premium remains embedded.

Thus when things go wrong, as in the last few days, oil prices need to rise 
again, and other assets need to fall. But the broader binary approach 
continues: This is not going to lead to destruction and death on the scale of 
Iraq or Vietnam, and the scope for market damage is limited. While risks of 
geopolitical disaster remain contained, the immense amounts of money being 
spent on AI will continue to move markets more.

The jaded reaction was in line with the mantra of Macquarie’s Viktor Shvets, 
who recommends “downplaying macro by assuming a ‘dystopian’ backdrop (i.e. “if 
things look bad, they might be worse tomorrow”). Paying too much attention to 
macro risks consuming a lot of energy without requisite returns, he argues. And 
that appears to be one of the lessons of the Iran conflict so far.

Inflation

Only days ago, the discussion around oil supply had shifted to an imminent glut
 <https://bloom.bg/44TacRK> as producers opened the spigots after the Strait 
reopened. As Points of Return laid out yesterday, that left inflation 
expectations significantly lower than they had been on Feb. 27, the eve of the 
war. Even after sharp rises on Wednesday, bond market breakevens continue to be 
lower than they were then, while inflation swaps are back to their prewar 
levels — despite continuing much higher oil prices:

That would have aided oil importers at the expense of exporters. Now, it’s 
overshadowed by the possibility of renewed deterioration and an intensified 
energy crisis. The two contrasting outcomes threaten more bumps in the market, 
and would create very different lasting winners.

Elevated inflationary pressures had already prompted a hawkish stance 
<https://bloom.bg/3Tr6DQb> from the new leadership at the Federal Reserve.  The 
minutes <https://bloom.bg/4vl6eMI> of the first Federal Open Market Committee 
meeting chaired by Kevin Warsh, released Wednesday, made clear that the war was 
putting upward pressure on rates. A “few” members <https://bloom.bg/3Tr6DQb> 
even saw a case for a hike last month, while:

Most participants pointed to scenarios in which, in the context of stable 
labor market conditions, inflation would remain elevated due to strong 
AI-related demand, the conflict in the Middle East, or the effects of tariffs. 
In such scenarios, almost all of these participants indicated that some policy 
firming would likely be warranted

Protracted uncertainty over the Gulf, we can infer, likely means the next move 
in rates is higher, not lower.

Emerging Markets

That complicates monetary policy for emerging markets. For countries like India
 <https://bloom.bg/4aKwIjc>, precariously exposed to the energy shock, the 
renewed fighting was felt almost instantly. Trump’s declaration thatthe 
ceasefire <https://bloom.bg/44RVXMX> was over sent the rupee tumbling to a 
month low, as Indian bond yields rebounded:

Trump’s threat of further strikes <https://bloom.bg/4vqR60d>, irrespective of 
whether they’re carried out, means that debilitating uncertainty for countries 
that depend on Middle East oil will continue. The best scenario, Capital 
Economies’ Hamad Hussain suggests, would be a messy ceasefire that allows oil 
flows to increase while crude prices settle around the $80 per barrel mark — 
still more than 30% up from the start of the year:

This is partly because the exodus of oil-laden tankers that had been trapped 
in the Strait for months accounts for some of the recent increase in oil flows, 
and so loadings would need to rise further to sustain the current rate of oil 
flowing out of the Strait.

There could even be bigger troubles should Iran further restrict traffic 
through the Strait and/or the US reinstate its naval blockade of Iranian ports. 
This is how activity through the narrow waterway has performed since the June 
ceasefire:

A return to prewar levels was always unlikely, and the renewal of hostilities 
will likely keep it that way. But the recovery seen in recent weeks has been 
meaningful.

The return of risk provided a tailwind for the strengthening dollar. 
Emerging-market currencies had been the conspicuous victims of the Iran 
conflict, which snuffed out an impressive rally. The JPMorgan EM FX index had 
regained all the ground it lost to the dollar during March, but now it’s 
falling again:

It’s plausible that the worst of the inflation shock may be over for emerging 
markets. Analysis by Niamh Cahill of Oxford Economics suggests that, so far, 
the war’s impact on import prices has proved smaller and narrower than that of 
the Russia-Ukraine war and other major historical shocks. The 7.5% 
month-on-month rise in median EM import prices in March was roughly half the 
peak seen in 2022 and largely concentrated in energy:

Pricing pressures, Cahill notes, should be moderate but still significant as 
some second-round effects via wage bargaining and higher fertilizer prices are 
already in train. The impacts will be limited, given a smaller and shorter 
shock compared with 2022, mostly resilient EM FX, and interventions by central 
banks to limit passthrough that appear to have been handled appropriately. She 
said:

Based on historical norms, our models suggest the import price shock will have 
a moderate long-term impact of around 0.4% on the median Central and Eastern 
Europe and Middle East and Africa economy and a much more modest 0.1% impact on 
Latin America and Asia. Persistence could be lower than history suggests.

All of this tends to confirm why the market reaction has been jaded rather 
than shocked. A return to hostilities is undeniably bad news, but most of the 
world has shown that it can muddle through.

NATO

The latest resurgence of hostilities with Iran came as the leaders of the 
North Atlantic Treaty Organization tried once more to show that their house was 
in order, and failed. A (wholly infeasible <https://bloom.bg/44kQ7DG>) threat 
by Trump to cut off all trade withSpain <https://bloom.bg/4wcfFiR>, with whom 
the US enjoys a trade surplus <https://bloom.bg/4wytvMa>, grabbed the 
headlines. That’s a shame, because his abrasive approach toward getting other 
countries to pay their dues has been successful. Rheinmetall, Germany’s biggest 
arms contractor, has dropped more than 40% since peaking in the wake of the US 
intervention in Venezuela — but its gains remain phenomenal:

To grasp the full impact that Trump has had, look at how Europe’s arms 
contractor stocks have fared relative to the market, in comparison with the US:

The success of Iran and Ukraine in embarrassing their superpower opponents with
cheap drones <https://bloom.bg/3RqJXir> has dampened the excitement around 
old-line manufacturers of high-tech expensive weaponry. But if you want an 
example of Trump’smegaphone diplomacy <https://bloom.bg/44SjKwl> hitting its 
target, this would be it.
