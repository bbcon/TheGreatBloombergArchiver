# FX Outlook: Dollar, Real Rates, and the Fed Path as Commodity Drivers
*June 5, 2026*

---

## Dollar Narrative Arc: From Consensus Short to Contested Ground

Entering 2026, the dollar short was the modal institutional view — endorsed by more than 60 Wall Street houses. The analytical basis was a combination of expected narrowing rate differentials (Fed cutting faster than peers), twin-deficit dynamics (fiscal expansion + current account deficit), and reserve diversification (non-US central banks and SWFs reducing dollar concentration). The BofA global fund manager survey in February showed the largest-ever overweight on the euro versus the dollar. Non-resident EM portfolio inflows approached $100 billion in January — the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades.

That consensus has been materially disrupted by the Iran shock, which set off a sequence of events that now dominate the FX picture.

---

## The Macro Regime Sequence

**January — Dollar weak, thesis intact**: Tariff ruling uncertainty, rotation from US assets to Europe and EM. World ex-US equities outperforming the S&P 500 by approximately 7.7 percentage points YTD by late February. ECB and Fed both on easing trajectories. The IIF reported broad-based EM inflows across debt, equity, China, and ex-China.

**February/early March — Dollar weak, regime holds**: US-Iran tensions building but not yet a live supply shock. Supreme Court ruling on IEEPA tariffs reduced perceived US fiscal revenue, dollar-negative at the margin. EM inflows sustained.

**March — Dollar strengthens sharply**: The oil shock triggered risk-off safe-haven demand. Bloomberg Dollar Spot posted its largest monthly gain since July (~+2%). G7 bond selloff synchronized around the energy shock. Gold collapsed (see below) as dollars and Treasuries absorbed the flight-to-quality flow. EM central banks faced simultaneous currency pressure and inflation — Indonesia, India, and Thailand among those most exposed.

**April–May — Dollar elevated but contested**: The FOMC's increasingly hawkish pivot (four dissents, pricing hikes by 2027) supports the front end. But the debasement debate — Fitch warning, CBO projecting debt to 108% of GDP by 2030, 30-year yields at 5.19% — adds a fiscal risk premium that is dollar-ambiguous. Near-term carry and policy differentials favor the dollar; medium-term fiscal trajectory and reserve diversification work against it.

Notable cross-rates: the yuan is on course for its sixth consecutive quarterly gain against the dollar — its longest winning streak since 2013. The BoJ's intervention (~$30B deployed) only temporarily arrested yen weakness toward ¥160; structural energy import costs create persistent downward pressure. Sterling under political pressure (Labour leadership uncertainty → fiscal risk premium from markets pricing a more expansionary successor). The euro caught between an ECB hawkish pivot (narrowing differentials, somewhat dollar-negative) and deteriorating eurozone growth (flight-to-quality into dollars partially offsetting).

## Real Rates: The Dominant Variable

The most consequential FX and commodity driver over the period has been the repricing of real rates. The 30-year UST yield reached 5.19% — highest since June 2007. Bloomberg's average sovereign yield gauge hit its highest since July 2008. Across the US, UK, eurozone, and Japan simultaneously, long-end yields rose in a synchronized selloff: a common energy-inflation driver propagating through different institutional contexts.

The consequence for commodities priced in dollars is textbook: higher real rates raise the opportunity cost of holding non-yielding commodities, reduce the NPV of speculative positions, and support the currency in which most commodities are denominated. The partial reversal of the dollar short thesis was thus directly commodity-bearish for metals and agriculture, even as the oil shock simultaneously drove energy higher.

The copper/gold ratio at 4-decade lows is consistent with this interpretation. Gold (real-rate sensitive, safe-haven) has been less bid than its geopolitical premium would suggest *because* rising real rates are an offsetting headwind, while copper is both compressed by real rates and lacking a near-term China demand catalyst.

**Gold's trajectory** illustrates the tension acutely. From above $5,000/oz in January/February, gold fell approximately 20% from its peak by end-May. The initial collapse in March was a "dash for liquidity" — the worst weekly decline since 1983. Large speculative long positions, dollar strength, and genuine risk-off flows into Treasuries (where duration earns a yield) overwhelmed the safe-haven bid. Gold traded around $4,490 in late May, with UBS trimming its year-end forecast to $5,500 — the inability to sustain a safe-haven bid despite ongoing geopolitical risk reflects the dominance of rising real yields over traditional geopolitical premium mechanisms.

## Where the Market Pricing Could Be Wrong

**Hawkish direction (market may be too aggressive on hikes)**:

The Hormuz disruption's resolution, if it comes, delivers a sharp disinflationary impulse. Goldman's Q4 Brent forecast is already $90, well below current spot. If Brent corrects toward $80–90 on a ceasefire, headline CPI could fall materially within 1–2 months (energy is the primary driver of the above-consensus CPI print). In that scenario, Warsh may use trimmed-mean inflation measures — the Dallas Fed's trimmed PCE reads 2.33% versus 3.0% for core PCE, a 70bp gap — to argue that underlying inflation dynamics don't justify hiking. Structural disinflationary forces (AI productivity, trade re-normalization, China overcapacity exporting goods deflation) could reassert quickly. The realistic ceiling on Warsh's dovishness appears to be *holding*, not cutting — UBS's Pingle and ABN Amro both suggest changes to the SEP cadence and press conference schedule are more likely near-term moves than an aggressive rate hike cycle.

**Dovish direction (market may be underpricing inflation persistence)**:

The second-round effects of the energy shock are becoming more entrenched. Sticky prices re-crossed 3%, supercore services rose sharply, and consumer prices outpaced wages in April for the first time since 2022. PPI rose 6% YoY with electronics components inflating 27% — AI-driven memory shortages ending decades of technology-sector disinflation. If Hormuz remains closed into Q3, the inflation sequencing complicates any easing pivot. Oxford Economics revised its PCE deflator projection to 3.8%, foreclosing near-term easing. FOMC minutes show "almost all" participants expect persistent energy-driven inflation.

**The key asymmetry for commodity investors**:

The oil forward curve at approximately $80 for later 2026 implies a Hormuz resolution that is not operationally confirmed (Polymarket assigns below 50% odds to normal Hormuz traffic by end-June). If resolution is delayed, the curve reprices sharply upward — commodity-positive for energy, commodity-negative for metals (via stronger dollar and higher real rates). If resolution occurs on schedule, oil falls sharply, inflation expectations decline, the Fed retains optionality, dollar weakens, and the macro regime shifts back toward the original "dollar short + ex-energy commodities bid" thesis. In that scenario, the large dollar short position re-establishes, real rates decline from the Hormuz-inflated level, and industrial metals (copper, aluminum) become the preferred commodity expression.
