 Apple goes its own way 



These days, when most Big Tech stocks zig, Apple’s shares zag 
<https://bloom.bg/4aoanZ1>. That’s making the iPhone maker an appealing haven 
in a market that’s been upended by fears over AI.

Apple’s 40-day correlation to the Nasdaq 100 Index tumbled to about 0.2 last 
week, the lowest since 2006. That’s down from about 0.9 in May. (A correlation 
of 1 means the two securities are moving in unison, while a reading of -1 
signals they are moving opposite each other.)


Apple’s decision to mostly sit out the AI arms race has turned it into a tech 
outlier.The company isn’t participating in the data-center spending bonanza and 
also doesn’t have a major business line at risk from AI. The stock is 1.7% 
higher for the month of February, compared with a 3.3% decline in the Nasdaq 
100.

“Apple’s lack of correlation is 100% a positive right now,” said Art Hogan at 
B. Riley Wealth. “We’re in an environment of AI whack-a-mole, where investors 
are so nervous about what will be disrupted next they’re shooting first and 
asking questions later.” —Ryan Vlastelica <https://bloom.bg/3Xk5l8K>

 Election bets in your brokerage account 



Wall Street’s product machine wants to combine two hot finance trends — exotic 
ETFs and event prediction markets <https://bloom.bg/3OnYA4j>. 

Roundhill Investments has asked regulators for permission to launch six ETFs 
that would let investors wager on US election outcomes. The funds, with names 
like Roundhill Democratic President ETF and Roundhill Republican Senate ETF, 
would cover presidential and legislative races.

It’s the most ambitious attempt yet to bring prediction markets into 
mainstream finance.

Each fund would hold derivatives that settle at either $1 or $0. Pick the 
winning party and the contract pays out. Pick wrong and the contracts settle at 
zero, but the fund rolls into the next election cycle and resets — presidential 
ETFs from 2028 into 2032, congressional funds from 2026 midterms into 2028.

“This is yet another example of pushing the ETF envelope,” said Todd Sohn, 
chief ETF strategist for Strategas. “ETFs are usually involved whenever there 
is a ‘hot’ asset or new way to gain exposure. It just takes one filing to get 
the ball rolling and prediction markets are the next forefront.” —Isabelle Lee 
<https://bloom.bg/4gRZU8q> and Denitsa Tsekova <https://bloom.bg/3DxgA6N>
