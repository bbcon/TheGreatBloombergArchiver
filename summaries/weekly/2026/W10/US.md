**Geopolitical Shock and Market Resilience**
US equity markets endured a turbulent week as the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran, which commenced over the preceding weekend, rapidly became the dominant variable in financial markets. The S&P 500 opened Monday under pressure with futures declining approximately 1.0%, but the benchmark displayed notable resilience relative to global peers, ending the week down less than 1% in aggregate — a divergence Goldman Sachs chairman David Solomon described as "benign" and somewhat surprising. The structural explanation is straightforward: the US is a net energy exporter, insulating it from the Strait of Hormuz disruption that proved acutely damaging to Asian and European markets. Defense names were the clearest equity beneficiaries, with Lockheed Martin rising 7.1%, RTX gaining 6.5%, and Northrop Grumman advancing 5.2% in Monday premarket trading, while airlines fell sharply — United Airlines down 5.7%, Delta off 5.1% — on higher fuel costs and regional travel disruption.

**Bond Market and Monetary Policy Complications**
The Treasury market behaved counter-intuitively for a risk-off environment throughout the week. Rather than attracting haven demand, the 10-year yield rose from approximately 3.97% on Monday to 4.163% by Friday, as investors priced out Federal Reserve rate cuts in response to the oil-driven inflation signal. The ISM manufacturing survey for February, released Tuesday, showed the prices-paid component surging to its highest level since 2022, surprising to the upside by the widest margin on record. Gasoline prices rose 25 cents to $3.32 per gallon over the week — the highest level under Trump's presidency — while diesel surged nearly 30%. Former Treasury Secretary Yellen stated the Iran conflict leaves the Fed "even more on hold," and money-market funds swelled to a record $8.27 trillion as investors sought shelter. The transition to a new Fed chair in roughly two months — with Kevin Warsh reportedly the frontrunner — adds institutional uncertainty at a particularly sensitive juncture.

**Political and Fiscal Crosscurrents**
The domestic political picture grew more complicated across the week. The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs, according to the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, reduces government revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036, with the administration's replacement 10% levy recouping only approximately $925 billion. The president dismissed Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem on Friday, naming Oklahoma Senator Markwayne Mullin as her replacement — raising questions about FEMA leadership continuity ahead of the spring disaster season. The Trump administration also ruled out tapping the Strategic Petroleum Reserve despite WTI crude rising above $84 per barrel by Friday.

**Corporate Developments**
Corporate news reflected the week's stress. MongoDB shares sank 27% following a weaker-than-expected revenue forecast. Blackstone's flagship private credit fund faced a record redemption request equivalent to 7.9% of shares, approximately $3.8 billion, with more than 25 senior executives collectively contributing approximately $150 million to support payments. Fitch reported a trailing 12-month private credit default rate of 5.8% in January — the highest since tracking began in 2024 — and short interest against Blue Owl Capital reached an all-time high. Marvell Technology surged 12% in premarket Friday on data center demand, while Gap fell 6% on Old Navy underperformance. Berkshire Hathaway's Class B shares slipped 1.7% after reporting a near-30% decline in operating profit in Warren Buffett's final quarter as CEO.
