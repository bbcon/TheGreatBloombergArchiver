An Existential Threat to the Islamic Republic
Iran’s negotiators are dealing with an unpredictable US president.

Iran’s diplomats are used to meeting Westerners 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-26/trump-puts-fate-of-world-s-top-conflicts-in-the-hands-of-two-men?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 for nuclear talks — they’ve been doing it for almost 20 years.

But the Islamic Republic’s latest discussions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-26/us-iran-to-hold-talks-at-technical-level-next-week-oman-says?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 with the US, which adjourned late yesterday in Geneva, are happening against 
the backdrop of some extraordinary challenges unlike anything its experienced 
negotiators have had to deal with before.

The country’s fortunes have transformed since its top diplomat, Abbas 
Araghchi, first met with US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff last year. They’ve been 
througha war with Israel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-16/why-israel-attacked-iran-how-nuclear-ambitions-are-fueling-crisis-what-s-next?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 and they’ve had their biggestnuclear facilities 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-26/us-iran-nuclear-talks-what-we-know-about-tehran-s-atomic-program?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 bombed by the US.

At the heart of their dilemma is that they are dealing with an unpredictable 
US president theydo not understand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-iran-protects-nuclear-sites/?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

The bitter experience of being attacked by Israel — a top US ally 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/netanyahu-in-hasty-trip-to-warn-trump-against-narrow-iran-deal?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 — last June taught them a lesson. At any moment, any political capital they 
spend on engaging with Washington and dangling financial incentives in front of 
Trump can be wiped away in minutes by another strike by Israel or oneordered by 
Trump 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/trump-says-regime-change-in-iran-best-thing-that-could-happen?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 himself.

They are strategizing for the very real possibility of a war, while at the 
same time preparing for what the oil marketsseem to believe 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/brent-crude-hits-70-a-barrel-as-trump-ramps-up-iran-threats?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 is the less likely outcome of an agreement with the US.

At home, levels of domestic dissent are as threatening 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-11/iran-edges-closer-to-a-revolution-that-would-reshape-the-world?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 to Iranian officials as the US flotilla that’s encircling the Middle East. 
It’s set to be boosted by a second carrier strike group reportedly already in 
Israeli waters.

Iranians are still reeling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-15/iran-protests-will-khamenei-s-regime-survive-and-what-lies-ahead?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 from the violence and trauma unleashed on them by the Islamic Republic’s 
security forces during therecent uprising 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-14/iran-protests-how-inflation-and-a-currency-crash-fueled-unrest?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

Opposition to the theocratic system Araghchi represents is so widespread that 
it’s hard to imagine it can be fixed with some sanctions relief.

For Araghchi, a deal with Trump won’t just deliver an economic lifeline — as 
it did back in 2015 — but, he hopes, the very survival of the Islamic Republic.

A Kenyan rail project that stalled just over halfway through its planned route 
and became a symbol of how China’s Belt and Road-era push is fading isset to 
resume 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/kenya-revives-railway-to-nowhere-this-time-without-china-loans?cmpid=BBD022726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 next month — this time without taking new loans from Beijing. It will 
ultimately be the East African nation’s biggest infrastructure project since 
the country’s independence from Britain in 1963. The decision to forgo Chinese 
funding reflects shifting circumstances for developing nations triggered by 
strains in China’s public finances.
