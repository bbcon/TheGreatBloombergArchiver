**Monetary Policy in Transition**

The dominant theme across February was the Federal Reserve's succession dynamic and its interaction with increasingly complex inflation data. President Trump's nomination of Kevin Warsh on the final Friday of January set the tone for the month: Wall Street's initial characterization of Warsh as a relative hawk sat in direct tension with Trump's persistent demand for lower rates, and Warsh's subsequent public alignment with the president provided only partial reassurance given his institutional record. Senate confirmation appears probable, but the operative question — how Warsh balances political pressure against the Fed's credibility — remained unresolved through month-end. The FOMC minutes released in the final week of February came in more hawkish than the market had anticipated, with several members suggesting rates may need to rise if inflation remains elevated. This tone sat awkwardly against constructive disinflation signals: the trimmed mean and median CPI reached their lowest readings since 2021, and January CPI averaged 2.16% across the twelve large metros tracked by the BLS. Futures markets pushed the next anticipated cut to July following the stronger-than-expected January payrolls print released in the second week of the month, though two cuts remain priced for the full year — an assumption that may face further tests. The delayed PCE release on the final Friday of February carried unusual weight given that core PCE had been running near 3%.

**Equity Markets and the AI Rotation**

US equity markets spent February rotating beneath a relatively calm headline index level. The S&P 500 sat near all-time highs in mid-month but trailed world ex-US equities by roughly 7.7 percentage points year-to-date and ranked 69th among 92 global indices tracked by Bloomberg — a stark illustration of the divergence between index-level calm and underlying factor stress. The iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF fell more than 17% year-to-date as the market priced AI disruption of incumbent software business models, a dynamic the term "SaaSpocalypse" briefly captured in financial media. AMD fell 17% post-earnings early in the month, Alphabet guided 2026 capital expenditure to as much as $185 billion against a consensus near $120 billion, and Amazon disclosed $200 billion in planned AI-related capex — driving its shares down approximately 8% on the announcement. The aggregate implied spend from the four major hyperscalers approached $650 billion, a scale with few historical analogs that a growing number of fund managers are beginning to characterize as potential overinvestment rather than rational capital allocation. Breadth, however, remained broadly constructive: the equal-weighted index hit a record mid-month, value outperformed momentum by the widest margin since November 2020, and Q4 blended earnings growth reached a four-year high near 14.5%.

**The Supreme Court's Tariff Ruling**

The week's most consequential domestic policy development arrived in the final days of February, when the Supreme Court struck down in a 6-3 decision the administration's reciprocal tariffs imposed under emergency powers, potentially cutting the US average effective tariff rate by more than half. The court left unresolved whether importers are entitled to refunds — Bloomberg Economics estimates the total could reach $170 billion — remanding that question to lower courts. The White House signaled it would reconstruct its trade architecture under alternative statutory authorities. Separately, the Republican-led House passed a bill terminating tariffs on Canadian imports mid-month, with six Republicans crossing the aisle — the most significant legislative pushback to the administration's trade agenda to date, though the measure is unlikely to survive a presidential veto.

**Fiscal and Labor Dynamics**

New CBO projections showed net interest payments rising from $1 trillion in 2026 to $2.1 trillion by 2036, reaching 4.6% of GDP — a figure that alone exceeds Treasury Secretary Bessent's 3% total deficit target for 2029. Q3 GDP was revised to a 4.4% annualized pace, and Bank of America projected front-loaded 2026 growth, citing approximately $135–140 billion in fiscal stimulus and post-shutdown normalization tailwinds. Against this, consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in over a decade despite real wage gains of 3.8% year-on-year in December — a disconnect the Goldman Sachs housing analysis helps contextualize: the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160–200% of annual income from 90–120% in 2000. The partial government shutdown that ended in early February added noise to the labor data; the January payrolls print of 130,000 jobs added — released with a delay — was the strongest monthly gain in over a year. AEI and Brookings researchers separately estimated the US may have experienced net negative immigration in 2025, a potential labor-supply headwind whose full implications for trend growth remain to be seen.
