Saturday, May 09, 2026

US

The Federal Reserve's path toward rate cuts appears increasingly constrained by the ongoing US-Iran conflict, with surging oil and fertilizer prices sustaining inflationary pressure that pushes accommodation further out of reach. This represents a direct tension with President Trump's publicly stated desire for lower interest rates — a policy goal that the administration's own military engagements in the Gulf may be actively undermining. The self-reinforcing dynamic between energy prices and monetary policy expectations deserves close attention from investors pricing near-term Fed action. On the dollar, the question of reserve currency durability has moved to the forefront at this week's Milken Institute conference in Los Angeles, where a range of institutional investors and policymakers expressed growing unease about dollar-asset concentration. EQT's Per Franzén, overseeing roughly €270 billion in private-market assets, noted that clients are actively reassessing their historical overweight to dollar-denominated holdings — a sentiment that, while not new, appears to be gaining institutional traction. Critically, the source of dollar vulnerability in this framing is not competitive displacement by the renminbi or another currency, but rather the accumulation of US policy errors. As Gateway Partners' V. Shankar observed, the dollar's demise, if it comes, will be self-inflicted rather than externally imposed. The Gulf conflict is creating specific transmission mechanisms worth monitoring. Iran's reported collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin represents a de minimis but symbolically notable bypass of dollar payment infrastructure. More consequentially, China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US sanctions on Iranian oil directly challenges the enforcement architecture that gives dollar primacy its coercive utility. Treasury Secretary Bessent has identified dollar supremacy as the administration's top financial objective; the current operating environment is testing the internal coherence of that goal against other policy choices. Whether these strains prove transitory or structural remains genuinely uncertain.

Asia

China's posture on Iranian oil sanctions has emerged as one of the week's more significant geopolitical-financial developments. Beijing's directive to private refiners to disregard US secondary sanctions targeting Iranian crude purchases represents a deliberate challenge to American financial enforcement mechanisms, and carries implications beyond energy supply alone. It signals that China is prepared to absorb diplomatic friction with Washington in exchange for discounted oil access and a principled demonstration that US sanctions architecture has limits — a message with resonance across emerging markets. On the dollar-alternative infrastructure front, China remains a central participant in Project mBridge, the BIS-coordinated cross-border settlement system developed alongside Hong Kong, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Thailand. The system's design explicitly routes around traditional US-dominated financial channels. While mBridge remains nascent in transaction volume relative to SWIFT-based flows, its development alongside elevated geopolitical tensions and rising oil prices creates conditions under which adoption could accelerate faster than baseline projections assume. Wall Street analysts have reportedly begun calling a potential turn in China's property market, according to Bloomberg's New Economy coverage this week — a development worth monitoring given the sector's outsized role in domestic credit dynamics and household wealth. Chinese truck operators are also reportedly accelerating electric vehicle adoption as fuel costs rise sharply in the wake of the Hormuz disruption, illustrating how the Gulf conflict is producing second-order demand shifts with relevance for EV manufacturers and battery supply chains across the region. India warrants a forward-looking note: Bloomberg New Economy signals an editorial pivot toward more sustained India coverage ahead of the New Economy Forum in New Delhi this October, reflecting the economy's growing prominence in global investor and policy discussions. Near-term India-specific data was limited in this period's coverage.

Europe

European corporate and regulatory developments this week centered on the intersection of sustainability obligations and industrial strategy. Polestar, the Gothenburg-based EV manufacturer spun out of Volvo, published its annual sustainability report indicating a 7.3% reduction in carbon footprint per vehicle in the prior year, driven in part by its Polestar 4 SUV. The company maintains a stated objective of producing a net-zero vehicle by 2035 — a target that stands in contrast to the broader industry trend of deprioritizing emissions commitments amid margin pressure, rising tariff costs, and competition from lower-cost Chinese EV producers. The European regulatory backdrop provides structural context. The EU's forthcoming digital product passport requirement will mandate that automakers compile detailed sustainability and recyclability data for EVs and their batteries by February, creating compliance obligations that differentiate producers who have invested in carbon accounting infrastructure from those who have not. Polestar's multi-year head start in this area may translate into competitive advantage as the regulatory timeline tightens. Meanwhile, the European Commission's partial retreat from its earlier aggressive phase-out timeline for internal combustion engines reflects the political difficulty of maintaining ambitious climate commitments under simultaneous energy price and industrial competitiveness pressures. The broader European macro picture is shaped significantly by the Hormuz disruption's energy price pass-through, which complicates the ECB's rate trajectory in ways that parallel the Fed's dilemma — elevated energy costs sustaining inflation at a moment when growth headwinds would otherwise argue for accommodation. No specific ECB communications or eurozone data releases were covered in this period's source material, and UK-specific developments were absent from available coverage.

Global

The defining cross-regional theme this week is the compound effect of the US-Iran conflict on energy markets, trade flows, and the architecture of dollar-denominated global finance. The joint American-Israeli operation against Iran has produced consequences that extend well beyond the immediate theater: retaliatory attacks on Persian Gulf infrastructure, paralysis of Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic, and sharp increases in oil and fertilizer prices. Even if hostilities were to cease in the near term, the supply chain and inflationary aftereffects appear likely to persist for months, with meaningful implications for global growth trajectories and central bank sequencing across multiple jurisdictions. The Hormuz disruption is simultaneously accelerating certain structural transitions. Chinese commercial truck operators are reportedly accelerating electrification in response to surging diesel costs — a concrete illustration of how energy price shocks can compress the adoption timeline for alternatives. More broadly, the Iran conflict has elevated the perceived security premium of domestic clean energy capacity, reinforcing investment rationales for renewables and EVs that had faced headwinds from US policy reversals and subsidy reductions. De-dollarization dynamics, long discussed but slow-moving, appear to be gaining modest momentum through specific, observable channels: Iran collecting Hormuz tolls in Bitcoin, China defying secondary sanctions, Gulf sovereign wealth funds reassessing their US asset allocations as American security guarantees prove less reliable, and central banks across emerging markets increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves. The long-run dollar story remains contested — stablecoin growth creates new Treasury demand, and many smaller economies have no practical payments alternative — but the accumulation of stress points is becoming harder to dismiss as purely episodic. The more salient risk, as several Milken conference participants noted, is not displacement by a rival currency but erosion through sustained US policy inconsistency.
