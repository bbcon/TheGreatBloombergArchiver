Weekly Brief

Mar 23 – Mar 29, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 futures whipsawed across the week, declining sharply on Monday before partially recovering midweek on ceasefire diplomacy, with the index now down roughly 5% since hostilities began — a relative resilience that reflects as much caution on the short side as genuine fundamental optimism.
  • Fed funds futures briefly priced a non-trivial probability of a rate hike at the April meeting, the first time since 2023 that outcome was live, as oil-driven inflation repricing combined with a weakening growth outlook to produce a textbook stagflationary configuration.
  • Goldman Sachs raised its 12-month US recession probability to 30%, projecting unemployment at 4.6% by year-end; the OECD put US inflation at 4.2% for 2026, 1.2 percentage points above its December forecast.
  • The Pentagon's $200 billion supplemental funding request for the Iran conflict threatens to push the 2026 federal deficit toward $2 trillion, roughly double Treasury Secretary Bessent's original 3%-of-GDP target.
  • Apollo Global Management capped redemptions from its $25 billion credit vehicle at 5% after clients sought to withdraw 11.2%, the most visible sign yet of mounting stress in private credit markets.

Equity and Credit Markets

US equity markets spent most of the week in defensive mode, with institutional desks accumulating cash rather than taking directional positions. Bank of America's latest fund manager survey recorded the largest jump in cash allocations since March 2020, with holdings reaching 4.3%. The S&P 500 has now logged five consecutive weeks of losses — its longest such streak since 2022 — though would-be bears remain reluctant to press the short side, given the memory of April 9, 2025, when the index surged 9.5% on tariff relief. Monday's intraday reversal, when markets briefly rallied on Trump's Truth Social post claiming "productive conversations" with Tehran before quickly fading as Iran denied any negotiations, illustrated the structural difficulty of pricing an adversarial, social media-dependent foreign policy process. Vanda Research data showed Monday marked the first net selling day for individual stock buyers since 2023, with retail risk appetite declining sharply from February highs.

Monetary Policy and the Stagflation Bind

The Federal Reserve's position deteriorated meaningfully across the week. Entering 2026, rate cuts were consensus; by week's end, futures were briefly assigning a non-trivial probability to a hike at the April meeting. Three Fed officials expressed growing concern over inflation risks, with at least one explicitly ranking the oil price spike above labor market deterioration in the hierarchy of policy concerns. Chair Powell has signaled the committee will not even fully engage the question of whether to "look through" energy-driven inflation until the separate tariff impact has been assessed — a sequencing that effectively extends the Fed's holding pattern. Fed Governor Barr indicated rates may remain steady "for some time." The anticipated arrival of Kevin Warsh as the next Fed chair is also beginning to influence market pricing, arguably reinforcing the hawkish margin and complicating the debasement narrative that had underpinned gold's January surge.

Fiscal and Corporate Developments

The fiscal picture darkened as the week progressed. The Pentagon's $200 billion supplemental request, the Supreme Court's earlier invalidation of most of the tariff regime reducing anticipated revenue, and oil-driven inflation suppressing the probability of rate relief have combined to put Goldman Sachs economists projecting the deficit will exceed 6% of GDP through 2029. US import costs rose in February by the most in nearly four years, suggesting inflationary pressure was building before the Middle East conflict began. On the corporate side, unusual oil futures trading in the two minutes before Trump's Monday Truth Social post — approximately 6 million barrels changing hands against a five-day average of 700,000 — drew regulatory scrutiny. Apollo's redemption cap and the jury verdict finding Meta and Alphabet liable in a social media addiction case added to a corporate news backdrop that, while not individually decisive, collectively reflects a tightening risk environment. Average US diesel prices surpassed $5 per gallon by week's end, and United Airlines' CEO warned of potential fare increases of up to 20%, with Bloomberg Intelligence estimating sustained elevated jet fuel prices would raise US carrier costs by 15–23%.

Housing and Energy Policy Tensions

The housing market entered the spring selling season under visible stress. Thirty-year fixed mortgage rates climbed back to 6.4%, a six-month high, reversing sub-6% readings seen as recently as late February. KB Home lowered full-year guidance, citing the conflict as a contributing factor, while industry contacts at a JPMorgan homebuilder conference described conditions as "fragile." The administration's "drill, baby, drill" posture meanwhile faces a practical contradiction: US E&P stocks have risen only around 30% against a 40% rise in Brent, and the consumer impact — retail gasoline up roughly a third from a month ago — complicates the political calculus in ways the administration appears not yet to have fully resolved. Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac's $200 billion mortgage-backed securities purchase directive sits in evident tension with the broader rate environment. ---

Asia

  • Asian markets suffered their sharpest single-session decline since the conflict began on Monday, with MSCI Asia Pacific falling 3.7%, South Korea's Kospi tumbling 6.5%, and multiple regional currencies — the Korean won, Indian rupee, and Philippine peso — reaching multi-year or record lows.
  • China's relative insulation from the energy shock — rooted in renewables, EV penetration, and continued access to Russian and discounted Iranian crude — has positioned Beijing as a relative regional winner, with port throughput running more than 6% ahead of year-ago levels through mid-March.
  • South Korea, the Philippines, Vietnam, and Japan have all moved to emergency energy footing, with Manila declaring a national energy emergency and Tokyo executing its largest emergency oil stockpile release on record.
  • The Trump-Xi summit has been confirmed for May 14–15 in Beijing, providing a tentative diplomatic anchor for US-China relations, though Beijing has not formally confirmed the dates.
  • India faces a distinct BRICS-chair dilemma: unable to unify the bloc around a common position on the conflict while navigating its own acute energy vulnerability, with LPG delivery times stretching from five to twenty-five days in some regions.

China's Strategic Positioning

Xi Jinping's studied silence on the Iran conflict has crystallized into a coherent strategic posture. China's structural advantages — an accelerating renewables buildout, electric vehicles comprising roughly half of new car sales, and continued access to discounted Russian and Iranian crude following a limited US waiver on tanker-loaded Iranian oil — mean Beijing faces fewer immediate economic pressures than its regional peers. State-owned refiners have begun quietly exploring purchases of Iranian crude, with representatives of National Iranian Oil Company sounding out Asian buyers and China representing the most consequential potential customer. The development reflects Beijing's careful navigation between energy security and US diplomatic sensitivities. Simultaneously, China launched retaliatory trade investigations into US supply chain and renewable energy practices ahead of the May summit — staking out negotiating leverage rather than signaling premature accommodation. ChangXin Memory Technologies reportedly more than doubled revenue to approximately $8 billion in 2025, and Moonshot AI is exploring a Hong Kong IPO, reflecting continued momentum in China's AI and semiconductor sectors even amid the broader disruption.

Energy Emergency Across the Region

The contrast between China's relative resilience and the acute stress facing most of the rest of Asia is striking. South Korea stood up an emergency economic task force, implemented vehicle-usage restrictions in the public sector, and its prime minister invoked the need to prepare for "worst-case scenarios" — a signal of genuine institutional concern, compounded by reported redeployment of US air defense assets from the Korean Peninsula toward the Middle East. South Korean exports to China surged 69% in the first twenty days of March, with semiconductor shipments up 164%, illustrating how AI-driven trade flows are partially offsetting energy disruption. The Philippines declared a national energy emergency, with President Marcos warning grounded aircraft remain a "distinct possibility." Vietnam Airlines suspended select domestic routes. Japan executed its largest emergency oil stockpile release on record and its Finance Ministry made market inquiries about potential crude futures intervention. The IEA's executive director Fatih Birol, speaking in Canberra, characterized the supply disruption as equivalent in magnitude to the combined effect of the two 1970s oil crises and the 2022 European gas crisis — and emphasized that Asia bears disproportionate exposure given its structural dependence on Gulf crude.

Geopolitical Realignment

The energy crisis appears to be accelerating a cautious but meaningful reassessment of US alignment across the Indo-Pacific. Manila's exploration of cooperative oil and gas development with Beijing in disputed South China Sea waters — despite deepening US-Philippines security ties since 2022 — is the most striking illustration. President Marcos's approach reflects a wider pattern: the Iran war is forcing governments to weigh the costs of US alignment against immediate energy survival needs, particularly as Washington simultaneously applies trade pressure and reduces regional security commitments. India's dilemma as BRICS chair is similarly revealing — unable to unify a bloc spanning multiple sides of the dispute while managing its own acute vulnerability, with Prime Minister Modi discussing Hormuz directly with Trump. Wall Street banks including Bank of America, Citigroup, and Goldman Sachs have revised China's inflation forecasts upward for 2026, and BofA has withdrawn its call for two rate cuts. Bloomberg Economics analysis suggests China's property downturn is approximately 70% complete, with stabilization potentially achievable by 2027 — a considerably more benign trajectory than Japan's two-decade correction.

Japan and the BOJ Path

Japan's largest labor union secured average wage increases above 5% for a third consecutive year, maintaining the Bank of Japan's gradual tightening trajectory. However, rising oil prices combined with yen weakness are stoking stagflation concerns that complicate that path, prompting the Finance Minister to flag potential currency intervention and driving a rise in Japanese super-long bond yields. Sumitomo Mitsui Financial Group is reportedly developing contingency plans for a potential opportunistic takeover of Jefferies Financial, and Nintendo reduced Switch 2 production guidance by more than 30%, citing weaker-than-expected US holiday demand and rising memory chip costs. ---

Europe

  • UK ten-year gilt yields briefly breached 5% this week, surpassing even the extremes of the Liz Truss episode in duration if not velocity, as markets pivoted from pricing at least two 25 basis-point cuts to pricing at least two hikes by year-end.
  • The S&P Global Eurozone Composite PMI fell to 50.5 in March from 51.9 in February, with the largest deterioration in future output expectations since Russia's invasion of Ukraine and input prices rising at their fastest pace since February 2023.
  • Italy is preparing to cut its 2026 GDP growth forecast to as low as 0.5%, and the OECD concluded the UK faces the largest output hit among G7 nations this year.
  • Goldman Sachs estimates euro area headline inflation could rise by nearly 3 percentage points over the next year in a severely adverse oil scenario — the largest projected impact among major economies.
  • Trump's US envoy to the EU warned that American LNG could be redirected to other buyers if Brussels fails to accelerate trade deal progress, making explicit the energy-as-leverage dimension of the transatlantic relationship.

Sovereign Bond Stress and Monetary Policy

UK gilt markets were the most acute developed-economy sovereign stress point of the week. Ten-year yields briefly breached 5% before partially retracing following Trump's temporary extension of his strike deadline, returning to levels last seen before the Global Financial Crisis. The driver this cycle is less directly fiscal than in 2022 and more about the Bank of England's constrained policy options: markets have pivoted sharply from cutting to hiking expectations as inflation break-even spreads over US equivalents — which had narrowed under the Labour government's stabilizing market credibility — reversed sharply. UK gilts remain on track for their worst monthly performance since the Truss-era crisis. Bank of England Governor Bailey attended an emergency cabinet meeting convened by Prime Minister Starmer, underscoring the severity of the dual fiscal-monetary stress the UK faces. British factory input costs rose in March at the fastest pace in more than three decades. The ECB, by contrast, is moving more deliberately. President Lagarde used the ECB Watchers Conference in Frankfurt to signal the bank will not act until it has "sufficient information on the size and persistence" of the energy shock — retaining more credibility cushion than the Bank of England, though ECB Vice President-designate Vujcic explicitly warned that stagflation risk is increasing and that the institution must remain "very agile and vigilant."

Growth Deterioration and Corporate Developments

The first comprehensive economic health check since the conflict began confirmed deterioration across the eurozone. Germany's composite PMI declined more than expected; France recorded a third consecutive month of contraction in services. Italy is revising its 2026 GDP forecast toward 0.5%, and UK retail sales declined for the first time in three months. Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Hatzius noted that his estimates "may underestimate the nonlinear impact" of a severe gas shock — an unusual acknowledgment of tail risk not captured in central forecasts. German economist Isabella Weber drew a pointed parallel to the early weeks of 2020, when catastrophe was evident but its full dimensions still unclear. On the corporate side, Italy's Poste Italiane made a €12.5 billion offer for full control of Telecom Italia, with Poste shares falling 7% on Monday and Telecom Italia rising 4.2%. Pernod Ricard entered preliminary discussions to acquire Brown-Forman, owner of Jack Daniel's, with Brown-Forman Class B shares rising as much as 21% and Pernod falling 5.7% in Paris. Henkel reached a deal to acquire hair-care brand Olaplex for $1.4 billion, with Olaplex shares surging nearly 50%. Novo Nordisk's market capitalization has declined by nearly $500 billion over twenty-one months as patent expirations in India and China enable generic GLP-1 drugs at prices as low as $14. Airfare disruption adds further consumer cost, with Asia-Europe route prices rising as much as 560% this month according to Alton Aviation.

Political Developments

European political news was active across multiple fronts. In France, Socialist Emmanuel Grégoire won the Paris mayoral race, extending the left's hold on the capital, while Marine Le Pen's National Rally fell short in its bid to take control of major cities — a result that may temper expectations ahead of next year's presidential election. In Germany, Chancellor Merz's CDU dislodged the Social Democrats from power in Rhineland-Palatinate after thirty-five years, adding complications for the SPD within the governing coalition. Slovenia's Prime Minister Golob secured a narrow victory over nationalist Janša in the country's closest election on record, with coalition negotiations expected to be protracted. Italian Prime Minister Meloni suffered a referendum defeat on judicial reform, a self-inflicted setback approximately a year before a general election. UK Prime Minister Starmer, whose prediction market probability of exiting office before year-end stands at 68%, signaled publicly he will not be pressured by Trump, while Trump's criticism of NATO's refusal to support US military operations — warning he would "remember" the alliance's response — added institutional tension across the Atlantic. Germany is separately drafting contingency plans to counter US pressure, including the potential targeting of White House-aligned tech firms in any future trade dispute. ---

Global

  • Brent crude traded near $108–$113 per barrel across the week, on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990, with BloombergNEF's base case at $106.70 for April and a mid-June Hormuz reopening scenario implying $154.40.
  • Gold posted its worst weekly decline since 1983 — down 10.5% — before recovering modestly on ceasefire headlines, with the metal now roughly 18% below its January peak above $5,000 per ounce.
  • Trump extended his military strike pause by ten days to April 6, citing diplomatic progress, though Iran publicly dismissed US overtures and continued missile and drone attacks on Gulf states and Israel through the week.
  • The OECD formally revised its 2026 global growth projection, leaving it unchanged at 2.9% while trimming 2027 by 0.1 percentage point to 3.0% and raising G20 average inflation to 4.0% from 2.8% in December.
  • Reports that Iran is permitting Hormuz passage for vessels paying for oil in yuan have prompted Deutsche Bank to flag the conflict as a potential catalyst for erosion in petrodollar dominance.

The Strait and the Strategic Impasse

The US-Iran conflict, now one month old, remains the organizing fact of global markets. Trump's weekend ultimatum — threatening to bomb Iranian power plants unless the Strait of Hormuz was reopened within forty-eight hours — was effectively rolled back Monday after markets sold off sharply, with the deadline extended by five days and subsequently by a further ten, to April 6, following what the administration described as diplomatic progress. Iran publicly rejected those characterizations and continued striking Gulf states and Israel throughout the week. Former MI6 chief Alex Young assessed that the US "lost the initiative to Iran about two weeks ago." The structural difficulty is that even a ceasefire would not immediately resolve the Strait question: a CBS report indicated approximately a dozen Iranian mines are now present in the waterway, raising questions about the timeline and logistics of any reopening. Iran's reported draft legislation to impose passage fees on commercial vessels transiting the strait — and its reported practice of permitting passage for vessels paying in yuan — represents a form of de facto toll extraction and monetary leverage that persists regardless of near-term political outcomes. Prediction markets place the probability of a ceasefire by end of April at no better than fifty-fifty.

Energy Markets and the Commodity Transmission

The supply disruption is, by the IEA's assessment, comparable in magnitude to the combined effect of the 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis. Dallas Fed modeling suggests a Strait shutdown lasting through June would reduce global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points next quarter. Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — the world's largest — has sustained damage estimated to require years to fully repair, affecting both near-term supply and long-run investment calculus. Iran stopped natural gas exports to Turkey following an Israeli strike on the South Pars gas field. Saudi Arabia and the UAE have reportedly taken steps toward joining the conflict on the US-Israeli side, including Riyadh granting US military access to King Fahd Air Base. Saudi tanker freight rates from Yanbu — the Red Sea alternative routing — have eased somewhat as capacity arrives but remain well above pre-conflict levels. The Brent-WTI spread has widened to levels exceeding those seen during the Ukraine invasion, reflecting the US's relative insulation as a major domestic producer. Urea, the world's most widely used nitrogen fertilizer, has reached its highest level in more than three years, with the risk of a feedback loop from energy into food prices real and precedented in the 2008 rice crisis. A suspected sea-drone attack on a crude tanker in the Black Sea added a further dimension to energy market risk. Iran appears to be a net financial beneficiary of the disruption — as the only major exporter currently able to utilize the strait freely, Tehran has reportedly earned hundreds of millions in incremental oil revenue since hostilities began.

Gold, the Dollar, and Financial Markets

Gold's behavior continues to challenge conventional safe-haven narratives. The metal fell 10.5% in its worst weekly decline since 1983 before recovering modestly on ceasefire headlines — the selloff appearing to reflect a confluence of overbought positioning entering the conflict, dollar strength as an alternative haven, and the shift in rate expectations undermining the debasement trade thesis. Turkey sold approximately $8 billion — around sixty tons — of gold reserves this month to defend its currency, a pace larger than concurrent ETF outflows; should other energy-importing central banks follow, the narrative of central banks as consistent net buyers that has underpinned gold's more-than-doubling since 2022 would face meaningful revision. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on track for its largest monthly gain since July, an approximately 2% advance that complicates the global inflation picture by raising local-currency oil and food costs for importing nations. JPMorgan strategists turned bullish on the dollar for the first time in a year, with speculative positioning flipping from the most net-short in five years to net-long. The correlation between gold and real yields that anchored its valuation framework through the post-GFC era appears to have broken down; whether this represents a structural regime change or a temporary dislocation remains genuinely uncertain.

Geopolitical Realignment and the Energy Transition

The conflict is functioning as a stress test for the post-Cold War architecture simultaneously across multiple dimensions. Nations that had pivoted away from China and Russia toward deeper US alignment are reassessing that strategy under the combined pressures of trade tariffs, energy disruption, and reduced security commitments — a dynamic visible from Manila to Ankara. European Commission Executive Vice-President Ribera argued explicitly this week that fossil fuel market disruption would prove "a very relevant driver of the green economy," and early data suggest the conflict is accelerating EV adoption, rooftop solar uptake, and electrification of cooking across several markets. Pakistan stands to save an estimated $6.3 billion this year by substituting solar for oil and gas at elevated prices — a structural response the war may be advancing beyond what the energy transition alone would have achieved. Satellite data show elevated flaring at impacted Gulf facilities, adding an underappreciated emissions dimension to the conflict's footprint. The EU-Australia free trade agreement, concluded this week in Sydney with European Commission President von der Leyen taking an implicit swipe at US tariff policy and Chinese critical minerals restrictions, reflects Europe's broader effort to diversify strategic partnerships amid simultaneous pressure from multiple directions. The honest assessment is that the range of outcomes remains wide, and current market pricing may not fully reflect the left tail of the distribution.
