European markets are navigating the Iran shock from a position of genuine but incomplete resilience. The Stoxx Europe 600 gained 0.3% on the day, masking significant divergence: France's CAC is down 7.6% in March, Switzerland's SMI off 8.1%. The European Central Bank meets this week against a backdrop where market expectations have shifted sharply toward rate hikes — a marked reversal from pre-war positioning — reflecting the continent's acute energy import dependence. Natural gas prices in Europe have risen more than 60% since hostilities began, and Dubai crude contracts for Gulf-loaded oil are up over 80% this month.

One cushioning factor is Europe's expanded renewable fleet. Rabobank estimates that electricity prices would be approximately one-third higher absent the growth in solar and wind capacity since the 2022 energy crisis, with France's nuclear fleet also operating more reliably than during that earlier episode. Evening price spikes in several countries nonetheless point to ongoing vulnerability, and analysts warn that seasonal support from solar output will fade heading into autumn.

On the fiscal front, Germany's €600 billion defense spending program is beginning to generate tangible economic stimulus, with defense suppliers reporting record order books across artillery shells, drones, and related equipment. However, Chancellor Friedrich Merz's political standing remains precarious: recent polling shows fewer than a quarter of Germans hold a positive view of him, and critics argue his broader reform agenda has stalled. The European Commission is reported to be in the final stages of concluding a long-delayed trade agreement with Australia, with Commission President von der Leyen potentially traveling to sign the deal as early as this weekend. In Switzerland, a People's Party initiative to cap the national population at 10 million ahead of a June referendum retains 45% support — a level the government views with concern.
