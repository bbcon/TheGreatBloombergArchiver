**ECB Policy and Succession**

February's most consequential European institutional development arrived late in the month when reports emerged that ECB President Christine Lagarde may step down before her mandate's formal October 2027 conclusion — calibrated, according to reporting, around the French presidential election in April 2027 and designed to preserve outgoing President Macron's ability to influence the appointment before a potential hard-right outcome. An ECB spokesperson confirmed only that Lagarde "has not taken any decision regarding the end of her term." Hawkish board member Isabel Schnabel stated she intends to serve her full term; Spain is actively campaigning for its own candidate; Dutch governor Klaas Knot appears the most plausible consensus choice. The succession process threatens to expose familiar fault lines between hawks and doves and between national and supranational interests. The policy backdrop is itself unsettled: euro-area HICP sank further below the 2% target through the month, sharpening the Governing Council's debate about how its reaction function handles sustained undershoots versus the overshoots of 2022–23. The February flash PMI surprised to the upside, with manufacturers recording their best performance since 2022 driven by an unexpected improvement in Germany. Q4 negotiated wages rose 3%, a figure the ECB will monitor carefully as it balances disinflation progress against residual services-sector price pressures. ECB Executive Board member Schnabel called for accelerated structural reform throughout the month, underscoring official concern that monetary policy alone cannot close the growth gap with the US. The BofA fund manager survey showed the largest-ever overweight on the euro versus the dollar, reflecting a broad institutional reappraisal of US asset concentration risk.

**UK: Political Risk and Monetary Easing**

Prime Minister Starmer's political position deteriorated materially through the month. The fallout centered on Peter Mandelson's appointment as US ambassador — complicated by disclosure that Mandelson had shared market-sensitive policy decisions with Jeffrey Epstein while serving as deputy prime minister — prompting the resignation of Starmer's chief of staff and communications director within 48 hours of the most intense coverage. Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar's public call for Starmer to stand down represented the most significant internal rupture to date; parliament voted to compel disclosure of related documents. Starmer secured a temporary reprieve by winning public backing from his full cabinet, though allies characterized the relief as tactical rather than strategic. Prediction markets by mid-week of the final week priced departure by end-June. Gilt yields reflected a renewed political risk premium, and hedge funds extended short positions in sterling against the euro, citing the combination of political turmoil and Bank of England rate-cut expectations. The succession question may prove the more durable concern for fixed income: the most plausible candidates sit to Starmer's left and are perceived as more fiscally expansive, adding a medium-term risk premium that gilt markets remain especially sensitive to following the 2022 Truss episode. On the monetary side, the BOE held in a notably close 5-4 vote earlier in the month, and Governor Bailey's subsequent endorsement of roughly 50% probability of a March cut shifted the interpretation toward resumed easing. By month's end, UK unemployment rose to its highest since the pandemic, youth unemployment climbed above the EU average for the first time, and wage growth eased, prompting traders to fully price 50 basis points of cuts by year-end. Against that, the UK recorded its largest budget surplus on record during the period, boosted by surging capital gains tax receipts and lower debt servicing costs — a brief fiscal respite for a government navigating difficult spending decisions. Q4 GDP barely expanded, with services stagnating. Chancellor Merz meanwhile pursued Middle Eastern energy suppliers to reduce German reliance on US LNG, and German factory orders rose at their fastest pace in two years, offering a constructive data point for an economy that closed 2025 with stronger-than-expected growth.

**Corporate Developments and Auto Sector Restructuring**

February produced a notable dispersion of corporate outcomes across Europe. Novo Nordisk's fall of as much as 20% in Copenhagen — one of the largest single-day declines in its history — on sharply weaker sales guidance reset consensus assumptions on GLP-1 competitive dynamics and carried index-level implications for Denmark; the company subsequently announced approximately 5,000 job cuts among the largest layoffs in Danish history. Stellantis's €22 billion in EV-related charges — exceeding analyst expectations and covering model cancellations and supplier compensation — extended the auto sector's retreat from electrification timelines, with implications for European supply chains and further questions about the continent's path toward 2035 combustion-engine phase-out targets. Airbus's disclosure that Pratt & Whitney has failed to deliver engines in sufficient volume to meet production targets is a material setback, complicating an already complex order backlog and shifting accountability toward the supply chain, contributing to a 6.7% fall on Thursday. Shell reported adjusted net income of $3.26 billion in Q4, down 11% year-on-year, on softer crude and weak trading, but maintained its $3.5 billion quarterly buyback. BP, by contrast, suspended its $750 million quarterly buyback and withdrew capital-return guidance, with shares falling as much as 7.7% across the final week — a divergence from Chevron, Exxon, Shell, and TotalEnergies that will weigh on its relative valuation until incoming CEO Meg O'Neill communicates a credible deleveraging pathway. Equinor cut its 2026 buyback to as much as $1.5 billion from $5 billion in 2025. UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat. Nuveen agreed to acquire Schroders for $13.5 billion at a roughly 29% premium, ending more than two centuries of independence for the UK's largest standalone asset manager. The proposed Rio Tinto–Glencore tie-up collapsed within 24 hours in the third week over price disagreements; Glencore shares fell roughly 11%. Bayer agreed to pay more than $7 billion to resolve Roundup litigation, potentially closing a years-long legal overhang. Adyen fell 20% in Amsterdam and Dassault Systèmes dropped around 20% in Paris, extending the AI-displacement selloff across European software and fintech. EU carbon allowances fell to their lowest since July as Slovakia, Germany, and France openly clashed over ETS reform at the Brussels summit, with JPMorgan analysts flagging that the resulting uncertainty is already impairing long-term green capex planning. French customs data provided concrete evidence of US tariff pass-through: Q4 2025 spirits exports fell 47%, wine 39%, and perfumes and cosmetics 25% — meaningful declines for export-sensitive French consumer categories.
