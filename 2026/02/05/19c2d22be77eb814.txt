That noise you hear is the sound of bubbles bursting <https://bloom.bg/46rlvBJ>
 or, at the very least, bouncing violently. Silver, which Citigroup last week
predicted <https://bloom.bg/45Rod3j> would continue to rally to $150 an ounce 
within three months, plunged as much as 17% <https://bloom.bg/3Zjt0XB> on 
Thursday to around $74 an ounce. That takes its loss in just five days to 
almost 40%.

Bitcoin — heralded by its proponents as a safe haven <https://bloom.bg/45JfCzO>
 during market stress— continues to dive. Back in October (or ‘Uptober 
<https://bloom.bg/3NWp5gZ>’ as the bulls call it), option traders were betting 
<https://bloom.bg/4qmvyzt> Bitcoin would climb to $140,000. Instead the 
cryptocurrency is on the verge of falling below $70,000 with the market gripped 
by what one fund manager calls a “crisis of faith” and Michael Burry warns 
could turn into a “death spiral <https://bloom.bg/46xmY9H>.” 

The tech industry, the jet fuel firing the stocks rocket for the past few 
years, is starting to hiccup. In the span of two days, hundreds of billions of 
dollars have beenwiped off <https://bloom.bg/3ZSdIJA> the value of equities, 
bonds and loans of companies across Silicon Valley. Software stocks areat the 
epicenter <https://bloom.bg/4qgPkMw>, with the value of those tracked in an 
iShares ETF falling by almost $1 trillion in seven days.

Sure, the selloff in tech <https://bloom.bg/45Nz9iq> began not by fears of a 
bubble but concern that AI is on theverge of demolishing 
<https://bloom.bg/45QQxD0> software company business models. Yet the selling 
quickly spread to chipmakers — with Advanced Micro Devicessinking 17% 
<https://bloom.bg/46pbR2D> after its sales forecast underwhelmed investors. 
Asia is not immune, with Samsung Electronics tumbling and a gauge of Chinese 
tech shares close to a bear market in Hong Kong.

“Three quarters of software stocks are in oversold territory, and the momentum 
trade that has been the way to play tech and software last year now is under 
severe pressure,” said Andrea Gabellone, head of global equities at KBC 
Securities.

Talk of bubbles <https://bloom.bg/4rv9lQm> has risen along with asset values 
in recent months, andcalling a top <https://bloom.bg/4qSqJ1I> is a fool’s 
business <https://bloom.bg/4rwARNs>. Yet with stomach-churning volatility 
<https://bloom.bg/4qVBiB3> becoming an almost every day occurrence, investors 
rotating into defensive (ie less bubbly) sectors and traditional havens looking 
more and morelike meme stocks <https://bloom.bg/4r59M4g>, the party mood is 
getting strained.—Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



The madness of crowds can be music to a shortseller’s ears. A Chinese trader 
has made a profit of almost $300 million betting against 
<https://bloom.bg/46uTnh6> silver. 

Bian Ximing, through his brokerage Zhongcai Futures, began ramping up silver 
shorts in the final week of January. He gained notoriety in China’s futures 
markets for aggressively betting on gold <https://bloom.bg/4qTlaA4> that began 
nearly four years ago, while his online philosophical musings about investing 
have gained a dedicated following.
Winning big: Bian Ximing (center). Zhongcai Merchants Investment Group Co. 



The extreme market swings from metals to currencies are also fueling a hiring 
push at hedge funds <https://bloom.bg/4ryiiJ0> and banks looking for traders to 
capitalize on the volatility.

Among the prime targets for multi-strategy firms are specialists in so-called 
volatility arbitrage — a way to profit from the difference between the price 
swings the market expects and those that actually occur. “In the last few days 
these guys have printed money,” said Tony Ernest, managing partner at hedge 
fund talent consultant Monroe Partners Asia.




HSBC is preparing to hand some bankers little or zero bonuses as the British 
lender seeks to emulate its Wall Street rivals with an “eat-what-you-kill” 
stance.

The bank  plans to encourage <https://bloom.bg/45Lgxj9> underperforming staff 
in divisions including investment banking and wealth management to depart 
following bonus payouts in the coming weeks, according to people familiar with 
the matter. This will also include those at managing director levels. For 
employees, this is the latest round of tough messaging coming from the 
leadership of Europe’s largest bank, which is in the midst of a major 
restructuring.
Signage for HSBC atop a bank in Shanghai, China. Photographer: Raul 
Ariano/Bloomberg 



China is said to have asked state firms to halt talks over new projects in 
Panama worth billions of dollars, asBeijing retaliates 
<https://bloom.bg/45SvFLI> after the Central American country voided a Hong 
Kong company’s contract to operate two ports along its strategic canal. China’s 
customs authorities are also stepping up inspections on Panamanian imports, 
such as bananas and coffee. The move came after Panama’s top court decision 
<https://bloom.bg/4qYO8yr> last week handed a win to President Donald Trump’s 
campaign to curb Chinese influence over strategic infrastructure in the 
Americas.




Shell said its fourth-quarter profit slumped, undershooting expectations as 
lower crude prices, a weak oil-trading performance and a struggling chemicals 
business dented earnings.

Europe’s largest oil company reported <https://bloom.bg/3ZPgxLo> adjusted net 
income of $3.26 billion for the quarter, down 11% from a year earlier and lower 
than the average analyst estimate of $3.51 billion. Shell maintained its 
quarterly share buyback of $3.5 billion even as lower oil prices challenge its 
plan toboost investor returns <https://bloom.bg/3NX2DnY> through 2030.




When private equity titan Leon Black needed help with creative tax and wealth 
strategies, he turned to <https://bloom.bg/3OpEW7O> Jeffrey Epstein. But when 
Black needed help with Epstein, he turned to a powerful Wall Street attorney 
and fixer, Brad Karp.

The largest release yet of Justice Department records on Epstein reveal that 
Karp, the chairman of elite law firm Paul Weiss, was often in near orbit as 
Black paid <https://bloom.bg/3ZQCPMV> the convicted sex offender $158 million 
for financial advice over the years — with Karp sometimes helping set the 
amounts. He and Epstein also formed what they both called a “friendship.” Those 
old ties just came to an abrupt head, with Karp resigning from the perch he 
held atop one of New York’s biggest law firms since 2008.
Brad Karp Photographer: Robin Platzer/Twin Images/Sipa USA 



The last major arms-control agreement between the US and Russia just expired, 
increasing the risk of anew arms race <https://bloom.bg/3NYTlI3> between the 
world’s two largest nuclear powers amid growing global instability.

The 2011 Strategic Arms Reduction Treaty, or New START, limited the size of 
the Russian and US nuclear arsenals and allowed for inspections and exchanges 
of information. Its demise leaves Moscow and Washington without a framework to 
regulate their strategic stockpiles for the first time since the depths of the 
Cold War in the 1980s.

The end of the accord “definitely doesn’t make the world safer,” said Pavel 
Podvig, a senior researcher at the United Nations Institute for Disarmament 
Research in Geneva. “The real loss will be a loss of transparency and it will 
increase political risks.”

 For Your Commute 



In the span of five years, Second Foundation has come from nowhere to dominate 
Europe’s most volatile power market, driven by algorithms and riddle-loving 
mathletes raised on science fiction and the ideas of American author Isaac 
Asimov.

The Prague-based company is now one of the biggest traders in Europe’s 
intraday market, where contracts are bought and sold almost in real time to 
match constantly shifting, renewable electricity supply. Once a niche corner 
left to the most junior traders, it’s now the fastest growing segment where 
gaining even a millisecond can give you an edge.

Second Foundation’s three founders, including Vojtech Kacena, draw inspiration 
from science fiction where elite mathematical minds shape civilization and 
artificial intelligence is boundless. The company itself takes its name from 
the third book in Asimov’sFoundation series, and each new employee is presented 
with a copy. Read the full story here <https://bloom.bg/45PbZZ6>.
