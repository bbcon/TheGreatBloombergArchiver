Daily Brief

Monday, May 25, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 futures rose 1.0% and Nasdaq 100 futures gained 1.5% on US-Iran deal optimism, while WTI crude fell 5.9% toward $90.93.
  • Fed Chair Kevin Warsh is expected to prioritize inflation-fighting credibility over White House pressure for rate cuts; rising real yields remain a structural headwind for long-term rates regardless of Hormuz resolution.
  • Trump administration's effective halt on onshore wind permits is putting roughly $50 billion in investments and 150,000 jobs at risk, according to industry groups.
  • Trump's upcoming medical examination ahead of his 80th birthday is drawing renewed scrutiny of his health.

Equity and Rate Markets

US equity futures rallied sharply as diplomatic signals around the Strait of Hormuz improved sentiment and oil prices fell, lifting risk appetite broadly. However, the rate picture remains more complicated. Investors appear to be looking through near-term energy price relief: rising real yields — rather than inflation expectations alone — explain much of the move higher in long-term US Treasury rates. ING's analysis suggests that even a full Hormuz reopening may leave long-duration rates "a tad stranded at elevated levels," as the structural driver of higher real yields is unlikely to reverse quickly.

Monetary Policy

At the Federal Reserve, markets appear to be pricing Chair Kevin Warsh as resistant to political pressure from the White House on rate cuts. Kevin Hassett, the president's chief economic adviser, argued that a Middle East deal would create room for the Fed to ease, but the market's working assumption is that Warsh will not preempt the data. The tension between a potential cyclical oil-price disinflationary impulse and persistently elevated real yields leaves the Fed's near-term path genuinely uncertain.

Energy Policy

The Trump administration's effective freeze on onshore wind permitting is creating measurable economic consequences, with a trade group estimating $50 billion in stranded investment risk. The policy diverges sharply from energy transition momentum elsewhere, and comes at a moment when Middle East supply disruptions are reinforcing the strategic case for domestic renewables diversification. ---

Asia

  • Global equities climbed toward record highs and Japanese government bonds rallied as Asia markets opened with optimism on US-Iran diplomacy; the dollar weakened.
  • Beijing launched its most forceful crackdown on cross-border equity trading, triggering an estimated $1.7 billion single-day wealth loss for Futu Holdings founder Leaf Li; broader capital outflow concerns are intensifying.
  • Singapore flagged a weakening 2026 outlook despite a strong Q1, citing Middle East energy disruption, tariff risks, and potential AI capex pullback.
  • Huawei's semiconductor chief outlined a new pathway to narrow the gap with TSMC, claiming potential breakthroughs without access to advanced equipment.

China Capital Controls

Beijing's crackdown on illicit cross-border stock trading — described as the most forceful in recent years — sent an unambiguous signal about the authorities' intent to arrest capital outflows that reached an estimated $1 trillion in 2025, the largest annual figure since records began in 2006. The immediate market casualty was Futu Holdings, whose US-listed shares fell sharply, cutting Leaf Li's fortune by more than half from its October peak. The episode illustrates a structural tension: Beijing wants financial engagement with global markets on its own terms, but retail investors have been voting with their capital.

Singapore and Regional Macro

Singapore's Ministry of Trade reported Q1 GDP growth of 1.0% quarter-on-quarter, well ahead of the 0.2% consensus, driven substantially by AI-related investment flows. However, the government's forward guidance struck a notably cautious tone, identifying the Middle East energy disruption, US tariff escalation risk, and a possible correction in AI capital spending as meaningful downside scenarios. Malaysia separately reported tangible economic fallout from the Iran conflict. The contrast between strong first-quarter momentum and deteriorating forward visibility is becoming a recurring theme across the region.

Huawei Semiconductors

Huawei's semiconductor chief He Tingbo, in a rare public appearance at an industry conference, outlined a strategy to close the manufacturing gap with TSMC — currently estimated at approximately five years — through a new developmental pathway that does not rely on leading-edge lithography equipment. The claim warrants scrutiny, as similar assertions have historically preceded more gradual progress than initially suggested, but the directional intent reinforces that US export controls have accelerated rather than halted China's domestic chip ambitions. ---

Europe

  • The ECB is edging toward a rate hike at its June meeting unless a durable US-Iran peace agreement is reached and sustained; President Lagarde confirmed the bank will revise its inflation outlook higher.
  • UK political paralysis deepens as Starmer's leadership position weakens, with government decision-making slowing and the left of the Labour party pressing wealth tax proposals.
  • Turkish police stormed the main CHP opposition party headquarters in Ankara using pepper spray, seizing control of the building in a politically charged escalation.
  • A Bloomberg investigation found companies across at least nine European countries purchased Chinese carbon credits from projects that may not exist, exposing significant governance failures in the EU's emissions offset framework.

ECB Policy

Two Governing Council members indicated the ECB is moving toward a rate increase at its June meeting, conditional on the Strait of Hormuz situation remaining unresolved. President Lagarde separately confirmed the bank will revise its inflation projections upward next month. The policy pivot is notable given the ECB's recent easing cycle: the energy shock from Middle East hostilities has effectively overridden the disinflationary forces that had been guiding the institution toward neutral. The conditionality on a "lasting peace deal" introduces genuine two-way risk to the rate path in coming weeks.

UK Political Risk

The UK's political situation continues to deteriorate. Commentary from Westminster suggests Starmer's administration is entering a period of effective policymaking paralysis as the leadership question remains unresolved. Market participants appear to be watching whether fiscal policy credibility is maintained during the transition — bond markets have thus far been relatively stable, though the risk of a leftward policy shift during any leadership contest is a live consideration. Andy Burnham's potential candidacy is drawing both attention and some skepticism from fixed-income investors.

Carbon Market Integrity

A Bloomberg investigation found that companies in at least nine European countries — including BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, EDF, ExxonMobil, and OMV — purchased carbon credits from Chinese projects in the EU's upstream emissions reduction scheme that appear to have been fraudulent or non-existent. German authorities have already voided credits from 30 projects claiming 2.1 million tons of CO₂ savings. No criminal penalties are expected for the purchasing companies, as acquisitions were made in good faith, but the episode raises fundamental questions about the governance architecture of European carbon markets ahead of the EU's planned expansion of credit-based mechanisms toward its 90% emissions reduction target for 2040. ---

Global

  • US and Iran are inching toward a deal to reopen the Strait of Hormuz; Brent crude fell below $100 and WTI dropped nearly 6%, though significant obstacles remain including disagreements over asset unfreezing.
  • Iran's Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei — who has not appeared in public since being injured in the war's opening strikes — faces the defining decision of his nascent leadership: accept an interim ceasefire or hold out for terms Washington will reject.
  • Russia launched a major missile-and-drone assault on Ukraine, including a hypersonic Oreshnik ballistic missile strike near Kyiv, in one of the conflict's larger single-day attacks.
  • Ebola may have killed more than 200 people in the Democratic Republic of Congo, with flights suspended and cross-border containment at risk.

Hormuz Diplomacy

The most consequential development across global markets remains the trajectory of US-Iran negotiations. Secretary of State Rubio struck an upbeat tone in New Delhi, and some vessel movement — including an Iraqi crude supertanker crossing the US blockade line into the Arabian Sea — has added practical weight to diplomatic optimism. ADNOC has reportedly been moving oil and gas shipments through the Gulf using its own fleet, suggesting informal clearances on both sides. However, Iran's Foreign Ministry explicitly stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent," and Tehran's demand to unfreeze assets remains a blocking issue. The gap between market pricing and diplomatic reality appears to be narrowing but not yet closed.

Geopolitical Crosscurrents

Beyond Hormuz, the geopolitical backdrop remains unsettled. Russia's use of a hypersonic Oreshnik missile in a mass strike involving 90 missiles and 600 drones against Ukrainian cities represents a continued escalation in the quality of munitions deployed, even as the front lines remain relatively static. In China, Xi Jinping reportedly expressed sharp criticism of Japan's Prime Minister Takaichi over Tokyo's defense buildup during the Beijing summit with Trump, a signal of persistent Sino-Japanese friction. In Turkey, the forced entry into CHP opposition headquarters marks a further erosion of political norms under the current government. These developments collectively suggest the global geopolitical risk premium, while partly priced through energy markets, has additional dimensions that remain underweighted in equity valuations.
