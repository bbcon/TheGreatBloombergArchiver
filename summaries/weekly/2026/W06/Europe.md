**Monetary Policy: ECB Holds, BOE Divided**
The week's most consequential policy development was the Bank of England's Thursday decision to hold rates on a notably close 5-4 vote, signaling a more divided committee than consensus had anticipated and leaving open the possibility of a resumption of easing in coming meetings — only one of 32 economists surveyed had expected a cut. Governor Andrew Bailey subsequently appeared to endorse market pricing of a roughly 50% probability of a March cut, shifting policy risk toward the labour market where softening signs have accumulated. Sterling and short sterling will remain sensitive to incoming wage and employment data. The ECB held rates as expected and Governing Council member Yannis Stournaras downplayed concerns about the euro's recent appreciation, characterizing the move as one being monitored rather than warranting alarm. Euro-area inflation continued to run below the ECB's 2% target, sharpening the policy debate; if the undershoot persists into the spring projection round, markets will likely lean toward pricing additional accommodation. This sits in some tension with firmer activity signals: German factory orders unexpectedly rose at their fastest pace in two years, reinforcing tentative signs of manufacturing recovery in an economy that closed 2025 with stronger-than-expected growth.

**UK Political Risk**
Prime Minister Keir Starmer's position deteriorated materially during the week, with Labour MPs describing a "febrile, mutinous mood" within the parliamentary party over his association with Peter Mandelson. Parliament voted to compel disclosure of documents related to Mandelson's appointment as US ambassador given his links to Jeffrey Epstein. The political strain began to register in markets, with sterling under pressure and gilt yields reflecting a renewed political risk premium, though no credible challenger has yet emerged. How long Starmer can persist absent a formal leadership challenge remains an open question that may increasingly weigh on UK asset sentiment.

**Corporate Developments**
Novo Nordisk plunged as much as 20% in Copenhagen Monday — among the largest single-day declines in the company's history — after forecasting a steep decline in sales, providing concrete evidence that competitive dynamics in GLP-1 obesity drugs are deteriorating faster than the sell-side had modeled. The move carries outsized implications for Denmark's equity index given Novo's index weight. Stellantis fell as much as 24% in Milan Thursday — a record intraday decline — after announcing €22 billion in charges tied to unwinding its EV strategy, including model cancellations and supplier compensation; the writedown joins similar moves at Ford, GM, and Porsche. Shell missed Q4 expectations with adjusted net income of $3.26 billion, down 11% year-on-year, though it maintained its $3.5 billion quarterly buyback. UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat, reinforcing the constructive narrative around large-cap European banks. Thomson Reuters fell as much as 20% during the week on AI disruption concerns. The proposed Rio Tinto–Glencore tie-up collapsed within 24 hours, reportedly over price, with Glencore shares falling roughly 11%. French customs data showed Q4 2025 US tariffs weighing materially on exports: spirits shipments fell 47%, wine 39%, and perfumes and cosmetics 25%. Chancellor Merz courted Middle Eastern energy suppliers to diversify German dependence on US LNG, a notable strategic pivot.
