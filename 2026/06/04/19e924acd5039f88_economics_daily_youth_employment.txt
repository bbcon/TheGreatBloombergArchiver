Youth Future

With AI killing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-30/ai-s-takeover-of-entry-level-tasks-is-making-college-grads-job-hunt-harder?cmpid=BBD060426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5164>
 some entry-level jobs and remote work leaving recent graduatessidelined 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-01/remote-work-is-what-s-hitting-young-grad-hiring-fed-study-says?cmpid=BBD060426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5164>
, the US job market has beenparticularly rough 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2026-job-hunt-stories/?cmpid=BBD060426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5164>
 for young workers, especially those with a college degree.

The weak hiring levels of the past couple of years have been punishing for 
those who are seeking work for the first time or have little experience — 
regardless of their level of education. But the job prospects for young adults 
are not as dim as they seem, according to a recent BNP Paribas note titled “The 
kids are alright.”

Contrary to the prevailing narrative, younger workers tend to be in sectors 
that aren’t as exposed to the impact from artificial intelligence as their 
older colleagues, economist Andrew Husby argues. A fraction of people age 16-24 
are software engineers, while many work in leisure, retail and other jobs that 
require personal interaction and being on-site.

Husby says the resilience of the labor market for younger people is 
underappreciated, and recent data appear to back him up. After spiking to a 
five-year high last year, the unemployment rate for people 16 to 24 has come 
down this year. At 9.5%, it is slightly below the average of the five years 
that preceded the Covid pandemic — well before anyone wasvibe coding 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/ing-s-vibe-coding-ai-is-building-its-new-trading-systems?cmpid=BBD060426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5164>
 and finance CEOs were talking about “lower-value human capital 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/stanchart-to-cut-corporate-functions-roles-by-more-than-15?cmpid=BBD060426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5164>
.”

Youth employment, which tends to be more sensitive to economic cycles than 
that of older workers, serve as a signal for the broader market, Husby argues. 
It can be a good gauge of both the effects from AI and for overall activity — 
which itself is boosted by the construction of data centers and the AI-fueled 
stock rally that’s been propping consumer spending in the country.

For every economist looking at the brighter side of the youth’s outlook and “
shorting the AI jobs apocalypse 
<https://forklightning.substack.com/p/shorting-the-ai-jobs-apocalypse>,” as 
Harvard’s David Deming put it, there’s another one providingevidence 
<https://digitaleconomy.stanford.edu/app/uploads/2025/11/CanariesintheCoalMine_Nov25.pdf>
 that, no, the kids are not alright.

Indicators over the coming months, starting with the May employment report due 
Friday, will help figure whether last year’s spike in youth unemployment above 
10% was a fluke or a peek into the future.

Need-to-Know Research

As Fed watchers await confirmation on whether newly installed Chairman Kevin 
Warsh will continue the practice of holding press conferences after policy 
decisions, they may need to prepare for not getting as much as they used to in 
the policy statement itself.

Back when Alan Greenspan ran the US central bank — and the practice of issuing 
regular statementsbegan <> — the verbiage was sparser than nowadays. Warsh has 
regularly praised the Greenspan era. Torsten Slok, chief economist at Apollo 
Global, also notes that the number of words in Federal Open Markets Committee 
statements tends to go up when financial markets are unsettled, as measured by 
the VIX index of equity volatility. Lately that gauge has dropped.

“With geopolitical risks easing and Fed Chair Kevin Warsh focused on 
simplifying Fed communication, the number of words in the FOMC statement could 
move down to the levels seen under Alan Greenspan,” Slok wrote in a note 
Wednesday.
