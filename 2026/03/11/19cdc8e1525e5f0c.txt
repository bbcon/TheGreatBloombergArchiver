Double-Edged Sword

As policymakers mull measures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/world-races-to-shield-oil-flows-after-iran-war-hits-supplies?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 to shield their economies from spiking energy prices stemming from thewar in 
Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-10/iran-s-attack-drones-and-missiles-put-us-military-under-unexpected-strain?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
, the nation using the largest share of oil coming via the Strait of Hormuz may 
prove best placed to weather the storm.

China has spent years diversifying its energy supplies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/china-s-energy-security-push-pays-off-as-war-roils-asia-refiners?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 and building a massive stockpile of oil reserves, providing a much-needed 
buffer. And while the prospect of a lengthy oil shock risks jolting prices 
higher in economies that had only recently got a handle on inflation, China’s 
leaders have been grappling with years of deflation, giving more room to digest 
increased energy costs.

But when it comes to inflation, there’s the good kind and the bad kind.

Morgan Stanley economists including Robin Xing point out that there could be 
some benefit from rising oil costs if it helps rein in deflation expectations, 
which have been relatively sticky in the past three years. But without stronger 
demand, any cost push will squeeze margins and weigh on the job market.

“For China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/pboc-employs-volatile-yuan-fixing-to-manage-iran-war-fallout?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
, stronger demand plus modest imported inflation would be the optimal 
combination,” they wrote. “For now, the bigger risk would be persistentenergy 
price surges 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/hormuz-disruptions-in-iran-war-will-hit-food-prices-not-just-oil-un?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 and the attendant slower global trade.”

There were glimmers of that Goldilocks scenario in data for February — before 
the US and Israeli attack on Iran began.Consumer-price growth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/china-consumer-prices-rebound-driven-by-record-holiday-spending?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 accelerated to the quickest in over three years and factory deflation 
moderated again, as household spending boomed during a later-than-usual Lunar 
New Year holiday.

Bloomberg Economics analysts David Qu 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AT63ATn0yGw/david-qu>, Eric Zhu 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/AVK6e_X298w/eric-zhu-economist> and Chang Shu 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/ATwU2T7qXnU/chang-shu> say that for China, the 
main risk stemming from the Iran war isn’t inflation, it’s the secondary shock 
of a sharp downturn in global demand for exports. They ran the numbers on two 
scenarios: external demand down 5% or 10%.

In the lighter scenario, annual GDP growth is 0.16 percentage point weaker. In 
the more severe scenario, the drag widens to 0.31 ppt.

As China’s leaders detailed their economic plans for the year last week, they 
pledged to curb the kind of “rat race 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/china-s-new-push-against-rat-race-what-s-changed-versus-2025?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
” competition that’s led to years of deflation and squeezed profits. It remains 
to be seen whether the war in Iran and resulting energy price increases aid or 
complicate that effort.

Need-to-Know Research

A supersized tax refund season was supposed to juice American household income 
and spending this year — before, as this newsletter noted yesterday, the Iran 
war and surge in oil costsupended things 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-10/iran-war-trump-tax-cut-risks-getting-getting-canceled-out-by-energy-price-surge?cmpid=BBD031126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
. Now a snapshot of the eve of the conflict shows that, indeed, refunds 
appeared to be boosting demand.

Credit and debit card spending climbed 3.3% last month from a year before, 
according to data from Bank of America, a giant issuer of such cards. That’s 
the strongest growth in more than three years, representing in part a rebound 
from winter weather in January, BofA Institute economists including David 
Tinsley wrote in a report Tuesday. While enlarged tax refunds have skewed 
toward higher-income households, lower-income ones also benefited, they said.

Early tax-filers have been spending refunds on electronics, home improvement, 
clothing and travel, the data showed. While the gap in spending growth between 
higher- and lower-income households narrowed slightly, the “K-shape” divergence 
“remains very substantial,” the economists wrote. There’s another large gap 
between higher- and middle-income groups, they said. “Divergence in wage growth 
underpins these trends” and means the “K” will likely remain.
