Jacinda Ardern is heading across the Tasman. The former New Zealand prime 
minister and her family arerelocating to Australia <https://bloom.bg/3ZTooYC>, 
becoming the most high-profile addition to a record wave of Kiwis leaving for 
greener, and often better-paid, pastures. After spending much of the past three 
years abroad following her 2023 resignation, Ardern, 45, will now base herself 
in Australia, where she has work commitments, according to a spokesman. The 
move also means more frequent trips back to New Zealand. Australian media have 
reported the family was recently spotted house hunting in Sydney’s Northern 
Beaches.

Ardern’s shift mirrors a broader trend: more than 60,000 
<https://bloom.bg/4tRSutn> New Zealand citizens left the country in 2025, with 
61% choosing Australia. The exodus, driven largely by higher wages and 
cost-of-living pressures at home, is set to loom large in November’s general 
election. Ardern, who rose to global prominence after taking office in 2017 and 
later led Labour to a historic 2020 landslide, has largely stepped away from 
frontline politics sincequitting <https://bloom.bg/4kXKTW2>, taking up 
fellowships at Harvard, joining the Earthshot Prize board and publishing her 
memoir. Now, she finds herself part of the very migration story reshaping the 
country she once led.-Jui Chakravorty



 What You Need to Know Today 



Some of Asia’s biggest lenders are circling HSBC’s Indonesian retail business, 
lining up bids <https://bloom.bg/4tX1jC4> in a deal that could fetch more than 
$200 million, sources said. Contenders including Singapore-based DBS, 
Oversea-Chinese Banking and United Overseas Bank as well as Malaysia’s CIMB and 
Japan’s Sumitomo Mitsui Financial are said to be preparing binding offers due 
in mid-March, the sources said, as HSBC presses ahead with astrategic review 
<https://bloom.bg/4sfZqyM> of consumer operations in some countries. The 
potential sale is part of CEO Georges Elhedery’s broader overhaul of the bank, 
which has seen job cuts and business exits as it sharpens its focus. For 
regional rivals, meanwhile,fast-growing Indonesia <https://bloom.bg/47dLTPZ> 
(Southeast Asia’s largest economy) is proving hard to resist, especially as 
global banks rethink their footprint.




Billionaire investor Raamdeo Agrawal, long one of India’s loudest market 
bulls, is strikinga rare cautionary note <https://bloom.bg/3MylZj4>: the 
country’s post-pandemic stock boom has largely enriched a narrow slice of the 
population, leaving more than 90% of Indians untouched by the wealth effect. 
While the flagshipNifty 50 <https://bloom.bg/4p9KUr0> has tripled since 2020, 
ownership of financial assets remains concentrated in just 5% to 7% of the 
population, raising concerns that weak income growth and soft consumer demand 
could weigh on the broader economy. With valuations among Asia’s priciest and 
foreign investors pulling billions this year, Agrawal warns that unless 
earnings and household incomes pick up, the rally risks running ahead of 
reality, even as he maintains his long-term faith in India’s growth story.
Raamdeo Agrawal. Photographer: Dhiraj Singh/Bloomberg 



Meanwhile India’s stock market is feeling the squeeze as authorities double 
down on efforts <https://bloom.bg/3ZZyjvL> to cool trading excesses, tightening 
bank lending <https://bloom.bg/3OLZI1I> to brokers and proprietary traders 
while raising taxes and margin requirements on derivatives. The coordinated 
push, aimed at “de-risking” the financial system and shielding bank balance 
sheets from volatility, threatens to damp volumes and risk appetite at a time 
when earnings growth is modest and valuations remain rich. With prop firms 
accounting for a hefty share of options and cash equity turnover, the curbs 
have rattled exchange and brokerage stocks, underscoring a clear message from 
regulators: stability first, even if it takes some froth out of the rally.




Apple is laying the groundwork to bring Apple Pay to India, holding talks with 
major lenders <https://bloom.bg/4sdQXvT> including ICICI, HDFC and Axis, as 
well as card giants Mastercard and Visa, for a potential mid-2026 launch, said 
people familiar with the matter. The service is expected to integrate with 
India’s dominant Unified Payments Interface alongside traditional card 
payments, a must in a market where UPI powers tens of billions of transactions 
each month and global players like Google Pay and Walmart’s PhonePe already 
compete fiercely. For Apple, the move would deepen its push into a fast-growing 
digital economy of 1.4 billion people, potentially boosting both services 
revenue and hardware appeal as itexpands manufacturing 
<https://bloom.bg/4aBhCNg> and retail footprints <https://bloom.bg/4kYwRmT> in 
the country.
A customer uses an iPhone to pay via the Apple Pay system in London, U.K. 
Photographer: Chris Ratcliffe/Bloomberg 



Hong Kong’s Court of Appeal has overturned Jimmy Lai’s 2022 fraud conviction, 
scrapping the prison term of five years and nine months 
<https://bloom.bg/4tXldN6> in a rare courtroom victory for the jailed media 
tycoon and pro-democracy figure. Judges ruled prosecutors failed to prove 
78-year-old Lai, who is already serving a 20-year sentence on national security 
charges, had acted deceitfully in relation to the use of his newspaper’s former 
headquarters. While the government could still take the case to the top court, 
the decision offers a small but notable reprieve in what has otherwise been a 
relentless legal campaign against one of Beijing’s most outspoken critics in 
Hong Kong.




The US and Iran meet again in Geneva for high-stakes nuclear talks 
<https://bloom.bg/4tMUaEe> just days before President Donald Trump’s deadline, 
and his threat of military action, kicks in. As Washington stages itslargest 
regional military buildup <https://bloom.bg/4u1vvw0> in decades, new satellite 
images show <https://bloom.bg/4u1aq4M> Tehran reinforcing and repairing key 
nuclear and missile sites hit last year, hardening tunnels and burying 
facilities in ways that challenge claims of an “easy” win. US air power can 
destroy buildings, but it cannot eliminate scientific expertise, stockpiled 
material or the political will to rebuild. Both sides say they want a deal, but 
the visuals are a reminder that even overwhelming force may only delay, not 
dismantle, Iran’s capabilities, keeping oil markets and the wider region firmly 
on edge.
A mural depicting a US drone at the former US embassy in Tehran.Photo by ATTA

Toyota is preparing to unwind about ¥3 trillion ($19 billion) 
<https://bloom.bg/4shXANX> in cross-shareholdings held by banks and financial 
institutions, Reuters reported, in a move that would mark a significant step in 
Japan’s long-running push to improve corporate governance. The potential sale, 
which could come as early as this year according tothe report 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZSJBlv>, would accelerate Toyota’s efforts to untangle 
strategic stakes amid pressure from regulators and investors to boost returns 
and transparency. While details remain fluid, the plan is being viewed as a 
governance positive even as the broader Toyota groupfaces scrutiny 
<https://bloom.bg/4sbRuyq> over its controversial bid to privatize Toyota 
Industries.

 For Your Commute 



Paris may have its skeptics, but this spring the city is making a compelling 
case for a culinary comeback, with a wave of creative chefs reimagining French 
and global flavors for a new era. From the Marais’ buzzy Eme, where French 
technique meets South American fire, to the fashion-set polish of 
Michelin-starred Trente-Trois near Avenue Montaigne, the scene spans power 
dining and tasting-menu artistry. Add in the soulful Persian comfort food at 
Namak, the effortlessly cool bistro fare at walk-in-friendly Le 52 Faubourg 
Saint-Denis, and the vegetable-driven brilliance of two-star David Toutain, and 
it’s clear: Paris isn’t coasting on nostalgia, it’s setting the table for a 
deliciously modern chapter.More here <https://bloom.bg/4aF1JFy> from my 
colleague Kavita Mokha.
