**Fed Chair Nomination Dominates the Week**
The week's defining development arrived on Friday when President Trump named Kevin Warsh as his nominee to succeed Jerome Powell when Powell's term concludes in May. The choice generated immediate friction between the administration's stated preferences and market interpretation: Trump has consistently pressed for lower rates, yet Warsh carries a reputation as a relative hawk, having criticized accommodative policy during and after his prior tenure as a Fed governor. Warsh has moved to align himself publicly with the president's monetary policy views, but his institutional record and historical concerns about asset-price distortions suggest any pivot toward easier policy would be constrained rather than immediate. Senate confirmation appears probable given the Republican majority, though it is not guaranteed. The substantive question — how Warsh balances political pressure against the Fed's institutional credibility — remains unresolved and is likely to dominate monetary policy discourse through the spring.

**Markets React to a Dual Headwind**
Friday's session reflected the compounding effect of the Warsh nomination alongside a hotter-than-expected PPI print, which reinforced concerns about sticky upstream inflation even as the Fed's tightening cycle appeared to be in its terminal phase. US equities declined on the day, with rate-sensitive sectors bearing the most pressure. The combination of a perceived hawkish successor and firmer producer prices effectively narrowed the market's window for early Fed easing — a narrative that had provided meaningful support to risk assets through much of January. The S&P's intraday reaction illustrated the degree to which monetary policy expectations remain the primary valuation anchor for US equities in the current environment.

**Precious Metals Reverse Sharply**
Gold and silver fell sharply on Friday, unwinding a portion of the safe-haven trade that had accumulated through January. The reversal was notable in its timing: both metals had earlier in the week approached or established record levels, fueled by geopolitical risk premia tied to Trump's renewed demands for US control over Greenland and the associated "Sell America" positioning that periodically surfaced through 2025. The speed of Friday's pullback suggests the trade had become crowded, with the Warsh nomination and PPI print providing the catalyst for a positioning squeeze rather than a fundamental reassessment of the underlying geopolitical environment.

**Advisor Positioning Tilts Toward Value**
Against a backdrop of elevated volatility, Bloomberg's wealth adviser survey revealed a measured rather than defensive response. Few managers counseled moving meaningfully into cash; instead, the tilt was toward value equities and selective international exposure, with Japanese equities cited favorably as a relative value alternative to stretched US mega-cap technology. The posture is consistent with a view that current market turbulence reflects repricing rather than regime change — though the Warsh nomination introduces enough policy uncertainty to keep that judgment provisional through the confirmation process and the May transition.
