China's economy posted stronger-than-expected first-quarter growth, driven in significant part by an export surge as manufacturers moved to front-run US tariff escalation. While the headline figure offered reassurance, the composition of growth raises questions about durability: domestic consumption remains subdued relative to the export contribution, and the tariff environment that incentivized the front-loading may itself constrain external demand in coming quarters. Beijing's 15th five-year plan, approved in March, provided additional clarity on the government's long-term orientation, including a measured approach to climate commitments that analysts characterize as prioritizing energy security alongside emissions reduction rather than accelerating the transition. The plan signals continued state direction of industrial policy, with implications for capital allocation across technology, manufacturing, and energy sectors.

India's position in the evolving US-China technology decoupling faces fresh complications. Controls on Chinese technology exports are creating friction for Indian manufacturers attempting to scale production in sectors such as electronics and battery components — industries that New Delhi has identified as central to its manufacturing ambition. India simultaneously published its own climate plan for 2035, which observers assess as cautious relative to the pace of emissions growth the country is experiencing. The broader theme across both economies is one of managed transition under significant resource and geopolitical constraint, rather than transformational acceleration.

Elsewhere in the region, Japan and South Korea continue to navigate the twin pressures of dollar strength — partly a function of US tariff uncertainty — and domestic inflation dynamics that complicate central bank communication. No material policy shifts were signaled this week from either the Bank of Japan or the Bank of Korea.
