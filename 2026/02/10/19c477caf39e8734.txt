Au Revoir

It turns out that the end of Jerome Powell’s term 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/kevin-warsh-and-the-fed-will-his-nomination-for-chair-change-monetary-policy?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 as Fed chair in May will begin a changing of the guard in the Group of Seven.

That’s because the Bank of France governor, Francois Villeroy de Galhau, just 
unexpectedly announced hisdeparture in June 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/villeroy-quits-bank-of-france-letting-macron-pick-next-governor?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
. He was due to leave in October next year.

“This decision may come as a surprise to you,” he wrote to staff on Monday, 
explaining his “personal” choice to run a charity for troubled youngsters 
instead.

The cause may be noble, but such a move is also fairly exceptional. G-7 
central bank chiefs normally finish their terms. If they do leave early, it 
tends to be for big economic jobs: one such example was the exit of a previous 
French governor, Jean-Claude Trichet, to lead the European Central Bank.

Villeroy’s departure suddenly means that the choice of his successor 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-10/villeroy-exit-sparks-race-for-pivotal-bank-of-france-chief-role?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 will fall toEmmanuel Macron 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/macron-gifted-a-chance-to-start-making-france-more-le-pen-proof?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
, rather than to whomever succeeds him as president at elections next year. 
Current polling suggests that may be either Marine Le Pen or Jordan Bardella of 
the far-right National Rally.

So the current administration now has a golden chance to insulate one of 
France’s most important institutions from populist influence with a six-year 
appointment. And given Trump’s recent attempts to cajole the Fed into easing — 
a push againstcentral-bank independence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-13/global-central-bankers-in-full-solidarity-with-fed-s-powell?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 that Villeroy himself recently decried, along with global peers — the decision 
now looming for Macron might seem a serendipitous one.

“If it turns out that there’s more to this than personal reasons, then I see a 
defensive move by an elite that is trying to keep the republic standing,” said 
Sofia Ventura, a professor at the University of Bologna and author of books on 
French politics. “Leaving economic policy in the hands of people who don’t 
share certain values is troubling to some.”

Europe’s central banking community remains wary of Le Pen after her 2017 
election campaign centered on pullingFrance out of the euro 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/politics/articles/2017-02-06/le-pen-s-plan-to-break-up-the-euro-and-make-france-great-again>
. She has reversed course, but still wants to challenge Europe’s federal 
powers. Bardella meanwhile said in November that his party would push the ECB to
relaunch quantitative easing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-06/france-s-far-right-eyes-ecb-easing-as-solution-to-debt-problems?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 to tackle France’s bloated debt.

So did Villeroy jump, or was he pushed? The governor himself is adamant that 
any speculation on alternative motives isunfounded 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-02-10/the-french-elite-is-sounding-the-alarm-on-the-far-right-taking-power?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
.

“All those who know me as a free man, and who know my independence, know that 
if I had been asked, I would have refused,” he told newspaper Les Echos. “This 
is a personal decision.”

Need-to-Know Research

A typical lower-income British household would have to wait 137 years to see 
its living standards double, according to theResolution Foundation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-10/work-is-no-longer-a-route-out-of-poverty-says-uk-think-tank?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 — more than three times longer than in the past.

Publishing a new analysis of living standards, the think tank said high rents 
and low pay mean work is “no longer a route out of poverty” for millions of UK 
families. In-work poverty has replaced unemployment at the heart of Britain’s 
“economic malaise,” the report said, warning offurther political upheaval 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/starmer-shores-up-position-for-now-as-uk-rivals-bide-time?cmpid=BBD021026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 unless the trend can be reversed.

Resolution’s report, Unsung Britain 
<https://www.resolutionfoundation.org/publications/unsung-britain/>, 
highlighted that a slowdown in living standards among the 13 million 
working-age households in the poorest half of the country is fueling a turn to 
populism.

“If politicians want to regain the trust of the families, they need to get the 
economy growing again so that pay rises pick up, while also putting their 
specific needs at the heart of efforts to turn the country around,” said Ruth 
Curtice, Resolution chief executive. “If they fail to do so, the economic 
malaise facing Unsung Britain risks fueling further political disruption.”
