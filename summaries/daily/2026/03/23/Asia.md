Asian markets experienced their sharpest single-session decline since the Iran conflict began, with MSCI's Asia Pacific gauge falling 3.7% to near its year-to-date breakeven. South Korea's Kospi tumbled 6.5%, its worst session in years, while Hong Kong's Hang Seng declined 3.5%. Currencies across the region extended their depreciation: the Korean won fell to its lowest level since 2009, the Indian rupee and Philippine peso reached record lows, and the Australian dollar dropped 1.3%. Partial recovery followed Trump's decision to extend his Strait of Hormuz deadline.

The IEA's executive director Fatih Birol, speaking in Canberra, framed the supply disruption as equivalent to the two major 1970s oil crises and the 2022 European gas crisis combined — and emphasized that Asia bears disproportionate exposure given its structural dependence on Gulf crude. More than 40 energy assets across nine Middle Eastern countries have been severely damaged. Governments across the region are responding with fuel rationing measures: Myanmar has introduced barcode-based purchase limits; Pakistan has redirected its premier cricket league to a watch-from-home format to conserve fuel.

On China specifically, state-owned refiners have begun exploring purchases of Iranian crude following a limited US waiver allowing the sale of oil already loaded onto tankers. Representatives of National Iranian Oil Co. are quietly sounding out Asian buyers, with China the most consequential potential customer. The development reflects Beijing's balancing act between energy security needs and US diplomatic sensitivities. Wall Street banks — Bank of America, Citigroup, and Goldman Sachs — have revised China's inflation forecasts upward for 2026, and BofA has withdrawn its call for two rate cuts totaling 20 basis points. Separately, Bloomberg Economics analysis suggests China's property downturn is approximately 70% complete, with stabilization potentially achievable by 2027 — a timeline comparable to the US housing correction of 2006–2012, and considerably shorter than Japan's two-decade slump. Japan's largest labor union secured average wage increases above 5% for a third consecutive year, keeping the Bank of Japan on a gradual tightening path.
