The dominant story for US markets on March 9 is the cascading economic fallout from the US-Israeli war against Iran, now in its tenth day, compounded by a dismal February employment report that preceded the conflict's intensification. Brent crude surged as high as $119.50 per barrel intraday before partially retracing after reports of a prospective G7 coordinated strategic reserve release, but remained firmly above $100. S&P 500 futures declined 0.9%, a relatively contained move compared with broader global equity markets, though approximately $6 trillion in global market capitalization has been erased since hostilities commenced.

The February payrolls report, released Friday, delivered an unambiguous negative surprise: private sector payrolls recorded their first decline in five years, while the unemployment rate rose. Under normal circumstances, such data would reinforce expectations for Federal Reserve easing and satisfy political pressure from the Trump administration to cut rates. Instead, the implied fed funds rate in overnight index swap markets has risen over the past week, as oil-driven inflation expectations overwhelm the labor market signal. Oxford Economics maintains its baseline of Fed inaction until June, with two cuts thereafter, but acknowledges the war materially clouds the path. The February CPI report due Wednesday is expected to show benign core readings of approximately 0.2% month-on-month — a pre-war snapshot that will offer limited forward guidance given oil's near-30% surge in March alone.

The stagflationary tension is the central macro challenge: deteriorating labor markets argue for accommodation while an energy price shock argues against it. ING's Carsten Brzeski characterized this as a second supply-side inflation shock arriving while tariff pass-through remains unresolved. Consumer balance sheets were already showing stress — New York Fed data on credit card delinquencies indicated elevated household financial pressure — before gasoline prices reached their highest level in more than 18 months. President Trump dismissed the oil price surge as "a very small price to pay" for safety and peace, though midterm elections in November create political sensitivity around an affordability squeeze that the administration had previously sought to contain.

On the corporate side, Hims & Hers Health surged 49% after Novo Nordisk agreed to distribute its weight-loss drugs on the telehealth platform, ending a prolonged commercial dispute. Live Nation is reportedly close to settling its DOJ antitrust case on terms that would preserve the Ticketmaster business. Airline stocks fell more than 3% across American, Delta, and United on fuel cost exposure.
