Not as easy as it looks

Prediction markets are being touted on social media as a lucrative side hustle 
for young Americans squeezed by rent and student loan bills. In reality, most 
traders are losing money, and a significant amount in many cases.

Over 100,000 accounts lost at least $1,000 on Polymarket, one of the largest 
prediction markets, according toa Bloomberg News analysis 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/most-prediction-market-traders-are-losing-money-while-bots-rack-up-gains?cmpid=042926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3942>
 of every wallet active since the beginning of 2025. That is almost twice the 
number that made at least that much.

Among the winners, a majority of the profits were raked in by a tiny slice of 
what look to be automated bots.Everyone else, in aggregate, lost $131 million.

“If you want to participate and you want to make a living out of this, you 
better be pretty darn good,” said Charles Martineau at the University of 
Toronto’s business school. —Carolyn Silverman 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWvKP2K2lFM/carolyn-silverman>, Nathaniel 
Popper <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AXOt2i-IqnE/nathaniel-popper>, and 
Marie Patino <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AT8YdozwhFs/marie-patino>

Public debt markets get a fresh look

As cracks appear in the world of private credit, investors are rediscovering 
the virtues of publicly traded debt markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/private-credit-s-rude-awakening-gives-bond-funds-a-new-edge?cmpid=042926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3942>
.

Money managers at Pimco, Janus Henderson and Baird Asset Management say retail 
clients have been receptive to their simple pitch on the merits of bond funds: 
solid yields, asset-price transparency and no restrictions on pulling money out.

Fixed-income funds, which can include government bonds, corporate debt and 
municipal bonds, have seen big inflows this year.Investors poured a record $260 
billion into US bond funds and ETFs in the first quarter, according to the 
Investment Company Institute. European fixed-income funds pulled in about $18 
billion, according to EPFR data cited by Bank of America Corp.

“There’s just a renewed appreciation for the liquidity and the attractiveness 
of public markets right now,” said Warren Pierson of Baird, which manages about 
$190 billion in bond funds. —Michael MacKenzie
