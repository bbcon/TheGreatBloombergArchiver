Time to Sell the F****** Market?

Last week, the global stock market reacted to some noises out of Iran 
suggesting that a negotiated settlement could happen as though it marked a 
major inflection point in the war. After the best rally in months on Tuesday, 
those gains stayed intact into the quiet Easter weekend period:

Will that judgment hold up? As trading begins to resume — while many nations 
are off for Easter Monday — traders will have to digest the news that:

 * Iran brought down two US warplanes, and American special forces rescued the 
weapons officer of an F-15E after a harrowing two days. 
 * The US  destroyed <https://bloom.bg/3Oneufx> the biggest bridge in Iran. 
 * Iranian forces ramped up attacks on  Gulf energy facilities 
<https://bloom.bg/4mc1jKV>. 
 * And President Donald J. Trump, after convincing nobody in his speech 
Wednesday, told Iran to “Open the f****** Strait!” <https://bloom.bg/3O1e1ja> 
(there were no asterisks in his original post) or Tuesday would see attacks 
<https://bloom.bg/4twqmLb> on Iranian power plants and bridges.
He concluded: “You'll be living in Hell — JUST WATCH! Praise be to Allah.”

After all that, it seems fair to worry that Holy Week’s optimism might have 
dissipated by the time traders get to their desks again. Brent crude rebounded 
after Trump’s unfocused televised address Wednesday, and advanced further at 
the start of Monday’s trading in Asia:

Prediction markets similarly viewed that TV appearance very negatively. 
Bettors on Polymarket <https://bloom.bg/3N8tpK8> sharply marked down the 
chances of a ceasefire this month, or by the end of June — which is now seen as 
only a 50/50 shot:

A war that endures beyond June would be a disaster for the financial world. 
Futures suggest that US indexes will come under pressure when they open Monday. 
Everyone is at the mercy of the news flow, but it will need a very positive 
development to stave off another reverse for stocks.

A Good Good Friday (for Jobs)

If there’s reason for hope, it came on Good Friday from the US jobs market. To 
great surprise, non-farm payrolls rose by 178,000 last month, the biggest 
increase since 2024:

That followed surprisingly strong supply manager surveys of the global 
manufacturing sector. Set so that numbers above 50 show expansion rather than 
recession, the data collected by S&P Global suggest that manufacturing is 
expanding despite the war:

The supply manager data strengthens the evidence from the jobs numbers that a 
recession isn’t coming. The Sahm Rule, named for Bloomberg Opinion colleague 
Claudia Sahm, is triggered once the unemployment rate rises a half-percentage 
point from its base. That threshold was briefly breached in the summer of 2024, 
prompting the Federal Reserve to make a much-criticized “jumbo” cut to the fed 
funds rate. That rule now suggests little to worry about:

However, the market impact of the figures, released when most trading was 
closed for Easter, may not be great. That’s in part because labor data have 
been heavily prone to revision of late. Also, this jump doesn’t fit with other 
recent numbers. Wage rises are dwindling, in a sign that workers have less 
bargaining power and therefore that the market is losing oomph. Different 
measures of this have diverged over the last few years, but agree that wage 
growth is declining:

Then there’s the Job Openings and Labor Turnover Survey (JOLTS), which comes 
out with a month’s lag but also suggests a clear slowing. Workers are quitting 
less (suggesting reduced confidence they’ll find another job), while hiring is 
almost down to its lows from the recession that followed the Global Financial 
Crisis:

Meanwhile, in a sign that the employment picture is growing ossified as 
companies await some clarity in the midst of tariffs and wars, the average time 
people spend unemployed has risen back above six months:

On the face of it, the month’s data so far suggest that the economy is in 
decent shape and that rate hikes will be hard to justify. But traders might be 
excused if they don’t put too much weight on it.


Six Months of Takaichi

Japan’s Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi celebrates six months since she was 
elected leader of the Liberal Democratic Party in one of the country’s biggest 
and most consequential postwar surprises. She went on to establisha strong 
mandat <https://bloom.bg/4tAgss5>e in a snap election — but the galvanizing 
effect she had on equities hasn’t survived the shock of the Iran war. As Japan 
is a big energy importer, it is more exposed than most.

However, Takaichi has succeeded in jolting bond yields higher and convincing 
the world that the deflationary slump is over <https://bloom.bg/3NP5n7k>. Even 
though the returns on JGBs are far more competitive, the run on the yen to ever 
weaker levels continues:

Something will have to give soon. Takaichi has already proved herself a 
remarkable politician, but the toughest tests probably lie ahead.


A Year of Liberation

In another landmark, markets have now had a year to digest the Liberation Day 
<https://bloom.bg/3O1e3Yk> tariffs announced last April 2 
<https://bloom.bg/4bQ9edq>. The winners and losers are perhaps surprising. In 
general, whoever benefited most from globalization has lost the most — with war 
in Iran providing an extra kicker.

According to Bloomberg, the most successful stock markets since then have been 
Ghana <https://bloom.bg/4sf3bDW>, Zambia and Nigeria. While emerging and 
frontier markets prospered, particularly in Africa, Latin America and Eastern 
Europe, the Gulf Cooperation Council has had a terrible time as have China and 
particularly India:

Twenty years ago, “Chindia” was a popular portmanteau term. The world’s two 
most populous nations have continued to grow their economies impressively since 
then, with the popularity of India in recent years counteracting investors’ 
alarm at Xi Jinping’s attack on the Chinese private sector.

But Liberation Day and then the Iran war have been too much for Chindia: 

On a long-term view, it really should be time to buy Chindia 
<https://bloom.bg/4srBNTG> soon — perhaps once there’s some clear and viable 
resolution in Iran.


Deals Reign

You’re forgiven if you didn’t notice, but global dealmaking activity “quietly” 
recorded its most impressive first-quarter performance in history. Transaction 
values of about $1.3 trillion were almost 20% higher than the same period last 
year, according to Bloomberg, thanks to deals like Sysco Corp.’s $29.1 billion 
takeover of wholesaler Jetro Restaurant Depot andUnilever Plc’s $44.8 billion 
sale of its food business to McCormick & Co <https://bloom.bg/3NL3DvR>. — 
announced last week. With deals likeSpaceX-XAi <https://bloom.bg/4vvfi2X> still 
to come, this year’s haul is on track to eclipse 2025’s disappointment:

This has happened despite the Iran conflict. Inflationary pressures from the 
six-week-old war risk economic growth and raise the prospect of interest rate 
hikes. But the deal pipeline looks promising, and the prospect of a lucrative 
year remains.

That doesn’t mean the war hasn’t had an impact. Deal values have fallen about 
15% since it started. That suggests deals were frontloaded to start the year, 
while companies may now be holding back to gain more clarity on the war’s 
trajectory. It’s hard to criticize such inertia, given the mixed messaging 
surrounding objectives and timelines — while the hit to earnings multiples over 
the last month, almost as severe as the one that followed Liberation Day, 
suggests that this isn’t the best time to sell:

The war’s impact so far is following last year’s script, when tariffs 
curtailed optimism. Whether 2026 continues the same way and rebounds later will 
depend on the news from Iran.

Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon, who is cautiously bullish about dealmaking, 
notes that while it is difficult to predict the conflict’s broader economic 
effects, he expects this upswing to continue, though a protracted war or 
another exogenous event could change the current sentiment.

Bankers’ pain threshold is also in question. Bloomberg Opinion colleague Paul 
Davies points out thatthey seem <https://bloom.bg/3PPMtxH> to have good reasons 
to get deals over the finish line despite the war, but there’s no indication 
that those justifications will persist.

Troubles in the $2 trillion private credit market <https://bloom.bg/4duM1Ph> 
add an extra complication. The private market <https://bloom.bg/4dOWZzl> is an 
important source of capital for deals. To quote Solomon, “While we remain 
optimistic about the operating environment, it is not hard to come up with 
scenarios where risks become a lot more pronounced.”

As Dean Bell of KPMG notes, rising risk aversion tends to tighten lending 
standards, pushing up the cost of debt for dealmakers and limiting 
availability. If inflation persists and forces the Fed into rate hikes, deal 
financing becomes less compelling. But Bell remains optimistic:

Despite these headwinds, the fundamental drivers for M&A remain intact. Both 
corporate and private equity players are still focused on long-term strategic 
goals: portfolio transformation, market expansion and growth, and that will 
continue to fuel the M&A engine.

The war is also creating some winners. The Institute for Mergers, Acquisitions 
and Alliances notes capital is shifting toward defense and aerospace, 
cybersecurity, sovereign AI infrastructure and domestic energy, particularly 
LNG and renewables. The pivot makes sense as the conflict has elevated the 
importance of cybersecurity, and early-mover acquirers might be prepared to pay 
premium multiples to secure strategic positions. But like so much else, whether 
it continues will depend on the news from Iran.

— Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



I’m not a Catholic, but four years in Mexico gave me deep respect for the 
church’s teaching. As an antidote for the increasingly profane religious 
rhetoric coming from Washington and Tehran, I commend the pope’sUrbis et Orbi 
<https://bloom.bg/4vbY0Yf>. Jesus’ power, he said, was “wholly nonviolent”:

It is like that of a grain of wheat which, having rotted in the earth, grows, 
breaks through the clods, sprouts, and becomes a golden ear of wheat. It is 
even more like that of a human heart which, wounded by an offense, rejects the 
instinct for revenge and, filled with compassion, prays for the one who has 
committed the offense.

Brothers and sisters, this is the true strength that brings peace to humanity, 
because it fosters respectful relationships at every level: among individuals, 
families, social groups and nations. It does not seek private interests, but 
the common good; it does not seek to impose its own plan, but to help design 
and carry out a plan together with others.

You don’t have to be Catholic to find this appealing. You just need to be 
human. I hope that’s a positive thought, and have a great week everyone.
