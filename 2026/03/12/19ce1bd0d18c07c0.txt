Two Inflations

To know where you’re heading, it can help to know where you’ve been. With that 
in mind, the pre-Iran war readings on US inflation aren’t entirely devoid of 
information. But something striking has emerged with regard to the two main 
measures of prices facingAmerican consumers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/ceos-gain-confidence-in-us-economic-outlook-despite-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
.

The gauge that almost always runs cooler than the other one is now running 
materially hotter 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/us-core-inflation-slowed-as-expected-before-war-with-iran?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
. That’s the core PCE, or the cumbersomely worded personal consumption 
expenditures price index, taking out thevolatile food 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/hold-the-salad-as-lettuce-and-tomato-prices-surge-in-february?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 andenergy components 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/trump-softens-iran-tone-as-gas-prices-show-their-political-power?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
. It’s published a little later each month than the other one, the core 
consumer price index.

On Friday, economists see the government reporting the core PCE up 3.1% in 
January on a year-on-year basis — the most since April 2024. By contrast, the 
core CPI rose 2.5% that month, the least since the inflation breakout of spring 
2021. And Wednesday’s reading on February prices showed the CPI paceheld at 
that low rate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/tame-cpi-still-spells-trouble-for-fed-s-favored-inflation-gauge?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
.

Sifting through CPI figures that cross over to the PCE, things aren’t looking 
good for February. And that was all before the Iran war broke out and sent 
energy and other prices — such asfertilizer costs 
<https://www.agweb.com/news/policy/politics/farmers-face-skyrocketing-fertilizer-prices-there-short-and-long-term-fix>
 — soaring.

This is likely to send policymakers into different camps. The Trump 
administration has already been touting the CPI reports in arguing that price 
pressures are contained and the Federal Reserve ought to be slashing rates. 
While hawks at the US central bank point to a PCE gauge that showsinflation is 
running 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/global-bonds-erase-2026-gains-as-war-fuels-inflation-angst?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
 at a full percentage point above policymakers’ 2% target.

Worryingly for the doves, the chart above shows the PCE gauge started climbing 
before the CPI one in 2021 as inflation took off. If the January core PCE comes 
in as economists forecast, it would exceed the annual core CPI by one of the 
widest margins in decades.

The division stems from how each inflation measure weights certain items. CPI, 
which is produced by the Bureau of Labor Statistics, places a strong emphasis on
housing costs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/sub-6-mortgage-rates-vanish-as-iran-war-sparks-inflation-fears?cmpid=BBD031226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260312>
. A key metric known as rent of primary residence rose 0.1% from January, the 
least in five years. It also assigns a higher weight to used-car prices, which 
have fallen for three straight months.

The PCE focuses on certain goods much more, such as computer software and 
jewelry. Those both jumped notably in February’s CPI. Forecasters at Barclays 
Plc, Morgan Stanley and Bank of America see PCE core goods prices advancing at 
least 0.8% in February — some 10 times the increase shown in the latest CPI 
report.

PCE figures “should keep Fed officials cautious on inflation risks and on hold 
for now,” Citigroup economists including Veronica Clark wrote in a note 
Wednesday.

Need-to-Know Research

Gasoline prices have been soaring thanks to the jump in crude prices resulting 
from the war in Iran, but some locations are seeing bigger rises than others. 
On a country-by-country basis, the poor ones are getting hit harder. Within the 
US itself, Republican states are more at risk, according to Bloomberg Economics.

“Our analysis of personal consumption expenditures reveals consumers in states 
that voted for Trump in 2024 are likely to be hit hardest,” said BE’sChris 
Kennedy <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AXiAjm5I6so/chris-kennedy>. At $100 
a barrel crude oil prices — above current levels but below the Bloomberg 
Economics benchmark for a protracted Iran conflict — the nationwide share of 
spending going to fuel climbs to 2.3% from 1.9%, he wrote.

“The increase is more dramatic for the average consumer in states that voted 
for Trump in 2024, Kennedy, economic statecraft lead, wrote in a note 
Wednesday. Their fuel spending increases to 2.7% of all spending, from 2.2%, he 
said. “That shock is 70% larger than for consumers in states that voted for the 
Democratic candidate, Kamala Harris.”
