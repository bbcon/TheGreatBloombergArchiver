Kenya plans carbon exchange

By Eric Ombok 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AOc28s-IeZ0/eric-ombok?cmpid=BBD071126_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6415>
 andAdelaide Changole 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASvh4ZS5ZYw/adelaide-changole?cmpid=BBD071126_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6415>

Kenya is advancing plans to set up an exchange to trade carbon credits in the 
nation and East African region in the next 12 months as it looks to position 
itself as a hub for environmental finance on a continent wanting to monetize 
its vast natural resources.
A forest in Nairobi.Photographer: Simon Maina/AFP/Getty Images
State entities including the capital markets regulator and the Nairobi 
Securities Exchange are jointly setting up a team that will review 
carbon-market rules to boost the nationтАЩs competitiveness, Nairobi 
International Financial Center Chief Executive Officer Daniel Mainda said in an 
interview.

тАЬSector regulators are aligned, National Treasury is aligned, Ministry of 
Environment is aligned,тАЭ Mainda said. тАЬEverybody would like to get this thing 
going.тАЭ

KenyaтАЩs carbon-market regulations, published in 2024, established a national 
registry for projects. The rules require participating firms to obtain 
certification and validation by independent auditors and create incentives to 
support reduction and removal targets that are in line with nationally 
determined contributions. They also provide guidance on the annual social 
contribution for any carbon projects registered in the country.

The exchange will seek to formalize and scale trading of offsets that span 
reforestation, renewable energy, and clean cookstove initiatives.
