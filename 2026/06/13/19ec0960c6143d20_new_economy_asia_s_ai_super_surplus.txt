The US-Israel war with Iran has proven a major blow to Asian economies reliant 
on energy imports, but there’s another dynamic that — for some — is more than 
counteracting those headwinds: the massive US-led artificial intelligence 
investment surge.

Analysts continue to ramp up estimates for what Amazon.com Inc., Alphabet Inc. 
and other hyperscalers are likely to shell out. Morgan Stanley’s team as of 
last week placed the figure at $805 billion for this year — a cool 79% above 
2025 outlays — and some $1.12 trillion for next year. Perhaps even more 
extraordinary is that these amounts are unlikely to keep up with demand. 
Britain’s central bank chief has even signaled apotential need for AI rationing 
<bbg://news/stories/TG5MGOKJH6V4>.

Whether deploying AI at scale will end up igniting the productivity 
acceleration 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-27/why-today-s-ai-boom-won-t-repeat-the-1990s-economy?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
 some economists predict remains to be seen. But here and now, the investments 
are having real effects. One is a spike in Asian exports of advanced-technology 
products. Goldman Sachs Group Inc. economists have dubbed it the “AI super 
surplus” with regard to South Korea and Taiwan.

These current-account surpluses raise questions for policymakers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-28/the-economic-policy-dilemma-posed-by-340-000-bonuses?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
, including whether to channel surging corporate profits or stock-market gains 
into broader parts of the economy. For the moment, the amounts involved are 
seen as too large to be fully deployed domestically. Which means instead we’re 
hearing something of anecho from the past 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/circular-ai-boom-goes-global-as-asia-windfall-funds-hyperscalers?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
: a recycling of Asian surpluses back into the US.

US companies, of course, aren’t alone in plowing money into AI. President Xi 
Jinping has made all types of advanced technology a national priority in China. 
But Chinese companies are running into AI supply constraints, limiting the 
impetus they’re generating for the global economy.

“The impact of US export controls on high-end semiconductors is becoming more 
obvious as the scale of AI investment rises exponentially,” Tilly Zhang, China 
technology and industrial policy analyst at Gavekal, wrote in a note last week. 
“US export controls may be leaky, but they do prevent Chinese companies from 
making large-scale purchases of the key restricted products.”

American companies also are using their financial firepower to “lock up” 
AI-related supplies, Zhang wrote. For example, Taiwan Semiconductor 
Manufacturing Co. has reportedlyreserved a majority 
<https://www.cnbc.com/2026/04/08/tsmc-nvidia-advanced-packaging-intel.html> of 
its advanced packaging capacity for Nvidia Corp. And South Korea’s SK Hynix 
Inc. has sold out its 2026 high-bandwidth memory production, thanks in part to
Nvidia orders 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/nvidia-green-lit-big-three-memory-firms-to-supply-hbm4-ceo-says?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
. Chinese companies are falling “further down the waiting list,” Zhang said.

All that American-driven demand is showing up in a number of macroeconomic 
measures.

South Korea recently recorded its biggest increase in exports since the 1980s 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-01/s-korea-s-export-momentum-continues-backing-bok-s-hawkish-case?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
, bolstered by chips and IT product shipments. The nation’s central bank last 
month raised its 2026 growth forecast to 2.6% from 2%, largely a reflection of 
the booming semiconductor sector.

Taiwan, which is even more closely tied to tech, is seeing even greater 
impact. The island economy late last month boosted its 2026 GDP forecast to a 
blistering pace of 9.6%, off the back of the biggest export surgein 50 years 
<bbg://news/stories/TFSIHUKK3NY8>.

The flip side is that, while the US trade deficit has been shrinking thanks in 
part to President Donald Trump’s protectionist agenda, imports of capital goods 
including computer hardware, machinery and data center cooling equipment have 
been soaring.

Goldman economists say this giant stimulus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/goldman-sees-rates-pressure-as-ai-fuels-k-shaped-korea-taiwan?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
 could help South Korea’s AI-related exports climb to 30% of GDP this year, 
with Taiwan’s figure even higher. That would translate to current-account 
surpluses of more than 10% for Korea — a record — and 20% for Taiwan, a Goldman 
team including Goohoon Kwon wrote in a note last month.

Goldman is by no means alone in its assessment. Bank of America economists 
jacked up their estimate for South Korea’s surplus to 15% of GDP for the year, 
from a 10.1% prediction as recently as April. The new figure amounts to $289 
billion.

Where will all this extra cash go? Some will get deployed to domestic 
economies. One channel of transmission: Samsung Electronics Co. agreed last 
month to distribute the equivalent of$340,000 bonuses 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/samsung-chip-workers-to-get-average-340-000-bonus-in-ai-boom?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
 to chip workers.

But so much is coming in so fast that a lot will likely get recycled abroad, 
according to analysts including Louise Loo, head of Asia economics at Oxford 
Economics. “Export outperformance is now converting into corporate cash flow 
faster than domestic economies can absorb,” she wrote in a recent note.

In the past, large-scale Asian current-account surpluses used to end up 
pouring into US Treasuries — a phenomenon known as the Asian savings glut — 
which has the effect of depressing American interest rates. This time, the 
money is likely to appear at least in part as direct investment into the US. 
The Trump administration has been pushing the region to do exactly that, with 
Japan and Korea having pledged a combined $900 billion.

Nomura Holdings Inc. economists including Sonal Varma described the process as 
marking “a significant shift in the direction of capital flows, where Asia will 
help build US advancedmanufacturing facilities 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/us-cuts-agricultural-equipment-tariffs-citing-rising-farm-costs?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260613&utm_content=5040>
 to avert tariffs and qualify for subsidies, even as concerns grow that this 
will hollow out domestic manufacturing.”

Whether that’s exactly how things pan out remains to be seen. What’s not in 
question is that the scale of these cross-border cash flows is climbing higher, 
with the likelihood of significant macroeconomic effects in coming years.
