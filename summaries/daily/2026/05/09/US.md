The Federal Reserve's path toward rate cuts appears increasingly constrained by the ongoing US-Iran conflict, with surging oil and fertilizer prices sustaining inflationary pressure that pushes accommodation further out of reach. This represents a direct tension with President Trump's publicly stated desire for lower interest rates — a policy goal that the administration's own military engagements in the Gulf may be actively undermining. The self-reinforcing dynamic between energy prices and monetary policy expectations deserves close attention from investors pricing near-term Fed action.

On the dollar, the question of reserve currency durability has moved to the forefront at this week's Milken Institute conference in Los Angeles, where a range of institutional investors and policymakers expressed growing unease about dollar-asset concentration. EQT's Per Franzén, overseeing roughly €270 billion in private-market assets, noted that clients are actively reassessing their historical overweight to dollar-denominated holdings — a sentiment that, while not new, appears to be gaining institutional traction. Critically, the source of dollar vulnerability in this framing is not competitive displacement by the renminbi or another currency, but rather the accumulation of US policy errors. As Gateway Partners' V. Shankar observed, the dollar's demise, if it comes, will be self-inflicted rather than externally imposed.

The Gulf conflict is creating specific transmission mechanisms worth monitoring. Iran's reported collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin represents a de minimis but symbolically notable bypass of dollar payment infrastructure. More consequentially, China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US sanctions on Iranian oil directly challenges the enforcement architecture that gives dollar primacy its coercive utility. Treasury Secretary Bessent has identified dollar supremacy as the administration's top financial objective; the current operating environment is testing the internal coherence of that goal against other policy choices. Whether these strains prove transitory or structural remains genuinely uncertain.
