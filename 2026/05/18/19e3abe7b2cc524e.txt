Bond Jitters

“Every beating heart, becomes a part, of Paris in the spring,” is how the 
classic song ends. But analarming bond-market selloff 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/japan-10-year-yield-jumps-on-global-inflation-angst-as-oil-rises?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
, rather than the flowery season in the French capital, may be more the cause 
if finance ministers’ pulses turned out to be racing slightly faster on Monday.

Their gathering of the Group of Seven club was meant to dwell on global 
imbalances 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/world-imbalances-trouble-g-7-in-wake-of-trump-s-china-encounter?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
, focusing on the US budget deficit, weak European investment and feeble 
Chinese domestic demand. But another day of spiking bond yields following a 
surge on Fridayovershadowed the meeting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/bond-ructions-gatecrash-talks-by-g-7-on-skewed-world-economy?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
 as it began.

At the center of the market move before it eased off was a deepening slump in 
Japanese government bonds, adding fuel to a global selloff driven by fears that 
the standoff at the Strait of Hormuz could drive more inflation.

“The length of conflict just hasn’t been helpful,” Kim Crawford 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-18/conflict-has-raised-the-floor-for-bond-yields-jpmorgan-video?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
, a portfolio manager at JPMorgan, told Bloomberg TV. “The longer the Strait of 
Hormuz remains closed or impaired, the more global inflationary pressures 
build, the higher the risk of second-round effects.”

With the state of the global economy as their first topic, such matters were 
set to dominate the discussion of ministers and central bankers early on Monday.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-18/lescure-says-european-economy-proving-quite-resilient-video>
“We want to make sure the G-7 sends a message: We are in charge, we are 
following things, we are acting if necessary,” French Finance MinisterRoland 
Lescure 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-18/lescure-says-european-economy-proving-quite-resilient-video?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
 told Bloomberg TV, before reassuring reporters that the bond market is 
“correcting, but I wouldn’t go as far as to say it’s collapsing.”

European Central Bank chief Christine Lagarde, questioned on the debt gyrations
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/g-7-to-discuss-bond-selloff-sending-yields-to-multi-decade-highs?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
, said that “it’s always my job” to think about such things. Treasury Secretary 
Scott Bessentavoided the topic 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/bessent-calls-on-g-7-nations-others-to-increase-iran-sanctions?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
 in his own remarks to reporters.

The matter of imbalances is still likely to feature heavily, not least because 
the skewed pillars of global prosperity form some of the structural backdrop to 
any bond-market repricing.

Among those, for example, are fiscal deficits — with a reminder on Monday 
provided by Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, as she called for anextra 
budget 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/japan-s-takaichi-set-to-announce-extra-budget-amid-mideast-shock?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
. Data out Beijing on Monday meanwhile underscored theChinese element 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/china-economy-slows-sharply-as-investment-returns-to-contraction?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
 of weak domestic consumption in the imbalance equation.

Beyond such topics, the meeting will also cover matters such as critical 
minerals before turning to terror financing. Meanwhile Lescure was at pains to 
emphasize that, despite the test of nerves from investors, he and his 
colleagues do still see the global economy growing.

“Obviously I’m preoccupied — I mean who wouldn’t be,” he said. Even so, “there 
is a slowdown but there’s no recession. So no stagflation.”

The Week Ahead

crunch will again overshadow business surveys in the coming week gauging the 
impact of a third month of war in the Middle East.

Among the purchasing manager indexes for May measuring industrial activity in 
key economies, all of those for which Bloomberg polls analysts are projected to 
show continued expansion, in many cases bolstered by front-loaded stockbuilding.

Need-to-Know Research

As if the energy crisis weren’t enough, Nomura economists now warn that unless 
something changes,food prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/el-nino-odds-rise-as-storm-and-crop-threats-intensify-globally?cmpid=BBD051826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4570>
 may be heading higher as elevated energy and fertilizer costs combine with a 
potential extreme weather event in the form of the coming El Niño.

“The positive news is that there has been strong grain production in recent 
years, lifting stock-to-use ratios for wheat, maize and rice to healthy 
levels,” Nomura economist Rob Subbaraman and Yiru Chen wrote in a note Friday. 
“However, we posit this supply/demand equation could change quickly later this 
year.” A food-price surge could also become amplified by protectionism, 
financial speculation and hoarding, as seen in 2007-08 and 2010-11, they said.
Source: Nomura Global Markets Research
Nomura updated its “food vulnerability index” gauging 110 countries’ exposure 
to large food-price surges, depending on nominal GDP per capita, share of food 
in household budgets and net food imports. The calculations show the 
most-exposed nations include Montegro, Libya and Bangladesh, with New Zealand, 
Ireland and, perhaps surprisingly, Ecuador among the least vulnerable.
