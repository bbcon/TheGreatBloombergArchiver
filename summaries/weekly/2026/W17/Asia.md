**China: Growth Narrative and Structural Debate**

China's economic narrative shifted meaningfully across the week. GDP data had surprised to the upside in recent weeks, property indicators showed tentative green shoots, and equity markets recovered to near decade highs. Two influential NBER papers published this month examined whether Beijing has escaped the "Japanification" comparison: Rogoff and Yang warned that without structural reform, today's managed adjustment could evolve into stagnation, particularly if EV and solar exports face external constraints; Eurizon SLJ's Stephen Jen and Joana Freire argued more constructively that fierce domestic competition and US-China technological rivalry are sustaining productivity in ways that distinguish Beijing from Tokyo. The debate remains unresolved. The People's Bank of China injected additional liquidity mid-week as data revealed an unexpected deterioration in the labor market among early-career workers, with AI adoption cited as a contributing factor. China's "national team," led by Central Huijin Investment, appears to have stepped back from dominant ETF positions that stabilized markets during the earlier tech-led rally, with first-quarter filings showing holdings falling below the 20% disclosure threshold.

**China: Clean Technology and Trade Posture**

China's export profile continued to reflect both the structural demand shift toward clean energy and Beijing's ability to capitalize on the Hormuz disruption. Lithium-ion battery shipments rose 34% year-on-year in March, electric vehicles surged 53%, and solar cells increased 80%. US ethane imports are expected to hit a record 800,000 tons in April — approximately 60% above the monthly average — as Beijing pivots to alternative feedstocks. At the IMF spring meetings, China's delegation mounted a robust defense of its trade surpluses, attributing imbalances to cultural savings preferences rather than policy distortions, drawing skepticism from other delegations including Japan's finance minister. The State Council published rules holding provincial officials directly accountable for emissions targets including carbon peaking before 2030, with clean energy equities advancing on the news, Huadian Energy gaining as much as 10%. Regulators also announced plans to restrict technology companies from accepting US capital without government approval, framed as a response to Meta's acquisition of startup Manus.

**AI and Semiconductors Across the Region**

South Korean chip exports surged 183% year-on-year in the first 20 days of April, with overall exports up 49%, driven by AI-related semiconductor demand. Taiwan's export orders jumped 66% in March, the fastest pace in 16 years. Samsung shares have tripled and SK Hynix quadrupled since late August. SK Hynix reported a five-fold jump in quarterly profit on Thursday and reaffirmed plans to increase capital spending. Victory Giant, a Chinese Nvidia supplier, surged as much as 60% in its Hong Kong debut after raising $2.6 billion. TSMC shares rose as much as 5.1% to a record on Friday after Taiwan's Financial Supervisory Commission moved to relax a rule capping single-stock fund holdings at 10% of net asset value, a change JPMorgan estimated could draw more than $6 billion in inflows. DeepSeek unveiled preview versions of its V4 flagship model on Friday, introducing V4 Flash and V4 Pro with advances in coding benchmarks, reasoning, agentic capabilities, and a 1 million-token context window. Tencent and Alibaba are in preliminary discussions to participate in DeepSeek's maiden financing round.

**Japan and Strategic Reorientation**

Japan's cabinet approved the country's first overseas arms export framework since World War II, with government funding earmarked for defense R&D and a deal to supply warships to Australia already concluded. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi is pursuing a vision of greater defense self-sufficiency in response to rising threats from China and North Korea, though domestic polling shows 67% of Japanese respondents opposed. Japan participated for the first time in major naval exercises with the US and the Philippines, even as China's navy conducted drills in the western Pacific. March export data showed faster-than-expected growth on rebounding Chinese demand. The Bank of Japan is leaning toward holding rates unchanged at its next meeting, citing war-related uncertainty. The Topix Nonferrous Metals sub-index has gained over 400% since the Liberation Day tariff reversal, driven by data center component demand and corporate governance reform. North Korea conducted five tactical ballistic missile tests during the week, variants of the Hwasong-11, amid ongoing concern about Pyongyang accumulating combat experience through troop deployments supporting Russia in Ukraine.
