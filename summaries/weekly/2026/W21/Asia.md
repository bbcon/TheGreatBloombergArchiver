• Samsung averted a general strike on Wednesday after reaching a deal with its largest union — potentially distributing roughly ₩40 trillion ($26.6 billion) in AI-linked bonuses averaging $340,000 per chip employee — while SK Hynix's market capitalization reached $910 billion.

• Bank Indonesia surprised with a 50 basis point rate hike to 5.25% on Wednesday, while President Prabowo announced state centralization of palm oil, coal, and nickel exports — a move that blindsided senior officials and extended the Jakarta Composite's year-to-date decline to roughly 26%, the worst among major global benchmarks.

**China: Growth Disappointment and Post-Summit Ambiguity**

The week opened with April activity data that fell short across every major indicator. Retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year — the softest print since the post-Covid reopening of December 2022 — with auto sales down 15% and jewelry off 21%. Industrial production at 4.1% was the weakest in nearly three years, and fixed-asset investment unexpectedly contracted 1.6% year-to-date, a result no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated. The figures arrived against the backdrop of the post-Trump-Xi summit narrative, which produced a commitment to at least $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchases through 2028 but little operational detail on the broader trade framework. The "placeholder summit" characterization increasingly resonates: Beijing's reluctance to add domestic stimulus is now harder to sustain given the breadth of weakness, and the trade board established by the summit appears to cover only roughly $30 billion in flows. Capital flows nonetheless continue to favor China, with domestic banks receiving $61.9 billion in net inflows on behalf of clients in April — the sixth month of inflows in seven — though exchanges are reportedly scrutinizing AI-driven rallies and the so-called "national team" is cutting ETF holdings by roughly 90% in the first half of the year, suggesting officials are managing froth rather than endorsing the advance.

**The AI Rally and Korea's Concentration Risk**

Thursday's Nvidia-driven surge crystallized Asia's structural position in the AI supply chain. Samsung and TSMC have together added approximately $1.2 trillion in market value year-to-date, and South Korean export data for May 1–20 showed chip shipments up 202% year-on-year, lifting the broader trade balance into surplus. Japan posted a third consecutive monthly trade surplus as semiconductor exports rose 44%, partly offsetting a 64% collapse in crude import volumes reflecting Hormuz disruptions. The gains, however, sit alongside notable concentration risk: Korea's Kospi year-to-date gain of roughly 73% has been driven almost entirely by Samsung and SK Hynix, and retail margin debt reached a record ₩36.5 trillion ($24 billion) earlier in the week before Samsung's labor settlement provided some relief. Oxford Economics frames the resulting external surpluses as a narrower echo of the 2000s Asian savings glut, with capital recycling indirectly supporting US asset prices even as Treasury yields press higher.

**Indonesia's Policy Turn and Regional Stress**

Bank Indonesia's 50 basis point hike — double consensus expectations — and President Prabowo's sweeping reassertion of state control over commodity exports together represent the week's most consequential policy shift in emerging Asia. The export centralization through Danantara echoes earlier authoritarian-era practices and is likely to unsettle foreign buyers of Indonesian coal and palm oil, even as Jakarta frames the move as targeting under-invoicing. A Moody's warning over centralization risks followed. India faces analogous pressures: the rupee touched nearly 97 per dollar during the week and the Reserve Bank of India is reportedly weighing its 2013 taper-tantrum toolkit, including rate hikes and expanded FX swaps. The Philippines, Thailand, and Malaysia each face secondary effects from elevated energy costs — Thai and Philippine fishing fleets are idling on diesel prices, and Thai authorities have prepared a 200 billion baht ($6.1 billion) cost-of-living package.

**Japan and the BOJ Dilemma**

Japan's Q1 GDP expanded an annualized 2.1%, accelerating from a revised 0.8%, on stronger consumption and trade — a positive surprise that nonetheless sat alongside mounting fiscal and monetary tensions. PM Takaichi, after weeks of insisting a supplementary budget was unnecessary, reversed course and instructed the finance ministry to compile one, targeting roughly $19 billion including revived energy subsidies. The move pushed 30-year JGB yields to fresh post-1999 highs, with Finance Minister Katayama publicly flagging markets as a policy risk. Core inflation, meanwhile, slowed to a four-year low — ostensibly providing the BOJ room to pause — though a BOJ official signaled support for a near-term hike, placing June increasingly in play. Pimco has turned constructive on the Japanese long end, though the combination of fiscal expansion and tightening monetary bias remains an uncomfortable juxtaposition for JGB markets.
