Monday, May 25, 2026

US

Federal Reserve Under Competing Pressures

Fed Chair Kevin Warsh finds himself navigating a sharp tension between White House demands for rate cuts and the imperative to defend the central bank's inflation-fighting credibility. Markets appear to be betting Warsh will prioritize the latter. Kevin Hassett, the president's chief economic adviser, signaled confidence that a Hormuz deal would lower oil prices and thereby create space for easing — but that transmission remains contingent on a durable agreement that has yet to materialize.

Real Yields and the Rates Outlook

Even if a US-Iran agreement is reached and energy prices ease, long-term US rates may not follow correspondingly lower. ING analysts note that rising real yields — rather than inflation expectations alone — account for much of the recent move higher in nominal yields, suggesting that term rates could remain elevated even after any geopolitical resolution. This structural dynamic complicates both the Fed's optionality and the fiscal calculus in Washington.

Wind Energy Investment at Risk

Approximately $50 billion in onshore wind investments and an estimated 150,000 jobs are at risk following the Trump administration's effective halt on new project permit approvals, according to a wind industry trade group. The stall adds to broader questions about the trajectory of US clean energy deployment under the current administration, at a moment when the energy transition is already being tested by Middle East supply disruptions.

Political Backdrop

Trump's upcoming medical examination ahead of his 80th birthday is drawing renewed scrutiny of his health, while a California chemical tank incident that prompted the evacuation of over 50,000 people in the Los Angeles suburbs appears to have eased somewhat after a potential crack in the tank may reduce explosion risk. ---

Asia

China Capital Controls Rattle Markets

Beijing's most forceful crackdown yet on illicit cross-border equity trading sent shockwaves through Chinese financial markets. Futu Holdings founder Leaf Li saw his net worth fall $1.7 billion in a single session, with his fortune now less than half its late-October 2026 level. The move is part of a broader effort to stem capital outflows estimated at roughly $1 trillion in 2025 alone — the largest annual figure since data began in 2006. Chinese retail investors are reportedly scrambling to identify alternative channels, underscoring the depth of demand for offshore exposure even as authorities tighten controls.

Huawei Semiconductor Push

Huawei's semiconductor chief He Tingbo used a rare public appearance at an industry conference to outline a new pathway to close the gap with TSMC. The company and manufacturing partner SMIC currently lag TSMC by approximately five years in process capability; the announced approach suggests a potential acceleration, though the timeline and technical specifics remain unverified independently. The development will be closely watched by governments and chipmakers monitoring China's progress toward greater self-sufficiency in advanced semiconductors.

India: Rupee Pressure and Energy Costs

Reserve Bank of India Governor Sanjay Malhotra made the unusual step of publicly suggesting the rupee may now be undervalued following its recent depreciation — a signal analysts at ANZ interpret as an effort to arrest the currency's slide amid significant balance of payments pressures. The comments come ahead of the RBI's June 5 rate decision. Separately, India raised fuel prices for the fourth time in May, reflecting the direct pass-through of elevated global energy costs from the Hormuz disruption.

Singapore Flags Macro Risks

Singapore revised down its 2026 economic outlook despite a stronger-than-expected first quarter, citing Middle East energy disruption, US tariff risks, and potential pullback in AI capital spending as material downside risks to the outlook. ---

Europe

ECB Tilts Toward Hike

Two ECB Governing Council members indicated the central bank is moving toward a rate increase at its June meeting, absent a durable and sustained US-Iran peace agreement. President Christine Lagarde separately confirmed the ECB is set to revise its inflation forecasts higher next month. The juxtaposition is notable: the ECB faces the prospect of tightening into a growth environment already weakened by elevated energy costs — a configuration that presents a challenging policy trade-off between demand management and inflation control.

UK Political Paralysis

The leadership crisis surrounding Prime Minister Keir Starmer is beginning to have tangible policy consequences, with government decision-making reportedly slowing as Westminster waits for resolution. The uncertainty has reignited debate within the Labour Party over wealth taxes, with the left of the party gaining confidence to advocate more aggressively for redistribution. Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham has emerged as a potential successor figure, though bond markets appear to be underestimating his capacity to pursue fiscally credible governance, according to some commentary.

Carbon Market Integrity Under Scrutiny

A Bloomberg investigation has found that companies across at least nine European countries purchased carbon credits from Chinese projects that appear not to exist or were verified through a conflicted auditor-developer structure. In Germany alone, 30 invalidated projects had claimed to eliminate 2.1 million tons of CO₂. Major energy companies including BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil, and OMV were among purchasers. None face penalties, as purchases were made in good faith under existing rules — but the findings raise serious questions about governance integrity at a moment when the EU is targeting a 90% emissions reduction by 2040 with carbon credits playing a role. ---

Global

US-Iran Negotiations: Cautious Progress, Meaningful Uncertainty

The dominant market theme of the day is cautious optimism around a potential US-Iran agreement to reopen the Strait of Hormuz. Brent crude slid below $100 per barrel and WTI fell roughly 6%, while global equities climbed toward record levels. Secretary of State Rubio struck an upbeat tone from New Delhi, suggesting a deal could be close. However, Iran's Foreign Ministry pushed back on characterizations of imminent agreement, and Tehran's negotiating position — which includes demands for asset unfreezing that the US has resisted — remains a live obstacle. Trump has publicly said he will not rush, and any final approval may require several additional days.

Hormuz: Physical Flows Resume Partially

Even amid the diplomatic uncertainty, some physical oil movement has resumed. An Iraqi crude supertanker crossed the US blockade line into the Arabian Sea, and Abu Dhabi National Oil Company has been quietly moving shipments through the Gulf using its own fleet, navigating both Iranian naval presence and US warships. The partial resumption provides modest relief but falls well short of normal throughput.

Iran's Leadership Calculus

The strategic decision ultimately rests with Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei, who assumed power in March following the death of his father in US-Israeli strikes. Khamenei faces a genuine dilemma: accepting a deal would unfreeze significant Iranian assets but may be seen domestically as capitulation; holding out preserves economic leverage through elevated energy prices but extends the toll of a war that has battered Iran's military and economy. The mistrust runs deep on both sides, and the risk of a deal collapse remains real.

Ebola Outbreak Escalates

The Ebola outbreak in eastern Democratic Republic of Congo has now claimed more than 200 lives, with flights to Bunia suspended and regional health ministers warning of cross-border transmission risk. Violence and overwhelmed surveillance infrastructure are severely hampering containment efforts — a humanitarian situation that warrants close monitoring given the region's historical difficulty in controlling outbreaks in conflict-affected areas.
