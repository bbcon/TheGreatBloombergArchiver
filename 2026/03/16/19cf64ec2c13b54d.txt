Central bankers in the spotlight

The world’s top central banks convene this week facing a renewed inflation 
threat from the war in Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/world-s-top-central-banks-to-confront-fresh-inflation-threat-as-war-jolts-oil?cmpid=031626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 and the possibility that they’ll be forced to delay interest-rate cuts and in 
some cases consider hikes.

The message from markets is clear: Investors worried about surging oil prices 
and a widening US budget deficit have pushed bond yields higher. A gauge of 
Treasuries haserased its gains for the year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/us-treasuries-erase-2026-gains-as-inflationary-angst-rises?cmpid=031626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
. Bets that fully anticipated easier monetary policy in the US have eroded, and 
traders now are pricing in possible hikes in the UK and euro zone later in the 
year.

In the stock market, the specter of stagflation has sent prices lower around 
the globe.

Policy changes aren’t imminent: The Fed, ECB and Bank of England are all 
expected to keep borrowing costs steady as they assess how much surging energy 
costs will feed through to consumer prices and growth.(Some bond investors are 
sayingTreasuries are starting to look attractive 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/bond-market-in-oil-s-grip-ponders-shifting-focus-to-growth-worry?cmpid=031626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 with the 10-year yield above 4.25%, given the prospect that soaring energy 
prices will eventually depress the economy.)

Central banks are likely to offer a cautious tone as they acknowledge the risk 
of another inflation shock.

Much hinges on how long the conflict lasts — something markets are struggling 
to gauge. Investors have been whipsawed by oil-price swings and uncertainty 
over Donald Trump’s next move, raising questions about how quickly central 
bankers would react to fresh price pressures. —Jana Randow 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/APgqIL003O8/jana-randow>

Asia’s currency defense

Foreign-exchange bears beware. Asia’s financial authorities are stepping up 
efforts to support their currencies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/currency-bears-beware-asia-s-central-banks-are-drawing-a-line?cmpid=031626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 and they have plenty of cash set aside precisely for times like these.

Policymakers in Indonesia, India, the Philippines and Taiwan have all 
intervened in foreign-exchange markets over the past two weeks as the shock 
from the Iran war weighed on their currencies. China has signaled support for 
the yuan and Japan hasamped up warnings against a weaker yen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/japan-s-katayama-says-ready-to-take-bold-steps-on-fx-as-needed?cmpid=031626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
.

Asian nations have at least $8 trillion stashed away to support their case. 
That’s handy at a time when their exchange rates are under pressure due to the 
dollar’s resurgence as a haven, their own exposure to soaring oil costs and the 
risk of a global economic slowdown.

Nevertheless, stresses are mounting across global currency markets before the 
central bank meetings, as traders try to second-guess policy makers’ response 
to the risk of a burst of inflation at the same time as growth sags.

There’s a possibility market swings will intensify before the end of the week, 
testing those FX backstops across Asia once again. —Marcus Wong 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUJMXaEciqU/marcus-wong> and Malavika Kaur 
Makol <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVR2xLDFerA/malavika-kaur-makol>, with
