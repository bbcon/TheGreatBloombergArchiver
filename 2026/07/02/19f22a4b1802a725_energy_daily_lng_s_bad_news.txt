Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Energy Team Leader Stephen Stapczynski explores why the latest setback in 
LNG shipments from Qatar is keeping global consumers on edge.

An abrupt slowdown in liquefied natural gas shipments through the Strait of 
Hormuz brings bad news for consumers in Asia and Europe.

While oil tankers have been moving in and out of the Persian Gulf in greater 
numbers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-30/hormuz-traffic-picks-up-as-supertankers-sail-into-persian-gulf?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 since the US-Iran peace deal, LNG shipments from Qatar have been 
near-paralyzed since fresh Iranian attacks on vessels last week.

It’s a sharp reversal of the flurry of activity in the days prior, when empty 
tankers werelining up 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/empty-lng-tankers-mass-outside-qatar-as-exports-tick-higher?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 near Qatar’s Ras Laffan complex, the world’s largest LNG export facility.

The impact on prices is stark. While crude futures have retreated to prewar 
levels, benchmark LNG prices remain around 70% above where they were at the end 
of February.

There are several possible reasons for the faltering revival in the country 
that accounted for about a fifth of global LNG supply before the conflict.

The fact that one of the vessels struck 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-27/ship-struck-in-hormuz-as-naval-authorities-raise-threat-level?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 recently by Iran was carrying Qatari crude will have raised alarm bells in the 
tiny Gulf state. If the country were to face high insurance costs and crew 
safety concerns, that could slow the recovery of LNG exports,according to 
BloombergNEF 
<https://blinks.bloomberg.com/screens/DOCV%20RES%20THDWYAVTTCZP?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
.

It’s also more technically demanding to resume operations at a site as complex 
as Ras Laffan.An explosion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/qatar-says-malfunction-causes-blast-at-domestic-gas-plant?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 and fire during the restart of a plant that supplies gas locally, which killed 
13 people and injured 66, raised concerns about potential delays to the 
resumption of separate LNG export facilities.

State-owned QatarEnergy said it didn’t expect 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/qatar-expects-no-impact-on-lng-exports-from-domestic-gas-blast?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 the accident to affect LNG shipments.
The Ras Laffan industrial complex in Qatar houses the world’s biggest LNG 
export facility.Source: Picture-alliance/AP Photo
These events are challenging expectations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/qatar-plans-to-rapidly-restart-lng-production-after-hormuz-opens?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 that Qatar, the world’s second-largest LNG supplier, will rapidly resume 
exports.

QatarEnergy has extended a force majeure on some shipments to Asia and Europe, 
while continuing to sublease vessels — both signs that it doesn’t see exports 
quickly rebounding.

Shell Plc warned 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-30/shell-sees-lng-trade-flat-in-2026-as-hormuz-chokes-supply?cmpid=BBBXT070226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6156>
 this week that, if disruptions in Hormuz continue for weeks to come, annual 
global LNG exports could decline for the first time in more than a decade — a 
remarkable setback for what has been the world’s fastest-growing fossil fuel.

For Europe, this would complicate efforts to refill gas storage ahead of 
winter, especially following a recent slowdown in the region’s imports.

Key buyers in Asia would be pushed deeper into the expensive spot market and 
may eventually turn to other fuels. From Pakistan to Thailand, LNG is central 
to plans for economic growth and a shift away from dirtier coal — but only if 
it is affordable.

—Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Key oil agencies revised their market balances as shifts in Hormuz traffic 
changed supply and demand assumptions. The International Energy Agency and 
BloombergNEF narrowed projected deficits for this year to 900,000 and 500,000 
barrels a day, respectively. The US Energy Information Administration widened 
its deficit to 3.9 million barrels a day, seeing slower flow normalization. All 
three see a surplus of 4 million to 5 million barrels a day in 2027.
