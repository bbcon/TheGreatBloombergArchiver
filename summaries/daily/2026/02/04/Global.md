# Daily Briefing — February 4, 2026
## Global Cross-Currents: Energy, Policy Divergence, and Geopolitics

**Middle East tensions keep a bid under crude, but markets remain sanguine.** Brent advanced to around $68 a barrel for a second consecutive session after the US Navy shot down an Iranian drone heading toward an American aircraft carrier in the Arabian Sea. Oil swung between gains and losses through the session as traders balanced geopolitical risk against a reported sharp drawdown in US inventories. Notably, Bloomberg Opinion's John Authers argued that markets are under-pricing the probability of a major oil shock — a view that sits uncomfortably with current term-structure pricing and suggests asymmetric risk should hostilities escalate toward the Strait of Hormuz. Separately, discounts on Russian Urals to Indian refiners have widened to more than $10 below Brent over the past 10 days, testing New Delhi's commitment to a US trade agreement predicated on lower Russian purchases.

**Monetary policy divergence is widening.** The Reserve Bank of Australia has delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, with markets and economists positioning for a further increase — the first tightening cycle of the year globally. The contrast with the euro area is stark: HICP has sunk further below the ECB's 2% target, sharpening the debate over the next move. In the US, Kevin Warsh's challenge as incoming Fed chair will be building internal consensus, while Governor Stephen Miran has stepped down from his White House role. The splintering policy backdrop argues for sustained FX volatility and continued cross-market dispersion.

**Gold extended its rebound** above $5,000/oz for a second day as dip-buyers returned following the recent collapse, while Michael Burry warned of a self-reinforcing "death spiral" risk in Bitcoin, down roughly 40% from October highs. The divergence reinforces the thesis that bullion — not crypto — is absorbing safe-haven flows tied to deficit and policy concerns.

**Climate-energy transition:** Germany's Merz is courting Middle East suppliers to dilute LNG dependence on the US, and Australia is weighing a critical-minerals price floor to counter Chinese dominance — both incremental but directionally meaningful for supply-chain realignment.
