US equity markets enter a pivotal stretch of earnings season with the fundamental picture remaining broadly constructive, even as the geopolitical backdrop grows more complex. With slightly over a third of S&P 500 companies having reported first-quarter results, the beat rate stands at approximately 80%, above the historical average of 77%, while the aggregate earnings surprise of roughly 12% materially exceeds the typical 7% premium. Societe Generale estimates that 12-month forward profits are now approximately $600 billion higher than four months ago — a 12% revision that would represent the largest upward revision on record outside a post-recession recovery. The concentration of those gains, however, is notable: semiconductors alone account for some 43% of the 2026 consensus profit upgrade, with IT hardware and energy together explaining close to 98% of the extraordinary revision.

Wednesday's hyperscaler earnings reports from Microsoft, Alphabet, Meta, and Amazon will be consequential. Ahead of those results, shares of SoftBank fell sharply in Tokyo following a Wall Street Street Journal report that OpenAI recently missed its internal targets for new users and sales, dragging down correlated names including Oracle, CoreWeave, and AMD in pre-market trading. Microsoft and OpenAI also formally ended their exclusivity arrangement, a development that may reshape the competitive AI partnership landscape.

The Federal Reserve's policy meeting, which begins today, is widely expected to produce a hold. The decision itself may be less consequential than the tone adopted by Chair Powell in his subsequent press conference, given the currency implications for the yen and the broader signal it would send about the Fed's tolerance for elevated inflation driven by the oil shock. Analysts from BCA Research note that while the US economy's structural tendency supports earnings growth, projecting continued 20% profit expansion heading into year seven of the cycle requires assumptions that have no modern precedent, AI tailwinds notwithstanding. Credit markets remain supportive, with investment-grade and high-yield spreads near their tightest since the Global Financial Crisis.
