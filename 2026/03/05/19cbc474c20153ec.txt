All Turn

It’s still not clear whether the US can win the swift and conclusive victory 
it wants in Iran. It’s very unclear what the future holds for the country and 
its regime. And judgments on those huge questions can shift by the minute on 
news reports.

In markets, however, there is clarity. So far, the winner of this conflict has 
been America <https://bloom.bg/4u7Euvu>. The losers have been more or less 
everyone else. That’s in part because of the way markets were positioned before 
the outbreak of hostilities, with the rest of the world enjoying its strongest 
streak of outperforming the US in years. That created the grounds for a reversal
 <https://bloom.bg/4rRjh7H>. It’s also because the US dollar still functions as 
a safe haven in markets. There was refuge in the dollar, particularly as it had 
been falling.

Then there’s geography. The US is protected by oceans on either side from the 
events in Iran. Everyone outside the Americas is closer to it. It hurts them 
more.

For a dramatic illustration, Europe has barely had time to recover from the 
energy crisis that hit it after the Ukraine invasion of 2022 when natural gas 
prices <https://bloom.bg/3MW5cGP> spiked. Now it’s happened again, and that’s 
hugely dangerous politically for governments if not reversed quickly.

Meanwhile, the US has almost completely shed its dependence on Middle Eastern 
oil. High gasoline prices are politically toxic for any US government, and they 
have been rising in the futures market since President Donald Trump took 
office. Suddenly, European natural gas seems a much bigger problem:

This isn’t all good for the US, of course. Rising commodity prices will always 
arouse fears of inflation. This latest dose of geopolitical risk makes it far 
harder for the next chairman of the Federal Reserve, presumably Trump’s nominee 
Kevin Warsh, to cut rates <https://bloom.bg/4l7mqgR> as advertised. 
Market-based estimates of the fed funds rate at year-end have jumped since the 
shooting started and are now as high as they’ve been in nine months. Treasury 
yields had been falling dramatically, and that has also reversed:

Thankfully for the US, this crisis has broken when conditions were not 
particularly tight. There might be more of an issue for emerging markets 
<https://bloom.bg/4l7mr4p>, which are traditionally treated as an inverse for 
the dollar — any strength for the greenback is taken as a problem for 
developing countries trying to finance their debt. EM had enjoyed an impressive 
run of late, with its stock markets comfortably outperforming the US. The speed 
and ferocity with which that flipped after the first reports from Iran has been 
something to behold:

For one illustration,  look to India <https://bloom.bg/4aSVtdq>. The world’s 
most-populous country isn’t directly affected by the conflict. Indirectly, 
however, it has close links to the Gulf states, and imports much energy through 
the bottleneck of the Strait of Hormuz. As it has large and liquid markets, 
it’s also an easy place to take a profit.

Put this together, and the rupee has dropped to its weakest against the dollar 
on record. Meanwhile, the main Indian stock benchmarks have, in dollar terms, 
shed all of their gains since the Liberation Day turmoil 11 months ago:

America’s victory might not last long. Indeed, its market fortunes are almost 
the inverse of its fate on the battlefield; these financial trends will extend 
and intensify if this turns out to be a long drawn-out conflict 
<https://bloom.bg/4l7mrRX>, while a swift conclusion could well bring a relief 
rally for emerging markets. Until these questions are resolved, the perfect 
storm driving US assets to beat everyone else is likely to continue.

A True Infestation

Even as the world watches the  Roaring Lion <https://bloom.bg/46K8TGi> in 
Iran, it’s not safe to forget about some much smaller and peskier creatures. The
 cockroaches within private credit <https://bloom.bg/4l7mtcx>, against which 
JPMorgan’s Jamie Dimon warned last year, are emerging from the crevices and 
scurrying into view. Some startled investors are scrambling for the exits — and 
finding that the pathways out of the $1.8 trillion asset class are narrow and 
hard to find.

So-called semi-liquid private credit funds, including Blackstone Inc., have 
had to increase their quarterly redemptions <https://bloom.bg/4u4trDf> to 7% 
from 5% due to elevated withdrawals. Other funds are offering alternative 
payouts in a move to placate anxious investors concerned that private credit 
funds <https://bloom.bg/3MXElds> may not withstand the sustained pressure that 
might come if artificial intelligence upends the software sector in coming 
years. Shares inbusiness development companies <https://bloom.bg/4usJ7Rd>, or 
BDCs, which specialize in offering credit to small or struggling firms, are 
down more than 10% this year even after paring losses:

Is all this apprehension justified? That depends ultimately on how much AI can 
disrupt the software business <https://bloom.bg/4u8YuxF> model. What is clear, 
however, as this chart shows, is that US private credit funds have piled into 
software at a scale unmatched by any other sector. They are exposed:

As long as doubts linger over software, it will be hard to convince private 
credit investors that all is well. And the sector’s performance in the public 
markets makes clear that doubts remain intense. Software groups have risen a 
little compared to the market of late, but still lag by 30% in barely six 
months:

Still, this turbulence does not necessarily mean the imminent end of private 
credit. Greater clarity could come from a reset of credit valuations reset to 
take account of AI disruption risk. On this basis alone, Morgan Stanley’s 
Vishwas Patkar forecasts wider spreads, even if they affect only a relatively 
narrow part of the market.

What seems more certain is that private credit will not be seen through the 
same lens again, even if it weathers the storm. So far,as Sam Williams at 
iCapital <https://bloom.bg/4lavwJH> points out, nearly every asset manager, 
particularly in the current climate, describes themselves as “highly 
conservative.”

Given that credit is about avoiding losses, he argues, it isn’t surprising 
that managers are speaking more about their downside forecasts and contractual 
protections. In other words, they sell protection as much as yield, with 
lenders offering new origination yields at or near market rates, a general 
indicator that they’re staying within their underwriting standards. The risk, 
Williams contends, would be greater if lenders sought higher returns by 
compromising credit quality or chased higher yields by making loans to risky 
borrowers.

The prevalence of this explains the current predicament of rising default 
rates in early 2026, withFitch Ratings <https://bloom.bg/4laDXoA> reporting a 
5.8% trailing 12-month default rate in January, the highest since tracking 
began in 2024. Easy financial conditions, comparable to those of the pre-Global 
Financial Crisis era, partially explain this uptick in defaults. Despite these 
conditions, banks have largely sat out, not easing business loan standards 
since the second quarter of 2022. BCA Research’s Doug Peta points out that this 
discipline is unusual and may mitigate systemic risk:

While the share of banks tightening standards fell significantly once it 
became clear that the Fed was done hiking rates, Peta notes that 15 quarters is 
easily the longest banks have gone without easing standards outside of a 
recession in the Senior Loan Officer Opinion Survey’s (SLOOS) four-decade 
history:

On its face, the banks’ restraint is likely a good omen for defaults beginning 
in 2027. Correlations evolve, however, and the fact that bond and private 
credit markets remained frothy after banks pulled in their horns may signal 
that the private credit complex has become the de facto arbiter of corporate 
lending standards, provided it survives this cycle.

While there are valid concerns about the extent of troubles in private credit, 
questions about whether the asset class poses a systemic risk to the financial 
system are another matter.Morgan Stanley’s Vishy Tirupattur 
<https://bloom.bg/4l75M0O> notes that, despite BDCs’ troubles, leverage remains 
fairly small compared to pre-crisis levels two decades ago.

Without a lasting solution to the proverbial cockroach infestation in private 
credit, such assurances offer investors little comfort. Still, banks’ 
relatively limited exposure should spare regulators an additional headache in 
already uncertain times. That is not to say they are untroubled, only that they 
are unlikely to be confronting a systemic collapse.

—Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



Travel broadens the mind. Or at least it helps your mind grasp how fixed its 
perspective had been. This is even true of what the humorist PJ O’Rourke called
Holidays in Hell <https://bloom.bg/4ltKxXz> — an extremely funny book about 
surviving in some unpleasant places, which I recommend. Flying from London to 
Mumbai has been a great lesson in just how central Iran is to routes between 
east and west. It’s also salutary to be reminded just how awful relations 
between India and Pakistan continue to be. Air India can’t fly over Pakistani 
airspace, which forced them to cancel flights while others didn’t. In the 
current cricket World Cup, venues for the knock-out stages couldn’t be known in 
advance, because if Pakistan were still involved they would have to be in Sri 
Lanka, not India. And as for the war that preoccupies us all, it’s taking place 
in West Asia. I have to admit it took me a while to work out that that was the 
Middle East. And the more I think about it, the more that seems like a more 
accurate name. Anyway, seeing things from a different perspective is good for 
you.
