**China: Growth Beats, but Structural Imbalances Persist**
China's first-quarter GDP print of 5.0% year-on-year, released on Wednesday, April 16, beat consensus expectations and offered policymakers a measure of reassurance. The headline, however, conceals a pronounced divergence: industrial output grew 5.7% in March while retail sales rose only 1.7%, falling short of forecasts. A significant portion of the export surge reflected manufacturers front-running US tariff escalation, a boost that is by definition temporary. The Canton Fair in Guangzhou provided a more granular signal of the headwinds ahead — Middle Eastern buyers, who had increasingly substituted for absent American customers since the pandemic, were conspicuously absent this year due to the conflict and flight disruption, with exporters describing conditions as potentially the worst in memory. March trade data showed export growth slowing sharply while imports surged at the fastest pace since late 2021, as elevated energy commodity purchases reflected the war's disruption to Gulf supply. The two-speed economy — robust supply side, fragile demand side — remains a central policy challenge, and the tariff environment that incentivized front-loading may itself constrain external demand in coming quarters.

**Energy Squeeze and the Teapot Refiners**
China's independent oil refiners — the so-called teapots, accounting for roughly a fifth of national refining capacity — remain under acute pressure as the primary transmission channel of Hormuz disruption into the domestic economy. Heavily reliant on discounted Iranian crude, the sector is now exposed to both US sanctions enforcement and blockade risk. Floating stockpiles of approximately 38 million barrels of Iranian crude in Asian waters provide a buffer of perhaps two months, and Beijing has released strategic reserves and granted additional import quotas. Saudi Aramco's official selling prices have risen to a record, and Riyadh's crude shipments to Chinese customers are set to approximately halve in May — to around 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April. China's overall oil and gas imports declined in March. Russia's potential to fill part of the gap creates a structural incentive for Beijing to reinforce that relationship, as Xi's meeting with Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov on Wednesday made clear, with Xi emphasizing that "the stability and certainty of China-Russia relations are particularly valuable." Yuan-denominated crude oil transactions reached a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS during the week.

**Geopolitical Positioning and the Xi Diplomatic Offensive**
Xi's most diplomatically active week in nearly two years — receiving Spain's Prime Minister Sánchez, Abu Dhabi's Crown Prince, Vietnam's President, and Russia's Foreign Minister — served a deliberate purpose: framing Beijing as the responsible stakeholder in a disordered world. Xi's description of the international order as "crumbling into disarray," delivered in the context of his Sánchez meeting on Tuesday, was notably stronger than his customary characterizations of global turbulence. He proposed a four-point Middle East peace plan and vowed to deepen ties with Moscow. The direct question of whether US warships will interdict vessels carrying Iranian oil to China — a scenario that would constitute a significant escalation in bilateral tensions — remains unresolved, as illustrated by the Rich Starry incident on Tuesday, when a US-sanctioned tanker linked to China transited the Strait without interdiction, challenging the blockade's credibility. A Trump-Xi summit in approximately a month's time looms as the next significant test. China has condemned the US-Israeli strikes on Iran as "unacceptable," but Xi has not publicly commented on the blockade.

**Taiwan, Japan, and Korea: AI Tailwinds Meet Macro Headwinds**
Taiwan Semiconductor's first-quarter results on Wednesday provided the clearest evidence yet that AI infrastructure investment is sustaining its own momentum largely independent of the broader geopolitical disruption. The 58% profit surge and guidance raise to above 30% full-year revenue growth drove Taiwan's Taiex to a record high on Tuesday, April 14, posting a year-to-date gain of 25%. South Korea's Kospi has rallied approximately 23%, recovering sharply from an 18% two-day decline at the conflict's onset; BlackRock flagged Korean equities as a preferred position. In Japan, the more cautious backdrop is evident: BOJ Governor Ueda's pullback on rate hike signaling on Monday, combined with the 10-year JGB yield reaching 2.49% on the same day — its highest since 1997 — reflects the difficulty of navigating tightening global financial conditions while domestic supply chains absorb naphtha shortages. Toilet manufacturer Toto suspended new prefabricated bathroom orders citing feedstock constraints, illustrative of a growing list of Japanese manufacturers cutting production or raising prices. Japan's Finance Minister Katayama warned of "bold action" to support the yen following bilateral discussions with Bessent in Washington, a statement widely interpreted as an intervention threat that received no pushback from the US side. Macro hedge funds in China suffered notable losses in March, with Bridgewater's All Weather Plus strategy declining 5.6% from late February through early April, and the peer-group average loss of 6.3% described as the worst on record.
