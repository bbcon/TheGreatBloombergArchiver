The defining development of March 11 in global markets was the IEA's announcement of an emergency release of 400 million barrels of oil from member-country reserves — the largest such action in the agency's history. Executive Director Fatih Birol characterized it as "an emergency collective action of unprecedented size," and the decision was unanimous. Japan separately announced a unilateral release of approximately 80 million barrels, beginning as early as March 16. The announcement temporarily capped Brent crude below $90 per barrel, though analysts were quick to note that execution details — pace, duration, and logistics — will determine market impact. Some traders remain skeptical that reserves can be deployed rapidly enough to meaningfully offset the roughly 7 million barrels per day of supply disrupted by the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

The geopolitical picture remains fluid and the risk of a prolonged closure is arguably underpriced. UK naval forces reported three vessels struck in the Strait of Hormuz and the Persian Gulf on Wednesday, and Dubai International Airport briefly halted operations following drone strikes that injured four people. Analysts at BCA Research raise the uncomfortable possibility that even a ceasefire may not reopen Hormuz, as remnants of Iran's regime — or factions beyond Tehran's control — could sustain drone harassment of shipping indefinitely. Oil briefly traded above $100 during the week, and at $88 Brent, the market appears to embed a meaningful probability of conflict resolution that the military and diplomatic evidence does not yet support.

The dollar has strengthened since hostilities commenced, driven by the US's position as the world's largest crude producer and the currency's role in global oil trade — a dynamic that partially reverses the roughly 6% decline in the Bloomberg dollar index over the past year. LNG supply flows are also shifting, with at least nine Europe-bound cargoes diverting to Asia, tightening availability in both regions simultaneously. Pakistan is navigating acute pressure, having raised fuel prices by a record 21% and suspended gas supplies to fertilizer companies following the suspension of Qatari gas flows, while trying to balance obligations to Saudi Arabia against domestic Shia sympathies and historic ties with Iran.
