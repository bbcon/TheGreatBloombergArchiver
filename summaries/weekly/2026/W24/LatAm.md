**Peru: Electoral Uncertainty and Institutional Resilience**
The presidential count's evolution — Sánchez leading after rural ballots, with diaspora votes potentially reversing the margin — has produced a risk premium on Peruvian assets that is likely to persist until mid-July adjudication. The investment implications may be more limited than the political drama suggests: Sánchez lacks the legislative support required for market-concerning structural reforms, while Fujimori's Fuerza Popular controls the largest congressional bloc. Central bank Governor Velarde's near-two-decade tenure provides an institutional anchor that arguably matters more for investor confidence than the electoral outcome. Colombia's President Petro publicly criticized Trump for expressing support for the opposition candidate, adding a geopolitical dimension to domestic political stress in the region's third-largest economy.

**Trade, Tariffs, and Bilateral Tensions**
The Trump administration's use of environmental and labor standards — Amazon deforestation, forced-labor prohibitions — as tariff justifications against Brazil represents a strategically durable framing that future US administrations would find politically difficult to unilaterally abandon. Brazil's trade minister held discussions with US Trade Representative Greer during the week to negotiate arrangements. Mexico faces a more acute structural challenge: automakers formally complained that the average tariff on Mexican auto exports stands at approximately 19%, above the 15% rate applied to some Asian competitors, while USMCA renewal uncertainty deepens the medium-term investment risk for a manufacturing sector central to the country's export model. Mexico did, however, award 37 renewable energy projects adding 7,411 megawatts of expected capacity — a constructive signal for the energy transition.

**Regional Energy and Venezuela**
Iran war disruption to global crude flows is generating consequential trade realignment within the region. Venezuelan exports to Asia are rising as commodity traders redirect flows, with India now absorbing volumes previously taken by China — providing Caracas with some fiscal support, though Trump administration sanctions maintain ongoing uncertainty. US sanctions on Cuba's state energy company threatened to block a privately arranged fuel supply deal, an acute development given the island's deepening energy crisis. Bolivia's President Paz authorized a harder crackdown on protests extending beyond five weeks, reflecting economic grievances amplified by commodity market disruption that suppression alone is unlikely to resolve.
