It’s not just stock valuations that are stretched, but belief in the longevity 
of the rally. The S&P 500 is back at levels first reached in October, while its 
10-day moving average has plateaued for much of this month — despite sharp 
daily swings in the US benchmark.

Investors are seeing traps everywhere as excitement over the AI-fueled boom 
turns to paranoia about which industries could be ravaged by the technology. 
IBM became thelatest casualty <https://bloom.bg/3ZU5Gjt>, tumbling the most 
since thedotcom bubble burst <https://bloom.bg/4r49Q3C> after AI startup 
Anthropic PBC said its Claude Code tool can help modernize COBOL, a dated 
programming language that’s mostly run on IBM computers.

The nervy tone <https://bloom.bg/4s2xdet> was set by a weekend report 
published by a little-known firm called Citrini Research, which outlined 
potential risks to different segments of the global economy. The report 
highlighted food delivery services and credit card companies as facing trouble 
from the disruptive power of AI.

Aggravating sentiment was a warning by The Black Swan author Nassim Taleb, who 
saidinvestors should brace <https://bloom.bg/4qWWOov> for escalating volatility 
and possible bankruptcies in the software sector.

If that wasn’t enough, JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon, asked about fierce 
competition across the financial industry, said he’sstarting to see parallels 
<https://bloom.bg/4c6a2LL> to the era before the 2008 financial crisis, when a 
rush to make loans ended disastrously.

“Unfortunately, we did see this in ’05, ’06 and ’07, almost the same thing — 
the rising tide was lifting all boats, everyone was making a lot of money,” 
Dimon told investors on Monday. While JPMorgan isn’t willing to make riskier 
loans to boost net interest income, he said, “I see a couple people doing some 
dumb things. They’re just doing dumb things to create NII (net interest 
income).”

Unless conviction — and faith — in the rally return, investors are likely to 
continueenvisioning phantoms <https://bloom.bg/3OI3itI> where before they 
glimpsed riches.—Richard Frost

More on Citrini. Alap Shah, who co-authored the weekend report, says AI may 
significantly displace <https://bloom.bg/4qPhFd1> workers and disrupt 
consumption-driven economies like the US in the near future.

To offset such an impact, governments should consider taxing incremental or 
windfall gains from AI, Shah, chief investment officer at Lotus Technology 
Management,told Bloomberg TV <https://bloom.bg/4qRdkpO>. Without it, rising 
unemployment will hit consumption, with the US likely among the most affected. 
Shah sketched out a scenario where 5% of white-collar workers could be cut 
within 18 months.

“We generally have a set of shorts out against businesses that we think are 
going to be disrupted by AI,” he said. “On the other side of that, we own a lot 
of semiconductors that we think are going to benefit.”

The Citrini report <https://bloom.bg/4qUbVyK> imagines a 2028 world in which 
rapid advances in machine intelligence turbocharge productivity but render 
large swaths of human labor obsolete, sparking job losses, collapsing consumer 
spending and dragging down stock indexes like the S&P 500.




Bitcoin’s downfall provides a stark illustration of the shift in mood. The 
cryptocurrency fell as much as 2.9% to $62,701 on Tuesday and is now down more 
than 19% in February, set for itsworst monthly <https://bloom.bg/4s7RfUT> 
performance since June 2022.

That year saw the $40 billion collapse of Terraform Labs, which roiled the 
crypto markets and contributed to the collapse of FTX. Somewhat ironically, 
Jane Street on Mondaywas sued for <https://bloom.bg/3ZTuWX7> alleged insider 
trading by the administrator winding up the affairs of Terraform.




Against such a bumpy backdrop, Donald Trump’s new 10% global tariffs have just 
gone into effect <https://bloom.bg/4tRAoaP>. While he previously said tariffs 
would be raised <https://bloom.bg/4rcWySt> to 15%, Trump did not officially 
issue a directive to increase the rate by Tuesday at 12:01 a.m. Washington time 
when the 10% levy went into effect.

The White House is working on a formal order that will increase the global 
tariff rate to 15%, but the timeline hasn’t been finalized, according to an 
administration official.

Japanese Trade Minister Ryosei Akazawa called on the US 
<https://bloom.bg/4tTxTEW> to ensure that new tariff measures would not leave 
Tokyo with tougher conditions than those agreed in last year’s trade deal, 
during a call with Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick.




Doubts over the pace of future Japanese interest-rate hikes triggered a sharp 
decline in the yen, with the currency sinking 1% to trade around 156 against 
the dollar.

The move was sparked  by a report <https://bloom.bg/4qUbZhY> in the Mainichi 
newspaper, which said Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi voiced apprehension over 
more rate increases in a meeting with Bank of Japan Governor Kazuo Ueda last 
week. Takaichi took a “tougher stance” than in their previous meeting in 
November, the newspaper reported, citing unidentified people.

“Takaichi was expected to shift toward more market-friendly policies, so in 
this context, the Mainichi report came as a major shock,” said Rinto Maruyama, 
FX and rates strategist at SMBC Nikko Securities.
Sanae Takaichi Photographer: Kiyoshi Ota/Bloomberg 



China blacklisted 20 Japanese entities and tightened scrutiny on a raft of 
other firms, signaling Beijing’spressure campaign <https://bloom.bg/3MCWHjL> on 
Tokyo is continuing.

The Ministry of Commerce added companies including affiliates of Mitsubishi 
Heavy Industries to an export control list effective immediately. The move 
marks the first time Japanese firms were added to the list since it began in 
January 2025.

Beijing also placed an additional 20 entities, including Subaru and Mitsubishi 
Materials on a monitor list. While not an outright ban, this status subjects 
exporters to stricter checks when they apply for a license to export dual-use 
items, which refer to goods with both civilian and military applications.




North Korean leader Kim Jong Un’s sister has been promoted, the latest sign of 
the Kim family consolidating its grip on power as the ruling party convenes a 
key party meeting to chart policy direction for the coming years.

Kim Yo Jong, previously a vice department director of the Central Committee of 
the ruling Workers’ Party of Korea, was named a director of one of the 
departments, the official Korean Central News Agency reported. Her elevation 
comes as North Korea’s closely-watched party congress, the first one in five 
years, is underway in Pyongyang.
Kim Yo Jong Photographer: Patrick Semansky/Getty Images 



Shein plans to invest over 10 billion yuan ($1.45 billion) to strengthen its 
supply chain in southern China, as the fast‑fashion online retailerseeks to 
reinforce ties <bbg://news/stories/TAY7N2KK3NY9> with Beijing amid a stalled 
Hong Kong IPO and mounting external pressures.

Shein will channel the investment into intelligent supply chain systems in 
Guangdong province, home to its vast manufacturing networks, the company’s 
founder and Chairman Xu Yangtian said in a rare public speech at a conference 
in Guangzhou. His comments mark a tactical shift from the company’s previous 
strategy of distancing itself from its Chinese roots, having relocated its 
headquarters to Singapore several years ago and initially planning to seek a 
New York listing.

Trump’s efforts to end Russia’s invasion of Ukraine are stalling with peace 
talks deadlocked and the fighting largely at a stalemate after four years of 
war.

Allies say the US is pushing for a deal before Trump hosts the 250th 
anniversary celebrations of American independence on July 4. But there’s no 
indication that Russian President Vladimir Putin is ready to reach an agreement 
that doesn’t grant his central demands, according to senior European and NATO 
officials.

The talks have already blown through several deadlines and even some US 
officials admit privately that they see no signs Putin is willing to budge from
