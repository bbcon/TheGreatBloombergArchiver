# Global Daily Briefing — February 11, 2026

**Cross-currents in monetary policy divergence sharpen.** Norway delivered the day's most striking macro signal, with January CPI printing well above expectations and triggering a steep selloff in NOK government bonds. Ten-year yields climbed to levels not seen since early 2009, and markets now price a March hike from Norges Bank — joining the RBA, whose Deputy Governor Hauser reiterated that Australian inflation remains "too high." The divergence is becoming material: Norwegian-Swedish yield spreads have widened sharply even as euro-area yields drifted lower on the same session. Norway's energy-heavy economy and historically erratic inflation prints argue against extrapolating globally, but the episode underscores how country-specific dynamics are reasserting themselves as the post-Covid synchronization fades. In the US, by contrast, soft December retail sales and a benign employment cost index pushed traders to price two Fed cuts under incoming Chair Warsh, with the 10-year Treasury yield falling six basis points back below its 200-day moving average. The MOVE index continues its steady decline from last April's Liberation Day shock — a notable disconnect given the geopolitical backdrop.

**Iran diplomacy enters a delicate phase.** Netanyahu travels to Washington today to meet Trump, with the explicit aim of pressing for tougher concessions from Tehran. Recent US-Iran exchanges have struck a constructive tone on preliminary talks, though analysts remain skeptical that a substantive agreement is within reach. Oil gained on the session as Middle East tensions outweighed mounting evidence of a building supply glut — a tension that will resolve one way or the other in coming weeks.

**Energy transition and commodity policy.** Indonesia ordered Weda Bay Nickel — the world's largest nickel mine — to cut permitted output to 12 million tons from 42 million, an aggressive supply-side intervention aimed at supporting global prices. Separately, the Trump administration is expected to unveil federal support for coal-fired generation, while the EPA moves to repeal the 2009 endangerment finding underpinning US greenhouse gas regulation. BP halted buybacks to address roughly $60 billion in debt and obligations, diverging from peers Chevron, Exxon, Shell and TotalEnergies, all of which maintained shareholder returns.
