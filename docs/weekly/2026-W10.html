Weekly Brief

Mar 02 – Mar 08, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 futures fell approximately 1% on Monday before stabilizing through the week, with the index declining less than 1% overall — notably resilient relative to Asia and Europe.
  • The 10-year Treasury yield rose from roughly 3.97% on Monday to 4.163% by Friday, defying conventional haven dynamics as oil-driven inflation expectations suppressed rate-cut pricing.
  • ISM manufacturing prices-paid surged to their highest since 2022 in February, compounding the inflationary signal from an oil shock that pushed WTI above $84 and gasoline to $3.32 per gallon by Friday.
  • Private credit stress intensified, with Blackstone's flagship fund facing a record $3.8 billion redemption request and Fitch reporting a trailing 12-month default rate of 5.8% — the highest since tracking began.
  • The Supreme Court's invalidation of IEEPA-based tariffs reduced projected federal revenue by an estimated $1.7 trillion through 2036, complicating the fiscal outlook.

Equity Markets

US equity markets exhibited an unusual combination of resilience and internal stress throughout the week. The S&P 500 opened under pressure Monday, with futures off roughly 1% as the US-Israeli strikes on Iran unsettled investor sentiment, but the index held broadly flat through mid-week before edging lower Friday as WTI crude reached $84.46. The relative calm masked significant sectoral divergence: defense names surged on Monday — Lockheed Martin rose 7.1%, RTX gained 6.5%, and Northrop Grumman advanced 5.2% — while airlines sold off sharply, with United Airlines falling 5.7% and Delta declining 5.1% on higher fuel costs. Oil majors advanced, Exxon Mobil gaining roughly 5% and ConocoPhillips rising 6%. The US market's outperformance relative to Asia and Europe reflects a structural advantage: as a net energy exporter, the US is substantially less exposed to a sustained Strait of Hormuz disruption than economies that depend overwhelmingly on Gulf crude. That insulation is partial, however, and the week's events have placed a meaningful ceiling on any near-term equity rerating.

Monetary Policy and Rates

The bond market's behavior was the week's most analytically significant development. Rather than rallying on safe-haven demand, the 10-year Treasury yield rose from approximately 3.97% Monday to 4.163% by Friday — a dynamic that Société Générale's historical analysis of five oil supply shocks over the past fifty years suggests is consistent: Treasuries have underperformed at one-week, three-month, and six-month horizons following comparable episodes. The February ISM manufacturing survey added to the discomfort, with prices-paid surging to its highest level since 2022 and respondents explicitly citing domestic steel and aluminum tariffs as simultaneously raising input costs and compressing demand. Market-implied year-end fed funds rates reached their highest level in nine months, and former Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen characterized the situation as leaving the Fed "even more on hold." Gasoline prices rose 25 cents to $3.25 per gallon in a single week, reaching $3.32 by Friday — the highest under Trump's presidency — reversing what had been one of his administration's more tangible consumer affordability wins.

Fiscal and Political Developments

The fiscal backdrop deteriorated on two fronts. The Supreme Court's invalidation of IEEPA-based tariffs, which the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimates reduces government revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036, leaves the administration's replacement 10% levy recovering approximately $925 billion — a material gap on any trajectory. Federal debt accumulation continues regardless of the scenario modeled. Politically, the administration faces the uncomfortable reversal of three stated market preferences — lower oil, lower yields, and a weaker dollar — all of which moved in the wrong direction this week. The dollar reclaimed safe-haven status, yields edged higher, and pump prices hit a presidency high, complicating the domestic political calculus as midterm elections approach in November. Trump dismissed Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem on Friday, naming Oklahoma Senator Markwayne Mullin as her replacement, raising questions about institutional continuity.

Corporate News

In individual corporate developments, Morgan Stanley announced it is cutting approximately 3% of its global workforce, roughly 2,500 employees, citing individual performance. MongoDB shares fell 27% on a weaker-than-expected revenue forecast, dragging enterprise software broadly. Berkshire Hathaway's Class B shares slipped 1.7% after reporting a near-30% decline in operating profit in Warren Buffett's final quarter as CEO. Moderna gained 5.8% after settling Covid delivery-technology litigation for $950 million. Marvell Technology surged 12% in premarket trading Friday on accelerating data center demand. Executives at the Bloomberg Invest conference, including Apollo's Marc Rowan and Soros's Dawn Fitzpatrick, warned of "a painful 18 to 24 months" ahead for private credit and private equity as default risks rise and liquidity remains constrained. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi fell 7.2% on Tuesday and a further 12% on Wednesday — the largest single-session decline in the index's history — before surging 10% on Thursday following activation of a 100 trillion won stabilization program.
  • China's National People's Congress set a GDP growth target of 4.5%–5%, the lowest since 1991, and unveiled a 15th Five-Year Plan emphasizing AI, technological self-reliance, and a pivot toward household consumption.
  • Global funds sold a net $11 billion of emerging Asian equities excluding China in the week — the largest outflow since March 2022 — with Taiwan absorbing $7.9 billion in withdrawals.
  • A US submarine sank the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka on Thursday, the first American submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II, placing India in a diplomatically untenable position.
  • Mainland Chinese investors sold a record HK$27.7 billion of Hong Kong-listed shares in a single session, the largest net selling on record.

South Korea: Historic Volatility

The week's most extreme market moves occurred in South Korea. The Kospi — which had risen roughly 50% year-to-date, fueled by AI-driven optimism around Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix, retail margin participation, and active political encouragement from President Lee Jae Myung — suffered a two-day collapse of approximately 18%, with the 12% Wednesday decline representing the largest single-session fall in the index's history. Samsung fell 12% Wednesday, SK Hynix declined roughly 10%, and Hyundai Motor lost 16%. Bank of Korea Governor Rhee Chang Yong abandoned scheduled travel to Bangkok to convene an emergency meeting. President Lee, attending events in the Philippines, called an extraordinary cabinet session. The political consequences are significant: his public identification with the equity rally has made the rout a direct test of his credibility. The Thursday rebound of 10% following invocation of the stabilization fund illustrates the market's extreme sensitivity to policy signals, but the underlying volatility profile remains elevated.

China: NPC and Conflict Exposure

China's National People's Congress delivered the week's most consequential Asian policy signals. Premier Li Qiang's government work report set a growth target at its lowest level since 1991 while framing the reduction as a deliberate quality-over-quantity pivot. Officials made their strongest commitment yet to eliminating deflationary conditions in 2026, and the accompanying 15th Five-Year Plan doubled down on technology — emphasizing AI, brain-computer interfaces, and pharmaceutical innovation — as the primary growth driver. Defense spending rose 7%, the slowest pace since 2022. Beijing simultaneously instructed its largest refiners to suspend diesel and gasoline exports, reflecting precautionary energy conservation as Gulf crude flows remained disrupted. Diplomatically, China called for safe Strait of Hormuz passage — hardly surprising given that nearly half of China's crude imports transited the waterway as recently as December — while simultaneously preparing for preparatory trade talks with US counterparts in Paris next week. That the bilateral economic agenda continues despite the Iran crisis appears to signal that both sides treat it as structurally distinct from the geopolitical environment.

India and the Maritime Dimension

India faced an acute dilemma following the sinking of the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka Thursday — a vessel that had participated in an Indian Navy-hosted multilateral fleet review. The Indian rupee fell to a record low against the dollar, and Indian equity benchmarks erased all dollar-term gains since last April's Liberation Day market turmoil. The US Treasury's temporary waiver allowing Indian refiners to resume Russian crude purchases provided some economic relief while simultaneously highlighting India's strategic exposure and benefiting Moscow's war finances. Prime Minister Modi's call for "dialog and diplomacy" navigates carefully between Washington and Tehran but satisfies neither fully.

Japan and Regional Periphery

The Bank of Japan maintained its cautious posture, with a deputy governor declining Monday to signal any imminent rate increase, and a former official placing April move odds at approximately 50/50. Toyota's landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over Toyota Industries — at ¥20,600 per share, a 9.6% premium on the prior bid, valuing the deal at approximately ¥6.7 trillion — was the week's most significant Japanese corporate development, largely overshadowed by conflict-driven market moves. Australia's Reserve Bank Governor Michele Bullock signaled that patient inflation management has limits and a rate hike remains possible, underscoring that the energy shock compounds pre-existing inflation challenges across the region. ---

Europe

  • European natural gas futures surged more than 50% on the week — the largest weekly move since the 2022 energy crisis — following Iranian drone strikes on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility and the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz.
  • The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 1.45% Monday and a further 3.16% Tuesday before partially recovering Wednesday, with banks and insurers — including Société Générale, UniCredit, Santander, and BNP Paribas — each declining more than 4% at their lows.
  • The ECB faces a sharpening stagflationary dilemma, with traders fully pricing a 2026 rate hike by Thursday and Bundesbank President Nagel stating the inflation impact of the Iran war currently outweighs the growth drag.
  • President Trump threatened to cut off trade with Spain after Prime Minister Sánchez denied Washington access to Spanish military bases, while UK Prime Minister Starmer publicly distanced Britain from US "regime change from the skies."
  • The UK's Office for Budget Responsibility indicated that Labour's commitment to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035 would require approximately £40 billion in additional outlays.

Energy Shock and Growth Risks

Europe entered the week as the region most structurally exposed to the Iran conflict outside of Asia. European natural gas prices surged more than 52% intraday on Monday — the largest single-day gain since the 2022 energy crisis — and extended gains through the week, breaching €50 per megawatt-hour by Thursday as Qatar's Ras Laffan facility remained offline. Approximately 30% of EU diesel and kerosene imports originate from the Middle East, with roughly two-thirds of those volumes transiting the Strait of Hormuz. Goldman Sachs estimates a month-long Hormuz halt could cause European gas prices to more than double. Bloomberg Economics has framed the scenario analysis in binary terms: a conflict lasting roughly a month may amount to a manageable headwind for a continent showing tentative recovery; a prolonged disruption would constitute a materially more serious challenge, with recession risk rising meaningfully. Euro-area fourth-quarter GDP was simultaneously revised lower, with trade weighing on output — a stagflationary combination that complicates any simple monetary policy response. The euro declined roughly 2% against the dollar across the week, consistent with the terms-of-trade deterioration that characterized the 2022 energy crisis.

ECB Policy Dilemma

The ECB's policy trajectory appears to be shifting in real time. As recently as last month, markets had been pricing a continuation of the easing cycle that began in 2024. By Thursday, traders were fully pricing a 2026 rate hike, and Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel was explicit that the inflation impact of the Iran war currently outweighs the growth drag — a statement that, if representative of the broader Governing Council, would mark a meaningful pivot. The dilemma is genuine: a supply-side energy shock simultaneously pushes inflation higher and suppresses growth, leaving the ECB with no clean policy response. One partial offset emerged from Germany, where unusually strong solar generation in March — with installations feeding over 40 gigawatts into the grid for five consecutive days — helped cap domestic electricity prices, illustrating the buffering capacity of domestic renewable generation during external supply shocks.

UK Politics and Fiscal Position

UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves delivered her spring fiscal statement against an unusually turbulent backdrop on Tuesday. Tax receipts came in approximately £22 billion ahead of OBR forecasts, driven by stronger-than-expected self-assessed income tax and VAT revenues, providing modest relief on gilt issuance pressure — though UK yields remain the highest in the G7. The statement was deliberately low-key and largely overshadowed by conflict developments. Prime Minister Starmer sharpened his public distancing from US military action, stating Britain does not support "regime change from the skies" and questioning the legal basis for the strikes. Trump publicly criticized Starmer for refusing US base access. A suspected drone attack on the UK's Cyprus base occurred hours after Starmer's announcement, illustrating the tangible costs of British association with the campaign even at arm's length. Over 100 Labour MPs separately wrote to the Home Secretary urging a rethink of immigration reforms, signaling parliamentary management strain on multiple fronts. The FTSE 100 outperformed materially throughout the week, with Citigroup upgrading UK equities to overweight, citing the index's structural tilt toward energy, commodities, defence, and defensives as an effective geopolitical hedge.

Corporate and Country-Level Developments

Spain emerged as a diplomatic flashpoint after Trump threatened to "cut off all trade" following Sánchez's refusal to grant military base access — a threat complicated by the fact that bilateral trade is governed by EU-level agreements. Spanish equities fell to their lowest in nearly three months, and Banco Santander executive chair Ana Botín sought publicly to downplay the rift. Adidas fell 7.6% in Frankfurt Wednesday after its profit forecast missed expectations due to US tariffs and currency headwinds. Novo Nordisk rose 3.5% after US regulators moved against copycat GLP-1 drugs. Italy's fiscal credibility received a modest setback after data confirmed it failed to meet the EU's 3% deficit ceiling last year. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz traveled to Washington on Tuesday for meetings covering tariff policy and the broader geopolitical situation. ---

Global

  • Brent crude surged approximately 18% on the week, trading above $82 per barrel by mid-week, with US diesel futures on track for a weekly gain exceeding 40% and European natural gas up more than 50%.
  • The Strait of Hormuz effectively ceased functioning as an oil transit corridor by Thursday, with only two commercial vessel transits recorded in a 24-hour period and no oil shipments passing through at all.
  • Iran's geographic footprint of retaliation expanded to at least five countries, a US submarine sank the IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka, and NATO air defenses downed a ballistic missile over Turkey — the conflict acquiring genuinely global military characteristics.
  • The "Sell America, Buy Asia" macro trade that defined early 2026 sharply reversed, with the dollar recording its largest weekly gain in over a year and emerging market equities giving back recent outperformance at speed.
  • A new study published in Geophysical Research Letters provided the first statistically significant evidence of an acceleration in global warming, with the planet warming at approximately 0.35°C per decade since 2015 — nearly double the average rate between 1970 and 2015.

The Conflict and Energy Markets

The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike represents the most consequential geopolitical rupture since Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Brent crude surged approximately 12.5% on Monday before partially retracing, then extended gains through the week as the Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil exports and a comparable share of LNG transits — effectively closed to commercial shipping. The binding near-term constraint has proven to be the insurance market rather than an explicit military blockade: more than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs announced withdrawal of war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels starting Thursday, causing commercial shipping to self-suspend. Saudi Arabia's 550,000 barrel-per-day Ras Tanura refinery suspended operations following a drone strike, QatarEnergy shut LNG production at the world's largest export facility, and Iraq began cutting output as onshore storage filled. Aluminium Bahrain declared force majeure on some deliveries, with aluminum prices reaching their highest since 2022. Airlines canceled over 12,300 flights, stranding crews and equipment, with Gulf hub carriers — Emirates, Etihad, and Qatar Airways — collectively accounting for an estimated 13% of global air freight capacity. The range of oil price scenarios remains wide and genuinely uncertain. A brief conflict and rapid regime transition might return Brent toward $60 per barrel; a full Hormuz disruption sustained for weeks could push prices toward $108 or beyond in Bloomberg Economics' severe scenario; a middle case of constrained but continuing flows might see prices stabilize in the $80–$90 range. Goldman Sachs flagged the plausibility of Brent crossing $100 if low flows persist for another five weeks. President Trump announced US naval escorts and insurance guarantees for tankers transiting the strait — a partial solution at best, as ING analysts noted US Navy escorts risk becoming targets and it is ultimately individual ship captains who determine whether to sail.

Monetary Policy in a Supply Shock

The macro implications are particularly uncomfortable for central banks globally. A sustained $20-per-barrel oil shock could add up to one percentage point to global inflation, according to Société Générale, at a moment when most major economies have not yet returned inflation durably to target. The IMF's Kristalina Georgieva warned that a sustained 10% energy price increase would push global inflation up 40 basis points and shave 0.1–0.2% from growth. The traditional policy response to a supply-side shock is ambiguous: tightening to contain inflation risks compressing already-fragile growth, while easing to support activity risks embedding higher inflation expectations. Traders unwound rate-cut expectations across the US, UK, Turkey, and Hungary simultaneously — producing the counterintuitive result that sovereign bonds sold off alongside equities, leaving the dollar as the dominant haven vehicle. Gold, after initial gains to $5,385 per ounce Monday, wavered and was trading at a steep discount in Dubai by week's end, an unusual dislocation reflecting local market stress.

Geopolitical Alignments and the Conflict's Endgame

The conflict's endgame remains opaque. Trump's stated objectives shifted across the week — variously described as a campaign lasting four to five weeks, seeking regime change on a "Venezuela model," or opening negotiations with new Iranian leadership. Iran's own signals were contradictory: President Pezeshkian vowed retaliation while security chief Larijani publicly rejected US talks, even as the Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries, and a New York Times report of indirect Iranian-Washington contact prompted a temporary equity rebound Wednesday. The Assembly of Experts must choose Khamenei's successor — the choice between a continuity hardliner and a more pragmatic figure carrying profound implications for conflict duration. Russia has emerged as the clearest early economic beneficiary, with elevated energy revenues and India's return to Russian crude purchasing replenishing the Kremlin's finances at a critical juncture. The minerals competition between the US and China in sub-Saharan Africa — where China conducts roughly $200 billion in annual trade and controls an estimated 80% of Congo's copper output, approximately four times the US trade figure — adds a further dimension to the administration's resource-security logic. Markets are trading probabilities rather than resolved outcomes, a posture that appears likely to persist.
