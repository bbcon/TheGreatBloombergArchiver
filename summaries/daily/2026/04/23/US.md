US equity markets paused a record-breaking rally on Thursday as the Strait of Hormuz impasse deepened, with S&P 500 futures off roughly 0.5% and Brent crude pushing further above $100 per barrel. The pullback followed weeks of remarkable resilience: nearly 80% of S&P 500 companies reporting first-quarter results have beaten estimates, and realized volatility under the current administration has, counterintuitively, tracked levels seen during the relatively calm Biden years. Analysts at Barclays note that the market's equanimity in the face of sustained geopolitical stress mirrors the pattern seen through 2016-2018, which ultimately resolved in the sharp "Volmageddon" correction of February 2018 — a precedent worth monitoring as low-volatility conditions persist.

Tesla delivered first-quarter earnings of $0.41 per share against a consensus of $0.34, but the beat failed to sustain an after-hours rally once management outlined plans to increase capital expenditure to $25 billion this year — $5 billion above prior guidance — in support of AI and robotics ambitions. Shares gave back gains as investors signaled a preference for clarity on the core automotive business over unquantified future ventures. Texas Instruments provided a sharper contrast, surging 12% after a stronger-than-expected forecast driven by data-center and industrial demand. ServiceNow fell 13% after cutting its margin outlook, citing deal delays attributable to the Middle East conflict.

On monetary policy, Fed Chair nominee Kevin Warsh used his Senate confirmation hearing to signal interest in overhauling inflation measurement, expressing a preference for trimmed-mean gauges. The Dallas Fed's trimmed PCE currently reads 2.33% versus 3.0% for core PCE — a gap of 70 basis points that, if adopted as the policy benchmark, would materially shift the perceived distance from the Fed's 2% target. Strategists at BMO flagged the significance in the context of the Fed's current rate-hold posture. Wells Fargo economists cautioned, however, that over a full decade the trimmed measure has not painted a meaningfully different picture of underlying inflation trends.

Consumer sentiment this month fell to an all-time low, with households increasingly concerned about war-driven inflation and gasoline prices approaching four-year highs — a divergence from equity market performance that several analysts describe as a growing tension for corporate earnings power.
