**China: Growth Beats, but Structural Imbalances Persist**
China's first-quarter GDP expanded 5.0% year-on-year — the strongest pace in three quarters — beating consensus expectations and suggesting limited direct spillover from the Iran conflict thus far. However, the composition of growth warrants scrutiny. Industrial output grew 5.7% in March while retail sales rose only 1.7%, missing forecasts. High-tech exports surged nearly 30% in the first quarter, partly reflecting manufacturers front-running US tariff escalation, but the Canton Fair in Guangzhou offered a more sobering ground-level view: Middle Eastern buyers — who had increasingly substituted for absent American customers since the pandemic — were conspicuously absent due to the conflict and flight disruption. Exporters described 2026 as potentially the worst in memory, facing the sequential blows of tariffs and war simultaneously.

**China: Energy Exposure and Strategic Positioning**
Iran is a critical oil supplier to China, and the Hormuz disruption has exposed the country's refining sector to acute input cost pressure. Saudi Arabia's crude shipments to Chinese customers are set to halve in May, to approximately 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April, as Aramco raised official selling prices to a record. China's independent teapot refiners — accounting for roughly a fifth of national capacity — are heavily reliant on discounted Iranian crude, with floating stockpiles of approximately 38 million barrels providing a buffer of perhaps two months. Beijing has released strategic reserves and granted additional import quotas to sustain output. Separately, yuan-denominated crude oil transactions reached a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS on Thursday, and China's lithium battery exports rose 50% year-on-year in the first quarter as buyers sought alternatives to oil-dependent energy.

**China: Diplomatic Signaling and Xi's Strategic Posture**
Xi Jinping had his most diplomatically active week in nearly two years, receiving delegations from Spain, Abu Dhabi, Vietnam, and Russia, among others. His meeting with Russian Foreign Minister Lavrov was the most consequential: Xi emphasized that "the stability and certainty of China-Russia relations are particularly valuable," signaling strategic alignment at a moment when Russian energy has become more valuable to China as Middle East flows tighten. Xi framed China as a stabilizing force against a "crumbling" global order and proposed a four-point Middle East peace plan. His positioning appears deliberate and builds on the 2023 Saudi-Iran rapprochement, but Beijing's concrete contributions to conflict resolution have so far remained limited. A Trump-Xi summit in approximately a month looms as the next significant test.

**Taiwan and South Korea: AI-Driven Outperformance**
Taiwan Semiconductor delivered a first-quarter profit surge of 58% on Thursday and raised full-year revenue growth guidance to above 30%, reinforcing its status as the primary proxy for global AI infrastructure investment. TSMC's market capitalization now exceeds $1.7 trillion, and Taiwan's total equity market has overtaken the UK to become the seventh largest globally at $4.14 trillion. The Taiex ended the week with a year-to-date gain of approximately 25% and posted a record close on Tuesday. South Korea's Kospi gained approximately 23% since its sharp two-day decline at the onset of the war, with BlackRock flagging Korean equities as a preferred position. Taiwan's export data surged to an all-time high as AI chip demand more than offset supply chain disruptions.

**Japan and Regional Central Banks**
BOJ Governor Ueda signaled a more cautious stance on rate hikes ahead of the next policy meeting — a notable pullback from earlier market expectations — while Japan's 10-year government bond yield climbed to 2.49% on Monday, its highest since 1997. Finance Minister Katayama warned she was prepared to take "bold action" to support the yen, a statement widely interpreted as an intervention threat; the absence of pushback from Treasury Secretary Bessent was read as implicit US tolerance. Singapore became the first Asian central bank to tighten monetary settings in response to rising energy-driven inflation, a move that may signal broader regional policy divergence if crude remains elevated. Chinese macro hedge funds suffered notable losses, with Bridgewater's All Weather Plus strategy falling 5.6% between late February and early April and Shanghai Longlife's top-performing fund declining 25% in March alone.
