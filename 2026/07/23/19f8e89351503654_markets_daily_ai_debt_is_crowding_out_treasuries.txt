AI debt vs Treasuries

All that AI capex needs to be funded, and the long-maturity debt that tech 
companies are pouring into the market iscompeting with Treasuries for bond 
buyers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/us-30-year-yield-raises-alarm-in-longest-run-above-5-since-2007?cmpid=072326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6876>
.

That’s one of the reasons the US 30-year bond yield has held above 5% for the 
longest stretch since the start of the financial crisis. The deluge of issuance 
to fund AI infrastructure is coinciding with growing concern about a 
deteriorating fiscal picture.

As of Wednesday, the 30-year has traded beyond 5% for 27 days in 2026 — 
including the last 12 days in a row — or about 19% of all sessions, according 
to data compiled by Bloomberg.That’s the most number of days and longest 
consecutive run since 2007, when it traded above that level for 50 days.

Since 2007, the Treasury market has ballooned to $31 trillion from $4.5 
trillion while debt as a percentage of US gross domestic product has doubled to 
exceed 100%.

Traditional buyers of 30-year bonds, such as pensions and insurers, now have 
“a wider menu of options than they’ve had in years past,” said Alex Payne at 
Vanguard Capital Management. He isn’t sure yields have reached the highs yet.

For fund managers, the over $500 billion financing linked to AI is a reason 5% 
plus yields are here to stay — unlike similar spikes in the past.

“Whoever’s issuing, be it a government or a hyperscaler or a non-hyperscaler, 
credit is now competing with more borrowers on that long end for the same group 
of investors,” said Tony Rodriguez at Nuveen Asset Management. —Michael

Intel’s test

Intel is expected to report strong second-quarter earnings after the market 
close, but even blowout results likelywon’t be enough to reverse the stock’s 
July slide 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-23/intel-needs-more-than-blowout-earnings-as-chips-rally-falters?cmpid=072326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6876>
.

The shares are down 27% this month, putting them among the 10 worst performers 
in the S&P 500 Index, after soaring 278% in the first half.

The move comes as investors are suddenly selling this year’s winners and 
souring on chipmakers in particular. Enthusiasm for semiconductors is starting 
to fizzle as investors grow concerned about how long the heavy spending on 
artificial intelligence can last.

The Philadelphia Stock Exchange Semiconductor Index, or SOX, has lost 13% this 
month and briefly sank into a bear market last week before recovering somewhat.

“How investors feel about the semiconductor space is going to call what Intel 
does here more than what Intel tells you,” said Matt Bryson of Wedbush 
Securities. — Carmen Reinicke
