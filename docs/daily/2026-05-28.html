US

Equity Markets

US equities remained largely range-bound on Wednesday, with the S&P 500 gaining a marginal 0.2% despite a sharp intraday decline in crude prices — a decoupling that reflects the degree to which AI-driven earnings momentum has eclipsed energy as the dominant market narrative. Ed Yardeni's "FEMO" framework — fabulous earnings momentum rather than fear of missing out — appears to have genuine empirical support: the S&P 500's forward P/E of 20–22x looks defensible if the economy avoids recession, and Yardeni maintains the highest 2026 target on the Street at 8,250. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon and Bank of America's Brian Moynihan both projected strong second-quarter trading revenues at the Bernstein Strategic Decisions Conference, reinforcing near-term earnings optimism.

Corporate Earnings and AI Spending

Micron's sprint to $1 trillion is the standout corporate story of the week. Revenue nearly tripled in its most recent quarter — the fastest growth in the company's recorded history — yet the stock trades at only ~10x forward earnings, a valuation that reflects investor skepticism about the durability of the memory cycle. Snowflake's blowout guidance and its $6 billion Amazon infrastructure agreement underscore the continued build-out of enterprise AI infrastructure. Meta's launch of paid consumer subscriptions for its Meta AI chatbot represents a meaningful step toward monetizing its substantial AI capital expenditure.

Monetary Policy and Macro Backdrop

No Federal Reserve speakers or macro data releases materially moved markets Thursday. The VIX remains suppressed, and DataTrek's Nicholas Colas argues that robust labor market readings — initial claims and the unemployment rate both staying contained through last year's trade war and the current Iran conflict — have shifted the implicit "Trump Put" strike price well below current market levels. This macroeconomic resilience, paradoxically, may be reducing pressure on the administration to resolve the Hormuz standoff quickly. ---

Monetary Policy

The Bank of Korea's new governor delivered a hawkish pivot, signaling rate increases ahead — a significant shift in tone from the prior easing bias. The move appears consistent with persistent inflationary pressures from elevated energy costs tied to the Hormuz disruption, and may reflect concern that a weakening won would amplify imported inflation. Goldman Sachs's Korea desk separately flagged leveraged ETFs tied to Korean equities as potential volatility accelerators in a market that has already reached record highs.

China: Currency and Trade

The yuan's six-quarter rally against the dollar — a streak not seen since 2013 — reflects a structural evolution in China's export mix. AI-related capital goods and technology exports have surged alongside the global investment boom in data center infrastructure, generating sustained dollar inflows and making Beijing more comfortable tolerating a stronger currency. This is a materially different dynamic from prior yuan strength episodes, which were typically driven by manufacturing trade surpluses alone.

Geopolitics and South China Sea

China expelled a Dutch naval frigate, the HNLMS De Ruyter, from waters near the Paracel Islands, citing violations of Chinese airspace by the ship's helicopter. The incident is a rare direct confrontation between Chinese and European naval forces and underscores the contested nature of South China Sea boundaries. The De Ruyter had recently participated in joint exercises with Philippine forces — a detail likely to amplify Beijing's objections. Separately, the US has deepened military coordination across the western Pacific, deploying additional missile systems, fighter jets, and expanding joint training, even as European NATO allies explore greater strategic autonomy.

Corporate

Nio's ES9 launch — China's largest electric SUV — drove a share price rally and signals the company's ambition to compete at the premium end of the domestic EV market, where margins are more defensible than in the mass segment. ---

UK Labor Market and Cost Pressures

The UK's youth unemployment surge is emerging as a structural concern rather than a cyclical one. The government-commissioned review cited a "whole-system failure" spanning entry-level job creation, mental health provision, and educational outcomes, with AI-driven reductions in graduate and junior roles compounding existing weaknesses. Separately, the Food and Drink Federation's survey showing more than 80% of producers planning price increases is a forward-looking inflation signal that the Bank of England will need to weigh carefully — particularly given that much of the cost pressure traces to the Middle East conflict and elevated energy prices rather than domestic demand.

Political Economy: Hungary

With the Champions League final taking place in Budapest this weekend, the occasion spotlights the political legacy of Viktor Orbán, who was ousted in Hungary's election last month. Football served as a vehicle for patronage and subsidy under Orbán's 16-year tenure, with clubs, academies, and stadiums channeling public funds toward political allies. The incoming government now faces the fiscal and institutional cost of unwinding a system built around a single leader — a transition with broader implications for Hungary's relationship with EU institutions and the structural integrity of its public finances.

Monetary Policy

The ECB made no new policy announcements in this period. However, the combination of sticky food and energy inflation driven by the Hormuz disruption, and softening demand in core eurozone economies, maintains the central bank in a difficult calibration exercise between supporting growth and containing embedded price expectations. ---

Cuba Energy Crisis

The diversion of the Russian diesel tanker is a material setback for Cuba, which is navigating one of its most severe fuel shortages in years. The cargo was intended to provide meaningful relief to an energy system under sustained stress, and its failure to arrive tightens an already constrained supply picture. The episode illustrates the degree to which smaller, sanctions-adjacent economies remain exposed to the secondary effects of geopolitical disruption in global energy markets — including shipping route uncertainty amplified by the US-Iran conflict. The broader regional implication is that countries with limited access to spot energy markets face disproportionate vulnerability when supply chains are disrupted. No other significant macro or market developments for Latin America were reported in this period. ---

Global

  • Gold remains approximately 3% higher year-to-date — well below its late-January record high — as higher real yields, a stronger dollar, and reduced safe-haven demand weigh on the metal; UBS cut its year-end forecast to $5,500/oz from $5,900.
  • The US-Iran conflict has entered a period of strategic stasis: the US has conducted strikes twice this week while officially describing the ceasefire as intact, with Trump caught between Iranian demands for financial relief and Republican hawks pressing for a more decisive outcome.
  • Global AI investment is generating measurable macro effects: Chinese export composition is shifting toward technology goods, supporting yuan strength; US chip earnings are decoupling equity markets from energy price signals.

US-Iran Conflict and Hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz remains the central axis of global macro risk. US forces shot down four Iranian drones targeting a commercial vessel and struck a drone launch unit near the strait — actions described as defensive with the ceasefire nominally intact. Iran targeted the US base from which the strikes originated, and Kuwait separately reported responding to missile and drone threats. Trump's public framing — "they're negotiating on fumes" and "maybe we have to go back and finish it" — signals negotiating posture, but the gap between Iranian conditions and Republican hawk expectations remains wide. The longer this stasis persists, the greater the cumulative economic drag from elevated energy prices, shipping disruption, and uncertainty premia across financial markets.

Oil and Market Decoupling

The most striking market dynamic of the period is the near-complete decoupling of equity markets from oil prices. A 5.5% single-day decline in WTI produced virtually no movement in the S&P 500 — a stark contrast to historical patterns. The mechanism is straightforward: AI-driven earnings growth has become the dominant driver of equity valuations, and analysts' profit forecasts have improved materially since the conflict began. Treasuries remain somewhat more sensitive to crude — the 10-year yield continues to track forward oil prices as a proxy for embedded inflation expectations — but even this relationship appears to be attenuating as the market prices in a higher probability of conflict resolution.

Gold and Precious Metals

Gold's inability to reclaim its late-January record high despite ongoing extreme geopolitical risk reflects a confluence of structural pressures: rising real yields (gold generates no income), a stronger dollar since the conflict began, central bank liquidity needs prompting marginal selling, and speculative position reduction following the peak. UBS's reduction of its year-end target to $5,500/oz from $5,900 is consistent with this diagnosis, though the bank maintains a recovery scenario to prior highs. Cyclical commodity strategists, including Longview Economics, argue the ongoing industrial upswing favors copper over gold — a view supported by gold's elevated valuation relative to lumber and copper on historical comparisons.
