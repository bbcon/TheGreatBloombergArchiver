**US-Iran: Military Pressure and Diplomatic Clock**

The US-Iran confrontation dominated global risk sentiment through the week. Indirect nuclear talks in Geneva on Monday and Tuesday concluded on a cautiously constructive note, with Iran's delegation expected to return with detailed proposals within two weeks. President Trump nonetheless stated Iran has ten to fifteen days at most to reach an agreement, and the administration indicated military strikes remain explicitly on the table, with a second aircraft carrier deployed to the region. The Wall Street Journal reported the administration is evaluating a limited early strike designed to pressure Tehran toward negotiations. Prediction market participants assigned roughly 70% probability to a US strike by Thursday. Brent crude surged 4.3% on Wednesday to re-cross $70 per barrel, with WTI trading near $66 — close to a six-month high — as the combination of diplomatic uncertainty and military posturing removed whatever supply-risk discount had accumulated in prior weeks. Iran's calibrated scheduling of Strait of Hormuz naval exercises on Monday, even as talks were underway in Geneva, illustrated the deliberate two-track strategy both sides appear to be running. The UK's reported reluctance to grant US access to Diego Garcia for potential operations adds a further complication to military planning. The timeline for resolution, and whether diplomacy retains genuine viability, remains the single most consequential near-term variable for global energy and risk markets.

**Energy Markets and IEA Fracture**

Beyond Iran, the IEA ministerial summit in Paris crystallized a genuine institutional fracture. US Energy Secretary Chris Wright assailed the agency's climate focus, the US threatened withdrawal unless the IEA abandons net zero framing, and the meeting ended without the customary joint communiqué — an outcome without modern precedent for an institution the US co-founded in the 1970s. Members found limited common ground on critical minerals and energy security, but the divisions appear structural rather than transient. Meanwhile, South Korea's Sinokor group accumulated roughly 120 VLCCs over recent weeks, helping push benchmark VLCC earnings above $120,000 per day — a more than fourfold increase in a month — a market signal that traders are positioning for a regime change in tanker demand regardless of the diplomatic outcome with Iran.

**AI Sovereignty as a Geopolitical Theme**

The week's second dominant cross-cutting theme was AI sovereignty. India's summit crystallized a concern shared across Asia, Africa, Europe, and Latin America: the most consequential AI systems are controlled by a small number of US and Chinese entities, and access to them can, in principle, be withdrawn. India's response — domestic small language models, shared compute facilities, semiconductor ambitions, and active capital attraction — represents a state-directed variant of a logic other governments are independently reaching. OpenAI's concurrent move to finalize a funding round exceeding $100 billion at a valuation above $850 billion, anchored by Amazon, SoftBank, Nvidia, and Microsoft, illustrates the scale of capital concentration at stake. Rishi Sunak's framework for sovereign resilience — secure a niche in the AI supply chain, diversify providers, build switching capabilities — is practically sound but difficult to execute against incumbents with compounding resource advantages.

**Climate Policy Divergence**

Climate policy divergence between the US federal government and the rest of the world widened further. EU carbon permits fell to their lowest since July as Slovakia called for ETS suspension and Germany's Merz floated revision before softening his stance; the Commission committed to a reform proposal in Q3 but JPMorgan noted the uncertainty is already impairing corporate green capex planning. The EPA's rollback of Mercury and Air Toxics Standards and the administration's own cost-benefit analysis suggesting costs from the endangerment finding rollback could exceed $1.4 trillion materially complicate the administration's savings claims and are likely to face sustained legal challenge. Former US officials were simultaneously in Mumbai urging the Global South to accelerate green transitions without waiting for Washington — a reorientation of climate diplomacy that reflects a genuine, if uncertain, shift in where ambition now resides.
