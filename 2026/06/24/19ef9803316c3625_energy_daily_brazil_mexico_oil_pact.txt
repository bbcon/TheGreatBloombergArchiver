Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporters Mariana Durao, Peter Millard and Scott Squires look at cooperation 
efforts between Latin America’s two biggest oil producers.

Latin America’s two dominant oil producers are a study in contrasts.

Pemex, wholly owned by the Mexican state, has struggled for years under a 
crushing debt load 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/pemex-posts-third-straight-loss-despite-war-fueled-oil-rally?cmpid=BBBXT062426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260624&utm_content=5888>
, slumping output and a string of accidents and other missteps.

Meanwhile, Petrobras has managed to tap new deepwater reserves while remaining 
consistently profitable and a vital source of cash to the Brazilian federal 
government. But it’s under pressure to find ways of extending itsoil boom 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/brazilian-oil-jobs-are-at-16-year-high-on-offshore-drilling-boom?cmpid=BBBXT062426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260624&utm_content=5888>
.

The two national oil champions, also known as Petroleos Mexicanos and Petroleo 
Brasileiro SA,agreed yesterday to cooperate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/pemex-and-petrobras-to-team-up-on-oil-and-refining-projects?cmpid=BBBXT062426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260624&utm_content=5888>
 on production. That decision reveals challenges at both companies.

For Pemex, it’s been been decades since it announced a significant new 
discovery. Finding a way to reverse the long-term decline in output may be its 
best option for digging itself out of such a deep fiscal hole.

Its next big oil find may lie on the company’s doorstep. As Petrobras’s boss 
Magda Chambriard points out, the Gulf of Mexico contains significant salt 
formations, similar to the geological formation found off Brazil’s Atlantic 
coast, where Petrobras has developed deepwater exploration expertise.

But it’s not just Pemex that will be crossing its fingers once the drill rigs 
are deployed.
A floating production and storage vessel in the Buzios field off the coast of 
Rio de Janeiro.Photographer: Dado Galdieri/Bloomberg
Petrobras also hasn’t had much luck recently exploring at home. Its role as a 
leading source of non-OPEC production growth is expected to end in a few years 
when Buzios, the biggest oil field ever discovered in deep waters, completes an 
expansion and output peaks.

To be clear, the agreement between the two companies is non-binding, and it 
says as much about the political considerations in Brasilia and Mexico City as 
it does about the hard realities of the oil business.

Brazil’s President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva is heading into a difficult 
reelection campaign, and an energy partnership with a like-minded 
administration allows him to project a role as an international statesman. For 
Mexico, a partnership with the more widely respected and accomplished Petrobras 
diverts attention from decades of mismanagement.

This is hardly the first time that Latin America has pursued energy 
integration. Yet many of the region’s grander political visions in that space 
have struggled to materialize.

One notable example was former Venezuelan leader Hugo Chávez’s proposal for a 
continent-spanning natural gas pipeline network. Two decades later, the project 
remains unrealized.

—Mariana Durao, Peter Millard and Scott Squires, Bloomberg News

shipments collapsed as China rolled out its export-control regime globally. The 
throttling of goods began after Takaichi angered Beijing last November with 
comments about self-ruled Taiwan.
