Oil once served as RussiaтАЩs economic salvation, having kept a sclerotic Soviet 
Union going in the 1970s under Leonid Brezhnev. In theearly 2000s <>, high 
energy prices helped power a recovery from 1990s financial chaos.

But this time, things are different. The surge in crude prices sparked by the 
Iran war, and even a temporary lifting of US sanctions on Russian oil, hasnтАЩt 
kept the Eurasian giantтАЩs economy out of trouble. The cracksthis newsletter 
spotlighted last year

Why isnтАЩt oil washing away MoscowтАЩs economic woes? Part of the answer is 
RussiaтАЩs success earlier this century in diversifying beyond oil and gas, which 
now only accounts for roughly 15% of GDP, according to Ekaterina Vlasova of 
Bloomberg Economics. That sector isnтАЩt big enough to overcome major trouble in 
the main part of the economy: the non-oil, private sector.

And that engine, making up roughly three quarters of GDP, is stalling. 
Ironically, itтАЩs being restrained by тАЬRussiaтАЩs own macro discipline,тАЭ says 
Vlasova, who once served at the nationтАЩs central bank.

An underappreciated point about President Vladimir Putin is his belief in 
tight monetary and fiscal policies, as explained below. With interest rates 
high and no help coming from non-military government spending, the civilian 
economy is getting squeezed. Frustration is growing as ordinary Russians 
increasingly feel the strain of the Ukraine war and private business costs 
mount, Vlasova says. MoscowтАЩs response, she says, is likely to be even tighter 
control to suppress discontent.

Putin, the 73-year-old former KGB officer, rose to power in post-Soviet Russia 
at the tail end of an economically disastrous decade. The nation saw not only a 
devastating 1998 default, butpanic-inducing moves 
<https://www.nytimes.com/1993/07/25/world/russia-withdraws-older-bank-notes.html>
 like the invalidation of huge swathes of ruble banknotes. The hammering of 
household wealth made for a volatile political backdrop, and the economic 
turmoil left Moscow dependent on the West, with loans from the International 
Monetary Fund.

In his early years as RussiaтАЩs leader, Putin prioritized paying down <> that 
debt while assembling an economic-policy framework that emphasized stability. 
Since his full-scale invasion of Ukraine in 2022, he has defended Bank of 
Russia Governor Elvira Nabiullina in her efforts to contain inflationary 
pressures with interest-rate hikes that took the benchmark to 21%.

While that rate is now down to 14.5%, it remains one of the highest in the 
world 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/kremlin-s-war-leaves-russia-with-some-of-world-s-costliest-money?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260530&utm_content=4874>
 when measured in real terms тАФ making it tough for companies to borrow and 
expand. Real rates are higher than 9%, as consumer prices are rising less than 
6%. One might ask, in the context of an authoritarian government, whether 
RussiaтАЩs inflation figures can be believed.

Vlasova, who previously worked at the Economy Ministry, says thereтАЩs been no 
return to the Soviet practice of maintaining separate data sets тАФ one for the 
public and one for the wielders of power. Indeed, theministry 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/russia-cuts-growth-outlook-despite-putin-s-push-to-rally-economy?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260530&utm_content=4874>

growth to just 0.4%, from 1.3%.

On the fiscal side, RussiaтАЩs spending on the war has blown out its budget 
deficit to a record 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-08/russia-s-budget-gap-widens-to-record-despite-oil-revenue-boost?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260530&utm_content=4874>
, data showed this month. Yet the 2.5% deficit-to-GDP ratio is one that most 
developed nations would pine for. (The US is on course toexceed 6% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/us-records-smaller-april-budget-surplus-on-lower-tax-receipts?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260530&utm_content=4874>
 this year.)

Within the budget, spending has shifted, however. Since 2022, military outlays 
have become the largest line item, accounting for about a third of total 
expenditures. Spending on state procurement jumped 41%.

Deputy Prime Minister Alexander Novak, in an interview with the Vedomosti 
newspaper earlier this month, acknowledged that a shift in budget priorities 
has added to the economyтАЩs headwinds.

The bottom line is that policymakers are prepared to live with cutbacks to 
growth and investment in pursuit of lower inflation. Alexander Shokhin, the 
head of a lobbying group representing major Russian industries, explained last 
month that, in the absence of sufficient investment, some sensitive sectors are 
effectively experiencing a тАЬdeep-shock freeze 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-22/putin-s-oil-windfall-won-t-revive-his-slowing-wartime-economy?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260530&utm_content=4874>
.тАЭ

Besides relatively tight monetary and fiscal policies, RussiaтАЩs civilian 
economy is coping with amassive labor shortage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-19/russia-s-wartime-labor-crunch-will-weigh-on-economy-for-years?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260530&utm_content=4874>
. Bloomberg Economics estimates the nation would need to add 1.5 million 
workers to restore a balanced labor market. Part of that is the 700,000 
Russians who officials say are currently fighting in Ukraine. On top of that, 
academic estimates put the wave of 2022тАУ2023 emigration at roughly 650,000.

тАЬThe economy is running at full capacity, with war-linked sectors sucking up 
all spare capital and labor, crowding out private enterprise,тАЭ Tom Holland, 
deputy global research director at Gavekal Research, wrote in a note this week. 
тАЬIn the long run, this model is not sustainable. But that does not mean 
RussiaтАЩs economic implosion is imminent.тАЭ

What the increasing strain has done is make it tougher for the Kremlin to 
sustain any sense of economic normality, Vlasova says. Robust expansion of the 
civilian economy had тАЬhelped maintain passive acceptance across much of the 
population, if not outright support for the war,тАЭ she says.

With the economic costs of the conflict becoming more visible at home, Vlasova 
concludes, the trade-off is becoming clearer. PutinтАЩs government is likely to 
react by moving towards tighter domestic controls to contain any popular 
blowback.
