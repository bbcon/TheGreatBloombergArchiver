Fiscal Dominance?

Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi has led the ruling Liberal Democratic 
Party to an historiclandslide win 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/japan-s-takaichi-set-for-resounding-election-win-nhk-poll-shows?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 that paves the way for her to push through a potentially market-rattling 
policy program.

A super majority 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/historic-win-pits-takaichi-s-vision-of-japan-against-the-markets?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 in the lower house will make it easier to pass legislation and even puts a 
contentious bid to amend the nation’s pacifist constitution back on the cards. 
While geo-strategists will scrutinize her stance toward China after she rankled 
Beijing with comments over Taiwan, bond and currency traders have a nearer term 
focus: what will a resurgent Takaichi do onfiscal policy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/japan-s-takaichi-vows-fiscal-sustainability-fast-tax-cut-talks?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
?
Sanae Takaichi speaks to the media at her party’s headquarters in Tokyo on 
Sunday.Photographer: Toru Hanai/Bloomberg
That’s where Takaichi may find that — for all the political strength the 
weekend election result gives her — global investors aren’t swayed by such 
outcomes.

With the first meaningful inflation bout in decades unnerving households 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/japan-s-real-wages-drop-extends-to-full-year-as-prices-advance?srnd=phx-economics-v2&cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
, Takaichi has said she’ll accelerate talks on cutting the sales tax on food, 
which risks adding strains on a budget that’s already deeply in the red. 
Problem is, concerns over the fiscal trajectory are weakening the yen, thereby 
driving up import prices and exacerbating the price pressures Takaichi is 
trying to offset.

Plans to boost defense spending and ramp up state-backed investment add to 
those fiscal pressures in a country already carrying the developed world’s 
biggest public debt burden of about 230% of gross domestic product.

Evercore ISI economists including Krishna Guha reckon the election outcome 
improves the base case for Japan’s economy, with Takaichi secure enough 
politically not to push fiscal policy beyond what the bond market can bear.

“But we also see a fat right tail – in which Takaichi uses her authority to 
marginalize voices of restraint including in the Ministry of Finance, forgets 
the bond market gets a vote too, and ends up causing a Japanese version of aLiz 
Truss episode 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-12/trump-s-liz-truss-moment-helps-uk-remember-bond-market-is-boss?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
,” they wrote.

They see her win as “part of a global theme of fiscal deficits and aggressive 
support for defense and strategic high tech industry in a dog-eat-dog world of 
hard power competition, that will support growth but also favors higher term 
premia on longer-term debt.”

Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama tried to reassure markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/japan-s-katayama-ready-to-talk-with-market-on-monday-if-needed?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 on Monday, saying that the planned tax cut wouldn’t require additional bond 
issuance. Markets seem to have listened, with stocks surging, the currency 
steady, and gains in bond yields muted. But those fiscal pressures aren’t going 
away anytime soon, meaning the potential for a bond market rebellion is still 
there.

Another upshot of Takaichi’s big win: more pressure on the Bank of Japan to 
avoid further interest-rate increases. (Back in 2024 she described the idea of 
a hike as “stupid.”) But again, that maycome down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/markets-give-takaichi-benefit-of-doubt-as-stocks-and-yen-climb?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 to markets — if the yen keeps weakening and bond yields rising, the BOJ may 
find it has little choice.

In the interim, Japan’s top currency official said the government remains on 
high alert 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/japan-s-fx-chief-says-monitoring-market-after-takaichi-s-win?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 as it monitors the FX market. The currency weakened to as far as 157.76 to the 
dollar before paring losses to trade around 157 mid-morning in Tokyo. Many 
strategists see 160 as a level that may trigger intervention.

The Week Ahead

Investors and policymakers are gearing up for a busy week of US economic 
reports that includes arguably the two most consequential data snapshots — 
employment and inflation.

The January jobs report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/-consequential-us-jobs-report-will-define-labor-market-slowdown?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 on Wednesday and the consumer price index, due Friday, are unusually close on 
the calendar after the partial government shutdowndelayed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/january-us-jobs-report-rescheduled-for-feb-11-bls-says?cmpid=BBD020926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 each by a few days. Normally, CPI lands in the week following the jobs data.

The employment report will be even more substantive than usual. In addition to 
the monthly payrolls and unemployment numbers, each January release includes an 
annual revision to the jobs count. The so-called benchmark update is expected 
to reveal a notable markdown to payrolls growth in the year through March 2025.

Elsewhere, inflation data from China to Switzerland to Brazil, GDP in the UK, 
and rate decisions from Russia to Peru may focus investors.

Need-to-Know Research

In an unconventional bid to cut through the fog of central bank speak, 
Nomura’s economists have just released a report where they play the roles of 
key global policymakers and share their fictional assessments in a series of 
“letters.” Chief Economist Rob Subbaraman plays the role of General Manager of 
the Bank for International Settlements, with a letter to heads of the Fed, ECB, 
BOE, BOJ, PBOC and RBI — all represented by Nomura’s economists for each region.

While we don’t have room here to summarize all of those fictional letters, 
here’s a snapshot of the main concern Subbaraman raised:

 * The combination of high public debt and populist leadership creates a 
dangerous scenario: if bond yields rise or growth slows, fiscal finances could 
become unsustainable. That could lead to fiscal dominance.
 * If executive authority expands unchecked and the rule of law erodes there 
could be more US domestic policy mistakes and crony capitalism.
 * A bursting of the AI bubble would be particularly severe for the global 
economy, given US equity market cap is now over twice GDP and the rest of the 
world is so heavily invested in the US tech sector.
 * The erosion of trust among world leaders leaves little hope for rapid 
global coordination during crises, be it an AI-driven cyberattack, financial 
collapse or pandemic.
While the report is meant to be a bit of fun, as the old saying goes, many a 
true word is spoken in jest.
