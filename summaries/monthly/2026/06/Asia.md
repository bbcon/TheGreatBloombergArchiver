• China's May retail sales fell 0.6% YoY — the first contraction since the Covid reopening, badly missing consensus — while fixed-asset investment contracted 4.1% in January–May, worse than forecast and the weakest reading since 2020.
• South Korea and Taiwan are the clearest AI-cycle beneficiaries: Goldman Sachs projects Korea's current-account surplus exceeding 10% of GDP in 2026, while Taiwan raised its full-year growth forecast to 9.6% — the largest export surge in 50 years.
• The Bank of Japan raised its benchmark rate 25 basis points to 1.0% in W25, the highest since 1995, though the yen remained above 160 per dollar throughout the period, illustrating the limits of rate policy in resolving Japan's structural imbalances.
• Bank Indonesia delivered three emergency rate hikes in approximately one month, raising its benchmark to 5.75%, as the rupiah hit a record low and the Jakarta Composite extended year-to-date losses to nearly 40% — the worst equity performance globally.
• China's May exports rose 19.4% YoY in W24, materially beating the 15% consensus, led by a 111% surge in integrated circuit sales; yet the MSCI China index fell approximately 20% below its October peak, approaching bear market territory.

**China: Industrial Strength, Consumer Stagnation**
China's macro picture presents a deepening internal contradiction. Exports are surging — the May trade surplus of $105.4 billion was the largest since January, driven by AI-related hardware demand — while domestic consumers are retrenching at a pace not seen outside the pandemic. Retail sales contracted 0.6% YoY in May, with auto purchases down 16% and home appliance sales declining by double digits as government trade-in subsidies were reduced. Fixed-asset investment shrank 4.1% in January–May, with private capex down 7.1% and manufacturing investment contracting for the first time in six years. Industrial production accelerated to 4.5% growth, driven by high-tech manufacturing expanding 15% in value terms — a demand-side rather than supply-side failure. Macquarie analysts draw structural parallels with 1990s Japan: a burst asset bubble, subdued consumption, an aging population, with home prices down approximately 30% from their 2021 peak. Bloomberg Economics held its 2026 China GDP forecast at 4.5%, though some analysts estimate April growth near 4%, below the official target range.

**The AI Super Surplus and Its Limits**
South Korea and Taiwan are producing the period's most structurally significant macro development. SK Hynix has reportedly sold out its entire 2026 high-bandwidth memory production predominantly to Nvidia; Goldman Sachs estimates Korea's current-account surplus could reach 10%–15% of GDP. Taiwan's 9.6% growth forecast would represent its largest export surge in half a century. Yet concentration creates acute volatility: the Kospi fell 10% on Tuesday of W26, triggering a circuit-breaker halt, before recovering sharply following Micron's results. Korean regulators expressed regret over having approved leveraged single-stock ETFs after an SK Hynix double-leveraged product listed in Hong Kong translated a 13% single-name decline into a roughly 26% loss for holders. HSBC's Frederic Neumann cautions that any pullback in AI hardware demand would expose underlying fragilities — a risk difficult to quantify but rising as valuations extend.

**Southeast Asia and Japan**
Bank Indonesia's three consecutive hikes reflect the severity of a crisis that is institutional in origin: President Prabowo's interventionist policy signals have not materially shifted, and Capital Economics characterised the hikes as "merely a sticking plaster." The BOJ's trajectory is reinforced by Tokyo CPI accelerating for the first time in eight months in June, with futures markets assigning roughly 50/50 odds to a further hike as early as October. India's monsoon running approximately 40% below normal as of mid-June introduces additional risk to agricultural output and rural incomes. China's extension of rare earth and tungsten export controls against Japan and the US — with flows of key minerals to Japan at zero since January — adds a geopolitical dimension that markets have not fully priced.
