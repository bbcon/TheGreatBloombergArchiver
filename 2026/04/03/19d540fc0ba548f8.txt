This year was supposed to be a turning point for carbon markets, with the 
United NationsтАЩ long-delayed country-to-country trading system coming into 
force and airlines preparing to enter a mandatory program to offset their 
emissions.

But the high-profile collapse of a World Bank-backed clean-cooking company has 
delivered another blow to confidence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-02/how-koko-networks-collapse-is-denting-trust-in-carbon-markets-again?cmpid=BBD040326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260403>
 in the market, which has been battered by credibility issues for years and, 
more recently, by a climate commitment retreat by governments and companies.

Koko Networks Ltd., which sold cooking stoves and clean-burning ethanol to 
about 1.3 million households in Kenya, folded in January amid concerns it was 
issuing more carbon credits than its products warranted, a claim the company 
has denied.

The bankruptcy has had a ripple effect across projects selling carbon credits, 
companies buying them and the marketplaces on which those offsets are traded. 
Prices on Corsia, the marketplace for airlines where Koko was looking to sell 
its credits, fell as low as $12.25 from about $15 just before the firmтАЩs 
collapse, according to data compiled by Bloomberg, and now sit at $12.85.

тАЬThe events of Koko have definitely created an undermining of confidence,тАЭ 
said Douglas Greenwell, commercial director and head of the carbon business at 
Burn, the worldтАЩs largest clean-cookstove developer and a seller of carbon 
credits.

Buyers have been unnerved by KokoтАЩs failure to deliver credits they had been 
counting on and are reluctant to commit until there is more certainty, 
Greenwell said. He expects the softening of prices to be short term. тАЬCorsia 
buyer demand [that] was emerging last year has not gone away, but itтАЩs 
elongated,тАЭ he added. тАЬDecision-making cycles have increased.тАЭ

Carbon offsets have long been a Rorschach test among climate experts. Some 
carbon markets allow companies to offset their greenhouse gas emissions by 
purchasing credits from businesses whose activities reduce the production of 
pollutants. Each credit represents a ton of carbon dioxide; Koko generated 
credits by attempting to quantify the deforestation avoided byshifting its 
customers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2022-11-01/selling-a-cleaner-affordable-cooking-fuel-for-kenyan-homes?cmpid=BBD040326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260403>
 away from cooking over wood and charcoal fires.

Proponents argue that carbon credits channel crucial funds from companies and 
rich governments to poorer nations and communities, incentivizing the 
protection of important natural environments. Others argue that because the 
vast majority of projects focus on avoided emissions тАФ trees that arenтАЩt cut 
down or fossil fuels that arenтАЩt burned тАФ the sector is prone to unreliable 
carbon math or even fraud.

тАЬAny time there are concerns about a prominent projectтАЩs ability to deliver

Harrison, head of sustainability research at BloombergNEF. тАЬItтАЩs a fairly 
persistent trend with projects around the world.тАЭ

The UNтАЩs International Civil Aviation Organization, which oversees Corsia,

voluntary basis, but most airlines will be required to comply from 2027.

Pulling all levers

By Linda Poon <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUc4draqlcE/linda-poon> and 
Sarah Holder <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUc4d1kiuto/sarah-holder>

As the great oil shock of 2026 enters its fifth week, cities around the world 
have met the prospect of an extended period of higher fuel prices with adiverse 
mix of measures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-02/iran-war-revives-1970s-style-gas-saving-policies-transit-ridership?cmpid=BBD040326_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260403>

In Melbourne, for example, fares for trains, buses and trams have been

road,тАЭ said Jacinta Allan, premier of the Australian state of Victoria, in a

European Union officials in Brussels are urging residents to work from home

тАЬMetro is always going to be cheaper than driving a car,тАЭ Randy Clarke, general

Across the globe, lawmakers are quickly pushing out a range of

For those of a certain age, these measures will sound familiar: The 1973-1974

But not all governments are embracing efficiency this time around. The Trump 
administration has sought to downplay the pain Americans are feeling at the 
pump and has shown little interest in rolling back its campaign against

global oil prices will rise much further, mobility advocates are urging 
policymakers to not miss yet another opportunity to drive changes that could 
permanently reduce reliance on fossil fuels and shield communities from the 
next oil shock.

Past oil crises suggest that higher prices at the pump can nudge more people
