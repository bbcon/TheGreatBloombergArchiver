**ECB: A Consequential but Contested Decision**
The ECB's Thursday hike stands as the most significant developed-market monetary policy action of the week. Policymakers concluded that oil-driven inflation — now running well above target for more than three months — combined with evidence of broadening price pressures made further inaction untenable. President Lagarde argued the eurozone economy retains sufficient resilience to absorb tighter conditions; critics, including Berenberg's Holger Schmieding, explicitly invoked the ECB's 2011 error of hiking into a deteriorating growth environment. Markets are pricing approximately 75 basis points of total tightening by year-end, a path the consensus views as likely too aggressive given current growth dynamics. JPMorgan Asset Management called Thursday's move "one and done"; Pictet and Carmignac argued the case for holding had been legitimate. The tension between anti-inflation credibility and growth risk has not been resolved by the decision itself.

**German Economy and European Politics**
The German data remain internally contradictory. Factory orders declining more than expected in April reinforces the case for a second-quarter contraction, while industrial production growing for the first time since the Iran war began suggests stabilization rather than sustained recovery. Germany also failed this week to win a non-permanent UN Security Council seat for the first time in its history — a diplomatic signal that Foreign Minister Wadephul attributed partly to Berlin's Ukraine and Israel positions, but which appears to reflect a broader erosion of standing in the Global South. Chancellor Merz faces record disapproval ratings domestically. Austria separately lost its final AAA credit rating from a major assessor. European leaders including Starmer, Merz, and Macron convened in London with Zelenskyy and called on Putin to accept an immediate ceasefire, though Russia has not accepted direct talks.

**UK: Growth Fading, Politics Deteriorating**
April GDP data showed British economic momentum fading after a solid early-2026 start — arriving alongside Healey's resignation, which leaves Starmer vulnerable to leadership pressure ahead of NATO defence spending discussions. Bloomberg Economics' ten-year Brexit assessment, published Monday, concluded the departure has cost the UK between 2% and 4% of GDP, with a central estimate of 2.5% equating to roughly £30 billion in forgone annual tax revenue. A UK poll showing 52% of Britons now favor EU rejoining has made the topic mainstream, though the political path remains heavily contested. Chancellor Reeves separately announced plans to ease double-taxation burdens on wealthy US investors relocating to Britain — a signal of deliberate capital attraction strategy amid bilateral trade repositioning.
