Weekly Brief

May 18 – May 24, 2026

US

  • The 30-year Treasury yield touched 5.19% on Tuesday — a level last seen in June 2007 — as energy-driven inflation from the Hormuz closure eroded rate-cut expectations and elevated term premia across the curve.
  • Kevin Warsh was sworn in as Federal Reserve Chair on Friday, inheriting an FOMC that has pivoted sharply hawkish; minutes from Powell's final meeting showed "almost all" participants expected persistent inflation pressure and "many" favored dropping the easing bias entirely.
  • Nvidia reported 85% revenue growth and authorized an $80 billion buyback, while SpaceX filed its S-1 targeting up to $75 billion in proceeds — together sustaining a narrowing AI-led equity rally even as rate pressures mount.
  • NextEra announced a $67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy on Tuesday — the largest US utility deal on record — positioning the combined entity to capture surging data-center power demand across Florida and Virginia.
  • Gulf petrodollar retrenchment is emerging as a structural headwind: Saudi Arabia's PIF has cut its foreign asset target to 18% from ~50%, reducing the marginal bid for US Treasuries and equities at a moment of elevated supply.

Bond Markets and the Inflation Repricing

The week's defining development in US markets was the continued repricing of the long end of the Treasury curve. The 30-year yield rose to 5.19% on Tuesday, with Citigroup flagging 5.5% as the next technical level, while the 10-year touched 4.63% — its highest since February — and fed funds futures shifted to price roughly a 70–75% probability of a quarter-point hike by December. The proximate driver remains the Strait of Hormuz closure, now entering its third month, which has kept Brent above $100 and April CPI running at 3.8% year-on-year. The FOMC minutes released Wednesday confirmed the shift: "almost all" participants expected supply-shock inflation to persist, and "many" preferred dropping the easing bias. DoubleLine's Jeffrey Gundlach declared a June cut off the table; Ed Yardeni publicly urged the Fed to abandon its easing bias altogether. Barclays' Ajay Rajadhyaksha argued that only a meaningful deterioration in macro data is likely to arrest the yield move absent a Hormuz reopening. TD Securities now projects 30-year mortgage rates ending 2026 near 6.15%, pointing to continued stagnation in housing activity. Jamie Dimon warned rates "could be much higher than they are today."

The New Fed Era

Kevin Warsh's arrival as Chair on Friday introduces genuine uncertainty about the policy path. Nominated with a reputation for hawkish instincts, Warsh inherits a committee whose consensus has already moved toward him — though Standard Chartered's Steve Englander noted he may attempt to delay hikes by emphasizing trimmed-mean inflation and unit labor costs. ABN Amro analysts suggested Warsh may scale back the SEP and press conference cadence within six to twelve months and could commission an external review of the Fed's framework. The realistic ceiling on his near-term optionality appears to be holding rates rather than cutting them, given the energy shock's inflationary persistence.

Equities: A Narrowing Rally

The S&P 500 was on track for its eighth consecutive weekly gain by Friday — its longest streak since 2023 — yet the breadth picture has deteriorated materially. Ten technology names, led by Nvidia, Amazon, Apple, Alphabet, and Broadcom, accounted for essentially the entirety of the cap-weighted index's gain since the Iran conflict began, while the equal-weighted S&P 500 was flat over the same period. Nvidia's earnings, released Wednesday evening, delivered 85% revenue growth and 62% gross margins, but guidance disappointed elevated expectations and the stock dipped in after-hours trade. BofA's fund manager survey recorded a record monthly jump in equity allocations alongside the sharpest cash drawdown since February 2024 — readings strategist Michael Hartnett characterized as near-complete "bull capitulation," with early June flagged as a window for profit-taking contingent on yield trajectory. Morgan Stanley's Michael Wilson cautioned that a sustained yield grind could produce the first meaningful correction since late March.

Corporate Activity and Policy

Beyond NextEra-Dominion, Google and Blackstone agreed Wednesday to form an AI cloud business with an initial $5 billion equity commitment scaling to roughly $25 billion including leverage, sending shares of smaller cloud competitors lower. Meta announced it was reassigning 7,000 employees to AI roles ahead of broader restructuring. SpaceX's S-1, potentially the week's most consequential filing, disclosed $18.7 billion in 2025 revenue and a $4.94 billion net loss, alongside a super-voting structure and performance milestones tied to a Mars colony; Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley are reported to lead the offering. On fiscal policy, Senate Republicans rebuffed Trump's $1.8 billion request for a "weaponization" account — a notable intra-party setback — while the EU finalized the text of its US trade deal ahead of the July 4 tariff deadline, capping EU export tariffs at 15% in exchange for removal of EU duties on US industrial goods. The emerging constraint from Gulf capital retrenchment — with Saudi Arabia's PIF cutting its targeted foreign allocation and withdrawing from commitments including LIV Golf — adds a structural, if gradual, headwind to Treasury demand that deserves monitoring as the conflict extends. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi surged 8.4% on Thursday following Nvidia's earnings and the SpaceX IPO filing, with Samsung climbing 8% after averting a general strike through an eleventh-hour union deal; SK Hynix's market capitalization reached $910 billion.
  • Putin's state visit to Beijing — his 25th — underscored the depth of the Sino-Russian axis, though the structural leverage in Power of Siberia 2 pipeline pricing sits firmly with Xi, who can negotiate from a position of diversified supply options.
  • Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike to 5.25% mid-week, while President Prabowo announced a state entity to centralize palm oil, coal, and nickel exports — moves that together reflect deepening economic nationalism and sent the Jakarta Composite to the worst year-to-date performance among major benchmarks at roughly -26%.
  • China's April data — retail sales +0.2% year-on-year, industrial production +4.1%, fixed-asset investment -1.6% year-to-date — delivered a uniformly weak reading that has reopened the debate over Beijing's stimulus restraint.
  • Japan's Q1 GDP expanded an annualized 2.1%, yet super-long JGB yields approached 3% as PM Takaichi unveiled a roughly $19 billion supplementary fiscal package, complicating the BOJ's hiking path.

China: Domestic Weakness and Strategic Positioning

The week's China narrative was pulled in two directions. April activity data, released Monday, delivered a broad-based disappointment: retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year — the softest since December 2022 — with auto sales down 15% and jewelry off 21%, while industrial production grew a below-consensus 4.1% and fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% year-to-date. No Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated a downside surprise of this breadth. The figures confirm that the export channel is no longer offsetting domestic demand weakness and have revived calls for additional stimulus, though Beijing's precise policy response — whether fiscal, monetary, or property-targeted — remains unclear. Complicating the picture further, domestic banks received $61.9 billion in net inflows on behalf of clients in April, the sixth month of net inflows in seven, providing some underlying support for the yuan and onshore equities. Yet authorities appear to be managing rather than encouraging the AI-fueled equity rally: exchanges are scrutinizing momentum trades and the "national team" is reportedly reducing ETF holdings by roughly 90% in the first half of the year. The post-summit US-China framework — including a $17 billion annual agricultural commitment through 2028 and new trade boards — remains thin on operational detail, with Beijing retaining meaningful leverage through rare earth export controls that underpin roughly $1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity.

The Putin Visit and Regional Geopolitics

Vladimir Putin arrived in Beijing on Monday with an unusually large delegation — including five deputy prime ministers, central bank Governor Nabiullina, and heads of major state enterprises — seeking to leverage Middle East energy turmoil into better terms on the long-stalled Power of Siberia 2 pipeline. The optics told a more complicated story: the visit followed a record Ukrainian drone strike on Moscow over the weekend, Russia's economy remains heavily dependent on Chinese demand, and Xi had just concluded his own successful summit with Trump. Xi publicly cautioned Washington against resuming strikes on Iran and called for a "comprehensive ceasefire," positioning Beijing as an alternative diplomatic pole. Progress on pipeline pricing remained unconfirmed by week's end. Separately, Taiwan's President Lai delivered remarks casting Taipei as a stabilizing regional actor, and Trump's renewed suggestion of a direct call with Lai introduced fresh tail risk to the recently stabilized US-China relationship. The Philippines' Marcos reiterated that Manila would likely be drawn into any Taiwan conflict, a stance certain to irritate Beijing.

Korea, Japan, Indonesia, and India

South Korea's week encapsulated the AI cycle's distributional tensions: the Kospi had gained an extraordinary 73% year-to-date entering the week, driven almost entirely by Samsung and SK Hynix, with retail margin debt at a record ₩36.5 trillion. Samsung's union negotiations collapsed Tuesday, sending shares down as much as 4.4%, before an eleventh-hour deal on Thursday — which could distribute roughly ₩40 trillion in AI-linked bonuses averaging around $340,000 per chip employee — triggered an 8% rebound. Korean export data for May 1–20 showed working-day-adjusted chip shipments up 202% year-on-year, providing tangible evidence of the AI cycle's real-economy impact. Japan's GDP surprise was undercut by bond market stress: the 30-year JGB yield approached 3% as PM Takaichi's fiscal reversal added to supply concerns, prompting Finance Minister Katayama to flag markets as a policy risk, though Pimco turned constructive on the long end. In Indonesia, the combination of Bank Indonesia's surprise 50 basis point hike and President Prabowo's commodity export centralization — a move that reportedly blindsided senior officials and drew a Moody's warning — encapsulates the policy improvisation under financial pressure that characterizes several Southeast Asian economies this cycle. India's rupee approached 97 per dollar, with the RBI reportedly weighing measures echoing its 2013 taper-tantrum playbook, including rate hikes, FX swaps, and offshore dollar issuance. ---

Europe

  • The EU finalized the text of its US trade deal on Wednesday, averting a threatened rise in auto tariffs from 15% to 25% ahead of Trump's July 4 deadline, though the arrangement formalizes a materially less favorable trading relationship than the pre-tariff status quo.
  • The eurozone composite PMI fell to 47.5 in May — the sharpest contraction in two and a half years — as the energy shock from the Iran conflict weighed particularly heavily on France, whose reading dropped to its lowest since 2020.
  • UK M&A activity has risen more than 250% year-to-date to roughly $150 billion — the strongest pace since 2015 — as overseas acquirers exploit depressed sterling valuations, even as domestic political uncertainty intensifies.
  • ECB policymakers, including Belgian Governor Pierre Wunsch, signaled a June rate hike is likely, marking a notable repricing from the easing bias that characterized earlier cycle positioning.
  • UK retail sales fell at the sharpest pace in nearly a year on Friday, while Chancellor Reeves moved to close a corporate tax loophole used by oil and gas producers, projecting roughly £300 million annually to fund cost-of-living relief.

Trade Deal and the Industrial Outlook

The EU-US trade framework, finalized Wednesday after months of negotiation, removed EU tariffs on American industrial goods in exchange for a 15% ceiling on bloc exports, critically averting the threatened increase to 25% on European automobiles. The political relief was genuine, though the economic arithmetic is sobering: EU shipments to the US fell 37% year-on-year in March — the sharpest decline in records dating to 2003 — and the new arrangement formalizes lower market access than the pre-tariff baseline. European industry simultaneously absorbs the energy shock from the Iran conflict, with Ryanair's Michael O'Leary warning of potential corporate casualties among European airlines if Hormuz disruptions persist. The combination of the trade framework, elevated energy costs, and the weakest composite PMI in two and a half years frames a challenging environment for European exporters through the second half.

ECB, Bond Markets, and the Inflation Dilemma

The ECB's policy path has grown meaningfully more complicated. Belgian Governor Wunsch indicated a June hike is likely; Emmanuel Moulin, confirmed as Banque de France governor by French lawmakers, reinforced that the ECB must be "ready to act"; and at least one Governing Council member kept the door open to further tightening thereafter. ECB President Lagarde maintained that medium-term inflation expectations remain anchored at target, though the tension between that message and bond-market positioning is increasingly visible. G7 finance ministers convening in Paris on Monday confronted a global sovereign selloff — led by Japan but extending across major curves — against a backdrop French Finance Minister Lescure characterized as "correcting, but not collapsing." The European Commission projected the fastest euro-area inflation since 2023, and Rapidan Energy warned that a Hormuz disruption persisting through August could push Brent toward $130, with macroeconomic consequences comparable in scale to 2008. Germany's gas storage sat below 30% at week's end, with the inverted forward curve discouraging injections and traders expecting Berlin may need to intervene.

UK Politics and Markets

UK political dynamics generated sustained market attention throughout the week. Wes Streeting's first public appearance since resigning as health secretary laid groundwork for a Labour leadership challenge, while Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham committed to contesting a June by-election as a route to challenging Keir Starmer — and notably pledged to preserve existing fiscal rules, a signal aimed at reassuring gilt markets wary of a leftward fiscal shift. Hedge funds and asset managers piled into bearish sterling option bets, reflecting concern about policy uncertainty under a potential Burnham-led Labour. Reform UK's Richard Tice separately warned investors against participating in the AR7 offshore wind auction, pledging to unwind Labour renewables contracts — a policy risk that warrants reassessment given Reform's recent local election performance, though the legal basis for retroactive intervention remains untested. Against this political backdrop, UK M&A activity has surged, with bids for UK companies up more than 250% year-to-date as overseas acquirers exploit sterling weakness.

Publicis rose 2.4% in Paris after agreeing to acquire LiveRamp for approximately $2.5 billion. Marks & Spencer guided full-year profit above pre-cyberattack levels, gaining 4.7% in London on Thursday. Shell faced Dutch supreme court arguments from climate activists seeking court-mandated emissions cuts, while Germany's climate advisory council warned the country is likely to miss its 2030 emissions targets by up to 100 million tons of CO2. ---

Global

  • Brent crude traded in a wide range between roughly $105 and $111 across the week as Iran-US diplomacy generated conflicting signals — with Iran's Foreign Ministry confirming it was reviewing a US counter-proposal by Thursday, but Supreme Leader comments insisting Tehran retain its uranium stockpile underscoring the gap still to bridge.
  • Global sovereign bond yields reached their highest levels since approximately 2007–2008, with US 30-year yields hitting 5.19% on Tuesday, Japanese 30-year yields approaching 3%, and Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields touching its highest since July 2008.

  • Gulf sovereign wealth funds are turning materially inward — with Saudi Arabia's PIF cutting its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from ~50% — contracting the petrodollar recycling mechanism that has historically supported global asset prices and US Treasury demand.

The Hormuz Closure: Physical and Financial Channels

Nearly three months into the US-Iran conflict, the largely shuttered Strait of Hormuz has become the dominant variable shaping the global macro picture. The physical disruption is severe: Goldman Sachs estimates inventories are drawing down at a record 8.7 million barrels per day, drones have reportedly destroyed close to $1 billion in US MQ-9 Reaper equipment, and Japan's crude import volumes collapsed 64% in the period reflected in this week's trade data. Diplomatic signals were mixed across the week: President Trump called off a planned strike Monday after Gulf allies requested more time, Iran confirmed it was reviewing the US counter-proposal Thursday, but Supreme Leader comments insisting on uranium retention and a dispute over a proposed Iran-Oman toll regime in the strait complicated the picture. Trump reiterated strike threats Friday if talks collapse, and Republican Senate members are reportedly pushing back on further escalation. Rapidan Energy warns that a Hormuz disruption persisting through August could push Brent toward $130, delivering a macroeconomic shock comparable in scale to 2008. Bloomberg Intelligence's survey showed market participants converging on oil capped near $100 over the next year — implying demand destruction rather than supply restoration does the rebalancing work. Workarounds are advancing but insufficient: the UAE is accelerating bypass pipeline capacity to Fujairah, Singapore's state LNG buyer has secured non-Middle East cargoes through year-end, and India is preparing tankers to transit the strait. NATO is reportedly discussing vessel escort options if the chokepoint remains closed by early July.

A Synchronized Monetary Tightening

The energy shock is producing a globally synchronized shift toward hawkish monetary policy at a moment when fiscal positions across major economies are already stretched. TS Lombard's Dario Perkins characterized the environment as potentially heralding "rate hikes everywhere," with 2% inflation targets increasingly functioning as a floor rather than a ceiling. The FOMC minutes confirmed the Fed's internal pivot: "almost all" participants expect persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" favored dropping the easing bias. Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike to 5.25% to defend the rupiah. Iceland tightened. The ECB is signaling a June hike. The RBA has already delivered three hikes this year. The UK represents the notable exception: CPI fell to a one-year low, prompting traders to pare Bank of England hike expectations — a divergence that partly reflects the UK's own demand shock from fiscal tightening and retail spending weakness. Kevin Warsh's arrival at the Fed on Friday adds a further variable: any perception of dovish drift risks accelerating the bond selloff rather than easing financial conditions. HSBC argues a 2026 Fed hike "can't be ruled out," a formulation that would have seemed implausible six months ago. Pimco's Dan Ivascyn cautioned that prior energy shocks have historically culminated in recession — a tension that becomes harder to ignore as tightening bias collides with demand destruction risk.

Gulf Capital Retrenchment

The Hormuz closure is reshaping not only energy markets but the global capital cycle that has depended on recycled petrodollars. Saudi Arabia's first-quarter war-related costs amounted to roughly $17 billion, or 1.5% of GDP. Qatar faces potential losses of $20 billion from damaged gas infrastructure that could take five years to restore. The UAE is redirecting capital toward bypass infrastructure. The PIF has cut its targeted foreign asset allocation from ~50% to 18%, withdrawn from LIV Golf, and abandoned a $200 million Metropolitan Opera commitment. For a global bond market already absorbing elevated fiscal supply, AI-related capex, and aging-demographic headwinds, a structurally smaller Gulf bid at the margin is directionally significant even if the near-term magnitude remains modest. Sovereign borrowers historically reliant on Gulf bilateral support — Pakistan, Egypt, Jordan — face a more constrained environment; and pledges to the Trump administration worth trillions in investment commitments are increasingly in question.

The Putin-Xi Axis and Rare Earths

Vladimir Putin's Beijing visit this week highlighted the Sino-Russian relationship's asymmetric structure: Russia arrived weakened by a record drone strike on Moscow and almost entirely dependent on Chinese demand, while Xi had just concluded a successful summit with Trump. Power of Siberia 2 pricing progress remained unconfirmed. More durably, China's dominance over rare earth separation and magnet production — underpinning roughly $1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity — is likely to persist well into the next decade despite approximately $10 billion in global public funding for alternatives this year. The asymmetry will continue to shape US-China engagement ahead of the September trade truce expiry, with Trump's July 24 tariff replacement deadline representing the first significant test of the post-summit détente's durability.
