Things Change

The surface doesn’t tell the whole story. US markets returned from the long 
weekend for a non-event of a day. The S&P 500 gained 0.1% and the 10-year 
Treasury yield rose one basis point. That overall picture of strength — the S&P 
is 2% off its all-time high, while the rest of the world is only 0.5% below a 
record — coincides with remarkably benign financial conditions.

The surface is important and can’t be ignored. Just below, however, the 
tectonic plates are shifting in a dramatic way. The main driver is of course
artificial intelligence <https://bloom.bg/4aCOvbg>, and the massive sums being 
spent on it. After a generation in which investors habitually complained that 
companies had no means to grow and were underinvesting, the latest survey of 
global fund managers by Bank of America Corp. shows that suddenly most money 
managers are now worried about overinvestment:

Investors are acting on this perception in a number of ways. One of the most 
eye-catching, aided by growing anecdotal evidence that the business models of 
existing software companies could be fatally damaged by the multiplying 
applications of AI, has seen chipmakers suddenly and massively outperform. 
Usually, soft- and hardware move roughly together; now the fear is that huge 
increases in computing capacity will require ever more purchases from 
chipmakers andobliterate software codewriters <https://bloom.bg/4ayfULi>:

The sea change in spending combines with truly exceptional macro conditions. 
The economy seems to be in a good place, and capex will juice it further. Yet 
expectations are firmly for both fiscal and monetary easing.

These factors have driven a dramatic shift in opinion on whether the global 
economy will suffer a “hard landing” (or recession) or overheat into a “no 
landing” scenario, which would ultimately lead to inflation and drastic 
measures to bring it to a halt. For the last few years, policymakers have 
helped execute an exemplary “soft landing” after the post-pandemic inflation 
spike. Last year’s Liberation Day tariffs briefly drove fears of coming down 
hard. And now, for the first time, more than half of the fund managers in the 
survey believe in a no landing:

This is happening as the full effects of the fiscal loosening from last year’s 
One Big Beautiful Bill begin to make themselves felt with Americans receiving 
tax refunds. And despite this, two more rate cuts from the Federal Reserve are 
priced as a certainty, with 50/50 odds of a third within a year. One clear sign 
that the market is reading the situation that way, and bracing for an eventual 
slamming of the brakes (but not yet), is from BofA’s question on bond yields. 
Money managers expect them to rise:

The last time investors were this certain of higher yields ahead, short-term 
rates were still at zero. This is different.

Ways to hedge against a screeching halt next year, while taking advantage of 
continuing stock market growth, includerotating <https://bloom.bg/4rmdTc9> into 
non-US stocks (also aided by a falling dollar), into value (the leading AI 
over-investors are all emphatically growth stocks), out of the Magnificent 
Seven tech platforms, and into bonds rather than stocks. All of these things 
are happening:

In all cases, a rally after the Liberation Day tariffs ran out about three 
months ago. At that point, nerves began to set in that the AI buildout was 
going too far. Markets have been driven ever since by sensible attempts to take 
evasive action to guard against this. In BofA’s survey, it also shows up in the 
biggest-ever overweight for the euro against the dollar, and the largest 
overweights for emerging markets since 2021.

Bespoke Investment gauges the intensity of the rotation among individual 
stocks by tracking those overbought (more than one standard deviation above 
their 50-day moving average) and oversold (the inverse). Normally, these move 
in opposite directions — more overbought stocks means fewer oversold. Not now:

This is very rare. Since Bespoke started measuring in 2007, last Friday was 
only the 13th day when the proportion of both oversold and undersold stocks had 
increased by 10 percentage points over a month.

For the longer term, the critical question is whether the record capital 
expenditures can succeed in generating a healthy return on investment. If they 
do, and the market’s continuing overall strength makes clear that most 
investors think this will happen, then more robust gains are ahead. If they 
don’t, then the rotation of the last three months will at least mitigate the 
damage.

Emerald <https://bloom.bg/4aESSCs> Blues 

Of all the steps a country could take to reassure investors, firing heads of 
financial regulators after a sudden market plunge should rank low on the list. 
That’s not to suggest such officials are above reproach. But in a democracy 
where the basic rules of engagement are intended to check unbridled executive 
power, investors were entitled to react to the sudden departures of the heads 
of the Financial Services Authority and the Indonesian Stock Exchange with a 
two-day market selloff.

Markets have long treated erratic executive actions with suspicion. They’re a 
warning sign that institutional safeguards may be weakening and that the 
protections behind their investments could prove unreliable. Performance 
suffered:

Indonesia’s market at one point shed almost $80 billion in market cap and is 
now mostly trading sideways well below its peak. Long before theofficials’ 
departure <https://bloom.bg/4tGAEcD>, investors were getting jittery about the 
country’s direction under President Prabowo Subianto.

While his predecessor Joko Widodo maintained Indonesia as an EM darling 
economy, <https://bloom.bg/4rrt2sW> early signals from Prabowo are proving far 
less reassuring. Investors have at no point given the one-time general the 
benefit of any doubt, with the nation conspicuously missing out on the EM rally 
ever since he was elected two years ago. This is the MSCI Indonesia relative 
performance to the broader emerging markets benchmark:

The pernicious slide is disturbing. Ordinarily, this should prod Prabowo into 
action. Instead, he’s kept faith with his nationalist tendencies, especially 
calls for greater state control over natural resources, further unsettling 
investors. This plays into the concerns MSCI raised earlier about Indonesia’s 
uninvestability <https://bloom.bg/4tMZrvM>, when it cautioned that its EM 
classification could be reconsidered and lowered to frontier. Such a downgrade 
from the index provider, which still exerts immense influence over the entire 
field of emerging markets investing, is potentially disastrous for Indonesian 
companies in need of capital. Of the other major index providers,FTSE Russell 
<https://bloom.bg/4tIyIQS> has followed MSCI’s lead, while  S&P Global 
<https://bloom.bg/3Mv4gZM> says that it will continue with its annual 
rebalancing on unchanged terms.

Such doubts cut against Indonesia’s ambitious goal of joining the world’s five 
largest economies by 2045. Authoritieshave outlined a roadmap to address MSCI’s 
concerns <https://bloom.bg/3MBBwyv>. That may pacify the index providers in the 
interim, but political risk still hangs over the market. Investor jitters are 
showing up in the currency:

From performing at par with a basket of emerging-market currencies a year ago, 
the rupiah has fallen behind the benchmark, with the divergence widening 
recently. This weakness plunged the currency to levels not seen in almost three 
decades and prevents the central bank from cutting interest rates despite 
political pressure and a benign inflation outlook.

Are investors overreacting, or do Prabowo’s supposed reforms provide a genuine 
cause for alarm? Gavekal Research’s Udith Sikand and Tom Miller note that the 
ouster of the respected Sri Mulyani Indrawati asfinance minister last year 
<https://bloom.bg/4aUBk6K> was the most high-profile of a series of personnel 
changes where loyalty to Prabowo’s policy goals seemed to trump technical 
competence. Add the recent appointment of his nephew as deputy governor of Bank 
Indonesia, and worries about institutional independence seem amply justified. 
To quote Gavekal:

Foreign investors worry that mismanagement and cronyism will continue to crimp 
the economy, despite its enormous potential. Since taking office in October 
2024, President Prabowo Subianto has presided over a degradation of 
institutions and a steady erosion of economic fundamentals.
Prabowo at Davos in January. Photographer: Mandel Ngan/Getty Images 
Earlier this month, Moody’s Ratings cut Indonesia’s credit outlook to 
negative, citing a deteriorating fiscal position, weakening foreign reserves, 
and indebtedness of state-owned firms. Officials can point to faster economic 
growth, which rose to 5.39% in the last quarter. But that was spurred by 
government stimulus, and Jonathan Anderson of Emerging Advisors Group questions 
the data:

The official GDP data are, ahem, not very reliable as a rule. For the past 20 
years reported headline real growth has been a straight line, rarely deviating 
from 5% y/y… whereas our real activity proxy is far more volatile, ranging from 
double-digit expansion (when implied growth often shot up to the mid-teens) to 
outright contraction (as in 2008-09 during the Global Financial Crisis or again 
in 2014-15, when the economy was suffering from collapsing commodity prices and 
capital outflows pressures).

Unlike his predecessor, Prabowo is not prioritizing infrastructure. Instead, 
he’s freezing new mining licenses and deprioritizing the policy of 
“downstreaming” from metals mining into battery production.
Free school lunches have a price. Photographer: Aditya Aji/AFP 
As Sikand and Miller point out, Prabowo’s signature program of free school 
lunches for 80 million children is neither sustainable nor growth-oriented. 
(The program aims to address widespread stunting <https://bloom.bg/4ays7j3> 
from child malnutrition, especially in poorer parts of the archipelago.) 
Costing an estimated $28 billion a year, it also chips away at precious fiscal 
space in a country with a narrow tax base. Once credibility is questioned, no 
administration can restore it overnight. It’s growing hard to see how anything 
short of a clear ideological shift will repair Prabowo’s standing in the eyes 
of investors.

 —Richard Abbey
