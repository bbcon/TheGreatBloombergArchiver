**Eurozone Macro and ECB**
The week's most significant European data point was the April flash PMI reading showing the first eurozone contraction since late 2024, driven by a steep decline in services activity. The figure crystallizes the ECB's dilemma: war-driven energy inflation is pushing headline consumer prices higher — UK CPI accelerated to 3.3% in March on a 9% monthly surge in motor fuel costs, the largest since Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine — while the growth picture is deteriorating simultaneously. ECB President Lagarde described this as a "double uncertainty," effectively arguing for a data-dependent approach that delays further action. Analysts at TS Lombard have raised the risk of a "Trichet 2.0" scenario, in which the ECB tightens into a weakening economy, though the base case remains a hold through June. Germany's Bundesbank estimated slight first-quarter expansion, but the ZEW collapse to -17.2 in April signals that forward-looking confidence has deteriorated sharply and rapidly, leaving the eurozone's industrial core increasingly exposed to a sustained energy shock.

**UK Politics and Fiscal Outlook**
Keir Starmer's political position eroded incrementally but persistently across the week. The central controversy — whether Downing Street was aware of Peter Mandelson's vetting failure and pressured the Foreign Office to proceed with his appointment as US ambassador anyway — gained evidentiary weight when former senior civil servant Olly Robbins testified on Tuesday that Downing Street was "dismissive" of concerns raised during the process. The episode has triggered what amounts to an open revolt among senior civil servants, a distracting and damaging conflict for a government that entered office promising institutional restoration. London Mayor Sadiq Khan offered qualified support, framing political stability as a UK competitive asset, but the calls for Starmer to resign ahead of next month's local elections signal the controversy is far from resolved. UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves received a near-term fiscal reprieve as government borrowing fell to its lowest in three years, though analysts caution that the Iran war's inflationary pass-through may erode that buffer materially.

**German Politics and Corporate News**
Chancellor Friedrich Merz faces a deeply negative approval environment, with 80% of respondents expressing disapproval in a recent poll. His measures to cushion energy price impacts — including a €1,000 worker payment intended to be voluntarily funded by employers, in which the federal government itself declined to participate — were widely criticized as inadequate. The AfD continues to gain political oxygen from coalition infighting ahead of September's eastern German state elections. On the corporate front, UniCredit CEO Andrea Orcel pressed his case for acquiring Commerzbank, arguing deep structural change is required at the German lender, even as their most recent talks failed to produce agreement. SAP reported strong cloud revenue growth, gaining 7.3% in Frankfurt, while Siemens Energy rose 4.9% after raising its annual outlook on grid and gas turbine demand.

**Aviation and Energy Stress**
Europe's aviation sector faces what may be the most acute near-term commercial challenge on the continent. Persian Gulf refineries supplying roughly 20% of EU jet fuel are effectively cut off, Asian refineries face their own crude shortfalls, and India's Reliance has prioritized domestic LPG output over jet fuel exports. Shell's Pernis refinery is operating at maximum throughput and Nigeria's Dangote refinery has raised European shipments to record levels, but these measures appear insufficient to meet summer demand. Europe's ban on Russian LNG imports took effect Saturday — poorly timed given current energy market stress — and the EU is preparing jet fuel distribution optimization measures among member states. The structural implication is that Europe is entering the summer travel season with a genuine supply constraint that pricing alone cannot resolve quickly.
