Daily Brief

Thursday, June 18, 2026

US

  • The FOMC held the fed funds rate unchanged at 3.50–3.75%, as expected, but new Chair Kevin Warsh delivered a decisively hawkish debut: half of the 18 submitting Fed members project at least one rate hike by year-end, with the average projected end-2026 rate rising from 3.24% to 3.83%.
  • Two-year Treasury yields surged 13 basis points — the largest single-day move in over a year — to 4.18%, while fed funds futures shifted sharply toward pricing a hike as soon as September.
  • Intel shares surged approximately 9% in premarket trading after Trump indicated the chipmaker would work with Apple to produce semiconductors domestically; Apple also disclosed plans to raise product prices to offset rising memory and storage chip costs.

Monetary Policy

Kevin Warsh's inaugural FOMC press conference reinforced the Fed's inflation-fighting credibility rather than signaling accommodation. The committee's statement was cut from 341 to 130 words, stripped of forward guidance and any reference to the maximum employment mandate, with the sole concluding line being "The Committee will deliver price stability." Warsh declined to submit his own dot plot — an unusual move suggesting he intends to de-emphasize that communication tool — but the projections of his 18 colleagues moved meaningfully hawkish, with half anticipating rate increases before year-end. Goldman Sachs vice chairman and former Dallas Fed president Rob Kaplan said the Fed may need to act as soon as September if inflation remains elevated. Warsh also announced five task forces covering communications, the balance sheet, alternative data, AI and productivity, and the inflation framework, signaling structural regime change ahead — though outcomes likely won't be known until year-end.

Markets

The hawkish Fed signal produced a sharp tightening of financial conditions. The 10-year TIPS yield rose to its highest level since May 2025, and the dollar recorded its strongest single-day gain of the year, an outcome that complicates the administration's stated preference for a weaker currency. Equity markets initially slumped on the rate-hike repricing but S&P 500 futures recovered roughly 0.9% the following morning, supported by the US-Iran interim peace deal and the associated decline in oil prices. Credit spreads, which remain near historic lows, face increasing scrutiny as the prospect of further monetary tightening prompts investors to reassess rich valuations. ---

Asia

  • China May retail sales fell 0.6% year-over-year, well below already-subdued consensus forecasts, marking the first contraction in consumer spending since the pandemic and raising fresh doubts about Beijing's ability to achieve its domestic demand targets.
  • Bank Indonesia raised its benchmark BI-Rate 25 basis points to 5.75% — its third hike in approximately one month — while the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas raised its reverse repurchase rate 25 basis points to 4.75%; Taiwan's central bank held for a ninth consecutive quarter.
  • The MSCI China index fell 1.5% and is now approximately 20% below its October peak, approaching bear market territory, as weak consumer data and disappointing earnings from Alibaba and Tencent weigh on sentiment.

China: Consumer Deterioration

The May retail sales print of -0.6% year-over-year laid bare the depth of China's consumption challenge. Sub-components were more alarming: home appliance and automobile purchases each declined by double digits, reflecting the exhaustion of government trade-in subsidies that were reduced to 250 billion yuan in 2026 from 300 billion yuan in 2025. Even catering expenditure — historically more resilient — rose at its slowest pace since late 2022. Bloomberg Economics' Gavekal Dragonomics economist Wei He cautioned that "weak labor-market conditions will continue to weigh on household income growth, and the slow-burn property price decline will sap both the balance sheets and confidence of anxious consumers." Separately, global holdings of Chinese interbank bonds rose $13.3 billion in May — the first monthly increase in over a year — suggesting Chinese fixed income retained safe-haven appeal amid the Iran war's energy shock.

Southeast Asia and Regional Policy

Indonesia's three consecutive rate hikes reflect the urgency with which Bank Indonesia is defending the rupiah amid global risk-off sentiment and dollar strength amplified by the hawkish Fed pivot. India's monsoon deficit — running approximately 40% below normal as of mid-June according to the India Meteorological Department — introduces an additional risk to agricultural output and rural incomes in the coming months. An impending wave of roughly 62 million barrels of crude oil trapped in the Persian Gulf is set to reach Asian markets following the Hormuz reopening, likely producing near-term disinflationary pressure for energy-importing economies across the region. ---

Europe

  • UK core CPI came in below consensus, easing pressure on the Bank of England ahead of its June meeting; the MPC voted to hold Bank Rate unchanged in a split decision, with market pricing now implying only one additional hike this year versus three previously.
  • The Bank of England decision follows the ECB in holding rates, while Norway and Switzerland also kept borrowing costs unchanged; Czech policymakers were weighing a potential first rate hike since 2022.
  • UK political uncertainty has risen as the Makerfield by-election — held June 18 — is seen as a likely pathway for Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham to challenge Prime Minister Keir Starmer; prediction markets assign Burnham a 70% probability of becoming prime minister by year-end.

UK Inflation and Monetary Policy

UK core CPI printed below expectations, with the measure now running below its US equivalent despite the UK's heavier exposure to energy import costs — a somewhat counterintuitive outcome reflecting the partial easing of energy-related price pressures as Hormuz reopens. The softer data, combined with declining oil prices, shifted the MPC's calculus: only one dissenter (Chief Economist Huw Pill) had voted for a hike at the prior meeting, and the additional support for tightening that had been anticipated did not materialise. Market pricing compressed from three projected hikes to one. Notably, UK rate expectations have been tracking oil prices with unusual fidelity, suggesting the path for monetary policy remains heavily contingent on the durability of the Hormuz-driven energy price decline.

UK Political Outlook

A Burnham victory in Makerfield — the most probable outcome per available polling and prediction markets — would likely precipitate a Labour leadership contest, introducing a period of policy uncertainty. Burnham's stated skepticism of bond market discipline and interest in utility nationalisation have attracted scrutiny, though gilt markets appear to have partially priced political risk, with UK sovereign yields already trading above US Treasuries. The disciplining precedent of the 2022 Truss episode is likely to constrain any incoming government's fiscal ambitions, limiting the practical consequences of a leadership transition. ---

LatAm

  • Brazil's central bank cut its benchmark rate at its June meeting, moving in the opposite direction from most emerging market peers amid a complex domestic inflation and fiscal backdrop.

Brazil Monetary Policy

Brazil's Banco Central do Brasil delivered a rate cut at its June meeting, diverging from the tightening bias adopted by several regional and emerging market peers including Indonesia and the Philippines. The cut reflects the BCB's assessment that domestic disinflation has progressed sufficiently to warrant easing, though the move occurs against a backdrop of still-elevated global risk-free rates following the Fed's hawkish signal and a stronger US dollar — both of which represent potential headwinds for Brazilian asset markets and the real. The durability of the easing cycle will depend in part on how quickly oil price relief from the Hormuz reopening passes through to domestic fuel and food costs, and whether the external environment stabilises. ---

Global

  • President Trump signed an interim memorandum of understanding to end the US-Iran war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz; oil and gas vessels have begun transiting the strait, WTI crude fell approximately 3% to around $74 per barrel, and US gasoline prices dipped below $4 per gallon for the first time since March.
  • Goldman Sachs estimates Hormuz oil flows may recover to only approximately 70% of pre-war levels in the near term as producers utilise alternative routes, and significant operational questions — including mine clearance, traffic management, and post-60-day arrangements — remain unresolved.
  • The G7 pledged to diversify supply chains away from Chinese rare earths and critical materials, adding momentum to the EU's parallel effort to reduce its trade imbalance with Beijing.

Hormuz Reopening: Relief With Caveats

The signing of the interim accord represents a material de-escalation of the most significant energy supply disruption since the 1970s. Approximately 31 supertankers carrying roughly 62 million barrels of crude were positioned inside the Persian Gulf awaiting transit, and at least 12 million barrels appeared to be moving through the strait on June 18 per Bloomberg ship-tracking data. However, the main internationally recognised traffic separation scheme remains uncleared of mines, forcing vessels to hug Iranian or Omani coastlines. Trade groups including Lloyd's Market Association and Intertanko have flagged unresolved questions around mine clearance, traffic management authority, and the legal framework governing the waterway after the 60-day MOU expires — Tehran has indicated intent to retain some traffic management role. Goldman Sachs projects near-term flows recovering to approximately 70% of pre-war levels, implying a slower-than-expected normalisation of energy markets.

Nuclear Diplomacy and Geopolitical Risk

The interim accord opens a 60-day negotiating window on Iran's nuclear programme, the central unresolved issue. Iran was operating more than 20,000 centrifuges when the conflict began — far in excess of the 5,060 permitted under the 2015 JCPOA and orders of magnitude above the symbolic levels discussed in 2005. The number of centrifuges ultimately permitted under any final agreement will be the clearest indicator of which negotiating camp — maximalist or pragmatist — has prevailed. Meanwhile, a separate geopolitical flashpoint intensified as Ukraine conducted a record drone strike on the Moscow oil refinery overnight, underscoring that European energy supply risks have not abated even as Middle Eastern ones recede.
