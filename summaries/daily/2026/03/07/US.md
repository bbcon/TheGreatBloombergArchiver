US electric vehicle market dynamics present an apparent contradiction: new EV sales fell nearly 40% year-over-year in Q4 2025, yet public charging infrastructure continues to expand and utilization is rising. ChargePoint Holdings posted a 7% increase in quarterly revenue despite the sales decline, while EVgo plans to add up to 1,650 new charging slots in 2026, a 38% increase over last year. The resolution of this paradox lies in the cumulative installed base — approximately 5.8 million EVs now operate on American roads — which sustains charging demand even as new purchase rates soften. Ultra-fast charging capacity expanded roughly 48% in 2024, with nearly one in four new chargers installed in Q4 capable of delivering 250 kilowatts or more. Analysts at Needham characterize the weak new-car sales trend as an overhang, and shares of both ChargePoint and EVgo remain near historical lows despite the constructive operating data, reflecting investor uncertainty about the medium-term trajectory.

Two factors may yet shift the demand calculus. Elevated oil prices driven by the US-Iran conflict are providing an indirect subsidy to the economics of electric and hybrid ownership, and a cohort of new affordable electric SUVs entering the market could reignite consumer interest. Hybrid vehicle sales have risen since federal EV incentives expired, with S&P Global Mobility noting continued demand momentum in that segment. Charging infrastructure operators appear to be building for a 2035 demand horizon rather than optimizing for current conditions, suggesting capital deployment will continue even if the pace moderates near term.

On the broader industrial policy front, the Trump administration's push to secure critical minerals — copper and cobalt in particular — through direct investment in the Democratic Republic of Congo adds a supply-side dimension to the US energy transition narrative, though execution timelines remain uncertain.
