By Eric Roston 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQdgGkZhizQ/eric-roston?cmpid=BBD070626_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6231>

Much of the US just sweltered through the July 4 holiday weekend as an intense 
heat dome bore down, putting strain on power grids and prompting the 
cancellation of many events. In Europe, punishing temperatures are set to 
return days after a deadly heat wave pushed thermometers as high as 43.8C 
(111F) in France.

A troubling pattern has emerged in this summerтАЩs heat: Not only has it broken 
records, itтАЩs done so often by margins far above the previous all-time highs.

These heat jumps are part of a larger shift. Sea levels are rising and polar 
ice is melting faster than before, while new records for daily rainfall are 
being set at a rapid clip. The pace of global warming itself has quickened in 
recent years.

TodayтАЩs climate can тАЬseem like an unexpected step changeтАЭ from that of a few 
years ago, said atmospheric scientist Katharine Hayhoe of Texas Tech University.

El Ni├▒o weather systems exacerbate the heating brought about by greenhouse gas 
pollution. Some models suggest the current El Ni├▒o could push warming briefly 
as high as 2C. And тАЬit is possible for a single month to surpass this 
threshold,тАЭ said Sarah Kapnick, global head of climate advisory at JPMorgan 
Chase & Co. The world is currently at 1.4C above pre-industrial levels.

There were four times as many new heat records set from 2016 to 2024 as there 
would have been without climate change. Unprecedented rainfall occurs 40% more 
often than it otherwise might, and record dryness, 10% more. Meanwhile, record 
cold is on an extinction pathway.

тАЬExtreme events are so far outside anything we have expected,тАЭ said Friederike 
Otto, an Imperial College London climate scientist and co-founder of World 
Weather Attribution. тАЬItтАЩs not so important whether itтАЩs what we expected as 
experts. ItтАЩs whether itтАЩs what we expect as people on the ground.тАЭ

No one experiences heat or rain as a global average. Insurers, bankers, 
emergency responders, infrastructure managers, homeowners, beachgoers тАФ all 
need to know which plausibly bad or worst-case scenarios to plan for, where. 
And theyтАЩre not the same ones they were even a decade ago.

Meanwhile, melted ice accounts for more than half of sea level rise, and the 
rate of melting more than doubled from the period 1976-1995 to 2006-2025. And

The clean energy transition, and the rapidly falling cost of renewables and
