Good News in the Iranian Price

Markets react to things before they happen. That’s their job. So it’s not that 
surprising that they’re already pricing in an open Strait of Hormuz and a 
cessation of hostilities in Iran, even though neither has happened yet.

It’s a little surprising, however, that they’re so confident in this view when 
 hostilities plainly continue <https://bloom.bg/4gcfDCk>, the US and Iran don’t 
agree about what they’ve signed in their Memorandum of Understanding 
<https://bloom.bg/4amO99l>, and traffic through the Strait remains far below 
usual. Oil prices suggest we’re back to normal, despite grave doubts on 
prediction markets that shipping will get going in earnest any time soon:

Financial markets’ reaction begins to look overdone. A fall in oil prices 
should very directly reduce inflationary pressure, but one-yearinflation swaps 
<https://bloom.bg/3Ras3jQ> are now as low as they’ve been since the pandemic. 
Investors (just like central bankers!) missed the last big surge in prices, and 
it’s striking that the swaps market is showing such confidence again:

That suggests that the considerable reduction in crude prices will feed 
through swiftly to prices at the pump. Yet US gasoline prices are still 20% 
higher than they were a year ago, so there’s a way for this to go:

Meanwhile, the latest evidence on US inflation isn’t wholly encouraging (even 
if the conflict’s impact onEuropean prices <https://bloom.bg/4aLarlt> appears 
to have been less severe than expected). Last week brought the Personal 
Consumption Expenditure inflation numbers for May. They appear with more of a 
lag than the consumer price index data, so tend not to attract so much 
attention, but the Federal Reserve officially targets them.

Core inflation as measured by the PCE rose again and is now well above the 3% 
target. Outside of the big post-pandemic surge, this is the most menacing rise 
in 20 years, and it predates the conflict in Iran:

If there is reason for hope it comes the trimmed mean measure, which excludes 
outliers in both directions and continues to fall closer to the 2% target. 
Kevin Warsh, the Fed’s new chairman, has said that he follows this measure, and 
it supports the notion that this inflationary episode has been driven by 
energy-exposed outliers. That would back the exceptionally optimistic view 
coming from swaps.

There are certainly some big inflation optimists out there. Jay Hatfield of 
Infrastructure Capital Management suggests:

Based on the 30% drop in oil prices and the 18% drop in gasoline prices over 
the last month, we are forecasting that CPI will be negative .18% for June with 
core at only positive .24%... These negative prints make the chance of Fed rate 
hike this year near zero.

He also predicts that core PCE will drop below 2.5% over the next year thanks 
to falling airline fares, declining shelter inflation, and the rolling off of 
tariffs from year-on-year calculations. This would allow three Fed cuts in 12 
months — which, to be clear, is well out of consensus.

Others are less positive. That’s in part because of the overall macroeconomic 
effects. Falling fuel prices act like a tax cut for consumption, sparking more 
expenditures. Theboom in artificial intelligence <https://bloom.bg/4v764sf> 
should also tend to be inflationary. The Fed, for now, seemsready to be hawkish 
<https://bloom.bg/4ezB2Ef>. 

Another issue is that the oil shock has had very different impacts on 
different countries, with some taking a hit to growth rather than prices. 
Variant Perception’s leading indicators for inflation and growth, which now 
include the sharp fall in oil prices, show inflation in all developed markets 
with growth prospects widely dispersed (the UK is at greatest risk of 
stagflation). There is every risk of turbulence as different central banks make 
probably very varied responses: 

Meanwhile, emerging markets seem much more healthily positioned, with Brazil, 
and India and South Africa (both among the few stock markets down for the year) 
all appearing in the “sweet spot” of low inflation and positive growth.

Markets seem confident that the Iranian conflict can now be ignored. The US 
has shown that it’s not prepared to escalate, it’s in Iran’s interest for oil 
exports to resume, and so one way or another the oil interruption shouldn’t 
last too long. But the impact on rates and inflation is still unclear. To quote 
Eric Robertsen of Standard Chartered:

Oil-related volatility may have subsided, but we expect it to be replaced by 
rates and currency-related volatility in H2. Enjoy the reprieve – it is likely 
temporary, in our view.

That sounds right. At present, markets are braced for lower inflation and 
higher rates in the next 12 months. That’s highly unlikely to be correct. 
Expect some excitement as it becomes clearer which view prevails.

Healthy Skepticism

As the first half of the year draws to a close, it’s been dominated by two 
immense shocks — from the war and its effect on oil supply, and the AI boom and 
its impact on demand for semiconductors. Ructions in technology are hard to 
ignore, and sentiment has turned sharply against the hyperscalers who are going 
to build out the infrastructure. TheMagnificent Seven <https://bloom.bg/44wGFx4>
 tech platforms have now done no better than the average stock in the S&P 500 
over the last 12 months:

It’s probably fair to view much of what happens in the overall index as a 
battle between the impact of these two shocks — but that does mean you could 
miss one corner of the market that’s enjoying what looks like a 
once-in-a-lifetime rally: healthcare stocks.

The past six years have seen plenty of rallies of this magnitude, generally 
coinciding with breakthroughs in Covid vaccines andblockbuster weight-loss drugs
 <https://bloom.bg/4v4N51l>. The latest, however, has happened without any 
medical development of note. So it’s possible that investors are rotating out 
of technology and into more defensive sectors.

In theory, medical products and services should be less sensitive to economic 
downturns and likely to outperform in a typical risk-off environment — but 
there is no such thing at present. UBS notes that healthcare has beaten the S&P 
500 on 85% of the days when the benchmark was down 1% or more, suggesting 
downside for the broader market should translate into (relative) gains for 
healthcare. On this basis, this rally as tech sells off highlights the extent 
of investors’ defensiveness:

But the rotation doesn’t quite mean that investors are getting jittery over 
AI. Healthcare is instead becoming a major beneficiary of the new productive 
tool that AI is shaping up to become. UBS’ AJ Rice notes that AI is rapidly 
becoming a core operating layer across the healthcare system, with both payers 
and providers deploying it to try to improve efficiency in admin and in 
clinical operations.

Whether this will help all health operators equally is another thing. US 
hospitals <https://bloom.bg/4xVpjHK> are dominated by nonprofit systems, which 
often adopt new technology more slowly due to their capital constraints and 
complex governance. That creates a multi-year window for early adopters to 
extend their lead. Rice says:

AI-driven productivity gains in hospitals may not be competed away as quickly 
as in managed care, allowing leading operators to compound benefits over time 
and potentially widen the gap compared to rival non-profits.

For now, however, robust spending is probably helping to fuel the sector's 
remarkable rally.The US is expected to spend more than $560 billion 
<https://bloom.bg/4vF7kno> on prescription medications alone in 2026, up 8.2% 
from last year as demand for obesity and diabetes drugs continues to surge.

Taken together, the nation’s total healthcare bill is projected to exceed $6 
trillion in 2026, driven in large part by prescription drug spending, according 
to the Centers for Medicare and Medicaid Services’ latest report:

This isn’t surprising. A 2025 KFF poll <https://bloom.bg/4ePEQ3e> found that 
nearly one in five US adults had taken a GLP-1 drug at some point in their 
lives, with roughly a quarter of them paying out of pocket. Weight-loss drug 
makers — reflected in Bloomberg’s Obesity Aggregate index — have had a tough 
time of late, but their recent rally suggests investors now have hopes for the 
evolution toward more accessible formulations, such as pills:

Ultimately, healthcare is a pragmatic way for investors to try to gain some 
exposure to the likely winners from AI. As a defensive sector, it offers a 
potential win-win. If AI does deliver a big step change in productivity, 
healthcare stands to benefit; if it doesn’t, at least the sector is a good 
place to shelter from the market fallout.

—Richard Abbey




 Survival Tips 



How to survive the World Cup? Based on going with my son to see England play 
Panama at the weekend, I'd say that if you’re in New York, you should 
definitely use the NYNJ Stadium shuttle. It’s only $20 for a round trip in a 
yellow school bus from Manhattan to the stadium in New Jersey. Most of the vast 
car park isn’t available for some reason, so don't even think of driving. 
Getting into a school bus is quite fun — although it’s galling that they can’t 
guarantee to start your journey home until three hours after the game ends. The 
decision to hold the final in the New Jersey marshes is, I can confirm, 
unconscionable.
England fans cheer their team’s 2-0 victory Saturday against Panama. 
Photographer: Jewel Samad/AFP/Getty 
Buy any merch and satisfy your hunger ahead of time, and release your inner 
camel, because you don’t want to get thirsty while there. Everything is even 
more ridiculously expensive than at an NFL game.

All that said, even Gianni Infantino and his minions at FIFA cannot ruin the 
World Cup, despite their strenuous efforts to do so. Everyone there was in the 
right frame of mind to have fun, they played Wonderwall 
<https://bloom.bg/3UR9SOh> and  Three Lions <https://bloom.bg/4eYSJgw> to  keep 
the England fans happy <https://bloom.bg/4uXiOBr>, and I had some great 
conversations with Panamanian fans, who were lovely people. It is the World Cup 
after all, and after so much money you might as well enjoy it. Have a great 
week everyone.
