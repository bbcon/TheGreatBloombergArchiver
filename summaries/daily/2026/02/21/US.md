Federal climate and energy policy developments dominated the domestic agenda on February 21. The Environmental Protection Agency moved to roll back the Biden administration's 2024 strengthening of the Mercury and Air Toxics Standards for power plants, reversing rules requiring reduction of mercury, arsenic, and lead emissions from coal-fired facilities. EPA Administrator Lee Zeldin framed the action as relieving undue burden on the coal sector, consistent with the administration's broader posture of loosening environmental constraints in pursuit of baseload power expansion. The regulatory reversal is the latest in a sequence of actions benefiting coal producers, though the sector's structural economics — including cheap natural gas and declining capital investment — remain a headwind regardless of the regulatory environment.

On the energy infrastructure front, a proposed 9.2-gigawatt gas-fired power plant in Ohio, envisaged as a SoftBank-led development with a reported cost of approximately $33 billion, continued to attract scrutiny for its potential emissions footprint. Analysts at BloombergNEF estimated the facility could emit roughly 19.4 million metric tons of CO₂ annually assuming a 65% capacity factor with combined-cycle gas turbine technology, which would make it among the largest single point sources of carbon emissions in the US electricity sector. The project, backed by the Trump administration's Commerce Department, highlights the tension between the administration's energy security objectives and its stated ambition to grow the economy while protecting public health — goals that, in this instance, pull in opposite directions.

The US-Indonesia trade deal, described as lowering tariffs between the two economies, was reported as complete, adding a modest but symbolically notable win for the administration's bilateral trade agenda in Southeast Asia. Separately, reports indicated the administration is planning a revamp of the Peace Corps with an AI-competitiveness dimension, framing the initiative as a tool to extend US soft power and counter Chinese influence in developing markets.
