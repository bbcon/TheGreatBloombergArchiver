**Bank of Japan: Hawkish Hold and Currency Pressure**
The Bank of Japan's April meeting produced a 6-3 vote to hold its benchmark rate at 0.75% — the most divided outcome under Governor Ueda's tenure — with three board members, including the consensus-leaning Junko Nakagawa, voting for an immediate hike. Bloomberg Economics now assigns high probability to a move at the June meeting. Despite the explicitly hawkish signal, the yen remained stubbornly weak near 160 per dollar through much of the week, reflecting investor skepticism about how far normalization can proceed under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, who has appointed two dovish board members and publicly criticized a trade minister for suggesting rate hikes could strengthen the currency. Spring Shunto wage negotiations produced a third consecutive year of approximately 5% wage increases, and five-year inflation breakevens have reached 2.7%, suggesting bond markets believe the deflationary era is genuinely over — yet the currency's weakness persists, creating a tension between fundamentals and market pricing that is likely to require resolution.

Japan intervened in currency markets on Thursday for the first time since 2024, purchasing yen and selling dollars after issuing a "final" warning to investors. The move triggered intraday strengthening of as much as 0.7%, with dollar-yen reaching 155.5 before partially retracing to around 156.5. Analysts cautioned that intervention can stabilize markets only briefly: Japan's structural dependence on imported energy — now made substantially more expensive by the Strait of Hormuz disruption — continues to exert downward pressure on the currency through the current account. Japan's top currency official also indicated readiness to intervene in crude oil futures markets, an unusual extension of currency-management tools into commodity markets.

**South Korea: Record Performance and Concentration Risk**
South Korea's equity market has emerged as the year's strongest performer among major indices, with the Kospi rising approximately 60% and total market capitalization surpassing the UK's at $4.04 trillion. Samsung Electronics reported a 755% year-on-year profit increase in the March quarter, and Samsung and SK Hynix together now represent 42% of Kospi capitalization, up from 28% two years ago. That concentration amplifies both momentum and tail risk. Labor tensions are building: thousands of Samsung employees rallied over the weekend demanding higher wages, with the union threatening an 18-day strike beginning May 21. South Korea's April export data showed continued semiconductor strength, providing some offset to the regional drag from elevated energy costs.

**China: Growth, Governance, and Technology Control**
China's April PMI data presented a bifurcated picture. Manufacturing remained in expansion territory, supported by resilient export demand, while services and construction slipped back into contraction, with building activity registering its worst performance since the early stages of the pandemic. The divergence reflects a familiar structural tension: external demand is sustaining factory output while weak domestic consumption and a deepening property downturn weigh on the broader economy. The Politburo pledged to counter external shocks and enhance energy security, citing better-than-expected first-quarter growth, and Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable.

Beijing's handling of the Meta-Manus transaction represents a significant escalation in China's approach to technology governance. Authorities ordered the two companies to unwind the completed $2 billion acquisition within weeks, barred Manus co-founders from leaving the country, and instructed several private firms via the NDRC to reject US-origin capital unless explicitly approved. The stated rationale centers on preventing strategically important AI capabilities from flowing to the United States, but the broader signal appears directed at domestic entrepreneurs contemplating offshore restructuring to access US capital. The episode risks reinforcing perceptions of elevated political risk in China's technology sector. Separately, China's tax authorities launched a crackdown on circular invoicing in the metals trading sector, rattling copper, aluminum, and silver traders in Shanghai who rely on such invoices for bank financing. The State Taxation Administration framed the investigation as targeting fraudulent activity that has artificially inflated output measures.

**India: Heat, Energy, and Trade**
India faces an unusual convergence of pressures. Every one of the world's 50 hottest cities this week was located in India, with temperatures exceeding 40°C across densely populated states. The McKinsey Global Institute estimates heat-related productivity losses could reach $250 billion, with monsoon relief not expected until June. The Federation of Indian Airlines warned that carriers are "on the verge of closing down" without government relief on jet fuel prices, with Brent crude trading near $111 a barrel. Against this backdrop, the trade policy liberalization underway is a structurally meaningful development. India signed a free trade agreement with New Zealand, following earlier deals with the EU and UK, representing a departure from the protectionist posture that saw average tariff rates rise from approximately 13% to 18% between 2017 and 2020. The pivot appears driven partly by urgency to secure alternative market access as the US turns more protectionist, and partly by an acknowledgment that manufacturing's share of GDP has declined from 16% to 13% over the past decade. The liberalization has practical limits — most-favored-nation tariff rates have not been broadly reduced, agriculture remains shielded, and structural regulatory constraints persist — but the directional shift is meaningful. Sun Pharmaceutical's agreement to acquire Organon for approximately $11.75 billion on an enterprise basis is separately notable as one of the largest outbound acquisitions in Indian corporate history.
