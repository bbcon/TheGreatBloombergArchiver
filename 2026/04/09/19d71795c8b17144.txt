In Washington, the pressure is shifting to allies. The US wants specific 
commitments from European allies on their pledge to help secure the Strait of 
Hormuz after the fighting in Iran stops, andit wants them within days 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/us-asks-allies-to-pitch-plans-within-days-to-secure-hormuz?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, a senior NATO official told Bloomberg News. That push for concrete plans 
marks a shift from rhetoric to execution, as President Donald Trump presses 
partners to move beyond statements and into actual maritime security. The 
urgency comes with familiar friction: Trump is once again questioning NATO’s 
value, even asVice President JD Vance prepares for talks with Tehran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/us-iran-ceasefire-claims-diverge-as-hormuz-stays-blocked?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
. The message is clear: reopening Hormuz isn’t just about ending a blockade, 
it’s about who shows up after.

Out on the water, the standoff is almost literal. A small armada, including two
fully loaded Chinese supertankers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/china-tankers-join-queue-to-test-hormuz-exit-and-us-iran-truce?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, is hovering at the mouth of the strait, poised to test whether “open” means 
open. So far, not quite. Traffic remains minimal, and even the boldest 
operators are waiting for something more convincing than political statements 
and loosely defined “safe routes.” The first successful transit will matter 
less for the barrels it carries than for the signal it sends: whether this 
ceasefire has teeth, or just headlines.

Meanwhile the world’s most important energy chokepoint is technically on the 
verge of reopening, and functionally still shut. A fragile US-Iran ceasefire 
was supposed to bring ships back into the Strait of Hormuz within days. 
Instead, tankers are idling, insurers are hedging, and everyone from Washington 
to Tehran is arguing over what, exactly, was agreed. Oil markets, meanwhile, 
have snapped from panic to whiplash, withprices rebounding toward $100 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-9?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 as traders bet disruption is far from over.—Jui Chakravorty 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AXD7_0-0mSE/jui-chakravorty>

What You Need to Know Today

China is weighing a fresh bailout for its state-run airlines as the Iran war 
drives fuel costs sharply higher,said people familiar <>, with options on the 
table ranging from subsidies and tax breaks to cheap state-backed loans, and 
even mergers. The move underscores how fragile the sector remains, with China’s 
“Big Three” carriers still deep in the red years after Covid. If it goes ahead, 
this would mark the industry’s biggest lifeline since the pandemic, and a 
reminder that for airlines, higher oil prices can quickly turn into an 
existential problem.

TPG is lining up advisers including Maybank and UBS, said people familiar with 
the matter 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/tpg-is-said-to-pick-advisers-for-asia-onehealthcare-sale-or-ipo?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, to weigh a potential sale or IPO of Asia OneHealthcare. Nothing’s locked in 
yet, but this kicks off early-stage plans that could value the Southeast Asian 
hospital group atup to $7.5 billion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/tpg-is-said-to-consider-sale-or-ipo-of-asia-onehealthcare?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
. A listing would likely bring more banks into the mix, but the move signals 
TPG is exploring an exit from a healthcare platform it bought for $1.2 billion 
in 2019. For now, it’s a classic private equity fork in the road: sell, float, 
or hold out for something better.

India’s state elections are doubling as a real-time referendum on Prime 
Minister Narendra Modi’shandling of a war-driven energy crunch 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/india-state-polls-test-modi-s-handling-of-war-driven-shortages?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, with fuel shortages and industrial disruptions cutting through campaign

the Middle East crisis. For Modi, it’s a test of how far a development 
narrative can stretch when everyday essentials start running short.

Meanwhile foreign investors are heading for the exits from Indian equities at 
a record clip 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/foreign-selling-streak-in-indian-stocks-hits-record-on-oil-spike?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, dumping $17.8 billion over 23 straight sessions as the oil shock from the 
Iran war rattles an import-heavy economy. Higher crude prices are fanning 
inflation fears, squeezing margins and dimming the outlook just as any 
ceasefire optimism starts to wobble. Domestic funds are stepping in with steady 
buying, but they’re mostly playing defense, and so far, it’s not enough to 
fully offset the scale of the global selloff.
