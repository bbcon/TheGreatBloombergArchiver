Hi, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai.

As I sit around in my man cave wondering if I should grant OpenClaw access to 
my personal laptop, I am intrigued by the potential for this open-source AI 
platform to do all the boring tasks that worker ants like me have to do.

I’m equally concerned about the security risks and my own job security.

That’s the same conundrum the Chinese government is facing as it considers 
whether to allow OpenClaw, an AI agent that requires unusually broad access to 
private data, to thrive as it did with DeepSeek just a year ago, or squash it 
as itpretty much did with Bitcoin 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-10/crypto-winter-grows-progressively-colder-in-china?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

The stakes are high. China is in a race with the US for AI supremacy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-03-12/the-us-china-race-for-ai-dominance-video?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, and is pinning its hopes for growth on advanced tech and the ability of its 
private companies to lead it into the future, whether with humanoid robots, 
robotaxis or AI agents.

It is incredible how OpenClaw, developed by OpenAI’s Peter Steinberger, has 
garnered such a huge following in China in such a short time — more so than in 
the US, where the tech giants have largely roped it off from joining their 
lucrative ecosystems. Read moreabout the enthusiasm in China here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/openclaw-frenzy-drives-china-s-agentic-ai-adoption-raises-security-concerns?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

Since launching in November last year, there have been well-attended 
gatherings in cities like Beijing, Shanghai and Shenzhen so geeks and office 
workers could meet to share tips on how to improve the open-source model, whose 
symbol is a lobster.

Some of the nation’s most valuable tech and ecommerce firms, like Tencent and 
JD.com, have rolled out OpenClaw apps, while local governments have made 
millions of yuan in subsidies available for companies that develop atop the 
platform.

A big moment occurred this month in Shenzhen, when hundreds of people gathered 
outside Tencent’s headquarters to watch a free demonstration of OpenClaw and 
get help from the firm’s engineers installing the software on laptops. Read an 
opinion pieceabout the lobster craze here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-11/openclaw-ai-agent-china-s-ai-lobster-craze-comes-with-claws?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-03-11/why-is-china-embracing-openclaw-video>
WATCH: Why is China embracing OpenClaw so enthusiastically?
OpenClaw has suddenly become a massive growth opportunity for an economy that 
is expected to slow this year. It is refueling animal spirits in the tech world 
anda stock market rally led by big gains 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/tencent-zhipu-shares-jump-on-launches-of-ai-agents-tapping-into-openclaw?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 for companies like Hong Kong-listed Zhipu and MiniMax, which are developing 
tools off of OpenClaw.

It is easy to see why people in China are intrigued 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-10/openclaw-in-china-has-users-and-tech-companies-buzzing-for-open-source-ai-agents?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
. OpenClaw is a way to connect with the popular trends of the wider world, 
while remaining compatible with domestic social media platforms like WeChat and 
performing tasks as varied as booking travel and drafting emails.

“In China, we are undergoing a cognitive shift from chat-only AI to productive 
AI,” said Chance Peng, a tech entrepreneur in Shanghai. “For Chinese people who 
deal with heavy workloads on a daily basis, OpenClaw offers a much-needed tool 
to ease their workload. For the time being, the value of the application 
outweighs the risks.”

Nevertheless, all of the excitement may go up in smoke, if the government gets 
too worried. In an echo of China’s earliercurbs on iPhones 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-12-15/china-s-apple-iphone-ban-accelerates-across-state-firms-government?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 and Teslas for some individuals and entities due to security concerns, 
authorities have moved to restrict state-owned and government agencies, 
including the largest banks, from running OpenClaw AI apps on office computers. 
Read moreabout those restrictions here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/china-moves-to-limit-use-of-openclaw-ai-at-banks-government-agencies?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 and about the new techtesting the government’s approach to regulating AI here. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/china-s-openclaw-frenzy-tests-xi-s-approach-to-regulate-ai?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>

Besides President Xi Jinping’s push to prevent Chinese data from getting into 
foreign hands due to national security worries, there are other reasons why 
Beijing is suddenly taking a deeper look at OpenClaw. One of them has to do 
with hackers.

One critical flaw, known as ClawJacked, allowed intruders to take control of a 
user’s OpenClaw agent by getting them to visit a malicious website. That defect 
was fixed but researchers found thousands of vulnerabilities in the software. 
Read an explainer that digs intothe pros and cons of OpenClaw here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/what-is-the-openclaw-ai-agent-and-why-is-it-popular-in-china?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.
OpenClaw can book a flight, schedule meetings and deal with an email inbox.
Visual China Group
Ultimately, it is hard to see the Chinese government crushing OpenClaw without 
a backlash from companies trying to profit from it and people keen to use it. 
After all, it took years for the nation’s tech firms to regain their footing 
aftera government crackdown on the industry 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-17/china-investors-are-starting-to-believe-xi-tech-crackdown-is-over?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, one that dented economic growth.

Instead, authorities will probably seek to tame OpenClaw some or support the 
development of a Chinese alternative likethey did with Huawei’s HarmonyOS 
operating system 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-01-28/huawei-harmonyos-next-review-new-phone-seeks-to-break-apple-google-dominance?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 over foreign alternatives. That’ll take time, though.

In the meantime, the rest of the world should pay attention to what’s 
happening with OpenClaw in this country, writes Bloomberg Opinion columnist 
Catherine Thorbecke.

“China is now running the world’s biggest real-world trial of AI agents, with 
ordinary people as the beta testers and their money and livelihoods on the 
line,” she said. “In the rush to automate everything, China will be the first 
to show where this experiment snaps.”

Left-Behind Workers

Hi, this is Colum Murphy in Beijing.

On a recent trip to the southern province of Guangdong, I met a garment worker 
named Chen Meifeng, 42. She was looking at her phone during a break from the 
night shift making clothes for a supplier tothe e-commerce giant Shein 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/china-apparel-makers-brace-for-price-hikes-as-oil-surges?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

Chen works 12-hour shifts, gets one day off a month and earns around 6,000 
yuan (around $870) for her labors. That isn’t a lot but it is down a quarter 
from 2024.

She’s not alone in her plight. Many of the people I spoke with told similar 
stories. For ordinary workers in China’s traditional industries like 
construction materials and apparel, wages are declining, jobs harder to come by 
and living standards are slipping. Read moreabout these folks here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-09/china-s-tariff-defying-export-boom-leaves-its-factory-workers-behind?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

While China clocked a roughly $1.2 trillion trade surplus last year, many of 
its factory-floor workers are not feeling the benefits. (Read aboutexports 
soaring in the first two months of 2026 here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/china-s-exports-surge-tops-forecasts-as-iran-war-threatens-trade?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.)

“It was easier to make money in the past,” Chen said.
Day laborers and recruiters for garment workshops in Datang, Guangzhou, in 
February.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
In line with national policy to shift toward higher value-added manufacturing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-29/china-wants-to-dominate-high-and-low-tech-industries-new-economy?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, Chinese companies are upgrading their output — think chips and electric cars. 
Yet many of these industries have less need for workers than traditional ones, 
hence fewer job opportunities.

Beijing’s push to embrace industrial automation and humanoid robots 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/china-prioritizes-rare-earths-robotics-in-manufacturing-agenda?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 will make this even more pronounced in years to come.

China’s yearslong property downturn 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/latest/china-s-property-crisis> is also behind the 
problems faced by people like Chen. That’s because the economic boom in 
Guangdong, and many other places around China in recent decades, was tied to 
massive building projects, like apartment complexes.

When I visited a formerly bustling ceramics market, the streets were empty and 
the shops were shuttered. Nearby hotels and restaurants sat vacant. One big 
reason for that is the real estate slump means fewer homes are being built so 
the need for tiling, toilets and bathroom cabinets is much diminished.

The gig economy offers something of a buffer for workers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-31/china-s-gig-economy-shows-why-quitting-the-corporate-grind-isn-t-so-simple?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, accounting for roughly 40% of urban employment. But those jobs typically lack 
benefits, and the industry faces broad disruption from robots or AI. Anxiety 
over driverless taxis replacing human drivers is already surfacing across the 
nation.

Guangdong was once the very picture of China’s economic boom. If the province 
was a country, it would rank sixth globally by exports. Yet its GDP growth has 
lagged the national pace for four years now, and it now highlightsthe economic 
challenges the Asian nation is facing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/china-economy-6-charts-explain-why-how-economic-growth-is-slowing-down?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

China’s embrace of “new productive forces” — fine jargon for tech goods — 
risks leaving low-skilled workers in the province and elsewhere behind — and 
possibly disgruntled.

While China’s vast censorship apparatus makes nationwide demonstrations rare, 
the country did witness a 44% jump in protest activity led by workers last 
year, according to Freedom House’s China Dissent Monitor. Guangdong was the top 
hotspot for the unhappiness.

Chen, the garment factory worker, hopes the future for her two teenage 
children will be brighter than hers “as long as they don’t go into making 
clothes,” she said.

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

China has been careful in choosing its official words 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/china-eyes-landmark-year-for-us-ties-urges-end-to-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 about the Iran War. Its public has been somewhat less diplomatic, calling 
America a “warmonger,” “disgrace” and more for the deadly strikes across Iran.

Beijing’s balancing act has to do with the fact that it doesn’t want anything 
to undermine Xi’s big summit with Donald Trump later this month in China. Read 
about howplanning for the sitdown seems to have some issues here

 and about China’s neighborsworried about the US moving weapons here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/us-allies-near-china-on-edge-as-weapons-shift-from-asia-to-iran?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

Most people on Chinese social media discussing the war voiced support for 
Iran. They also flooded the Iranian embassy’s website with armchair warfighting 
advice.

The pundits referred to themselves as “online military advisers,” and their 
suggestions included ideas like deploying drone swarms andstriking desalination 
plants 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/iran-war-why-desalination-plants-are-key-vulnerability-for-gulf-states?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 and oil facilities.

“The US sends missiles, we send the Art of War,” one person wrote on Weibo, 
getting some 128,000 likes. “They fight with firepower. We fight with brains. 
We don’t send weapons, we send strategies.”
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
There are also voices of moderation calling for a peaceful end to the 
conflict, which tracks with what the Foreign Ministry in Beijing has been 
saying since combat started. A few have been more hawkish, supporting the joint 
attacks by the US and Israel, partly because ofIran’s links to terrorism 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/eu-poised-to-list-iran-s-revolutionary-guard-as-terrorist-group?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

Others see the war as an opportunity for China. They argue that the longer the 
conflict lasts, the moreAmerica’s battered friends in the Middle East 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/strait-of-hormuz-how-is-iran-war-disrupting-oil-trade-can-naval-escorts-help?cmpid=BBD031326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 will be distrustful of the world’s preeminent power for its handling of the 
situation.

“Maybe this Iran War will rewrite the world’s power structure,” said a Weibo 
user with 300,000 followers, adding that would “accelerate the world’s power 
transfer from the old America to China.”

Well, as a wise man once said, “We’ll see.
