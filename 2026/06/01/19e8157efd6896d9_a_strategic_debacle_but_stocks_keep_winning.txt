Three Months Later

The conflict in Iran has now lasted a quarter of a year. It’s 10 weeks since 
President Donald Trump, flanked by Japan’s Sanae Takaichi, told the world’s 
press that Iran’sentire navy and air force had been “demolished.” 
<https://bloom.bg/4fhXFOn> Despite this,  an Iranian attack 
<https://bloom.bg/4fj5wLy> on a Kuwaiti military base injured several Americans 
this weekend. Iran’s demolished forces are still succeeding in placing a tight 
limit on traffic through the Strait of Hormuz — although there are signs that 
the US is finding ways to help ships get through. <https://bloom.bg/4x1uoOg> 

It’s a  strategic debacle for the US <https://bloom.bg/4x7S03V>. Iran’s regime 
remains in place, fortified by discovering just how easily it can close one of 
the world’s most vital waterways. And so the market reaction has, of course, 
been euphoric:

The extraordinary boom in the chips needed to power artificial intelligence is 
of course critical to this. But there’s also an underlying confidence that the 
Iranian situation will not cause serious damage. The latest quarterly survey of 
global asset allocators by Absolute Strategy Research is a useful gauge, as the 
previous edition came out in the week before war broke out. The most powerful 
money managers’ optimism appears undented:

Perhaps more surprisingly, they increasingly believe that multiples will 
expand, even though many measures are historically high. This is the first time 
a majority have expected multiples to rise since the worst of the pandemic:

Historic earnings multiples, looking back at profits over the last decade, 
appear extreme: The widely followed cyclically adjusted price/earnings ratio 
mapped by Robert Shiller <https://bloom.bg/4nVHPK3> of Yale University is at 
its highest since September 2000, exceeding even where it was on the eve of the 
Great Crash in 1929. But earnings forecasts have been through a dramatic surge 
in the last three months. If current profit forecasts are accurate, there is 
indeed some room for multiples to expand a bit further. But sales multiples are 
by far the highest in history, so much rests on both revenues and margins 
delivering on their promise:

Historically, multiples interact with inflation. When prices are rising 
faster, rates tend to increase, and price/earnings ratios are lower. That again 
makes asset allocators’ optimism rather curious. Expectations for fed funds at 
the end of this year have surged by 75 basis points since the war started, and 
there’s been little correction:

This judgment, like many other economic calls at present, depends on the 
future of traffic through the Strait. Long-term crude oil futures suggest it 
will have a lasting impact, although not necessarily a disastrous one:

Prediction markets, as pure a measure as we have of the perceived chance that 
passage through the waterway is resolved, have almost given up hope that it 
reopens in June:

Why then the optimism from allocators? They do show some concern about the 
economy in the more detailed questions, particularly on inflation. The central 
judgment is that while it will worsen as a result of the conflict, it will be 
manageable. There was more evidence on this last week…

Inflation Unease

There’s no question that the war has brought higher inflation in its wake, so 
far. At a headline level (including energy), prices rose close to 4% in the 
year to April. The Federal Reserve’s preferred Personal Consumption 
Expenditures metric has long suggested more moderate inflation than the more 
widely watched Consumer Price Index. They’re now in agreement:

On the face of it, any hope of rate cuts is over if inflation is this far 
above even the 3% upper band target (marked on the chart). But there are 
arguments that this latest dose of inflation is skewed. Take the trimmed mean 
version, excluding the biggest outliers in either direction, and PCE inflation 
is declining — although the conventionally defined core, excluding food and 
fuel, says exactly the opposite. As the new Fed Chair Kevin Warsh has said that 
he follows the trimmed mean, this might yet give him room to ease policy later 
this year, depending on Middle East events:

Warsh might also take heart from inflation swaps, which show that one- and 
two-year inflation expectations have dropped sharply in the last two weeks. 
Once up 100 basis points since the beginning of the war, they’ve now retraced 
half of that:

As for the fund managers and asset allocators interviewed by Absolute 
Strategy, they expect core US PCE inflation to rise from its current high base 
over the next year — a position that on the face of it is incompatible with 
anything other than rate hikes. It suggests deep gloom about the prospects for 
resolution in the Gulf:

This is probably the core debate that will dominate the rest of the year. 
Lengthy supply chains and hefty inventories mean that the worst of the shock 
has not yet been felt, and supply may yet resume soon. The war is perceived to 
have made the inflation picture more difficult for the US, but not to any 
seriously unmanageable extent; we will slowly find out over the months to come 
whether that’s right.


No Longer Invincible 

In the world’s other economic superpower, the energy crisis is steadily 
puncturing the notion <https://bloom.bg/4nXScP3> of an invincible China. Layer 
by layer, the buffers that Beijing has built to shield itself from external 
shocks are showing their limits. May’s data exhibited broad-based weakness, 
suggesting that constrained energy shipments and supply chains in limbo are 
exacting a heavier toll on the Chinese economy than previously anticipated. The 
latest data byChina Beige Book <https://bloom.bg/4322iEV> found both revenues 
and margins were weakening:

Services were an exception. Broad disappointment showed in decelerating output 
and weak retail sales as consumers cut back travel and leisure spending. The
housing market <https://bloom.bg/4fj5ByQ>, a constant source of economic pain, 
also delivered a fresh batch of disappointing data:

Exports’ robust growth, however, suggests they remain war-proof — although 
there’s no indication of how long this will last if the stalemate in Iran 
persists. For now, export orders surged, partly thanks to an increase in 
US-destined goods following a broad easing of tariffs. Meanwhile, with Asia 
weakened by the energy crisis, the Beige Book, an independent research group, 
suggests some exports originally bound for the region may be headed elsewhere:

Despite producer prices climbing their most since the pandemic, consumer 
inflation remains muted. With households already struggling to boost spending, 
sensitive demand limits how much pain companies can pass on before a 
catastrophic collapse.

So far, every sector tracked by the China Beige Book appears to show a greater 
shock to growth than to inflationary pressure, with all reporting weaker 
on-year and on-month pricing power. Retailers saw the biggest setback compared 
to a year ago:

Does this portend a downward spiral or merely a bump? Capital Economics’ Hamad 
Hussain points to a mismatch between oil supplied and consumed in China, which 
suggests that inventories are being drawn down. That eases the upward pressure 
on oil prices but also suggests that worse may lie ahead:

Against the backdrop of the US-Iran negotiations, the drawdown of oil 
inventories in China could buy some more time and push back the potential 
“tipping point” for global oil prices into the third quarter.

This growing evidence of the war’s impact strengthens the case for large-scale 
consumer stimulus, an intervention that leader Xi Jinping has doggedly avoided. 
His recent meeting withTrump <https://bloom.bg/4fj5CCU> offered little sign of 
Beijing pressuring Iran to soften its stance on the Strait, suggesting that 
China may turn to other means to shield itself from the conflict. In addition 
to the vast oil inventories, it’s also buoyed by strong AI-linked industrial 
growth, which jumped to a 29-month high:
Sources: CEIC, Capital Economics 
Given rising producer prices <https://bloom.bg/4dI3PWS>, this profit surge 
seems rather odd. Capital Economics’ Julian Evans-Pritchard explains that while 
the cost of some raw materials has jumped, widespread overcapacity means many 
firms are sitting on large inventories bought at lower prices. Other 
significant production costs, including wages and electricity, have not 
changed. As a result, the average cost of production has risen by less than 
output prices, expanding profit margins.

Still, China’s best case is a swift resolution in Iran. Without that, Louise 
Loo of Oxford Economics notes that fiscal policy may need to do some heavy 
lifting in coming quarters to offset any softening in exports:

Policymakers will need to manage the pace of manufacturers’ gains in global 
export share to pre-empt a global pushback that could break their export-driven 
growth strategy. While peak demand destruction from China’s housing correction 
appears to have passed, the economy’s transition to the new growth model is 
still in its early stages and far from complete.

The yuan is at its strongest in more than three years, since the immediate 
aftermath of the disastrous Covid-Zero lockdowns Beijing imposed during the 
Omicron variant of Covid:

This has coincided with tighter offshore stock-trading rules 
<https://bloom.bg/4oa0Dqr>. Gavekal Research’s Wei He attributes part of the 
currency’s strength to exporters converting more foreign-currency receipts into 
renminbi:

Yet financial institutions, which dominate foreign-currency trading, are 
paring back their appreciation expectations, likely in response to signals from 
the central bank. Wei He argues that market forecasts have historically been a 
better predictor, so the base case is that renminbi appreciation cools off from 
here.

Bloomberg Economics’ Chang Shu and David Qu suggest that the revised offshore 
trading rules appear more like a pre-emptive move to tighten controls before 
external conditions deteriorate. While the clampdown has drawn similarities with
the market panic <https://bloom.bg/3PCmFp7> of 2015, they rule out a repeat. 
This time, the yuan’s strength gives regulators room to tighten oversight 
before markets come under strain.
