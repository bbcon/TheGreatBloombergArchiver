The central tension in global markets on April 14 was the coexistence of a formal US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz and active diplomatic engagement aimed at avoiding a full rupture. President Trump's blockade, cutting off vessel traffic to and from Iranian ports, represents a significant escalation; yet simultaneous reports of prospective face-to-face negotiations before the April 7 ceasefire expires next week gave markets a constructive signal to focus on. WTI crude fell to approximately $97 per barrel as diplomacy dominated sentiment, though Brent remains near $99, roughly $27 above pre-war levels.

The blockade's enforcement is already being tested. A US-sanctioned tanker linked to China — the Rich Starry — transited the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, creating a direct challenge to the mechanism's credibility and raising the possibility of US-China friction over energy access. The episode underscores a point made by several analysts: the blockade is, in part, a pressure tactic directed at Beijing as much as Tehran, given that China absorbs the overwhelming majority of Iranian crude exports. Iran, for its part, warned it would target ports across the Persian Gulf if its own shipping infrastructure is threatened.

The International Energy Agency issued a striking forecast: global oil demand is expected to decline in 2026 for the first time since the pandemic, as the price surge caused by the conflict offsets underlying demand growth. The agency warned that "energy markets and economies around the world need to brace for significant disruptions in the months to come."

The conflict is accelerating several longer-run shifts. China's lithium battery exports rose 50% year-on-year in the first quarter as buyers sought alternatives to oil-dependent energy, while the petroyuan concept has gained renewed traction after Iran accepted yuan payments for Strait transit. Delta Air Lines quietly downgraded its net-zero emissions goal to an "aspiration" and removed a 2030 sustainable aviation fuel target, illustrating how corporate climate commitments are being re-evaluated under the combined pressure of energy market disruption and political headwinds. Lebanon and Israel began the first direct US-hosted talks in decades, though Hezbollah's refusal to participate in or abide by any agreement reached limits the realistic scope of what can be achieved.
