Friday, July 10, 2026

US

  • AI-driven business formation is accelerating, with professional services formations projected to surge 24% within the next year — no formal consensus estimate, but Apollo's chief economist describes the pace as historically elevated.
  • Major bank earnings arrive Tuesday, with five of the six largest US banks reporting; analysts anticipate near-record trading revenues amid months of market volatility.
  • S&P 500 futures slipped 0.1–0.2% ahead of the weekend as traders reduced risk amid a fragile Middle East ceasefire; the KBW Bank Index has risen 13% year-to-date, outpacing the S&P 500's 10% gain.

Business Formation and AI

The US is experiencing a second wave of entrepreneurial activity driven by artificial intelligence, following the post-pandemic surge that plateaued in 2021. Business formations in AI-adjacent professional services are projected to rise 24% over the next year, according to independent economists. The dominant form appears to be single-person "solopreneurs" rather than employer firms, leaving open the question of whether the wave will generate net employment or merely redistribute it. A Federal Reserve Board paper released this week cautioned that transformational technologies historically generate overinvestment cycles, and that AI would need to "repeatedly beat investor expectations" to sustain a durable investment boom — a threshold not yet met.

Markets and Bank Earnings

Equity futures drifted lower ahead of the weekend, with S&P 500 futures off 0.1–0.2%, as geopolitical risk from the US-Iran situation remained in focus. Investors are positioned for a consequential week ahead: five of the six largest US banks report Tuesday, with consensus expecting second-best-ever equity trading revenues. The KBW Bank Index's 13% year-to-date outperformance of the broader market, if sustained, would mark its third consecutive year of beating the S&P 500. Goldman Sachs separately banned employees from trading on prediction markets — excluding sports and entertainment — citing regulatory and insider-trading risks raised by the rapid growth of event-betting platforms. ---

Asia

  • China conducted its first submarine-launched ICBM test into the Pacific, firing a missile carrying a dummy warhead several thousand kilometers — Japan expressed "grave concern" and Australia called it "destabilizing."
  • SK Hynix raised $26.5 billion in the largest-ever US first-time share sale by a foreign company, with leveraged ETF products tracking the ADRs set to launch next week.
  • Japan's Finance Minister called on pension funds, including the ¥293.6 trillion GPIF, to increase domestic asset allocations, boosting the yen from near four-decade lows.

China Geopolitics and Property

China's test of a submarine-launched intercontinental ballistic missile — likely a JL-3 variant with an estimated range of approximately 10,000 kilometers — represents the clearest demonstration yet of its nuclear triad development. While neighbors registered alarm, the primary signal appears directed at the US: Beijing is demonstrating, or approaching, a credible second-strike capability from sea-based platforms widely regarded as harder to neutralize than land-based systems. Separately, China's property market recovery remains narrow. Small, older apartments in major cities such as Shanghai have seen modest price appreciation — up 2.4% from November lows through April — but the trend is confined to prime urban locations and does little to address the broader glut of developer inventory. A broader market recovery appears distant.

Japan Pension Policy and Yen

Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama's call for pension funds to shift toward domestic assets produced an immediate yen rally, though structural headwinds persist. The yen has declined approximately 32% against the dollar over the past five years, and government intervention totaling $72.6 billion earlier this year failed to arrest its slide. Global positioning remains heavily against the currency — hedge funds are the most net-short yen since 2007. Whether a policy signal, absent compulsory reallocation mandates, can sustainably reverse capital outflows from a country holding nearly $5 trillion in overseas assets remains uncertain. ---

  • EasyJet shares surged 14% in London after Apollo Global Management submitted a takeover offer topping a rival Castlelake proposal, establishing a potential bidding war.

UK Political Transition

The Labour leadership contest to replace Keir Starmer has effectively resolved after a single day of nominations. Andy Burnham's 322-of-403 MP vote share leaves his rivals with little viable path to the membership stage, and markets appear to be pricing a smooth transition. Burnham indicated he sees his mandate as representing "a new approach to politics," though the policy contours of that approach remain to be elaborated. The scale of his parliamentary backing — 80% of the caucus — suggests the party is prioritizing unity over ideological differentiation at a moment of likely electoral pressure from Reform UK.

European Corporate Developments

EasyJet's 14% single-session gain reflects the premium Apollo's offer carries over the existing Castlelake proposal, though neither bid price has been formally disclosed. The situation sets up a competitive auction dynamic and raises broader questions about the valuation gap between European and US-listed aviation assets. Volkswagen's separate announcement that it intends to cut its model lineup by as much as half underscores the depth of restructuring pressure facing European automakers — the company's China profits have deteriorated materially, removing a historically significant earnings cushion. The full scale of the restructuring, described internally as the most significant in decades, is still being finalized. ---

LatAm

  • Peru's central bank held its benchmark rate at 4.25% for a tenth consecutive meeting, signaling that policymakers view a recent inflation uptick as transitory rather than structural.
  • President-elect Keiko Fujimori declined to align Peru explicitly with either the US or China, describing the country's investment posture as "fusion" — complicating Washington's effort to reduce Chinese economic influence in the region.

Peru Between Washington and Beijing

Keiko Fujimori's election last month appeared to offer the Trump administration a natural ally given her US education and pro-business platform. Her refusal to endorse automatic alignment with Washington, however, reflects the structural reality of Peru's economic relationships: China is Peru's largest trading partner and a dominant investor in mining, energy, and infrastructure, most visibly through the $1.3 billion Chancay megaport inaugurated by President Xi Jinping. The port has become a focal point of US-China rivalry in the Pacific, and the incoming Fujimori government's pragmatic neutrality suggests Lima intends to maximize leverage from both powers rather than accept conditionality from either. This dynamic illustrates a broader pattern south of Colombia where Beijing's economic penetration limits Washington's ability to enforce geopolitical alignment.

Regional Rate Policy

Peru's tenth consecutive hold at 4.25% reflects a central bank that remains cautious despite a modest inflation uptick, consistent with a regional pattern of monetary authorities in hold mode after aggressive tightening cycles. The decision implies that the Banco Central de Reserva del Perú views current price pressures as manageable within the existing policy stance, though any sustained pass-through from fuel price volatility linked to the Hormuz disruptions could complicate that assessment. ---

Global

  • Refined fuel markets are tightening materially despite crude prices nearly erasing their wartime rally — refinery margins surged to their highest since 2022 in early July, per the IEA.
  • US-Iran technical talks are continuing despite two days of clashes that strained the ceasefire and brought Strait of Hormuz tanker traffic to a near-standstill.
  • Goldman Sachs finds carry trade conditions the most compelling since 2000, reflecting elevated global interest rate differentials and persistent dollar strength.

The Oil Market Bifurcation

The IEA's July report highlights an increasingly bifurcated oil market: crude supplies have recovered as Gulf exports resumed under an interim peace arrangement, but refined product and LPG exports from the region remain below 50% of prewar levels, with key export-oriented refineries not yet operational. This structural divergence is driving crack spreads — the margin between crude input costs and refined output prices — to their highest since 2022, creating inflationary pressure at the consumer level even as headline crude benchmarks stabilize. Brent traded near $76 per barrel. The summer driving season in the US compounds supply constraints on the product side. Ukrainian strikes on Russian refineries are a secondary amplifying factor, as Moscow has redirected crude exports while restricting diesel shipments.

Geopolitical Risk and Monetary Divergence

The US-Iran ceasefire remains fragile. Technical-level talks are ongoing, but two days of renewed clashes demonstrated the vulnerability of the arrangement. The IEA explicitly warned that resumed hostilities risk derailing efforts to rebuild global oil inventories later this year. Separately, global monetary policy divergence is generating notable currency dynamics: New Zealand's dollar reached a three-week high after hawkish RBNZ signals and strong manufacturing data, while the yen remains structurally pressured despite Tokyo's domestic-investment push. Goldman Sachs' assessment that carry trades face their best conditions since 2000 reflects the breadth of global rate differentials — a dynamic that may persist as long as the Fed holds rates elevated and smaller economies cycle independently.
