The most consequential development of the day was President Trump's announcement of an indefinite ceasefire extension with Iran, delivered within 30 minutes of news that Vice President Vance's planned trip to Islamabad for renewed talks had been cancelled. The sequencing — bad news followed almost immediately by a de-escalatory action — encapsulates the dynamic that markets have priced: Trump retains escalation optionality but consistently elects the off-ramp. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed, however, and the US naval blockade continues. Iran's UN envoy indicated Wednesday morning that Tehran had received "some sign" Washington is ready to lift the blockade, suggesting backchannel communication is active even as formal talks have stalled. The UK and France convened a summit of military planners in London to discuss keeping the Strait open; separately, an Iranian gunboat fired on a container ship near Hormuz on Wednesday.

The commodity dimensions of the conflict deserve attention beyond crude oil. The Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index has risen meaningfully since hostilities began, with wheat and rice both moving higher, though the moves remain below 2022 levels — possibly because markets anticipate a short conflict, or have not yet fully priced fertilizer supply disruptions from the Gulf. Bank of America flags corn as carrying the largest upside risk, given mounting pressure in nitrogen fertilizer markets. Industrial metals have also rallied sharply: copper and aluminum are each up more than 11% since mid-March, supported both by supply chain disruption and robust structural demand from AI data center buildout and grid electrification. The copper-to-gold ratio recently touched four-decade lows, a level historically associated with the onset of commodity supercycles.

Commodity trading houses have been direct financial beneficiaries of the volatility. Vitol, Trafigura, and Gunvor together raised $7.5 billion in contingency liquidity facilities at the war's outset, and preliminary first-quarter figures suggest profits comparable in magnitude to the $30 billion-plus earned collectively in 2022. Russia's oil export revenues have risen to their highest since early in the Ukraine invasion, but elevated domestic borrowing costs and a military-distorted economy mean the windfall is unlikely to translate into broader growth — a meaningful constraint on any Putin-mediated diplomatic initiative.
