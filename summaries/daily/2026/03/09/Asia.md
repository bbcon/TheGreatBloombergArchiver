Asian equity markets absorbed the sharpest session losses of any region on Monday. The MSCI Asia-Pacific index fell 3.7%, extending its weekly decline to nearly 10%. Japan's Nikkei 225 dropped 5.2%, with the index now in correction territory from its recent highs, as the oil shock interacts with a structurally weak yen to amplify Japan's import cost burden — raising the risk of stagflation in an economy where activity was already sluggish. South Korea's Kospi fell 6%, with Seoul reporting that US military transport aircraft, including C-17 and C-5 variants, had flown out of Osan Air Base in recent days amid discussions around potential redeployment of American assets as the Iran conflict broadens. Gauges in Pakistan and Vietnam fell more than 6%.

The macro stress on Asia's energy-importing economies is acute. Fitch Ratings identified India and Pakistan as particularly exposed to deteriorating credit metrics from sustained high oil prices. India imports approximately 90% of its oil needs; a widening trade deficit and currency pressure compound an already fragile external position. South Korean President Lee Jae Myung called for fuel price caps, while the Philippines ordered government offices to operate four days weekly to reduce energy consumption and initiated talks with suppliers in the Americas, Australia, and Africa. Taiwan set a weekly cap on oil price increases. Vietnam faces potential airfare increases of up to 70% as jet fuel costs have tripled.

China presents a more buffered picture. As the largest single buyer of Hormuz-transiting oil, China holds substantial strategic petroleum reserves that analysts believe can absorb near-term supply disruption. Separately, Chinese consumer price growth accelerated to its fastest pace in over three years. On the diplomatic front, Foreign Minister Wang Yi described 2026 as a potentially defining year for US-China relations, signaling Beijing's intent to insulate the bilateral relationship from the Iran conflict despite maintaining an upgraded partnership with Tehran. China simultaneously warned Japan of unspecified consequences after Tokyo allowed a visit by Taiwan's Premier Cho Jung-tai — a reminder that regional geopolitical tensions remain layered beneath the Iran-dominated headlines. Taiwan's dollar fell 0.8% on the session.
