Freight transportation costs in the US continue to skyrocket, fueling concerns 
about another flareup of inflation.

The transport price gauge in the latest Logistics Managers’ Index rose to 96, 
the fastest rate of expansion for any metric in the 10-year history of the 
report. Capacity is quickly contracting and utilization is elevated, according 
to thereport <https://www.the-lmi.com/may-2026-logistics-managers-index.html>.

“Supply chains have been resilient despite these ongoing disruptions,” the LMI 
report stated. “However, in the past this level of elevated costs has 
eventually led to significant levels of supply-driven inflation.”

Overall logistics costs, which include transportation, warehousing and 
inventories, rose to the highest level since March 2022, a peak that “was part 
of a run of high logistics inflation that led to the highest US inflation in 40 
years,” the report continued.

That’s been a welcome development for freight companies. In the latest episode 
of Bloomberg’s Talking Transportspodcast 
<https://podcasts.apple.com/us/podcast/white-arrow-sees-freight-market-shift/id1717602502?i=1000770765430>
, Chris Ceausu, CEO of trucking company White Arrow, said the carrier is seeing 
“significant improvements across the board” in the second quarter and looks 
like “the start of long cycle upwards.”

Talking Transports: White Arrow Sees Freight Market Shift Upwards

The logistics managers’ report noted that it’s harder for the Federal Reserve 
to counter rising prices during a supply shock than a demand-driven surge, 
because higher interest rates can’t add to supply.

On Wednesday, the Fed’s latest Beige Book survey of regional business contacts 
containedsimilar worries 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/fed-s-beige-book-shows-steady-employment-higher-inflation?cmpid=BBD060426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5001>
 about supplies and freight costs:

 * In the Cleveland Fed’s district, home to much of the nation’s auto 
industry, “some freight contacts added fuel surcharges to cover higher costs 
and noted that reduced capacity in the sector had increased their pricing power”
 * In the Richmond district, “one furniture retailer noted that they were 
receiving 50% fuel surcharges on domestic freight along with tariff surcharges, 
which were extremely hard to pass along via retail prices”
 * Contacts in the Minneapolis region “continued to report substantial fuel 
surcharges for freight”
 * The Boston Fed reported that “manufacturers and retailers said increased 
shipping costs cut into profits, and manufacturers also faced higher raw 
materials costs”
According to an different outlook 
<https://www.anderson.ucla.edu/news-and-events/ucla-anderson-forecast-says-oil-shock-has-replaced-tariffs-leading-risk-us-economy>
 released Wednesday, UCLA’s Anderson School of Management said the oil shock has
replaced tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/us-proposes-broad-tariffs-of-at-least-10-citing-forced-labor?cmpid=BBD060426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5001>
 as the leading risk to the US economy.

Hormuz Latest: Iran Says No Progress in US Talks Amid Lebanon Clashes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-04/iran-says-no-progress-in-us-talks-as-lebanon-sees-more-clashes?cmpid=BBD060426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260604&utm_content=5001>

The university sees GDP growing staying near 2.1% in 2026, with core CPI 
inflation peaking at 4.5% by year end, up from its May level of 3.8% and driven 
by the energy crunch.

“Tariff inflation, by contrast, appears to have peaked earlier in 2026,” the 
UCLA report said. “The overturning of many previous tariffs is expected to put 
modest downward pressure on goods and core inflation, but the report asserts 
that this effect will be small compared with the price disruptions caused by 
the conflict in Iran.”

A Bloomberg Economics analysis <> published explained that supply disruptions 
globally appear to be easing in some regions, but lingering friction suggests 
there’s no short route to normalization.

Extended supply chain disruptions “can trigger inflation spikes more than a 
year down the road,” Eric Zhu of Bloomberg Economics wrote.
