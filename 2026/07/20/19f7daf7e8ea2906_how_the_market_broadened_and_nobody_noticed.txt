Never Mind the Beat, Feel the Breadth 

Second-quarter earnings are all but assured <https://bloom.bg/4aXGYVw> to beat 
expectations. That doesn’t really matter. Corporate earnings management is such 
that it’s a given. As earnings season gets into gear this week, the dominant 
questions are whether corporate momentum is as strong as it appears, and 
whether it’s expanding beyond direct beneficiaries of the 
artificial-intelligence boom.

Wall Street banks’ earnings outperformance <https://bloom.bg/4yvxivi> last 
week was impressive. Trading revenues helped, but so did the AI buildout; it 
drives higher financing activity and generates capital markets revenues for 
financial groups. But the market is not positioned for a big AI effect for 
other sectors.

Tracking headline earnings per share forecasts using Bloomberg's earnings 
estimate graph function shows growth stubbornly concentrated in a few sectors. 
Healthcare has endured a brutal negative rerating this year. Outside of 
technology, energy is set to provide the largest contribution, thanks to the 
Iran conflict and the oil-price rally:

State Street’s Michael Arone argues that earnings results shouldn’t be 
dismissed as over-concentrated. In the first quarter, he points out, seven of 
the 11 economic sectors delivered double-digit earnings growth, and all 11 
reported higher revenues. That doesn’t diminish technology’s disproportionate 
contribution to aggregate earnings. Rather, it suggests that leadership is 
broadening beneath the surface, even as technology continues to account for an 
outsized share of overall profit growth.

Deutsche Bank’s Binky Chadha <https://bloom.bg/3RO5a69> notes that after 
little headline growth in the prior two years, profits for the “rest” of the 
S&P 500 (excluding tech and AI) are projected to grow by 14.3%, adding 9.1 
percentage points to overall growth. That reflects a cyclical rebound, with the 
ISM manufacturing index back in expansionary territory:

The ISM’s turnaround <https://bloom.bg/4fJq6Ev> suggests that US consumers are 
resilient and could prop up this bull market for a while longer. Bank of 
America's Savita Subramanian finds that the historical relationship between 
manufacturing ISM surveys and S&P 500 earnings on its own implies 10% 
second-quarter growth. Services PMI points to something closer to 20%.

Both estimates, remarkably, fall short of the 23.4% growth currently expected 
by consensus. That’s because technology’s outperformance isn’t fully captured 
by the historical relationship between the ISM surveys and aggregate earnings. 
But the strong ISM does indeed point to broadening:

Meanwhile, consumers have resisted a difficult 18 months of price pressures 
from Washington’s tariff brinkmanship and the energy crisis. Josh Jamner of 
ClearBridge Investments notes that a turnaround in consumer discretionary and 
retail expectations, quite bearish coming into the year, is likely to 
contribute to the robust second-quarter earnings growth story. Revisions, 
Jamner notes, indicate that the retail sector has held up well, while consumer 
discretionary companies are on track to deliver high-single-digit EPS growth 
this quarter.

Taken together, the revisions suggest US consumers continue to defy 
expectations. Still, the resilience story is not impregnable. Jamner said a 
materially cooling labor market would be “the first thing that would get us to 
change our view.”

The recent tech selloff raises questions over whether earnings are really 
growing fast enough to merit prevailing valuations. If based on forward 
expectations for the next 12 months, multiples since the launch of ChatGPT in 
2022 haven’t looked extreme, oscillating around 20 times. Earnings forecasts 
have risen enough for the S&P 500 to rise at 16.6% annualized since then, 
roughly twice the long-term average:

ClearBridge Investments’ Jeff Schulze calls earnings the oxygen tank powering 
the lofty valuations, with next-12-month expectations up 155% over that period:

Simply put, the S&P 500 appears to be in a higher-valuation regime, with 
earnings doing all the heavy lifting behind the market’s climb — a dynamic we 
expect to continue in the second half.

For now, the market has spent four years rewarding a narrow set of winners. 
However, if the next phase is instead defined by a broader mix of companies 
meeting lofty earnings expectations, it would mark an important inflection 
point that shifts the rally from one driven by exceptionalism to one 
underpinned by a healthier corporate backdrop. While that is unlikely to dispel 
concerns over stretched valuations, it would make betting against the expansion 
considerably more difficult.

—Richard Abbey

A Market Definition of Breadth

One slight paradox of breadth: While earnings are concentrated heavily in 
those with stuff to sell to AI hyperscalers, share prices have been broadening 
nicely. Or at least, the developments of the last few months would have eased 
the concerns about breadth that started the year. The only problem is that they 
would have prompted new ones. One theory has been that AI isn’t a bubble, but 
that it’s inflated froth that has spread from one sector to another — a rolling 
bubble. And that seems to be happening.

The Magnificent Seven hyperscalers have had a miserable time this year, 
lagging the average stock. When people worried about breadth six or 12 months 
ago, this was exactly what they hoped would happen — a passing of the baton 
from companies that appeared overdone. The torch is now with semiconductor 
groups, but there has been a form of market widening:

Adam Parker of Trivariate Research <https://bloom.bg/4yrCKzv> points out that 
Apple Corp., the best performing <https://bloom.bg/4yqqnnn> Magnificent so far 
this year, is only the 134th best performing stock in the S&P 500, while all 
the big hyperscalers bar Apple and Alphabet Inc. have done worse than the 
median stock. He said:

The question is — why doesn’t this dynamic feel better? We suspect there are 
some investors that thought they would be outperforming by more than they are 
with their underweight in the [hyperscalers], and, we worry that a big risk for 
some long-only investors is they are positioned for the group to continue to 
lag.

Contrary to impressions, then, if the biggest AI groups show that their 
capital expenditures are paying off in the next few weeks, that could be bad 
news for a big segment of active managers. Meanwhile, if this is a rolling 
bubble, the next pressing questions are where it rolls next, and whether fund 
managers have handled their exposure to semiconductors as well as they seem to 
have played the Magnificent Seven.


Time to RTO?

The good news for hard-pressed New Yorkers is that it’s much easier to find an 
empty seaton the subway <https://bloom.bg/4yrChNC> at rush hour than it used to 
be. But that could be very bad news for investors in office property, and also 
have far-reaching economic implications.

Torsten Slok of Apollo Management offers this chart <https://bloom.bg/3TIw93y> 
comparing subway ridership during weekends (always lower) and the working week:

Covid is still around, but far less of a presence in New Yorkers’ lives. Yet 
the volume of commuting is still down by about a third compared to the 
pre-pandemic norm. This begins to look like a permanent change in behavior.

Broader surveys show the same result. WFH Research <https://bloom.bg/4ymu4u2>, 
a joint effort of Stanford University’s Hoover Institution and the ITAM 
university in Mexico City, has beentracking the percentage of fully paid days  
<https://bloom.bg/4ywBMll>worked from home. It is still declining very gently, 
but remains far ahead of its levels from early 2000:
Source: WFH Research 
Digging into historical data, the researchers show that working from home had 
been gradually increasing over the decades, as technology made it more 
practical. But the pandemic plainly increased that far ahead of schedule:
Source: WFH Research 
The obvious implication is that companies should be economizing on office 
space. There’s scant evidence of this from the New York skyline, which has seen 
a number of spectacular additions since 2020,led by One Vanderbilt 
<https://bloom.bg/4vYcPwZ>, which dwarfs the nearby Chrysler Building, and the  
new headquarters for JPMorgan Chase & Co. <https://bloom.bg/4yKA00i> at 270 
Park Avenue, which opened last year. The latter can accommodate 10,000 workers.

The eye-catching new supply has coincided with a collapse in office property 
real estate investment trusts. The pandemic was a first major shock, but 
underperformance has intensified since the launch of ChatGPT, which may have 
convinced even more workers that they don’t need to sit in the office and talk 
to colleagues — particularly if that involves taking a crowded New York subway:

Compared to the rest of the real estate sector, offices have enjoyed a pick-up 
of late. It’s possible to believe that the bottom is in — but that might 
require more people and companies to decide that they need to return to the 
office, and there’s little sign of that:

For the future, the questions about working from home that were raised six 
years ago still remain. The accidental conversations and encounters that happen 
in an office environment are still more valuable than any amount of chat on 
Slack or Zoom, and the understanding that comes with occupying the same space 
as a fellow human and looking them in the eye remains hard to beat.

There’s also a question of whether the edifice will eventually fall. If you’ve 
been working at the same firm for many years, and you’ve built personal 
connections and imbibed the culture, not so much is lost by sitting at a 
computer at home. But it’s quite possible that new generations will live to 
discover that remote working isn’t enabling them to build those same 
connections.

Society has already seen reactions against social media, and a punch back 
against AI. Is WFH next? <https://bloom.bg/4bmtnr1> And will it happen in time 
to save office real estate investments?



 Survival Tips 



Many congratulations to Spain, who deservedly won a taut World Cup final that 
won’t linger long in the memory. When the stakes aren't high, the play can be 
better. Just watch the10-goal thriller <https://bloom.bg/4pEmeIj> that England 
and France <https://bloom.bg/4wVonSv> played out for the bronze. Both teams 
were trying, neither resorted to foul play as it simply wasn't worth it, and 
the result was extremely entertaining. Compare it to thetense 
<https://bloom.bg/4wVSDg4> and low-scoring <https://bloom.bg/4ptUbLD> 
semifinals and final, and there are arguments for clamping down on discipline. 
Let the game flow and it’s fun.

As for my dire predictions, I argued against expanding the tournament to 48 
teams and starting knockouts with 32. Quality would be diluted and winning 
would be perceived as a lottery. I was wrong about that. There were lots of 
goals, and several greatgiantkiller stories <https://bloom.bg/4hjFeJV>, led by 
Cape Verde <https://bloom.bg/4wMgRJl>. The rounds of 32 and 16 produced much 
more exciting games than the later stages. The last four teams standing were 
the top four in theworld rankings <https://bloom.bg/4pyov7V>. And the Cup went 
— by almost universal agreement — to the best team.

But it probably was too long. A number of greats — Haaland 
<https://bloom.bg/4bLEg5x>, Kane, Mbappe, even Messi — started on fire, and 
faded badly toward the end. The oppressive heat began to affect games. 
Innovations like hydration breaks on a number of occasions allowed teams to 
regroup.Maximizing ticket revenues <https://bloom.bg/44zqTBI> diminished joy 
and in future may diminish what the TV revenues can earn. And please, can we 
not have anotherSuper Bowl half-time show <https://bloom.bg/4pw06zU> dragging 
out the feeling of tedium?

Goal of the tournament: this one <https://bloom.bg/44zqXBs>. Next question: 
What shall we all do now? Have a great football-free week everyone.
