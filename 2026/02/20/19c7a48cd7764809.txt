We’re seeing a lot of geopolitical risk <https://bloom.bg/4rSAddi> and saber 
rattling in this first week of the Year of the Horse.

There’s the US amassing a vast array of forces in the Middle East 
<https://bloom.bg/40lQxYm> to pressure Iran into a deal over its nuclear 
program, for one. Here in Asia, US and Chinese fighter jets reportedly briefly 
faced off above waters near the Korean Peninsula this week, marking arare 
confrontation <https://bloom.bg/3OR0SJi> in that area between the two 
superpowers. Less rare is this bit of drama from US President Donald Trump, 
saying he’s directing federal agencies to release government documents that 
discussextraterrestrial life <https://bloom.bg/40knQuR> and unexplained aerial 
phenomenon.

Beyond those headlines, the US continues to reshape trade flows and supply 
chains. The world’s biggest economyimported more from Taiwan 
<https://bloom.bg/4qOWR5D> than China for the first time in decades. Trump and 
Indonesian President Prabowo Subianto finalized atrade agreement 
<https://bloom.bg/40jB4If> Thursday that’s expected to lower US tariffs and see 
Jakarta facilitate the purchase of an estimated $33 billion in American goods. 
And India formally joined a US-led initiative to protect supply chains, 
including on critical minerals.

Markets remain focused on the risk side of the equation, with oil rising to a 
six-month high and Asianstocks edging lower <https://bloom.bg/4qL9VJa>, though 
losses narrowed through the session. Saddle up.—Stephanie Phang 
<https://bloom.bg/4gs6Wkp>



 What You Need to Know Today 



On the upside, shares of China’s generative AI startups Zhipu and MiniMax Group
soared in Hong Kong <https://bloom.bg/3OScCLB> as the market reopened after the 
Lunar New Year, with investors piling into pure AI plays and rotating out of 
traditional Internet giants. Androbots once again stole the show 
<https://bloom.bg/40p8Iwc> during China’s annual Spring Festival gala, helping 
to push up shares in robotics firms even as the broader market sagged.




Indian data center–related shares have rallied, as Prime Minister Narendra 
Modi reiterated the country’s ambition to emerge as a global hub for AI in the 
presence of OpenAI’s Sam Altman and Anthropic PBC’s Dario Amodei. Shares of 10 
companiesadded about $4 billion <https://bloom.bg/4s4rXY3> in combined market 
value this week as the country hosted a high-profile gathering of leading AI 
companies in New Delhi. Follow Bloomberg’s coverage of the India AI Impact 
Summithere <https://bloom.bg/46RVtb9>. 




Also hot? Japan’s cultural icons. From Hello Kitty to Demon Slayer, pop 
culture is now the country’s second most valuable export. That cultural pull 
has hardened into a formidable wealth-creation engine.Read how mascots and manga
 <https://bloom.bg/3ZLVJoo> have turned into billion-dollar businesses that 
have minted fortunes for the executives, founders and heirs who control the 
rights to its most coveted symbols.




Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi vowed to repeatedly “hit the button” on 
economic growth and adopt a bolder diplomatic stance without spooking markets 
during a speech setting out her policy vision. Speaking in parliament after her 
historic election landslide earlier this month, Takaichi said she wouldboost 
strategic investment <https://bloom.bg/4qMYUHk>, carry out active fiscal policy 
responsibly and pursue assertive diplomacy.
Sanae Takaichi Photographer: Kiyoshi Ota/Bloomberg 



Some decent economic numbers are coming out around Asia. India’s economic 
activity accelerated in February assentiment improved <https://bloom.bg/3ZIwQd9>
 after the USslashed <https://bloom.bg/4qPpLCy> tariffs on Indian goods, a 
flash survey by HSBC showed. Malaysian exports, driven by asurge in electronics 
shipments <https://bloom.bg/3ZMUEfR>, rose at the fastest pace in over three 
years in January. And Japan’s manufacturing activity stayed expansionary for a 
third month, signalingfirmer corporate sentiment <https://bloom.bg/40qeu0B>.




Jollibee, the Philippines’ largest restaurant operator, said it signed a 
definitive agreement toacquire a Korean hot pot brand <https://bloom.bg/4qN3Dsq>
 for around $87 million. A subsidiary will buy All Day Fresh, which is behind 
Seoul-based hot pot and eat-all-you-can chain Shabu All Day. The deal follows 
Jollibee’s July acquisition of a 70% stake in South Korea-based Compose Coffee 
for $340 million.
A Jollibee restaurant in Quezon City, Metro Manila, the Philippines, in 2023. 
Photographer: Veejay Villafranca/Bloomberg 



India’s market regulator has issued a show-cause notice to Zee Entertainment 
and its leadership,reviving allegations <https://bloom.bg/4qSjxlr> of fund 
diversion and governance lapses that derailed its planned merger Sony Pictures 
Networks India. The Mumbai-based broadcaster said it will respond formally to 
the Securities and Exchange Board of India.

Dressed in a sharp black suit, gang leader Rehman Dakait swaggers onto a 
makeshift stage, leans into the microphone and addresses a rally. “
Assalamualaikum, Lyari <https://bloom.bg/3ZQzLjY>,” he says, as firecrackers 
explode and dust fills the air. In the packed Mumbai cinema hall, audiences 
erupt in whistles and cheers.

The rally scene, now a viral hit on social media, is from Dhurandhar, a spy 
thriller set between New Delhi and Karachi, Pakistan, that has become the 
highest-grossing Hindi-language film of all time. Set against decades of 
violence,Dhurandhar has topped Netflix charts on both sides of the border — 
even as it fuels a debate about nationalism and propaganda. Here’s how this one 
movie captured the zeitgeist <https://bloom.bg/40p8GEA>.
