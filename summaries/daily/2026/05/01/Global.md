The Strait of Hormuz impasse remains the single most consequential variable in global markets. President Trump affirmed the US naval blockade of Iranian ports will continue, stating "their economy is crashing, the blockade is incredible." WTI crude rose to approximately $105 per barrel with Brent near $112, representing a 12% weekly gain for WTI. The forward curve tells a more nuanced story: oil for delivery later in the year trades closer to $80, reflecting market expectations that the disruption is unlikely to persist indefinitely — though the timeline for any resolution remains genuinely uncertain. Iranian officials warned the blockade will continue pushing prices higher, and traders are increasingly pricing in an extended closure rather than a near-term diplomatic breakthrough.

The UAE's decision to exit OPEC adds a further structural dimension to the energy picture. The departure removes meaningful spare production capacity from the cartel's toolkit — the same firepower deployed in 2014 and 2020 to pressure higher-cost producers — while signaling that Abu Dhabi intends to pursue an independent, volume-oriented strategy once the Strait reopens. US shale producers, pumping near-record volumes of 13.6 million barrels per day, are well-positioned in the current price environment but are cautious about overextension given the forward curve's implied reversion. Exxon Mobil and Chevron both exceeded profit expectations, underpinned by elevated realized prices.

Ukraine's strikes on Russian energy infrastructure reached their highest monthly level since December, with at least 21 attacks on refineries, export terminals, and pipelines in April. The campaign appears designed partly to limit Moscow's ability to benefit financially from the Iran war-driven oil price rally. Meanwhile, G-7 monetary policy is converging toward tightening across most members — with the notable potential exception of the US — as central banks in Europe, Japan, and Canada all signal that persistent energy-price inflation may require a policy response. Singapore's Prime Minister Lawrence Wong captured the broader unease, warning in a May Day address that "the pace of change will be faster than anything we have seen before."
