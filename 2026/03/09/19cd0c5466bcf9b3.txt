Lengthening Arabian Nights

Meet the new boss,  same as the old boss <https://bloom.bg/40mIsDg>. Iran has 
chosen a new leader, and it’s Mojtaba Khamenei <https://bloom.bg/4rmXmEh>, son 
of the slain former supreme leader. This isn’t regime change, or the choice of 
a country about to surrender, and it contributed to a shocking open to trade in 
Asia. Brent crude is now above $110 per barrel, up 60% for the week, while in 
Japan, the Nikkei 225 is down 7% at the time of writing. Crude is at a 
four-year high:

Khamenei’s appointment could be critical in the longer term, but for markets, 
it’s the length of the war that now matters more than anything else. Who wins 
doesn’t matter — the goals remain unclear, and Iran is highly unlikely to 
emerge as a “victor” in any meaningful sense. The new supreme leader will impact
the “endgame” <https://bloom.bg/4uj5E2E> of the future governance of Iran, 
which could in turn have profound economic consequences long into the future 
(just as the Iranian revolution of 1979 has done).

But for now, prices rise and fall on the chances of a drawn-out conflict, and 
with it a protracted delay to the flow of oil from the region. Prediction 
markets, the flavor of the moment, see the chances of a swift conclusion as 
having ebbed swiftly over the last week, and now put a 48% probability on the 
notion that there still won’t be a ceasefire by the end ofnext month:

For a more positive outlook, Saudi Arabian stocks obviously stand to be 
grievously affected by a long war, and investors there seem optimistic. The 
market was closed on Friday and traded Sunday, by the end of which the main 
Saudi stock benchmark was higher than before war broke out:

That said, the weekend news that President Donald Trump requires  
“unconditional surrender” <https://bloom.bg/4uAN0nd> by Iran and that he’s 
contemplating sending in US special forces <https://bloom.bg/4bvuy7M> to 
capture Iranian uranium doesn’t soothe market nerves. In a telling reaction, 
bond yields are up across the world over the last week, even though they would 
normally be regarded as a safe haven. That’s almost certainly a response to the 
risk of a prolonged conflict and with it a lengthy stop to oil supplies:

Gauging how long the the conflict lasts depends primarily on both sides’ armed 
capabilities, and the US willingness to keep fighting (Iran is highly unlikely 
to give up while it has the means to fight). Andrew Bishop of Signum Global 
Advisors offers a long versus short war checklist, with factors pointing to a 
drawn-out conflict including:

 * Only 50% of missiles <https://bloom.bg/4upQTez> destroyed. 
 * Nuclear program mostly untouched. 
 * Incentive to keep bombing <https://bloom.bg/4uhfaTR> until the ‘right’ 
leader emerges. 
 * Drone attacks on Gulf don’t just continue; they’re getting worse.
Pointers to a short war include:

 * Only 120 launchers <https://bloom.bg/4bwNLG7> (or fewer 
<https://bloom.bg/4uhfbXV>) left. 
 * Trump admin keeps saying ‘ahead of schedule’; war ‘already won’; Iran 
‘already defeated.’ <https://bloom.bg/4rj28Cw> 
 * Trump admin working on insurance backstop solutions. 
 * Trump has said he’ll unilaterally define <https://bloom.bg/4aZ0b9z> 
capitulation.
These are the factors to watch in what will surely be another very difficult 
week for markets.

Far From Nourishing



The true cost of this war may not to be the billions spent on ammunition or 
advanced weapons systems. For average consumers, it’s about the price increases 
at the pumps or in groceries. Presently, US consumers arefacing gasoline prices 
at their highest <https://bloom.bg/4udXQ2h> in more than a year and a half amid 
the closure of the Strait of Hormuz.

Worse still, crop prices are on the rise with further room to surge should the 
war persist. The Iran situation is stalling access to the country’s low-cost 
urea and ammonia facilities, vital for agricultural fertilizers, which account 
for about 5% and 11% of global trade in fertilizers, respectively. This assumes 
much of their infrastructure survives the bombardment. So far, urea prices have 
shot up by about 25% since the outbreak of war, taking the commodity to levels 
not seen since Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, which disruptedboth grains and 
fertilizer <https://bloom.bg/4upQZTt> exports through the Black Sea region.

How does the latest supply crunch compare with the constraints experienced 
four years ago? Roughly 45% of global urea trade is sourced from producers with 
manufacturing sites in the Persian Gulf and shipped to major import regions, 
including India, Europe and Brazil, via the Strait of Hormuz. The disruption 
has created a mad rush for the critical nutrients <https://bloom.bg/4rkJASq>. 
The commodity is benefiting from an additional lift from rising prices of 
natural gas, a crucial element in fertilizer production:

How seriously should the ongoing global fertilizer choke point 
<https://bloom.bg/4rkJASq> be taken? Very seriously indeed. Bloomberg 
Intelligence notes that crop yields are tightly correlated (0.63) with 
increasing fertilizer consumption. Invariably, farmers’ inability to access, 
whether necessitated by increased costs or unavailability, points to only one 
thing — it’s only a matter of time before global food prices reflect the 
scarcity. So far, the surge in crop prices is reminiscent of the turbulent days 
of the Ukrainian invasion:

For the American consumer, this means the affordability crisis 
<https://bloom.bg/4bvsUTv> is likely to take a turn for the worse — exactly the 
opposite of what the Trump administration wants as midterm elections approach 
in November. Food prices, in particular, became a big issue in the last 
presidential election, and have appeared to be coming under control. A reversal 
would be unwelcome.

This shock arrived too late to show up in the consumer price index data for 
February, due on Wednesday. Oxford Economics’ Nancy Vanden Houten expects last 
month’s core inflation to have been benign. That is in line with the forecasts 
of economists polled by Bloomberg News, who expect a headline rate of 2.4%, the 
lowest in five years:

But the change in market expectations, as shown in inflation swaps, has 
already been dramatic:

Until war broke out, February’s inflation number seemed likely to open a 
useful window into the Federal Reserve’s monetary policy trajectory, helping 
investors gauge whether Kevin Warsh, Trump’s pick for chair, could deliver the 
promised rate cuts. That is cloudier now, although Vanden Houten’s outlook is 
unchanged:

The US/Israel war with Iran complicates things for Fed officials but doesn’t 
warrant a change to our forecast for the Fed to keep policy on hold until June, 
when we expect it to implement the first of the two rate cuts in our baseline.

Bloomberg’s World Interest Rates Probability, which shows the implicit policy 
rates discounted on the overnight index swaps market, suggest that investors 
see less policy easing since the war started:

This is startling because Friday brought news of frankly awful US unemployment 
numbers for February. Completely unaffected by the war, they showed payrolls 
falling as unemployment increased, in a big shock to expectations. Private 
sector payrolls saw their first decline in five years:

The Fed <https://bloom.bg/3OUJB25> has made clear that it’s strongly 
influenced by employment data, and it’s under pressure from the administration 
to cut rates — so the fact that the expected fed funds rate has risen in the 
last week shows that the war has turned market sentiment on inflation.

Prewar inflation <https://bloom.bg/4ui0Nyx> should still provide a useful 
baseline. The magnitude of the surge in February’s energy CPI after a decline 
in the prior month will be interesting, but with oil on the verge of shattering 
the $100-a-barrel ceiling, and rising 28% so far this month, the war’s impact 
on energy prices won’t be visible in March’s reading.

How devastating March proves to be will depend on how the conflict evolves, 
whether production in the Middle East can recover, and whether the Strait of 
Hormuz is deemed safe enough for normal traffic flows to resume. The escalation 
in hostilities over the weekend makes such a best-case scenario look 
increasingly unlikely. Carsten Brzeski at ING points out the ill-timing of this 
conflagration:

A second supply-side inflation shock, while the inflationary impact from 
tariffs is still unfolding, could make further rate cuts hard to justify, at 
least in the near term. At the same time, if the conflict drags on and 
uncertainty weighs on business investment and consumer confidence, the growth 
outlook darkens too.

January’s retail sales data <https://bloom.bg/4ruWOfK> provided the latest 
snapshot of a fragile consumer confidence. Consumers can ill-afford the price 
surges the war heaps on them without crushing under the weight. The cracks on 
the lower-income consumer front have been widening, with the Federal Reserve 
Bank of New York’s most recenthousehold debt and credit-card delinquency 
<https://bloom.bg/4uiQzOs> data indicating elevated consumer stress. 

The stimuli provided by artificial intelligence investment, and by rising tax 
refunds, haven’t gone away. But it’s growing clearer that a prolonged conflict 
would hit the US and world economies when they were looking vulnerable to 
stagflation.

—Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



This newsletter has been written from Mumbai, to the accompaniment of the 
noise of fireworks across the city as the locals celebrate the victory of 
India’s national team in the final of the T20 Cricket World Cup. The team is 
seriously good and deserving of this triumph; if possible, their victory is an 
even bigger deal thanBollywood <https://bloom.bg/4uxzkJA> around here.

As a lot of readers aren’t in the cricket-loving world, let me recommend 
watchinghighlights of the semifinal <https://bloom.bg/4bulrnM>, in which India 
beat England in probably the best game ever played in this format. People tend 
to findthe length of cricket games <https://bloom.bg/4unY3A2> off-putting, and 
don’t understand how anyone can be prepared to watch one for five days (the 
term over which international Test matches are played) with a risk that itends 
in a draw <https://bloom.bg/3OVMbF2>. But the T20 is a modern format, which 
lasts no longer than a typical baseball game, and in these parts attracts the
kind of crowd behavior <https://bloom.bg/4ufEjOM> you usually only associate 
with European or South American soccer. There’s an argument that this kind of 
cricket, in which batters blast for the fences, is about as valid as deciding a 
baseball championship with a home run derby. But tactics have developed, and 
it’s getting good. Give the England-India semifinal a taste. And have a good 
week everyone.
