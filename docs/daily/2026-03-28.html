Saturday, March 28, 2026

US

US equity markets enter the week's final session absorbing the compounding pressures of elevated energy costs and geopolitical uncertainty stemming from the now month-old US-Israel military operation against Iran. The airline sector is particularly exposed: United Airlines' CEO publicly warned of potential airfare increases of up to 20% as jet fuel costs rise. The warning is consistent with broader consumer price pressures building across the economy, though the magnitude and duration of any inflation pass-through remains contingent on how the conflict evolves. Energy Secretary Chris Wright has been at the center of a separate but related policy debate, having signaled prior to the Iran conflict that the US might withdraw from the International Energy Agency unless the body curtailed its net-zero modeling work. That posture now faces a more complicated political environment, as surging oil prices are reinforcing, for many allied governments, the strategic rationale for energy transition investments — even as Washington remains at odds with that consensus. On fiscal and political dynamics, the administration continues to navigate the foreign policy costs of the Iran engagement. Trump's war has generated notable friction with key allies, several of which have declined to express public enthusiasm for the military campaign and have been publicly rebuked by Washington in response. The domestic political calculus remains unclear: rising energy prices and allied estrangement represent real second-order risks, while the administration continues to send mixed signals about the conflict's intended scope and duration. Markets appear to be pricing some probability of further escalation, though options markets have not yet moved to levels consistent with acute crisis pricing. The interaction between energy inflation and the Federal Reserve's policy path is a mounting concern — any sustained commodity-driven inflation re-acceleration would complicate the rate trajectory at a time when the Fed has limited room to accommodate both growth support and price stability. ---

Asia

The economic shockwaves from the US-Iran conflict are landing with particular force across Asia, where dependence on Gulf energy supplies is structurally acute and fiscal buffers vary widely. South Korea has moved to implement crisis-level measures including a fuel-price cap, and its prime minister has explicitly invoked the need to prepare for "worst-case scenarios" — language that signals genuine institutional concern rather than routine contingency planning. Compounding Seoul's calculus is the reported redeployment of US air defense assets from the Korean Peninsula toward the Middle East, raising questions about the credibility of American security commitments in Northeast Asia at a moment of elevated regional tension. The Philippines presents a starker case. President Marcos has declared a national energy emergency, citing the risk that domestic aviation could be grounded entirely due to jet fuel shortages — a materially different order of concern from the airfare increases flagged by US carriers. The contrast is instructive: richer economies face price shocks, while more vulnerable ones face supply disruption. Marcos's response is geopolitically significant as well. Despite deepening military and diplomatic ties with Washington since 2022 — positioning Manila as a key US partner in the South China Sea — the energy crisis is now prompting Manila to explore collaboration with Beijing on oil and gas development in disputed waters, alongside a broader diplomatic re-engagement with China. This realignment, however tentative, reflects a wider pattern across the Indo-Pacific. The Iran war appears to be accelerating a reassessment among US allies of the costs and benefits of Washington alignment, particularly given Trump's simultaneous trade pressure, reduced regional security presence, and diplomatic abrasiveness. Hong Kong's re-emergence as a wealth management destination and China's positioning as a stable counterweight to US unpredictability — framed by some observers as Beijing casting itself as the "adult in the room" — suggest capital and political flows in the region may be gradually, if cautiously, reorienting. ---

Europe

Europe enters the weekend confronting a familiar but newly intensified energy vulnerability. The continent's previous exposure to Russian gas, substantially reduced since 2022 through a painful and costly transition toward US LNG and other alternatives, has inadvertently created a new form of dependence — this time on Washington's goodwill. Trump's envoy to the EU, Andrew Puzder, made that leverage explicit this week, warning that American LNG could be redirected to other buyers if Brussels fails to accelerate progress on a trade deal, or that terms "may not be as favorable." The European Parliament approved the trade agreement on March 26, though the longer-term implications of energy supply being instrumentalized as diplomatic leverage remain unsettling for European policymakers. Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jan Hatzius quantified the inflation risk in a "severely adverse" oil scenario, estimating that euro area headline inflation could rise by nearly 3 percentage points over the next year — the largest projected impact among major economies, driven by the dual exposure to crude oil and natural gas prices. The OECD separately concluded that the UK faces the largest output hit among G7 nations this year, a sobering assessment for a government already managing sluggish growth. Hatzius's analysts added a notable caveat: their estimates "may underestimate the nonlinear impact" of a severe gas supply shock — an acknowledgment that tail risks are not fully captured in central forecasts. Germany is responding with a characteristically structural approach, drafting contingency plans to counter US pressure — including the potential targeting of White House-aligned tech firms in any future trade dispute — while simultaneously implementing domestic energy support measures. German economist Isabella Weber has drawn a pointed historical parallel, comparing the current moment to the early weeks of 2020, when the Wuhan outbreak had signaled catastrophe but its full dimensions remained unclear. Whether other European economies have sufficient fiscal capacity to replicate Germany's support measures is an open and consequential question. ---

Global

The US-Iran conflict, now one month old, is functioning as a stress test for the post-Cold War architecture of US-led alliances, global energy markets, and the political economy of the energy transition simultaneously. Oil prices have surged globally — the commodity's fungibility means no economy is insulated — but the distribution of harm is highly unequal, shaped by import dependence, fiscal capacity, and currency strength. The gap between a US airline CEO warning of 20% fare increases and a Philippine president warning of grounded aircraft captures that distribution vividly. In energy markets, the conflict is reinforcing a structural argument that had been gaining traction before the war: that dependence on fossil fuel imports from politically volatile regions carries security costs that renewables and electrification can partly mitigate. European Commission Executive Vice-President Teresa Ribera made the point explicitly this week, arguing that the disruption of global fossil fuel markets would prove "a very relevant driver of the green economy." Early data suggests the conflict is accelerating consumer uptake of electric vehicles, solar panels, and other low-carbon technologies in several markets — a feedback dynamic that, if sustained, could compress the long-run demand outlook for Gulf producers even as near-term prices remain elevated. Geopolitically, the war is reshaping alliance calculus in ways that may prove durable. Nations that had pivoted away from China and Russia toward deeper US alignment are now reassessing that strategy under the combined pressures of trade tariffs, energy disruption, and reduced security commitments. Turkey's $8 billion drawdown of gold reserves has added a further wrinkle to commodity markets. Forecasters acknowledge significant uncertainty: the conflict's trajectory remains genuinely unclear, Washington continues to send mixed signals about scope and duration, and the nonlinear risks — to energy infrastructure, shipping chokepoints, and confidence — are by definition difficult to model. The honest assessment is that the range of outcomes remains wide, and the current market pricing may not fully reflect the left tail.
