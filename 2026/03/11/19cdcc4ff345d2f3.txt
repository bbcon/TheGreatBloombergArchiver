Globalization Is Still Here, New Report Shows
A new report shows global goods trade is proving resilient to protectionism 
and wars
Read in browser 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-11/globalization-is-proving-resilient>

<https://sli.bloomberg.com/click?s=1149074&li=17233013&m=11b724c487f61753eb1a7a4a5f3ef261&p=03112026&lctg=6689f0ec-2ca1-4687-9972-bdcb0c2dfbdf&stpe=default>
Walloped by the pandemic, protectionism and regional wars, globalization is 
proving more durable than predicted.

That’s among the main conclusions in the latest DHL Global Connectedness 
Tracker 
<https://www.dhl.com/global-en/microsites/core/global-connectedness/tracker.html>
, an annual report produced by the Germany-based parcel delivery giant and New 
York University’s Stern School of Business.

“A reversal of globalization is a risk but not a current reality,” the report 
said.

Released this week and based on research conducted before the US and Israel 
attacked Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/us-says-it-s-escalating-war-on-iran-dampening-chance-for-talks?cmpid=BBD031126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 on Feb. 28, it offers a counter-narrative to politicians and analysts arguing 
that economic disengagement and the formation of new, distinct geopolitical 
blocs are happening in any seismic way.

Read More: Hormuz Disruptions Will Hit Food Prices as Well as Oil, UN Warns 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/hormuz-disruptions-in-iran-war-will-hit-food-prices-not-just-oil-un?cmpid=BBD031126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>

Just 4%-6% of goods trade, greenfield foreign-direct investment and 
cross-border mergers-and-acquisitions “have shifted away from geopolitical 
rivals over the past decade,” the report said. “Trade flows shifted more toward 
neutral countries than to close allies, implying more ‘de-risking’ than 
‘friend-shoring.’”

It also questioned whether the push to relocate supply chains closer to home 
was catching on.

“Goods trade and greenfield FDI crossed their longest average distances on 
record in 2025, while the shares of these flows occurring within major 
geographic regions fell to new lows,” the report said. “It remains to be seen 
whether near-shoring strategies will ultimately lead to more regionalized 
business patterns.”

Looking ahead, the DHL-NYU Stern report said global goods trade should hold up 
pretty well against President Donald Trump’stariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/us-tells-tariff-refund-judge-it-can-t-comply-with-court-order?cmpid=BBD031126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
. For the next four years, total volumes are expected to increase by an annual 
average of 2.6% — in line with actual rates achieved over the past decade.

Read More: US and China Flip the Global Script as Capital Flows Reverse 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-11/us-china-capital-flows-reverse-as-global-economic-order-shifts?cmpid=BBD031126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>

“Global flows of trade, capital, information, and people are not retreating — 
they are holding firm,” the report authored by Steven Altman and Caroline 
Bastian said.

“A key factor behind the expected resilience of trade growth is the relatively 
modest role the US plays in global trade — accounting for just 13% of global 
goods imports and 9% of exports” from January through September 2025, the 
report concluded. “And the fact that other countries have not followed the US 
on its current path of across-the-board tariff increases.”
