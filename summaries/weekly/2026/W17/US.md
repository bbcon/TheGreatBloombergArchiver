**Equity Markets and Sentiment Divergence**

US equity markets navigated a volatile week that opened on a cautious note — S&P 500 futures fell approximately 0.5% on Monday following a turbulent weekend in the Strait of Hormuz — before resuming their remarkable April advance. The index closed above 7,000 for the first time last week, capping a 12.33% gain over just 14 trading days, a pace of appreciation seen only four times this century. By Thursday, however, the rally showed signs of fatigue, with futures off roughly 0.5% as Brent crude pushed above $100 per barrel. Friday brought a technology-led recovery: Intel surged approximately 20% in premarket trading on a stronger-than-expected AI-driven revenue forecast, AMD rose 7.2%, and Arm gained 6.8%, putting the Nasdaq 100 on course for a fourth consecutive weekly gain. The rally's technical foundations, however, remain open to question — fewer than half of S&P 500 members traded above their 50-day moving averages at the index's record, and volume has run 11% below the six-month average, with investor sentiment indicators declining even as the index advanced.

**Fed Chair Confirmation and Monetary Policy**

Kevin Warsh's Senate Banking Committee confirmation hearing on Tuesday and Wednesday dominated domestic policy attention. The nominee pledged Federal Reserve independence repeatedly while simultaneously signaling comfort with the administration's "economic statecraft" agenda, describing a Fed role in supporting dollar stability through the Bessent-Rubio framework. More substantively, Warsh expressed a preference for narrower Fed mandates, a smaller balance sheet, and overhauling inflation measurement toward trimmed-mean gauges — the Dallas Fed's trimmed PCE currently reads 2.33% versus 3.0% for core PCE, a 70 basis-point gap that would materially shift the perceived distance from the 2% target if adopted as the policy benchmark. His confirmation remains complicated by Senator Thom Tillis's hold, tied to the Justice Department's investigation of incumbent Jerome Powell over Fed renovation expenditures, creating a scenario in which Powell could remain beyond his May 15 term expiry.

**Corporate Earnings and the Technology-AI Nexus**

First-quarter earnings provided broadly constructive signals, with nearly 80% of reporting S&P 500 companies beating estimates. Tesla delivered $0.41 per share against a $0.34 consensus on Wednesday, but gave back after-hours gains after guiding capital expenditure to $25 billion this year — $5 billion above prior guidance — as investors signaled a preference for automotive clarity over unquantified AI and robotics ventures. Texas Instruments surged 12% Thursday on a stronger-than-expected data-center-driven forecast. ServiceNow fell 13% after cutting its margin outlook, citing deal delays attributable to the Middle East conflict. Meta and Microsoft announced combined workforce reductions of as many as 23,000 positions, framed by markets as AI cost discipline rather than a demand signal. JPMorgan raised its year-end S&P 500 target to 7,600. Goldman Sachs separately estimated US potential GDP growth at approximately 2.3% annually through 2028, underpinned by AI productivity gains of around 2% per year.

**Political Economy and Fiscal Policy**

The administration's tariff posture generated a notable development: Trump signaled that companies seeking refunds from the Supreme Court-ordered repayment of struck-down IEEPA tariffs — a process potentially totaling more than $160 billion — might face implicit political consequences for pursuing those claims. Trump also invoked the Defense Production Act to direct federal funds toward energy infrastructure including coal, LNG, and petroleum. A US Army soldier was criminally charged with using classified intelligence on the timing of Nicolás Maduro's capture to trade on prediction markets, marking the first application of the so-called Eddie Murphy Rule by the CFTC. Consumer sentiment fell to an all-time low this month as households expressed concern about war-driven inflation, with gasoline prices approaching four-year highs — a growing tension with equity market performance that several analysts view as a meaningful risk to corporate earnings power.
