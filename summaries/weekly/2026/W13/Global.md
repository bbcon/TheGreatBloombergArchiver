**The Conflict's Unresolved Trajectory**
The US-Israel military operation against Iran, now entering its fourth week, remained the organizing fact of global markets throughout the period. The week opened with Trump extending a 48-hour ultimatum on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure by five days, following what the administration described as diplomatic contact — a move that provided temporary relief before overnight Iranian missile and drone strikes on Eilat, Dimona, Tel Aviv, US regional bases, and Gulf Arab infrastructure reasserted the conflict's momentum. By Thursday, Trump had extended the pause by a further ten days to April 6, citing negotiating progress, though the talks lack clarity on interlocutors given the deaths of multiple senior Iranian officials. Iran publicly rejected US overtures, continued missile exchanges, and its parliament is reportedly drafting legislation to impose passage fees on Hormuz transit — an arrangement that would amount to de facto toll extraction on global shipping rather than a genuine reopening.

**Energy Markets at a Structural Inflection**
Brent crude held near $108–$113 per barrel through the week and is on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990. BloombergNEF's base case sees Brent averaging $106.70 in April; a delayed Hormuz reopening until mid-June would push that to $154.40. Macquarie warned of a path toward $200 in a prolonged closure scenario. Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — the world's largest — has sustained damage that could take years to repair. Iran stopped natural gas exports to Turkey following an Israeli strike on the South Pars gas field. The IEA estimated the cumulative supply disruption as equivalent to the combined effect of the two 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis — a framing its executive director delivered in Canberra on Monday. Dallas Fed modeling suggests a Strait closure lasting through June would reduce global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points next quarter. Paradoxically, Iran appears a net financial beneficiary: as the only major exporter able to utilize the strait freely, Tehran has reportedly earned hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental oil revenue since hostilities began.

**Commodities Beyond Oil**
The conflict's commodity footprint is broadening in ways that extend well beyond crude. Urea, the world's most widely used nitrogen fertilizer, rose to its highest level in more than three years as energy-linked production costs surged and export restrictions proliferated. Fertilizer supply chains — both nitrogen and phosphate — face meaningful disruption, with implications for global food production that may not yet be fully reflected in agricultural commodity pricing. Gold posted its worst weekly decline since 1983 — down roughly 10.5% — a counterintuitive move for a traditional safe haven that reflects a dash for liquidity, dollar strength, and the collapse of the "debasement" trade as real rate expectations rose. From its January intraday peak above $5,000 per ounce to its trough this week, gold fell approximately 27% — its worst multi-week stretch in over a decade — before recovering modestly on ceasefire headlines. Turkey sold approximately $8 billion — around 60 tons — of gold reserves this month to defend its currency, a pace larger than concurrent ETF outflows and a potential challenge to the central bank net-buying narrative that underpinned the metal's post-2022 advance.

**Dollar, Alliances, and Petrodollar Questions**
The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on pace for its largest monthly gain since July, approximately 2%, reflecting haven demand and the repricing of Fed easing. JPMorgan strategists turned bullish on the dollar for the first time in a year; speculative positioning flipped from the most net-short in five years to net-long. The stronger dollar compounds the oil shock for energy-importing emerging economies by raising local-currency import costs. Reports that Iran is permitting Hormuz passage for vessels paying for oil in yuan prompted Deutsche Bank to flag the conflict as a potential catalyst for petrodollar erosion — a scenario uncertain in magnitude but significant in its long-run monetary ramifications. The geopolitical realignment underway is equally consequential: nations that had deepened US alignment are reassessing that posture under the simultaneous pressures of trade tariffs, energy disruption, and reduced security commitments. European Commission Vice-President Ribera framed the disruption explicitly as a driver of accelerated energy transition, and early consumer data — EV queues in the Philippines and Indonesia, rooftop solar uptake in India — suggests structural demand destruction for fossil fuels may be occurring faster than pre-conflict projections anticipated.

**Institutional Forecasters Begin to Respond**
The OECD was the first major institution to formally revise its global outlook, leaving its 2026 growth projection unchanged at 2.9% while trimming 2027 by 0.1 percentage point and raising G20 average inflation to 4.0% from 2.8% in December. The institution warned that "monetary policy adjustments may be needed if price pressures broaden." Central banks globally are pivoting away from rate cuts: the ECB is explicitly in wait-and-see mode, the Bank of England faces market pricing of hikes, and the Fed is in a holding pattern. The honest institutional assessment, captured by German economist Isabella Weber's comparison to the early weeks of 2020, is that the conflict's full dimensions remain unclear — the range of outcomes is wide, and current market pricing may not fully reflect left-tail risks.
