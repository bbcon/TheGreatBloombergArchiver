Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Energy Team Leader Stephen Stapczynski explains why a wartime supply shock 
is jeopardizing the long-term future of LNG in emerging economies.

This was meant to be a breakout year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-08/global-lng-market-faces-looming-supply-glut-after-years-of-scarcity?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 for liquefied natural gas — a wave of new supply at affordable prices winning 
long-term customers in emerging economies.

Not any more.

The war in the Middle East has throttled a fifth of global supply, with 
shipments via the Strait of Hormuz still blocked after seven weeks. The world’s 
biggest LNG export plant in Qatar is counting the cost ofmissile and drone 
strikes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-19/iran-war-strikes-on-qatar-s-lng-crown-jewel-reshape-the-future-of-gas?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/video-how-the-iran-war-triggered-a-global-natural-gas-shock>
A year that began by promising the most dramatic surge in LNG exports since 
2019 could end with a decline in output, according to some analysts’ forecasts.

The squeeze is already hitting Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/iran-war-pushes-asia-to-think-twice-before-doubling-down-on-lng?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 — and hard. LNG flows have slumped to the lowest since 2020 on a 30-day moving 
average, forcing buyers to curb consumption just as demand was meant to 
accelerate.

While prices have receded in recent days, thanks largely to demand 
destruction, they remain 50% higher than before the war.

The longer-term damage may be more profound.

A second price shock in just four years, after Russia’s invasion of Ukraine, 
is persuading governments across Asia to build supply chains that don’t rely on 
LNG.

Vietnam is reconsidering a gas-fired power project in favor of renewables 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/vietnam-lng-power-project-eyes-green-pivot-on-soaring-gas-prices?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
. Pakistan, grappling withextensive blackouts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/pakistan-faces-extensive-blackouts-as-gas-shortfall-worsens?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
, is scrambling to revive its own gas production, while Malaysia’s state-owned 
energy giant willreinvest 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/iran-war-pushes-asia-to-think-twice-before-doubling-down-on-lng?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 a windfall from higher oil prices into its domestic gas fields.

China offers perhaps the clearest signal. Burned by past shortages, Beijing 
has spent years diversifying its energy mix so that, although LNG imports are 
down 25% from last year, the country isn’t facing a deficit. Its consumers can 
lean on domestic gas, pipeline imports and evenrecord coal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-10/china-could-lift-coal-output-this-year-due-to-indonesian-curbs?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 production.

For most of its history, LNG has been a premium fuel, confined largely to 
wealthier nations able to absorb the cost and build the necessary 
infrastructure. Big Oil later pitched it as a bridge foremerging economies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-04-03/shift-in-lng-demand-to-developing-nations-is-making-market-more-price-sensitive?cmpid=BBBXT041726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260417>
 — cleaner than coal and flexible enough to backstop intermittent renewables.

But that proposition hinges on low prices and rock-solid reliability. The 
longer disruptions persist, the faster these nations will seek alternatives to 
meet their growing energy needs.

— Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Artificial intelligence data centers have created new incentives for the 
adoption of battery energy storage systems in the US, according to 
BloombergNEF. On-site use-cases could add 19.8 gigawatt-hours of capacity 
annually, equating to a 27% to 30% increase to average annual demand of 66 
gigawatt-hours for BESS in the US over the period 2026-2030.
