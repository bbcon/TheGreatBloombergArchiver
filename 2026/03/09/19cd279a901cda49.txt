Springing into Action

weight-and-watch stance to action mode as oil shot above $100 a barrel.

The Week Ahead

A pair of inflation reports, including the Federal Reserve’s preferred price 
gauge, surface in the coming week after a dismal February jobs report that 
challenged perceptions the labor market is stabilizing.

The consumer price index report on Wednesday is projected to show a core 
inflation measure, which strips out volatile food and energy costs, rose just 
0.2% in February. That would suggest some easing in price pressures before the 
outbreak of the war in Iran introduced new uncertainty about the inflation 
outlook.

Data out Friday, will likely paint a picture of more stubborn inflation. 
Economists see the Fed’s favored core personal consumption expenditures price 
index up 0.4% again in January. Compared with the same month last year, the 
median forecast calls for a 3% increase — little changed from the end of 2024.

Elsewhere, Japanese wage numbers, UK gross domestic product, and Mexican 
consumer prices may be among the highlights.

Need-to-Know Research

Global capital spending saw a major upswing last year, with the biggest 
advance — excluding China (for data availability reasons) — since 2017, 
according to JPMorgan Chase calculations. In other words, the strongest since 
much of the industrial world was bouncing out of a mini 2015-16 recession.

It was “a tale of two sectors,” Maia Crook and Bruce Kasman wrote in a note 
last week: there was a boom in technology investment, and a stalling in the 
rest of the business world. Some of that reflects arotation in demand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-10-24/ai-data-center-boom-threatens-trump-s-manufacturing-revival?cmpid=BBD030926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
 from non-tech areas toward AI projects companies think will generate higher 
returns, they wrote. “But we believe non-tech spending was held back by an 
independent factor: businesses’ caution amid trade war uncertainty.”

The 2025 world capital-spending total was up 7% excluding China, and JPMorgan 
sees the potential for a 5% to 7% gain this year. While the Middle East war 
poses a risk, “there is a building case for an acceleration in non-tech 
spending to combine with still strong tech demand to deliver” another strong 
year, Crook and Kasman said.
