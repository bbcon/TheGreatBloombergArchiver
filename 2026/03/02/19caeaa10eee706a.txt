Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Grant Smith looks at the oil market impact from the escalating Middle 
East war.

The conflict roiling Iran and the wider Middle East has turbocharged a shift 
in global oil markets, from signs that supplies are excessive to concerns about 
their availability.

Even before the weekend’s tumultuous US-Israeli strikes on the Islamic 
Republic and Tehran’s retaliation around the region, the crude market saw 
surprising strength in the first weeks of 2026.

While leading forecasters projected a substantial glut 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-global-oil-supply-prices/?cmpid=BBBXT030226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 for this year, the overhang has been pared by winter storms in North America, 
outages in Kazakhstan, sanctions on Russia and Iran and China’s undimmed 
appetite to shunt barrels into storage.

The US-led military operation marks the latest and most significant oil market 
upheaval 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/oil-traders-rush-to-hedge-iran-risk-after-wild-start-to-year?cmpid=BBBXT030226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 triggered by President Donald Trump, whose interventions in Venezuela — and 
now Iran — have deposed the leaders of two OPEC nations in as many months.

Middle East oil production is so far unaffected, but energy infrastructure and 
logistics have been hit.

Tanker traffic in the Strait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/oil-s-worst-nightmare-is-here-as-gulf-conflict-hits-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT030226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, through which oil flows from the Persian Gulf to international customers, has 
largely halted. Saudi Arabia has suspended operations at itsRas Tanura refinery 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/saudi-arabia-s-ras-tanura-refinery-shuts-down-after-drone-attack?cmpid=BBBXT030226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 following a drone strike in the area, and Qatar has suspended liquefied 
natural gas output.

Brent crude futures, up 19% in the first two months of 2026, have soared 
further to briefly surpass $80 a barrel. European gas prices have risen even 
more sharply.

On Sunday, the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-28/opec-to-consider-bigger-hike-after-iran-strike-delegate-says?cmpid=BBBXT030226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 and its partners made a modest gesture to stabilize the market, agreeing a 
small production increase for April. Some members, like Saudi Arabia and the 
United Arab Emirates, have already started to bolster exports.

But any extra volumes will count for little if tankers can’t transit the 
Strait of Hormuz.

Prior to this weekend’s events, projections from the International Energy 
Agency and other forecasters indicated that world markets should remain on 
track for a sizable surplus — a comfort that may temper further price rallies.

Nonetheless, the Iran crisis seems almost certain to further push the focus of 
oil traders and refiners from dealing with a surfeit of barrels to wondering 
how safe they are.

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Pipeline gas trade disruptions in the Middle East due to the widening war 
could raise spot liquefied natural gas demand from Egypt and potentially Turkey.
BloombergNEF <bbg://news/stories/TB9I73KK3NYB> expects the increase from Egypt
