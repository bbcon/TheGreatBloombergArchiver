The next stranded assets?

By Alastair Marsh <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AQvVwWuhxcQ/alastair-marsh>

In an age dominated by war, inflation and AI, investors may be underestimating 
the risks now associated withrenewable energy assets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/barclays-bankers-warn-distressed-valuations-can-hit-clean-energy?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
.

That’s according to a group of bankers at Barclays, who just published a paper 
laying out their views on the future of the clean energy transition.

“The classic stranded-asset story focused on fossil fuels, but what we are now 
seeing is stranded-like outcomes also emerging for renewables,” Daniel Hanna, 
group head of sustainable and transition finance and a contributing author to 
the paper, said in an interview.

He says risks are now arising for renewables where “systems integration 
fails.” And if clean energy can’t connect to the grid, it starts to risk 
obsolescence.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/barclays-bankers-warn-distressed-valuations-can-hit-clean-energy>
Despite record investments in renewable energy, Barclays notes that global 
fossil-fuel consumption has never been higher. And with an escalating military 
conflict in the Middle East, the price of oil and gas is once again soaring. 
The development highlights that having secure and affordable access to energy 
continues to be a bigger priority globally than reducing emissions.

Valuations will therefore depend on how efficiently an energy source can feed 
into distribution systems, according to the Barclayspaper 
<https://home.barclays/content/dam/home-barclays/documents/news/Insights/Barclays%20Transition%20Realism%20White%20Paper%20February%202026.pdf>
, whose main author wasNiall Mac Dowell <https://profiles.imperial.ac.uk/niall>
, the bank’s head of climate technology and a professor of energy systems at 
Imperial University.

The observation shouldn’t be seen as a critique of renewables, but as a 
reflection of what happens when energy generation of any type moves fast and 
“the grid doesn’t keep up,” said Hanna.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/barclays-bankers-warn-distressed-valuations-can-hit-clean-energy>
A customer tries to use his mobile phone during a power outage in Spain
Photographer: Brais Lorenzo/Bloomberg
Renewables are regularly hampered by long queues 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/hundreds-of-uk-clean-power-projects-face-fresh-grid-delays?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 to connect to the grid, and by a system in which grid operators often have to
pay 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-01-07/uk-set-to-spend-1-8-billion-as-wind-power-chokes-grid?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 wind farms toshut down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2024-uk-wind-farms-overstate-output/?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 when excess power is generated. Barclays says such a dynamic opens the door to 
“a new class of distressed energy transition opportunities in under-connected 
renewables.”

It’s a development that’s been percolating for a while now.

While solar and wind will “undoubtedly” be the stars of the energy transition 
in the long run, new renewables capacity isuseless without the infrastructure 
<https://about.bnef.com/insights/clean-energy/a-power-grid-long-enough-to-reach-the-sun-is-key-to-the-climate-fight/>
 to transport that clean electricity from where it’s produced to the end user, 
BloombergNEF noted as far back as 2023. In December last year, BNEFsaid 
<bbg://news/stories/T6LHM7KIP3IZ> grids remain a bottleneck with queues for new 
demand “lengthening rapidly” across the US and Europe.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/barclays-bankers-warn-distressed-valuations-can-hit-clean-energy>
Solar panels near electrical transformers in PolandPhotographer: Damian 
Lemanski/Bloomberg
In its 2025 renewables report 
<https://iea.blob.core.windows.net/assets/76ad6eac-2aa6-4c55-9a55-b8dc0dba9f9e/Renewables2025.pdf>
, the International Energy Agency noted that issues with distribution, 
transmission and interconnection capacity have curtailed the pace at which new 
renewable power plants can be connected to the grid, threatening project 
delays, higher costs and reduced auction participation.

For investors, these risks can be translated into opportunities to generate 
market-beating returns, according to the Barclays report.

“Where you are able to identify these bottlenecks and provide mechanisms to 
ease them, that will then create alpha,” said Hanna, who noted the report 
doesn’t reflect the bank’s house view but that of the authors.

Oil shock

By Bloomberg Economics 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-03/iran-war-oil-price-surge-put-global-economic-recovery-at-risk?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>

President Donald Trump’s war with Iran threatens to deal a severe blow to a 
global economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-03/iran-war-oil-price-surge-put-global-economic-recovery-at-risk?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 still grappling with the impact of his historic tariff hike.

For Europe, sustained higher energy prices would take the economy to the brink 
of recession. For the US, they would place the Federal Reserve in an impossible 
position — stuck between a war that pushes inflation higher and a president 
demanding that interest rates come down. For China, the end of discounted 
Iranian oil imports adds to strain from Trump’s tariffs and a real estate 
collapse.

In the first days of the fighting, the intensity is high and the endgame 
uncertain. Bloomberg Economics has modeled scenarios for what lies ahead, and 
what they mean for oil prices, major economies, and the future of Iran.

It is, of course, possible that Washington and Tehran find an off-ramp, oil 
settles back at its pre-escalation average of $65 a barrel, and the global 
economy dodges a blow.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-03/iran-war-oil-price-surge-put-global-economic-recovery-at-risk>
The latest signs, though, suggest there’s worse to come. Saudi Arabia’s 
largest oil refinery 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/saudi-arabia-s-ras-tanura-refinery-shuts-down-after-drone-attack?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 is closed. Qatar hasshuttered 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/european-gas-rallies-more-than-30-as-qatar-halts-lng-production?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 the world’s biggest liquefied natural gas facility. The Strait of Hormuz is
effectively paralyzed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-2?cmpid=BBD030426_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
. Oil and gas prices have already rocketed higher. Stocks have taken a hit. 
Treasury yields have risen as traders curb bets on Fed cuts.

About 20% of global oil supply passes through the Strait. Drawing on academic 
studies and the experience of past supply outages, Bloomberg Economics 
estimates that a 1% drop in supply pushes prices up by about 4%.

That suggests a prolonged closure of the Strait would raise prices by 80% from 
pre-war levels – taking them to around $108 a barrel. In a severe scenario, 
Bloomberg Economics assumes prices would stay that high into the fourth quarter 
of the year.

Sharply higher oil prices impact the economy through multiple channels. They 
add to costs for consumers and businesses, reducing spending power and taking a 
chunk out of growth. They also add to inflation, pushing up prices for 
transport, and anything with petrochemicals as an input.

 in the US and elsewhere under four scenarios modeled by Bloomberg Economics.
