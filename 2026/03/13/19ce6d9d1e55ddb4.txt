Trump Flatterers Privately Fuming at Iran War
America’s allies are increasingly anxious about the Trump’s escalating war in 
the Middle East

From Asia to the Gulf and most of the European Union, the strategy adopted by 
America’s allies for dealing with Donald Trump has been to praise and flatter 
him in the hope that he eventually plays nice.

As the US president’s war on Iran drags on 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/trump-warns-iran-to-watch-what-happens-as-war-hits-two-week-mark?cmpid=BBD031326_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, it is (with very few exceptions) no different.

Almost nobody dares call Trump out 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/why-spain-s-pm-pedro-sanchez-is-standing-up-to-trump-on-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031326_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
. Both in public and private, many have commended and even thanked him for his 
actions in Iran, according to multiple officials briefed on those conversations.

Any questioning of the lack of answers on the war’s goals and endgame has been 
timid.

But in discussions among themselves, many of those same governments are 
nervous, if not fuming, the officials say.

One Gulf leader told a European counterpart on a recent call that they 
privately despaired at what Trump was doing and thought he didn’t have a plan 
but did not want to communicate those concerns directly to the Americans.

A person close to a western European leader said they were furious with the 
likely impact ofTrump’s war on the global economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-13?cmpid=BBD031326_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 and on security but argued little would be gained by expressing that publicly. 
Europeans are particularly riled by the US decision toease sanctions on Russian 
oil 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/us-treasury-gives-green-light-for-sale-of-more-russian-oil?cmpid=BBD031326_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

With Trump and Iran’s new supreme leader refusing to back down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/trump-warns-iran-to-watch-what-happens-as-war-hits-two-week-mark?cmpid=BBD031326_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, oil prices remain stubbornly high at around $100 a barrel as disruption 
continues.

The consequences in Europe, the Gulf and much of Asia could be lasting and 
profound if the war continues for many more weeks.

The tactics of allies toward Trump aren’t new. They’ve tried to cajole him on 
trade, on Russia over the war against Ukraine, and to influence his dealings in 
the Middle Eastor with China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/trump-xi-agenda-to-be-mapped-out-as-negotiators-meet-in-paris?cmpid=BBD031326_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
.

Success has been scant, the lessons learned few.

The difference now is the stakes are rapidly getting higher. World leaders are 
increasingly anxious that Trump may be driving them all toward a precipice. — 
Alberto Nardelli 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AU7VEMIerb0/alberto-nardelli> and Alex 
Wickham <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AV1oEPH6RqA/alex-wickham>
