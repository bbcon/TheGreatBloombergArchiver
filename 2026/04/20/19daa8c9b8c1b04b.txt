With US negotiators heading to Pakistan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/pakistan-gets-ready-to-host-second-round-of-us-iran-talks?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 again to hash out a deal to either extend a ceasefire with Iran or the terms 
of a more enduring peace, the world again is pondering President Donald Trump’s 
deal-making prowess.

The view of Trump’s supporters is, of course, that the president is a master 
deal maker.Every escalation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/iran-war-deepens-china-s-dependence-on-the-us-for-niche-gas?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 or threat, his backers say, is about leverage on the way to an agreement that 
will serve American interests. In Trump’s world everything is up for 
negotiation. The process may even matter more than the end result.

But after almost a decade of watching Trump do deals on behalf of the American 
people, it’s worth at the very least questioning how durable anything Trump 
negotiates ever is? And what that tells us about what to expect?

Hormuz Latest: Gunfire, U-Turns, Threats Mark a Chaotic Weekend in the Strait 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/gunfire-u-turns-and-threats-mark-a-chaotic-weekend-in-hormuz?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>

It would be unseemly to treat Trump’s Iran war as an analog of his trade wars 
given the death and devastation over the past two months. Or to liken the 
threat a nuclear Iran would pose to the rest of the world to economic disputes. 
There is also a very real possibility that theenergy shock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-18/the-jet-fuel-surge-is-making-global-flight-connections-disappear?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 provoked by the Iran war and the continuing closure of the Strait of Hormuz 
will end up being more devastating economically than any of Trump’s tariffs or 
other gambits.

Still, the most obvious place to study Trump’s negotiating work is in his 
trade deals. Of which there are actually fewer than you think, or Trump claims.

The economist Richard Baldwin offered his take 
<https://www.linkedin.com/pulse/trumps-conduct-trade-war-what-lessons-his-real-richard-baldwin-jrfae?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_ios&utm_campaign=share_via>
 over the weekend on what lessons Trump’s trade negotiations over the years 
might offer for an Iran process. His basic conclusion is that almost all 
Trump’s actions end in a retreat reframed as a victory.

In Baldwin’s analysis all Trump conflicts start with a small provocation meant 
to test the waters, escalate and then move rapidly toward retreat when it 
becomes apparent that the domestic political costs to Trump have risen. Trump, 
Baldwin writes, likes to “use negotiations and ‘napkin deals’ as a smokescreen.”

Read More: Iran Wavers on Peace Talks After US Seizes Ship 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/iran-wavers-on-peace-talks-as-tensions-rise-after-us-seizes-ship?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>

Which is especially easy to do, Baldwin says, if you follow three simple 
rules: “Avoid writing things down. Don’t sign anything. Claim that everything 
is provisional and time-limited.”

“Critically important for your understanding of Trumpian policy is the fact 
that the shocking moves are less about standard policy objectives and more 
about making the President look good to his political base,” Baldwin concludes.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/hormuz-at-standstill-denting-us-iran-peace-deal-hopes>
None of what Baldwin describes adds up to durability for the deals Trump does 
negotiate. Few of the trade pacts his administration has negotiated have 
actually become permanent rather than “frameworks” for discussions. In fact, 
threatening torip up deals 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/trump-tariff-rates-could-be-restored-by-early-july-bessent?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 is what Trump seems to be turning to since the Supreme Court in February ruled 
the majority of the tariffs he has threatened in his second term are illegal.

Trump’s biggest trade deal, the North American pact with Canada and Mexico he 
renegotiated and rebranded during his first term, is up for renewal this 
summer. And already Trump is using the threat to pull out as leverage.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/us-weighs-tougher-auto-import-rules-to-accelerate-reshoring?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>

His displeasure with the UK’s Keir Starmer and the British reluctance to 
support Trump’s war with Iran hasled to threats 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/bessent-and-uk-s-reeves-at-odds-over-iran-war-ahead-of-meeting?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 to at the very least amend a pact negotiated just last year.

A first-term deal with China was labelled “phase one” and yet it’s hard to 
point to concrete progress since. When Trump heads to China to meet with Xi 
Jinping next month, the expectations are low. Commercial deals such as plane or 
chip orders, discussions about investments, and the extension of a ceasefire 
seem likely. But a lasting commercial treaty? That seems like a distant 
prospect. At this point the talks are about managing the economic rivalry 
rather than resolving substantive disagreements.

Just what will emerge from any Iran talks this week is unclear for now. The 
distances between the two sides on key issues like Iran’s nuclear ambitions 
remain significant and seem unlikely to be resolved quickly. Last week’s 
announcement of a reopening of the Strait of Hormuzrang hollow 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/hormuz-traffic-at-standstill-as-us-vessel-seizure-widens-risk?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 over the weekend as the US seized an Iranian cargo ship and Iranians were 
accused of firing at others.

Talking is better than shooting, though. And maybe that points to what CEOs, 
investors and everyday people in a global economyalready suffering 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/us-energy-chief-says-gasoline-may-not-dip-below-3-til-next-year?cmpid=BBD042026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 from the shock of war will have to learn to adapt to: A conflict that takes a 
back seat to negotiations and a deal that may never materialize, or last if it 
does.
