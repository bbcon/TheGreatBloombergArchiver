bail on clean tech (or at least view it with some skepticism). But investment 
bank Jefferies is taking a different view, telling its clients to keep betting 
on renewables.

Stay the course?

By Alastair Marsh <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AQvVwWuhxcQ/alastair-marsh>, 
Gautam Naik <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AVyoWbluWhQ/gautam-naik> and Olivia 
Raimonde <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AUdOpjWq8Dk/olivia-raimonde>

For green investors, the current war-fueled surge in oil and gas prices 
conjures up painful memories of 2022, when renewables lurched into a selloff 
that lasted three years. But at Jefferies, clients are being urged todouble 
down on a bold upbeat call 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/green-investors-told-to-sit-tight-through-war-fueled-turmoil?cmpid=BBD030926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
.

Last year, Jefferies started telling green investors they were entering the “
glory days 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-02/green-investors-enjoy-huge-returns-that-defy-trump-attacks?cmpid=BBD030926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
” of a strategy that had long seemed like a losing bet. That advice ended up 
coinciding with a 44% surge in clean energy stocks in 2025, trouncing the 16% 
gain in the S&P 500 Index.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/green-investors-told-to-sit-tight-through-war-fueled-turmoil>
Construction workers at a solar farm in the UKPhotographer: Chris 
Ratcliffe/Bloomberg
Now, despite the prospect of higher inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-03/iran-war-oil-price-surge-put-global-economic-recovery-at-risk?cmpid=BBD030926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
, higher interest rates andsupply chain disruptions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/iran-war-how-oil-prices-are-surging-as-hormuz-shipping-production-disrupted?cmpid=BBD030926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
 — a cocktail that in the past has derailed green investments — Jefferies is 
telling clients not to panic and to stick with the clean energy sector.

The guidance of 2025 is “still very much” the narrative at Jefferies today, 
according to Aniket Shah, the New York-based bank’s global head of 
sustainability and transition strategy. Jefferies hasn’t changed its outlook 
“at all,” and expects the Iran war to trigger a new wave of investment in 
renewables as governments race to “increase energy independence,” he said.

Investors staying the course have been rewarded with market-beating returns so 
far in 2026. The S&P Global Clean Energy Transition Index is up 6.3%, compared 
with a slight decline in the S&P 500. Even so, the weeks and months ahead look 
set to test the conviction of green investors.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/green-investors-told-to-sit-tight-through-war-fueled-turmoil>
Wind turbine towers under construction in VietnamPhotographer: Linh 
Pham/Bloomberg
There’s already evidence that the disruption to energy markets caused by the 
Iran war is buoying the dirtiest fossil fuel markets. BloombergNEF notes that 
after months of pressure, Europe’s coal and lignite power generators are now 
back in the money, as a direct result of the spike in gas prices.

“Elevated oil and natural gas prices will strengthen fossil fuel earnings in 
the short term and they are going to temporarily outperform clean energy 
equities,” said Patrick J. Murphy, executive director of the geopolitical unit 
at Hilco Global and a former under secretary of the US army.

At the same time, for long-term investors such as pension funds and sovereign 
wealth funds, “there is no doubt” that they’ll remain “extremely committed to 
decarbonization,” he said.

Warning for UK banks

By Alastair Marsh 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQvVwWuhxcQ/alastair-marsh> and Olivia 
Rudgard <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWBnLoYN_tg/olivia-rudgard>

The UK’s flood insurer of last resort says the country’s mortgage banks aren’t 
doing enough 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/uk-banks-slammed-by-flood-re-s-ceo-for-ignoring-mortgage-risks?cmpid=BBD030926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
 to prepare for the risks facing homeowners across Britain.

“Their assumption is they don’t need to do anything,” Perry Thomas 
<https://www.floodre.co.uk/about-us/the-team/>, chief executive of Flood Re 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/1391068D:LN>, said in an interview. “And by 
not doing anything, they’re just making the problem worse.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/uk-banks-slammed-by-flood-re-s-ceo-for-ignoring-mortgage-risks>
Flood water following heavy rains covers agricultural land in the UK
Photographer: Jason Alden/Bloomberg
Set up a decade ago to help vulnerable homeowners get affordable insurance, 
Flood Re is due to be wound down in 2039. Since its creation, however, flood 
losses have increased while government efforts to build protections haveproved 
inadequate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2024-uk-home-flood-re-insurance/?cmpid=BBD030926_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
. The program, which currently has£3.2 billion 
<https://www.floodre.co.uk/wp-content/uploads/Flood-Re-Limited-ARA-2025-FINAL-EY-signed.pdf>
 ($4.3 billion) in reinsurance capacity, is now stepping up its reliance on 
capital markets to replenish its coffers.

An unfortunate side effect of having Flood Re act as a backstop is the moral 
hazard it’s injected into the banking sector, Thomas said. Over the past 
decade, the UK has gone “backwards, not forwards” in addressing the financial 
implications of flood risk, because it’s removed incentives for lenders to act 
responsibly, he said.
