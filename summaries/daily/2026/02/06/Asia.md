# Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — February 6, 2026

**Markets stabilize after risk-off rout.** Asian equities steadied Friday as the heavy selling that battered global risk assets earlier in the week showed signs of abating. The recovery followed a punishing three-day stretch in which the Nasdaq 100 fell roughly 4.6%—its worst run since April—on concerns about stretched valuations and the scale of hyperscaler AI capex, which for the four largest US tech names is forecast to approach $650 billion in 2026. Bitcoin, which briefly traded near $60,000 in Asian hours, rebounded more than 3%. Precious and base metals also found a footing after volatile sessions, though direction remains unsettled.

**Chinese hot money amplified the metals cycle.** Last month's surge and subsequent reversal across gold, silver and copper was meaningfully driven by mainland retail flows, with Shanghai copper futures registering record January volumes and China's top four gold ETFs seeing nearly $1 billion of outflows on Tuesday. The episode underscores a persistent challenge for Beijing: with property and equity returns subdued, surplus household savings continue to chase speculative outlets rather than productive investment. Structural drivers—central bank gold buying, dollar diversification, and strategic demand for industrial metals—appear intact, but near-term positioning has clearly overextended.

**EV sector under pressure.** BYD's Hong Kong shares extended a selloff that has erased over $60 billion in market value since May, weighed by softer domestic sales and rising input costs. Separately, Beijing's ban on concealed door handles—effective 2027—will require redesigns across much of the premium EV lineup, with per-model costs reportedly exceeding 100 million yuan. The rule signals China's growing willingness to set global automotive safety standards unilaterally.

**Southeast Asia headwinds; Japan and Thailand vote Sunday.** Moody's cut Indonesia's outlook to negative, citing fiscal risks; Jakarta's finance ministry pushed back. The Philippines posted its weakest non-pandemic growth in 15 years amid a deepening corruption scandal. India's RBI held rates, citing improved visibility from the US trade deal. Thailand's three-way election and Japan's lower house vote—where PM Takaichi's majority is in question—pose meaningful event risk for regional rates and FX into next week.
