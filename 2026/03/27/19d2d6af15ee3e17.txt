China-US Talks Are Back On With Xi in Pretty Strong Position
A key US-China meeting appears to be on again. Xi Jinping should have a good 
hand when it happens

Hello, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai.

A summit between President Donald Trump and Chinese leader Xi Jinping is on 
again, and that is probably welcome news for Beijing.

The two leaders will hold their highly anticipated meeting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/trump-to-travel-to-china-on-may-14-15-for-summit-with-xi?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 in Beijing on May 14 and 15, according to the White House. The US president 
was due to visit at the end of March butpostponed to focus on the Iran war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-20/trump-delaying-his-visit-to-beijing-is-a-big-relief-for-xi?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

The Chinese side will probably confirm the dates only a few days before the 
talks, standard practice for Beijing. That’ll give them room to maneuver if 
Trump does something unpredictable or the war is rumbling on.

The US announcement of a date several weeks out is one gift for Xi. China was 
apparently frustrated atthe perceived lack of preparation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/china-frustrated-by-last-minute-scramble-to-plan-xi-trump-summit?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 by the US for the initial summit date. The optics of hosting Trump while the 
US attacked one of Beijing’s friends would also have been a worry.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/video-why-xi-jinping-may-be-playing-the-long-game-with-donald-trump>
WATCH: How China is playing the long game with Trump
More importantly, the date the White House set is a faint signal at least that 
Trump sees the war winding down – though that’s no given considering Iran’s 
resistance. An end to the fighting would be good for China because signs of 
economic discomfort are appearing.

While its formidable oil reserves 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/sinopec-to-ensure-china-s-fuel-supply-and-cut-chemicals-losses?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 and decade-long pivot to renewable energy provide some protection from the 
kinds ofenergy worries that other nations like India 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/modi-seeks-to-calm-india-as-iran-war-causes-acute-gas-shortage?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 are dealing with, China’s manufacturing sector is starting to feel the pinch.

The Asian nation’s economy can weather a short war, and it may even benefit 
diplomatically in the sense that Beijing wants topresent itself as a fount of 
stability for the world 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/china-s-no-3-leader-urges-peaceful-solutions-as-iran-war-drags?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

That line is intended to contrast with the US, which has had to divert some 
military resources from the Pacific,to the consternation of Asian allies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/us-allies-near-china-on-edge-as-weapons-shift-from-asia-to-iran?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
. On top of that, energy worries have rattled US allies in the region. The 
Philippines is even considering setting aside its territorial dispute with 
Beijing and possiblycooperating on oil exploration in the South China Sea 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/marcos-says-war-may-spur-energy-talks-with-china-in-disputed-sea?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

The Middle East conflict could even turn into a net win for Beijing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-20/china-has-already-notched-its-first-major-win-from-the-iran-war?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 if it spurs more nations to diversify away from fossil fuels and toward clean 
energy and electric vehicles — technologies in which China excels.

The war may bring about some other benefits for China. For one, it tests the 
dollar’s dominant role 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/iran-war-could-be-making-of-the-petroyuan-deutsche-bank-says?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 as the currency of choice in the global oil trade. Beijing would like nothing 
more than for nations to move away from the US financial infrastructure that 
Washington has increasingly weaponized.

Citing media reports that Iran is allowing the passage of ships through the 
Strait of Hormuz if oil payments are made in yuan, Deutsche Bank strategist 
Mallika Sachdeva said “the conflict could be the catalyst for erosion in 
petrodollar dominance and the beginnings of the petroyuan.”
An employee at a plastics factory in Yiwu, Zhejiang province.Photographer: 
Adek Berry/AFP/Getty Images
A prolonged conflict that hurts global demand for China’s exports is a 
different story. That scenario coulddamage an economy that’s reliant on 
manufacturing and trade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-23/iran-war-is-costing-china-dearly-too?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

“A slowdown in exports will likely create more overcapacity, trigger fiercer 
price wars at home and trim corporate profits,” Bloomberg columnist Shuli Ren 
wrote.

That dark picture is already starting to play out to some extent in Yiwu, in 
eastern China, where small manufacturers and wholesalers of various products 
congregate. Buyers from the Middle East are nervous, in no small part due to 
worries about getting goods shipped home. Chinese suppliers fret about rising 
shipping costs and prices of inputs, especially those linked to the oil 
industry.

Chen Yubing, manager of a factory in Jiangsu province that makes polyester and 
nylon fabric, saidhe hasn’t encountered such a difficult situation in more than 
a decade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/china-s-official-calm-belies-a-war-battering-its-small-factories?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

So far, China’s trade is holding up. Nearly 20 million containers passed 
through the nation’s ports in the first three weeks of March, up more than 6% 
from the same period a year ago. That’s down a bit from earlier in the year but 
suggests shocks from the conflicthave yet to become a serious drag on commerce 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/ai-demand-is-shielding-china-s-booming-trade-from-iran-war-shock?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

For now.

It is difficult to say when the fighting will end, and the White House setting 
a date for Trump’s travels offers no guarantees. The US and Irandon’t seem to 
be on the same page in negotiations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/trump-questions-if-iran-deal-possible-as-he-ratchets-up-pressure?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

Still, if Trump does visit in mid-May, people in the halls of power in Beijing 
will probably be feeling confident.

Setting the Pace

Geely Auto’s research hub in eastern China is a hive of activity 24 hours a 
day. The constant buzz epitomizes why Geely is a great example of what has 
becomeknown in the EV industry as China Speed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-23/china-s-ev-boom-sees-byd-geely-leapmotor-rewriting-global-auto-industry-rules?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.

In short, that means the concept-to-production cycle for a new model has been 
slashed to just 24 months — about twice as fast asthe traditional automakers in 
Detroit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-02/detroit-enters-another-oil-shock-making-massive-vehicles?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 or Stuttgart can move.

This shift is happening through a total rewrite of the automotive industry’s 
rulebook. For one thing, the sheer intensity of the EV ecosystem is wild. At 
BYD, dominance is baked into work culture through what the company calls 
“hard-working credits.” That system literally ties a person’s paycheck to how 
much overtime they work and “dedication” they show.
A BYD Yangwang U8 demonstration vehicle.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
The pressure to move fast is contagious. Take UAES, a joint venture between 
Bosch and SAIC in China that supplies key electronic components and whose reps 
often scramble to keep up with clients wanting immediate solutions. If an 
engineer finds a bug at 11 p.m., someone from the supplier is expected to be a 
few desks away despite the late hour.

A fundamental shift in how cars are built is leading to the rapid pace. 
Companies like Xiaomi 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/xiaomi-sales-grows-slowest-in-years-in-sign-of-poor-phone-demand?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 and Xpeng treat EVs more like a product such as a smartphone than a mechanical 
system.

Like phones, EVs are often put on the market with core functionality ready but 
some software bugs very much expected. The automaker is prepared to fix those 
on the fly via performance tweaks, while new features are also rolled out. As 
with smartphone apps, these are delivered wirelessly via what the auto industry 
calls Over-the-Air (OTA) updates.

This ship-now, fix-later approach does come with challenges, particularly with 
complex assisted-driving tech. For example, BYD’s God’s Eye system — which the 
company marketed as being as vital as seatbelts — hascome under scrutiny 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-21/china-is-reining-in-smart-driving-tech-weeks-after-fatal-crash?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 following reports itsometimes drives dangerously 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/byd-s-god-s-eye-flaws-expose-risk-of-rush-into-high-tech-cars?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
. Tesla, the pioneer in software-driven EVs, is also subject to probes into its 
so-called Full-Self Driving tech, which actually requires constant human 
supervision and intervention.

Western automakers are learning that Chinese companies have changed the 
rulebook so dramatically that it is unwise to try to compete from a distance. 
Instead, many are diving headfirst into the Chinese ecosystem. Volkswagen has 
teamed up with Xpeng to share platforms, andStellantis is looking at using 
Leapmotor’s tech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-16/stellantis-mercedes-eye-china-deals-to-stay-competitive-on-cars?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 for its European brands.

The strategy is clear: use Chinese R&D, strong manufacturing capability and 
far-reaching supply chains 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-06-28/why-china-s-ev-industry-roared-into-the-fast-lane?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 to save themselves. The examples are plentiful. Renault is developing its new 
Twingo EV in Shanghai, and Bosch iscutting thousands of jobs in Germany 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-25/germany-s-industry-crisis-deepens-as-bosch-cuts-13-000-workers?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 to shift its focus toward battery and driver-assistance development in China.

As VW CEO Oliver Blume put it, Germany can learn from China’s “systemic 
approach” and discipline. And Ford CEO Jim Farley has called the entry of 
Chinese EVs into the US market an “existential threat,”prompting discussions at 
the highest levels of government 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/ford-ceo-trump-officials-discussed-china-us-carmaking-ventures?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 on how to adjust when that day comes.

The old rulebook for the global automotive industry is out the window. For 
now, companies that can move quickly have the advantage. And nearly all of them 
are in China.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

A debate has kicked off in China lately over a profession that is facing 
challenges from AI: acting.

Youhug Media, a Shanghai-based production company known for churning out hit 
TV dramas, recently caused a stir by unveiling two AI-generated digital actors 
to star in its short shows.

In many ways, the company is taking its cue from Hollywood 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2025-ai-hollywood/?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
, which has been abuzz since the controversial creation last year of 
AI-generatedTilly Norwood <https://www.tillynorwood.com/>. Then there is the 
film that’sset to star an AI-resurrected Val Kilmer 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-25/ai-generated-val-kilmer-is-a-test-for-hollywood?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
, who died a year ago. And a convincing but fake clip ofTom Cruise and Brad 
Pitt brawling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-20/ai-won-t-kill-movies-tom-cruise-and-brad-pitt-can-relax?cmpid=BBD032726_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 had people worrying whether filmmakers will need hugely expensive actors.

Some Chinese people took to social media to complain that Youhug Media’s AI 
creations, Qin Lingyue and Lin Xiyan, seemed to resemble real, popular 
thespians or appeared to be composites of others. A few people referred to the 
latter possibility as “crowded-face actor.”

China’s film and TV studios, especially the smaller ones, like the idea of 
performers made by AI for cost reasons. And a handful of people online said 
they were fine when AI was used for special effects, stunts and extras.

But many people complained that the faces of the AI actors looked stiff and 
soulless. Some called for boycotting shows featuring fake performers.

AI “can never replicate the real-life experience, delicate emotional tension 
and unique personal charm,” said Cute Zhang, a Weibo user with 13 million 
followers.

Youhug Media responded to the criticism by promising to use the creations for 
AI-only short dramas and never for main or supporting roles in traditional 
dramas.

Other people pointed to the impact that AI could have on salaries and jobs in 
the entertainment industry, and called for more government oversight.

“I wouldn’t use AI actors in a million years,” said Maggie Li, co-producer of 
the Oscar-nominated documentaryAscension. “What is the point of making the 
film? Is it to mass produce content so people can brainlessly feed on it? I 
know short video is absolutely pushing for that trend but it is something I 
hate and should be moderated, regulated and controlled.”
