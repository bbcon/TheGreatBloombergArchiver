 Selling the Bitcoin bounce 



From crypto diehards to first-time ETF buyers, the people who would usually be 
buying Bitcoin right now can’t bring themselves to do it 
<https://bloom.bg/4qZTKb8>. And the data explains why.

Nearly 9 million Bitcoin — 45% of all tokens in circulation — is worth less 
than what its holders paid for it.That helps explain why every rally is dying 
on arrival — burned holders sell into any bounce, sapping upward momentum.

Of the first 22 days in February, 19 recorded net losses — meaning more 
holders sold below what they paid than above it, Glassnode data show. This is 
not a market pausing for breath. It is a market in which participants are 
locking in pain, day after day.

That capitulation has a compounding effect. Every sale at a loss removes a 
holder who might otherwise have been a future buyer. And every rally — however 
brief — is met by a wall of sellers who bought higher and now see an exit 
rather than a floor. The result is a price ceiling that goes lower with each 
bounce: rallies get shorter, shallower and less convincing.

“Every small bounce is being used as a liquidity event,” said Roxanna Islam at 
ETF shop TMX VettaFi. “A clear catalyst is needed before the market can build a 
more durable recovery, especially since this is the first major collapse since 
Bitcoin’s widespread adoption.” —Vildana Hajric <https://bloom.bg/3DSeO0i> and 
Olga Kharif <https://bloom.bg/4ljE7IU>

 Asia’s year 



If there was any doubt as to where the momentum is in global financial markets 
this year, here are a few data points from overnight:

 * Overseas investors  bought a net $2.77 billion of Taiwan equities 
<https://bloom.bg/40tCZtT> yesterday, the most since December 2005. Foreign 
funds have been buying for six days straight. 
 * The value of South Korean stocks  has surged to $3.76 trillion 
<https://bloom.bg/4qUsMBs>, surpassing France to become the world’s 
ninth‑largest equity market. (In late January, Korea’s market value overtook 
Germany’s.) 
 * Taiwan Semiconductor shares are up 30% this year, Samsung Electronics has 
soared 70% and South Korean memory-chip makerSK Hynix is up 57%. 
The bottom line is, investors for now see the makers of AI picks and shovels 
as the only clear winners from the disruptive technology.They’re shunning Big 
Tech companies that are spending hundreds of billions of dollars on data 
centers, and they’re absolutely fleeing software and services companies that 
might see their businesses disrupted by AI.

The monster performance of the Asian hardware makers explains why MSCI’s 
emerging market and Asia-Pacific indexes are leaders this year: Taiwan Semi, 
Samsung and SK Hynix are among the five biggest companies in both benchmarks. —
Phil Serafino <https://bloom.bg/42HUmtw>
