Weekly Brief

Jul 13 – Jul 19, 2026

US

  • June CPI fell month-on-month for the first time in six years on Tuesday, with core CPI flat — well below consensus and described by Oxford Economics as reflecting the absence of tariff pass-through, AI-driven pricing, and oil simultaneously.
  • Fed Chair Warsh declined to declare "mission accomplished" in Congressional testimony Tuesday–Wednesday, acknowledging a "family fight" among policymakers over the extent and timing of any future tightening.
  • Major Wall Street banks reported record or near-record results: JPMorgan posted its highest-ever quarterly profit and Goldman Sachs recorded $7.42 billion in quarterly trading revenue; IBM fell more than 25% Thursday — its largest single-day decline in history — on disappointing earnings.
  • Netflix fell nearly 10% Thursday after guiding for slowing sales growth, reinforcing a broader pattern in which AI-adjacent earnings season is beginning to test return assumptions.
  • Corporate insiders are selling shares at a near-record pace, a potential warning signal from those with the most granular knowledge of business conditions.

Inflation

June CPI delivered the most broadly encouraging inflation print in years. Headline prices fell month-on-month for the first time since 2020 while core CPI was flat, with the Atlanta Fed sticky-price gauge falling to its lowest since the pandemic and the Cleveland Fed trimmed mean dipping below 3%. The relief was genuine but narrow: headline PPI's concurrent improvement was heavily driven by a 12% drop in gasoline prices rather than broad disinflation, and core pipeline pressures — PPI ex-food and energy at 4.7% YoY — remained elevated. Labor-intensive services linked to immigrant workers were an exception to the benign picture, with home healthcare costs up 10.7% YoY. The tension between backward-looking June data and the forward trajectory of energy prices — Brent ending the week roughly 33% above its start-of-year level — is the central analytical challenge for the inflation outlook.

Monetary Policy

Fed Chair Warsh used his House and Senate testimony to push back firmly against premature optimism. He reintroduced M2 money supply as a policy dashboard input in the semiannual Monetary Policy Report, signaling a broader deliberative framework. Futures pricing for a July hike collapsed following the CPI release — the second-largest post-CPI repricing of Fed meeting odds since 2008 — yet Capital Economics maintained a rate hike remains a question of when, not if, given the AI investment boom and rebounding consumer demand. FOMC minutes from the prior meeting had already signaled a hawkish lean, and Warsh's framing of the rate debate as an open "family fight" confirmed the committee has not closed the door on tightening.

Markets and Earnings

The S&P 500's muted 0.38% gain Tuesday on the CPI print reflected residual uncertainty over oil and the Hormuz situation rather than unambiguous relief. By Thursday, Nasdaq 100 futures had declined 1.8% and the semiconductor ETF dropped 4.4% in premarket as investors rotated out of AI-adjacent positions — a move amplified by Netflix's forward guidance miss and Intuitive Surgical falling 10% on slowing procedure growth. The BofA global fund manager survey, conducted July 2–9, found cash allocations below 4%, a level BofA historically treats as a contrarian sell signal, suggesting positioning was established on assumptions that may prove too optimistic if energy disruption persists. ---

Asia

  • China's Q2 GDP grew 4.3% YoY — the weakest reading since at least 1992 outside the pandemic, missing consensus and prompting Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, and ANZ to cut full-year forecasts; fixed-asset investment contracted 5.7% and property investment fell 18% in H1.
  • SK Hynix's US-listed ADRs trade at a 51% premium to Seoul shares; the Kospi has fallen 28% from its June high, with volatility measures reaching levels unseen since February 1998, and regulators halted new listings of leveraged single-stock ETFs tied to Samsung and SK Hynix.
  • TSMC reported a 77% YoY jump in quarterly profit Thursday, beat consensus, and raised full-year capex guidance to as much as $64 billion — yet its ADRs fell approximately 3.8% in premarket as semiconductor valuation concerns outweighed the fundamental beat.
  • The Bank of Korea raised interest rates for the first time since 2023 Thursday, explicitly citing AI-driven demand as a structural inflationary force and flagging persistent won weakness.
  • China's GDP deflator turned positive for the first time in three years, but economists characterize the shift as "bad inflation" — reflecting oil-shock cost pressures rather than restored consumer demand.

China Growth and Policy

The 4.3% Q2 GDP print crystallizes China's structural imbalance. Exports remain the primary engine — semiconductor shipments rose at triple-digit rates for a second consecutive month — while domestic demand is uniformly weak, with consumer spending barely expanding and fixed-asset investment recording a historic contraction. The positive deflator reading is technically a milestone after three years of economy-wide deflation, but it widens margins between upstream commodity producers and downstream manufacturers in a way that suppresses rather than stimulates consumption. Goldman Sachs estimates roughly 1.8 trillion yuan in undeployed government bond issuance quota as a fiscal buffer, though Beijing's willingness to deploy it aggressively remains uncertain. Bloomberg Economics argues the Iran conflict represents only a "ripple" for China given its lower import dependency, leaving deflation as a domestic problem requiring a domestic demand cure.

Korea and Semiconductor Markets

The Kospi's 28% peak-to-trough decline has been amplified well beyond what fundamentals alone justify. Smart money outflows preceded the retail-driven collapse — tech funds recorded $9.3 billion in weekly outflows through late June and BlackRock's iShares MSCI South Korea ETF suffered a record $2.7 billion monthly outflow in May — while leveraged single-stock ETFs tracking Samsung and SK Hynix mechanically magnified daily moves. The Bank of Korea's rate hike adds a further headwind for leveraged domestic investors. The 51% ADR premium for SK Hynix reflects structural impediments to arbitrage rather than fundamental divergence, and regulators' decision to halt new leveraged ETF listings signals recognition that the product design itself contributed to the volatility spiral.

AI Geopolitics and Regional Markets

Xi Jinping's personal appearance at the World AI Conference in Shanghai Thursday — an elevation from prior years — and the launch of a China-led AI Cooperation Organization with 29 member nations including Brazil and Indonesia formalized Beijing's bid to shape global AI governance. Chinese startup Moonshot's Kimi K3 model, described as rivaling top OpenAI and Anthropic offerings, contributed to the regional equity selloff by prompting investors to question US AI capex return assumptions. Taiwan's Taiex shed 6.5% Thursday — its largest single-day decline since April 2025. The Bank of Korea's identification of AI semiconductor demand as a structural inflationary force illustrates that the boom's macro transmission remains narrow: generating output and profits without yet producing the wage and consumption gains needed for sustained domestic reflation. ---

Europe

  • ASML raised its full-year net sales guidance Wednesday to €43–45 billion, approximately 12% above the prior analyst consensus midpoint of €39.3 billion, driven by accelerating AI-related chip equipment demand; shares rose 4% in Amsterdam before declining 3.8% Thursday in the broader semiconductor selloff.
  • Andy Burnham entered Downing Street as UK Prime Minister Friday, with early reports indicating he will announce new North Sea oil and gas drilling and public control of Thames Water among his first acts.
  • UK GDP unexpectedly grew in May, printing above consensus Wednesday, offering Burnham a marginally better macro starting point than markets had assumed.
  • France faces a fiscal gap widening to nearly 7% of GDP by 2030 without corrective action, per a Finance Ministry-commissioned report, requiring €126 billion in savings over five years.
  • Europe is experiencing its third heat wave of the season, with researchers estimating more than 10,000 deaths across the UK, France, Spain, and Germany combined.

UK Political Economy

Sterling rose to its strongest level in a year on a trade-weighted basis Wednesday on reports Burnham would appoint centrist Shabana Mahmood as Chancellor rather than soft-left favourite Ed Miliband — a market signal of preference for fiscal continuity over expansion. The stronger-than-expected May GDP print provides a marginally constructive starting point, though durability remains uncertain given ongoing trade disruptions and elevated energy costs. Burnham's reported willingness to expand North Sea production represents a pragmatic departure from his party's prior energy stance. The political context matters for gilt and sterling pricing: six prime ministers since 2016, none completing a full term, means markets will be alert to early signals on the fiscal framework.

Eurozone and Continental Europe

ASML's guidance upgrade provided the clearest market signal of the week that AI-driven semiconductor capital expenditure is running well ahead of earlier projections — CEO Christophe Fouquet's characterization of customers "accelerating all their plans" implies demand durability rather than a pull-forward. That optimism was partially unwound Thursday as the global chip selloff spread, with ASM International and BE Semiconductor each falling 5.0%. France's fiscal trajectory stands out as a medium-term risk: the Finance Ministry's own analysis projects a deficit approaching 7% of GDP by 2030, driven primarily by rising debt-service costs, in a politically difficult environment ahead of presidential elections. Germany's Rhine River water levels fell to their lowest for the time of year since 1990, threatening manufacturing supply chains at a moment when Europe's largest economy remains fragile. The ECB is expected to pause at its next meeting before delivering a final rate hike in September — a divergence from the Fed's current posture that could exert modest upward pressure on the euro. ---

LatAm

  • The US announced 25% tariffs on selected Brazilian imports effective July 22 Wednesday, following an unfair trade practices investigation — the most concrete bilateral trade action against a major Latin American economy in the current cycle.
  • US tariffs arrive three months before Brazil's October presidential election, with President Lula's Workers' Party deploying the "TariFlávio" slogan to link opponent Flávio Bolsonaro to the measure, exploiting the Bolsonaro family's Trump ties.
  • Chile's Senate approved the president's wide-ranging economic reform bill Thursday, clearing a key legislative hurdle, though the country remains at risk of recession despite having elected a pro-market administration to revive growth.
  • Argentine President Milei's approval rating has fallen to its lowest level in over two years, with corruption concerns compounding pre-existing economic vulnerabilities ahead of 2027 elections.

Brazil: Trade and Political Economy

The 25% tariff on Brazilian goods creates an asymmetric political dynamic rather than straightforwardly damaging the incumbent. Lula has used the measure to construct a sovereignty narrative — a framing he has deployed successfully before — while the Bolsonaro family's cultivation of Trump ties provides a ready political target. Whether the tariffs cause sufficient economic damage to offset Lula's nationalist framing remains to be seen, but the near-term political calculus appears to favor the incumbent. The timing is materially awkward for Brazil's external accounts: commodity markets are already under stress from Hormuz-related fertilizer disruptions, and the additional tariff burden risks compressing export margins precisely when external support is most needed.

Argentina and Regional Dynamics

Milei's political capital has eroded meaningfully, with corruption — once weaponized against the Peronist opposition — becoming a liability for his own administration. Deteriorating political conditions could complicate Argentina's ongoing IMF engagement and its ability to maintain the fiscal surpluses central to the stabilization program. The broader regional macro backdrop remains shaped by the Iran conflict's commodity pass-through: fertilizer price increases of more than 30% from the conflict's initial phase, if sustained, will weigh on agricultural-export economies including Brazil and Argentina at a critical point in the production cycle. Chile's legislative progress on economic reform removes a significant obstacle, though implementation effects will take time to materialize against a global backdrop of elevated rates and softer commodity demand. ---

Global

  • Brent crude rose 4.7% to approximately $79/barrel Monday after Iran announced a renewed Strait of Hormuz closure and the US struck roughly 140 targets over the weekend; by Thursday oil was trading near $84–$85/barrel, heading for its largest weekly gain since April, after the US struck a sanctioned supertanker near Iran's primary crude export terminal at Kharg Island.
  • Prediction markets now assign less than 50% probability to normal Hormuz traffic resuming by year-end, a sharp deterioration from earlier in July; the IEA's executive director warned the global economy faces a renewed challenge if disruption extends beyond several weeks.
  • The IMF projects 3% global GDP growth for 2026, only modestly below the prior two-year average of 3.5%, despite historically elevated tariffs and energy disruption — though that projection was established before the latest escalation in Hormuz hostilities.
  • A Pew Research Center survey found more countries view China favorably than the US for the first time in nearly 20 years, reflecting a collapse in US favorability linked to tariffs, Greenland demands, and the Iran conflict.

Strait of Hormuz

The conflict has evolved from a discrete escalation into a pattern of persistent, operationally expanding military pressure. The US completed a seventh consecutive night of strikes by Friday, targeting coastal surveillance, air defense, and maritime assets. The decision to strike a supertanker deep within the Persian Gulf near Kharg Island — well beyond the strait's mouth — signals a meaningful expansion of enforcement scope that raises the operational stakes for commercial shipping throughout the Gulf, not merely at its chokepoint. Kpler data confirm that most remaining transits are occurring with transponders off, with India subsequently barring its seafarers from Hormuz passages. Crude timespreads moved into heavy backwardation and refined product premiums reached historic highs, signaling acute near-term supply anxiety. The market's central unresolved question is whether US political tolerance for higher oil prices — a domestic constraint the Trump administration has not publicly addressed — will eventually force a de-escalation.

Energy Transmission and Macro Implications

Asian refiners are accelerating purchases of US crude and Canadian grades as Persian Gulf supply becomes effectively inaccessible near-term, a substitution analysts say may outlast the conflict itself. Asian spot LNG prices climbed to their highest level since March Thursday. The more consequential second-order channel may be fertilizer: urea prices spiked over 30% during the conflict's initial phase, and with El Niño simultaneously weighing on crop yields in Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, food inflation risks are becoming a genuine macroeconomic concern across emerging markets where food represents a large CPI share. Trump's proposed 20% Hormuz cargo fee was abandoned within 24 hours under Gulf state pressure Monday, replaced by a pledge to seek trade and investment deals — an episode illustrating the administration's difficulty reconciling military objectives with economic consequences. **
