Good morning. The AI scare trade is back. We talk to the co-author of the 
Citrini Research report that was one of the triggers. And the UK sees an easing 
in its crime wave.Listen to the day’s top stories <https://bloom.bg/4nmEQLf>.

— Lily Nonomiya and Nasteho Said



The AI scare trade erupted again on Monday as growing concerns about the 
disruptive power of the technology <https://bloom.bg/4b79nbR> dragged down 
shares of payments and software companies, sending IBM to its worst plunge in 
25 years.

It all began with a bearish report published over the weekend by a little 
known firm called Citrini Research, which outlined the potential AI risks to 
parts of the global economy.Black Swan author Nassim Taleb also had a warning 
<https://bloom.bg/4kPoaeA>: brace for escalating volatility and potential 
bankruptcies in the software sector as the AI rally enters a more fragile phase 
(Watch the video here <https://bloom.bg/4axxkJj>). Check out our Markets Today 
live blog <https://bloom.bg/4gqxcM0> for all the latest news and analysis 
relevant to UK assets.

We spoke to Alap Shah, a co-author of the Citrini report, who argued that 
governments should consider taxing AI to cushion theeffects of sweeping job 
losses <https://bloom.bg/4qSalxq>. The smarter AI gets, the more jobs it can 
replace, the chief investment officer at Lotus Technology Management told 
Bloomberg TV.

We’re four years into Vladimir Putin’s war on Ukraine, but plans to push for 
peace <https://bloom.bg/4aT5iad> appear to be stalling. Peace talks are 
deadlocked and the fighting is largely at a stalemate. Allies say the US is 
pushing for a deal before Trump hosts the 250th anniversary of American 
independence on July 4. But there’s no indication that Putin is ready to reach 
an agreement that doesn’t meet his key demands, according to officials.

An EU assessment found that Trump’s new tariff policy will increase levies on 
some of the bloc’s exportsabove the level permitted <https://bloom.bg/4qP5Wvj> 
in their trade agreement. The European Commission, which handles trade matters, 
told lawmakers Monday that the new global tariff will be added to levies that 
are already in place. The new cumulative rate means some goods would be above 
the 15% ceiling.

 Opinion 



The US is warning the EU again over looming “Made in Europe 
<https://bloom.bg/4kPqylv>” rules aimed at shoring up its industrial base after 
a painful shellacking from Chinese competition and Trumpian tariffs, Lionel 
Laurent writes. While local-content mandates could stem de-industrialization, 
their costs and retaliation risks highlight the need for a sharper 
competitiveness fix.

 Before You Go 


 <https://bloom.bg/4aT5iqJ> Police officers stand in sight of the Elizabeth 
TowerSource: Justin Tallis/AFP 
In the UK, a post-pandemic spike in violence and abuse against retail workers 
is easing as more spending on security measures andimproved police responses 
<https://bloom.bg/4aT5iqJ> begin to pay off. Reported incidents fell by a fifth 
to an average 1,600 a day in the 12 months through August, according to the 
British Retail Consortium. Retailers spent more than £5 billion in the last 
five years on improved security—from staff to CCTV—to tackle therise of 
shoplifting <https://bloom.bg/4ccrIW3> and abuse against staff.
