The defining macro theme on Friday was the intensifying feedback loop between the Iran war, oil prices, and global bond markets. With the Strait of Hormuz remaining largely closed under a fragile ceasefire, Brent crude has risen more than 60% over three months to approximately $109 a barrel — far enough to inflict broad demand destruction, but not yet resolved in either direction. The US EIA now projects global oil demand growth of only 200,000 barrels per day in 2026, against a pre-war forecast of 1.2 million; the IEA goes further, projecting an outright contraction of 420,000 barrels per day. Down years for oil demand are rare and historically coincide with severe economic stress. TD Securities warns that depleted inventories could drive a fresh surge toward $150 a barrel absent a Hormuz resolution; BloombergNEF estimates $160 if closure persists through July.

The Trump-Xi summit produced a shared rhetorical commitment to reopening the strait and ending the Iran conflict, but no operational mechanism. Trump's comment to Fox News that the US does not need the strait open "at all" may complicate diplomatic signalling, even if intended for a domestic audience. The UAE, frustrated by Gulf neighbours' refusal to join a coordinated military response to Iranian strikes earlier in the conflict, announced plans to double its Hormuz-bypass export capacity via the Fujairah pipeline by 2027 — a structural adaptation to a prolonged disruption scenario.

Sovereign bond markets are now pricing a coherent inflation narrative across jurisdictions: US 10-year yields at 4.54%, UK gilts at 5.14%, and Japan's 20-year rate at its highest level since 1996. The correlation of losses across US, UK, eurozone and Japanese government bond indices this year reflects a common driver in energy prices, layered over country-specific fiscal and political factors. Producer price indices in the US, China and Japan all surprised to the upside this week, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. Fed rate hike expectations for December have moved from a tail risk to a base-case probability for a meaningful share of market participants — a repricing with implications for equity valuations, credit spreads, and emerging market capital flows that have not yet fully resolved.
