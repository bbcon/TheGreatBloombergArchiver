**Russia: Macro Discipline and Economic Fragility**
Russia's economy presents an unusual configuration: a wartime state running relatively orthodox macroeconomic policy. The Bank of Russia's benchmark rate, now at 14.5%, implies real rates above 9% given sub-6% consumer price inflation — among the highest real rates globally. The Russian Economy Ministry has cut its 2026 growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3%, and the budget deficit has reached a record in nominal terms, though at 2.5% of GDP it remains modest by developed-market standards. The core problem is structural: military spending crowds out civilian investment, a labor force shortfall of approximately 1.5 million workers constrains capacity, and high borrowing costs suppress private sector activity. The oil price windfall from the Iran conflict has provided incremental revenue but cannot resolve these supply-side constraints. Bloomberg Economics assesses that the model is unsustainable in the long run, though the timing of any more acute deterioration remains highly uncertain.

**Climate: Physical Risk and Adaptation Costs**
The one-year anniversary of Blatten's destruction by a glacier collapse serves as a marker for the accelerating pace of Alpine glacier loss. Switzerland has experienced average warming of 3°C since pre-industrial times — more than double the global average — and more than 1,000 glaciers have disappeared since 1973. The fiscal dimension of this process is becoming politically contentious, as urban Swiss taxpayers increasingly bear the cost of subsidizing infrastructure and insurance in high-risk mountain communities. The Oxford Economics analysis of climate-driven food price inflation adds a further transmission channel: physical climate risk is increasingly a macroeconomic and monetary policy variable, not merely an environmental one.
