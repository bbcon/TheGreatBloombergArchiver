Is China Back?

China looks to have shrugged off the Japanification tag — at least for now.

Property data last week showed some rare green shoots and GDP data 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/china-s-growth-rebound-tops-forecasts-with-iran-war-impact-muted?cmpid=BBD042126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260421>
 surprised on the upside. The war in Iran is helping pull the economy out of
deflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-10/china-exits-factory-deflation-after-war-oil-shock-boosted-prices?cmpid=BBD042126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260421>
 and the stock market is back near decade highs.

This month, a couple of influential papers have examined China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/china-s-aluminum-output-fueled-by-middle-east-s-stranded-cargoes?cmpid=BBD042126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260421>
’s long property slump, comparing it with Japan’s lost decades. They draw 
contrasting conclusions, but find common ground on what Beijing needs to do to 
sustain a recovery and bury the comparisons with Japan once and for all.
Road construction outside China Vanke Co.’s Donglu residential compound in 
Beijing, China, on Sunday, Dec. 21, 2025.Source: Bloomberg
Former IMF chief economist and long-time Japan watcher Kenneth Rogoff’s paper, 
written alongside Yuanchen Yang and published as a working paper by theNational 
Bureau of Economic Research <https://www.nber.org/>, sees China in the middle 
stages of a multi-year correction that has some structural and cyclical 
characteristics that closely resemble Japan’s pre- and post-bubble conditions.

“China’s post-boom adjustment is unfolding in a more difficult macroeconomic 
and demographic context than Japan’s,” Rogoff and Yang write. But crucially, 
“China possesses a state-dominated financial system backed by implicit 
government guarantees and a highly proactive policymaking apparatus capable of 
large-scale interventions, which have thus far prevented the crisis from 
precipitating a financial sector breakdown.”

Stephen Jen, chief investment officer at Eurizon SLJ Capital, is more upbeat 
in his paper co-written with Joana Freire and titled “China Is (Almost) Back.”

“We have likely seen the worst in this property correction, which would be in 
stark contrast to the early pessimistic commentary in the market that compared 
China to Japan in the 1990s,” the pair write. “China has turned a corner, 
finally.”

While the two papers diverge on the state of China’s property cycle 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/china-bonds-set-for-best-month-since-october-on-ample-cash?cmpid=BBD042126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260421>
, they both have the same prescription for a sustained recovery: productivity.

Where tepid productivity was a binding constraint for post-bubble Japan, that 
doesn’t seem to be the case inChina 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/chinese-manufacturing-growth-overtaken-by-finance-amid-ipo-boom?cmpid=BBD042126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260421>
, Jen and Freire argue.

“There is probably no country more Darwinian than China,” they write. That 
fierce competition can lead to slim profits and attrition, but “when the dust 
settles, the last one standing is indeed the fittest and the most resilient.” 
Meantime, they see the rivalry with the US propelling China’s technological 
advancements and adoption of new technologies.

To Rogoff and Yang, emerging from the property funk will “depend on whether 
the economy can maintain productivity growth and restore real demand.”

“Without structural reforms to rebalance the growth model, the risk remains 
that today’s managed adjustment could evolve into tomorrow’s stagnation or 
instability, particularly if the current strength in areas such as electric 
vehicles, solar panels and ship-building turn out to run into export 
constraints, and these sectors, which remain still much smaller collectively 
than real estate and infrastructure, run into similar constraints that 
subsequently curtail investment.”

Need-to-Know Research

From economists to central bankers and politicians, many are anticipating — or 
hoping — that artificial intelligence will eventually turbocharge productivity 
and economic growth in the US, but at what cost to the environment?

A working paper <https://www.nber.org/> published by the National Bureau of 
Economic Research found that data centers in the US consume 5% to 6% of the 
nation’s electricity, causing about $25 billion in gross external damage — 
mainly air pollution and greenhouse gas emissions.

The range is wide, $10 billion to $33 billion, but the damages are 
concentrated geographically, with Texas and Virginia representing 30% of the 
total, Nicholas Z. Muller of Carnegie Mellon University wrote in the paper.
Measuring the Impact of Data Centers in the United States Economy: Monetary 
Damage from Air Pollution and Greenhouse Gas Emissions. Nicholas Z. Muller.
http://www.nber.org/papers/w35100
With tech companies planning to spend hundreds of billions dollars 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/how-much-is-big-tech-spending-on-ai-computing-a-staggering-650-billion-in-2026?cmpid=BBD042126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260421>
 on data centers in the coming years, their electricity consumption and 
environmental toll could rise by as much as 85% in the near term, the author 
found. And yet, “preliminary comparisons suggest that the damages attributable 
to AI-related energy use are small relative to potential productivity gains,” 
Muller said.
