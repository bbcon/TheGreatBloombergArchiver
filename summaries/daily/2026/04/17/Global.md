The dominant macro narrative remains the trajectory of the US-Iran conflict and its implications for energy markets and global growth. Trump expressed optimism about a permanent ceasefire, claiming Iran had conceded on nuclear ambitions and Hormuz access — assertions Tehran has not publicly confirmed. Talks may resume this weekend, though Gulf Arab and European officials believe a durable agreement is more likely six months away. The ceasefire in Lebanon, announced this week, offers modest positive signal, but the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker completing an exit with crude cargo since the US blockade began — underscoring the gap between diplomatic tone and operational reality.

Oil responded to the peace signals, with Brent falling toward $98 per barrel and WTI near $93, paring weekly gains. Price volatility has cooled markedly from the record $38 intraday range seen in mid-March, with Brent trading in approximately a $10 band this week. Industrial metals tell a different story — the London Metals Index reached a record high on aluminum supply concerns, with the six-metal LME index up roughly 12% over four weeks.

The broader structural damage from the Hormuz disruption is becoming clearer. Panama Canal congestion has reached its worst since the 2023-2024 drought, with vessels facing 3.5-day waits and one ship paying $4 million to jump the queue. LNG's credibility as a long-term energy solution for emerging Asia has been materially set back — a second price shock in four years following Russia's invasion of Ukraine is accelerating diversification toward renewables, domestic gas, and coal, with implications for Big Oil's growth strategy in developing markets.

Anthropic's Mythos AI model — described as highly capable of identifying system vulnerabilities and orchestrating cyberattacks — emerged as an unexpected focal point at the IMF-World Bank spring meetings in Washington. Central bank governors from the ECB, Bank of England, and New York Fed all raised the issue publicly within days of each other, reflecting institutional alarm at a moment when international coordination capacity is already strained. The dollar's safe-haven rally appears to be fading, with Wall Street banks including Deutsche Bank and Wells Fargo recommending short positions as ceasefire prospects reduce haven demand.
