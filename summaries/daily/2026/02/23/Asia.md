The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariffs has materially shifted the regional trade calculus. China emerges as the clearest beneficiary: Morgan Stanley estimates average US levies on Chinese goods fall to 24% from 32%, while the Asia-wide weighted average tariff rate drops to approximately 17% from 20%. Chinese equities in Hong Kong rallied 2.65% on the session, with the Hang Seng China Enterprises index among the stronger performers globally. Mainland China and Japan markets were closed for holidays, limiting broader regional price discovery.

The geopolitical significance extends beyond the tariff arithmetic. President Xi Jinping is scheduled to host Trump in Beijing beginning March 31, and the court ruling has meaningfully altered the negotiating dynamic. Xi enters those discussions with Chinese export volumes running at full capacity, average levies reduced, and a US counterpart stripped of his most flexible coercive instrument. Whether Beijing opts to capitalize on this position or maintains its measured posture to avoid fresh friction ahead of the summit remains to be seen.

India presents a more complicated picture. New Delhi had been advancing toward an interim trade deal with Washington, but Indian officials postponed the relevant trip to the US following the court ruling, citing uncertainty over the new tariff framework's legal durability. South Korea, by contrast, is demonstrating resilience: the Bank of Korea told parliament that the semiconductor cycle is exhibiting unusual strength, and exports jumped 47% in the first 20 days of February on a working-day-adjusted basis. The central bank expects "significantly higher" GDP growth in 2026 relative to 2025. South Korean President Lee Jae Myung's market reform agenda continues to attract attention, with the Kospi having gained 115% since he took office in June, supported by corporate governance improvements that have broadened shareholder returns.
