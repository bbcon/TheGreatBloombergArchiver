Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
energy reporter Josh Saul examines the persistent troubles that have slowed 
offshore wind development in the US.

A new legal fight between the developer and main supplier of America’s first 
large offshore wind project shows how the industry’s woes extend beyond aWhite 
House campaign 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-again-vows-to-block-wind-turbines-during-his-presidency?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 to thwart its growth.

Vineyard Wind 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/1712023D:US?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
, off the coast of Massachusetts, suedGE Vernova Inc. 
<bbg://securities/GEV%20US%20Equity> in state court last week, accusing the 
turbine maker of improperly terminating agreements and abandoning construction 
and maintenance work on the $4.5 billion development.

Vineyard, whose developers include Iberdrola SA’s Avangrid and Copenhagen 
Infrastructure Partners, said it withheld payments from GE Vernova following 
delays to the project, including when a busted turbine bladewashed ashore 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-08-13/nantucket-beach-turbine-pollution-puts-offshore-energy-in-spotlight?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 in 2024.

That incident left debris strewn across beaches on Nantucket and triggered 
anger from residents on the wealthy island.
Debris from a broken wind turbine blade washes up on Nantucket, Massachusetts.
Photographer: Bloomberg
GE Vernova said Vineyard Wind failed to pay more than $300 million for more 
than 18 months of work, noting it recently completed installation of all 62 
turbines.

“The dispute exposes how execution risk has intensified to the point where 
even near‑completed, 800‑megawatt projects with tier‑one partners can face 
potential default and foreclosure,” said Diego Espinosa, a senior research 
analyst at Wood Mackenzie.

The lawsuit is the latest sign that little comes easy for US offshore wind. 
Even with support from former President Joe Biden earlier this decade, 
inflation and supply-chain snagsscuppered contracts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-11-01/the-great-us-offshore-wind-power-boom-has-begun-to-falter?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 and caused delays. Donald Trump has since issued multiple orders tohalt 
projects 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-27/martha-s-vineyard-wind-project-trump-stopped-is-revived-by-judge?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
, further battering the sector.

Vineyard Wind’s developers won a court injunction 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-27/martha-s-vineyard-wind-project-trump-stopped-is-revived-by-judge?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 in January that allowed them to resume construction. The Trump administration 
had more luck last month with French energy giant TotalEnergies SE, which agreed
not to develop 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/totalenergies-released-from-1-billion-us-offshore-wind-lease?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 offshore leases in return for about $1 billion in compensation.

Among left-leaning US Northeast and Mid-Atlantic regions, the industry was 
once seen as key to replacing fossil fuels and to meeting surging power demand 
from data centers.

Now, lawmakers in states including New York 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/hochul-moves-to-weaken-aggressive-new-york-state-climate-law?cmpid=BBBXT041526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 andRhode Island 
<https://ecori.org/renewable-energy-programs-would-take-hit-under-mckees-budget-plan/>
 are moving to weaken climate policies that supported offshore wind as they try 
to lower energy costs for consumers.

“We were already very pessimistic for the market” in the US, said Harrison 
Sholler, an offshore wind analyst at BloombergNEF. “Besides the five projects 
under construction, we don’t see any other projects coming online by 2040 
unless there’s significant policy change.”

—Josh Saul, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

Asian refiners could be forced to reduce operations further if the Iran war 
continues to disrupt crude flows from the Middle East. Processors are tapping 
stockpiles and sourcing alternative grades from other regions, but those can’t 
fully replace lost volumes from the Persian Gulf. Should refiners make deeper 
run cuts, crucial supplies of jet fuel and diesel will likely tighten further.

BNEF today

Copper producers, particularly in Chile, will feel the pinch from China’s 
decision to ban exports of sulfuric acid, according toBloombergNEF <>. The 
blockade of the Strait of Hormuz has restricted the supply of sulfur, used to 
make the acid that’s essential for some copper extraction. Prices in China have 
risen 90% since the start of the war.
