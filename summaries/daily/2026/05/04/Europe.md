European assets continued to underperform relative to US markets, a divergence that reflects the continent's greater structural exposure to the Strait of Hormuz disruption given its reliance on Middle Eastern energy flows. Euro Stoxx 50 futures fell 1.7%, in contrast to modest gains in US and Asian futures. That vulnerability has been compounded by two fresh US actions: President Trump announced a tariff increase on European automobiles to 25%, and the Pentagon confirmed a drawdown of US troops from Germany beyond the initial 5,000 previously disclosed. Both moves appear connected to German Chancellor Friedrich Merz's public criticism of US Iran policy. The European Commission signaled it is prepared to respond to the auto tariff threat, though the much-delayed US-EU trade deal — initially reached last July but still unratified by EU lawmakers — remains a complicating backdrop.

The diplomatic calendar offered some counterweight. Dozens of European leaders convened in Yerevan, Armenia, for the European Political Community summit, with the gathering underscoring the EU's push to expand its strategic footprint in the Caucasus — a region that offers an alternative trade corridor bypassing Russia. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced Britain's intention to join the EU's €90 billion Ukraine loan plan, a notable step in post-Brexit alignment on European security matters. NATO Secretary-General Rutte and Canadian Prime Minister Carney also attended, the latter marking the first non-European leader at such a summit.

In Swiss financial regulation, lawmakers convened to begin a legislative process that could impose up to $20 billion in additional capital requirements on UBS — a proceeding with significant implications for the institution's capital allocation and European banking sector sentiment. UK local elections on May 7 are expected to reveal further erosion of support for both Labour and the Conservatives, with polling suggesting Nigel Farage's Reform UK and other insurgent parties stand to benefit.
