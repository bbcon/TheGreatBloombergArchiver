Former South Korean President Yoon Suk Yeol was sentenced to life in prison after a Seoul court found him guilty of leading an insurrection following his declaration of martial law in late 2024. Prosecutors had sought the death penalty, arguing the act posed an existential threat to South Korea's constitutional order. Yoon denied wrongdoing and retains the right to appeal, but the verdict formally closes one chapter of what was the country's most severe political crisis in decades. The ruling carries significant implications for South Korea's institutional trajectory and near-term political calendar.

In Japan, Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi faces an early test of her intentions toward monetary policy, with nominations for two open Bank of Japan board seats expected as soon as February 25. The seats — those of dovish member Asahi Noguchi and consensus voter Junko Nakagawa — give Takaichi, historically associated with reflation, an opportunity to shape the board's composition at a delicate moment. Inflation running above target and the yen's sensitivity to rate expectations mean the appointments will be closely parsed by bond and currency markets. A like-for-like swap may not dramatically alter the balance; a hawkish deviation would carry more signal.
