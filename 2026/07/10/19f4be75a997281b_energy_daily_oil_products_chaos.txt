Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Europe oil markets team leader Alaric Nightingale looks at the persistent 
shortages in refined fuels in light of the Iran war.

The world’s benchmark crude prices might have given up almost all their 
wartime gains, but markets for refined fuels are looking increasingly 
concerning.

The margins that oil refineries make from turning crude into products like 
gasoline, diesel and jet fuel surged to the highest since 2022 in early July, 
the International Energy Agency warned today. It reflects a growing gap between 
the prices of the raw material the plants buy and the end products that 
consumers get.

The divergence has even caught the attention of a bondholder 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/vanguard-buys-inflation-protected-bonds-on-an-oil-market-oddity?cmpid=BBBXT071026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6421>
 who’s watching crack spreads — the difference between crude and fuels — as an 
indicator that inflation could surprise to the upside.

The dominant factor is the Iran war, which has choked off exports from the 
Middle East. That disruption is increasingly being compounded by waves of 
Ukrainian attacks onRussia’s oil refineries 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/russian-gasoline-shortage-begins-to-spill-into-central-asia?cmpid=BBBXT071026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6421>
, forcing Moscow to boost crude shipments while banning exports of diesel.

The Iran war caused a loss of refining capacity in the Persian Gulf region, a 
vital and growing source of supply over the past decade or so, and also 
curtailed fuel-making elsewhere because refineries weren’t able to obtain the 
crude they needed.
A Bharat Petroleum Corp. oil refinery in Mumbai.Photographer: Abeer 
Khan/Bloomberg
While crude supplies have gushed out of the region since an interim peace 
deal, the IEA warned that product and liquefied petroleum gas exports from the 
Gulf remain severely constrained, at less than half of prewar levels with 
several key export-oriented refineries yet to restart.

The IEA’s analysis shows an oil market that’s effectively split in two: crude 
markets are becoming well supplied, but refined product markets remain 
exceptionally tight. This “disconnect” has underpinned the rally in cracks and 
refinery margins, it said.

And with the summer driving season in the US now underway while supply 
restrictions persist, the situation doesn’t look like it will normalize any 
time soon.

—Alaric Nightingale, Bloomberg News

India’s frenzied efforts to counter disruptions in cooking gas supplies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/india-s-wartime-buying-spree-leaves-behind-a-cooking-gas-surplus?cmpid=BBBXT071026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6421>
 over the past months has left state-run fuel retailers with the opposite 
problem — more than they can sell. The three refiners run by the government 
booked liquefied petroleum gas supplies amounting to as much as 40,000 tons a 
day, according to people familiar with the situation, but the daily import 
requirement is now closer to 30,000 tons to 32,000 tons.

BNEF today

Between 2022 and June 2026, Japan awarded $4.5 billion in battery subsidies to 
rebuild domestic manufacturing. The planned overcapacity may force 
manufacturers to seek export markets where lower-cost rivals have the 
advantage, according toBloombergNEF <bbg://news/stories/THWLRAKK3NY8>. If all 
announced projects proceed, Japan’s domestic cell manufacturing capacity could 
rise from around 22.5 gigawatt-hours today to 79 gigawatt-hours by 2030.
