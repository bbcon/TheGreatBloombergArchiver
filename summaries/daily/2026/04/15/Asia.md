Chinese and broader Asian markets recovered further on Wednesday, with the Hang Seng rising 0.3% and stock benchmarks in China and the US having now recouped all their war-driven losses since the conflict began in late February. The recovery reflects cautious optimism that US-Iran negotiations will produce a durable de-escalation, though the structural disruption to energy flows continues to impose real costs on the region's industrial base.

The most consequential geopolitical signal from Beijing was Xi Jinping's meeting with Russian Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov at the Great Hall of the People. Xi emphasized that "the stability and certainty of China-Russia relations are particularly valuable" amid global turbulence — a formulation that signals Beijing is deepening its strategic alignment with Moscow at a moment when Russia's energy exports, particularly oil and gas, have become more valuable to China as Middle East flows tighten. Chinese oil imports from Saudi Arabia are set to halve next month, and China's overall oil and gas imports declined in March as the Hormuz disruption took effect. Russia's potential to fill that gap creates a structural incentive for Beijing to reinforce the relationship regardless of the war's outcome.

Southeast Asian sovereign credit quality is under pressure. S&P Global Ratings flagged Indonesia as particularly exposed to a prolonged conflict scenario, given that higher energy prices will widen its subsidy burden and current account deficit simultaneously. S&P's base case assumes Hormuz disruption peaks in April, but acknowledges that some disruption could persist for months given infrastructure damage. For Indonesia, and other energy-importing ASEAN sovereigns, the resolution timeline matters more than the headline ceasefire.

India's manufacturing ambitions remain constrained by China's dominance in batteries, EVs, and critical minerals, despite substantial Make in India investment. That dependency is deepening rather than narrowing, with up-and-coming Indian sectors becoming more reliant on Chinese technology even as geopolitical logic argues for diversification. The Port of Los Angeles chief's assessment — that supply chain normalization through Hormuz will take roughly four and a half months even after the strait reopens — implies sustained disruption to container flows through Asian trade hubs, where cargo for the Middle East is already piling up at ports in China, India, Japan, South Korea, and Singapore.
