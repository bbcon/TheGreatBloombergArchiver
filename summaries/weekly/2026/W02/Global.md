**Venezuela: Geopolitics, Energy, and Debt**

The single most consequential cross-regional development of the week was Venezuela's abrupt return to investable status following Nicolás Maduro's removal from power. Maduro pleaded not guilty this week to US narco-terrorism charges, and President Trump signaled Washington's intent to commit billions toward rehabilitating Venezuela's degraded oil infrastructure — effectively endorsing a US capital-backed reconstruction program. Sovereign and PDVSA bondholders captured an estimated $4 billion in mark-to-market gains in a single session, with the rally reflecting optionality on eventual restructuring rather than near-term debt service. Venezuela holds the world's largest proved crude reserves, though current output remains a fraction of its early-2000s peak following years of disinvestment and sanctions. A credible, US-backed reinvestment program would, over a multi-year horizon, add incremental heavy-sour barrels to global supply and represents a directionally bearish medium-term consideration for the oil curve, though lead times and execution risk are substantial. The layered creditor structure — defaulted sovereign and PDVSA bonds, Chinese and Russian bilateral claims, expropriation arbitration awards — makes a clean restructuring process far from assured, and the political transition in Caracas is not yet consolidated.

**The 2026 Macro Framework: Growth, Inflation, and the Limits of Consensus**

The sell-side's compiled outlooks for 2026, drawn from more than 60 institutions, frame the year as a late-cycle extension powered by AI capital expenditure and accommodative fiscal policy — an unusual configuration that has repeatedly confounded more cautious scenarios. The internally embedded tension, which most house views acknowledge rather than resolve, is that the same forces sustaining growth are likely to keep inflation stickier than the easing priced into G10 curves, creating recurring repricing risk around every data release that validates the upside scenario. The dollar-bearish consensus faces a similar internal inconsistency: if the Fed has less room to cut, narrowing rate differentials — the principal mechanical driver of dollar weakness — arrive later and more modestly than the consensus currently assumes.

**Trade and Geopolitics**

The broad characterization of 2026 as another difficult year for global trade reflects the accumulated weight of unresolved tariff regimes, bilateral tensions, and supply-chain reconfiguration that accelerated materially through 2024 and 2025. For export-dependent economies across Asia, Europe, and Latin America, the policy environment remains a more significant constraint on growth than the cyclical data alone would imply. Coverage during the week was thin on US-Iran dynamics, Strait of Hormuz risk, and energy transition developments; these themes remain in the analytical queue and will be revisited as the news flow develops.
