Hi, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai.

It was a done deal. Until it wasn’t.

Meta Platforms had paid $2 billion late last year to acquire Chinese AI 
startup Manus, whose employees had already moved into the Facebook owner’s 
Singapore offices.

Then very late in the game — seemingly after the final buzzer sounded — Beijing
decided this week to call a foul 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/china-blocks-meta-s-2-billion-acquisition-of-ai-startup-manus?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
.

Manus caused something of a sensation when it launched last year because as an 
AI agent it is capable of carrying out tasks at the level of, say, a junior 
stock analyst or a travel agent. It canwrite and run code 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/china-s-meta-backlash-renders-manus-model-officially-dead?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
, manage files and book flights – though like most new tech it had glitches to 
work through.

Beijing told the two companies to undo their deal within weeks and restore 
Manus’s Chinese assets to their original state, with unspecified penalties 
considered if they don’t comply. It is unclear if this is even possible, though 
there is a precedent of sorts when Beijing forced Didi to undo its 2021 IPO in 
New York because of regulatory concerns.

Chinese authorities were apparently concerned about the loss of an important 
new AI advance and talent to the US 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/xi-tests-china-s-reach-by-blocking-meta-deal-that-s-already-done?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
, its main geopolitical rival, at a time when global leadership in the sector 
is at stake. The decision to block the deal also seems to bea warning shot to 
other homegrown startups 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/china-s-meta-backlash-renders-manus-model-officially-dead?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 thinking about setting up shop overseas to attract US capital and avoid 
regulatory scrutiny.

That’s a pretty chilling message to such companies, especially since the dust 
has barely settled on Beijing’s yearslong crackdown on the tech industry.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-04-28/why-is-china-blocking-meta-s-acquisition-of-manus-video>
The Manus deal drew fire in China for leaking tech to the US.
While reversing the Manus deal may indeed be the top priority for Beijing, 
sending a strong message to those firms 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/chinese-billionaire-overhauls-ai-startup-after-warning-on-manus?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 appears to be pretty important. Killing the chicken to scare the monkey is the 
Chinese phrase that often surfaces in these situations.

Manus backers such as Tencent may have to return their windfall. The pressure 
on Meta is less clear given Facebook is blocked in the country, though the firm 
still derives a significant chunk of revenue via advertisers based in the 
world’s No. 2 economy.

Manus co-founders Xiao Hong and Ji Yichao have been barred from leaving China, 
and it remains unclear what other action Beijing could take to unravel the 
transaction, which catapulted Meta into competition in AI agents with leading 
firms like Google, OpenAI and Anthropic.

Chinese authorities seem also to have been miffed that the startup 
restructured to make a sale to a foreign company possible before Beijing did 
any regulatory review. Manus was a Singapore-based firm but its founders hailed 
from China.

Regulators in the country have already issued rules to prevent them from being 
blindsided in the future 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-24/china-to-curb-us-investment-in-tech-companies-after-meta-deal?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
. Agencies including the National Development and Reform Commission, the 
country’s powerful state planner, have apparently told several private firms in 
recent weeks they should reject capital of US origin in funding rounds unless 
explicitly approved.

China’s maneuvering has rekindled fears that it hasn’t learned from past 
experience thatheavy-handed moves 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-17/china-investors-are-starting-to-believe-xi-tech-crackdown-is-over?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 that run counter to the rules of capitalism undermine the country’s mantra 
that it’s open for business. One of the main downsides of the tech crackdown 
was that it stifled innovation and discouraged foreign investment.

Beijing’s move could send a message to entrepreneurs and foreign investors 
that political risk can curdle commercial success, and maydamage the country’s 
efforts to lure back international capital 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-26/china-s-manus-ai-clampdown-will-backfire?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 and support the private sector, according to Bloomberg Opinion columnist 
Catherine Thorbecke.

Nevertheless, Beijing seems willing to pay that price to prevent US investors 
from taking stakes in sensitive sectors where national security is a priority. 
After all,Washington is taking action in this regard 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-23/ai-export-control-measures-aimed-at-china-gain-steam-in-us-house?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
. It has restricted investments into certain Chinese technology sectors for 
fear of helping advance its military or economic might. In 2025, US rules 
designed to curb investment in Chinese-owned semiconductor, quantum and AI 
companies took effect.

Parallel fundraising structures have gained popularity since the US restricted 
American investment into certain Chinese tech companies including those in 
advanced semiconductors and AI systems.

Ultimately, China’s concern may be about protecting its own investment in tech 
as the superstructure supporting decades of future economic growth. Bloomberg 
columnist Shuli Ren writes that the nation can make “some claims to Manus’ 
success because innovative breakthroughs would not have happened had Beijing 
not spent big on higher education, creating a young, cheap and abundant 
engineering pool.”

If more startups followed Manus’s path, she wrote 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-04-28/china-s-xi-is-right-to-close-the-pandora-s-box-manus-opened?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
, “what’s the point of funding higher education and academic research in China?”

Banking on Biotech

As biotechnology emerges as a new front in geopolitical rivalries, China is 
racing to build a vast network of labs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-04-27/china-races-to-build-world-s-largest-biobank-to-rival-us-drugs-research?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 to collect, process, store and analyze biological data.

Biobanks — huge repositories of biomedical data and biological samples like 
blood, saliva and cells — form the backbone of the global bioresearch economy 
set to be worth trillions of dollars.

Some of the world’s most influential biobanks have already reshaped modern 
medicine. The UK’s contains genetic and health data from half a million people. 
It has helped inform more than 18,000 scientific publications, including 
research on Covid-19 vaccines, dementia and early cancer detection.

In the US, the All of Us program has supported almost 25,000 research projects 
and helped identify 275 million previously unreported genetic variants.
A technician at the biobank at the China Institute for Brain Research in 
Beijing.Photographer: Andrea Verdelli/Bloomberg
China’s efforts in this field began later than in many Western countries. For 
decades, its researchers have relied on foreign databases for high-impact 
research but more systematic state backing came in the past decade.

China designated biomedical data collection and storage as a national priority 
more than a decade ago and listed building a “biomedical big data platform” as 
a key innovation target in 2016. In its latest five-year plan, Beijing
reiterated the importance of “self-reliance” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/china-s-new-push-against-rat-race-what-s-changed-versus-2025?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 in science and tech facilities.

The effort gained urgency as the US and its allies tighten controls on access 
and collaboration with China. Last year, Washington blocked researchers from 
China from accessing 21 biomedical datasets maintained by the National 
Institutes of Health, citing concern that sensitive genetic information could 
be exploited to threaten national security, public health, or provide military 
advantages.

In the UK, politicians have raised questions over Chinese access to its 
biobank data, while the European Union barred organizations from the country 
from participating in its €93.5 billion program Horizon Europe.

For Chinese scientists, having access to homegrown biobanks should accelerate 
the advance of biotechnology companies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-07-13/china-drugmakers-catching-up-to-us-big-pharma-with-new-medicine-innovation?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
 and cementthe country’s place as a global innovation powerhouse 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-18/china-ramps-up-animal-testing-chasing-medical-innovation-biotech-dominance?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
. Large-scale databases are particularly valuable in the era of AI because 
high-quality, standardized biological and medical data are essential to 
training medical systems that can assist in diagnosis, predict disease risk and 
accelerate drug discovery.

“China wants to achieve a self-reliance of the biomedical dataset, as part of 
its efforts achieving what Xi Jinping called holistic security,” said Huang 
Yanzhong, senior fellow for global health at the Council on Foreign Relations. 
“China doesn’t want to be subject to control by other countries, especially 
those they have geopolitical tensions with.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/audio/2026-04-28/big-take-asia-north-korea-nuclear-arsenal-worries-us-podcast>
A fragmented biobank system means becoming self-reliant will take time but 
many experts say closing the gap will happen sooner or later. The bigger 
question then is what happens next and whether the changes happening in China 
and elsewhere lead to more scientific collaboration or even worse divisions.

For starters, it is unclear what access the rest of the world will have to 
China’s growing biobank system. Although the country does not officially 
exclude overseas researchers from accessing data, permission is governed by a 
tiered system based on how sensitive data are and whether they are working in 
collaboration with Chinese scientists.

Joy Zhang, founding director of Centre for Global Science and Epistemic 
Justice at the University of Kent, said Western and Chinese scientists are 
entering an era of “unnecessary mutual alienation and suspicion.”

That isn’t ideal, she said, because “our common enemy is not China or the US. 
Our common enemy is public health issues.”

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

This week, a woman’s claim that she was mistreated by police after she tried 
to stop a man from smoking at a bus stop in Shenzhen sparked public anger, with 
many people accusing the authorities of being heavy-handed and selectively 
enforcing the law.

The 29-year-old woman said on Weibo she was waiting for a bus with her friends 
recently when a man began smoking at the stop — a violation of the city’s 
regulations, which prohibit lighting up on public transport and in waiting 
areas. She confronted him but he refused to stop, and the two got into an 
argument.

The woman said she poured her drink onto the man’s cigarette to extinguish it. 
The man picked up the cup and threw the remaining liquid on her. She then 
called the police.

She said officers took both of them to a station, where she was allegedly 
strip-searched and threatened with five days of detention on suspicion of 
“insulting others.” After being held for nearly five hours, she agreed to reach 
a settlement with the smoker and was allowed to leave.
A woman smokes a cigarette outside a shop in Beijing.Photographer: Wang 
Zhao/AFP/Getty Images
Her posts were later removed from Weibo after they set off a public backlash. 
Many social media users sided with the woman, accusing police of overreach 
while failing to enforce the city’s regulations.

“So why on earth was the woman subjected to a strip search?” one person wrote 
in a Weibo post that got nearly 160,000 likes. “This is absolutely ridiculous.”

Shenzhen introduced sweeping tobacco control regulations in 2014 that were 
among the strictest in China. The rules ban smoking in all indoor public 
spaces, workplaces, public transport and bus stops, subway entrances and 
high-speed rail platforms.

But, as in many Chinese cities, enforcement has been patchy at best 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-09-09/this-megacity-is-pushing-back-against-the-dangers-of-secondhand-smoke?cmpid=BBD043026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3921>
.

As criticism mounted, Shenzhen authorities issued a statement saying they had 
investigated the incident and fined the smoker. The statement said both parties 
underwent “physical safety checks” — without addressing the woman’s 
strip-search claim — and added that the two sides had “voluntarily” reached a 
settlement.

The woman told media outlet Caixin that the statement “glossed over the key 
points.”

“I feel humiliated,” she said. “I had no choice but to settle because I need 
to move on with my life and work.”
