 – even my 16-year-old son showing unprecedented curiosity about copper futures.

The sudden interest in metals was a worldwide phenomenon, as powerful rallies 
in everything from gold to copper and tin became an all-in momentum trade that 
ended with plunging prices and days of volatility.

And a lot of this so-called “hot money” was in China, where a wave of 
investors – driven in part by social media excitement and influential 
commentators – piled into hard commodities on the promise of big short-term 
gains. Faced with a slowing economy and diminishing returns from property or 
the stock market, many were keen to jump on themetals wagon 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-03/china-s-speculative-gold-rush-tests-risk-appetite-in-beijing?cmpid=BBD020626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
.

Deutsche Bank analysts note “meaningful signs that investors in China may be a 
significant driving force in precious investment trends,” fueled by the 
relative lack of other avenues to channel their cash.

But some lost hard when prices beat a retreat, such as this rookie trader

 who was left with a 750,000 yuan ($108,120) loss after opening a futures 
account on the advice of a friend. Her losses add to China’s long history of 
novice investors hurt when the excitement ebbed in spectacular fashion.

China’s top four gold exchange-traded funds – one route for exposure to the 
precious metal – racked up their biggest-ever daily outflows of nearly a $1 
billion on Tuesday as investors headed for the exits. The country’s only 
pure-play silver fund got pushback after switching its valuation model in 
response to the global ructions.

It looks like a classic case of a speculative run-up and collapse. But 
stepping back from the froth and frenzy of the past week, we can see that the 
market convulsions also reflect bigger shifts for China and profound changes in 
the global economy.

It all reminds me of an earlier China commodity frenzy — what I’ve decided to 
call the Great Steel Craze of 2016.

Then, one of Beijing’s last major bursts of property-and-infrastructure 
stimulus was supercharging steel demand. Investors pounced on products like 
rebar and hot-rolled coil used in construction, in a wave of super-velocity 
trading that triggered huge price spikes. (An aside: Shanghai steel futures 
just had one of the quietest weeks ever.)

This time, the market action looked similar but the drivers are very different.

Initial gains were triggered by concerns about currency debasement, the 
Federal Reserve’s independence and rising geopolitical tensions – stoked by 
President Donald Trump’s tumultuous foreign policy during January. China 
underpinned gold’s ascent in 2025 with its wave of central-bank purchases for 
state reserves, also part of the dollar diversification theme that’s 
intensified of late.

Base metals like copper, tin or aluminum have also pulled attention, not just 
because of growing demand from renewables, power grids and AI infrastructure, 
but because it’s increasingly seen as a strategic resource. Copper futures in 
Shanghai had by far their biggest monthly volumes on record in January.

Few of these drivers are expected to fade away, and it’s not hard to find 
investors in China who remain bullish on metals — especially gold — over the 
longer term. Any fresh geopolitical stresses or major dollar-bearish events 
could refresh interest in the complex.

This won’t be the last time that investment flows in Shanghai fuel big swings 
in global metals markets.

Speed Bump

China’s booming electric vehicle industry hit a speed bump this week as 
authorities announced aban on concealed door handles <> in an unprecedented 
move to boost driver safety following several deadly crashes.

Cars sold in China will be required to have mechanical release both on the 
inside and outside, the government said.

The new safety rules, which will take effect at the start of next year, follow 
several high-profile incidents where power failures after crashes weresuspected 
<> to have prevented electronically operated handles from opening. Critics say 
the design, popularized by Tesla, can complicate rescue efforts and puts the 
onus on passengers to find manual release latches they’re not accustomed to 
using.

The rules represent an historic shift in global safety standards, where China 
– not Europe and the US – will increasingly set the tone.

The Asian nation’s powerful position in EV manufacturing and smart driving 
technology means any new regulations are likely to be followed by the rest of 
the world, according to Bill Russo, founder of Shanghai-based consultancy 
Automobility.

“By moving first, Beijing can use its huge domestic market to lock in safety 
standards that both Chinese and foreign automakers must follow at home – and 
that may ultimately travel with Chinese EV exports and influence global norms,” 
he said.

Tesla’s doors are already the target of a safety probe in the US, while 
European regulators are looking to impose rules of their own.

About 60% of the top 100 selling new-energy vehicles in April featured 
concealed handles, the China Daily has reported. Tesla’s Model Y and Model 3, 
Xiaomi’s YU7 sport utility vehicle, Nio’s ES8 and Xpeng’s P7 are among the EVs 
to be affected.

Redesign efforts will be heavily tilted toward higher-margin luxury models, 
which typically have the sleek, futuristic construction that will soon be 
banned. While it’s unclear how expensive it will be to implement the new rules, 
one person familiar with the design process at a Chinese EV maker said it could 
cost more than 100 million yuan ($14.4 million) per model.

Shares of Chinese EV makers in Hong Kong fell <> on the news, with BYD, the 
world’s top EV maker, sliding 1.1% and Volkswagen-backed Xpeng dropping to the 
lowest level in almost 10 months.
Xiaomi Corp. YU7 EVs at one of the company’s stores in Beijing.Bloomberg
The regulation also delineates where the handles and signs should be placed. 
On the door’s exterior, there must be a recessed space measuring at least 6 
centimeters by 2 centimeters (2.4 inches by 0.8 inches) for a hand to grasp a 
handle. Inside, cars must have signage no smaller than 1cm by 0.7cm to indicate 
how to open the door.

Tesla, which became the subject of a US safety investigation after a Bloomberg 
News investigation <> into fatal incidents involving its doors, has said it 
will make necessary changes for China. BYD executive vice president Stella Li 
said in an interview with Bloomberg TV that the impact on the company was 
limited and it’s ready to make changes to its designs as required by the 
government.

Indeed. BYD read the writing on the wall as the early stages of the regulatory 
overhaul unfolded, going back to the future by reverting to traditional, 
exposed handles for its Seal 06 model.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

The internet is already getting in the mood for the Lunar New Year. Many users 
had a good laugh at a video by an online celebrity mocking the Spring Festival 
Gala — China’s most-watched, and arguably most-criticized — annual TV event, 
and were outraged when the clip was censored.

The comedian, Jiang Yilei, better known as Papi Jiang, rose to fame a decade 
ago for her rapid-fire satirical monologues ranting about everyday 
frustrations, from dating and dieting to workplace stress. Her latest video, 
released last weekend, took aim at the state broadcaster’s Chinese New Year’s 
Eve extravaganza.

Since its debut more than four decades ago, the Spring Festival Gala has 
become a tradition for millions of families. But its appeal has waned among 
younger viewers, who increasingly dismiss the show as too stale, formulaic, or 
simply dull.

In her video, Jiang plays a fictional gala director auditioning sketches for 
inclusion, dismissing one after another for a variety of reasons: some were not 
funny enough, others too funny but lacking “positive energy,” some were guilty 
of regional stereotyping or insufficient use of internet buzzwords.

“Listen, we shouldn’t worry about getting slammed by the audience,” Jiang 
quips in the clip. “The more they complain, the more they watch — and the more 
they watch, the more they complain. They watch every year just to criticize it, 
and they criticize it every year while they watch.”

The video was scrubbed from the internet two days later, triggering another 
wave of criticism.

“They’re too thin-skinned — no wonder nobody’s watching anymore,” a user said.
