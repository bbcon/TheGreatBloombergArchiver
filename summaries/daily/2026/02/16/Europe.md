# European Morning Briefing — February 16, 2026

**ECB signals global euro ambitions amid succession questions.** The European Central Bank said it stands ready to provide euro liquidity to monetary authorities globally, an attempt to forestall market stress and broaden international use of the single currency. The move fits a wider European push to assert monetary autonomy as the transatlantic relationship is recalibrated — US Secretary of State Marco Rubio said over the weekend that the US-Europe alliance "has to change." Separately, succession planning at the ECB is gaining urgency. François Villeroy de Galhau's surprise resignation from the Bank of France hands the appointment to outgoing President Macron rather than a potential National Rally successor, and with Christine Lagarde's term expiring end-2027, Philip Lane departing in May 2027, and Isabel Schnabel's board seat opening in December 2027, governments may pursue a package deal. Whether Macron's preference for an accelerated, bundled appointment finds support among other capitals remains to be seen.

**EU carbon market faces political stress test.** EU carbon permits fell to their lowest since July as governments openly debate weakening the Emissions Trading System. Slovakia's Fico has called for suspension, Germany's Merz floated revision before softening his stance, while Macron defended the program. Commission President von der Leyen pledged a reform proposal for Q3. JPMorgan analysts noted the lack of visibility is impeding long-term green capex planning — a tension worth monitoring given Europe's broader industrial competitiveness debate.

**UK: BOE in data-dependent mode; Starmer turns to AI.** A heavy UK data week — labor market Tuesday, CPI Wednesday, retail sales and public finances Friday — follows a 5-4 BOE hold this month, with rates tied with the Fed's upper bound at the G-7's highest. UK house prices stalled in February with buyers enjoying the widest choice in a decade, consistent with a softening demand backdrop. Prime Minister Starmer is set to outline regulation requiring AI chatbots to comply with child online safety laws.

**Periphery and corporates.** Sweden's establishment is tentatively reopening the euro adoption debate, framed increasingly through geopolitical rather than purely economic lenses. Switzerland's economy rebounded in Q4 despite outsized US tariffs. The Stoxx 600 rose 0.32% in quiet trade with US markets closed. Notable single-name moves: Norsk Hydro fell 4.4% on downgrades from Goldman and RBC following Friday's weak guidance; Pinewood Technologies tumbled 32% after Apax abandoned its bid citing market conditions; KGHM declined 3.3% on a Citi downgrade.
