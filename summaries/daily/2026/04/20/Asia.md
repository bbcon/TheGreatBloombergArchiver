Asian equity markets were largely muted Monday, with the CSI 300 edging up 0.6% even as oil prices surged, reflecting a degree of investor compartmentalization between energy market disruption and Chinese domestic equity sentiment. The divergence is notable given China's acute exposure to the Strait of Hormuz closure: roughly 20% of the world's oil transits the waterway, and tanker flows had fallen to near-zero in the weeks following the outbreak of conflict. China is responding at the margin by pivoting to alternative feedstocks — US ethane imports are expected to hit a record 800,000 tons in April, approximately 60% above the monthly average — while clean technology exports surged in March, with lithium-ion battery shipments up 34% year-on-year and electric vehicles up 53%, benefiting from heightened global demand for non-fossil alternatives.

At the IMF spring meetings in Washington last week, China's delegation mounted a robust defense of its record trade surpluses, arguing that imbalances reflect cultural savings preferences rather than policy distortions and pointing to rising imports as evidence of an improving trajectory. The argument drew skepticism from other delegations, including Japan's finance minister, who described the presentation as ultimately unconvincing. The episode signals a more assertive posture from Beijing on external criticism of its economic model.

Elsewhere in the region, North Korea conducted a series of five tactical ballistic missile tests, variants of the Hwasong-11, which Kim Jong Un praised effusively. The launches come amid ongoing concern about Pyongyang accumulating real combat experience through its troop deployments supporting Russia in Ukraine. Japan meanwhile participated for the first time in major naval exercises with the US and the Philippines, even as China's navy conducted drills in the western Pacific — a parallel display of posturing that underscores the widening military competition west of the First Island Chain. Singapore is procuring additional LNG supplies as the Hormuz disruption curtails flows from Qatar and the UAE.
