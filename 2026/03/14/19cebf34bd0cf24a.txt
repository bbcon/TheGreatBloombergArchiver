Iran War Darkens Outlook for Gulf Ambitions, Trump ‘Deals’
Why Trump’s “deals” may wilt.

When Donald Trump returned to the White House last year, Persian Gulf nations 
made a big bet on improved US ties. They ramped up oil output and made broad, 
multitrillion-dollar pledges of American investment and purchases. They even 
gifted the US ahighly controversial 
<https://www.cnbc.com/2025/05/20/qatari-pm-denies-jumbo-jet-gift-to-trump-is-bribery.html>
 luxury jet — for Trump’s use.

Hosting the US president on his first major overseas trip last May, Gulf 
leaders laid out grand visions for new initiatives, such as the build-out of
artificial intelligence infrastructure 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-15/trump-s-rush-to-cut-ai-deals-in-saudi-arabia-and-uae-opens-rift-with-china-hawks?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 using US-designed semiconductors. Enthusiasm in Saudi Arabia ran so high that 
parallels were made to the legendary 1945 meeting between President Franklin D. 
Roosevelt and King Abdulaziz Al Saud (as this newsletternoted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-05-17/saudi-arabia-plays-trump-card-in-us-china-dance?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 at the time).

Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman told Trump he would commit $600 billion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-18/trump-s-warm-welcome-for-saudi-crown-prince-masks-scant-rewards?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 in US investments, with vague promises of reaching $1 trillion. The United 
Arab Emirates told Trump it would invest$1.4 trillion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-21/uae-to-pledge-1-4-trillion-us-investment-after-trump-meeting?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 over a decade, while Qatar’s wealth fund promised him$500 billion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-15/qatar-s-wealth-fund-plans-500-billion-us-push-over-next-decade?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 in the same period. More concretely, OPEC+ last Aprildramatically reversed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-03/opec-stuns-oil-market-with-policy-switch-to-drive-down-prices?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 its push for high oil prices with a move to boost crude output.

Implicit to all those pledges and action was an expectation the US would 
maintain stability in the Middle East, says Ziad Daoud, chief emerging markets 
economist at Bloomberg Economics. But two weeks into the US-Israel war with 
Iran, that’s been entirely swept away. While in some ways Gulf economies may be 
more resilient than many might think, Daoud says, it’s hard not to see a 
significantdownsizing of all of those lofty promises 
<https://www.bbc.com/news/articles/cn5yxp2v77ro>.

 before the Iran war.

 disrupted by Iran war.
The implicit quid-pro-quo for showering Trump with pledges of investment was 
“do not set the Middle East on fire,” Daoud says. Yet disruptions began not 
long after he completed his three-nation,four-day tour 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-16/gold-palaces-jet-deals-and-mcdonald-s-trump-at-home-in-gulf?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
. In June came the so-called12-day war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-25/trump-says-us-iran-to-hold-talks-next-week-mideast-war-over?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 between Israel and Iran, during which the US launched a surprise attack on 
Iran’s nuclear development sites. Then in September, Israeli Prime Minister 
Benjamin Netanyahu ordered anattack on Doha 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-12/trump-to-meet-qatari-pm-friday-amid-uproar-over-israeli-strike?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
. Trump, who said hedidn’t know of Netanyahu’s plans 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-29/netanyahu-said-to-apologize-to-qatar-for-deadly-israel-missile-strike-on-hamas?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
, visited the city just four months earlier.

Now, Gulf Cooperation Council nations are finding their key source of revenue, 
energy exports, throttled by Trump’s decision with Netanyahu to wage war on 
Iran. Even with its capacities degraded by US weaponry, Tehran has well applied 
its leverage inshutting passage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-07/in-charts-how-the-iran-conflict-is-disrupting-global-trade?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 of all kinds of cargo through the Strait of Hormuz. GCC members including the 
UAE, Kuwait, Qatar and Saudi Arabia have also seen their oil and gas 
infrastructure attacked by Iranian drones and missiles.
Crude oil storage tanks at a refinery in Ras Tanura, Saudi Arabia.
Photographer: Simon Dawson/Bloomberg
For some Gulf nations, these amount to the first major attacks in their 
history. The war has dealt the region a large financial and economic blow, the 
magnitude of which only time will tell. Besides foregoneenergy revenues 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/iran-war-oil-and-gas-supply-squeeze-and-strait-of-hormuz-disruption-explained?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
, there’s also been lost business in other industries — such as tourism, 
walloped by the crippling of some of the world’s busiest airports.

Along with the hit to receipts, these countries will face fiscal pressure on 
the spending side, given the repair bill for fixing damaged infrastructure. And 
there will be more military spending, since it’s now clear the Pentagon can’t 
guarantee their protection (a prospect which, ironically, may ultimately 
benefit US defense contractors.)

With less money available, the simple math dictates there’ll be less spending 
abroad (outside of defense goods), including on the investment “deals” Trump 
made. Whatever the GCC was going to end up distributing before, the final 
figures will be less now, Daoud says.

More broadly, the Iran conflict has cast a shadow over the region’s visions of 
becoming a global economic heavyweight in areas other than oil. Even before the 
war, fiscal realities in Saudi Arabia had prompted ascaling back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-28/saudi-arabia-enters-new-era-of-restraint-on-megaproject-spend?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 of some initiatives. Now, the conflict raises questions in particular about 
ambitions for the Middle East to become a key cog in global AI infrastructure.

With abundant cheap energy, places including the UAE and Saudi Arabia had a 
natural advantage for the build-out of electricity-consuming data centers. 
Another lure was the absence of the permitting and regulatory constraints seen 
in some nations.
 <>The 747 aircraft that Qatar donated to the US as a new Air Force One for 
Donald Trump.Photographer: Roberto Schmidt/AFP/Getty Images
Gulf nations had also moved to address US concerns about links with China, 
creating the possibility of the Middle East becoming a powerful channel for 
expanding the American AI industry’sglobal footprint 
<https://www.csis.org/analysis/if-compute-new-oil-war-gulf-significantly-raises-stakes>
. G42, the UAE’s key AI holding company, said it cut ties with Huawei 
Technologies Co. and divested from Chinese assets. Its budding business in
places like Vietnam 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/abu-dhabi-s-g42-leads-1-billion-data-center-project-in-vietnam?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 showcased how a US-Gulf AI alliance could spread American technology versus 
that from China.

Now, the Iran war makes the “timeline and trajectory” of the Middle East’s AI 
push more uncertain, says Michael Deng, a geoeconomics technology analyst at 
Bloomberg Economics who previously served as a policy adviser at the US 
Commerce Department. Iran’s strikes on Gulf nations have includedattacks on 
data centers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/drone-strikes-damage-amazon-data-centers-in-the-uae-and-bahrain?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 belonging to Amazon Web Services Inc. in the UAE and Bahrain.

“The strikes highlighted a key vulnerability: Gulf states went to great 
lengths to meet Washington’s demands on chip security. Physical security was 
taken for granted,” Deng wrote in anote 
<https://blinks.bloomberg.com/screens/DOCV%20NSN%20TBE9IXT96OSO> last week. 
“The Gulf still has capital, energy, and ambition that few other regions can 
match. But a key assumption that underwrote the entire partnership — that the 
Gulf was a safe place for large-scale tech investment — has been tested.”

It’s also an open question how strong the region’s pull will be for the 
overseas talent that it requires for sectors including AI and finance.

Daoud is sanguine over the longer haul. One thing that won’t change is the 
advantages the Middle East’s geography has: It’s a natural hub for aviation, 
logistics, and intercontinental data flows.

And of course it’s still in command of immense pools of capital. But the more 
immediate horizon appears awhole lot less rosy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/iran-war-trauma-of-middle-east-conflict-will-reshape-gulf-arab-states?cmpid=BBD031426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260314>
 than it did last month.—Chris Anstey
