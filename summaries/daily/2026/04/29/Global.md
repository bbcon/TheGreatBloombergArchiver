The most consequential development of the day is the US decision to prepare for an extended naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, with President Trump reportedly concluding this represents a lower-risk path than either resumed bombing or disengagement. The implication is a prolonged period of neither active hostilities nor resolution — a structural supply shock rather than an acute one. Brent traded above $114 a barrel, with December futures reaching new highs, signaling that markets are now pricing the blockade as a durable condition rather than a temporary disruption. The US also threatened secondary sanctions on banks supporting Chinese private refiners purchasing Iranian oil, adding financial pressure on Beijing at a moment when the planned Trump-Xi summit in May was already navigating tensions over technology and trade.

The UAE's shock exit from OPEC after 60 years adds a further destabilizing dimension to energy markets. The decision appears driven less by frustration with Saudi Arabia's near-term output policy — Riyadh has itself embraced looser supply management recently — than by a strategic imperative to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand. Abu Dhabi's urgency to pump reflects a view that the demand plateau may arrive within the next decade, undermining OPEC's core price-defense mechanism. The departure blindsided other members and raises questions about the cartel's long-term cohesion.

Chip supply chains are emerging as an underappreciated casualty of the conflict. PGMEA, a photoresist chemical derived from naphtha and critical to semiconductor fabrication, has become harder to source since the war began. Shin-Etsu Chemical, Japan's largest chemical company, withheld its full-year guidance, citing naphtha-derived supply constraints; its most recent quarterly operating income fell 13% and missed estimates. Analysts note that photoresist disruptions take months to propagate through complex supply chains and are difficult to reverse — though for now, the broader semiconductor industry appears to have sufficient inventory buffers to absorb near-term pressure. The Bloomberg agriculture spot index has also hit a two-year high, up 13% over three months, as fertilizer supply disruptions and harvest uncertainty compound food price pressures globally.
