 Oil and the dollar 



As war spirals in the Middle East, one driver is increasingly overshadowing 
others when it comes to the dollar:the price of oil <https://bloom.bg/4cGPPwu>.

In just a week, the historic surge in energy prices has upended inflation and 
growth outlooks across the world. That includes the US, where Treasury prices 
have been sinking. The dollar, nonetheless, has marched higher.

Its rise has been catalyzed by the US’s position as the world’s top oil 
producer and by the dollar’s role as the currency for global crude trade.The 
surge stands in contrast to the dollar weakness since Donald Trump’s 
inauguration, prompted in part by his trade and economic policies. The 
Bloomberg dollar index is still down some 6% in the past year.

The only currencies that have strengthened against the greenback since the war 
broke out are the Canadian and Australian dollars, both of which are also 
highly linked to energy prices <https://bloom.bg/4b5QXZa>. A measure of the 
near-term correlation between oil and the dollar turned sharply positive this 
month.

“The market has quickly realized that only a couple of trades work,” said 
Nathan Thooft of Manulife Investment Management. “Those are oil and the 
dollar.” —Carter Johnson <https://bloom.bg/4gt6UZB> and Vassilis Karamanis

 China’s insulation from shocks 



Another relative investment haven that’s emerged as the war sent oil prices 
soaring is, paradoxically, the world’s largest crude importer: China.

Chinese stocks have fallen less than global peers since the conflict began 
<https://bloom.bg/3NjPfu1>, the yuan has held steady against the dollar and 
government bond yields have barely moved. Together, this amounts to surprising 
resilience in a crisis that, at first glance, appeared to leave the country 
vulnerable.

For decades Beijing has sought to insulate its economy from precisely this 
kind of shock. It poured investments into renewables, secured dominance across 
much of the clean-energy supply chain and promoted electric vehicles at a 
remarkable speed.The result is an economy still dependent on imported fossil 
fuels but less beholden to them than before — providing some protection as oil 
prices have jumped as much as 65%.

“Chinese asset classes are something that is missed by global investors as a 
safe haven,” said Cary Yeung, head of Greater China debt at Pictet Asset 
Management. —Iris Ouyang <https://bloom.bg/4cCa4v6> and Lin Zhu 
<https://bloom.bg/3N7TngN>
