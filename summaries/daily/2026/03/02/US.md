US equity markets opened under significant pressure on Monday, with S&P 500 futures declining approximately 1.0% as the US-Israeli strikes on Iran upended investor sentiment that, until the weekend, had sat at its most optimistic since the pandemic. Defense names proved a clear exception: Lockheed Martin rose 7.1%, RTX gained 6.5%, and Northrop Grumman advanced 5.2% in premarket trading. Airlines fell sharply — United Airlines down 5.7%, Delta off 5.1% — on the combination of higher fuel costs and regional travel disruption. Oil majors advanced, with Exxon Mobil up roughly 5% and Conoco gaining 6%.

The Treasury market behaved unexpectedly for a risk-off session, with the 10-year yield rising around 3 basis points to approximately 3.97%, rather than declining as a traditional haven bid would imply. The most plausible explanation is the inflationary signal embedded in an oil spike: rising energy prices reduce the probability of near-term Federal Reserve rate cuts, and money markets scaled back easing wagers accordingly. Société Générale's historical analysis of five oil supply shocks over the past 50 years found the 10-year Treasury consistently weaker at one-week, three-month, and six-month horizons. Gold, rising to $5,385 per ounce, appears to be the haven of preference in the current environment.

Politically, the conflict raises a distinct domestic variable. The Trump administration's decision to target Iran's supreme leader represents a departure from decades of US foreign policy convention, and the president's stated objectives have remained notably fluid — ranging from a deal with new Iranian leadership to a campaign lasting four to five weeks. With midterm elections in November, the inflationary consequences of a sustained oil price elevation carry political weight, as consumer affordability concerns remain elevated. The Federal Reserve transition, with a new chair assuming the role in roughly two months, adds a further layer of institutional uncertainty at a sensitive moment.

Berkshire Hathaway's Class B shares slipped 1.7% after the conglomerate reported a near-30% decline in operating profit in what was Warren Buffett's final quarter as CEO, with analysts flagging insurance business weakness.
