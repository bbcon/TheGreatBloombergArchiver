US-Iran Talks Make a Good Start But Oil Markets Can’t Relax Just Yet

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Anthony Di Paola looks at reactions to the first round of US-Iran 
talks.

Oil prices are sending a pretty clear signal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-feb-9?cmpid=BBBXT020926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
 on the first round of US-Iran negotiations aimed at avoiding war: Talking is 
good.

Brent crude opened lower on Monday following discussions in Oman about Iran’s 
nuclear ambitions. President Donald Trump said the talks were “very good 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/iran-says-talks-with-us-to-de-escalate-tensions-had-good-start-mlb106se?cmpid=BBBXT020926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
” and would continue this week, while his Iranian counterpart Masoud Pezeshkian 
called them “a step forward 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/iran-calls-us-nuclear-talks-a-step-forward-as-caution-lingers?cmpid=BBBXT020926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
.”

It’s a positive start, but oil traders can’t completely relax just yet.

Brent is still up more than 10% since the start of the year, and some of that 
premium is related to geopolitical tensions in the Persian Gulf.

“The Iranian risk premium cannot be fully defused as long as US warships are 
located where they are with their guns and rockets pointing toward Iran,” SEB’s 
chief commodities analyst Bjarne Schieldrop said in a note.
Abbas Araghchi, center, in Muscat, Oman on Feb. 6.Iranian Foreign Ministry
Even with the positive start to the talks, there are still plenty of potential 
sticking points 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-29/the-complex-history-of-us-iran-relations-a-visual-timeline?cmpid=BBBXT020926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260209>
.

While Trump has repeatedly said Iran must be prevented from gaining a nuclear 
weapon, other officials such as Secretary of State Marco Rubio have said 
negotiations should be much broader — addressing Iran’s ballistic missile 
program and funding of proxy groups in regional states, like Lebanon and Yemen.

Iranian officials have stressed that they will only entertain negotiations 
around the nuclear program.

Trump, who sent his Middle East envoy and long-time New York real estate pal 
Steve Witkoff along with son-in-law Jared Kushner to Oman, said over the 
weekend that a deal restricted to the nuclear program would be acceptable.

A limited accord may be what the president needs. Avoiding war in the Middle 
East and being able to crow about lower US gasoline prices would be a 
short-term win for the administration ahead of mid-term elections.

But Trump is unpredictable. The last time his administration was talking to 
Tehran about a nuclear deal, the US ended up joining Israel’s 12-day war on the 
Islamic Republic.

“There’s still plenty of uncertainty over how things will evolve,” analysts at 
ING said in a note.

—Anthony Di Paola, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

In Saudi Arabia, photovoltaic cells and batteries could supply 99.5% 
availability for a baseload demand source like a data center, for a cost of 
just $70 per megawatt-hour, BloombergNEF estimates. That’s more expensive than 
gas at $40-49/MWh, but not massively so. The United Arab Emirates, which has 
similar climatic conditions, also plans to capitalize on the favorable 
economics of solar and wind to run data centers.
