US equity markets maintained a broadly constructive tone on Friday, led by technology. Intel surged approximately 20% in premarket trading after delivering a blockbuster revenue forecast driven by AI infrastructure demand, with the stock on course to surpass its year-2000 record high. The Nasdaq 100 was on track for a fourth consecutive weekly gain. Semiconductor names benefited broadly, with AMD rising 7.2% and Arm up 6.8%. SAP's better-than-expected cloud revenue also lifted sentiment, reinforcing the narrative that AI spending is generating measurable earnings power across the technology supply chain.

The macro backdrop, however, carries meaningful tension. Economists raised their US inflation forecasts and now pencil in just one Federal Reserve rate cut this year, citing elevated energy costs stemming from the Iran conflict. The Fed meets next week, and the Dallas Federal Reserve's survey of oil-and-gas firms — released Thursday as a special supplement — adds nuance to the growth picture. A majority of energy companies do not anticipate meaningful hiring or production increases despite crude prices trading above $90 per barrel for nearly a month. Executives cited extreme policy uncertainty, price volatility linked to administration social media posts, and an inability to plan capital budgets as reasons for restraint. The implication for the broader labor market is meaningful: if the energy sector fails to expand even as oil prices remain elevated, and non-energy sectors face headwinds from those same high prices, the net employment effect could be negative.

Meta and Microsoft announced workforce reductions totaling as many as 23,000 positions, framed by markets as discipline around AI cost management rather than a demand signal. A US Army soldier was criminally charged with using classified intelligence on the timing of Nicolás Maduro's capture to trade on prediction markets, the first application of the so-called Eddie Murphy Rule by the CFTC. Goldman Sachs separately estimated US potential GDP growth at approximately 2.3% annually through 2028, driven by AI-related productivity gains of around 2% per year.
