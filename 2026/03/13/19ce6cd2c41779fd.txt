 Failed hedge 



The basic assumptions that have underpinned hedging strategies for decades are 
beingundone by the escalating war in Iran <https://bloom.bg/40YfERo>.

Government bonds, which traders look to as a cushion for equity losses, are 
now moving in the same direction as stocks. It’s all driven by fears of a 
stagflationary shock that could develop if higher oil prices ignite inflation 
and undercut economic growth.

Such a scenario would make the usual policy response of aggressive interest 
rate cuts unavailable in case of an economic downturn.Without central bank 
action, the traditional 60/40 stock-and-bond portfolio may fail to deliver.

“Since correlations have shifted, the obvious rebalancing between equities and 
bonds, and instruments such as inflation-linked bonds and gold, isn’t 
protecting portfolios,” said Rajeev de Mello at Gama Asset Management.

Where to turn? As we noted Wednesday, Chinese assets, oil and the dollar seem 
to bethe havens of the moment <https://bloom.bg/4rxY01V>. Brokers have plenty 
of other ideas, some of them only within reach of institutional investors.

One easy trade to put on: so-called buffer ETFs <https://bloom.bg/4124uel>, 
which use options to cap stock losses in exchange for limiting gains. The $8.6 
billion FT Vest Laddered Buffer ETF, the largest of the funds, is down 1.5% in 
March, compared to a 3% slide in the S&P 500. —Abhishek Vishnoi 
<https://bloom.bg/4izf0AT>, with Katie Greifeld <https://bloom.bg/3Wgvdlv>

 Jewell of a call 



Betting on clean-energy stocks a month after Donald Trump rolled back into the 
White House last year, championing his “drill, baby, drill” agenda, seemed like 
a fool’s errand.

For Helen Jewell at investment manager BlackRock, it was a  prescient call 
that solidified her reputation as an astute contrarian 
<https://bloom.bg/40pv2pM>. 

Jewell’s insight, in retrospect, was somewhat plain to see: Artificial 
intelligence’s unquenchable thirst for computing power would benefit all forms 
of energy production.

“We were very clear that in order for AI to succeed, you’re going to need so 
much energy that the story is no longer about ‘instead of traditional energy,’ 
it’s about ‘as well as traditional energy,’” Jewell said in an interview.

Fast forward a year: An S&P index of clean energy stocks has surged 61%, 
beating by far a 23% advance in the S&P 500 Index. It’s also nearly double the 
gain in oil stocks even as the commodity itself rises above $100 a barrel amid 
the Iran war.

There’s reason to expect the clean energy rally will continue, Jewell said, as 
the US-led war in the Middle East provides a stark reminder about just how 
dependent the world is on oil and gas from the region. —Sagarika Jaisinghani 
<https://bloom.bg/40hMJZe>
