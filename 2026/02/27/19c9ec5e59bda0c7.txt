 `Go-to haven trade’ 



Here’s evidence that investors still see Treasuries as the premier haven in 
turbulent times: US bonds are wrapping uptheir best monthly performance in a 
year <https://bloom.bg/4rFvVGH>.

During a month when warning signs flashed in other markets — from evidence of 
the disruptive and potentially disinflationary power of AI to rising 
geopolitical tensions and worries about dangers in private credit — traders 
flocked to government debt. The result: Treasuries returned 1.5% in February, 
while long-dated US securities gained 4%.

The rally is a reminder that, at least for now, the $30 trillion US government 
bond market has the edge as a safety play, despite doubts that have sprung up 
about the defensive appeal of US government securities in the turbulence of 
Donald Trump’s second term.

“Absolutely US Treasuries will remain a go-to haven trade,” said James Athey 
at Marlborough Investment Management. “The market is far too big, liquid and 
dominant for it to be completely or easily cast aside as a flight-to-quality 
destination.” —Michael MacKenzie <https://bloom.bg/422rgV4>, Ye Xie 
<https://bloom.bg/3PSSLJP>, and Mia Glass <https://bloom.bg/4hU34tq>

 European stocks on fire 



A few more factoids to chew over as February draws to a close:

 * European stocks are wrapping up their eighth straight month of gains, the  
longest monthly winning streak since 2013 <https://bloom.bg/474Yghh>. The 
region is loaded with old-economy companies that are relatively immune from AI 
disruption, helping the Stoxx Europe 600 Index rip higher by 3.7% in February. 
 * The world-beating rally in South Korean stocks is running out of steam, at 
least for now. Foreign funds  sold a net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks 
<https://bloom.bg/400lbXa> today, a record amount, after the equity benchmark 
rallied 20% for the month and almost 50% this year. 
 * The MSCI Emerging Markets Index is down today too, falling after seven 
straight days of gains. No surprise, given Korea has been a big part of this 
year’s surge — AI toolmakers Samsung and SK Hynix are among the five biggest 
stocks in the benchmark. The MSCI index is still up 5.5% in February and 15% 
year to date. 
 * The S&P 500, in contrast, is heading for its  biggest monthly drop since 
April <https://bloom.bg/4rMncCK>, with investors rattled by the disruptive 
potential of AI. It’s down only 0.4% on the month, but that’s painful for 
investors who are missing out on outperformance elsewhere. 
The gains to be had in other markets highlight “the possibility of a lingering 
overvaluation in some asset classes in the US,” said Guillermo Hernandez 
Sampere at German asset manager MPPM. “Barring an economic downturn in Europe, 
the outperformance should continue.” —Phil Serafino <https://bloom.bg/42HUmtw>
