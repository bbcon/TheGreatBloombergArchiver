The collapse of 21 hours of US-Iran talks in Islamabad over the weekend and President Trump's subsequent announcement of a full naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz represent a meaningful escalation of the conflict that has disrupted global energy markets since the US and Israeli strikes on Iran on February 28. Brent crude rose as much as 9.1% to approach $104 a barrel on Monday's Asian open, with European natural gas futures spiking nearly 18% at one point. Aluminum reached a four-year high on concerns about long-run supply disruption from Persian Gulf producers.

The strategic logic of the blockade, as articulated by advisers close to the situation, is to close Iran's toll-booth system while simultaneously working to reopen the mined half of the Strait through demining, escorts, and insurance backstops — thereby pressuring Tehran to concede politically. This is not an incoherent strategy, but it carries substantial execution risk: US warships would operate in closer proximity to Iranian drone and missile capabilities, and any vessel hit could trigger a rapid escalation cycle. Iran responded Monday by threatening that "no port in the Persian Gulf and Sea of Oman will be safe" if its own facilities are targeted. The UK government confirmed it will not participate in the blockade.

The broader economic consequences are coming into sharper focus. Bloomberg Economics' base case now projects global growth of 2.9% in 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — with a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 a barrel. The IMF will publish its downgraded World Economic Outlook on Tuesday, the fourth consecutive year its Spring Meetings have opened under conditions of acute global stress. Economist Carmen Reinhart argues the world is less prepared to respond to a crisis than at any point in the past two decades, constrained by a $348 trillion global debt burden, central banks still contending with inflation, and a fragmented multilateral order. Coordinated policy response appears unlikely. For now, the singular variable dominating the global outlook remains the duration and severity of the Hormuz disruption.
