European markets processed a complex set of signals on May 6. UK 30-year gilt yields reached 5.78% — the highest level since 1998 — as inflation and fiscal concerns keep the UK in the crosshairs of sovereign debt markets. Thursday's UK local elections carry meaningful political risk for Prime Minister Keir Starmer; Labour is on course to record its lowest share of London voters in over a century, with Reform and the Greens gaining ground. Analysts note that poor results could sharpen intra-party challenges to Starmer's leadership, adding another layer of uncertainty to an already strained fiscal picture.

In Germany, Chancellor Friedrich Merz — who entered office one year ago with sweeping ambitions to revitalize the economy and anchor European leadership — is, by most assessments, struggling on all three fronts. His coalition faces questions about longevity, and he has already ruled out forming a minority government should it collapse. The diplomatic backdrop worsened after Trump announced US troop withdrawals from Germany in response to Merz's public criticism, underscoring how transatlantic tensions constrain European policy space.

On corporate earnings, Novo Nordisk surged approximately 9% after its new Wegovy pill drove quarterly sales growth and the company guided for a smaller-than-expected revenue decline. Infineon's revenue outlook beat expectations on AI-driven power chip demand. BMW's earnings declined, reflecting intense pricing competition in China. In central bank governance, French President Macron nominated Emmanuel Moulin, a close adviser, to head the Banque de France and take a seat on the ECB's Governing Council. Separately, the EU reiterated its demand that Washington adopt the main elements of their trade deal by July and respect prior commitments, including a 15% tariff ceiling, as US Ambassador Puzder warned 25% tariffs on European vehicles could arrive "relatively soon."
