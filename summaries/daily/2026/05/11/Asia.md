China's inflation data for April provided a significant data point: producer prices rose 2.8% year-on-year, the fastest pace since July 2022 and well above consensus, while consumer inflation climbed unexpectedly to 1.2%. Several analysts declared that China has formally exited its deflationary spiral, though the drivers are partly imported — higher energy and raw material costs resulting from the Iran war and the Hormuz closure. The yuan has strengthened to a more than three-year high on the back of these dynamics, with Goldman Sachs estimating the currency remains over 20% undervalued against the dollar and projecting a move to 6.50 within twelve months. Chinese crude imports fell 20% year-on-year in April to their lowest since July 2022, a reflection of the supply disruption rather than weakening demand.

The geopolitical calendar is heavily loaded. A Trump-Xi summit in Beijing is scheduled for Thursday and Friday, the first US presidential visit to China in nearly a decade. Senior US officials indicate Trump intends to press Xi on Beijing's economic relationship with Iran, including revenue flows and potential weapons exports. Pre-summit talks between Treasury Secretary Bessent and Vice Premier He Lifeng are set for Seoul on Wednesday. In Tokyo on Monday, Bessent meets Prime Minister Takaichi and Finance Minister Katayama amid continuing tensions over yen intervention and Japan's monetary policy trajectory; US Treasury yields have on more than one occasion seen spillover from Japanese bond selloffs. Goldman economists separately flagged that AI-driven chip booms in South Korea and Taiwan are on track to push both economies' current-account surpluses to record levels, likely pressuring their central banks toward rate increases later this year. In India, Prime Minister Modi appealed to citizens to conserve fuel and avoid gold purchases as the rupee trades near record lows.
