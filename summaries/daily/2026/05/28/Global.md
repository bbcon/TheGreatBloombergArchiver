**US-Iran Conflict and Hormuz**
The Strait of Hormuz remains the central axis of global macro risk. US forces shot down four Iranian drones targeting a commercial vessel and struck a drone launch unit near the strait — actions described as defensive with the ceasefire nominally intact. Iran targeted the US base from which the strikes originated, and Kuwait separately reported responding to missile and drone threats. Trump's public framing — "they're negotiating on fumes" and "maybe we have to go back and finish it" — signals negotiating posture, but the gap between Iranian conditions and Republican hawk expectations remains wide. The longer this stasis persists, the greater the cumulative economic drag from elevated energy prices, shipping disruption, and uncertainty premia across financial markets.

**Oil and Market Decoupling**
The most striking market dynamic of the period is the near-complete decoupling of equity markets from oil prices. A 5.5% single-day decline in WTI produced virtually no movement in the S&P 500 — a stark contrast to historical patterns. The mechanism is straightforward: AI-driven earnings growth has become the dominant driver of equity valuations, and analysts' profit forecasts have improved materially since the conflict began. Treasuries remain somewhat more sensitive to crude — the 10-year yield continues to track forward oil prices as a proxy for embedded inflation expectations — but even this relationship appears to be attenuating as the market prices in a higher probability of conflict resolution.

**Gold and Precious Metals**
Gold's inability to reclaim its late-January record high despite ongoing extreme geopolitical risk reflects a confluence of structural pressures: rising real yields (gold generates no income), a stronger dollar since the conflict began, central bank liquidity needs prompting marginal selling, and speculative position reduction following the peak. UBS's reduction of its year-end target to $5,500/oz from $5,900 is consistent with this diagnosis, though the bank maintains a recovery scenario to prior highs. Cyclical commodity strategists, including Longview Economics, argue the ongoing industrial upswing favors copper over gold — a view supported by gold's elevated valuation relative to lumber and copper on historical comparisons.
