Daily Brief

Tuesday, February 24, 2026

US

Equity markets steadied early Tuesday, with S&P 500 futures rising 0.2% after a broad selloff the prior session driven by renewed anxiety over AI's disruptive potential. The catalyst was a weekend report from Citrini Research outlining AI-related risks across multiple sectors, which, combined with a warning from Nassim Taleb about escalating volatility and potential software-sector bankruptcies, sent IBM to its steepest single-day decline in 25 years — triggered specifically by Anthropic's announcement that its Claude Code tool can modernize COBOL, the dated programming language central to IBM's mainframe business. Indian IT exporters Infosys, TCS, and Wipro fell 3–4% in Mumbai as the selloff propagated globally. Elsewhere in corporate news, Paramount Skydance raised its offer for Warner Bros. Discovery, and PayPal was reported to be attracting acquisition interest after losing roughly half its market value. Against this backdrop, Jamie Dimon offered a sobering assessment at an investor event, drawing parallels to the pre-2008 period when competitive pressure to generate net interest income led banks toward poor underwriting decisions. "I see a couple people doing some dumb things," he said, a remark notable for its directness even if the systemic parallels remain contested. Bitcoin extended its February decline to approximately 19%, consistent with its behavior as a risk asset rather than the safe-haven narrative some proponents advance. On fiscal and trade policy, President Trump's 10% global tariff entered into force at 12:01 a.m. Washington time Tuesday — a replacement measure following the Supreme Court's invalidation of his original IEEPA-based duties. The administration is preparing a formal directive to raise the rate to 15%, though timing remains unfinalized. Separately, the administration signaled it intends to slow-walk refunds on the overturned tariffs, with Treasury Secretary Bessent framing repayments as "corporate welfare." Legal challenges are accumulating, with more than 1,500 refund lawsuits already filed. Bloomberg Economics estimates roughly $170 billion in contested duties. The macro backdrop ahead of Trump's State of the Union address was notably mixed: 2025 GDP growth came in at 2.2% — the slowest since the post-COVID expansion began — while core inflation ended the year at 3%, a full percentage point above the Fed's target. January payrolls of 130,000 represented a significant rebound from the meager 15,000 monthly average through 2025, and unemployment edged down to 4.3%. Housing, however, remained sluggish, with new home starts posting a fourth consecutive annual decline.

Asia

Two developments dominated Asian market attention Tuesday: renewed AI-driven risk aversion and a sharp move in the Japanese yen. The yen fell approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar following a Mainichi newspaper report that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi expressed apprehension over further Bank of Japan rate increases during a recent meeting with Governor Kazuo Ueda — taking what the newspaper described as a "tougher stance" than at their November meeting. The report appeared to revive concerns that political resistance could constrain the BOJ's policy normalization path. Markets had broadly anticipated that Takaichi, whose election victory earlier this month had been welcomed by investors, would adopt a more accommodative posture toward monetary tightening. The Mainichi report, if accurate, suggests that expectation may have been premature. Japan also raised concerns directly with Washington, with Trade Minister Ryosei Akazawa calling Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick to request that the new 10% global tariff not leave Tokyo in a worse position than the terms agreed under last year's bilateral trade deal. China's escalating pressure on Japan deepened further as Beijing blacklisted 20 Japanese entities — including affiliates of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries — on its export control list, effective immediately. This marks the first addition of Japanese firms to the list since its inception in January 2025. An additional 20 entities, including Subaru and Mitsubishi Materials, were placed on a monitoring list subjecting them to heightened scrutiny on dual-use export applications. The moves appear calibrated to signal continued displeasure following Takaichi's landslide election victory. Separately, Shein announced plans to invest over 10 billion yuan ($1.45 billion) in intelligent supply chain infrastructure in Guangdong province — a notable strategic pivot for a company that had previously sought to distance itself from its Chinese origins, relocating its headquarters to Singapore and initially pursuing a New York listing. The announcement, made by founder Xu Yangtian at a Guangzhou conference, signals a tactical reorientation toward Beijing as Shein's Hong Kong IPO remains stalled and external pressures mount. In North Korea, Kim Jong Un's sister Kim Yo Jong was elevated to department director within the Workers' Party Central Committee as the party's first congress in five years convenes in Pyongyang.

Europe

European markets absorbed a modest decline, with the Stoxx 600 off 0.12% as the AI-driven selloff that battered US software and payments stocks rippled through European equities. Teleperformance, among the earliest European casualties of AI disruption fears, saw short sellers renewing bets against the stock, which has shed roughly €20 billion in market value since January 2022. Goldman Sachs offered a partial counternarrative, promoting a basket of capital-intensive companies — utilities, miners, some industrials, and luxury goods — under the label HALO (Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence), arguing these sectors are structurally insulated from AI displacement. The basket has outperformed capital-light peers by 35% since early 2025. On trade, an EU assessment confirmed that Trump's new 10% global tariff, layered on top of existing levies, will push the cumulative rate on certain EU exports — including cheese and agricultural products — above the 15% ceiling permitted under the EU-US trade agreement. The European Commission, which presented the assessment to lawmakers, faces pressure to respond without triggering further escalation. Separately, the US warned the EU against advancing "Made in Europe" local-content rules, with Brussels caught between the need to rebuild industrial competitiveness following Chinese pressure and Trumpian tariffs on one side, and the risk of retaliatory friction with Washington on the other. In the UK, the political environment around Prime Minister Keir Starmer grew more complicated as two public figures — Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor and former US ambassador Peter Mandelson — were arrested on alleged misconduct charges. With Starmer registering as the most unpopular UK prime minister in modern times by some polling measures and local elections approaching, the episode adds unwanted turbulence. On a brighter note, retail crime — which surged in the post-pandemic period — appears to be easing, with the British Retail Consortium reporting a fifth reduction in daily incidents through August 2025, reflecting over £5 billion in security investment over five years.

Global

The most consequential cross-cutting theme Tuesday was the formal activation of Trump's 10% global tariff, a reconstituted trade barrier following the Supreme Court's invalidation of his original IEEPA duties. While the rate is lower than the tariffs it replaced in aggregate, Bloomberg Economics notes it creates a more complex set of winners and losers — with some adversarial nations faring relatively better and US allies generally worse. The White House has signaled intent to raise the rate to 15% via formal executive order, though the timeline is unset, leaving market participants uncertain about the trajectory of the broader trade regime. On the Iran front, US special envoys Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner were preparing to travel to Geneva for a further round of nuclear talks, even as Trump publicly reserved the right to strike militarily if diplomacy fails. Oil prices steadied near multi-month highs as markets weighed these competing signals — a potential deal implying resumed Iranian supply, military action implying Strait of Hormuz risk. Diamondback Energy separately pushed back against glut concerns, arguing that resilient demand is more durable than consensus forecasts assume. The Ukraine conflict marked its fourth anniversary with no resolution in sight. Peace talks remain deadlocked, the front line is broadly static, and senior European and NATO officials say there is no indication Putin is prepared to accept terms short of his maximalist demands. The US has been working toward a deal ahead of July 4th Independence Day celebrations, but the self-imposed deadline appears increasingly aspirational. Germany's defense chief publicly criticized Trump's negotiating approach. On energy, a decade of US LNG exports has materially reshaped global gas markets, with the US now the world's largest LNG exporter. Approximately 25% of global LNG supply now originates in the United States, a share projected to reach one-third by 2030. The flexibility of US cargoes — sold free-on-board and therefore tradeable across global destinations — has progressively eroded Russia's historical ability to leverage gas supply as a geopolitical instrument against Europe. Saudi Arabia's fiscal deficit widened to a five-year high as lower oil prices pressured revenues, forcing a deceleration of some Vision 2030 megaprojects.
