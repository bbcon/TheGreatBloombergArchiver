Weekly Brief

Apr 20 – Apr 26, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 closed above 7,000 for the first time last week, with the index up approximately 9% in April, but the rally showed signs of fragility: fewer than half of members traded above their 50-day moving averages at the record, and volume ran 11% below the six-month average.
  • Kevin Warsh's Senate Banking Committee confirmation hearing on Tuesday and Wednesday dominated monetary policy attention, with the nominee pledging Fed independence while signaling comfort with the administration's "economic statecraft" framework and interest in overhauling inflation measurement toward trimmed-mean gauges.
  • Tesla beat first-quarter earnings estimates on Wednesday but gave back after-hours gains after announcing a $25 billion capex plan; Intel surged approximately 20% on Friday on a blockbuster AI-driven revenue forecast.
  • Meta and Microsoft announced workforce reductions totaling as many as 23,000 positions, framed by markets as AI cost discipline; Alaska Air suspended profit guidance, citing Hormuz-driven fuel costs.
  • Consumer sentiment fell to an all-time low, with household concerns about war-driven inflation and gasoline prices approaching four-year highs diverging sharply from equity market performance.

Equity Markets

US equity markets delivered a week of broadly constructive performance against a backdrop of persistent geopolitical uncertainty, with the S&P 500 sustaining its April advance and the Nasdaq 100 logging a 13-session winning streak — its longest since 2013 — before pausing Monday. JPMorgan raised its year-end S&P 500 target to 7,600 following the index's breach of 7,000. The rally's technical characteristics, however, warrant scrutiny: the combination of below-average volume, narrow market breadth, and declining investor sentiment during an advance to all-time highs suggests the move is being driven by a relatively small cohort of large-cap names rather than broad-based conviction. Analysts at Barclays drew a parallel to the low-volatility conditions preceding the February 2018 "Volmageddon" correction, a precedent that merits monitoring even if it does not constitute a near-term forecast.

Corporate Earnings

First-quarter earnings provided genuine support to the market's constructive positioning, with nearly 80% of S&P 500 companies reporting beats — a rate above the historical average. Technology remained the dominant earnings driver. Intel's Friday surge of approximately 20% in premarket trading reflected a blockbuster AI infrastructure forecast and placed the stock on course to eclipse its year-2000 record. Texas Instruments surged 12% Thursday after a stronger-than-expected outlook driven by data-center and industrial demand. The contrast with ServiceNow, which fell 13% after cutting its margin outlook and citing deal delays attributable to the Middle East conflict, illustrates the differentiation within technology: companies with direct infrastructure exposure are outperforming those dependent on discretionary enterprise software spend. Tesla's Wednesday report — $0.41 per share against a $0.34 consensus — generated a muted response once the $25 billion capex commitment became clear, as investors signaled a preference for automotive clarity over unquantified robotics ambitions.

Monetary Policy and the Warsh Hearing

The Warsh confirmation hearing was the week's most consequential domestic policy event, though it generated more questions than answers. The nominee's preference for trimmed-mean inflation gauges — where the Dallas Fed's measure currently reads 2.33% against core PCE's 3.0% — carries material implications for perceived distance from the 2% target and, if adopted as the policy anchor, would shift the rate path considerably. Warsh's alignment with the Bessent-Rubio "economic statecraft" framework on dollar stability also drew attention, though he was careful not to commit to specifics. Senator Thom Tillis's hold on the nomination, tied to the Justice Department's probe of Jerome Powell over Fed renovation expenditures, creates a genuine possibility that Powell remains in post beyond his May 15 term expiry — an awkward institutional interregnum that bond markets have not yet fully priced.

Fiscal and Energy Policy

Trump invoked the Defense Production Act to direct federal funds toward energy infrastructure, including coal, LNG, petroleum, and grid projects, as the administration faces political pressure over elevated fuel prices. The internal divergence between Energy Secretary Wright — who said gasoline prices are unlikely to fall below $3 per gallon until next year — and Treasury Secretary Bessent on the energy price outlook is a notable crack in the administration's narrative. Goldman Sachs estimated US potential GDP growth at approximately 2.3% annually through 2028, driven by roughly 2% annual AI productivity gains, a constructive structural backdrop that sits in tension with the cyclical headwinds generated by sustained energy price elevation and consumer confidence at all-time lows. ---

Asia

  • South Korean chip exports surged 183% year-on-year in the first 20 days of April, and the Kospi reached a record, up 52% year-to-date, led by Samsung and SK Hynix as AI-driven memory demand sustains a potential supercycle.
  • Japan's cabinet approved the country's first framework for overseas lethal weapons exports since World War II, a significant strategic shift under Prime Minister Takaichi reflecting heightened threat perceptions from China and North Korea.
  • DeepSeek unveiled V4 Flash and V4 Pro preview models on Friday, citing advances in coding, reasoning, and a 1 million-token context window, driving a rally in Chinese chipmaker stocks.
  • China's People's Bank injected additional liquidity as an unexpected labor market deterioration — partly attributed to AI adoption — weighed on early-career employment, while the "national team" appeared to reduce its dominant ETF stabilization positions.
  • TSMC shares rose as much as 5.1% to a record on Friday after Taiwan's Financial Supervisory Commission moved to relax single-stock fund concentration limits, with JPMorgan estimating potential inflows exceeding $6 billion.

China: Growth Narrative and Policy Response

China's economic narrative carried genuine complexity this week. Recent GDP data surprised to the upside, property indicators showed tentative improvement, and equity markets have recovered to near decade highs — developments that have revived debate about whether Beijing has navigated beyond its "Japanification" moment. Two influential academic contributions framed the dispute: Rogoff and Yang at the NBER acknowledged that state-directed policy has prevented financial breakdown but warned that without structural rebalancing, managed adjustment could eventually evolve into stagnation. Eurizon SLJ's Jen and Freire argued more constructively, citing fierce domestic competition and US-China technological rivalry as productivity-sustaining forces that distinguish China's trajectory from Japan's lost decades. The debate remains genuinely unresolved, though the PBOC's mid-week liquidity injection — responding to an unexpected deterioration in youth unemployment as AI adoption accelerates — illustrated that the adjustment is not without cost. Chinese regulators also moved to restrict technology companies from accepting US capital without government approval, a measure framed as a response to Meta's acquisition of startup Manus, reinforcing Beijing's tightening control over strategic technology flows in both directions.

Semiconductors and Technology Exports

The AI-driven semiconductor boom continued to define the week's regional equity narrative. South Korean chip exports surged 183% year-on-year in April's first 20 days; Taiwan's export orders rose 66% in March, the fastest pace in 16 years; and SK Hynix reported a five-fold jump in quarterly profit while reaffirming plans to raise capital expenditure. Samsung shares have tripled and SK Hynix has quadrupled since late August. DeepSeek's Friday unveiling of V4 Flash and V4 Pro — with advances in agentic capabilities and a 1 million-token context window — added a Chinese dimension to the AI infrastructure theme, lifting domestic chipmaker stocks on expectations of sustained domestic semiconductor demand. Victory Giant, a Chinese Nvidia supplier, surged as much as 60% in its Hong Kong debut after raising $2.6 billion, while TSMC's record close on Friday reflected both structural demand and a technical catalyst from Taiwan's fund concentration rule relaxation.

Japan: Strategic Realignment

Japan's cabinet approval of a legal framework for lethal weapons exports — covering missiles, fighter jets, and warships — represents one of the more consequential strategic shifts in the region this week. Prime Minister Takaichi has absorbed the domestic political cost, with polls showing 67% opposition, in pursuit of a defense self-sufficiency agenda driven by threat perceptions from Beijing and Pyongyang. Japan's first major naval exercises with the US and Philippines — conducted simultaneously with China's western Pacific drills — underscored the widening military competition west of the First Island Chain. The Bank of Japan is meanwhile leaning toward holding rates at its next meeting, citing conflict-related uncertainty, even as March export data showed faster-than-expected growth on rebounding Chinese demand.

Regional Geopolitics and Markets

North Korea's five tactical ballistic missile tests early in the week were a reminder of Pyongyang's ongoing capability development, compounded by concerns about combat experience accumulating through deployments in Ukraine. Indonesia's equities declined after MSCI extended its market status review to June. Hyundai missed earnings estimates, citing US tariff disruption and Iran war-related supply chain strain. Danantara, Indonesia's sovereign wealth fund, confirmed early-stage talks to acquire Eramet's 38.7% stake in PT Weda Bay Nickel — the world's largest nickel mine — as President Prabowo moves to consolidate natural resource assets. China's IMF spring meeting defense of its trade surplus, which drew skepticism from Japan's finance minister, signals a more assertive Beijing posture on external criticism of its economic model. ---

Europe

  • The eurozone economy unexpectedly contracted in April for the first time since late 2024, with PMI data showing a steep services sector decline as war-driven energy costs weigh on confidence and activity.
  • Germany's ZEW investor expectations index collapsed to -17.2 in April from -0.5 in March — its lowest in more than three years — against a consensus estimate of -5.8, reflecting rapid deterioration in business confidence.
  • UK Prime Minister Starmer faces mounting political pressure as former senior official Olly Robbins testified that Downing Street was "dismissive" of vetting concerns surrounding Peter Mandelson's appointment as US ambassador, with local elections next month expected to be damaging for Labour.
  • Europe's airline sector faces an acute jet fuel shortage, with Lufthansa cancelling 20,000 short-haul flights, KLM eliminating 80 return services, and EasyJet warning of a potential first-half loss.
  • The EU finalized disbursement of its €90 billion loan to Ukraine after Hungary lifted its veto, and European leaders signaled preliminary conditions for Ukraine's formal membership process, though a committed timeline is absent.

Eurozone Macro and ECB

The week's most significant European data point was the April flash PMI reading showing the first eurozone contraction since late 2024, driven by a steep decline in services activity. The figure crystallizes the ECB's dilemma: war-driven energy inflation is pushing headline consumer prices higher — UK CPI accelerated to 3.3% in March on a 9% monthly surge in motor fuel costs, the largest since Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine — while the growth picture is deteriorating simultaneously. ECB President Lagarde described this as a "double uncertainty," effectively arguing for a data-dependent approach that delays further action. Analysts at TS Lombard have raised the risk of a "Trichet 2.0" scenario, in which the ECB tightens into a weakening economy, though the base case remains a hold through June. Germany's Bundesbank estimated slight first-quarter expansion, but the ZEW collapse to -17.2 in April signals that forward-looking confidence has deteriorated sharply and rapidly, leaving the eurozone's industrial core increasingly exposed to a sustained energy shock.

UK Politics and Fiscal Outlook

Keir Starmer's political position eroded incrementally but persistently across the week. The central controversy — whether Downing Street was aware of Peter Mandelson's vetting failure and pressured the Foreign Office to proceed with his appointment as US ambassador anyway — gained evidentiary weight when former senior civil servant Olly Robbins testified on Tuesday that Downing Street was "dismissive" of concerns raised during the process. The episode has triggered what amounts to an open revolt among senior civil servants, a distracting and damaging conflict for a government that entered office promising institutional restoration. London Mayor Sadiq Khan offered qualified support, framing political stability as a UK competitive asset, but the calls for Starmer to resign ahead of next month's local elections signal the controversy is far from resolved. UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves received a near-term fiscal reprieve as government borrowing fell to its lowest in three years, though analysts caution that the Iran war's inflationary pass-through may erode that buffer materially.

German Politics and Corporate News

Chancellor Friedrich Merz faces a deeply negative approval environment, with 80% of respondents expressing disapproval in a recent poll. His measures to cushion energy price impacts — including a €1,000 worker payment intended to be voluntarily funded by employers, in which the federal government itself declined to participate — were widely criticized as inadequate. The AfD continues to gain political oxygen from coalition infighting ahead of September's eastern German state elections. On the corporate front, UniCredit CEO Andrea Orcel pressed his case for acquiring Commerzbank, arguing deep structural change is required at the German lender, even as their most recent talks failed to produce agreement. SAP reported strong cloud revenue growth, gaining 7.3% in Frankfurt, while Siemens Energy rose 4.9% after raising its annual outlook on grid and gas turbine demand.

Aviation and Energy Stress

Europe's aviation sector faces what may be the most acute near-term commercial challenge on the continent. Persian Gulf refineries supplying roughly 20% of EU jet fuel are effectively cut off, Asian refineries face their own crude shortfalls, and India's Reliance has prioritized domestic LPG output over jet fuel exports. Shell's Pernis refinery is operating at maximum throughput and Nigeria's Dangote refinery has raised European shipments to record levels, but these measures appear insufficient to meet summer demand. Europe's ban on Russian LNG imports took effect Saturday — poorly timed given current energy market stress — and the EU is preparing jet fuel distribution optimization measures among member states. The structural implication is that Europe is entering the summer travel season with a genuine supply constraint that pricing alone cannot resolve quickly. ---

Global

  • The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with both the US and Iranian blockades nominally in place; Goldman Sachs estimated Persian Gulf oil output running approximately 14.5 million barrels per day below pre-war levels in April, roughly 57% below pre-conflict volumes.
  • Brent crude rose above $104 per barrel on Thursday, its fourth consecutive daily gain, while WTI traded near $94; oil markets slipped briefly mid-week on ceasefire extension news before resuming their advance.
  • Clean power surpassed coal's share of global electricity generation in 2025 for the first time since 1919, with renewables at 34% against coal's 33%, according to Ember, sharpening policy focus on energy independence.
  • Commodity trading houses — Vitol, Trafigura, and Gunvor — raised $7.5 billion in contingency liquidity at the war's outset, with preliminary first-quarter profit estimates comparable in magnitude to the $30 billion-plus earned collectively in 2022.
  • Approximately $300 billion in goods subject to US tariffs are estimated to be circumventing levies annually via transshipment, according to Altana data showing a 76% year-on-year surge in suspect transactions in 2025.

Strait of Hormuz: Diplomacy and Market Implications

The Hormuz impasse remained the week's defining global risk, and its evolution was characteristically volatile. Iranian Foreign Minister declared the strait "completely open" on Friday of the prior week, only for Tehran to reverse course within 24 hours after the US refused to lift its naval blockade. The US then boarded an Iranian-flagged cargo ship in the Gulf of Oman — the first such seizure since the blockade began — and separately intercepted two Iranian oil supertankers attempting to evade its blockade later in the week. President Trump's announcement of an indefinite ceasefire extension on Wednesday — delivered within 30 minutes of news that Vice President Vance's Islamabad trip had been cancelled — encapsulated the dynamic markets have priced: escalation optionality consistently resolved toward the off-ramp. The subsequent Friday sighting of the US-sanctioned supertanker Yuri carrying 2 million barrels of Iranian crude toward the strait suggested that enforcement resolve will continue to be tested. Iran's UN envoy indicated Wednesday that Tehran had received "some sign" Washington was ready to lift the blockade, suggesting backchannel communication persists even as formal talks remain stalled. Polymarket assigned only approximately a one-in-five probability to tanker traffic normalizing by end of April.

Oil, Commodities, and Supply Chains

The commodity dimensions of the conflict extend well beyond crude oil. The Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index has risen meaningfully since hostilities began, with wheat and rice moving higher; Bank of America flags corn as carrying the largest upside risk given mounting nitrogen fertilizer market pressure from Gulf supply disruption. Industrial metals have also rallied sharply, with copper and aluminum each up more than 11% since mid-March on a combination of supply chain disruption and structural demand from AI data center buildout and grid electrification. The copper-to-gold ratio recently touched four-decade lows, a level historically associated with commodity supercycle onset. Approximately 800 vessels remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, tankers face 3.5-day delays at the Panama Canal as diverted traffic surges, and DHL's CEO warned the global economy risks a "tipping point" if the supply shortfall persists. US shale producers, despite oil above $90 per barrel for nearly a month, have largely declined to increase drilling — citing policy uncertainty and price volatility linked to administration social media posts — with Continental Resources the only major producer to confirm expanded drilling plans.

Energy Transition Acceleration

The crisis appears to be functioning as an accelerant for structural energy transition. At the Petersberg Climate Dialogue in Berlin — the opening event on the road to COP31 in Turkey — delegates from nearly 40 countries framed the Hormuz disruption as a catalyst for reducing fossil fuel dependence. Battery installations are expected to jump roughly a third this year; China's solar cell exports rose 80% year-on-year in March, and EV exports surged 53%. China holds approximately 72% of cumulative long-duration energy storage installed capacity, though its dominance in that sub-segment appears less entrenched than in conventional lithium-ion, offering competitive openings for US and European developers. Brazil's program to electrify isolated Amazon communities via solar and battery storage illustrates how elevated diesel prices — a direct consequence of the Hormuz conflict — have improved the relative economics of clean alternatives, with the government projecting $171 million in subsidy savings and 800,000 metric tons of avoided greenhouse gas emissions by 2036.

Geopolitics: Defense, Trade, and Dollar Dynamics

Japan lifted most restrictions on lethal weapons exports for the first time since World War II, a development that drew formal warnings from Beijing about revived militarism. Chinese defense contractors made their largest-ever showing at the Defence Services Asia exhibition in Kuala Lumpur, with 192 firms and drone technologies prominently featured — a direct competitive response to the Western-aligned defense industry. The US unveiled measures targeting AI model distillation by Chinese developers, and Beijing moved to exclude Taiwan's president from African airspace by pressuring island nations to revoke overflight permits. On trade, the approximately $300 billion in tariff circumvention via Southeast Asian and Mexican transshipment is sharpening USMCA renegotiation tensions, with USTR Greer signaling potential enforcement action against Canada. Persian Gulf and Asian sovereigns seeking dollar swap lines with the US Treasury reflect the dollar's reinforced role in global trade under stress — a dynamic the IMF flagged as partly reflecting a relative deterioration in advanced-economy risk perception, with US Treasuries losing some of their traditional safe-haven premium amid fiscal trajectory concerns and questions about Federal Reserve institutional independence.
