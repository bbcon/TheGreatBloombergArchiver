• The ECB's policy stance shifted materially, with Bundesbank President Nagel indicating rate hikes are "increasingly likely" and Governing Council member Kazaks warning of stagflation risks.

• German industrial production fell unexpectedly for a second consecutive month in March, and France's unemployment rose to its highest level in five years.

**UK Political Crisis**
The week opened with Prime Minister Starmer delivering a reset speech following Labour's sweeping local election defeat, only to face an accelerating internal revolt. By Tuesday, he was publicly defying cabinet-level calls to set a departure timetable, telling ministers that "the country expects us to get on with governing" — a response that failed to stabilize investor sentiment. Home Secretary Mahmood and Health Secretary Streeting were among those privately or publicly pressing for transition; four junior ministers resigned in an attempt to force the issue. By Friday, Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham had secured a credible path to mount a leadership challenge, with the MP for Makerfield standing aside in his favour, making Burnham the frontrunner in what markets now price as a greater-than-50% probability of a change in leadership before year-end. The pound fell on the news, gilt yields surged, and Schroders disclosed it is actively avoiding UK bonds, citing the risk that a successor could pursue a more expansionary fiscal stance. Jamie Dimon added an external dimension, warning that any post-Starmer government raising taxes on banks would cause JPMorgan to abandon plans for a multi-billion-pound Canary Wharf headquarters. The King's Speech, meanwhile, represents a near-term test of whether Starmer can project governing authority through the noise.

**UK Economy and Gilt Markets**
Beneath the political turbulence, the UK's economic data produced a genuine positive surprise: Q1 GDP rose 0.6%, the strongest quarterly growth in a year, with the IT sector contributing meaningfully. The reading suggests the economy absorbed the early weeks of the Iran conflict without contracting. However, the sustainability of that pace is widely questioned — Bloomberg Economics projects near-stagnant growth over the remainder of 2026 as higher energy costs, tighter borrowing conditions, and political uncertainty compound. The gilt market has increasingly reflected this tension: 30-year yields reached levels last seen in 1998, and the spread between gilts and Treasuries has tracked the oil price since the outbreak of hostilities, suggesting Iran may be a more proximate driver of UK funding costs than domestic politics alone, even if the two reinforce one another. The UK simultaneously halved its pledge to the Green Climate Fund, reducing its 2024–2027 contribution from £1.6 billion to £815 million, a further withdrawal from multilateral climate commitments. British employers continued shifting toward temporary hiring in April, with permanent-role hiring falling at its fastest pace in three months.

**Eurozone: ECB Pivot and German Fragility**
The ECB's policy outlook underwent a meaningful shift this week. Bundesbank President Nagel told Handelsblatt that rate hikes are increasingly likely — a notable pivot given that the dominant expectation at the start of the year was for continued easing. Governing Council member Kazaks went further, warning that data are pointing toward stagflation risks and that a hike would be warranted if oil price increases feed through to inflation expectations. A Bloomberg survey now shows the ECB expected to hike twice in 2026, though Kazaks also described a June move as "less obvious" than imminent — reflecting the genuine tension between above-target inflation and softening growth that Oxford Economics estimates could add 0.6 to 0.7 percentage points of additional inflation pass-through given elevated public inflation awareness. Germany's underlying position remains fragile: industrial production fell unexpectedly for a second consecutive month in March, Chancellor Merz's approval ratings have fallen to historic lows, the AfD has extended its polling lead, and a six-hour coalition working session produced few concrete agreements. France compounded the eurozone picture, with unemployment rising unexpectedly to its highest level in five years.

**European Political and Structural Themes**
European political fragility extended well beyond the UK. Romania's government fell, Latvia's prime minister resigned over a drone-related controversy, and trade union protests greeted the German chancellor. Italy under Meloni stands as a notable exception to this pattern of leadership attrition. At the EU level, the Commission reached unanimous political agreement to sanction violent West Bank settlers — its first such consensus since the Gaza crisis — while reporting suggests von der Leyen's centralized management style is generating internal friction and diluting focus on the single market agenda. An analysis of London commercial property found that up to 78% of Westminster offices and 71% of City offices will fail to meet Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards expected in the early 2030s, raising stranded-asset risk across the sector. In eastern European bond markets, Hungary's borrowing costs fell below Poland's for the first time since 2020, reflecting investor optimism around new Prime Minister Magyar's EU alignment agenda.
