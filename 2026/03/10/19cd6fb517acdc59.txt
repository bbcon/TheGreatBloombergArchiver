After a wild Monday <https://bloom.bg/4sywRwt>, oil prices took a breather 
today with US President Donald Trump sending some signals that appeared tocalm 
markets <https://bloom.bg/4sVPOtt>. 

Trump said he would waive oil-related sanctions, have the US Navy escort 
tankers through the Strait of Hormuz and predicted on Monday that thewar with 
Iran <https://bloom.bg/4bchrqD> would resolve “very soon” as he confronted 
mounting economic and political pressure and days of dramatic fluctuations in 
oil markets.

Let’s see how long this sense of relief lasts. After all, Trump didn’t offer 
additional specifics beyond acknowledging he had discussed the topic with 
Russian President Vladimir Putin in a phone call earlier Monday. (Russia has 
faced a range of restrictions on its oil industry in a bid to deprive the 
country of revenue over its war in Ukraine.) But Trump is mulling arange of 
options <https://bloom.bg/3NpJCKW> to combat surging oil and gasoline prices, 
according to people familiar with the matter.

The bigger question is how this all ends. The president said he didn’t believe 
the conflict would be over this week, but insisted the operation wasahead of 
schedule <https://bloom.bg/4lqA0fI>. He also vowed bombing “at a much, much 
harder level” if Iran disrupted oil supplies. While US officials have 
repeatedly insisted they can continue the conflict indefinitely,Trump’s remarks 
<https://bloom.bg/4sExIvR> underscored a new willingness by the White House to 
publicly indicate that it could be moving soon to attempt to wrap up 
hostilities. Whether that happens is another matter.—Stephanie Phang 
<https://bloom.bg/4gs6Wkp> 



 What You Need to Know Today 



Oil prices may have fallen from stratospheric highs, but airlines in Asia are 
still raising ticket pricesand mapping out contingency plans that include 
grounding planes <https://bloom.bg/4sBmKai>. Indian carriers have hiked prices 
on long-haul routes by 15% and are considering further increases, people 
familiar with the matter said. Meanwhile, Hong Kong Airlines said it will raise 
fuel charges from Thursday across a range of routes. And Qantas said it’s 
boosting fares on international routes as fuel prices jump and demand surges 
for long-haul flights to Europe.




Governments are taking steps to prevent an energy crunch. The Thai government 
will require most government agencies to adopt full work-from-home arrangements 
starting immediately as part of itsemergency measures <https://bloom.bg/4sF6Mw0>
 to curb energy demand. The cabinet also urged government officials to suspend 
non-essential overseas travel for the time being. India has invoked emergency 
powers toredirect supplies <https://bloom.bg/4cyDtX3> of liquefied petroleum 
gas away from industrial users to households, as it seeks to shield ordinary 
people from the impact of the Middle East war.




At least a dozen clusters of ships have appeared near the Strait of Hormuz, a 
likely sign ofelevated electronic interference <https://bloom.bg/4bhSw50> 
around the waterway that’s central to the US and Israeli war against Iran. The 
groups, which can number more than 200 vessels for some clusters, point to 
ships’ navigational systems being interfered with by electronic jamming, making 
them appear on tracking platforms away from their actual locations. Such 
practices often spike at times of heightened geopolitical tensions, as 
militaries engage in electronic warfare. (Follow our ongoing Iran war coverage
here <https://bloom.bg/4riZ8X0>.)

Before all this chaos broke out, China’s export growth accelerated far faster 
than expected <https://bloom.bg/4rg9Ihf> in the first two months of the year, 
putting shipments on a record path before US and Israeli strikes on Iran
disrupted <https://bloom.bg/3NtXl3l> global trade. Exports soared almost 22% 
from a year earlier, compared with a 7.2% median estimate in a Bloomberg survey 
of economists. The escalating crisis in the Middle East now poses new risks for 
the world’s largest exporter as the economic fallout from the warspreads 
<https://bloom.bg/47v7Aer> outside the region.




Another risk: Apple increased iPhone production in India by about 53% last 
year and nowmakes a quarter <https://bloom.bg/3ORRvt9> of its marquee devices 
there, reflecting the US company’s efforts to avoid tariffs on China. The 
company assembled about 55 million iPhones in India in 2025, up from 36 million 
a year earlier, people familiar with the matter said. Apple makes about 220 
million to 230 million iPhones a year globally, with India’s share of the total 
increasing rapidly.




Behind China’s export juggernaut, the workers who powered a tariff-defying 
boom tell a grim story. President Xi Jinping’s increasingly powerful export 
machine stunned the world last year by generating a record $1.2 trillion trade 
surplus even after Trump raised American tariffs to the highest in nearly a 
century. Yet in China’s sprawling Guangdong province, long the main engine 
driving the nation’s manufacturing prowess, ordinary workers have seen a sharp 
decline in their living standards. We interviewed workers and business owners 
to reveal ableak picture of falling salaries <https://bloom.bg/4sB3Dgn> and 
disappearing jobs.
Day laborers looking for jobs in garment workshops and recruiters carrying 
samples of work mingle on the major thoroughfare in Datang, Guangzhou, in 
February.Photographer: Qilai Shen/ 



On the other end of the job market, senior bankers in Asia are leaving for 
competitors at an intensifying pace as the job market heats up again on rising 
deal flow, people familiar with the matter said. While post‑bonus job moves are 
common in banking, a resurgence in Asia-Pacific deals is sparking a fresh wave 
of poaching among the world’s largest investment banks. Read morehere

 For Your Commute 



When he started trading in 2020, Lito Chen followed mostly well-trodden 
advice, tucking his modest university student savings into the kinds of 
blue-chip stocks like liquor companies that analysts had touted as safe bets. 
They proved to be anything but, erasing his nest egg and eroding his confidence 
in mainstream financial analysis. So when doing research for his next round of 
investment picks in the first half of 2024, Chen turned to what many young 
people know best: AI chatbots and the social media hive mind. Investing in a 
mix of tech, defense, mining and other stocks recommended by internet friends 
and chatbots like Kimi and Zhipu, he’s clawed back his earlier losses—and then 
some.

“I’ll rely more on AI for stock selection in the future,” says Chen, a 
24-year-old student in Shanghai. “Some of the stocks it picked for me really 
just kept rallying nonstop.” Young, technologically savvy, even defiant, Chen 
represents China’s newest generation of retail investors. These bold Generation 
Z traders <https://bloom.bg/3NvM4zz> have earned a nickname in China: Xiao 
Dengs, or “little guys”—and their swelling ranks are propelling the country’s 
more than $14 trillion stock market.
