Hostage to Fortune, Vol. 2

Speculating about the likely duration of the war in the Middle East is 
difficult and dangerous enough at any time. As this edition needs to go to 
press earlier than usual thanks to a day of transit ahead, it’s particularly 
risky. But, assuming that Mojtaba Khamenei isn’t flying a largewhite flag 
<https://bloom.bg/4lnyfjj> over Tehran by the time you read this (which is a 
reasonable supposition), what conclusions can we reach about the length of the 
war?

The priority for the decision-makers in Iran is their own survival as a 
regime. There are limits to the punishment they can take, but they also have to 
avoid a humiliating defeat if they want to keep any perception of legitimacy in 
the Iranian population.

As for the US, it’s fair to say that President Donald Trump’s latest 
pronouncement that the war would be “short” suggests he feels apolitical 
imperative <https://bloom.bg/3PdwZTI> to declare victory and pull out. The 
problem is that his message is contradicted by the administration’s actions, as 
the Iran conflict appears to be intensifying, with ever more weaponry.

The other issue is that a TACO — “Trump Always Chickens Out” — scenario 
depends in part on the counterparties going along with it. As Marko Papic of 
BCA Research puts it, Iran is unlikely to “eat the taco” as readily as prior 
opponents and give him an apparent victory, whilethe ease 
<https://bloom.bg/4ulGCzJ> with which the Strait of Hormuz has been closed 
should be troubling:

Remnants of Iran’s regime — or factions that Tehran cannot control even in the 
case of a ceasefire — could continue to imperil Hormuz with cheap drones for 
weeks, months… even decades.

The focus is understandably on Trump, which helps explain why US stocks at the 
time of writing are higher than they were when hostilities commenced. But it 
may not be in the control of either the US or Iran to reopen the strait, and 
that risk deserves far more attention. At $88, Brent crude still prices 
considerable risks that stock markets are ignoring.

Postcard From Mumbai

Not long ago, India’s central bank governor, Sanjay Malhotra, said that the 
country was in a“rare Goldilocks period” <https://bloom.bg/4lmxFSE> of strong 
growth and low inflation. That was always tempting fate. Into the fairytale the 
bears have now entered — the attack on Iran and the retaliatory closure of the 
Strait of Hormuz, an artery that carries many exports upon which India relies.

In any case, several factors were moving against the world’s most populous 
country before they were accelerated by the conflict in the Gulf. That doesn’t 
mean they’re insuperable, but a week in India’s glorious financial capital has 
brought home that a successful period for its markets was already coming under 
pressure.

Indian stocks have been just about the most consistent performers in the 
emerging world for the last three decades, but they badly missed out on the big 
EM rally of the past year. SinceNarendra Modi <https://bloom.bg/4svDDDk> was 
elected to a third term as prime minister in 2024 but forced to govern as part 
of a coalition, the main Indian benchmarks are down in dollar terms. EM as a 
whole has gained 40% in that time, even after last week’s hit from the Gulf:

One key driver has been relentless pressure on the rupee, which last week 
forced the Reserve Bank of India to intervene. In real effective terms, taking 
inflation into account, the currency is at its weakest in more than a decade, 
and has suffered its sharpest selloff since the 2013 “Taper Tantrum.” The 
indecisive 2024 election seems to have been something of a catalyst:

In 2013, global markets were roiled by the then Federal Reserve Chairman Ben 
Bernanke’s comments that he would taper off quantitative-easing bond purchases, 
which had kept Treasury yields low. That led to extreme pressure on India and 
four other large emerging markets that were dubbed the “Fragile Five” because 
of their weak fiscal position.

India cannot possibly be accused of a fragile fiscal state now. Indeed, the 
problem may be the opposite: that it has been too frugal. In real terms, fiscal 
spending is actually falling, as this chart from BCA Research demonstrates. No 
other country has moved so swiftly to remove the massive stimulus adopted 
during the pandemic:

The problem, illustrated by another BCA Research chart, is India’s capital 
account. For decades, it was in surplus, with more capital flowing into the 
country than leaving. That has flipped, and India is settling into its second 
consecutive year of net capital outflows:

Capital flows come in many forms, but all channels are currently challenged. 
The biggest issue is thatIndia <https://bloom.bg/4s0qYbx> has long been, and 
remains, the most expensive emerging stock market. Measured by multiples of 
book value, Indian stocks until recently traded at a premium of the MSCI World 
index of developed world equities, and they’re far more expensive than anywhere 
else in the emerging world. If there’s ever any need to reduce EM exposure, 
India is the natural place to take profits:

India traditionally trades at a surplus to EM, but that has been accentuated 
by recent reforms that encourage saving and tax equity holdings more leniently 
than debt — which has meant retail money bidding up Indian stocks. The country 
also benefited from flows out of China during the era when President Xi 
Jinping’s attack on the private sector was deemed to have made it“uninvestable.”
 <https://bloom.bg/4bAkhXP>

High multiples also made it more appealing for foreign investors to cash out 
and repatriate their money. Foreign private equity entered India in a big way — 
Blackstone is the biggest owner of commercial property — and is taking profits 
where it can. That further starves the nation of capital at a point when others 
are opting for fiscal expansion and profiting from AI-backed capital 
expenditures.

All of this came before war in India’s backyard. The country’s long-term 
trajectory is hard to doubt. But even such a big emerging market has a problem 
when capital flows turn against it.

Nonetheless, Goldilocks is still intact. India’s real gross domestic product 
grew 7.8% last year, while inflation is comfortably within its target range:

But market decisions can sometimes be self-fulfilling. Indian assets are in 
for a difficult few years as they adapt to a world in which capital doesn’t 
automatically flow in.


Meanwhile, in Beijing...

As the US grows increasingly entangled in conflict with Iran, Chinese 
policymakers at theNational People’s Congress <https://bloom.bg/4bcVcSZ> are 
confronting a different problem — how to sustain the post–Covid-Zero recovery 
without bazooka-style domestic consumption stimulus. That’s not to suggest that 
China is immune to the conflict. Far from it.

The economy expanded “around 5%” last year, barely in line with the government 
target, but the backdrop was far from reassuring. A prolonged property slump, 
weak domestic demand, and aggressive US tariffs threaten China’s economic 
bulwark, its manufacturing base. This list isn’t exhaustive and the 15th 
Five-Year Plan (2026-2030) acknowledges that.

If anything, the deliberations at the eight-day policy meeting suggest that 
anyone still hoping for an outsize domestic consumption boost must wait longer. 
The 2026 draft budget prioritizes advanced manufacturing, semiconductor 
development, artificial intelligence and the so-called “new-productive” forces. 
This expenditure frontloading, which keeps the budget deficit at 4% of GDP, 
delays any injection for household demand.

Oxford Economics’ Sheana Yue says the budget signals continuity. Whether 
neglecting domestic demand will hamstring growth at some point is a question 
bureaucrats would constantly contend with. According to Gavekal Research’s 
Andrew Batson, the plan may be a more attractive political bargain, but “points 
to continuity rather than rupture”:

The 20 “main indicators” listed at the start of the plan, outlining its most 
important targets, are essentially the same as in the 14th Five-Year Plan for 
2021-25. The desired progress in each indicator is either similar to, or more 
moderate than in the last plan.

With annual growth projected to be in a “reasonable range” over the period, 
policymakers are treading cautiously:

Meanwhile, February’s headline inflation, surging to a three-year-high, showed 
China gaining in its battle with disinflation — but closer inspection reveals 
that the jump is closely linked to the holiday travel-related service price 
hikes:

This raises the stakes of any energy shock from the Iran conflict. However, 
Benson Wu of Bank of America argues that since China’s average CPI inflation is 
still under 1%, well below the PBOC’s 2% target, it’s unlikely that higher oil 
prices will trigger any immediate monetary policy change:

That said, we will watch very closely to the latest development in Iran as 
well as the Strait of Hormuz. Given over one-third of China’s oil imports came 
through the strait, any supply chain disruption could cause direct inflationary 
pressure domestically.

Gavekal Research’s Louis-Vincent Gave downplays China’s dependence 
<https://bloom.bg/46TXTWZ> on Iranian energy supply. He argues that Beijing 
isn’t among the countries most affected, even if the war is a US effort to 
tighten its strategic chokehold on China:

The US punched China on the nose in 2018, and China spent the past 
six-to-eight years going to the gym, getting fit, strong, self-sufficient, so 
that when the next punch came around, it wouldn’t get punched, and that’s where 
we are today.

Bloomberg Economics’ analysis notes that a sustained oil price above $108 per 
barrel would have a secondary shock on global demand for Chinese exports. David 
Qu points out that a 5% fall in exports would lower growth by 0.16 percentage 
points. That would be similar to the 2023 drop in exports, when rate hikes 
weighed on global demand. In a more severe scenario where exports plunge 10%, 
it would shave 0.31 percentage points off GDP growth — similar to the external 
demand shock in the first half of 2016 necessitated by widespread trade 
disputes.

As daunting as these scenarios may seem to the fragile economy, the analysts 
believe there’s still a way out. China has adequate tools to offset the impact, 
and they expect policymakers to ease monetary or fiscal stances if needed. 
Still, with global growth imperiled by the situation in the Strait of Hormuz, 
investors might have hoped for a more clearly positive message out of China.

—Richard Abbey
