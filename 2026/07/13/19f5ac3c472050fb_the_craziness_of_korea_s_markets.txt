The stock market seen benefiting most from the AI boom is experiencing the 
greatest volatility since the height of the 1998 Asian financial crisis.

Such instability is raising questions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/sk-hynix-shares-drop-in-seoul-after-much-hyped-us-trading-debut?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 about the duration and sustainability of a rally that sent the MSCI Korea 
Index up more than 250% in the 12 months through June. In other words, how does 
this end?

A measure of 60-day price swings on the MSCI Korea has climbed to the highest 
since February 1998, topping even the 2008 financial crisis, as the gauge peaks 
amid frenzied trading andpiles of leverage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/samsung-sk-hynix-and-leveraged-etfs-drive-70-of-korea-trading?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
. The index has now fallen 28% from its June high, having plunged 10% Monday, 
to trade at levels last seen more than two months ago. Extreme moves have 
triggered circuit breakers seven times this year, compared with six in the past 
25 years.

Such epic volatility, which isn’t being matched anywhere else, is head 
scratching. Sure, there are concerns about how single-stock ETFs aremagnifying 
swings 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-07-01/how-a-13-billion-leveraged-etf-drives-volatility-in-a-key-ai-memory-stock?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
. The sheer size of SK Hynix and Samsung, which together account for about two 
thirds of the MSCI Korea, means the country’s stock market isunduly influenced 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-28/south-korean-stocks-world-beating-surge-creates-bubble-risk?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 by their moves. And worries about the sustainabilityof AI spending 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-07-05/semiconductor-stocks-slide-amid-ai-spending-concerns-video?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 has weighed on shares of other chipmakers too, like Micron and Japan’s Kioxia.

But there is little doubt these companies are doing well out of AI. Samsung’s 
quarterly profitsurged 19-fold 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/samsung-scores-profit-beat-due-to-runaway-demand-for-ai-memory?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 in its most recent results. Memory-chip shortages willprobably persist 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/sk-hynix-shares-drop-in-seoul-after-much-hyped-us-trading-debut?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 beyond 2030, SK Hynix CEO Kwak Noh-Jung said on Friday, underscoring solid 
demand.His company 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/sk-hynix-what-to-know-about-us-listing-how-it-became-an-ai-powerhouse?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 was able to raise $26.5 billion in its US offering last week, making itthe 
largest ever 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/sk-hynix-indicated-to-climb-17-after-26-5-billion-adr-offering?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 US listing by a foreign company. Yet that didn’t stop SK Hynix plunging by a 
record 15% on Monday.

Whatever the cause, such wild trading suggests turbulence will remain 
elevated, undermining a great success story and making itharder for investors 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-12/funds-fret-over-4-4-trillion-ai-trio-s-grip-on-emerging-markets?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 to find winners in this transformational technology boom. As to how it ends, 
history is full ofpast examples 
<https://www.goodreads.com/en/book/show/162120.Extraordinary_Popular_Delusions_and_the_Madness_of_Crowds>
 of crowded trades. Given these companies’ promising outlook, will this time be 
different?

Rows of gold bars and stacks of US and Singapore dollars found inside a locked 
safe have become the defining images of one of the biggest scandals to hit 
Indonesia’s law-enforcement establishment in years.

Police said they recovered 74 kilograms (163 pounds) of gold, plus more than 
$15 million in multiple currencies from a house linked to Febrie Adriansyah, 
who until last week served as Indonesia’s deputy attorney general for special 
crimes, and is now suspected of corruption.

Indian equities have been beneficiaries of this recent tumult. Foreign 
investors poured more than $1 billion intothe nation’s stocks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/global-funds-return-to-indian-stocks-with-1-billion-in-buying?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6466>
 last week, with Goldman Sachs predicting further inflows as a stable currency 
and improved earnings expectations prompt funds to add to holdings.

Global funds were net buyers of $1.3 billion of Indian equities in the four 
days through July 9, set to be the biggest weekly purchase since at least June 
last year They bought an additional $272 million worth of local shares on 
Friday, provisional data showed.
