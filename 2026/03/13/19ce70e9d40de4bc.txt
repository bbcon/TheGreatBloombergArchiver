Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter David Wethe looks at the prospect that US oil production may keep 
growing in 2027.

As crude flirts with prices of $100 a barrel or more on the back of the war in 
Iran, could the US oil sector ride to the rescue of consumers?

So far, no producer has publicly stated an intention to deploy more drill rigs 
or increase output in response to the conflict, which has crippled supplies 
from the Persian Gulf.

There’s good reason to think they won’t act. Boosting production has became 
practically taboo in the shale industry in recent years, after an earlier 
profligate period of breakneck growth that was followed by a pandemic-era bust.

Thanks to investor demands for production and financial discipline, US oil 
executives now routinely pledge to keep output flat. That mindset, and the fact 
that the best shale rock has already been fracked, has led to predictions that 
Peak Shale is imminent.

Yet such calls last year proved to be premature 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-12-03/us-shale-s-downbeat-executives-are-all-talk?cmpid=BBBXT031326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, and US shale output expanded once again to a record.
Pumpjacks near Crane, Texas.Photographer: Justin Hamel/Bloomberg
There are early signs that US industry activity is ticking higher. Data last 
Friday showed anincrease <bbg://news/stories/TBHPH5T96OSH> in the deployment of 
drill rigs.

And earlier this week, the government published its latest monthly energy 
report in whichit forecast 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/us-raises-2027-oil-output-forecast-after-prices-soar-on-iran-war?cmpid=BBBXT031326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 oil production will now rise in 2027 instead of falling, citing disruption in 
the Middle East. Previously it saw output peaking this year.

Interior Secretary Doug Burgum 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/14007661> said this week that energy 
companies havetold <bbg://news/stories/TBRV2SKJH6V4> him they plan to increase 
activity in the Permian and the Bakken, the nation’s two largest shale basins.

The short-term financial incentive to do that may be enough to outweigh the 
risk of angering investors. West Texas Intermediate closed Thursday at its 
highest since August 2022. Prices could move even higher, with no clarity yet 
on when shipments will resume through the Strait of Hormuz.

Furthermore, drilling to help fill the supply gap could be a politically 
astute move for anyone bold enough to do so.

No US leader wants to see headlines about higher fuel prices, and having won 
an election on a pledge to keep energy costs in check, it may not be long 
before President Donald Trump implores Big Oil to step up and do its part.

—David Wethe, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

The Iran war is fueling a rally in European power prices, though the region is 
yet to see the kind of spike in electricity costs that accompanied Russia’s 
invasion of Ukraine in 2022. Germany offers a window into understanding why. 
Its higher wind and solar installed capacity, along with structurally lower 
demand, has reduced the need for gas to plug gaps in coal or lignite 
generation, which is gradually being phased out, according to BloombergNEF.
