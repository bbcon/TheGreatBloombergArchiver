Roars and Whimpers

China’s two-speed economy — roaring exports and whimpering demand at home — 
was on full display Tuesday when the latest data for May dropped.

Retail sales 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-s-first-consumer-spending-drop-since-covid-imperils-growth?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 declined 0.6% from a year ago, posting the first fall since the reopening from 
Covid lockdowns in late 2022. By contrast, industrial production climbed 4.5% 
as high-tech manufacturing soared 15% in terms of value added in May from a 
year ago. Fixed-asset investment also shrank.

The “data underscore a widening two-speed economy,” Chang Shu 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/ATwU2T7qXnU/chang-shu?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 andEric Zhu 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/AVK6e_X298w/eric-zhu-economist?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 at Bloomberg Economics wrote in areport <>. “The supply side remains robust, 
driven by faster-than-expected expansion in exports and AI tech sectors. The 
demand side has faltered, with consumption and private non-tech investment 
plummeting.”

It’s a theme that has defined China’s economy for years now as a post-Covid 
export boom and itsrapid ascent 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-boosting-aid-to-poor-countries-the-us-abandoned?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 up the technology ladder coincided with an ongoing property slump that’s 
sapped households’ wealth and confidence.

And just as the economy has two speeds, there’s two ways to interpret it all 
too.

To economists including Macquarie’s Larry Hu, Beijing is utilizing the export 
bonanza to allow the property correction to run its course, all while meeting 
its overall growth target of around 5%. If exports stall, policy makers will 
step in to do more to prop updomestic demand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/chinese-bonds-draw-foreign-funds-in-may-after-a-year-of-outflows?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
, but while ever the external sector remains robust there’s less need for 
significant stimulus.

But there’s another side to the story.

To Alicia Garcia Herrero, chief economist for Asia-Pacific at Natixis, the 
two-speed growth isn’t sometransitional imbalance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-s-oil-refiners-slash-output-after-crude-imports-plunge?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 awaiting correction, but rather a structural design engineered to win the 
strategic competition with the US.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-16/china-s-consumer-spending-drop-imperils-growth-video?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
WATCH: UBS Securities Chief China Economist Yu Song discuss how the drop in the 
country’s consumer spending is a threat to economic growth.
“The model relies on financially repressed households to fund state-directed 
investment in high-end manufacturing and technological self-reliance, with a 
closed capital account ensuring cheap domestic savings remain captive,” she 
wrote in a recent note. “Yet the model is generating deepening cracks.”

Profits are falling amid excessive competition, wages are stagnating, AI risks 
displacing workers, sky-high debt constrains policy options and global 
protectionism is rising. Without structural reform, “the dual economy’s 
internal contradictions will continue to compound,” according to Herrero.

Need-to-Know Research

Warsh made clear before becoming Fed chair that he viewed US central bankers 
as deliveringexcessive communication 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2017-10-11/central-bankers-fare-better-when-they-are-tight-lipped?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 to the public, and favoredwinding the clock back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/warsh-wants-less-fed-talk-risking-more-market-surprises?cmpid=BBD061626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5563>
 toward the era of Alan Greenspan, when guidance on forthcoming policy 
decisions was limited.

Goldman Sachs analysis suggests it would be unwise to completely undo the past 
three decades of steadily increasing transparency, however. A shift toward 
clearer communication from theearly 2000s 
<https://www.federalreserve.gov/boarddocs/speeches/2004/200401032/default.htm> 
helped to “reduce uncertainty and volatility in financial markets,” Friedrich 
Schaper, a macro research associate at the bank, wrote in a recent note.

The impact was such that it reduced the so-called term premium — the extra 
yield investors demand to buy longer-dated Treasuries vs. just rolling over 
short-dated ones — on 10-year US notes by about 5 basis points, Goldman 
estimates. Better communication was “low-hanging fruit to reduce borrowing 
costs,” Schaper wrote.

That said, Jerome Powell’s press conferences corresponded to “greater intraday 
volatility,” Schaper said, suggesting not every bit of added communication 
quelled uncertainty.
