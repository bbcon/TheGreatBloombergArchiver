Landmark cases defeated

By Keira Wright <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVpmg49A96U/keira-wright> 
andJennifer Hijazi 
<https://news.bloomberglaw.com/daily-labor-report/judge-rejects-novel-climate-case-against-north-carolina-utility>

A judge dismissed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/landmark-greenwashing-case-against-gas-producer-santos-dismissed?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 a long-running legal challenge against Santos, which had accused the 
Australian oil and gas producer of misleading investors over its climate 
strategy.

The case brought by the Australasian Centre for Corporate Responsibility 
<https://www.accr.org.au/>, a shareholder advocacy group, was rejected earlier 
today by Justice Brigitte Markovic. Details of her decision will be published 
Feb. 23, she told the Federal Court of Australia hearing in Sydney.

Adelaide-based Santos was first sued in 2021 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-08-26/gas-producer-s-net-zero-pledge-challenged-in-court-by-activist?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 by the group, which had alleged the company was deceptive in portraying itself 
as a clean energy producer ininvestor materials 
<https://www.santos.com/wp-content/uploads/2021/02/2020-Annual-Report.pdf> and 
by suggesting it had a “clear and credible” plan to hit net zero by 2040.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/landmark-greenwashing-case-against-gas-producer-santos-dismissed>
A sticker against the Narrabri gas fields in AustraliaPhotographer: Brendon 
Thorne/Bloomberg
ACCR also challenged the company’s description of natural gas as a clean fuel 
— amajor point of contention 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-02-29/biden-lng-approval-pause-influenced-by-cornell-methane-scientist?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 among climate activists. The case was the first of its type to legally 
challenge the validity of a company’s plan to reach net zero, according to the 
organization.

Santos is committed to “transparent, accurate and compliant reporting,” the 
company said in a statement, adding that it welcomes the court’s decision. 
Santos has developed a climate transition action plan that continues to evolve, 
it said.

“This court case was not about punishing climate ambition, it was about 
standing up for market integrity and ensuring that investors are given all the 
information necessary to confidently assess emissions targets and net zero 
plans,” ACCR’s co-Chief Executive OfficerBrynn O’Brien 
<https://www.accr.org.au/about/people/brynn-obrien/> said in a statement. 
“Today’s result is disappointing.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/landmark-greenwashing-case-against-gas-producer-santos-dismissed>
Pressure transmitters display readings on a Santos pilot well in Narrabri, 
AustraliaPhotographer: Brendon Thorne/Bloomberg
Also this week, in the US, a first-of-its-kind climate lawsuit 
<https://news.bloomberglaw.com/daily-labor-report/judge-rejects-novel-climate-case-against-north-carolina-utility>
 specifically challenging an energy utility was dismissed by a state judge in 
North Carolina.

In 2024, the town of Carrboro, N.C., sued Duke Energy, the state’s dominant 
energy utility, looking to put the massive electric and natural gas company on 
the hook for climate damages exacerbated by its alleged misinformation about 
fossil fuels.

But Special Superior Court Judge Mark Davis, of the General Court of Justice 
Superior Court Division for Orange County, called the claims against Duke 
Energy “clearly nonjusticiable” and decided this case is beyond the scope of 
judicial action.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/landmark-greenwashing-case-against-gas-producer-santos-dismissed>
Emissions rise from the Duke Energy coal-fired Asheville Power Plant in Arden, 
North Carolina, U.S.Photographer: Charles Mostoller/Bloomberg
This dismissal is of one of many that judges have handed down in similar cases 
in state courts. Cases in Hawaii and Colorado have seen more success, however, 
and those are still on the way to trial pending US Supreme Court review.

India’s solar conundrum

By Rajesh Kumar Singh 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQMyDU0Qjbo/rajesh-kumar-singh>

India’s solar manufacturing industry 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/india-s-solar-manufacturing-excesses-turn-a-boom-into-a-glut?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 is becoming a victim of its own success.

A push to expand local production — encouraged by pandemic disruptions and 
strained relations with China — has resulted in a 13-fold jump in capacity 
since 2020, according to BloombergNEF, to nearly triple domestic demand.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/india-s-solar-manufacturing-excesses-turn-a-boom-into-a-glut>
The scale of that surge prompted the government late last year to urge banks 
lending to the sector to exercise caution. Manufacturers, meanwhile, are 
beginning to pull back from lower-value output.

Capacity utilization at the country’s module-assembly plants has shrunk to 
around 40% from more than 70% in the year through March 2023, a time before 
punitive US tariffs when exports to the world’s largest economy were booming, 
according toAvinash Hiranandani 
<https://www.renewsysworld.com/board-of-directors>, managing director at module 
maker RenewSys India Pvt. Ltd.

“This is not a slowdown. It’s a structural glut,” Hiranandani said in an 
interview.

Opinion: Asia’s climate $$$

By Mihir Sharma 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/authors/AS4aIU7RcB4/mihir-sharma?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-02-16/asia-needs-to-play-smarter-to-win-climate-dollars>
Residential buildings damaged by floods in Jakarta, IndonesiaPhotographer: 
Dimas Ardian/Bloomberg
Every month, disasters caused by extreme weather kill more people in South and 
Southeast Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-02-16/asia-needs-to-play-smarter-to-win-climate-dollars?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
.

Some at-risk nations have done better than others by creating tight-knit 
collectives that pressure global organizations and leverage their symbolic 
weight. Small island nations, for example, pull together at occasions like 
climate summits and make sure their voices are heard; unsurprisingly, they 
often do better, in per capita terms, at snagging adaptation finance. Tiny 
Barbados, home of the Bridgetown Initiative to reform multilateral finance, gets
$24 per person 
<https://www.cgdev.org/blog/do-most-climate-vulnerable-countries-get-more-adaptation-finance-world-bank>
, while Indonesia, the world’s fourth-most populous country, gets $0.76.

South and Southeast Asia need to do better 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-02-16/asia-needs-to-play-smarter-to-win-climate-dollars?cmpid=BBD021726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260217>
.
