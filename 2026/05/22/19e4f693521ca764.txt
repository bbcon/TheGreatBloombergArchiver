Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Senior Editor James Herron looks at governments’ efforts to boost tax revenue 
from major oil companies.

Just a few weeks after Big Oil reported bumper trading profits 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/bp-profit-jumps-as-oil-trading-boomed-during-iran-war?cmpid=BBBXT052226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4762>
 stemming from the Iran war, Europe’s tax collectors are eyeing their share.

From new windfall levies to closing loopholes, governments from the UK to 
Poland are looking at ways to boost state coffers and shield their citizens 
from price inflation.

There’s money on the table, but with the locus of the oil and gas industry 
having long since shifted beyond Europe’s borders, only a sliver of global 
profits may be within reach.

And hitting the industry with another round of extra taxes, following similar 
steps after Russia’s 2022 full-scale invasion of Ukraine, could further dim the 
region’s appeal to international oil companies.

UK Chancellor of the Exchequer Rachel Reeves announced on Thursday that she 
wouldclose a loophole <> so oil and gas firms can’t structure their affairs to 
pay little or no corporation tax in the country.

The proceeds of about £300 million ($400 million) a year would be spent on 
measures to ease the cost of living for voters. That compares with a combined 
$3.7 billion in UK taxes paid last year by BP Plc and Shell Plc, on total 
global revenue of $456 billion, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

Poland’s government has proposed a windfall tax on producers of liquid fuels, 
which could raise an extra 6 billion zloty ($1.6 billion) from refiner Orlen SA.

In France, Budget Minister David Amiel has noted that TotalEnergies SE has 
made “exceptional profits 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/france-says-no-taboo-on-possible-totalenergies-exceptional-tax?cmpid=BBBXT052226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4762>
” and wouldn’t rule out new taxes on the company. For now, however, the 
government is sticking to a fuel-price cap as its main response to the war.

Squeezing oil majors for extra revenue is tempting in times of high energy 
prices, but carries some risk. The UK’s 2022 windfall tax has been blamed by 
the industry for a collapse inNorth Sea investment <> that could ultimately 
hurt the country’s energy security.

There was a hint of this in the French government’s hesitance to commit to 
“exceptional redistribution” of TotalEnergies’ profits.

“France is lucky to have Total, but Total is lucky to have France,” Amiel said.

—James Herron, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

The US is on track to build a record volume of new wind, solar and battery 
capacity in 2026 despite trade and policy headwinds from the Trump 
administration, according to BloombergNEF. The president’s signature One Big 
Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) tightened tax credit deadlines for solar and wind 
projects, but that’s only accelerated project starts as developers dash to 
comply and capitalize on data-center-driven electricity demand.
