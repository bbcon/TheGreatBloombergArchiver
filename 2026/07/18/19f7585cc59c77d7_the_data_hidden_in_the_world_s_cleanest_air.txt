A climate watchtower

By David Stringer 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ARMqcSMt218/david-stringer?cmpid=BBD071826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260718&utm_content=6689>

Relentless winds sweep more than 10,000 kilometers (6,214 miles) across the 
Southern Ocean, from the fringes of South America to Tasmania’s craggy 
northwestern coast, delivering some of theworld’s cleanest air 
<https://www.csiro.au/en/news/all/articles/2025/february/cloud-precipitation-experiment-kennaook>
 to intake pipes near the top of an80-meter-high tower 
<https://amplitel.com.au/blog/new-engineering-standards>.

Untainted by pollution from the cities and industries that crowd the Northern 
Hemisphere, the air provides a unique sample of Earth’s atmosphere. It has made 
this isolated corner of Australia one of the world’s most important sites for 
monitoring humanity’s impact on climate change.
The view from the Kennaook/Cape Grim Baseline Air Pollution Station in 
Tasmania, Australia, in MayPhotographer: Matthew Newton for Bloomberg
“It’s like looking back into the past,” says Sarah Prior, officer in charge at 
Kennaook/Cape Grim Baseline Air Pollution Station 
<https://research.csiro.au/acc/capabilities/cape-grim-baseline-air-pollution-station/>
, a research outpost perched on tussock-dotted cliffs overlooking a 
crescent-shaped bay. “It’s an atmosphere that’s as close to pre-industrial 
times as we can get.”

For the past 50 years — initially from a NASA-donated caravan 
<https://www.csiro.au/en/news/All/News/2026/April/50-years-measuring-the-worlds-cleanest-air>
 used to track Apollo missions from Australia — scientists have stored and 
analyzed the relatively pristine air, documenting rising concentrations of 
carbon dioxide and dozens of other pollutants.

“We are able to see things that you can’t see in a polluted air mass,” Prior 
says. “You’re able to see what is natural variability, you’re able to see those 
natural seasonal cycles, and then you’re also able to tease out what is 
changing due to the human impacts.”

A chart mounted in a corridor at the cabin-like facility gives a stark account 
of those observations. CO2 levels analyzed in Antarctic ice core records show 
only minimal fluctuations for hundreds of years until the dawn of the 
Industrial Revolution, before an increasingly rapid ascent outlined in data 
from both that location and the Australian site.

The Tasmanian lab sits within the Roaring Forties, a band of Southern 
latitudes known for powerful and consistent westerly winds. About 30% of the 
time, it receives air arriving from the Southern Ocean that is clean enough to 
qualify as “baseline” air for use in climate datasets, a higher share than at 
other research centers, according to Prior.

Along with Mauna Loa <https://gml.noaa.gov/obop/mlo/> in Hawaii and Alert 
<https://www.canada.ca/en/environment-climate-change/services/science-technology/centres/nunavut.html#neil>
, in Nunavut in the Canadian Arctic, Kennaook/Cape Grim is one of three major 
global climate monitoring stations whose observations are used by international 
policymakers, including in every report by the Intergovernmental Panel on 
Climate Change. Its importance is only growing now that President Donald Trump 
is curbing US science funding and other governments globally are struggling to 
support environmental research.

In Australia, the government’s Commonwealth Scientific and Industrial Research 
Organisation (CSIRO) — which isjointly responsible 
<https://research.csiro.au/acc/capabilities/cape-grim-baseline-air-pollution-station/>
 for Kennaook/Cape Grim with the Bureau of Meteorology — iscutting roles 
<https://csirostaff.org.au/news/2026/05/13/big-csiro-funding-boost-but-what-about-job-security-and-accountability/>
, even asoverall funding increases 
<https://www.csiro.au/en/news/All/News/2026/May/CSIRO-receives-additional-funding-in-the-2026-27-Federal-Budget>
. “For climate scientists, this looks like a false economy,” saysRobert Ryan 
<https://findanexpert.unimelb.edu.au/profile/657156-robert-g.-ryan>, an 
atmospheric scientist at the University of Melbourne, referring to a broader 
trend of governments squeezing budgets and staffing even as the risk of 
crossing climate tipping points grows. “Short-term savings made now are very 
likely to be leading to long-term losses and challenges.”

Today, on a weathered rooftop deck, a spinning anemometer and a series of 
masts poke up among mossy wooden platforms. Sturdy metal boxes house 
instruments that sample some of the more than 100 gases, air pollutants and 
aerosols tracked by the facility.
The Kennaook/Cape Grim Baseline Air Pollution Station on May 27.Photographer: 
Matthew Newton for Bloomberg
Six times a year, electropolished cylinders take in about 2,000 liters of 
baseline air, adding toan archive of more than 200 samples 
<https://research.csiro.au/acc/capabilities/cape-grim-air-archive/> dating to 
1978. They are stored in a suburb of Melbourne on the Australian mainland, 
which scientists liken to a climate wine cellar.

The station is not just a critical watch post for evidence of atmospheric 
change in the Southern Hemisphere. It also plays a key role in efforts to 
understand howdifferences in the formation of clouds 
<https://www.csiro.au/en/news/All/News/2024/May/International-effort-to-solve-Southern-Ocean-cloud-mysteries>
 — which can influence how much heat is trapped in the atmosphere — affect 
global warming, and how effectively the Southern Ocean absorbs CO2.

In the main laboratory, the prized air is fed through a network of pipes and 
driers. Tall gas canisters are stacked at the ends of narrow rows of work 
stations, each crowded with monitor screens, tangles of cabling and racks of 
microwave-size machines — some dating back about as long as the facility 
itself, whichbegan operations in 1976 
<https://research.csiro.au/acc/capabilities/cape-grim-baseline-air-pollution-station/>
.

“I’m still learning this guy,” says Ann Stavert, a Canberra-based senior 
research scientist at CSIRO, pointing to a bulky gas chromatograph. “It’s 
almost older than I am, and there is no instruction manual.”

China’s giant EV problem

By Bloomberg News 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/?cmpid=BBD071826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260718&utm_content=6689>

China’s new generation of supersized electric vehicles — some more than 5 
meters long and weighing three tons — is straining the nation’s roads while 
starving the government of fuel taxes to maintain them.

Six out of 10 new cars launched in the first half of this year stretched more 
than 5 meters (16 feet) — about the length of a Ford Explorer SUV. By 
comparison, just 2% of new models were compact vehicles under 4.5 meters, down 
from 13% a year earlier, according to Cui Dongshu, secretary-general of the 
China Passenger Car Association.
The BYD Great Tang.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
The popularity of big, heavy cars is creating a fiscal dilemma for China’s 
local governments. Beijing bankrolls major national highways and collects a 
fuel tax that is distributed to regional authorities to maintain local roads. 
However, that revenue is dwindling as EVs, which tend to be heavier than 
gasoline cars, account for an ever-growing slice of China’s vehicle fleet and 
cause more road damage. That raises the case for overhauling how roads are 
funded, potentially by introducing measures such as mileage-based road user 
charges.

The financial strain is already pushing China’s road network to a breaking 
point. A study by a research unit at the nation’s transport ministry found 
China faces an annual funding shortfall of roughly 50% for ordinary road 
maintenance and management. Around 40% of local roads are trapped in a critical 
bottleneck, described by the report as “listed for maintenance but starved of 
funds, and desperately needing repairs but lacking the budget.”

, andRakesh Sharma 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWI8OhLj4vk/rakesh-sharma?cmpid=BBD071826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260718&utm_content=6689>

Three hours north of Ahmedabad in Gujarat state, in one of India’s main dairy 
belts, the traffic at a fuel station begins early.

Passenger cars, small trucks and auto-rickshaws file steadily into the large 
forecourt to fill their tanks with fuel that is both renewable and cheaper than 
petrol or diesel — biological compressed natural gas (CNG) that is made from 
methane from cattle dung.

The station in the district of Banaskantha is backed by Japan’s Suzuki Motor 
Corp. and Banas Dairy, Asia’s largest dairy cooperative, and fuels roughly 600 
to 700 vehicles daily. It’s a small but important example of how India, a major 
energy importer, is trying to diversify its fuel sources in the wake of the 
Iran war. Most importantly, it’s utilizing a material that India has an 
abundance of: cow dung.

In India, where cows are considered sacred, repurposing dung as a modern 
transport fuel links one of the country’s oldest agricultural resources with 
its search for greater energy security. Cow dung has long been woven into 
everyday life across rural India — it’s dried into cakes for cooking fuel, 
plastered onto the floors and walls of village homes, spread on fields as 
manure and used in religious rituals and festivals.
Japan has signed a strategic plan with India to add 1,000 new biogas plants in 
the country.Photographer: Abeer Khan/Bloomberg
India has in fact been producing bio-CNG for years from municipal solid waste, 
agricultural residue and forage crops such as napier grass. In Indore, one of 
India’s cleanest cities, buses already run on biogas generated from wet waste, 
but efforts to scale the fuel are gathering fresh urgency.

Facilities like the Banas Suzuki Bio CNG station are in their nascent stages, 
but they received a major boost when Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi 
signed a strategic plan with India to add 1,000 newbiogas plants 
<https://www.mea.gov.in/press-releases?dtl/41391> in the country during her 
visit this month. India is also planning to boost theprice it pays 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/india-set-to-boost-biogas-prices-subsidies-in-new-cbg-program?cmpid=BBD071826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260718&utm_content=6689>
 to producers of biogas as part of its policy to lift production.

“The Strait of Hormuz crisis has proved that energy security and energy 
transition are not competing objectives,” said Srinivas Kasulla, founding 
partner of Tattva Engineering Systems, a biogas plant technical consultancy. 
“Compressed biogas is where they converge. It is domestic, renewable, 
dispatchable and contributes to farmer incomes. That’s a four-for-one policy 
win.”

For automakers too, the appeal of using eco-friendly fuels like CNG is 
growing. Companies like Suzuki Motor and its India unit have invested heavily 
in CNG vehicles, and see biogas as a way to cut emissions and boost demand for 
their vehicles running on compressed gas.

The company believes the industry is approaching an inflection point. Rahul 
Bharti, senior executive officer for corporate affairs at Maruti Suzuki India, 
said that with further government support, in a few years CBG could scale up to 
“become not just a mainstream energy option but also a very sizable part of 
overall CNG production in India.”
