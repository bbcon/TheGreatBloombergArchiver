**Federal Reserve at the Crossroads**

The Federal Reserve's March 19 FOMC meeting was the week's defining event for US markets. The committee voted 11-1 to hold the benchmark rate at 3.5%–3.75%, but Chair Jerome Powell's press conference generated significant volatility in its own right. Powell made clear that rate cuts remain contingent on demonstrated inflation progress — a bar that appears increasingly distant given the concurrent pressures of tariff-driven price acceleration and an escalating Middle East energy shock. The Fed's preferred core inflation gauge ran at an estimated 3% in February, a full percentage point above target, and core PPI rose to a three-year high, indicating the pipeline was already accelerating before the Strait of Hormuz blockade. Powell acknowledged "meaningful" internal movement toward fewer cuts and explicitly noted that colleagues had raised the possibility that the next move could be a hike. Futures markets repriced sharply: from two cuts anticipated earlier in the year to zero by week's end. The S&P 500 recorded its largest Fed-day decline since 2024 on Wednesday.

**Succession and Institutional Uncertainty**

Powell's remarks on his own tenure amplified market unease. He confirmed that if Kevin Warsh is not confirmed before his May term expiry, he will remain as chair pro tempore, and stated he will not vacate his board seat — running to January 2028 — until the Justice Department investigation into the Fed's building renovation project concludes. Senator Thom Tillis is blocking Warsh's confirmation vote pending the probe's resolution, and prosecutor Jeanine Pirro has signaled she will not abandon the inquiry. Compounding institutional concerns, senior Justice Department officials rallied behind a US attorney's challenge to Powell's authority, with the White House declining to oppose the effort — a development with potentially significant implications for central bank independence. Markets are increasingly uncomfortable with the prospect of a protracted, politically charged transition at the institution.

**Energy Prices and the Consumer**

US average gasoline prices rose roughly 27% since hostilities began on February 28, reaching approximately $3.70 per gallon by Sunday and approaching levels that historical precedent associates with meaningful electoral consequences. Diesel surpassed $5 per gallon for the first time since December 2022. SMBC Nikko Securities estimated the gasoline price increase could reduce the savings rate by approximately 60 basis points to roughly 3.9%. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to a three-month low in the March preliminary reading, and January consumer spending was essentially flat even before the energy spike fully registered. Oxford Economics cautioned that higher energy costs threaten to suppress household decisions in coming months. The administration, facing simultaneous tariff pressure and energy inflation, directed Sable Offshore to restore California offshore oil production and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent floated the idea of lifting sanctions on approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian crude already at sea — though analysts noted virtually all such oil flows to Chinese refiners, limiting the practical price relief.

**Equities and Corporate News**

US equity futures edged higher early in the week on Monday, with S&P 500 contracts up 0.6%, but sentiment deteriorated markedly as the week progressed. By Friday, futures were off 0.6% amid Iran war uncertainty and the Fed's hawkish tilt, with Goldman Sachs's trading desk noting that clients who had anticipated a quick resolution to the conflict are increasingly doubtful. FedEx climbed roughly 10% on Friday after raising its earnings forecast, providing a modest counterpoint. Super Micro tumbled 22% after federal prosecutors charged a co-founder with illegally diverting Nvidia-powered servers to China. Meta rose approximately 3% early in the week on reports of a $27 billion cloud infrastructure deal with Nebius. Lululemon fell roughly 2% after forecasting a second consecutive year of profit declines, while DocuSign rose after beating earnings. The US 10-year Treasury yield rose to approximately 4.30% by week's end. New York Governor Hochul's proposal to delay the state's 2030 emissions reduction target to 2040, citing energy affordability pressures, illustrated the political difficulty of sustaining decarbonization commitments when fuel costs are acute — a notable signal from a reliably Democratic state.
