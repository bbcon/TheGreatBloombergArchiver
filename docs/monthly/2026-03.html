Monthly Brief

March 2026

US

  • The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariff architecture and the administration's rapid pivot to a 15% replacement levy under Section 122 defined the month's opening policy framework, with legal durability remaining in question throughout.
  • US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities late in the month introduced a compounding energy and geopolitical shock, pushing WTI above $84 by mid-month and average gasoline to approximately $3.70 per gallon by month's end — the highest under Trump's presidency.
  • The S&P 500 posted its worst monthly performance since April, declining roughly 3% in March, with the Magnificent Seven approaching correction territory and treasury yields rising to 4.30–4.44% as rate-cut expectations evaporated.
  • The Fed held at 3.5%–3.75% at its March 19 meeting, with Powell explicitly acknowledging internal movement toward fewer cuts and markets pricing zero reductions by month's end — a complete reversal from consensus entering the period.
  • Private credit stress intensified materially, with Blackstone's flagship fund facing a record $3.8 billion redemption request, Apollo capping withdrawals from its $25 billion vehicle, and Fitch reporting a trailing 12-month default rate of 5.8%.

Tariff Regime in Transition

The month opened with the legal and practical fallout from the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariff architecture. By the first Tuesday of March, a 15% global levy under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 had entered into force. The legal footing was widely questioned from the outset: Section 122 applies specifically to "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath stated plainly the US does not meet, given the absence of dollar weakness or capital flight that a genuine balance-of-payments crisis would produce. The administration signaled it would slow-walk refunds on overturned levies even as more than 1,500 refund lawsuits were filed representing an estimated $170 billion in contested duties. Later in the month, a fresh Section 301 trade investigation was filed against more than a dozen economies — including China, the EU, Mexico, India, and Taiwan — with a public hearing set for May 5. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimated the IEEPA invalidation reduces projected federal revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036, with the Section 122 replacement recovering approximately $925 billion — a material gap.

Equity Markets and the AI Disruption Narrative

The month's dominant early equity theme was a rotation away from US software and services names perceived as vulnerable to AI displacement. IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years after Anthropic's Claude Code tool was demonstrated to modernize COBOL, threatening the mainframe franchise. C3.ai tumbled 24%, Salesforce declined roughly 4%, and Workday dropped 9.2%. Against this, hardware and infrastructure names provided partial offset: Dell gained 9.8% on AI server demand, and Block surged as much as 27% after announcing workforce reductions framed as an AI productivity bet. Nvidia's earnings — with revenue guidance of approximately $78 billion beating consensus — produced a muted response, reflecting the increasingly demanding valuation bar facing AI-adjacent equities. The S&P 500 options market showed elevated caution early in the month, with the put-call skew rising to a two-year high. By late March, the conflict-driven growth and inflation headwinds had taken over as the primary equity driver, with the Magnificent Seven gauge down nearly 10% from its peak and Goldman Sachs's trading desk noting that clients who had anticipated a quick geopolitical resolution were increasingly skeptical.

Federal Reserve: Policy Bind and Succession

The collision between a deteriorating labor market and an oil-driven inflation shock defined the Fed's operating environment across March. February payrolls recorded the first private-sector decline in five years, data that under ordinary conditions would reinforce the case for easing. Instead, the energy price shock overwhelmed the labor market signal. At the March 19 FOMC meeting, the committee voted 11-1 to hold, but Powell's press conference was notably hawkish, with the chair explicitly acknowledging that colleagues had raised the possibility that the next move could be a hike. The Fed's preferred core inflation gauge ran at an estimated 3% in February, and core PCE is tracking approximately 3.1% for January — the widest divergence from CPI in decades. Goldman Sachs pushed its forecast for the first rate cut to September, while the ICE BofA MOVE Index climbed to its highest level since June. By month's end, futures markets were briefly pricing a non-trivial probability of a rate hike at the April meeting — the first such pricing since 2023. The anticipated succession of Kevin Warsh as Fed chair added a further layer of hawkish market pricing, even as his confirmation remained blocked by Senator Tillis pending a Justice Department investigation. Powell confirmed he will remain in his board seat until January 2028 regardless of his chair tenure, and the White House's decision not to oppose a legal challenge to his authority raised uncomfortable questions about central bank independence.

Energy Economics and Fiscal Deterioration

US average gasoline prices rose approximately 27% from the onset of hostilities in late February, reaching $3.70 per gallon by month's end, while diesel surpassed $5 per gallon for the first time since December 2022. SMBC Nikko Securities estimated the gasoline price increase could reduce the household savings rate by approximately 60 basis points. Bloomberg Economics found that near $83 per barrel crude is sufficient to neutralize the approximately $800 average household benefit from "One Big Beautiful Bill" tax refunds entirely, with the net household position turning negative by roughly $1,960 at $110 per barrel — a distributional concern compounded by energy costs' regressive nature. The Pentagon's $200 billion supplemental funding request for the Iran operation threatens to push the 2026 federal deficit toward $2 trillion, roughly double Treasury Secretary Bessent's 3%-of-GDP target. Goldman Sachs raised its 12-month US recession probability to 30% by month's end, projecting unemployment rising to 4.6% by year-end. The administration directed Sable Offshore to restore California offshore oil production, and Treasury Secretary Bessent floated lifting sanctions on approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian crude at sea — though analysts noted virtually all such oil flows to Chinese refiners, limiting practical price relief. The OECD projected US inflation reaching 4.2% in 2026, up from 2.6% the prior year. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi experienced the most extreme single-market volatility of the period, falling approximately 18% across two sessions mid-month before a 10% rebound followed activation of a 100 trillion won stabilization program, with the market ultimately gaining roughly 5% late in the month on governance reform momentum.
  • China's National People's Congress set a 4.5%–5% GDP growth target — the lowest since 1991 — and finalized the 15th Five-Year Plan emphasizing AI, technological self-reliance, and household consumption, while February trade data released late in the month showed exports surging 21.9% year-on-year with a record $214 billion surplus.
  • Japan's monetary policy outlook became increasingly contested, with Prime Minister Takaichi nominating reflationist academics to BOJ board vacancies, the yen weakening to approximately 159.5 per dollar, and BOJ Governor Ueda citing Middle East uncertainty as materially clouding the timing of any further rate adjustment.
  • India's rupee fell to successive record lows against the dollar — breaching 93 and approaching projections of 95–96 by mid-year — as its energy import dependence intensified amid the Strait of Hormuz disruption and capital outflows accelerated.
  • Asian hardware and semiconductor names dominated global equity inflows in the first half of the month, with Taiwan Semiconductor up 30% year-to-date, Samsung up 70%, and SK Hynix up 57%, before late-month conflict-driven volatility partially reversed those gains.

China: Trade, Currency, and Strategic Positioning

China entered March as one of the clearer near-term beneficiaries of the Supreme Court's IEEPA ruling, with Morgan Stanley estimating average US levies on Chinese goods fell to 24% from 32%, and the Hang Seng China Enterprises index rallying 2.65% in the first week. The improved tariff arithmetic arrived ahead of the Trump-Xi summit — originally scheduled for March 31 before postponement to mid-May — materially altering the negotiating dynamic. The People's Bank of China moved in early March to temper yuan appreciation pressure by removing the 20% reserve requirement on foreign-currency forward contracts, effective March 2, reflecting concern that China's record $1.2 trillion trade surplus was generating appreciation pressure at odds with an economy still grappling with deflationary dynamics. Lunar New Year data offered a characteristically mixed signal: 596 million tourist trips with record total spending, but per-trip expenditure of 1,348 yuan fell marginally below the prior year — a consumer that is mobile but cautious. A record $112 billion discrepancy between Chinese reported export data and US Customs arrivals for 2025 suggested as much as a quarter of China-origin shipments may have bypassed US tariff controls. January–February industrial production released in the final week rose 6.3% year-on-year, the fastest pace since September, though the degree to which this momentum was sustained through the Hormuz disruption remains to be seen. FGE NexantECA indicated Beijing is close to drawing on its estimated 1.4 billion barrels of commercial oil reserves. State-owned refiners began quietly exploring purchases of Iranian crude following a limited US waiver, with Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, and Macquarie economists projecting Chinese producer prices could turn positive as early as March — potentially ending a deflationary streak dating to 2022, though whether such inflation reflects genuine demand recovery or purely cost-push dynamics compressing margins is uncertain.

China: Markets and the Five-Year Plan

The National People's Congress delivered the month's most consequential Asian policy signals. Premier Li Qiang's work report framed the reduced growth target as a deliberate quality-over-quantity pivot, and officials made their strongest commitment yet to eliminating deflationary conditions in 2026. The 15th Five-Year Plan doubled down on technology — AI, brain-computer interfaces, pharmaceutical innovation, and semiconductors — as the primary growth engine, while the plan's twenty main indicators were largely unchanged from the prior cycle, disappointing investors hoping for meaningful household consumption stimulus. Defense spending rose 7%, the slowest pace since 2022. Alibaba and Tencent combined lost approximately $66 billion in market value over roughly 24 hours in the final week after results from both companies disappointed investors seeking a clearer AI monetization path — Alibaba's quarterly net income falling 67%, its worst performance since early 2024. Nvidia confirmed it is ramping H200 production for Chinese customers following the administration's reversal on tech export controls. Chinese authorities moved to restrict state enterprises and government agencies from deploying OpenClaw AI on office computers, sending Minimax down 6.5% and Zhipu down 6.1% in Hong Kong on the relevant session, though both subsequently recovered on Jensen Huang's public endorsement of the platform.

Japan: Policy Tensions Mount

Japan's monetary policy framework faced sustained political pressure throughout the month. Reports that Prime Minister Takaichi had expressed apprehension over further BOJ rate increases — taking a tougher stance than at their November meeting — sent the yen down approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar in early March, reviving concerns that political resistance could constrain normalization. Her subsequent nomination of two reflationist academics to forthcoming BOJ board vacancies reinforced those concerns. Notably, former BOJ Governor Haruhiko Kuroda called publicly for rate hikes and warned the yen may be "somewhat too weak" — a striking inversion of his own legacy. By late March, the yen had weakened further to approximately 159.5 per dollar, amplifying Japan's import cost burden and raising stagflation concerns. Governor Ueda cited Middle East uncertainty as materially clouding timing of any further adjustment at the March meeting, with more than a third of surveyed economists nonetheless expecting an April rate hike. On corporate governance, Toyota's landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over Toyota Industries — at ¥20,600 per share, valuing the deal at approximately ¥6.7 trillion — and Toyota's preparation to unwind approximately ¥3 trillion in cross-shareholdings with banks represented meaningful steps in Japan's multi-year capital efficiency effort. Japan launched its largest emergency oil stockpile release on record and, separately, Japan and South Korea appear to be consolidating a pragmatic rapprochement, with Korean favorability toward Japan surpassing 56% and finance ministers agreeing to expand bilateral cooperation.

South Korea, Southeast Asia, and India

South Korea's Kospi endured the month's most extreme single-market episode. The index — which had risen roughly 50% year-to-date on AI optimism and active political encouragement from President Lee Jae Myung — suffered a two-day collapse of approximately 18% in mid-month, with the 12% single-session decline the largest in the index's history. Samsung fell 12%, SK Hynix roughly 10%, and Hyundai Motor 16% at their respective lows. Bank of Korea Governor Rhee abandoned scheduled travel to convene an emergency meeting; President Lee called an extraordinary cabinet session from Manila. The subsequent 10% Thursday rebound following invocation of the stabilization fund illustrated the market's extreme sensitivity to policy signals. Late in the month, the Kospi surged 5% after authorities moved to restrict listed companies from listing certain subsidiaries — a governance reform that drove Samsung up 7%. South Korea's won traded near its lowest level since 2009 through much of the period, and the reported redeployment of US air defense assets from the Korean Peninsula toward the Middle East added a security dimension to the economic stress. Southeast Asian economies moved to emergency footing: Thailand ordered civil servants to work from home full-time, the Philippines declared a national energy emergency with President Marcos warning that grounding domestic aviation remains a "distinct possibility," and Vietnam suspended select domestic airline routes. India's rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to record lows, with Nomura projecting 96 by end-June. Washington's one-month sanctions waiver allowing India to resume Russian crude purchases provided material relief — Indian refiners immediately absorbed available Urals spot cargoes — while Prime Minister Modi secured safe passage for two LPG tankers through Hormuz and engaged Trump directly on Strait access, illustrating New Delhi's characteristic approach of managing energy vulnerability through simultaneous engagement with multiple parties. ---

Europe

  • European equities outperformed US counterparts decisively in the first half of the month, with the Stoxx Europe 600 closing February with its eighth consecutive monthly gain — the longest winning streak since 2013 — before conflict-driven energy inflation reversed the narrative sharply in the second half.
  • European natural gas futures surged more than 50% in a single week following Iranian drone strikes on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility, with benchmark prices ultimately more than doubling from pre-war levels; Goldman Sachs estimated a month-long Hormuz halt could cause European gas prices to more than double from already elevated starting points.
  • The ECB held at 2.0% in March but dropped language describing policy as being in a "good place," with traders fully pricing two 2026 rate hikes by month's end — a dramatic reversal from the easing trajectory that prevailed entering the month.
  • UK 10-year gilt yields briefly breached 5% mid-month — levels not seen since before the Global Financial Crisis — driven by Bank of England pricing pivoting from at least two cuts to at least two hikes, with ING and RSM UK estimating UK inflation could exceed double the 2% target if current energy price levels persist.
  • UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank, Unilever confirmed it is evaluating a separation of its food assets, and HSBC announced it is weighing cuts of approximately 20,000 roles — corporate restructuring activity that would have led the news cycle absent the geopolitical backdrop.

European Equity Outperformance and Subsequent Reversal

The defining market theme of early March was European equities' relative strength against a US backdrop weighed by AI disruption fears. The Stoxx Europe 600 entered the month having gained 3.7% in February, with European companies announcing a record €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date for a January-February period, according to Barclays data. Goldman Sachs promoted what it labeled a HALO basket — Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence, spanning utilities, miners, industrials, and luxury goods — which had outperformed capital-light peers by 35% since early 2025, arguing these capital-intensive sectors are structurally insulated from AI displacement. Germany provided some of the firmest early datapoints: factory orders jumped in December by the most in two years, and the Ifo business confidence survey rose more than expected, suggesting early benefits from Merz's defense and infrastructure investment program were beginning to materialize. That constructive narrative was substantially complicated as the month progressed. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 2.0% on the Thursday following Ras Laffan strikes; France's CAC ended March down 7.6% and Switzerland's SMI off 8.1%. The euro declined roughly 2% against the dollar across the conflict period, consistent with the terms-of-trade deterioration that characterized the 2022 energy crisis. FTSE 100 proved a notable exception, with Citigroup upgrading UK equities to overweight, citing the index's structural tilt toward energy, commodities, defence, and defensives as an effective geopolitical hedge.

ECB and Bank of England: Policy Dilemma

The ECB's policy trajectory shifted materially across the month. The March meeting held at 2.0% in line with consensus, but the removal of language describing policy as being in a "good place" was significant. By month's end, traders were fully pricing two 25-basis-point hikes in 2026 — a configuration that was essentially unthinkable entering March. Bundesbank President Nagel was explicit early in the month that the inflation impact of the Iran war outweighs the growth drag; Governing Council member Kazimir stated the war risks forcing a rate increase sooner than anticipated; and Governing Council member Vujcic described stagflation risk as increasing and the bank as needing to remain "very agile." ECB President Lagarde, addressing the ECB Watchers Conference, signaled the bank will not act until it has "sufficient information on the size and persistence" of the shock, while pledging not to allow inflation to become entrenched. Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Hatzius estimated that in a severely adverse oil scenario, euro-area headline inflation could rise nearly 3 percentage points over the next year — the largest projected impact among major economies. The Bank of England similarly held, but Governor Bailey's explicit statement that "the prior expectation of scope to cut has changed" drove sharp gilt repricing, with UK 10-year yields rising to their highest level since 2008. February borrowing data exceeded forecasts due to a surge in debt-interest costs. Tax receipts came in approximately £22 billion ahead of OBR forecasts in Chancellor Reeves's spring fiscal statement — a modest relief on issuance pressure, though insufficient to alter the broader sovereign stress narrative. The UK's Office for Budget Responsibility indicated that Labour's commitment to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035 would require approximately £40 billion in additional outlays.

Growth Deterioration

The macro backdrop deteriorated concretely across multiple economies through the month. The S&P Global Composite PMI for the eurozone fell to 50.5 in March from 51.9 in February — the slowest expansion since May 2025 — as energy-driven cost pressures and supply chain disruption weighed on activity. Input prices rose at the fastest pace since February 2023, and future output expectations posted their largest deterioration since Russia's 2022 invasion. Germany's composite reading declined more than expected; France recorded a third consecutive month of services contraction. UK businesses reported near-stagnation, with factory input costs rising at the fastest pace in more than three decades. Euro-area fourth-quarter GDP was simultaneously revised lower, with trade weighing on output. Italy is now preparing to cut its 2026 GDP forecast to as low as 0.5%, and the OECD assessed the UK as facing the largest output hit among G7 nations this year. Germany's chemical industry faces compounding pressure: energy costs predating the conflict had already forced plant closures, and the war materially intensified the strain, with European chemical producers estimated to pay approximately €3 billion more annually if the post-war gas price surge persists. The iTraxx Europe investment-grade index reached its highest level since May, and the Crossover index crossed 300 basis points for the first time since June.

Politics, Geopolitics, and Corporate News

The political landscape shifted notably across several countries. UK Prime Minister Starmer publicly distanced Britain from US "regime change from the skies," with Trump criticizing Britain for declining base access and a suspected drone attack on the UK's Cyprus base occurring hours after the announcement. Over 100 Labour MPs wrote to the Home Secretary urging an immigration policy rethink, and prediction markets assigned a 68% probability that Starmer exits office before year-end. The Green Party's win in the Gorton and Denton by-election — Labour third — illustrated the two-front pressure on the government. German Chancellor Merz traveled to Washington on trade and geopolitics, while his CDU dislodged the Social Democrats from power in Rhineland-Palatinate after 35 years, though it lost Baden-Württemberg to the incumbent Greens, leaving his political standing mixed. Trump threatened to cut off trade with Spain after Prime Minister Sánchez denied base access; Spanish equities fell to their lowest in nearly three months. In France, Socialist Emmanuel Grégoire won the Paris mayoral race, extending the left's hold on the capital. On the corporate side, HSBC's full-year pretax profit of $29.9 billion exceeded expectations; UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank; Unilever confirmed it is evaluating food asset separation with a Barclays valuation of approximately €28–31 billion; Pernod Ricard entered preliminary discussions to acquire Brown-Forman; and HSBC announced evaluation of approximately 20,000 role cuts in an AI-driven restructuring. Adidas fell 7.6% after its profit forecast missed on tariff and currency headwinds. ---

Global

  • The US-Israel military campaign against Iranian nuclear facilities, launched late in the final week of February, and the resulting Strait of Hormuz closure — effectively shutting down approximately 20% of global oil flows — was the dominant event shaping all markets, central banks, and geopolitics throughout March.
  • Brent crude surged from approximately $70 per barrel at month's open to above $118 intraday in the final week — a gain of roughly 63% since hostilities began — before partially retracing toward $108–$113 following tentative de-escalation signals.
  • The IEA announced a record emergency release of 400 million barrels from member-country stockpiles, its largest in the agency's history, with Japan separately releasing approximately 80 million barrels beginning mid-month.
  • Gold posted its worst weekly decline since 1983 in the final week — down approximately 10.5% — a counterintuitive move reflecting a dash for liquidity and dollar strength as real rate expectations surged; from its intraday peak above $5,000 per ounce to its trough, the metal declined approximately 27%.
  • Global debt reached $348 trillion at end-2025, with $29 trillion added in the year — the fastest accumulation since the pandemic — providing the fiscal backdrop against which sovereign governments are absorbing an oil shock they have limited room to cushion.

The Iran Conflict: Escalation, De-escalation Signals, and Endgame Uncertainty

The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike represents the most consequential geopolitical rupture since Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Iran's retaliation expanded to at least five countries across the month — missile strikes on US bases in Qatar, Bahrain, the UAE, and elsewhere; drone strikes on Gulf Arab energy infrastructure; and a US submarine sinking of the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka in the second week, the first American submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II. NATO air defenses downed a ballistic missile over Turkey. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field in the third week — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities in the conflict — and Iran responded by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City, causing extensive damage and sparking fires, with two LNG trains expected to remain offline for up to five years. By the final week, Prime Minister Netanyahu stated Israel would no longer target energy infrastructure and said the war would end faster than people expect, while Iran's foreign minister signaled that recent restraint was deliberate. The Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries, and a New York Times report of indirect Iranian-Washington contact prompted a temporary equity rebound. Whether these signals constitute a durable de-escalation or tactical repositioning remains genuinely uncertain. Prediction markets placed a 52% probability on the conflict extending beyond April 30 at month's end. Iran's ceasefire conditions — permanent non-aggression pledges from both the US and Israel — appear incompatible with either government's stated positions, leaving the diplomatic path narrow. Iran's new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, vowed to keep Hormuz effectively closed and threatened additional fronts if strikes continued, and Iran's parliament is reportedly drafting legislation to impose passage fees on Hormuz transit — an arrangement that would amount to de facto toll extraction on global shipping.

Energy Markets: Scale and Complexity

The IEA has characterized the cumulative supply disruption as equivalent to the combined effect of the two 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis — a framing delivered by its executive director in Canberra. Brent crude surged approximately 18% in the first full week of conflict, peaked above $118 intraday in the third week, and held in the $108–$113 range through the final week, on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990. US diesel futures gained more than 40% in a single week at peak; European natural gas rose more than 50% in one week and ultimately more than doubled from pre-war levels. Qatar's Ras Laffan facility, which produces roughly 20% of global LNG supply, sustained damage potentially taking years to repair. Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura refinery suspended operations following a drone strike; Iraq agreed to resume pipeline exports via Turkey, contributing to price relief in the final days. The binding near-term constraint on shipping proved to be the insurance market rather than an explicit blockade: more than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs withdrew war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels, causing commercial shipping to self-suspend. Only two commercial vessel transits were recorded in a 24-hour period at the closure's peak, with no oil shipments passing at all. US naval escort and insurance guarantees provided partial mitigation, though ING analysts noted escorts risk becoming targets and individual ship captains ultimately determine whether to sail. BloombergNEF's base case sees Brent averaging $106.70 in April; a delayed Hormuz reopening until mid-June would push that to $154.40; Macquarie warned of a path toward $200 in a prolonged closure scenario. Dallas Fed modeling suggests a closure lasting through June would reduce global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points in the next quarter. Paradoxically, Iran appears a net financial beneficiary, reportedly earning hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental oil revenue as the only major exporter able to utilize the strait freely.

Global Monetary Policy: A Collective Pivot

The macro implications of the oil shock are particularly uncomfortable for central banks globally that had been in or approaching easing cycles. A sustained $20-per-barrel shock could add up to one percentage point to global inflation according to Société Générale; the IMF's Georgieva estimated a sustained 10% energy price increase would push global inflation 40 basis points higher and shave 0.1–0.2% from growth. The week of the most concentrated central bank calendar — covering approximately two-thirds of the global economy across 18 policy meetings — produced a historically unusual outcome: the Reserve Bank of Australia became the first major central bank to hike in response to the energy impulse; the Fed, ECB, and Bank of England all held but shifted tone materially toward caution. By month's end, traders had unwound rate-cut expectations across the US, UK, Turkey, and Hungary simultaneously, and the cumulative repricing — from an easing trajectory to a prospective tightening one within the space of three weeks — represents one of the more significant collective monetary policy pivots of the current cycle. Sovereign bonds sold off alongside equities, leaving the dollar as the dominant haven vehicle. Bloomberg Economics estimates March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections. The OECD, in its first formal revision, left its 2026 global growth projection unchanged at 2.9% while trimming 2027 by 0.1 percentage point and raising G20 average inflation to 4.0% from 2.8% in December — a forecast that likely understates the left-tail risk given the conflict's unresolved trajectory.

Commodity Contagion and Alliance Fractures

The conflict's second-order commodity effects accelerated through the month. Urea prices rose 25% since hostilities began, reaching levels last seen during Russia's Ukraine invasion, as Iran accounts for approximately 5% and 11% of global urea and ammonia trade respectively. Aluminum briefly hit a near four-year high. Coal prices reached their highest level in over a year on gas substitution effects. Over 46,000 flights were canceled in the conflict's third week alone. Economy-class round-trip fares from Sydney to London increased more than 80% through the month. Gold's collapse — down roughly 10.5% in its worst weekly decline since 1983, with Turkey selling approximately $8 billion in reserves to defend its currency — represents a significant dislocation in a traditional safe-haven asset. Reports that Iran is permitting Hormuz passage for vessels paying in yuan prompted Deutsche Bank to flag the conflict as a potential catalyst for petrodollar erosion. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on pace for its largest monthly gain since July at approximately 2%. Alliance management has been a persistent source of friction: the UK, Japan, Germany, and South Korea each declined to participate militarily; Trump publicly abandoned the request for allied warships after being rebuffed; and China's state media were openly dismissive of US appeals. Russia has emerged as a clear early economic beneficiary, with elevated energy revenues and the discount on Urals crude versus Brent nearly eliminated. Environmental consequences are also accumulating: Israeli drone strikes on refineries near Tehran triggered toxic black rain over a metropolitan area of approximately 18.5 million people, and analysts have identified over 300 incidents carrying environmental risk. The crisis is simultaneously accelerating structural energy transition dynamics — EV queues in the Philippines, rooftop solar uptake in India — in ways that may prove durable well beyond the conflict's resolution.
