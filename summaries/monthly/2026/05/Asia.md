• South Korea's Kospi surged approximately 73% year-to-date through May, driven almost entirely by Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix — both crossing $1 trillion in market capitalization — while Taiwan revised its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 9.6% from 7.7%, citing AI-driven semiconductor demand.
• China's April activity data broadly missed consensus: retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year (weakest since December 2022), industrial production grew 4.1% (weakest in nearly three years), and fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% year-to-date, reigniting the domestic stimulus debate.
• The Bank of Japan held rates at 0.75% in its most divided vote under Governor Ueda — 6-3 — with a June hike as the base case; Japan intervened in currency markets, deploying an estimated $30 billion to defend the yen near ¥160.
• The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a third consecutive rate hike in an 8-1 vote, unwinding all of last year's easing, leading developed-market peers in the hawkish response to the energy shock.
• Huawei unveiled its "LogicFolding" chip architecture, claiming 1.4nm-equivalent performance by 2031 without EUV lithography — a direct challenge to the effectiveness of US export controls and a development that sent SMIC and Hua Hong shares sharply higher.

**Semiconductors and the AI Super-Cycle**

Asian equity markets produced their most extraordinary performance in recent memory, with South Korea and Taiwan at the epicenter of a global AI infrastructure repricing. The Kospi posted a 24% monthly gain in May alone — its strongest monthly performance in recent memory — with Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix together crossing $1 trillion market capitalizations and the two names accounting for roughly 42% of Kospi capitalization, up from 28% two years earlier. Samsung reported a 755% year-on-year profit increase in the March quarter; SK Hynix controls 57% of the high-bandwidth memory market, a component for which demand is expected to outstrip supply through at least 2027. Korean chip exports for the first twenty days of May rose 202% year-on-year, and Goldman Sachs raised its Kospi target for the fourth time in the year, projecting an additional 20% gain with forecast earnings growth of 300% for 2026 — a characterization Morgan Stanley's chief Asia economist situated within a multi-year industrial super-cycle spanning AI infrastructure, energy transition, and defense spending.

Taiwan's revision of its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 9.6% — above even the most optimistic pre-year estimate — reflects an island economy that has become the physical backbone of global AI buildout. Taiwan's equity market overtook India in total market capitalization during the final week of May, driven primarily by TSMC. Taiwan's tech firms are borrowing a record $14.5 billion to fund expansion. The concentration of gains, however, introduces tail risk: some funds were forced to sell positions that breached internal concentration limits, and South Korea's retail margin debt reached a record ₩36.5 trillion ($24 billion) at its peak. An episode early in May illustrated the sensitivity: the Kospi shed more than $300 billion in market capitalization within 97 minutes after a presidential policy aide floated an AI-profit tax via social media, before partially recovering on a clarification. The index fell a further 6.1% in a single session later in May as elevated rate expectations repriced stretched valuations. Samsung averted a threatened 18-day general strike late in May by reaching an agreement with its largest union providing AI-linked bonuses averaging approximately $340,000 per chip employee — a settlement that resolves near-term production risk but sets a precedent for future labor negotiations in an industry generating extraordinary profits.

Huawei's disclosure of its LogicFolding architecture at a chip conference in China was the month's most consequential technology-policy development. Presented by semiconductor chief He Tingbo, the approach seeks to improve chip performance by shortening internal data-transmission distances rather than shrinking transistors — explicitly designed to circumvent China's inability to procure ASML's EUV machines. The company targets 1.4nm-equivalent production by 2031, roughly three years behind TSMC's current roadmap. Material engineering hurdles remain around heat dissipation and manufacturing scale, but the market reaction was immediate and the strategic implication — that export controls may be accelerating Chinese semiconductor self-sufficiency rather than halting it — has become harder to dismiss.

**China: Growth Disappointment, Governance, and Diplomacy**

China's economic trajectory through May presented a persistent gap between external strength and domestic weakness. April PMI data showed manufacturing holding in expansion territory, supported by resilient export demand, while services and construction slipped into contraction. April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above the 8% consensus, with technology the primary driver. Yet April activity data released in the final week constituted a broad miss: retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year, with auto sales down 15% and jewelry off 21%; industrial production grew 4.1%, the weakest in nearly three years; and fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% year-to-date — a result no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated. The Politburo pledged to counter external shocks and cited better-than-expected first-quarter performance; Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable. Nonetheless, independent analysts estimate true non-performing loan ratios at approximately 10% of total credit against an official 1.5% — implying roughly $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt — with regulatory forbearance slowing loan growth and trapping capital in unproductive enterprises. Domestic 10-year bond yields remain below 2%, now trading below Japanese government bond yields — a striking inversion that reflects the dichotomy between external competitiveness and subdued domestic demand. Chinese crude imports fell 20% year-on-year in April as state refiners drew on commercial inventories, with approximately two-fifths of China's oil imports historically transiting Hormuz.

The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing in the third week of May was the geopolitical centerpiece of the month. China renewed import licenses for US beef plants as an early gesture, and Treasury Secretary Bessent outlined a potential investment board and tariff relief on roughly $30 billion in non-critical trade. The summit produced at least $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchase commitments through 2028 and renewed trade truce language. Yet the "placeholder summit" characterization resonated: Xi's Taiwan warning — that mishandling could push the relationship into a "highly dangerous situation," published by Chinese state media while the leaders were still in the room — was the summit's sharpest moment, and no operational Iran mechanism emerged. Trump subsequently confirmed China has not approved Nvidia H200 purchases and intends to develop domestic alternatives, a reminder that semiconductor competition underlying the summit's cordiality remains unresolved.

Beijing's governance posture throughout May signaled escalating economic statecraft. The Meta-Manus AI acquisition was ordered unwound weeks after completion, with Manus co-founders barred from leaving the country and several private firms instructed via the NDRC to reject US-origin capital absent explicit approval. China's new supply chain coercion regulations — characterized by Rhodium Group as a shift from reactive to proactive economic statecraft — allow Beijing to retaliate against foreign governments or firms assisting actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial security. Tax authorities launched a crackdown on circular invoicing in metals trading, rattling copper, aluminum, and silver traders in Shanghai who rely on such invoices for bank financing. Beijing's crackdown on cross-border equity trading was the most forceful in recent years, costing Futu Holdings founder Leaf Li an estimated $1.7 billion in a single session; China also began restricting overseas travel for top AI professionals at firms including Alibaba and DeepSeek. The yuan nonetheless ended May on course for a sixth consecutive quarterly gain against the dollar — its longest winning streak since 2013.

**Japan, Southeast Asia, and Regional Policy Divergence**

Japan's monetary and fiscal positions evolved in tension through the month. The BOJ's April meeting produced a 6-3 hawkish hold, with three board members voting for an immediate hike and a June move widely anticipated. Spring Shunto wage negotiations delivered a third consecutive year of approximately 5% increases, and five-year inflation breakevens reached 2.7%, suggesting bond markets believe the deflationary era is genuinely over. Japan intervened in currency markets — deploying an estimated $30 billion — pulling the yen from above ¥160 toward ¥155–157, but analysts broadly characterized the effort as increasingly ineffective level-targeting absent a BOJ rate hike commitment. Japan's structural dependence on imported energy, made substantially more expensive by Hormuz, continues to exert downward pressure on the currency through the current account. The structural tension is reflected in JGB markets: 20-year yields touched their highest level since 1997, and Prime Minister Takaichi reversed course and instructed the finance ministry to compile a supplementary budget of roughly $19 billion including revived energy subsidies — a move that pushed 30-year JGB yields to fresh post-1999 highs. Japan's Q1 GDP expanded an annualized 2.1%, accelerating from a revised 0.8%, but household spending fell for a fourth consecutive month. Toyota issued a notably cautious earnings outlook, projecting ¥3 trillion in operating income for fiscal 2027 against a ¥4.6 trillion consensus, citing raw material cost increases from the Iran conflict.

In Southeast Asia, Indonesia faced simultaneous currency and political stress: the rupiah hit a record low, Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike to 5.25% — double consensus — and President Prabowo announced state centralization of palm oil, coal, and nickel exports through Danantara, a move that blindsided senior officials and pushed the Jakarta Composite's year-to-date decline to roughly 26%, the worst among major global benchmarks. A Moody's warning on centralization risks followed promptly. India's pressures compounded through May: every one of the world's 50 hottest cities was located in India at peak, with temperatures exceeding 40°C and McKinsey estimating heat-related productivity losses of $250 billion. The rupee touched nearly 97 per dollar, and the RBI is reportedly weighing its 2013 taper-tantrum toolkit. India signed a free trade agreement with New Zealand, following earlier deals with the EU and UK — a structural departure from the protectionist posture that saw average tariff rates rise from approximately 13% to 18% between 2017 and 2020 — while Sun Pharmaceutical's approximately $11.75 billion acquisition of Organon represents one of the largest outbound acquisitions in Indian corporate history.
