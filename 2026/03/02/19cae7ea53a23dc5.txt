Stress Test

Back in the 1950s, advertisers came up with a jingle for the Timex American 
watch brand: “Ittakes a licking 
<https://www.aaaa.org/blog/timeline-event/torture-test-timex-proves-takes-licking-sixty-years/>
 and keeps on ticking.” A Timex was seen going through dramatic tests yet still 
continuing to work. The global economy has gone through something similar the 
past several years.

After coming through Covid, the Russian invasion of Ukraine and dramatic US 
tariff hikes, and while confronting a broaderunravelling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-20/carney-stands-by-greenland-urges-allies-to-unite-against-coercion?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 of the previous world order, global economic prospects as of last month 
looked, if anything, remarkably decent. Citigroup economists even titled their 
latest outlook, “More Resilient Than Ever.”

After a 3% expansion last year, the Citi team led by Nathan Sheets predicted 
“similarly solid growth continuing over the next two years,” at 2.9% this year 
and 2.8% next. Among the risks the group cited was geopolitical stress in the 
Middle East. And sure enough, that’s nowexploded into reality 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/trump-pushes-for-iran-leadership-change-as-tehran-stays-defiant?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 with the weekend US and Israeli strikes on Iran, and Tehran’s retaliatory 
attacks.

The most obvious channel of transmission 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/snb-says-increasingly-prepared-to-intervene-in-fx-markets?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 from the conflict to the world economy will be the energy markets. TD 
Securities strategists highlight that “strikes have been reported in nations 
that account for more than one-third of global seaborne crude flows and the 
overwhelming majority of global spare capacity.”

Read more: Economists Gauge Hit From Mideast War 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/economists-gauge-hit-from-mideast-war-as-china-seen-among-losers?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>

As of early Monday New York time, oil surged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-2?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 the most in four years as tanker traffic all but halted through theStrait of 
Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-28/can-iran-close-the-strait-of-hormuz-oil-market-impact-explained?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 and abig refinery 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/saudi-arabia-s-ras-tanura-refinery-shuts-down-after-drone-attack?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 in Saudi Arabia stopped. Analysts at Bloomberg Economics led byDina Esfandiary 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/AV0Rw29Q1B0/dina-esfandiary> warned of a 
potential surge to as high as$108 a barrel <>.

Given the existential nature of the fight for the Islamic republic, this Iran 
conflict seems likely to last longer than last year’s days-long exchange of 
fire over Tehran’s nuclear program — suggesting a longer-lasting uplift to 
energy costs.

But, luckily for the northern hemisphere, spring has just begun, with heating 
demand set to drop. Developed-nation inflation rates are alsoa fraction 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/german-inflation-eases-to-2-as-economic-recovery-starts-slowly?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 of what they were in 2022, when much of Russian supply exited global markets.

And indeed economics could potentially put a constraint on the parameters of 
the conflict itself. Ajay Rajadhyaksha, global chair of research at Barclays, 
listed in a weekend note some “strong reasons for the situation to 
de-escalate.” One: “Iran’s economy is already in desperate shape – withmassive 
inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-10/why-inflation-may-be-what-brings-change-to-iran-new-economy?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, and scarcity of medicines and essential goods.”

As for the US, while price increases aren’t what they were years ago, 
Americans remain concerned about theaffordability 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-cost-of-living/?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 of living costs, something the Trump administration may have to take into 
account as the clock ticks down to November congressional elections.

Still, “the tail risk of a much wider conflagration is higher than it has been 
in the last several years,” said Rajadhyaksha.

In other words, it’s far too soon to say with confidence how the global 
economy will emerge from its latest Timex-like stress test.

The Week Ahead

US payrolls growth probably settled back in February after the strongest month 
of hiring in a year, returning to a more moderate and sustainable pace of 
hiring.

Economists project 60,000 jobs were added for the month — less than half the 
number created at the start of the year, according the median of a Bloomberg 
survey ahead of Friday’s report. The jobless rate is seen holding at 4.3%.

Need-to-Know Research

Before the by-now-famous Citrini Research paper posited that artificial 
intelligence would triggerwidespread unemployment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/citrini-s-dystopian-ai-vision-draws-global-investor-criticism?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, a National Bureau of Economic Research paper gauged the adaptability of 
different segments of the job market to AI.

Sam Manning and Tomás Aguirre at the Centre for the Governance of AI 
constructed an “adaptive capacity index” to assess the potential ability of 
workers to navigate job transitions if displaced. Theiranalysis 
<http://www.nber.org/papers/w34705> incorporates estimates of “net liquid 
wealth, skill transferability, geographic density and age,” aiming to focus on 
factors that may influence the ability to find new jobs.
Source: Sam J. Manning, Tomás Aguirre, Centre for the Governance of AI
The good news: US workers most exposed to AI are better positioned to deal 
with job transitions. The bad: While just 4.2% of the workforce in the 
economists’ sample, there were some 6.1 million workers who were in the top 
quartile of AI exposure, but the bottom quartile of adaptive capacity. 
Concentrated inclerical 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/mizuho-plans-to-replace-5-000-clerical-jobs-with-ai-in-10-years?cmpid=BBD030226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, administrative support and assistance roles, these people “may struggle most 
to transition to comparable new job opportunities.”
