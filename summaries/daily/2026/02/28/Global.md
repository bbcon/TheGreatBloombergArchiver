Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iran represent the most consequential geopolitical development in recent memory for global energy markets and supply chain stability. Iran produces approximately 5% of global oil supplies, and roughly one-fifth of global oil flows transit the Strait of Hormuz. Tehran responded rapidly by launching missiles at US military bases in Qatar, Bahrain, and the UAE, closing Iranian airspace, and prompting Gulf states and Israel to do the same. Emirates suspended all Dubai operations. Bloomberg Economics estimates that a Hormuz closure could push oil to $108 per barrel from levels already up nearly 20% year-to-date on US-Iran tensions. OPEC+ is reportedly considering a larger-than-expected supply increase when key members meet Sunday, which may represent a partial offset, though the group's capacity to compensate for Hormuz disruption is limited by logistics rather than production.

Risk assets responded defensively. Bitcoin fell as much as 3.8% to $63,038 and Ether declined 4.5% to $1,835, erasing approximately $128 billion in digital asset market value in the immediate aftermath. The broader implication for global monetary policy divergence is that a sustained energy price shock complicates the easing cycles underway or anticipated across multiple central banks, including the ECB and several Asian central banks, by reintroducing an inflationary impulse that policymakers had largely treated as resolved.

The dollar's reserve currency status appears further entrenched by the episode, at least in the near term. Despite sustained "sell America" rhetoric, global investors continue to accumulate dollar-denominated assets, and the structural barriers facing both the euro and yuan as alternatives — capital controls, market fragmentation, and inadequate liquidity depth — remain unresolved. As US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has noted, reserve currency status carries the burden of currency appreciation that neither Beijing nor Brussels has demonstrated willingness to fully absorb.
