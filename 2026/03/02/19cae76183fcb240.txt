Trump Ditches Economic Statecraft for the Power Politics of War
With his attack on Iran, Trump signals a shift away from the era of economic 
statecraft

<https://sli.bloomberg.com/click?s=1149074&li=17102240&m=11b724c487f61753eb1a7a4a5f3ef261&p=03022026&lctg=6689f0ec-2ca1-4687-9972-bdcb0c2dfbdf&stpe=default>
With the launch of his war against Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/trump-pushes-for-iran-leadership-change-as-tehran-stays-defiant?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 over the weekend, President Donald Trump reaffirmed that he was taking the 
world into a new era of power politics. He also, though, looks to be closing a 
chapter on another — the era of economic statecraft.

For a large part of the past decade, the world of security and economics has 
been focused on the use of economic tools in geopolitics.

Trump set off the trend with his first-term trade wars, which signaled his 
disdain at least for the postwar US-led order. It gained salience through the 
pandemic with its supply chain disruptions. The Biden administration embraced 
the movement with sanctions on Russia following its invasion of Ukraine and 
with export controls aimed at keeping China behind in the AI race.

In his first year back in office, Trump doubled down on his embrace of 
economic statecraft. He imposed the biggest package of US tariffs seen in 
almost a century and boasted that they gave him vast power over the global 
economy and even the ability to end wars.

Investment Pledges

Trump’s new wave of economic arm-twisting drew some successes on paper, like 
the trillions of dollars in pledged investments from trading partners likeJapan 
<bbg://news/stories/TB4038KJH6V5>, South Korea, the United Arab Emirates and 
Qatar.

And yet Trump also started to encounter the limits of his economic tools. A 
threat to impose tariffs on any country buying Iranian oil never really went 
anywhere. Tariffs aimed at getting India to stop buying Russian crude led to 
the broad outlines of anincomplete trade pact 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/india-postpones-us-trip-to-finalize-trade-pact-on-tariff-ruling?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 between New Delhi and Washington.

They also, however, hastened the closure of a more substantive deal between 
India and the European Union.Threats of tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/here-s-how-governments-are-reacting-after-trump-s-tariff-changes?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 on European nations resisting Trump’s plans for Greenland meanwhile only led 
to a defiant response and a Davos climbdown.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-2?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>

It probably didn’t help that the Supreme Court last month declared Trump’s 
invocation of emergency powers to impose his tariffs on any country he chose 
illegal and therefore defanged hisfavorite economic weapon 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-20/how-trump-will-change-us-trade-policy-after-supreme-court-ruling?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, or at the very least made its use more complicated.

The first signal that Trump was losing patience with economic statecraft came 
weeks before that Supreme Court decision with the January incursion he ordered 
into Venezuela, the arrest of leader Nicolas Maduro, and thede-facto seizure <> 
of the country’s oil wealth. One daring nighttime raid did what years of 
sanctions couldn’t. It validated the use of a different and more immediately 
potent weapon — military power.

Regime Change

But the biggest signal of Trump’s change in approach has come with the weekend 
attacks on Iran and the death of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei and other top figures 
in the Islamic republic.

As Peter Harrell, who oversaw the international economics portfolio on 
President Joe Biden’s National Security Council,put it 
<https://x.com/petereharrell/status/2027921416897634336?s=20> over the weekend: 
“2025 was the year of Trump using US economic power to upend the global trading 
order. 2026 is shaping up as the year of Trump using US military power to upend 
the global security order.”

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/economists-gauge-hit-from-mideast-war-as-china-seen-among-losers?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>

We shall see what comes next for Trump’s economic tools. His tariff policy now 
hangs largely on a 10% global tariff that will expire in July unless Congress 
improbably extends it. Even his threat to raise it to 15%seems fragile 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/us-to-keep-china-tariffs-steady-avoid-escalation-greer-says?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
. What comes after that is likely to be a protectionist quilt of slower-moving 
tariffs, each of which comes with the sort of technocratic investigations, 
public consultations and bureaucracy attached that Trump is likely to bristle 
at.

Then again, Trump and his aides also seem to have found domestic targets for 
his economic tools. The move on Friday to label leading AI lab Anthropic a
supply chain risk 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/trump-orders-us-government-to-drop-anthropic-after-pentagon-feud?cmpid=BBD030226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 and effectively ban any US company doing business with the US government from 
having a relationship with it indicates Trump may be willing to use tools of 
economic warfare meant for foreign adversaries against those who defy him at 
home.

But that would mark a whole other era in itself. And it’s hard after this past 
weekend not to come to at least a preliminary conclusion that Trump has latched 
on to a more powerful way to upend the global order and that therefore an era 
of economic statecraft shaping the world has come and gone.

We’ve learned over the past few years how American-led trade wars can disrupt 
the world economy. We’re about to find out just what damage will come from a 
real war with the US and its president as its leading protagonists.
