**China: Resilience and Risk**

China's January–February economic data released Monday surprised to the upside, with industrial production rising 6.3% year-on-year — the fastest pace since September — and retail sales and fixed-asset investment both exceeding forecasts. The data offered an encouraging snapshot of domestic momentum building before the Iran conflict's full weight could register. By midweek, however, the structural risk was becoming clearer: Jefferies' Christopher Wood argued that Chinese mainland equities remain among the most defensible in the current environment, citing robust domestic renewable capacity, battery storage, and reserve buffers as relative insulation. FGE NexantECA indicated Beijing is close to drawing on its estimated 1.4 billion barrels of commercial oil reserves as the Strait of Hormuz disruption persists. Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, and Macquarie economists projected Chinese producer prices could turn positive as soon as March, potentially ending a deflationary streak dating to 2022 — though the key uncertainty is whether such inflation reflects genuine demand recovery or purely cost-push dynamics that could compress margins further.

**China: Markets and Geopolitics**

Alibaba and Tencent combined lost approximately $66 billion in market value over roughly 24 hours on Friday after results from both companies disappointed investors seeking a clearer path to AI monetization. Alibaba's quarterly net income fell 67%, its worst performance since early 2024, partly reflecting heavy promotional spending. Alibaba separately raised AI computing and storage prices by as much as 34%, reflecting surging demand. The AI theme drove significant moves in Hong Kong, with LLM providers MiniMax and Zhipu gaining approximately 20% and 19% respectively following Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang's endorsement of OpenClaw, an agentic AI platform. Nvidia confirmed it is ramping production of H200 AI accelerators for Chinese customers following the Trump administration's reversal on tech export controls. The Trump–Xi summit, originally planned for late March, was postponed to approximately mid-May, with Beijing appearing quietly relieved at the delay. The trade truce remains intact, but a pending $14 billion US arms package for Taiwan represents a significant latent irritant.

**Japan and South Korea**

Japan's Bank of Japan kept its policy rate unchanged, with Governor Ueda citing Middle East uncertainty as materially clouding the timing of any further adjustment, though the conditional tightening bias was maintained. Prime Minister Takaichi characterized her White House meeting with Trump as "extremely difficult," with Japan's pacifist constitution foreclosing warship contributions to Hormuz operations. February export data showed shipments to the US falling 8% year-on-year and car exports by value down 14.8%, though overall exports beat expectations at +4.2%. Japan's petrochemical sector faces specific vulnerability from tightening naphtha supplies, with multiple firms announcing production cuts. On a more constructive note, Japan and South Korea appear to be consolidating a pragmatic rapprochement driven by convergent external pressures — Chinese economic coercion, the China-Russia-North Korea military alignment, and Washington's transactional posture. Korean favorability toward Japan recently surpassed 56%, described as a record high, while finance ministers from both countries agreed to expand bilateral cooperation including a review of their currency swap arrangement.

**Southeast Asia and India**

South Korea's Kospi surged 5% on Wednesday after authorities moved to restrict publicly listed companies from listing certain subsidiaries, a governance reform that drove Samsung Electronics up 7% after the company signaled a potential shift toward multi-year memory chip contracts. India's rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to a record low, with Nomura projecting the currency could reach 96 per dollar by end-June and Goldman Sachs projecting it could reach 95 over the next year. India imports approximately 90% of its oil, and Prime Minister Modi secured safe passage for two LPG tankers through Hormuz while simultaneously managing US relations — a difficult bilateral balance with state elections in April approaching. Indonesia's central bank delivered a hawkish hold while tightening foreign-exchange regulations to defend the rupiah, which is down approximately 14% in March. Pakistan's airstrikes on Kabul, which the Taliban government says killed at least 400 people at a drug treatment hospital, represent a serious separate escalation risk in the region that attracted alarm from the EU and China alike.
