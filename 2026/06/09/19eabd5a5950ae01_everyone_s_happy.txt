Xi Jinping and Kim Jong Un used two days of pomp and pageantry in Pyongyang to 
project a united front — and both appear to have walked away withwhat they 
wanted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-08/xi-vows-deeper-ties-with-kim-while-again-avoiding-nuclear-issue?cmpid=BBD060926_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5328>
.

For Xi, Kim calling ties with China “the most important top priority” 
reaffirmed Beijing’s role as North Korea’s principal backer, even though a 
deepening embrace of Moscow has given Kim more room to maneuver.

For Kim, Xi’s public silence on North Korea’s rapidly expanding atomic arsenal 
offered something just as valuable: another sign that Beijing is edging toward 
tacit acceptance of its neighbor’s nuclear status.

The visit was Xi’s first to North Korea in seven years, and much has changed 
in the interim.
Xi and Kim inspect an honor guard in Pyongyang yesterday.Photographer: Ding 
Lin/Xinhua/AP Photo
Kim has doubled down on his nuclear ambitions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-06/north-korea-s-kim-says-he-sees-nuclear-deterrent-role-for-navy?cmpid=BBD060926_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5328>
 and made clear he has no intention of scaling them back. Pyongyang has 
meanwhile signed a mutual defense pact with Russia and sent troops and 
ammunition to support Moscow’s war on Ukraine in exchange for military 
technology and economic assistance.

That gave Kim diplomatic leverage as he rolled out the red carpet for Xi.

The pair vowed to expand trade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-07/xi-visits-north-korea-to-reassert-influence-over-emboldened-kim?cmpid=BBD060926_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5328>
, tourism and technology cooperation, plus diplomatic and military exchanges.

The readout made no mention of eliminating nuclear weapons on the Korean 
Peninsula — a goal China publicly upheld for years but which has been missing 
from official statements since Kim’s visit to Beijing in September.

The omission may reflect Beijing’s assessment that meaningful progress toward 
denuclearizationis no longer realistic 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-04/kim-jong-un-tours-new-nuclear-site-says-output-capacity-doubled?cmpid=BBD060926_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5328>
, and that reasserting Chinese influence now matters more.

Today, Xi and Kim, joined by their wives, visited a friendship monument 
honoring Chinese soldiers who died fighting alongside North Korean troops in 
the 1950-53 Korean War.

The symbolism was hard to miss: where North Korean troops are now dying for 
Russia, Chinese soldiers once made the same sacrifice for Pyongyang.

research funding.

Japan is undergoing the biggest budgetary-reform process 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/katayama-says-japan-undergoing-biggest-budget-reform-since-1945?cmpid=BBD060926_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5328>

spending needs. Scrapping them would be a major change for Tokyo, and it would 
remove one of the easiest targets for opposition parties and skeptical 
investors who claim the government is too loose with fiscal policy.
