A three-day hearing hosted by the US Trade Representative kicks off on 
Tuesday, bringing together industries and government officials trying to plead 
their case for or againstmore tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/trump-builds-a-new-us-tariff-wall-in-shakeup-of-winners-losers?cmpid=BBD070726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6189>
 from the Trump administration.

Washington launched an investigation into the forced-labor prohibition 
policies of 60 countries in March, under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974. 
USTRdetermined 
<https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/june/ustr-makes-findings-and-proposes-action-60-section-301-investigations-relating-failures-take-action>
 in early June that each of them failed to impose and enforce bans against 
imports made with forced labor. Goods from those countries face an additional 
10% or 12.5% duty.

As part of the process, dozens of stakeholders will make their case to US 
trade officials starting today through Thursday, Bloomberg Newsreports today 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/us-holds-hearing-on-trump-tariffs-targeting-60-countries-over-forced-labor?cmpid=BBD070726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6189>
. White House advisers have already made it clear that they plan to use the 
forced labor and other investigations to restore import taxes to levels in 
place under TrumpтАЩs emergency tariffs.

Meanwhile, the administration has a temporary 10% global tariff in place that 
expires later this month.

Separately on Tuesday, USTR starts day two of a public hearing regarding 
another Section 301 investigation тАФ this one involving Brazil, with a focus on 
digital commerce, electronic payments, ethanol, intellectual property, 
preferential tariffs, as well as combating both corruption and illegal 
deforestation.
