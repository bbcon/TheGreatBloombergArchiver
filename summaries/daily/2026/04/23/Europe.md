The eurozone economy unexpectedly contracted in April for the first time since late 2024, according to preliminary PMI data, driven by a steep decline in the services sector as the Iran war weighs on consumer confidence and energy costs. The data compounds pressure on the ECB as it navigates a difficult trade-off: energy-driven inflation is running higher — UK CPI accelerated to 3.3% in March on a 9% monthly surge in motor fuel prices, the largest since Russia's invasion of Ukraine — while growth risks are deteriorating. Analysts at TS Lombard have raised the risk of a "Trichet 2.0" scenario, in which the ECB tightens into a weakening economy, noting that China's exports and a relatively firm euro are already suppressing core goods inflation, and that household and corporate financial buffers are thinner than in 2022.

European leaders gathered in Cyprus to discuss coordinated responses to the energy shock, while the ECB separately advanced the case for joint regional bonds. In Brussels, officials unveiled a plan to accelerate economic electrification. The parallel tracks of short-term crisis management and longer-term structural reform appear to be gaining political momentum, though execution timelines remain uncertain.

UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves received a near-term fiscal reprieve as government borrowing fell to its lowest level in three years — though analysts caution the Iran war's inflationary pass-through may prove short-lived in budgetary terms. Prime Minister Starmer faced mounting political pressure over the Peter Mandelson affair, with London Mayor Sadiq Khan offering qualified support while arguing that political stability remains a UK competitive asset. The UK and France separately concluded a £660 million deal targeting cross-Channel migration flows. UBS faces a potential $20 billion increase in capital requirements as a Swiss regulatory split widens, representing a material headwind for the bank's return targets.

Trump's executive action requiring pharmaceutical companies to price US drugs at parity with other wealthy nations is creating a difficult strategic calculation for European patients: companies including Biogen and Roche face the choice of raising European prices, cutting US prices, or bypassing select European markets — with tangible implications for patient access to recently approved therapies.
