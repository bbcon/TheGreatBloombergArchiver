 Risk exodus 



All across Wall Street, the headlong rush into stocks, precious metals and 
crypto has given way to asudden retreat from risk <https://bloom.bg/4kiJ4CN>.

Rather than a single trigger, anxiety about valuations has been stoked by a 
steady drumbeat of negative news. Big tech companies are embarking on 
eye-watering AI spending sprees, and it’s becoming clear that the technology 
poses a real threat to the business models of software and legal services 
companies.

The result has been a pretty dramatic selloff. Even though Nasdaq 100 futures 
were ticking higher on Friday morning, the index has fallen some 4.6% in the 
span of three days.

The losses have been particularly bruising in software, with some traders 
dubbing it “SaaSpocalypse.” (Read our full explainer <https://bloom.bg/3ZFeMRf> 
on what’s behind the plunge in software-as-a-service, or SaaS, companies.)

Crypto was another major loser. After a meteoric rise for much of last year as 
Trump’s victory set off a speculative rush into cryptocurrencies, the market 
for such tokens has tumbled sharply this month as investors yanked out cash. 
Bitcoin is trading near $65,000 today after a minor recovery — but is still 
roughly 50% below its record.

“Buyers attempting to step in are effectively trying to catch falling knives,” 
said Chris Weston, head of research at Pepperstone Group.

 Super Bowl punters vs. gambling sharks 



Americans have a new way to gamble on the Super Bowl: prediction markets. 

Almost overnight, prediction markets such as Kalshi have begun snapping up 
large amounts of money <https://bloom.bg/46tENX9> that would likely otherwise 
have ended up on traditional gambling forums. The platforms are attracting 
punters and gambling pros alike.

The fast-growing industry is reaching something like critical mass just in 
time for Sunday’s game between the New England Patriots and Seattle Seahawks.

Traders on Kalshi and its chief rival, Polymarket, have swapped more than $800 
million worth of contracts tied to the Super Bowl so far, compared with the 
$1.8 billion Americans are expected to wager on the game through regulated 
sportsbooks, according to the American Gaming Association.

One key difference between the upstart exchanges and traditional sportsbooks 
is that instead of betting against the house, prediction markets match traders 
on opposite sides of “yes” or “no” bets.That means that casual users are often 
going up against professional gamblers, or “sharks,” as they’re known in the 
industry.

“I want to just tell people, ‘Please be careful, because these places are 
infested by smart people,’” said Antonino De Rosa, a shark who lives in Port 
Saint Lucie, Florida. —Justina Lee <https://bloom.bg/3BKDerX> and Ira Boudway 
<https://bloom.bg/3VVgTP5>
