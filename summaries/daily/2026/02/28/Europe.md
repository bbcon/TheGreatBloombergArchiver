European airspace was disrupted materially on Saturday as Israel and Gulf states closed their airspace following the onset of US-Israel strikes on Iran, with Emirates suspending all Dubai flight operations. The immediate European economic exposure runs primarily through energy: the eurozone is a large net importer of oil and gas, and a sustained rise toward $108 per barrel — the Bloomberg Economics estimate under a Hormuz closure scenario — would exert renewed inflationary pressure on an economy the ECB has only recently guided toward its 2% target.

The ECB's strategic positioning is consequently complicated. Having spent the past two years managing the inflation shock partly attributable to energy price volatility following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, policymakers now face the possibility of another energy-driven cost-push episode. Whether that would alter the ECB's rate path depends heavily on the duration and severity of the oil shock — a point Christine Lagarde and her colleagues will be acutely aware of as they assess incoming data over coming weeks.

On the reserve currency front, the ECB this month unveiled a repo facility extending euro liquidity to most foreign central banks, representing the institution's most concrete step yet toward promoting the euro's international role. The initiative has drawn support across euro-zone governments and the European Parliament, and Italy's central bank governor Fabio Panetta and even Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel have indicated openness to a European sovereign bond that would create a liquid pan-European benchmark asset. However, the structural barriers remain formidable: fragmented national bond markets mean German Bund trading volumes in all of 2024 amounted to roughly €6.74 trillion, less than two weeks of US Treasury turnover. The EU's economy commissioner Valdis Dombrovskis also cautioned that a stronger international euro role carries exchange rate appreciation implications that European exporters would need to absorb.
