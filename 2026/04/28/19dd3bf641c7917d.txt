Global merchandise trade will increase in 2026 by half of the nearly 5% growth 
posted last year as the widening Mideastenergy crisis 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/china-s-leaders-vow-to-counter-shocks-as-economy-withstands-war?cmpid=BBD042826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260428&utm_content=3880>
 restrains economic activity and protectionism spreads beyond the US.

Those are among the takeaways in a new Oxford Economics report which estimated 
goods trade worldwide will expand about 2.5% both this year and next, after 
rising by 4.9% in 2025.

Oxford said the current oil-price shock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-28?cmpid=BBD042826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260428&utm_content=3880>
 “could plausibly cut world trade by 0.6%-0.9%.” On Tuesday Brent crude rose as 
much as 2.9% to $111.32 a barrel, as the commodityrallied 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/stock-market-today-dow-s-p-live-updates?cmpid=BBD042826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260428&utm_content=3880>
 for a seventh consecutive day.

“This not only threatens the flow of energy goods from the Middle East but 
will also sap purchasing power in much of the world via higher inflation, raise 
shipping costs, and potentially disrupt air travel and tourism,” wrote Adam 
Slater, lead economist at Oxford.

Texas Factories

According to special questions in a survey 
<https://www.dallasfed.org/research/surveys/tbos/2026/2604q#tab-tmos> released 
Monday by the Dallas Fed, 35% of 67 Texas manufacturers polled April 14-22 said 
they’re feeling a negative impact from the war in Iran, and another 36% said 
they expect to feel the hurt if the conflict drags on. Higher fuel and 
transport costs was the main culprit.

Some 35% said they’ve increased their selling prices, another 26% said they’ve 
adjusted their supply chains and 22% said they’ve eased up on hiring.

“Raw material prices spiked quickly, but uncertainty on supply has meant some 
ability to increase pricing,” according to a chemical maker’s response to the 
Dallas Fed’s questionnaire.

At the same time, the Oxford Economics research note said trade is enjoying 
some tailwinds, especially from the artificial intelligence boom that’s fueling 
demand for imported capital goods — computers, semiconductors and other tech 
gear. Also the Supreme Court’s ruling against much of President Donald Trump’s 
2025 tariffs has dropped theaverage US import tax 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/trump-tariffs-tracker/?cmpid=BBD042826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260428&utm_content=3880>
.

Here’s a breakdown by major region:

 * In Asia, the AI frenzy has lifted activity in Taiwan, Malaysia and Korea, 
as well as China where first-quarter trade data was “remarkably” robust 
especially to markets like Japan and Korea. But “stronger protectionist 
reactions to China’s export expansion remain a key medium-term forecast risk,” 
wrote Slater.
 * Trade barriers in Europe are rising with a record number of defense probes 
and “Made in Europe” restrictions, according to Oxford. “These trends will 
contribute to keeping world trade growth relatively slow,” Slater wrote. 
“Creeping protectionism elsewhere will add to this.”
 * In the US, a recovery in imports to near-trend levels is mostly due to 
AI-related demand but most other categories “are down 10%-25% versus trend,” 
Oxford said. For shipments abroad, “if we exclude re-exports and gold exports, 
both of which have risen very sharply, underlying export growth is close to 
zero.” Source: Oxford Economics, Haver Analytics
“US exports to some Asian destinations are doing well. But exports to China, 
Canada, Japan, and South Africa are falling,” Slater wrote. “Some of this 
represents official retaliation against US tariffs, but it may also reflect 
more subtle forms of trade discrimination by economies that have been on the 
receiving end of particularly aggressive tariff treatment from the US.”
