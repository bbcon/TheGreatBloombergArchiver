UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer survived another day under intense pressure, though his political position remains precarious. Four junior ministers have resigned in an attempt to force his departure, and market pricing implies roughly a one-in-five chance he remains in office through year-end. Traders are at their most bearish on sterling in five weeks. The King's Speech, scheduled to lay out a packed legislative agenda, provides a near-term test of whether Starmer can project governing authority while managing internal Labour dissent. The constitutional and procedural difficulty of removing a sitting Labour leader has so far protected him, but the parliamentary arithmetic and cabinet cohesion continue to deteriorate.

UK government bond markets reflected the political and fiscal anxiety. Thirty-year gilt yields hit a 28-year high, a move that underscores investor concerns about fiscal credibility at a moment when the prime minister's ability to deliver on spending commitments is in question. Jamie Dimon added an external dimension, warning that any post-Starmer government that raises taxes on banks would cause JPMorgan to abandon plans for a multi-billion-pound new headquarters at Canary Wharf.

In Germany, Chancellor Friedrich Merz's coalition held a six-hour working session aimed at narrowing policy rifts and restoring public confidence, but produced few concrete agreements. A year into office, Merz's approval ratings have fallen to historic lows, and the AfD has extended its polling lead. The absence of coalition cohesion at a moment requiring fiscal and industrial policy decisions carries macro consequences for the eurozone's largest economy.

ECB policy is shifting in tone. Bundesbank President Nagel told Handelsblatt that rate hikes are increasingly likely — a notable pivot given that rate cuts had been the dominant expectation for the eurozone earlier this year. France provided additional cause for concern, with unemployment unexpectedly rising to its highest level in five years, adding to evidence that the euro area's second-largest economy was already losing momentum before the Iran war began. The EU is also working to join a US-led initiative to secure AI and semiconductor supply chains, signalling Brussels' recognition that industrial and geopolitical alignment with Washington may be necessary regardless of ongoing trade tensions.
