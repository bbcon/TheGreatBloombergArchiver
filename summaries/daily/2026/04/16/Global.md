The defining macro tension of the moment is the divergence between policymaker pessimism and market optimism. At the IMF-World Bank spring meetings in Washington this week, the emerging consensus among officials was that investors are materially underestimating the economic damage from the Iran war. Qatar's Finance Minister described current market conditions as "the tip of the iceberg," and participants broadly expect structural consequences — higher costs, longer trade routes, elevated geopolitical risk premiums — to persist well beyond any ceasefire. Global Trade Alert has tracked more than 250 distinct government policy responses since hostilities began on February 28, the large majority of which are liberalizing import measures, energy subsidies, or demand-reduction programs, particularly across Asia. The response is notably broad-based rather than sectoral, reflecting the diffuse nature of the supply disruption.

The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, now entering its third day of formal US blockade activity. Two Iran-sanctioned vessels — the LPG carrier G Summer and the VLCC Hong Lu — were observed transiting a new route between Iran's Larak and Qeshm islands into the Persian Gulf, suggesting Tehran is testing alternative corridors. The US and Iran are reportedly considering a two-week ceasefire extension to allow further negotiations.

The aluminum market has entered what JPMorgan characterizes as a structural supply "black hole." Iranian strikes on smelters in Abu Dhabi and Bahrain at the end of March caused production losses that will take at least a year to recover, pushing London Metal Exchange prices to four-year highs above $3,600 per ton. JPMorgan now forecasts prices could reach $4,000 per ton — approaching the 2022 all-time high — as the market faces its largest supply deficit in over 25 years. The shortage falls disproportionately on Western manufacturers, for whom Chinese and Russian supply remains largely inaccessible due to sanctions and tariffs.

Food security represents a slower-moving but potentially more consequential transmission channel. Gulf-region countries account for at least 30% of global fertilizer supply, including urea, ammonia, and diammonium phosphate. A Farm Bureau survey finds over 70% of US farmers cannot currently afford sufficient fertilizer. Price effects will emerge with a harvest-cycle lag, and a prolonged conflict risks compounding input cost inflation with yield reductions, amplifying food price pressures globally. The US Justice Department has opened a probe into fertilizer and farm input pricing practices. WTI crude was trading near $92.50, with Brent around $95.
