Top trade negotiators from North America are set to meet virtually today to 
decide on the next steps for the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement on thesix-year 
anniversary 
<https://ustr.gov/sites/default/files/files/agreements/FTA/USMCA/Text/34_Final_Provisions.pdf>
 of the pact governing nearly $2 trillion in cross-border commerce.

At issue is whether to renew it or review it, and US President Donald Trump 
has all butruled out 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/trump-says-he-d-rather-not-have-usmca-teasing-termination?cmpid=BBD070126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260701&utm_content=5937>
 renewal of the deal he brokered in his first term. That leaves annual reviews 
as the likely path toward USMCA’s expiration in 2036, according toBloomberg 
News reporting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/usmca-limbo-looms-with-trump-cool-on-his-2-trillion-a-year-deal?cmpid=BBD070126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260701&utm_content=5937>
 on Wednesday.

Here’s a sampling of views about what Trump should do:

 * In a statement, International Association of Machinists President Brian 
Bryant said “the agreement has fallen short of delivering the broad economic 
gains and job security that were promised.”
 * In the same statement, Representative Rosa DeLauro, a Democrat from 
Connecticut, said “multinational corporations have continued to use the threat 
of offshoring as leverage against workers, the US trade deficit with Mexico and 
Canada has significantly increased, and surging USMCA imports have undermined 
American workers and farmers and firms in the auto sector, steel, aerospace and 
more.”
 * US Chamber of Commerce said 
<https://www.uschamber.com/international/support-usmca> “maintaining and 
reinforcing USMCA is essential to sustaining economic growth, promoting 
investment, and ensuring North America remains one of the most competitive and 
dynamic regions in the world.”
 * The Business Roundtable, which speaks for CEOs of major American companies, 
said that “it’s time to lock in these benefits for America,” offering several 
reasons to “extend and strengthen” USMCA: jobs and economic growth, 
supply-chain reliability, food and energy security, US investment and 
manufacturing competitiveness.
 * Oxford Economics said in a research note that as USMCA undergoes a series 
of reviews, “our new forecasts will build in a larger drag on confidence and 
investment in all three economies, with costs concentrated in Canada and 
Mexico.”
But perhaps the most colorful quotes 
<https://www.atlanticcouncil.org/blogs/econographics/the-five-stages-of-a-usmca-shakeup/>
 came from Madeline Chalecki, assistant director at theAtlantic Council’s 
GeoEconomics Center, who said USMCA’s unraveling will go through the typical 
stages of a personal breakup.

“First comes denial, as policymakers convince themselves that the relationship 
can continue as before,” Chalecki explained. “Then comes anger, expressed 
through tariffs and retaliation. Bargaining follows as governments search for 
side deals and companies begin diversifying.”
