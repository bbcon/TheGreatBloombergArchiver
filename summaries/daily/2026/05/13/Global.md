Oil markets remain the principal transmission mechanism for the Iran conflict. WTI crude traded above $100 per barrel during the session, having gained approximately 8% over the prior three days, before pulling back roughly 1% on the day. The IEA reported that global inventories are falling at a record pace and will continue declining for months. Satellite imagery suggests Iranian crude exports from the Kharg Island terminal have halted entirely over the past several days — the first sign of a prolonged stoppage since the conflict began — while a Chinese supertanker operated by Cosco units was tracked traversing the Strait of Hormuz, a rare attempted crossing that attracted immediate attention given the waterway's contested status.

The conflict's global economic fallout is becoming more visible across multiple dimensions. Hapag-Lloyd reported approximately $250 million in additional monthly operating costs since the war began on February 28, though its CEO noted that demand outside the Persian Gulf has been "surprisingly" resilient, with Asian export volumes holding firm through April and into the near term. The IEA's inventory assessment implies continued upward pressure on prices absent a resolution, while US crude export capacity — currently elevated by strategic reserve drawdowns — faces finite limits.

The asymmetric warfare dynamic is increasingly relevant to geopolitical risk pricing. Iran has deployed midget submarines to maintain partial Hormuz control despite significant US naval superiority, a tactical approach that has sustained the stalemate now entering its eleventh week. Trump dismissed Iran's most recent peace proposal as "garbage," a development that pushed Brent December futures back above $90 and further compressed expectations for near-term resolution.

The summit's energy dimension extends to Beijing's own exposure. China depends on Hormuz for a substantial share of its energy imports, and the prolonged closure is disrupting chipmaker supply chains at TSMC and Samsung. Whether Xi is willing to use Chinese influence on Tehran — and at what price — may be the summit's most consequential undisclosed negotiation. India's currency defence measures, Japan's bond yield spike, and Malawi's fertiliser access crisis each represent distinct manifestations of the same underlying shock propagating through the global economy.
