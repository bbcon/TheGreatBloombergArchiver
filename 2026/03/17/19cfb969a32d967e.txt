Renewables Cushion Europe’s Power Prices From Iran Shock
Europe’s power market is holding up amid its first serious geopolitical stress 
test since 2022.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Eva Brendel looks at why the unfolding energy shock hasn’t jolted 
Europe’s power markets as much as the 2022 crisis.

The war in Iran has rattled energy markets, driving up prices and reviving 
memories of the 2022 crisis, when disrupted Russian gas supplies dealt Europe a 
shock it took years to recover from.

Yet the continent’s power market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/why-europe-s-power-prices-aren-t-soaring-like-gas?cmpid=BBBXT031726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
, at least, is not reacting to the turmoil in the same way this time.

Europe has expanded its renewable fleet since 2022, which means that 
electricity prices are more often set by cheaper solar and wind instead of gas 
or coal-fired plants. As a result,higher gas prices 
<bbg://news/stories/TC15QUKK3NYA> are no longer feeding through to power 
markets to the same extent as in the past.

Renewables don’t only lower average electricity prices, they also damp price 
spikes, Rabobank said in a report. Seasonal factors are reinforcing that 
effect: with spring underway, heating demand is easing while solar output is 
improving.

Without those cushioning effects, European electricity prices would already be 
about one-third higher, the bank said.

In addition, France’s nuclear fleet — a key pillar of Europe’s power market — 
is on a much stronger footing than during the last energy crisis, adding more 
supply to the grid. Back then, the aging fleet required numerousill-timed 
upgrades 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2023-europe-energy-crisis-updates-france-nuclear-outage/?cmpid=BBBXT031726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 and repairs.

Still, the power market is not fully insulated. Evening prices have spiked to 
around three times their usual levels in several countries. The reason is that 
solar output fades at those times, while demand pushes higher in the evening 
when people come home, switch on lights and cook dinner.

If the war drags on for several months, the seasonal relief risks fading. “The 
market may look calm now, but sensitivity increases again toward the colder 
months,” said Uniper SE market analyst Thomas Thomas.

But for now, the cushion that renewables provide against the surge in gas and 
oil prices strengthens the case for further green electrification.

—Eva Brendel, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Polyethylene capacity was headed for a record growth rate in the next few 
years.BloombergNEF <bbg://news/stories/TC1A8GKGZALI> estimated in February that 
6.8 million metric tons per year on average would be added over from 2026 
through 2030 — roughly double the growth rate of 2000 to 2025. Asia and the 
Middle East account for more than 70% of the new capacity. Those markets rely 
on feedstocks that ship via the Strait of Hormuz, suggesting a prolonged 
conflict in the region could stall many of those upcoming projects.
