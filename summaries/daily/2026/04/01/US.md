US equity markets posted their best session in nearly a year on Tuesday, with the S&P 500 surging sharply after Iranian President Masoud Pezeshkian indicated to EU officials that Tehran was "willing to end the war" but required guarantees — the clearest signal from the Iranian side of any appetite for negotiation. The rally was amplified by heavily bearish end-of-quarter positioning rather than a wholesale shift in investor conviction; Wall Street trading desks noted that the magnitude of the move reflected a short squeeze as much as a fundamental reassessment. The S&P 500 still closed March down 5%, its worst monthly performance in a year, and remains roughly 8% below its pre-war peak.

President Trump subsequently told reporters he foresaw ending the conflict within two to three weeks, adding that a formal agreement with Tehran was not a prerequisite for US withdrawal and that the administration had "largely accomplished its military goals." He is scheduled to address the nation Wednesday evening. The statement raises significant strategic questions: the administration's own foreign policy document, published four months ago, listed keeping the Strait of Hormuz open as a core US interest. Trump's apparent willingness to leave that issue to other nations represents a meaningful departure from stated policy.

The consumer backdrop is becoming a more pressing concern for macro forecasters. Average US gasoline prices crossed $4 per gallon for the first time since the early months of the Ukraine war, following a 30%-plus surge in March. While fuel expenditure as a share of disposable income remains near historic lows at 1.6%, the household savings rate has declined to 4.5% from 6.4% in January 2024, narrowing the buffer. More consequentially, corporate equities now represent over a quarter of household net worth — a record in eight decades of Fed data — meaning that equity market performance may prove a more potent channel for consumer spending than pump prices. February retail sales data due Wednesday will offer a pre-war baseline.
