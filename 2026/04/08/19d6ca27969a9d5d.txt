Watching the strait

What happens next in markets depends, in large part, on whether energy 
shipments flow freely through the Strait of Hormuz.

ItтАЩs the route for about a fifth of global oil and liquefied natural gas 
supplies, and the near-halt of traffic pushed prices for real-world crude to a 
record. That caused inflation expectations to rise, and central bankers to warn 
they may have to tighten policy, upending markets.

Yet even when Hormuz transit does resume, the return of energy supplies is 
unlikely to be instant. Output has been reduced at oil and gas fields, while 
refineries have curtailed production or shut down. Some of those willtake weeks 
to return to normal
