The defining event of the past 24 hours is the breakdown of the month-old US-Iran ceasefire, which now appears increasingly fragile. US warships escorted American-flagged vessels through the Strait of Hormuz, prompting an exchange of fire and an Iranian missile and drone attack that caused a large fire at the UAE's Fujairah port — a facility Tehran has now explicitly designated within its "zone of maritime control." Hundreds of vessels clustered near Dubai as shipowners continued to avoid the strait. Iraq is offering substantial discounts to term buyers for crude loaded this month, but tankers would need to transit Hormuz to collect barrels, creating a commercial impasse.

The oil market implications are compounding daily. With the conflict entering its tenth week, analysts estimate a cumulative supply loss now approaching or exceeding one billion barrels, with a minimum of 10 million barrels per day not reaching global markets via the strait. Brent futures for end-2026 have moved above $90 per barrel — the highest since the conflict began — while WTI spot trades above $104. Prediction markets now assign less than 50% odds to a return to normal Hormuz traffic by end of June, down from around 90% two weeks ago. Two-year inflation swaps in the US have crossed 3%, their highest since 2022.

The geopolitical calculus points toward a negotiated resolution structured around something resembling the 2015 JCPOA framework, though the political costs for both sides remain high. Analysts at Macquarie characterize a return to a JCPOA-like arrangement as the only "modestly rational" outcome, while BCA Research notes that continued economic resilience — underpinned by the AI investment cycle — paradoxically reduces the pressure on either party to compromise. The Trump-Xi summit remains scheduled for May 14-15 in Beijing, with trade, Taiwan, and Iran all on the agenda. The US has also become the world's largest crude exporter by volume, shipping more than 250 million barrels in the past nine weeks to help offset the supply shortfall — though US domestic inventories are drawing down rapidly, raising questions about the sustainability of that role.
