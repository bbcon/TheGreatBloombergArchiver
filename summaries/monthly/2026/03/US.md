• US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities late in the month introduced a compounding energy and geopolitical shock, pushing WTI above $84 by mid-month and average gasoline to approximately $3.70 per gallon by month's end — the highest under Trump's presidency.

**Tariff Regime in Transition**
The month opened with the legal and practical fallout from the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariff architecture. By the first Tuesday of March, a 15% global levy under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 had entered into force. The legal footing was widely questioned from the outset: Section 122 applies specifically to "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath stated plainly the US does not meet, given the absence of dollar weakness or capital flight that a genuine balance-of-payments crisis would produce. The administration signaled it would slow-walk refunds on overturned levies even as more than 1,500 refund lawsuits were filed representing an estimated $170 billion in contested duties. Later in the month, a fresh Section 301 trade investigation was filed against more than a dozen economies — including China, the EU, Mexico, India, and Taiwan — with a public hearing set for May 5. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimated the IEEPA invalidation reduces projected federal revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036, with the Section 122 replacement recovering approximately $925 billion — a material gap.

**Equity Markets and the AI Disruption Narrative**
The month's dominant early equity theme was a rotation away from US software and services names perceived as vulnerable to AI displacement. IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years after Anthropic's Claude Code tool was demonstrated to modernize COBOL, threatening the mainframe franchise. C3.ai tumbled 24%, Salesforce declined roughly 4%, and Workday dropped 9.2%. Against this, hardware and infrastructure names provided partial offset: Dell gained 9.8% on AI server demand, and Block surged as much as 27% after announcing workforce reductions framed as an AI productivity bet. Nvidia's earnings — with revenue guidance of approximately $78 billion beating consensus — produced a muted response, reflecting the increasingly demanding valuation bar facing AI-adjacent equities. The S&P 500 options market showed elevated caution early in the month, with the put-call skew rising to a two-year high. By late March, the conflict-driven growth and inflation headwinds had taken over as the primary equity driver, with the Magnificent Seven gauge down nearly 10% from its peak and Goldman Sachs's trading desk noting that clients who had anticipated a quick geopolitical resolution were increasingly skeptical.

**Federal Reserve: Policy Bind and Succession**
The collision between a deteriorating labor market and an oil-driven inflation shock defined the Fed's operating environment across March. February payrolls recorded the first private-sector decline in five years, data that under ordinary conditions would reinforce the case for easing. Instead, the energy price shock overwhelmed the labor market signal. At the March 19 FOMC meeting, the committee voted 11-1 to hold, but Powell's press conference was notably hawkish, with the chair explicitly acknowledging that colleagues had raised the possibility that the next move could be a hike. The Fed's preferred core inflation gauge ran at an estimated 3% in February, and core PCE is tracking approximately 3.1% for January — the widest divergence from CPI in decades. Goldman Sachs pushed its forecast for the first rate cut to September, while the ICE BofA MOVE Index climbed to its highest level since June. By month's end, futures markets were briefly pricing a non-trivial probability of a rate hike at the April meeting — the first such pricing since 2023. The anticipated succession of Kevin Warsh as Fed chair added a further layer of hawkish market pricing, even as his confirmation remained blocked by Senator Tillis pending a Justice Department investigation. Powell confirmed he will remain in his board seat until January 2028 regardless of his chair tenure, and the White House's decision not to oppose a legal challenge to his authority raised uncomfortable questions about central bank independence.

**Energy Economics and Fiscal Deterioration**
US average gasoline prices rose approximately 27% from the onset of hostilities in late February, reaching $3.70 per gallon by month's end, while diesel surpassed $5 per gallon for the first time since December 2022. SMBC Nikko Securities estimated the gasoline price increase could reduce the household savings rate by approximately 60 basis points. Bloomberg Economics found that near $83 per barrel crude is sufficient to neutralize the approximately $800 average household benefit from "One Big Beautiful Bill" tax refunds entirely, with the net household position turning negative by roughly $1,960 at $110 per barrel — a distributional concern compounded by energy costs' regressive nature. The Pentagon's $200 billion supplemental funding request for the Iran operation threatens to push the 2026 federal deficit toward $2 trillion, roughly double Treasury Secretary Bessent's 3%-of-GDP target. Goldman Sachs raised its 12-month US recession probability to 30% by month's end, projecting unemployment rising to 4.6% by year-end. The administration directed Sable Offshore to restore California offshore oil production, and Treasury Secretary Bessent floated lifting sanctions on approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian crude at sea — though analysts noted virtually all such oil flows to Chinese refiners, limiting practical price relief. The OECD projected US inflation reaching 4.2% in 2026, up from 2.6% the prior year.
