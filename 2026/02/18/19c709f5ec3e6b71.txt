The Japan Template

Full-year 2025 trade data won’t be in until Thursday, but the deficit with 
Japan is shaping up to come in at something under $63 billion. Wind back the 
clock 30 years, and the annual gap wasnot much different 
<https://www.census.gov/foreign-trade/balance/c5880.html>, at $58 billion — 
without adjusting for inflation.

Key to that dramatic stasis in the US-Japan trade imbalance that once spooked 
American lawmakersand media 
<https://go.gale.com/ps/i.do?id=GALE%7CA7767591&sid=sitemap&v=2.1&it=r&p=LitRC&sw=w&userGroupName=anon%7Eb9396569&aty=open-web-entry>
: a major shift by Japanese manufacturers to build factories in North America. 
By 2021, Toyota had assembled 30 million vehicles in the US alone.

Donald Trump has indicated in the past that he’s open to the idea of Chinese 
companies emulating that same example, though his main objectives with Beijing 
so far have centered on curbing the bilateral deficit via tariff hikes and 
pressing Beijing for purchases of US agricultural goods.

Now, the manufacturing transplant scenario has suddenly come back into focus — 
with Bloomberg’s report on Friday that Ford Motor CEO Jim Farley had discussed 
with senior Trump administration officials a framework for Chinese automakers to
build cars in America 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/ford-ceo-trump-officials-discussed-china-us-carmaking-ventures?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
.
Camry vehicle engine parts at a Toyota plant in Georgetown, Kentucky, Aug. 28, 
2019Photographer: Luke Sharrett/Bloomberg
The idea involved Chinese carmakers partnering with US companies through joint 
ventures in which the American company holds a controlling stake. That would be 
notably different than the Japan template. It would also be different than what 
Chinese companies are able to do elsewhere, in partnerssuch as Hungary 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-10/byd-ramps-up-european-push-as-china-fortunes-flag?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
, which hosts EV giant BYD’s first European manufacturing facility.

Besides ceding control, Chinese enterprises might need to consider local 
politics. Even if Trump were ready to embrace Chinese investment, it’s possible 
he may not be able to control the wave of anti-China sentiment that he himself 
turbocharged a decade ago.

In 2023, Virginia — then run by a Republican governor — removed itself 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2023-03-11/america-s-anti-china-restrictions-are-going-local-new-economy-saturday?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 from consideration for an electric-battery plant proposed as a joint venture 
between Ford and the giant Chinese manufacturer known as CATL. Just last 
November, Oklahoma Governor Kevin Stitt, also a Republican, said he’d be “
reluctant 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-21/oklahoma-governor-kevin-stitt-reluctant-to-host-chinese-ventures?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
” to welcome Chinese-owned business ventures into his state.

Building capacity in the US would also hardly help address China’s own 
overcapacity crisis. It’s not immediately clear what Beijing would get out of 
some grand investment plan. (For 1990s Japan, benefits included continued US 
military protection — obviously, an entirely different situation from China 
today.)

Still, Trump has successfully shifted the parameters of policy discourse in 
the past. He’s also shown a wide capacity for changing tacks, such as last 
year’s bombing of Iran to the current rounds of negotiations. And he’s gota big 
summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-14/china-s-wang-yi-meets-us-rubio-ahead-of-planned-trump-xi-summit?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
coming with President Xi Jinping in Beijing in April. So the latest tea leaves 
bear watching.

Need-to-Know Research

How damaging are natural disasters 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/california-faces-more-wild-weather-as-new-storm-threatens?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 to US local economies, and for that matter their banking networks? The Federal 
Reserve Bank of New York has an answer.

The bank’s economists have processed figures from a National Oceanic and 
Atmospheric Administration database to illustrate, for each US county, the 
damage to property and crops from a variety of disasters, ranging from tropical 
storms to droughts and severe winters. This month, they’ve updated figures 
through the end of 2024. Theinteractive map 
<https://www.newyorkfed.org/research/policy/natural-disaster-losses/#interactive>
 illustrates that roughly half of the $18.2 billion in US disaster damage that 
year came from tropical storms, and indeedHurricane Helene 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-10-10/us-jobless-claims-jump-to-a-year-high-partly-boosted-by-helene?cmpid=BBD021826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
’s path is visible in the counties of the southeast.

The New York Fed notes that the figures are for direct damage only. “That is, 
monetized values of physical destruction. For example, a downed power line that 
falls on a home during a tornado results in direct damages. Subsequentindirect 
losses may occur if local businesses are forced to close due to a lack power 
from the downed line.”
