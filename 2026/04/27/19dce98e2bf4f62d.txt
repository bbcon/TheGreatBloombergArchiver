There is no better current example out there for how President Donald Trump 
has shaken things up in the global economy than thestill-closed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-26/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-27?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
 Strait of Hormuz. But if you want to grasp some of the ways beyond that in 
which Trump still plans to shake up global commerce, consider another 
transportation mainstay: little red wagons.

Officials from the US Trade Representative’s office are holding public 
hearings April 28 and May 5 as part of the tariff investigations Trump ordered 
after the US Supreme Court ruled the bulk of the duties he imposed last year
were illegal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/tariff-refunds-face-delays-over-slow-importer-opt-in-us-says?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
. It is all part of the statutory process Trump hoped to avoid and is now 
forced to go through as his team considers how to put duties on imports from 
countries they believe don’t enforce bans on goods made with forced labor or 
that have a “structural excess capacity” in manufacturing.

Hundreds of companies have already made written submissions. Which is where 
the little red wagon comes in.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/trump-tariff-rates-could-be-restored-by-early-july-bessent?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>

There are few American toys more iconic than The Radio Flyer Company’s red 
wagons. For generations, legions of kids have loaded them up with teddy bears, 
dolls, dirt, lemonade and other childhood essentials and pulled them around the 
house or neighborhood.

Radio Flyer was founded in 1917. It remains family-owned and headquartered in 
Chicago, and has diversified into analog and electric bicycles.

Since the early 2000s the company has relied on production in China. The 
tariffs proposed as part of theSection 301 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/trump-tariffs-revival-trade-investigations-launched-in-china-eu?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
 investigation into industrial overcapacity would make importing the wagons and 
bikes it makes in China more expensive. So in an April 8submission 
<https://comments.ustr.gov/s/commentdetails?rid=8BBDM28QP6> the company’s 
“Chief Wagon Officer,” Robert Pasin, requested the Trump administration refrain 
from imposing new tariffs on its products.

“American families — and American companies like Radio Flyer that have 
invested more than a century in bringing joy to children — cannot sustain 
further tariff escalation on top of the economic headwinds already facing our 
industry,” Pasin wrote.

What’s most interesting about the letter isn’t the existential plea against 
tariffs. It’s the economic case that Pasin makes that the Trump administration 
hasgotten its diagnosis 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/trump-tariffs-push-probe-launched?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
 of Chinese overcapacity and what ails the global economy all wrong.

To be clear, the US is not the only country now worrying about a new flood of 
Chinese exports and what some have called China Shock 2.0. But in his letter 
Pasin offers an interesting contrarian take by putting the current concerns in 
context.

Pandemic Surge

A surge in demand for Radio Flyer’s products coupled with supply chain 
problems and theinflation squeeze 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/goldman-hikes-oil-price-forecasts-on-extreme-inventory-draws?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
 on household budgets that followed the pandemic left the company with too much 
capital tied up in inventory and not enough going into investment and 
marketing. Tariffs will only make the situation worse, Pasin argues in his 
letter. Shifting production willtake years 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-20/how-trump-will-change-us-trade-policy-after-supreme-court-ruling?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
 and require yet more significant capital investments.

Pasin argues the big economic issue confronting companies like his isn’t too 
much supply, it’s lack of demand. Here is the relevant section in full (the 
bolded emphasis is Pasin’s):


“While this investigation focuses on alleged structural excess capacity and 
overproduction, we respectfully submit that the far more significant market 
disruption has been extreme and unusual demand volatility—not a deliberate 
oversupply strategy by foreign manufacturers.

“During the COVID sales boom, Radio Flyer and our suppliers struggled to 
produce enough product to meet explosive consumer demand. We faced significant 
supply constraints, extended lead times, and inventory shortfalls that cost us 
substantial revenue and disappointed our retail partners and end customers.
There was no sense of “excess capacity” during that period—quite the opposite.

“The current period of oversupply is a direct and predictable consequence of 
that demand surge, followed by the sharp post-pandemic correction. 
Producers—including those in the US supply chain—built capacity to meet peak 
demand and are now navigating the painful normalization. This is a cyclical 
economic phenomenon, not evidence of unfair trade practices. We believe demand 
will recover as economic conditions improve, and that the market will naturally 
absorb current inventory levels.

“Imposing punitive tariffs in response to normal post-boom market correction 
would inflict lasting harm on US companies like Radio Flyer without addressing 
any genuine unfair trade behavior.”


It seems unlikely that Pasin’s argument will register with the Trump 
administration or others in Washington who see tariffs as a tool to protect 
American industry and addressvast imbalances 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/markets-are-too-blase-on-war-s-economic-toll-policymakers-warn?cmpid=BBD042726_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260427&utm_content=3786>
 in the global economy. To be sure, the hearings over the next few weeks will 
also feature plenty of people calling for tariffs, as do a significant number 
of the written submissions already filed.

We will only really know when we see what a patchwork of new tariffs these 
investigations are meant to lead to look like. Which is still some months away 
as they are expected to replace temporary 10% duties Trump imposed after the 
Supreme Court’s February decision that expire in July.

But every so often in a world in which the news is dominated by war and other 
monumental events, it’s worth pausing for a beat. And taking the time to 
consider an alternative argument.

University of Michigan’s final April sentiment index dropped to 49.8 this month

reading, it remained the lowest in data back to 1978.
