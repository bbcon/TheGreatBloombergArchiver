Powell’s Pushback

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell gave no quarter in his continuing battles 
with President Donald Trump, suggesting on Wednesday that — for now at least — 
interest rates aren’t going anywhere, andneither is he 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/powell-says-no-intention-of-leaving-fed-until-investigation-over?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
.

Trump’s long-held position on rates, of course, is that they ought to have 
been slashed well below the current target range of 3.5%-3.75%. And he wants 
Powell gone. Neither outcome seems on the immediate horizon after the Fed 
chief’s press conference following the central bank’s latest policy meeting.

Powell tied any resumption of interest-rate cuts to evidence the inflationary 
effect of Trump’s tariff hikes has passed. And that’s before policymakers even 
begin to consider the impact of the US-Israeli war against Iran, which has sent 
energy prices skyrocketing.

The Fed’s preferred core gauge of inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/prices-paid-to-us-producers-increase-by-more-than-forecast?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 ran at an estimated 3% pace last month, Powell said — a full percentage point 
above the 2% target. That’s largely due to the import duties, he said, the 
impact of which should peter out “in the middle parts of the year.”

“So if we don’t see that progress, then you won’t see the rate cut 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/us-bonds-pare-losses-as-fed-keeps-outlook-for-one-2026-rate-cut?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
,” Powell declared. After all, “we have to be humble about knowing how long it 
will take for tariffs to go all the way through the economy” he said, noting 
that the Covid inflation shock took two years longer to subside than the Fed 
anticipated.

As for the Iran war effects, “we’re right at the beginning,” so there’s 
nothing for the Fed to do but watch and wait, Powell said.
Jerome PowellPhotographer: Kent Nishimura/Bloomberg
Surprising Fed watchers, Powell also explicitly addressed his position at the 
Fed, after repeatedly having declined to comment on the matter for months. 
First off, he said that if Trump’s pick to succeed him — Kevin Warsh — isn’t 
confirmed by the Senate before his term ends in May, he’ll stay on at the helm 
pro tempore.

And Warsh’s nomination is in limbo, because a key Republican senator, Thom 
Tillis, hasvowed to block 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/tillis-meets-with-warsh-vows-to-keep-blocking-trump-fed-pick?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 any confirmation vote until the Justice Department resolves what he regards as 
a “bogus” probe of the Fed’s building renovation project and Powell’s testimony 
on the subject to Congress last June.

What’s more, Powell confirmed he won’t vacate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/court-papers-show-powell-to-stay-at-fed-if-probe-continues?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 his seat on the Fed board, which runs until January 2028, “until the 
investigation is well and truly over with transparency and finality.”

The federal prosecutor leading that probe, Jeanine Pirro, on Friday made clear 
she’snot about to abandon 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/pirro-s-defiant-streak-on-display-in-case-against-fed-s-powell?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 her effort, leaving the entire Powell succession one giant question mark.

US Treasuries, already undermined in Wednesday trading by continuing damage to 
Mideast oil infrastructure, hit their lows of the session after Powell’s public 
declaration of staying at the Fed’s helm. And stocks saw their biggest Fed-day 
decline since 2024.

“The market is frustrated with this uncertainty and the possibility 
(increasing possibility) that the transition between this Fed chair and the 
next will not go smoothly,” said Mark Spindel, chief investment officer at 
Potomac River Capital.

Need-to-Know Research

Recessions used to happen a lot more often in the US. The current expansion — 
assuming the economy doesn’t tip into a downturn by the end of March — is in 
its 71st month. That would have marked the third-longest expansion over a 
period from the mid-1800s through the 1970s.

That comes from a chart prepared by Torsten Slok, chief economist at Apollo 
Global Management. “For investors, this means full-blown credit cycles occur 
less often,” he wrote in a note this week. But that’s not to say there aren’t 
major problems within particular sectors of the economy and financial world 
during an expansion, he said.

One example was the US energy bust of 2014-16, Slok highlighted. Another was 
the downturn incommercial real estate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-02-06/yellen-says-commercial-property-is-a-worry-regulators-are-on-it?cmpid=BBD031926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
, when the Fed ramped up borrowing costs and Covid decimated central business 
districts. The takeaway, Slok says: “Between recessions, investors should 
prepare for sector-specific cycles, such as the current downturn in software, 
where one or two subsectors face distress while the rest of the economy is 
fine.”
