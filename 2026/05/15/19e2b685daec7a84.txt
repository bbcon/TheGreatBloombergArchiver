Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Mia Gindis looks at how the Iran crisis has overturned expectations 
for fuel consumption.

The oil supply shock and price surge caused by the Iran war are now predicted 
to trigger the biggest hit to global demand growth since Covid.

Two key forecasts this week painted a stark picture. The US Energy Information 
Administration said globaloil demand 
<https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/report/global_oil.php> will grow this year 
by a mere 200,000 barrels a day, down from its prewar projection of 1.2 million 
barrels a day.

The International Energy Agency sees a bleaker outlook 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/oil-inventories-fall-at-record-pace-on-iran-war-shock-iea-says?cmpid=BBBXT051526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4530>
, with worldwide consumption shrinking by 420,000 barrels a day in 2026, 
compared with a forecast for 1.3 million barrels a day of growth before the 
start of the conflict.

Down years for oil demand are rare and typically bode ill for the global 
economy. The last annual contraction was triggered by Covid. Other examples 
include the great financial crisis in the late 2000s.

With the Strait of Hormuz still largely closed to shipping amid a fragile 
ceasefire, most oil production in the Persian Gulf is effectively cut off from 
world markets. US crude prices have surged more than 60% over the past three 
months to about $104 a barrel.

Signs of demand destruction were initially seen in Asia, with some countries 
declaringnational emergencies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-25/philippines-declares-energy-emergency-as-fuel-supplies-run-short?cmpid=BBBXT051526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4530>
 orurging citizens  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-10/modi-urges-indians-to-conserve-fuel-curb-costly-oil-imports?cmpid=BBBXT051526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4530>
to work from home to conserve fuel.

In recent weeks the effects have been felt more widely. Airlines in both the 
US and Europe havecut thousands 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/jet-fuel-concerns-hang-over-europe-as-summer-vacations-approach?cmpid=BBBXT051526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4530>
 of flights.

On the cusp of the US summer driving season, from Memorial Day Weekend to 
Labor Day, the national average for gasoline prices is inching closer to the 
record high seen in 2022 after Russia’s full-scale invasion of Ukraine.

Lower-income Americans are already pulling back on purchases at the pump. Some 
are likely to think twice about lengthy summer road trips.

There could be worse to come. Depleted inventories 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-09/iran-war-is-draining-world-s-oil-buffer-at-unprecedented-pace?cmpid=BBBXT051526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4530>
 and tighter fuel markets could eventually spur a fresh surge in crude prices 
to $150 a barrel, TD Securities said this week.

Without a resolution to the Hormuz situation soon, oil consumers will remain 
under pressure.

—Mia Gindis, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

Sunlight-blocking pollution from coal-fired power plants is significantly 
cutting global solar output, and potentially causing an overestimation of the 
world’s progress in tackling climate change, according to anew study 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/pollution-from-coal-is-hurting-global-solar-output-study-finds?cmpid=BBBXT051526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4530>
. The phenomenon reduced solar generation by 5.8% in 2023, on par with the 
annual output of 18 medium-sized coal plants.

BNEF today

The US ceasefire with Iran has given way to a low-intensity conflict in the 
Strait of Hormuz, meaning persistent disruptions to key global commodity 
markets from oil and gas to fertilizer and aluminum. If the closure continues 
until July it could push crude toward $160 a barrel, BloombergNEF estimates.
