A Month of War

With the war in the Middle East entering its second month, global policymakers 
are adjusting to the dawning realization that — whether or not peace breaks out 
any time soon — the impacts are now here to stay.

That reality was reinforced on Tuesday as Europe got its first hard data on 
the initial fallout for consumers, witheuro-zone inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/euro-zone-inflation-jumps-most-since-2022-as-war-drives-energy?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 in March jumping to 2.5%. That reflected surges seen inFrance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/french-inflation-quickens-to-highest-since-august-2024-on-war?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 andGermany 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-30/german-inflation-surges-to-highest-in-more-than-year-on-iran-war?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
.

What that means is that initial parallels to the 2022 shock just after the 
Russian invasion of Ukraine were ostensibly justified. The acceleration in 
price growth is indeed the most since March of that year. Just like then, it’s 
driven by a squeeze in energy supply.

On the other hand, there are also key differences. The crunch back in 2022 was 
far worse, inflation was already much higher, and monetary policy was 
distinctly looser than it is now.

The latest euro-zone numbers even offer some comfort about the starting point 
this time: both underlying price growth that strips out volatile items such as 
energy, and the gauge of services, actually weakened compared with February. 
The overall outcome was below the 2.6% median estimate of economists.

Even so, after having been noticeably slow to respond last time round, the 
European Central Bank isn’ttaking any chances 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/ecb-s-muller-says-probable-rates-will-rise-in-coming-quarters?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
.

“A highly uncertain environment is taking root and is likely to extend beyond 
the acute phase of the conflict,” Bank of Italy Governor Fabio Panettasaid in 
Rome 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/ecb-s-panetta-says-private-sector-key-in-cross-border-payments?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 just as the data were released. “It will be essential to monitorexpectations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/ecb-s-vujcic-says-rise-in-inflation-expectations-isn-t-surprise?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 closely and to prevent a wage-price spiral, while ensuring that monetary 
policy action remains proportionate and consistent with the ECB’s mandate.”

That leaves the question open about whether a hike is likely as soon as next 
month. EconomistsSimona delle Chiaie 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWiNbQNQyeE/simona-delle-chiaie> and David 
Powell <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AGJ3RF8xSlo/david-powell> at 
Bloomberg Economics don’t think so.

“The data suggest the ECB’s baseline scenario may have slightly overestimated 
the inflationary impact of the recent surge in commodity prices,” they said in 
a report, available on the Bloomberg Terminalhere <>. “That may provide the 
doves on the Governing Council another reason to keep interest rates unchanged 
in April.”

The euro-region numbers will kick off a period of assessment around the rich 
world about the fallout of Trump’s decision to start a war next to one of the 
pinch points of the global energy supply chain.

Poland’s own release on Tuesday showed a jump to 3% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/poland-s-inflation-rebounds-as-iran-war-boosts-fuel-prices?cmpid=BBD033126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>

Need-to-Know Research

As nations around the world look at ramping up defense spending, a fresh 
National Bureau of Economic Researchpaper <http://www.nber.org/papers/w35008> 
finds that US procurement budgets “played a major role in sustaining tight 
labor markets for low-skill workers in the decades immediately after World War 
II.”

“Compared to other explanations of widely shared post-war prosperity — high 
union density and minimum wages, confiscatory taxes, expanded access to 
education — government-funded production of deadly, military matériel fits less 
comfortably into an egalitarian economic playbook,” the trio of economists 
observed. Yet their analysis showed that spending — importantly,non-R&D outlays 
— boosted factory jobs and the quits rate (a sign of a tight job market).
Source: Economists Ilyana Kuziemko, Donato A. Onorato and Suresh Naidu
Through procurement, policymakers built up local manufacturing capacity and 
provided sustained demand for low-skill workers, Ilyana Kuziemko, Donato 
Onorato and Suresh Naidu wrote in their paper. The Defense Department 
“explicitly favored contracts performed in ‘labor surplus areas,’” they said. 
Also, high rates of active military duty cut unemployment for young men — “a 
group that often faces high rates of joblessness and low wages,” they wrote.
