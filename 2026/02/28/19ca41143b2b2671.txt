Europe and China Can Only Dream of Rivaling Dollar For Now
Europe and China have far to go.

A popular narrative in financial markets since Donald Trump returned to the 
White House has been the so-calleddebasement 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/dollar-fears-are-flaring-as-trump-rekindles-debasement-trade?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 of the US dollar, and it’s flared up again these past few weeks. This time, it 
comes with a complement: Authorities behind the two main pretenders to the 
dollar’s crown — the euro and yuan — are actively touting enhanced global roles 
for their own currencies.

Chinese President Xi Jinping has called for “a powerful currency” with wide 
use in international trade, investment and foreign exchange markets, and the 
status of a “global reserve currency.” Technically, hisremarks 
<https://www.qstheory.cn/20260131/487aa5b5e0804f7ea968118e541b4e91/c.html> 
dated from 2024 (when, indeed, this newsletter picked up on astrong-yuan policy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-02-03/bloomberg-new-economy-the-promise-and-peril-of-china-s-strong-yuan-policy?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
). But the Communist Party’s recent publication of them speaks to the 
leadership’scurrent priority 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-02/china-highlights-xi-s-old-speech-on-risks-powerful-currency?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
.

As for the euro, this month the European Central Bank introduced its biggest 
step yet to promote the single currency, saying it’s prepared to offereuro 
liquidity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-14/ecb-revamps-euro-liquidity-offer-to-boost-currency-s-appeal?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 to most of the rest of the world. Keeping in mind that collective action is 
often an issue with Europe, it’s notable that euro-zone governments are also
backing an expansion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/euro-area-finance-chiefs-push-for-bigger-role-of-single-currency?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 of the euro’s global role, as is theEuropean Parliament 
<https://www.europarl.europa.eu/doceo/document/TA-10-2026-0034_EN.html>.

The thing is, however, that having a truly global currency has its downside, 
and neither Chinese nor European policymakers have suggested they’re ready to 
accept that, as detailed below. Most importantly, Beijing and Brussels have yet 
to show they’re prepared tomake the hard yards 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/will-the-eu-collapse-trump-le-pen-and-ukraine-raise-the-stakes?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 needed to reach their goals. Which all helps explain why, despite all the 
“sell America” rhetoric, global investors continue to stock up on
dollar-denominated assets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-18/foreigners-rebuff-sell-america-and-pour-in-a-net-1-5-trillion?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
.

“What’s the most important thing I think about every day in my job?” That’s 
what a corporate treasurer asked me and my Columbia University classmates 
during one session of an international business course long ago. 
International-affairs students as we were, none of us ventured an answer to the 
guest lecturer. “Here’s a hint: You drink it...” Still nothing. Finally he gave 
up and told us: “Liquidity!”

Naturally, a treasurer needs to be able to make payments to a company’s staff 
and suppliers when needed, while minimizing transaction costs. In the same way, 
being able to easily move in and out of a currency is asine qua non for a 
global currency. And on that score, the euro and yuan don’t compare favorably 
with the dollar.

As a place to park cash, the US Treasuries market is without parallel. First 
of all, at $30 trillion, it’s the world’s biggest bond market. It’s also — 
outside of a crisis — extremely liquid. Averagedaily trading 
<https://www.sifma.org/research/statistics/us-treasury-securities-statistics> 
this year has exceeded $1 trillion. That means anyone getting in or out of, 
say, six-month bills or two-year notes would be able to do that easily given 
the enormous demand for these securities on any given day.

Chinese government bonds of various types total around $12 trillion 
<https://www.pbc.gov.cn/diaochatongjisi/attachDir/2026/01/2026013017193550904.pdf>
, according to the central bank. That’s a fair amount of supply. But a foreign 
enterprise needs to register as a qualified investor, unless trading via Hong 
Kong. Trading volumes arelimited 
<https://cif.mofcom.gov.cn/cif/html/upload/20260104101631148_2025%E5%B9%B411%E6%9C%88%E4%BB%BD%E9%87%91%E8%9E%8D%E5%B8%82%E5%9C%BA%E8%BF%90%E8%A1%8C%E6%83%85%E5%86%B5.pdf?utm_source=chatgpt.com>
 relative to major developed markets, as banks tend to buy bonds and hold them 
over the long term rather than make markets for them. Moving money in and out 
of China is also subject to capital controls.

While Chinese bonds outperformed much of the rest of the world in recent 
years, foreign ownership has dropped off to just under $400 billion as of last 
month. By comparison, overseas investors held $9.3 trillion of Treasuries alone 
at last count (in December). Add in corporate and other types of bonds and the 
grand total handily exceeds $15 trillion.

Nurturing an active and liquid bond market is hardly an overnight endeavor. 
For China, it would likely need deep structural changes to the nation’s 
financial system, such as the build-out of a pension-fund industry, 
encouragement and safe regulation of money-market funds and other institutional 
heavyweights that would be regularly active in trading fixed income.

Beijing would also need to set capital controls aside 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/china-economists-urge-looser-capital-controls-as-dollar-wobbles?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 to assuage the concerns of people like the corporate treasurer who worried 
about liquidity.
European Central Bank President Christine LagardePhotographer: Alex 
Kraus/Bloomberg
Turning to Europe, where capital controls aren’t an issue, the main challenge 
is fragmentation. There are German government bonds, Italian ones, French ones 
— you get the picture. All that segmentation limits liquidity. German 
government securities saw€6.74 trillion 
<https://www.deutsche-finanzagentur.de/en/federal-securities/trading/secondary-market#:~:text=The%20market%20for%20German%20Government,27%20bn%20per%20trading%20day.>
 ($7.9 trillion) of trading in all of 2024. That’s less than two weeks worth of 
Treasuries trading that year.

European Central Bank policymakers are well aware of the problem. President 
Christine Lagarde said last year that “greater international prominence for our 
currency will not happen by default: Itmust be earned 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-17/global-euro-moment-needs-decisive-actions-ecb-s-lagarde-says?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
.” Europe could issue cross-national securities for the financing of public 
goods, like defense, she said.

Italy’s central bank chief, Fabio Panetta, said last week that “a European 
sovereign bond would enable European public goods to be adequately financed 
and, at the same time, provide investors a safe and liquidbenchmark asset 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/ecb-s-panetta-says-tariffs-have-damaged-the-us-more-than-others?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
.”

Even Joachim Nagel, the head of the central bank in Germany — which has 
traditionally opposed joint financing, given voters’ concerns about subsidizing 
the rest of Europe — said to Politico this month that “a moreliquid European 
market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/bundesbank-chief-nagel-calls-for-joint-eu-debt-politico-reports?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 when it comes to safe European assets would support” international investor 
interest.

What the ECB did unveil this month was a plan to let most foreign central 
banks swap bonds for euro cash via a repo facility. Certainly a helpful piece 
of infrastructure, much as the US Federal Reserve has. But as with China, a lot 
more major structural changes would be needed to construct a true rival to the 
dollar and Treasuries.

If and when China and Europe succeed with all that, then they need to reflect 
on Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent’s warning. As he told Bloomberg last July, 
“They should becareful what they wish for 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-03/bessent-rejects-worries-over-dollar-s-decline-diminishing-its-global-role?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
.”

Having a reserve currency also means sometimes living with currency 
appreciation that can cause economic stress. Yet China, which recorded a record 
$1.2 trillion trade surplus last year, just this week was taking steps tostem 
gains 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/china-seeks-to-slow-yuan-gains-by-cutting-cost-to-short-currency?cmpid=BBD022826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260228>
 in the yuan. And the European Union’s economy commissioner, Valdis 
Dombrovskis, cautioned last week that “in a context of a stronger international 
role for the euro, we also must consider what implications it would mean for 
exchange rate.”

To paraphrase John McCain’s quip 
<https://www.presidency.ucsb.edu/documents/republican-presidential-candidates-debate-johnston-iowa>
 from the 2000 presidential campaign, Beijing and Brussels are mostly “hat” 
with little “cattle” when it comes to the reserve currency debate. For now, 
anyway.—Chris Anstey
