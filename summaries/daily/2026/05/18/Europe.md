# European Daily Briefing — May 18, 2026

**UK politics dominates as bond markets test European nerves.** Wes Streeting, in his first public appearance since resigning as health secretary, used the platform to lay groundwork for a potential Labour leadership bid and argued Britain should rejoin the EU. Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham, who intends to contest a June by-election as a route to challenging Keir Starmer, echoed the case for EU re-engagement. Hedge funds and asset managers responded by piling into bearish sterling option bets last week, reflecting concern that a Burnham-led Labour could pursue looser fiscal policy. Separately, *The Sun* reported Chancellor Rachel Reeves is set to abandon a planned fuel-duty increase, a politically expedient move given oil-driven cost pressures but one that complicates the fiscal arithmetic at a sensitive moment for gilts.

**Bond stress framed the G-7 finance ministers' meeting in Paris.** A renewed selloff—led by Japanese government bonds but extending across global curves—overshadowed the agenda of addressing structural imbalances. French Finance Minister Roland Lescure characterised the move as "correcting, but I wouldn't go as far as to say it's collapsing," while ECB President Christine Lagarde acknowledged the gyrations fell within her remit. German Finance Minister Lars Klingbeil pressed partners to deepen cooperation on raw materials, energy and supply chains. With Brent above $110 and the Strait of Hormuz closure persisting, inflation expectations are repricing higher across the bloc, complicating the ECB's policy path even as Lescure maintained the eurozone faces slowdown rather than recession or stagflation.

**UK M&A activity stands in marked contrast to political dysfunction.** Bids targeting UK companies have risen more than 250% year-to-date to roughly $150 billion—the strongest pace since 2015—with Unilever, Schroders, Beazley, Intertek and Tate & Lyle among the names in play, as overseas acquirers exploit depressed sterling valuations.

**In corporate news**, Standard Chartered named former banking analyst Manus Costello as CFO, ending a months-long search following Diego De Giorgi's February departure. Publicis rose 2.4% in Paris after agreeing to acquire LiveRamp for approximately $2.5 billion. In Spain, the ruling Socialists suffered their worst-ever result in the country's largest region. Germany's climate advisory council warned the country is likely to miss its 2030 emissions targets by up to 100 million tons of CO2 following the coalition's decision to dilute the buildings-sector heating law.
