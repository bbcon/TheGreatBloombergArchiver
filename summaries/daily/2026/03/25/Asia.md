Xi Jinping's studied silence on the Iran conflict, now nearly a month old, is becoming a substantive strategic posture rather than mere diplomatic reticence. China's relative insulation from the energy shock — rooted in an accelerating renewables buildout, electric vehicles comprising roughly half of new car sales, and continued access to discounted Russian and Iranian crude as US sanctions ease — means Beijing faces fewer immediate economic pressures than its regional peers. Chinese port throughput through the first three weeks of March ran more than 6% ahead of year-ago levels, with AI-driven electronics exports sustaining momentum even as oil-related supply chain disruptions mount. South Korea's exports to China surged 69% in the first 20 days of March, with semiconductor shipments abroad up 164%, reflecting the deep integration of regional supply chains around AI infrastructure demand.

The picture is considerably less benign elsewhere in Asia. South Korea has stood up an emergency economic task force and its prime minister is pushing for a supplementary budget to cushion the energy shock. The Philippines has declared a national energy emergency, with Philippine Airlines warning of potential jet fuel rationing, and Vietnam's aviation authority is cutting flights ahead of anticipated shortages from early April. Japan has begun its largest emergency oil stockpile release on record. Signs of fuel hoarding are proliferating across the region, with China, South Korea, and Thailand all restricting some fuel exports — a dynamic that risks deepening market dislocations rather than alleviating them.

India faces a distinct diplomatic dilemma. As current chair of BRICS, New Delhi is under mounting pressure to steer the bloc toward a firmer collective position on the conflict, a task complicated by the group's membership spanning multiple sides of the dispute. BRICS has so far failed to adopt any unified stance. Prime Minister Modi discussed the Strait of Hormuz in a call with President Trump, underlining the degree to which the conflict is forcing India to navigate between its strategic partnerships and its energy vulnerabilities.
