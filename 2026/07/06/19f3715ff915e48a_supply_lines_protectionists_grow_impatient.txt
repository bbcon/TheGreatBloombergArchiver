For a forthcoming story I spent part of my leadup to America’s birthday party 
last week sitting down with a union man who has spent a career spanning decades 
fighting free trade.

I can’t tell you who he is because that might give away the story I’m still 
working on. But it’s safe to say he has great convictions on trade. On the wall 
of his wood-paneled office is an old red-white-and-blue poster with the simple 
declaration: “Free Traders Are Traitors.”

What was striking about the conversation we had, though, was his resignation 
at a moment when some peopleare celebrating 
<https://x.com/USTradeRep/status/2073052310566002896?s=20> an American 
industrial rebirth.

This man has spent more than three decades representing thousands of workers 
at a sprawling plant in a community whose entire history is centered on it. The 
local history museum, which I visited Friday before embarking on a long trip 
back to Washington, is dominated by the company’s story and paintings that 
celebrate the plant’s place in the town’s life. And yet that factory closed its 
doors in 2024 and no one locally believes it will reopen any time soon.

Why does any of that matter? 

We are halfway through 2026 and racing toward an election in which the economy 
and President Donald Trump’s policies affecting it from tariffs to immigration 
to a disruptive war are all on the minds of voters.

Factory Jobs

But Trump’s biggest economic promise has always been a tariff-driven 
resurgence in manufacturing. And the case for that actually happening remains 
weak. Import taxes have contributed to higher consumer prices. Yet there are 
actually 38,000 fewer people working in American factories than a year ago, 
according todata released <https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/MANEMP> last 
week. Which explains why some of Trump’s blue-collar supporters have doubts 
about his methods.

In that context we are also about to learn more about just how Trump and his 
trade team are rebuilding his tariff wall.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/trump-poised-to-roll-out-new-tariffs-as-he-refunds-the-old-ones?cmpid=BBD070626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6166>

On July 24 the 10% temporary global tariffs that Trump imposed after the 
Supreme Court in February declared the bulk of his duties illegal will reach 
their statutory 150-day limit. What exactly replaces them is a question 
concerning not just businesses and trading partners but economists and 
political strategists pondering the long arc of US trade policy and electoral 
politics.

An investigation into the enforcement of forced labor laws <> by trading 
partners has yielded recommendations of tariffs of 10% to 12.5% on goods from 
60 different economies. A hearing is scheduled for Tuesday in Washington with 
that likely to be followed quickly by a presidential recommendation to impose 
the duties.

But another investigation on a shorter list of 16 economies including China, 
India and the EU over their alleged industrial overcapacity has been slower to 
surface. Which means it’s unlikely that additional tariffs associated with that 
will be rolled out this month. So it’s worth asking whether the administration 
might wait until after the midterm elections before it goes ahead given how 
unpopular Trump’s tariffs are in the polls.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/trump-builds-a-new-us-tariff-wall-in-shakeup-of-winners-losers?cmpid=BBD070626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6166>

All of that is happening in the broader context of a debate over what American 
economic policy should look like that refuses to go away.

In 2016 Trump offered protectionism as a solution to the economic woes of 
American factory towns like the one I visited last week. A decade on with a 
mid-term election looming some of the union members who cheered him on are 
having doubts about how things have played out.

UMSCA Reviews

The union man I sat down with campaigned for Trump in 2024 in the belief that 
his tariffs would help give the shuttered factory a new life. But they didn’t. 
And so now he feels betrayed and not so sure about the path Trump is on. He’d 
just like to find someone who can give a new life to the old plant and jobs to 
his members.

Trump, of course, is continuing his campaign of trade disruption. Last week 
the US opted totrigger a provision 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/us-decides-against-renewing-usmca-shifting-to-rolling-talks?cmpid=BBD070626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6166>
 that subjects the North American trade pact Trump renegotiated during his 
first term to annual reviews, a move likely to increase uncertainty for 
businesses trying to make long-term plans and investments in the region.

Read More: Mexico’s Export-Led Economy at Risk With USMCA Reviews 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/mexico-s-export-led-economy-at-risk-with-annual-usmca-reviews?cmpid=BBD070626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6166>

But even those disruptions are less dramatic than once promised. Last week’s 
move doesn’t end the largely tariff-free commerce that has taken place since 
the North American Free Trade Agreement went into effect in the 1990s. It’s a 
long way from Trump’s 2016 campaign promises to kill NAFTA altogether. If 
nothing further changes (and something probably will given the economic 
importance of the pact) Trump’s NAFTA update won’t expire until 2036.

We are living through a giant economic experiment while contemplating the end 
of global orders and the consequences. The reality, though, is that despite the 
certainty you often hear in Washington about this moment’s importance, many 
Americans aren’t convinced they are benefiting.

Which means you can sit down with a union man with proud anti-free trade 
credentials and be surprised. A protectionist may be in charge but that union 
man still isn’t sure anyone in Washington is delivering what he needs.
