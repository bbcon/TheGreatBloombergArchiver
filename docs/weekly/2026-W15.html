Weekly Brief

Apr 06 – Apr 12, 2026

US

  • US equity markets posted a seven-session winning streak by Friday, the longest since October, as the Iran ceasefire supported a broad risk-on recovery, with high-yield spreads returning to pre-war levels.
  • March CPI is expected to show the sharpest monthly headline increase since 2022, driven by a roughly $1-per-gallon gasoline surge, complicating Federal Reserve policy at a moment when labor markets are softening.
  • Fed funds futures remain priced for zero cuts in 2026, a stark reversal from two cuts fully priced before hostilities began, with swaps implying inflation above 3% over the next twelve months.
  • The Supreme Court's invalidation of emergency-powers tariffs and a Court of International Trade order mandating $160 billion in refunds to importers represent a significant fiscal and legal development that received less attention than geopolitical headlines.
  • An unusual joint action by Fed Chair Powell and Treasury Secretary Bessent summoned Wall Street CEOs to an emergency meeting over concerns that Anthropic's Mythos AI model could enable a new generation of cyberattacks, signaling that regulators are treating advanced AI as an emerging systemic risk.

Labor Market and Inflation Dynamics

Friday's March non-farm payrolls, released during the Good Friday holiday, showed a gain of 178,000 — the strongest print since 2024 — and the Sahm Rule currently shows no recessionary signal. The underlying picture is more nuanced, however. Wage growth is slowing across multiple measures, JOLTS hiring is near post-GFC lows, quit rates are declining, and average unemployment duration has re-extended above six months. The composite portrait is one of a labor market softening gradually rather than collapsing, consistent with a pre-recessionary plateau rather than durable resilience. Against that backdrop, March CPI — due Friday — is expected to show a roughly 0.9–1.0% month-on-month headline gain, the sharpest reading since June 2022, driven principally by gasoline's move above $4 per gallon for the first time since 2022. Retail diesel has risen nearly 50% to $5.62 per gallon, and the Logistics Manager's Index climbed to 65.7 in March, its highest since May 2022. The ISM non-manufacturing survey reinforced the concern, with more than 70% of purchasing managers reporting rising input prices — the largest monthly increase in 13 years.

Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy

The Fed's March meeting minutes, released Wednesday, offered insight into how policymakers are weighing the energy shock against mixed labor signals, though the core policy dilemma remains unresolved. Fed Vice Chair Philip Jefferson acknowledged the Iran conflict will push near-term inflation higher while characterizing current policy as appropriate. Bloomberg Economics now projects only two cuts in 2026, down from four forecast in January, and Fed funds futures have effectively priced out easing entirely. The five-year/five-year inflation breakeven remains below 2.2%, suggesting markets tentatively treat the energy shock as transitory, but that assessment is fragile and contingent on whether the Strait of Hormuz reopens in practice. Kevin Warsh's confirmation as incoming Fed chair remains pending in the Senate. The February core PCE data point — due Thursday and expected to show a 0.4% monthly gain keeping the year-on-year rate above 3% — will provide an additional read on whether the inflation impulse is broadening beyond energy.

Equity Markets and Corporate Developments

US equities navigated a turbulent week with notable resilience. The S&P 500 oscillated sharply around the ceasefire announcement — futures gained 2.6% on Wednesday and the index recorded its longest winning streak since October — while sector rotation was pronounced. Health insurers rallied sharply Monday after CMS proposed a 2.5% Medicare Advantage rate increase for 2027, with Humana gaining 11% and UnitedHealth rising 7%. Semiconductor and technology names benefited disproportionately from the ceasefire, with Micron rising nearly 10% and Western Digital gaining 8.3% on expectations of restored helium supply chains. Energy names moved in the opposite direction, with Exxon Mobil down 5.8% and Occidental falling 8.1% on ceasefire day. An index of SaaS stocks fell 4.8% on Thursday to its lowest in over three years, extending a roughly 40% decline in 2026, as Anthropic's Claude Managed Agents release and a new Meta model renewed fears that AI agents will erode incumbent software competitive moats. FactSet projects S&P 500 first-quarter earnings growth of approximately 12.5% year-on-year, with Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase results due next week. SpaceX has begun investor meetings for a potential IPO that could value the company above $2 trillion, and Morgan Stanley launched a Bitcoin-tracking ETF under the ticker MSBT, underscoring the asset class's institutional entrenchment even as Bitcoin trades near $71,000, roughly 40% below its October peak. ---

Asia

  • China's CSI 300 advanced 3.5% on Wednesday following the ceasefire announcement, though optimism faded quickly, with the index giving back 0.6% on Thursday as oil prices partially rebounded and the structural energy import burden reasserted itself.

  • China's factory-gate prices exited deflation in March, with PPI rising 0.5% year-on-year after a 0.9% decline in February, though Capital Economics cautioned the turn may not persist.

China: Policy Reorientation and Trade Dynamics

China's economic policy framework appears to be undergoing a meaningful structural shift. Rather than deploying demand-side stimulus in response to the energy cost surge triggered by the Iran conflict, Beijing is firmly committed to a supply-side reorientation articulated in its latest Five-Year Plan. State support is being directed toward technology firms and "new productive forces" rather than infrastructure or consumer transfer payments — a posture that Premier Li Qiang reinforced at last month's China Development Forum by rejecting external criticism of China's trade surpluses and signaling no course correction. Urban private-sector wage growth fell below 1% by end-2025, against a pre-pandemic rate of approximately 8%, and the absence of numerical consumption targets in the Five-Year Plan reinforces the view that domestic demand will remain structurally subdued. The implication — that China's already-record trade surplus is likely to persist or widen — sits in direct tension with rebalancing calls from Washington and Paris. On a more constructive note, EV and hybrid exports surged 140% year-on-year in March to a record 349,000 units, with BYD, Geely, and Chery leading shipments, though BYD's domestic sales fell more than 40%, illustrating the continued bifurcation between export competitiveness and subdued internal demand.

China: Energy, Finance, and Geopolitics

Beijing granted independent teapot refiners additional crude import quotas to sustain domestic fuel production, a practical measure revealing the extent to which China's energy security was stretched by the Hormuz closure. BloombergNEF projects Chinese LNG imports will fall to 62.3 million tons in 2026 from 68.4 million tons last year — a rare decline in nearly two decades of near-uninterrupted growth — with elevated prices and supply risks expected to persist regardless of ceasefire terms. China's central bank purchased its largest gold tranche in more than a year in March, consistent with continued reserve diversification. On the geopolitical front, Taiwan's KMT chair began a six-day Beijing visit Monday — later culminating in the Friday Xi meeting — with Xi calling for "cooperation and peace" while simultaneously reaffirming sovereignty claims, a combination calibrated to demonstrate to Washington that Taipei contains significant constituencies favoring engagement. The KMT has stalled a $40 billion Taiwanese defense expansion, and a $14 billion US arms deal has not received Trump's approval. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi also traveled to North Korea for the first time in six years, reflecting Beijing's effort to consolidate relationships across shifting geopolitical alignments.

Japan, India, and Regional Dynamics

Japan's Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi marks six months in office this week, a tenure that has delivered a credible pivot away from deflation and a sustained rise in JGB yields, though the galvanizing equity effect of her election has been largely offset by the Iran war shock. Real wages rose at the fastest pace since 2021, supporting the case for continued Bank of Japan normalization, but consumer confidence fell in March by the most since the early stages of the Covid pandemic, and the yen continues to weaken despite higher domestic yields — a divergence that appears increasingly unstable. Seven & i Holdings delayed the planned US listing of its convenience store operations, sending shares down 4.6% on Thursday. In India, the Reserve Bank held rates unchanged Wednesday, with Governor Sanjay Malhotra acknowledging the ceasefire while cautioning that supply chain disruptions continue to pose risks. The rupee strengthened 0.5% following his remarks, partially reversing a roughly 7% decline over the past year. State Bank of India separately disclosed approximately $5 billion in speculator positions against the rupee caught by the regulator's crackdown, with estimated losses of around $32 million from forced unwinds. India's record equity outflow pace and visible fuel shortages on the state election campaign trail indicate the energy shock is transmitting broadly into domestic politics. ---

ECB Policy and Eurozone Macro

The European Central Bank's policy path has shifted materially over the course of this week. Economists now view the next move as a rate increase rather than a cut, as energy-driven inflation from the Middle East conflict reasserts itself across the eurozone. The euro area's five-year/five-year inflation breakeven has risen, though it remains below 2% and below comparable US readings — a differential that reflects Europe's somewhat more anchored medium-term expectations but does not eliminate the tightening pressure. German industrial production data due Thursday arrived in the context of pre-existing structural weakness, with declines recorded even before the onset of hostilities, suggesting the energy shock is compounding rather than causing a cyclical downturn. The EU's senior economy official explicitly warned of stagflation risk despite the ceasefire, a concern that eurozone services PMI data due Tuesday and retail sales and PPI figures due Wednesday will help calibrate. Earnings estimate revisions have turned negative for European equities even as US estimates hold in positive territory — a reversal from the earlier-year narrative in which European stocks were outperforming on fiscal stimulus and ECB easing expectations.

UK: Politics, Markets, and Diplomacy

Britain's political and economic week was unusually eventful. Prime Minister Starmer's Gulf trip Wednesday positioned the UK as an active multilateral diplomatic actor on Hormuz, chairing a 35-nation coordination meeting in the absence of the US — a deliberate differentiation from Washington's posture that Starmer has also deployed as a domestic political asset ahead of May local elections, criticizing both Reform UK's Nigel Farage and Conservative leader Kemi Badenoch for their initial support of the US-Israeli campaign. The political strategy faces headwinds, however: Labour has slipped to fourth place in national polling, a notable deterioration. The economic backdrop is challenging — real wages are stagnating, consumer prices remain elevated, and UK mortgage rates rose above 5% in March, with homebuyer demand falling to its lowest since August 2023. The Bank of England, previously expected to cut, is now seen holding. UK fuel exposure is structurally significant: BloombergNEF identified Britain and France as the European markets most exposed to diesel and kerosene supply disruption, given reliance on refined product imports from Kuwait and Saudi Arabia respectively. On a more constructive note, UK EV registrations hit a record 86,120 units in March, illustrating that the energy shock is simultaneously pressuring affordability and accelerating electrification.

Energy Markets and the Hormuz Crisis

The defining macro variable of April 2026 is the Strait of Hormuz, which has been effectively closed since the US-Israeli offensive against Iran began on February 28. The waterway normally channels approximately one-fifth of global oil and LNG supply; the EIA estimates the conflict has removed approximately 314 million barrels from global supply to date — roughly 80% of the IEA's record emergency reserve release. Brent crude's prompt spread widened above $10 per barrel in backwardation early in the week, surpassing peaks reached after Russia's invasion of Ukraine, before the ceasefire announcement on Wednesday delivered Brent's sharpest single-day fall since April 2020. WTI tumbled nearly 19% on Wednesday before recovering approximately 4% on Thursday as Tehran alleged ceasefire violations and Israeli strikes in Lebanon intensified. By Friday, WTI held near $99 per barrel, on track for its steepest weekly loss in nine months but still commanding a material premium above pre-conflict levels. OPEC+ has warned that damage to Middle Eastern energy infrastructure will produce prolonged supply disruption extending well beyond any eventual ceasefire. Saudi Arabia separately disclosed that Iranian attacks have impaired its own production capacity, providing a partial floor to prices. Goldman Sachs has flagged Brent averaging above $100 through 2026 if the Strait remains closed for another month. Japan is reportedly considering releasing the equivalent of twenty days of consumption from strategic reserves.

Ceasefire, Diplomacy, and Geopolitical Reconfiguration

The US-Iran two-week ceasefire, brokered with Pakistan as intermediary and announced Tuesday evening, has provided intermittent relief to markets without resolving the underlying conflict. The historical parallel to the 1991 Kuwait crisis — in which oil more than doubled before returning to pre-crisis levels within twelve months — is instructive, though the current episode appears to be resolving more rapidly. Several Arab Gulf states reported continued Iranian missile attacks in the immediate aftermath of the ceasefire announcement, and both Kuwait and the UAE subsequently reported fresh strikes, underscoring the arrangement's fragility. Iran's stated intent to levy fees on vessels transiting the Strait introduces a novel dynamic that, if implemented, would effectively transform an international waterway into a controlled one. China's role in persuading Iran to accept a ceasefire — credited by Iranian officials to a last-minute Beijing push — illustrates the growing influence Beijing derives from its $270 billion financial footprint across the Gulf. Xi's position is nuanced: quietly facilitating de-escalation while avoiding overt alignment with either side, constrained by the same Gulf relationships that give him leverage. French President Macron publicly called on medium powers to assert greater independence from Washington, and not a single NATO member or Gulf ally directly joined the US-Israeli campaign — a structural erosion of the alliance architecture that predates this conflict but has been materially accelerated by it.

Global Monetary Policy and Financial Conditions

The conflict's most durable macroeconomic legacy may be the repricing of global monetary policy trajectories. The Fed, ECB, and Bank of England are all navigating an asymmetric pressure: energy-driven inflation constrains easing while decelerating growth limits tightening. The Fed Reserve Bank of New York's Global Supply Chain Pressure Index rose in March to its highest since January 2023. Foreign central bank holdings at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York fell approximately $82 billion since late February to $2.7 trillion — the lowest since 2012 — as Gulf producers liquidate Treasuries to defend currencies and fund wartime expenditures, representing a structural erosion of the petrodollar recycling mechanism. Emerging market capital flows, which had reached near two-decade highs earlier in 2026, suffered approximately $60 billion in equity outflows during the crisis, with the shock differentiated primarily by energy import dependence. Wednesday's ceasefire rally partially reversed those outflows. The dollar has risen roughly 2.2% since hostilities began, partially reversing an 8%-plus decline in 2025, though analysts characterize the move as reflecting safe-haven flows rather than a structural shift. The IMF is poised to cut its global growth forecasts, while the World Bank has urged governments to keep any fiscal response transparent, temporary, and targeted. The energy shock is simultaneously generating a coal renaissance in Asia — as LNG alternatives become unavailable — and accelerating EV adoption, illustrating the conflict's contradictory implications for the energy transition.
