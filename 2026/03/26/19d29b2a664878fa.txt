Traders are hoarding cash

Financial markets may be underpricing the risk that oil prices spike higher 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/blackrock-s-kapito-warns-investors-are-mispricing-iran-war-risks?cmpid=032626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
, denting growth and boosting inflation, even if the war in Iran ends shortly, 
says BlackRock President Rob Kapito.

After all, the S&P 500 has lost less than 5% since the war began almost a 
month ago, hardly a major selloff.

Ben Carlson at Ritholtz Wealth Management has a decent counter-argument to 
that.He points to April 9 last year, when the S&P 500 surged 9.5% after Trump 
showed signs of relenting on his tariff proposals.

“I’m convinced that one day is the only reason markets haven’t gone down more 
on the war/oil crisis,” Carlsonwrote on X 
<https://x.com/awealthofcs/status/2036839020890558937?s=20>. “Everyone who 
wants to be bearish is terrified of that scenario.”

That just about sums up where we are with markets right now. If the Strait of 
Hormuz stays shut, like it largely is now, the consequences on the global 
economy will be severe and stagflationary.

But a sudden peace accord and reopening the waterway would trigger another 
rapid market turnaround. That’s why many investors are holding cash, rather 
than taking big bets in either direction.

Examples of the rush to cash include:

 * Nomura’s Charlie McElligott says macro hedge fund traders he’s talked to in 
the past week or so indicated they’ve been told to stand down and avoid risk 
for now.
 * JPMorgan strategists led by Nikolaos Panigirtzoglou say there’s been a 
spike in cash allocations over the past month as investors ditch equities, 
bonds and gold.
 * Bank of America’s last fund manager survey 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/stock-market-euphoria-fades-as-investors-turn-bearish-bofa-says?cmpid=032626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
 showed the biggest jump in cash since March 2020, with holdings reaching 4.3% 
in March.
 * Similarly, Algebris Investments, a heavyweight in European bank debt, has 
increased cash levels in its credit funds 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/algebris-ramps-up-cash-warning-of-further-disruptions-from-war?cmpid=032626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
 to some of their highest levels ever, warning that markets are underplaying 
risks from the war.
But Wall Street is publishing plenty of research with subject lines like 
“Climbing a wall of worry” from Barclays’ Ajay Rajadhyaksha. That suggests many 
traders are still looking for a rebound, rather than going all-in on the 
bearish trades. —Paul Dobson
