**Strait of Hormuz: Costly Stasis, Tentative Resolution**

The trajectory of US-Iran negotiations remained the single most consequential variable for global markets across the week. The sequence illustrated the gap between headline optimism and operational reality: Monday's reports of a potential deal drove Brent crude down more than 7% in thin holiday trading; fresh US Central Command strikes on Iranian missile launch sites and mine-laying vessels Wednesday pushed prices back toward $97; and Thursday's tentative agreement on a 60-day ceasefire extension produced a further leg down that left Brent on course for its largest monthly decline since 2020. Trump has not yet formally approved the terms, and Iran's Foreign Ministry earlier in the week explicitly stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent." Secretary Rubio acknowledged talks could take several more days. Republican hawks including Senator Graham have argued the emerging terms concede too much to Tehran, while Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei declared that US military bases in the region will no longer be safe — language that complicates any clean resolution. The longer the stasis continues, the greater the risk that second-order economic effects — food and energy inflation, shipping cost pass-through — accumulate to a degree that becomes politically difficult to manage even without a market-based forcing event. China appears to be playing a quiet but consequential mediation role, with Pakistani Army Chief Munir shuttling between Beijing and Doha; the reported possibility of Iran's highly enriched uranium being transferred to China would represent an unusual expansion of Beijing's direct involvement in Middle Eastern security arrangements.

**AI Capital Flows and the Infrastructure Supercycle**

The scale of capital mobilizing around artificial intelligence infrastructure approached macro-relevant magnitudes this week. Anthropic's $65 billion fundraise at a $965 billion valuation — eclipsing OpenAI for the first time — was accompanied by a $36 billion private credit facility assembled by Apollo and Blackstone to finance AI chip procurement, effectively transforming advanced semiconductors into a financeable asset class with characteristics more akin to infrastructure than venture capital. ByteDance is in discussions around $70 billion in capital expenditure for 2026. Taiwan's tech firms are borrowing a record $14.5 billion. The Quad — US, Japan, India, Australia — committed up to $20 billion in support for critical-minerals supply chain development. These flows are compressing risk premia in private credit, concentrating equity gains in AI-adjacent equities globally, and creating feedback loops between chip demand, energy infrastructure requirements, and sovereign growth forecasts that are still being incorporated into official projections.

**Russia: War Economy Under Strain**

Russia's economy presents an unusual configuration: a wartime state running relatively orthodox macroeconomic policy. The Bank of Russia's benchmark rate at 14.5% implies real rates above 9% given sub-6% consumer price inflation — among the highest real rates globally. The Economy Ministry cut its 2026 growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3%, with the non-oil private sector — roughly three-quarters of the economy — stalling under tight monetary conditions and a labor shortfall estimated at approximately 1.5 million workers. Russia's mid-week use of a hypersonic Oreshnik ballistic missile in a mass strike on Ukrainian cities represents a continued escalation in the quality of munitions deployed even as front lines remain relatively static. Foreign Minister Lavrov's call to Secretary Rubio advising evacuation of US personnel from Kyiv signals Moscow's intent to maintain pressure on Ukrainian decision-making infrastructure. Russia's consideration of restricting diesel and jet fuel exports — a potential response to Ukrainian drone strikes on refinery infrastructure — would add a further layer of complexity to already strained global energy markets.

**Geopolitical Architecture in Realignment**

Beyond the primary theater of the Iran conflict, several developments this week signal a global order in active reconfiguration. The Pentagon's Indo-Pacific command report frames Taiwan preparedness through the same maritime chokepoint lens sharpened by the Hormuz experience, requesting billions for asymmetric sea-denial capabilities with a 2027 urgency benchmark. China expelled a Dutch naval frigate near the Paracel Islands Wednesday while coast guard activity around the Spratlys intensified, maintaining persistent Indo-Pacific friction beneath the headlines. The China-Canada diplomatic rapprochement — Wang Yi's first Ottawa visit in a decade — reflects Beijing's deliberate effort to widen commercial relationships in the Western Hemisphere as the US-China strategic competition intensifies. Russia's GDP contraction and the Romanian drone incident on Thursday, which Bucharest called a "serious and irresponsible escalation," collectively underscore that the global geopolitical risk premium, while partly priced through energy markets, carries dimensions in equity and credit valuations that appear underweighted relative to the breadth of active stress points.
