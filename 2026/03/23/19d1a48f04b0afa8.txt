Bond wipeout

The specter of stagflation caused by the Iran war has wiped out more than $2.5 
trillion from the value of global bonds <bbg://news/stories/TCBTZPKK3NY8> in 
March, on track for the biggest monthly loss in more than three years.

Bonds are tumbling as a surge in oil prices quickens inflation, which erodes 
the value of the fixed payments from debt. While the slide is less than the 
roughly $11.5 trillion lost in global equities, it’s perhaps more unexpected.
That’s because investors perceive debt, especially government bonds, as a haven 
in times of geopolitical turmoil.

Notable bond-market milestones:

 * US yields are perched at their highest in months after a third straight 
week of bond losses, with traders positioning for the possibility of a Fed 
interest-rate increase. Short-term notes led last week’s rout, with two-year 
yields climbing 17 basis points to 3.89%.
 * UK government bonds are headed for their worst month since the historic 
selloff 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/gilts-face-worst-month-since-truss-as-oil-spike-jolts-uk-market?cmpid=032326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
 that led to the ouster of Prime Minister Liz Truss. The yield on two-year 
gilts jumped today to its highest since February 2024, approaching 4.7%.
 * Australia’s 10-year government bond yields climbed today to the highest 
level since 2011.
“It is pandemonium out here,” said Ed Al-Hussainy of Columbia Threadneedle

Stocks’ reversal of fortunes

Investors are rapidly unwinding one of their favorite trades of the past few 
months, in which they preferred almost any stock market outside the US.

Since the US and Israel attacked Iran on Feb. 28, US shares have outperformed 
markets in Europe and Asia. The thinking is that the US, as a major producer of 
oil and gas, will be more insulated from the surge in energy prices than other 
countries.

The Stoxx Europe 600 Index is down 11% from its February peak 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/european-stocks-sink-into-correction-on-trump-s-iran-escalation?cmpid=032326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
, surpassing the 10% drop that technical analysts consider a “correction.” The 
MSCI index tracking emerging-market equities, which just before the war broke 
out was up 15% for the year, is on the verge of erasing all of those gains.

A similar dynamic is playing out in currency markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/hedge-funds-feed-dollar-gains-as-us-iran-exchange-threats?cmpid=032326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
: The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index has jumped 2.2% since the start of the war as 
investors rush to safe-haven assets.

“In real terms, the US economy is still looking better insulated against the 
energy shock, which we expect to support the USD over coming weeks,” said Jens 
Naervig Pedersen at Danske Bank. —Phil Serafino
