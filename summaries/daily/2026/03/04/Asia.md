Asian equity markets suffered their most severe week in nearly a year, with the region bearing a disproportionate share of the global fallout from the US-Iran conflict. South Korea's Kospi index fell 12% on Wednesday — the largest single-day decline in the index's history — compounding a 7.2% drop the prior session. The severity reflects both fundamental vulnerability and the unwinding of an overleveraged rally: the Kospi had risen roughly 50% this year, fueled by AI-driven optimism around chipmakers, retail participation on margin, and active encouragement from President Lee Jae Myung's administration, which had made crossing the 5,000-point threshold a political objective. Samsung Electronics fell 12%, SK Hynix declined approximately 10%, and Hyundai Motor lost 16%. Bank of Korea Governor Rhee Chang Yong delayed travel to Bangkok to convene an emergency meeting, while President Lee, abroad in the Philippines, announced an extraordinary cabinet session. The political consequences for Lee appear significant — his identification with the rally has turned a market rout into a direct test of his credibility.

Thailand's benchmark fell as much as 8.6%, and Taiwan's main index declined over 4%. The Indian rupee fell to a record low. Asia's vulnerability is structural: the region depends overwhelmingly on imported oil and gas, meaning a prolonged Hormuz disruption transmits directly into energy import bills, inflation, and current account deterioration. Capital is also flowing out of the region and into dollar assets, with the Bloomberg dollar index up 1.4% on the week.

China's National People's Congress convenes Thursday, where Xi Jinping's government is expected to unveil the 15th Five-Year Plan. The plan is anticipated to include a commitment to raise household consumption from roughly 41% of GDP toward 46% by 2030 — a more explicit pivot than prior plans. Fixed-asset investment contracted in 2025 for the first time in over three decades, and official PMI data released Wednesday showed factory activity contracting more than forecast. Chinese financial institutions are meanwhile scaling back exposure to Middle Eastern debt at regulatory direction.
