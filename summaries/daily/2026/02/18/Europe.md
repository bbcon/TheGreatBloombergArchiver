The most consequential European development of the day was the Financial Times report that ECB President Christine Lagarde is expected to depart before her mandate expires in October 2027, with the timing calibrated around the French presidential election in April 2027. An ECB spokesperson confirmed that Lagarde "has not taken any decision regarding the end of her term," preserving optionality, but the market implications of an early transition are nonetheless worth considering. An earlier-than-scheduled departure would allow French President Macron and German Chancellor Friedrich Merz to coordinate on her successor — a politically significant dynamic given the ECB's evolving role in an environment of fiscal expansion, German rearmament, and ongoing disinflation. The identity and orientation of the next ECB president will bear directly on the pace of any further easing and on the institution's longer-term credibility framework.

European equities set fresh records, with the Stoxx Europe 600 gaining 0.97% to 627.32, continuing a run that reflects a meaningful reallocation away from US assets. Fund manager positioning data from BofA shows the largest-ever overweight on the euro versus the dollar, and the shift appears driven by a combination of dollar weakness, relative valuation, and investor hedging against a potential AI-related correction in US tech. On the corporate side, Bayer agreed to pay more than $7 billion to resolve Roundup cancer litigation, a settlement that may finally close a years-long legal overhang. Carrefour fell 5% after disappointing full-year results, while Raspberry Pi surged 33% on AI agent-driven demand expectations. UK unemployment reached its highest level since the pandemic and youth unemployment rose above the EU average for the first time, adding to pressure on the Bank of England ahead of its next policy decision.
