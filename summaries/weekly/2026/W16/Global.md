**US-Iran Conflict: The Gap Between Diplomacy and Operational Reality**
The week opened with the aftermath of collapsed US-Iran talks in Islamabad and President Trump's announcement of a full naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — a significant escalation following US and Israeli strikes on Iran on February 28. By the week's end, Trump expressed optimism about a permanent ceasefire, claiming Iran had conceded on nuclear ambitions and Hormuz access, though Tehran has not publicly confirmed those terms. The ceasefire entered its second week, a second round of talks is being arranged, and a two-week extension is reportedly under consideration. Yet the gap between diplomatic tone and operational reality remains substantial: the Strait remains effectively closed, with only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker completing an exit with crude cargo since the blockade's formal imposition. A US-sanctioned Chinese-linked tanker transited the Strait on Tuesday, directly testing enforcement credibility. Iran warned that "no port in the Persian Gulf and Sea of Oman will be safe" if its own facilities are targeted. Gulf Arab and European officials believe a durable agreement is more likely six months away than within weeks.

**Energy Markets: Volatility Moderating, Structural Damage Accumulating**
Brent crude rose as much as 9.1% to approach $104 on Monday's Asian open before retreating toward $95–98 as peace signals accumulated through the week. WTI traded near $92–93 by week's end. Price volatility has cooled markedly from the record $38 intraday range seen in mid-March, with Brent trading in approximately a $10 band this week. European natural gas futures spiked nearly 18% at one point on Monday before partially recovering. The IEA issued a striking forecast: global oil demand is expected to decline in 2026 for the first time since the pandemic, as the price surge offsets underlying demand growth. Industrial metals diverged from the calming oil picture — aluminum reached four-year highs above $3,600 per ton on Thursday, with JPMorgan characterizing the market as entering a structural supply "black hole" following Iranian strikes on Gulf smelters and forecasting prices could approach $4,000 per ton.

**Global Growth: Deteriorating Baseline**
The IMF-World Bank Spring Meetings in Washington provided a sobering macro backdrop. The Fund cut its global growth forecast, with Bloomberg Economics' base case now at 2.9% for 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — and a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 a barrel. Global trade volume growth is projected at 2.8%, below global GDP growth of 3.1%. Qatar's Finance Minister described current market conditions as "the tip of the iceberg," and participants broadly expect structural consequences — higher costs, longer trade routes, elevated geopolitical risk premiums — to persist well beyond any ceasefire. Economist Carmen Reinhart argued the world is less prepared to respond to a crisis than at any point in the past two decades, constrained by a $348 trillion global debt burden, central banks still contending with inflation, and a fragmented multilateral order. Panama Canal congestion reached its worst since the 2023–2024 drought, with vessels facing 3.5-day waits. The Port of Los Angeles chief estimated full shipping normalization will take approximately four and a half months even after the Strait reopens.

**Food Security and Fertilizer**
Food security represents a slower-moving but potentially consequential transmission channel. Gulf-region countries account for at least 30% of global fertilizer supply, including urea, ammonia, and diammonium phosphate. A Farm Bureau survey found more than 70% of US farmers cannot currently afford sufficient fertilizer at prevailing prices. Price effects will emerge with a harvest-cycle lag, and a prolonged conflict risks compounding input cost inflation with yield reductions. The US Justice Department has opened a probe into fertilizer and farm input pricing practices. Global Trade Alert has tracked more than 250 distinct government policy responses since hostilities began, the large majority being liberalizing import measures, energy subsidies, or demand-reduction programs — a notably broad-based response reflecting the diffuse nature of the supply disruption.

**Longer-Run Structural Shifts**
The conflict is accelerating several structural transitions that were already underway. LNG's credibility as a long-term energy solution for emerging Asia has been materially set back — a second price shock in four years is accelerating diversification toward renewables, domestic gas, and coal across Vietnam, Pakistan, and elsewhere, with implications for Big Oil's strategy in developing markets. The geothermal case in Europe is gaining institutional momentum, with more than 60 companies urging EU leaders to strengthen support for a domestic, dispatchable, and infrastructure-resilient technology. Delta Air Lines quietly downgraded its net-zero emissions goal to an "aspiration" and removed its 2030 sustainable aviation fuel target. Anthropic's Mythos AI model — described as capable of identifying system vulnerabilities and orchestrating cyberattacks — emerged as an unexpected focal point at the Spring Meetings, with central bank governors from the ECB, Bank of England, and New York Fed all raising the issue publicly, reflecting institutional alarm at a moment of already-strained coordination capacity.
