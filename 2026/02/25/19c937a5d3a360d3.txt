Donald Trump said he will “never allow” Iran to have a nuclear weapon 
<https://bloom.bg/4u1P4Er> in his State of the Union address, while also 
reiterating his desire for a diplomatic solution to defuse tensions with 
Tehran. The president staged his version ofa pep rally for a beleaguered nation 
<https://bloom.bg/4kURBfr>, declaring the nation is “back bigger, better, 
richer and stronger than ever before,” at a time when skepticism of his 
policies and economic are growing.

Europe’s largest bank HSBC reported full-year earnings that surpassed 
expectations <https://bloom.bg/4kTQDjv>, helped by its wealth division. CEO 
Georges Elhedery told Bloomberg TV he was “very pleased” and the bank is 
confident about meeting upgraded targets. Thebank’s bonus pool 
<https://bloom.bg/4kQxvmq> rose 10% to $3.93 billion, the highest in at least a 
decade. Bad luck for thetwo top HSBC traders who left <https://bloom.bg/4kQwLxE>

 Deep Dive: Spain’s Housing Chaos 


 <https://bloom.bg/4tT977T> A pedestrian walks along a street of residential 
apartment buildings in Madrid, Spain.Photographer: Manaure Quintero/Bloomberg 
Homeowners are the next losers in Spain’s housing crisis as government 
protections—which are up for another one-year extension this week—havepitted 
squatters against stranded owners <https://bloom.bg/4tT977T>.

 * The measure to forbid evictions of vulnerable tenants has created 
incentives for strapped tenants to stop paying, critics say. 
 * Tens of thousands of desperate owners are looking for an exit path as they 
list their illegally occupied properties for sale, even at a discount. 
 * As tensions rise, a new kind of service company has emerged. For a few 
thousand euros, they promise to get people out of occupied properties within 
days or weeks.  <https://bloom.bg/4tT977T>

 Opinion 



Mentions of “net zero” have collapsed in rich nations’ 
<https://bloom.bg/4aOkPaZ> energy communiques, and with global demand for oil, 
natural gas and coal at an all-time high, the 2050 target now looks effectively 
dead, writes Javier Blas. Renewables will keep growing and emissions may bend 
toward 25,000-30,000 million metric tons by 2050, but that’s well above zero.
