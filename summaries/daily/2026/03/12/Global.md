The central dynamic in global markets on March 12 is an oil shock of a scale that stress-tests existing crisis management tools. Brent crude briefly exceeded $100 per barrel again in London after Iran escalated attacks on parts of Dubai and struck two oil tankers in Iraqi waters. Oman evacuated ships from one of the last viable regional export ports, and Iraq halted terminal operations. The IEA announced a record 400-million-barrel release from emergency stockpiles — exceeding any prior intervention, including during the 2011 Libyan war and the 2022 Ukraine invasion — yet the announcement provided only temporary relief. The IEA itself estimates the Strait of Hormuz closure has cut the 20 million barrels per day normally transiting the corridor by approximately 90%, forcing Gulf producers to shut in 10 million barrels per day of output. At the higher end of disruption estimates cited by analysts, the released reserves cover roughly 25 days of the supply shortfall, while prediction markets now assign a 52% probability that the conflict extends beyond April 30.

Iran's ceasefire conditions — a permanent pledge from both the US and Israel never to strike again — appear incompatible with either government's stated positions, leaving the diplomatic path narrow. Russia is exploiting the impasse adeptly: Putin has maintained a communication channel with Trump while simultaneously, according to reporting, providing intelligence to Iran to help target US forces. The fiscal windfall from elevated oil and gas prices is providing meaningful relief to Russia's war budget, while the diversion of US political attention reduces pressure on the Ukraine front.

The global aviation network has absorbed one of its largest shocks since the pandemic, with over 46,000 flights canceled as Asia-Europe routes are severely disrupted. Economy-class round-trip fares from Sydney to London have increased more than 80%. Bond markets globally have surrendered their year-to-date gains, and the selloff is explicitly being described by investors as a fiscal credibility story rather than a purely inflation-driven one — reflecting the intersection of war financing costs, energy subsidies, and already-elevated sovereign debt levels across major economies.
