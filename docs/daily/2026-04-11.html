Asia

China's economic policy framework is undergoing a structural shift that carries significant implications for global trade and capital allocation. Rather than deploying another round of demand-side stimulus in response to the energy-cost surge triggered by the Iran conflict, Beijing appears to be firmly committed to a supply-side reorientation articulated in its latest Five-Year Plan. The message, as Dong Tao of Springs Capital characterizes it, is unambiguous: fiscal and monetary policy will no longer serve as the primary levers of growth management. State support is being directed toward technology firms and the advancement of what Beijing terms "new productive forces," rather than infrastructure or consumer transfer payments. The consequences for household incomes are notable. Urban private-sector wage growth fell below 1% by end-2025, according to Natixis economist Alicia Garcia-Herrero, against a pre-pandemic rate of approximately 8%. The absence of specific numerical targets for consumption growth in the Five-Year Plan reinforces the assessment that domestic demand will remain structurally subdued. That, in turn, implies China's already-record trade surplus is likely to persist or widen — an outcome that sits in direct tension with calls from Washington, Paris, and others for external rebalancing. What is perhaps most consequential is the tone shift. Andrew Polk of Trivium China notes that at last month's China Development Forum, Premier Li Qiang rejected criticism of China's surpluses while signaling no meaningful course correction. China's plan, Polk argues, is to double down on industrial upgrading, which by definition requires export markets to absorb excess production. Separately, China ended more than three years of factory-gate deflation this week, a development that may signal early upstream price recovery, though the durability of that turn remains to be seen. India, meanwhile, roiled its $149 billion offshore rupee market by imposing a trading ban, adding a layer of regional financial uncertainty.

Global

The Iran conflict, which began with US and Israeli strikes on February 28, continues to reverberate through global energy markets. Oil and gas prices have risen materially since the onset of hostilities, with considerable volatility accompanying each new operational development. Even as the prospect of a Strait of Hormuz reopening provides intermittent relief to markets, prices remain elevated relative to pre-conflict levels, and the transmission to local economies — particularly across Asia — is becoming increasingly visible. The energy-cost surge is already compressing purchasing power in import-dependent economies and complicating the policy calculus for central banks navigating the growth-inflation tradeoff. The structural implications may prove more durable than the near-term price moves. Bloomberg's David Fickling argues the conflict may reshape Asia's energy systems over decades, potentially accelerating the reorientation of supply chains, LNG contracting, and energy infrastructure investment across the region. How quickly and completely the Strait reopens — and whether any resolution proves stable — remains a central uncertainty for commodity markets. On the climate and financial innovation front, prediction markets for weather and climate events are gaining traction, with trading volume on short-term weather contracts demonstrating measurable growth. Research by Patrick Brown at Interactive Brokers found that prediction markets outperformed US National Weather Service forecasts on at least some near-term weather events, lending credibility to the commercial application of these instruments. Institutional use cases — including reinsurance companies seeking better medium-term cyclone or El Niño forecasts through bespoke expert markets — represent a more targeted and arguably more defensible deployment of the prediction market framework. Analysts caution, however, that the complexity of longer-horizon climate projections may limit the applicability of popular market-based mechanisms beyond near-term meteorological events.
