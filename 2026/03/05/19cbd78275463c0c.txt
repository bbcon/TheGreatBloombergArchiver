China Shows It’s Going All In on Technologies of the Future
Beijing set a modest growth target for 2026. There was nothing modest about 
its tech plans

The figure is an acknowledgment that the model powering the country’s economic 
rise in recent decades is strained, and Li pretty much said that while 
delivering the government’s work report.

“The task of transitioning to new growth drivers is formidable,” he said in a 
speech to the 2,765 delegates gathered at the Great Hall of the People. (Read

.)

“The imbalance between strong supply and weak demand is acute, market 
expectations are weak, and there are many risks and hidden dangers in key 
areas,” he added.

So how will Beijing address that problem?

Well, there were few signs it intends to drastically ramp up measures to 
stimulateconsumer spending 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/xi-eyes-consumers-to-lead-new-era-for-china-s-unbalanced-economy?cmpid=BBD030526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
. And despitethe property market’s troubles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/china-vows-to-tackle-property-crunch-with-city-specific-policies?cmpid=BBD030526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
, language describing measures to support the sector was largely unchanged from 
last year.

The work report signals no immediate plans to strengthen the social safety net 
that economists say is keeping people from opening their wallets.

Instead, Li pointed to tech, namely building modern industries, achieving 
technological self-reliance and digitizing the economy. He said the goals were 
intended to “lay a solid foundation for delivering better performance in the 
coming years.”
Chinese Premier Li Qiang speaks at the National People’s Congress in Beijing.
Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
Also on Thursday, China unveiled a five-year plan that focused on ways to lead 
the next scientific revolution. The draft plan emphasized the need to better 
understand brain cognition, mechanisms and health; develop tech for neural 
activity detection and modification; and treat disorders.

The plan sought more investment in “high-risk, high-reward” initiatives, 
envisioning both state laboratories and private companies driving breakthroughs.

That focus signals that the rivalry with the US that’s already encompassing 
AI, production of critical minerals, andEVs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/canadians-say-they-ll-buy-cheaper-chinese-evs-as-tariffs-drop?cmpid=BBD030526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 andthe batteries that power them 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/china-s-big-battery-lead-includes-energy-storage-that-can-last-for-days?cmpid=BBD030526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 will only expand.

Because on top of those advances, China’s drugmakers are reshaping the world’s 
pharmaceutical pipeline 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-07-13/china-drugmakers-catching-up-to-us-big-pharma-with-new-medicine-innovation?cmpid=BBD030526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 and challenging Western dominance. And its startups are taking on US rivals in 
testing brain-computer interfaces that enable direct communication between 
human brains and machines.

In an example of the momentum, last year a Chinese hospital performed a 
successful kidney transplantfrom a genetically modified pig into a human patient
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-13/china-carries-out-asia-s-first-cross-species-kidney-transplant?cmpid=BBD030526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
, narrowing the gap with US advances in addressing a shortage of human organs.

If China follows through on the priorities laid out by Li and the 
five-year-plan, the tech rivalry with the US is about to hit warp speed — and 
loudly.
