China's posture toward global supply chains has shifted meaningfully, from defensive adjustment to proactive coercion. A report commissioned by the US Chamber of Commerce and prepared by Rhodium Group, released this week, characterized Beijing's recent regulatory moves as a transition from "reactive to proactive, future-looking economic coercion strategies." New regulations unveiled over the past several weeks allow China to retaliate against foreign governments or firms that assist in actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial and supply chain security — a legal architecture constructed explicitly to deter the kind of sourcing diversification that Washington and Brussels have been encouraging. Natixis chief Asia Pacific economist Alicia Garcia Herrero framed the implication bluntly: foreign governments seeking to coax companies out of Chinese supply relationships now face a Beijing that can characterize such interference as a violation of Chinese law.

The strategic intent appears clearly articulated at the highest levels of party leadership. Xi Jinping's latest Five-Year Plan, released in March, replaced the long-standing framing of "peace and development" as the defining feature of the global environment with language centered on geopolitical rivalry as the primary driver of an uncertain and unstable future — a wording shift that JPMorgan economists Jahangir Aziz and Tingting Ge describe as elevating national security to a "central and over-arching principle for economic policies." Consistent with that framing, Beijing is also screening outbound investment to ensure Chinese firms' overseas operations serve to enhance exports rather than displace domestic production, which may increasingly discourage the offshoring of higher-value manufacturing.

The Rhodium report warns of a potential self-reinforcing dynamic: as China's share of global manufacturing continues to rise, eroding margins for competitors in developed economies, the capacity of Western firms to reinvest in R&D diminishes, and private capital increasingly avoids sectors where Chinese state-backed competition is entrenched. For industrial communities in Germany, Japan, and Italy, the adjustment pressure is structural rather than cyclical.

Separately, Trump confirmed following his May 13–15 visit to Beijing that China has not approved purchases of Nvidia H200 AI chips, with the president noting that China intends to develop its own alternatives — consistent with a broader strategy of reducing dependence on the US technology stack.
