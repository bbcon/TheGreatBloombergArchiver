Mega Mergers Prove Elusive for the World’s Biggest Mining Companies
A failed attempt to combine Rio Tinto and Glencore shows mega deals in mining 
are hard to achieve.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Australia mining and metals reporter Paul-Alain Hunt explores why two of the 
sector’s giants were unable to combine into a $240 billion behemoth.

Breaking up is hard to do. For the world’s mining giants, getting together is 
even harder.

For much of the last month, Rio Tinto Group and Glencore Plc appeared ready 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-08/glencore-and-rio-tinto-have-restarted-merger-talks-ft-says?cmpid=BBBXT020626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 to pull off a $240 billion miracle, creating the world’s largest mining 
company. They would combine trading know-how and industrial clout, double down 
on copper and dilute some challenging shareholders on both sides in the process.

Then, at the last minute, Rio called it off 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-05/rio-tinto-glencore-said-to-abandon-merger-talks-over-price?cmpid=BBBXT020626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
.

Talks appear to have broken down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-05/a-years-long-push-to-unite-glencore-and-rio-fell-apart-in-a-day?cmpid=BBBXT020626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 over the oldest obstacle in the book — price. Glencore’s single biggest 
investor is still former boss Ivan Glasenberg, the man who listed the company 
in 2011 at 530 pence per share — significantly more than the average price of 
around 480 pence over the last month.

A combination that Glencore Chief Executive Officer Gary Nagle has described 
in private conversations as the most obvious deal in the industry fell apart, 
adding to the sector’s long list of scuppered mergers.

Over the past few years, big miners have been emerging from a dealmaking 
drought. A push for growth, particularly in copper, has encouraged more 
ambition and risk.

Getting those deals done, however, is another matter entirely.
A combined Rio Tinto and Glencore would have overtaken BHP to become the 
world’s largest mining company.Photographer: Matt Jelonek/Bloomberg
Partly it’s about large egos and clashing cultures. But there’s also the 
challenge of agreeing on a reasonable price when markets are fluctuating wildly 
and geopolitical risk is rising — all in a sector where cost synergies are 
tough to achieve.

Shareholders also remember all too keenly the mistakes of the past, such as 
Rio’sdisastrous $38 billion purchase <bbg://news/stories/QZGSJNDWX2Q0> of Alcan 
Inc. in 2007.

And where Rio and Glencore have struggled to add scale, so too has BHP Group, 
the world’s largest mining company. It mademultiple failed approaches 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-24/the-three-days-when-bhp-tried-to-crash-a-copper-mining-mega-deal?cmpid=BBBXT020626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 for Anglo American Plc only for its target to find itselfa different partner 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-16/anglo-and-teck-win-canada-s-approval-to-form-50-billion-miner?cmpid=BBBXT020626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260206>
 in Teck Resources Ltd.

For now, both Rio and Glencore are left with hangovers to nurse.

Some Rio shareholders have professed themselves relieved that a potentially 
expensive gamble is off the table. But it’s hard to get excited about cost cuts 
and small divestments — especially when such a plan was announced, only to be 
trumped by a possible deal.

For Glencore, whose shares slid close to 11% on the news, there is the 
pressing question of its direction of travel, especially when Glasenberg is 
lingering in the wings.

The two companies may talk again, eventually. For now, it’s yet another 
near-miss for a sector that sorely needs scale and growth.

— Paul-Alain Hunt, Bloomberg News
