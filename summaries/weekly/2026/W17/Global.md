**Strait of Hormuz: The Dominant Market Risk**

The unresolved status of the Strait of Hormuz remained the defining variable for global markets throughout the week. Iranian Foreign Minister declared the strait "completely open" on Friday of the prior week, only for Tehran to reverse course within 24 hours after the US refused to lift its blockade. The US Navy fired upon and boarded an Iranian-flagged cargo ship in the Gulf of Oman — the first such seizure since the blockade began — prompting Iranian authorities to reimpose controls. Brent crude surged as much as 7.9% to near $95 per barrel in Monday's Asian session, subsequently rising above $104 by Thursday — its fourth consecutive daily gain. Goldman Sachs estimated Persian Gulf oil output is running approximately 14.5 million barrels per day below pre-war levels this month, roughly 57% below pre-conflict volumes. Approximately 800 vessels remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, and the International Maritime Organization is developing an evacuation plan. On Wednesday, President Trump announced an indefinite ceasefire extension within 30 minutes of news that Vice President Vance's Islamabad trip had been cancelled — a sequencing that reinforced market wagers on consistent US de-escalatory behavior — though both blockades nominally remained in place. By Friday, a US-sanctioned supertanker, the Yuri, carrying 2 million barrels of Iranian crude, was tracked sailing toward the strait, apparently testing Washington's resolve.

**Diplomatic Dynamics and Geopolitical Positioning**

The gap between the two parties' stated positions remains wide: the US claims Iran has agreed to surrender enriched uranium and end its nuclear program; Iranian officials deny both. Iranian negotiators have reportedly grown less willing to engage, citing Trump's social media posts as intended to humiliate rather than facilitate a deal. Iran's UN envoy indicated Wednesday that Tehran had received "some sign" Washington is ready to lift the blockade, suggesting backchannel communication is active even as formal talks have stalled. Xi Jinping broke his public silence on the standoff, calling Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman to urge restoration of Strait transit — signaling Beijing's acute vulnerability as a major oil importer, though stopping short of concrete diplomatic intervention. Trump's cryptic CNBC remarks about a vessel carrying a "gift from China" — implying potential lethal aid to Iran — introduce a significant geopolitical variable that Beijing has not publicly addressed. Polymarket bettors placed only approximately a one-in-five probability on tanker traffic returning to normal by the end of April. Chinese defense contractors made their largest-ever showing at the Defence Services Asia exhibition in Kuala Lumpur, with 192 firms attending and drone and counter-drone technologies prominently featured.

**Energy Markets and Second-Order Effects**

The energy shock is propagating broadly. Pakistan is experiencing severe power and gas outages, Colombia is reckoning with energy demand disruptions, Singapore is scrambling to secure alternative LNG supplies, and tankers face 3.5-day delays at the Panama Canal as diverted traffic surges. The Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index has risen meaningfully since hostilities began, with wheat and rice moving higher. Bank of America flags corn as carrying the largest upside risk given mounting pressure in nitrogen fertilizer markets. Industrial metals have rallied sharply: copper and aluminum are each up more than 11% since mid-March, supported by supply chain disruption and structural demand from AI data center buildout and grid electrification. The copper-to-gold ratio recently touched four-decade lows, a level historically associated with the onset of commodity supercycles. Commodity trading houses are direct financial beneficiaries: Vitol, Trafigura, and Gunvor together raised $7.5 billion in contingency liquidity facilities at the war's outset, with preliminary first-quarter figures suggesting profits comparable to the $30 billion-plus earned collectively in 2022. Energy trader Pierre Andurand's largest hedge fund fell approximately 52% in the first half of April after bullish oil bets placed at the conflict's outset reversed as diplomacy reduced immediate supply fears.

**Clean Energy Acceleration and Structural Shifts**

The crisis is simultaneously acting as a catalyst for energy transition. At the Petersberg Climate Dialogue in Berlin, delegates from nearly 40 countries framed the disruption as an accelerant for reducing fossil fuel dependence. Clean power surpassed coal's share of global electricity generation in 2025 for the first time since 1919, with renewables at 34% against coal's 33%, according to Ember. Battery installations are expected to jump roughly a third this year. China holds approximately 72% of cumulative installed long-duration energy storage capacity, though its dominance in this segment appears less entrenched than in conventional lithium-ion, offering competitive openings for US and European developers. Brazil's program to electrify isolated Amazon communities via solar and lithium storage illustrates how the Iran war's upward pressure on diesel prices has counterintuitively accelerated the economics of off-grid electrification, with the government projecting $171 million in subsidy savings and avoidance of 800,000 metric tons of greenhouse gas emissions by 2036. Approximately $300 billion in goods subject to US tariffs are estimated to be circumventing levies annually via transshipment through Southeast Asia and Mexico, according to Altana data, a dynamic sharpening USMCA renegotiation tensions and signaling that tariff enforcement remains an unresolved structural challenge.
