By Lars Paulsson 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AHJvswEXujY/lars-paulsson>, Kari

High in the mountains of southern Norway, where winter is usually measured in 
meters of snow, engineers are confronting an unfamiliar sight.

Standing atop the Vatndals dam on a recent day, the hydrologist Sverre 
Eikeland looked out over craggy slopes that should still be blanketed in white 
powder. The reservoir, large enough to fill nearly half a million Olympic 
swimming pools, depends on spring melt to replenish and generate electricity. 
But after NorwayтАЩs driest winter in decades, the water level is far below where 
it should be, prompting companies to limit outflows.

тАЬLess snow means less energy stored in that snow,тАЭ said Eikeland, who advises 
power utility A Energi. тАЬThere should be more.тАЭ
Sverre Eikeland at the Vatndals Dam in March.Photographer: Dana 
Ullman/Bloomberg
Norway, with its thousands of dams, is often called EuropeтАЩs biggest battery. 
Under normal conditions, the countryтАЩs hydropower system produces enough 
electricity to meet domestic demand and export large volumes. Last year, net 
sales abroad amounted to about 15% of its production.

But months of dry weather have upended that pattern. This winter was NorwayтАЩs 
coldest since 2010, the result of persistent high pressure near Greenland that 
blocked flows of moist Atlantic air into the Nordic region. With little 
precipitation, snow reserves have fallen to their lowest levels in two decades, 
creating a deficit of about 25 terawatt-hours of energy, according to Tuomo 
Saloranta, a hydrologist at the Norwegian Water Resources and Energy 
Directorate. ThatтАЩs nearly a fifth of NorwayтАЩs total hydropower output last 
year.

The shortfall is already rippling through electricity markets, slashing 
exports to the UK and Germany and pushing Nordic prices sharply higher. More 
than half of the regionтАЩs supply comes from hydropower, and in Norway it 
accounts for nearly all generation.

тАЬWeтАЩre a weather-based system,тАЭ said Kari Ekelund Thorud, executive vice 
president for energy at Norsk Hydro ASA, one of NorwayтАЩs biggest power users.

This year, when A Energi measured the snowpack тАФ using rods to extract cores

тАЬThere was no snow in February, and now none in March,тАЭ said Lars Erik Omland,

Consumers are already feeling the pinch. Electricity sales to the UK and 
Germany тАФ major export markets тАФ have plunged by about 50% and 40% this year, 
respectively, while winter energy prices in northern Sweden have surged to more

The surge in electricity prices comes at a difficult moment for Europe, as the 
region contends with higher gas prices tied to the war in the Middle East. In 
the UK, windier-than-average weather has helped keep the country supplied 
despite a drop in imports from Norway. Even so, the most expensive hours are 
still often covered by fossil fuels тАФ a challenging dynamic as exports through 
the Persian Gulf have effectively come to a standstill.
