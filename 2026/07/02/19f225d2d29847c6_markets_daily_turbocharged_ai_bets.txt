Leverage, AI and volatile markets

Asian chip stocks are down sharply today, tracking tech losses on Wall Street. 
Shares of South Korea’s SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics slumped about 15% and 
9%, respectively. Japan’s Kioxia Holdings plunged more than 13%.

The trigger was news that Meta Platforms is developing a cloud infrastructure 
business 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/meta-is-building-a-cloud-business-to-sell-excess-ai-compute?cmpid=070226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6155>
 that would sell access to AI computing power and models, raising concerns that 
the company’s spending has led to overcapacity.

As has become routine, the focus is on South Korea, where the benchmark Kospi 
indexclosed down nearly 8% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/south-korean-stocks-tumble-6-as-ai-jitters-hurt-chipmakers?cmpid=070226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6155>
. Korea has been the world’s best‑performing market this year, but its heavy 
reliance on SK Hynix and Samsung has left it vulnerable to sharp bouts of 
volatility.

This thing isn’t helping, either: The CSOP SK Hynix leveraged ETF, launched 
nine months ago in Hong Kong, hasballooned into a $13 billion fund 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-07-01/how-a-13-billion-leveraged-etf-drives-volatility-in-a-key-ai-memory-stock?cmpid=070226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6155>
, the biggest of its kind anywhere. It has grown so large, so fast, analysts 
say, that it’s magnifying swings in both the stock and the entire Kospi index.

These kind of products are popping up across the globe as investors rush to 
turbocharge bets on the AI boom, turning leveraged ETFs into a $270 billion 
business. Nowhere are the stakes higher than in Seoul, where SK Hynix 
increasingly serves as a global barometer to measure AI bubble angst.

“I’m not saying that SK Hynix is going to bring down the S&P, but it’s part of 
a broader pattern,” said Dean Curnutt of Macro Risk Advisors. “Tech high flyers 
are delivering such strong returns and there’s sort of a feedback mechanism on 
the way up that ultimately can play out on the way down as well.”

So far, the enthusiasm has shown little sign of fading. Ron Ding, a 
26-year-old employee at a Hong Kong asset manager, invested $127,604 of his own 
savings in the CSOP ETF in late April.

“I already have a car,” he said. “I can now afford a Patek Philippe Nautilus.”

Fed silent treatment

Investors have been worrying for weeks that bond-market volatility could 
increase if Kevin Warsh follows through on his pledge to reverse a decades-long 
push at the Fed for more communication and moves to quiet the central bank.

On Wednesday, the new Fed chief doubled down. He reiterated that he’s going to 
refrain from offering “forward guidance” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/wall-street-worries-less-fed-talk-will-spur-market-volatility?cmpid=070226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=6155>
 regarding interest-rate decisions. The US two-year Treasury yield, which is 
sensitive to monetary policy, fell.

“Markets’ reliance on forward guidance has generally resulted in decreased 
uncertainty and more crowded trade positioning,” said Gennadiy Goldberg and 
Molly Brooks of TD Securities. “The removal of forward guidance should lead to 
increased volatility in markets as well as higher term premium.”
