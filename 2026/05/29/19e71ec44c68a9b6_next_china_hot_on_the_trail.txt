Hello, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai.

Huawei is trying to take a great leap forward to narrow the tech gap between 
it and industry leader Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co.

The Chinese maker of smartphones and chips says it has potentially achieved a 
breakthrough in making advanced semiconductors 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-25/huawei-touts-chipmaking-breakthrough-to-shorten-gap-with-tsmc?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 without cutting-edge equipment through its own “LogicFolding” technology.

That approach can boost the performance of a chip by optimizing data 
transmission speed.

It is a way to compete against chipmakers such as TSMC, Samsung Electronics 
and Intelwithout needing to shrink transistors 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/what-to-know-about-huawei-s-new-ai-chipmaking-plan-logicfolding-tech?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
, a process that requires extreme ultraviolet lithography machines made by 
Dutch supplier ASML. China can’t get that equipment, widely considered 
essential for production of advanced semiconductors that power the most 
sophisticated AI models.

If Huawei succeeds — a big if — it and manufacturing partner Semiconductor 
Manufacturing International Corp. could narrow TSMC’s five-year lead in the 
most advanced chips.

Success would also show that efforts by the US to expand export restrictions 
on China have failed to curtail its progress and instead bolster arguments made 
by people like Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang that curbs could just spur rivals to 
accelerate their own development.

Chinese authorities have yet to allow the import of Nvidia’s AI chips from the 
US, though there have been reports ofsmuggling of advanced semiconductors 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/taiwan-said-to-suspect-nvidia-chips-smuggled-to-china-via-japan?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 through countries such as Malaysia and even Japan.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-26/huawei-touts-chip-breakthrough-to-shorten-gap-with-tsmc-video?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>

Huawei’s possible breakthrough represents a boon for President Xi Jinping’s 
self-sufficiency drive. China’sincreasingly bifurcated economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/china-s-industrial-profits-surge-at-fastest-in-over-two-years?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 has drawn strength from the tech boom, with overseas sales of semiconductors 
and computers accounting for about half of its export growth in April.

The LogicFolding technology was revealed by Huawei’s semiconductor chief He 
Tingbo during a rare public appearance at a chip conference this week. It is 
based on Huawei’s Tau Scaling Law, a principle that aims to rivalMoore’s Law 
<https://www.investopedia.com/terms/m/mooreslaw.asp>, the guideline for the 
global chip industry.

Moore’s Law, named after Intel co-founder Gordon Moore, says that the number 
of transistors in a chip will roughly double about every two years, though many 
in the industry including He say the cadence has been slowing in recent years.

Tau Scaling seeks to move away from the industry model of improving 
performance through ever-shrinking transistors, and instead, shorten the 
distance that data must travel inside a processor.

Huawei’s He said that the Kirin mobile chips to be launched this fall will be 
the first to adopt the LogicFolding architecture. It plans to start making the 
equivalent of 1.4-nanometer chips by 2031 with this technology, just three 
years behind TSMC’s plans for mass production.

Besides engineering challenges, including manufacturing complexity, heat 
dissipation and power delivery, it remains to be seen whether the technology 
can be deployed economically and at scale.

Written off as a top smartphone maker after the US cut it off from American 
suppliers in 2019, it later staged a comeback andelectrified the chip industry 
in the process 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2023-09-04/new-huawei-mate-60-pro-smartphone-disassembled-video?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 by unveiling the Mate 60 Pro housing a 7-nanometer processor made by SMIC.

During a trek around Guangdong province in 2023, I discovered Huawei had built 
secret semiconductor-fabrication facilities 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-23/huawei-building-secret-chip-plants-in-china-to-bypass-us-sanctions-group-warns?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 that allowed the blacklisted company to skirt US sanctions and further the 
nation’s tech ambitions.

Investors reacted under the belief that Huawei’s latest idea could work, 
sending shares of Chinese semiconductor stocks like SMIC and Hua Hong soaring.

The Huawei technology, “once fully developed, promises to boost the 
performance of China’s future AI chips,” Bloomberg Intelligence senior analyst 
Robert Lea wrote in a note. This would help Chinese tech firms “maintain 
competitiveness, despite export restrictions on advanced Nvidia chips.”

Cities of the Future

The Chinese government is rarely short on ambition.

In what might be one of the most ambitious projects of all, it plans to 
seamlessly integrate nine major cities in Guangdong province with Hong Kong and 
Macau to create a megapolis of almost 100 million people, despite clashing 
legal and financial systems.

If successful, the so-called Greater Bay Area would serve as part of a larger 
transformation planned for China’s economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/china-has-a-plan-to-fuse-its-major-cities-into-megaregions?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
, which includes creating 19 megaregions to fuse hundreds of millions of people 
into urban engines of knowledge and labor.

In this documentary, Bloomberg Originals explains how the historic gamble 
could supercharge the country’s ascent while being a possible blueprint for 
humanity’s future.

The plan has been tossed around for a decade, and it’s only been in recent 
years that visible progress can be seen. Besides new bridges and airports, 
there are now rail links that cut what had been serious travel times to no more 
than a short commute.

It now takes just 14 minutes to arrive in Hong Kong’s bustling Kowloon area 
from Shenzhen’s business district. On any given weekend, more than 700,000 Hong 
Kongers cross the border into the tech hub to take advantage of everything from 
cheaper dim sum to foot massages.

Key to the success of the plan is the Northern Metropolis project, a 
megadevelopment in the former British colony that aims to turn a rural area 
into a tech hub with affordable housing. The project faces significant 
obstacles, including skepticism from local developers and the displacement of 
thousands of farmers and villagers, as well as threats to wildlife habitats.

While examples such as Indonesia’s new capital city of Nusantara 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/iran-s-khamenei-says-no-going-back-for-middle-east-rocked-by-war?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-24/china-coal-mine-blast-tests-limits-of-xi-s-energy-security-push?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>

Again, as in: Didn’t dozens of people just get killed in a fireworks factory 
blast 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-08/the-china-us-relationship-is-steady-which-suits-xi-and-trump-just-fine?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 a few weeks ago?

Again, as in: Another coal mine accident 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-26/china-coal-mine-blast-exposes-limits-of-nationwide-safety-drive?cmpid=BBD052926_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4828>
 in China?

Well, yes. A gas explosion at the Liushenyu coal mine in the northern province 
of Shanxi on May 22 was the worst such disaster in China in 17 years, prompting 
shock and fury online.

There’s been a drop in the frequency and scale of coal mine accidents over the 
past decade but many people were still appalled by details from state media 
about glaring safety failures. These included a woeful initial undercounting of 
the number of people trapped underground.

“It’s only been a few days since the Liuyang explosion,” one person wrote on 
Weibo. “Didn’t the whole country organize workplace safety inspections? Why 
hasn’t Shanxi done anything?”
Rescuers gather at the site of the Liushenyu coal mine following the accident.
Photographer: Wei Liang/China News Service/VCG/Getty Images
Much of the anger has focused on the private company operating the mine. Some 
people also accused local officials of failing to spot dangerous work practices 
before disaster struck.

Discrepancies in the official account of how many miners were underground at 
the time of the explosion left some incensed. Local authorities initially said 
that 201 people had safely escaped and only eight had died.

The reality was a lot grimmer. A local official later said the company had 
failed to keep an accurate account of the people on duty, resulting in the 
inaccurate figures.

“When the accident happened, they couldn’t even get an accurate headcount,” 
one person said on Douyin. “It just shows how chaotic their safety management 
is.”

State media says miners are required to use facial recognition tech when they 
pass through security gates and carry location-tracking devices into mines. Yet 
at the time of the blast, only 124 out of 247 people were in compliance.

That gap fueled questions over whether the mine was employing unregistered 
workers to boost production. State media also reported that the mine operator 
had opened tunnels that were left off official maps, going so far as to hide 
them during official inspections.

Many of these issues obviously hinder rescue efforts, prompting some internet 
users to render harsh judgments.

“This is not an accident but rather murder cloaked in a veneer of regulatory 
compliance,” said a Weibo user.
