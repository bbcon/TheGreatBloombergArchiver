**Energy-Driven Demand Shift**
The sustained elevation in gasoline prices — a direct transmission channel from the US-Iran conflict and Strait of Hormuz disruption — is producing measurable behavioral responses in US consumer transportation patterns. California prices exceeding $6/gallon for regular fuel are particularly acute, with San Diego light rail ridership jumping nearly 18% in February–April. The New York Fed's May report corroborates the directional shift, finding lower-income households substituting away from gasoline, potentially through carpooling or transit. Academic research suggests each 10% sustained increase in gas prices generates up to a 1.2% rise in transit demand, implying the current shock — well above that threshold — carries meaningful ridership implications.

**Structural Versus Cyclical Caution**
Whether this represents durable modal shift or a temporary price-induced response remains genuinely uncertain. Post-2008 and post-2022 oil shocks saw drivers revert to prior habits once prices normalized. This cycle differs at the margin — return-to-office mandates and proliferating alternatives (EVs, delivery, remote work) complicate attribution. With the Iran conflict at a stalemate, elevated pump prices appear likely to persist through 2026, extending the window for behavioral entrenchment, though analysts stop short of declaring a structural inflection.
