**The Hormuz Blockade: Strategy, Execution Risk, and Market Pricing**
The formal US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, announced after the collapse of US-Iran talks in Islamabad over the weekend, represents a material escalation in a conflict that has disrupted global energy markets since the February 28 strikes. The strategic logic, as articulated by advisers close to the situation, is to close Iran's revenue mechanism while simultaneously working to reopen the mined half of the Strait through demining, escorts, and insurance backstops — thereby pressuring Tehran to concede politically. The approach carries substantial execution risk: US warships operating in closer proximity to Iranian drone and missile capabilities face a rapid escalation cycle should any vessel be struck. Iran responded on Monday by threatening that "no port in the Persian Gulf and Sea of Oman will be safe" if its own facilities are targeted. The UK confirmed it will not participate in the blockade. The mechanism's credibility was directly challenged on Tuesday when the US-sanctioned tanker Rich Starry — linked to China — transited the Strait without apparent interdiction, and two Iran-linked vessels were later observed testing alternative corridors between Larak and Qeshm islands. By Thursday, only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker had completed a crude cargo exit since the blockade came into force, illustrating the gap between diplomatic tone and operational reality. Trump expressed optimism about a permanent ceasefire on Thursday, claiming Iran had conceded on nuclear ambitions and Hormuz access — assertions Tehran has not publicly confirmed. Gulf Arab and European officials believe a durable agreement is more likely six months away.

**Macro Consequences: Growth, Trade, and Supply Chains**
The IMF's Spring Meetings in Washington provided a sobering backdrop, with Bloomberg Economics' base case projecting global growth of 2.9% in 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — and a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 a barrel. Global trade volume growth is now projected at 2.8% for 2026, below global GDP growth of 3.1%. Qatar's Finance Minister described current conditions as "the tip of the iceberg," and economist Carmen Reinhart argued the world is less prepared to respond to a crisis than at any point in the past two decades, constrained by a $348 trillion global debt burden, central banks still contending with inflation, and a fragmented multilateral order. Global Trade Alert has tracked more than 250 distinct government policy responses since hostilities began — the large majority liberalizing import measures, energy subsidies, or demand-reduction programs. Panama Canal congestion has reached its worst since the 2023–2024 drought, with vessels facing 3.5-day waits and one ship paying $4 million to jump the queue. The Port of Los Angeles chief estimates that even after the Strait reopens, full shipping normalization will take approximately four and a half months. The IEA issued a striking forecast: global oil demand is expected to decline in 2026 for the first time since the pandemic, as the price surge offsets underlying demand growth.
