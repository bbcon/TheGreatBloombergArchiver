Daily Brief

Monday, June 22, 2026

US

  • No major US macro data releases on June 22; Thursday's PCE price index is the week's key inflation print, with consensus expecting acceleration on both a monthly and year-over-year basis in May.
  • US equity futures pointed to a weaker open despite signs of diplomatic progress on the Iran front, with S&P 500 futures at 7,562, down 0.1%.
  • SpaceX shares extended their decline in premarket trading, on pace for a third consecutive session of losses despite remaining 37% above their $135 IPO price.
  • Getty Images surged approximately 200% in premarket trading after announcing a licensing agreement with OpenAI.

Monetary Policy and Inflation Outlook

The Federal Reserve's preferred inflation gauge — the PCE price index — is due Thursday and is widely expected to show acceleration in May on both a monthly and annual basis, consistent with a string of upside inflation surprises this month. Prior releases have illustrated how the energy shock from the Iran conflict has been spreading across the broader economy. Fed officials remain attentive to this broadening, and the data are unlikely to challenge what appears to be a growing consensus within the central bank around the case for rate hikes this year. The Fed Board and New York Fed are also hosting a two-day conference beginning Monday on the international role of the dollar.

Equity and Corporate Markets

US equities found little support from lower oil prices or the Iran diplomatic signals, with futures softening heading into the open after the long weekend. Apogee Therapeutics rallied 54% on reports that AbbVie is nearing an approximately $11 billion acquisition. Couche-Tard is scheduled to report earnings after the close. The week's primary risk event for rates markets is Thursday's PCE release, with bond traders recently repositioning for a more hawkish path. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's financial regulator expressed regret over the launch of single-stock leveraged ETFs, warning that negative side effects — particularly around Samsung and SK Hynix — have grown significantly and may be amplifying volatility in the world's best-performing equity market.
  • South Korea's export growth remained robust in June, driven by continued strength in the semiconductor sector, though the policy chief flagged questions about how chip-led gains will diffuse into the broader economy.
  • Chinese AI stocks rallied on demand optimism and policy support; Hong Kong is separately seeking to expand IPO access for mainland Chinese investors and raise quota levels.
  • China imposed export controls on US rare earth producers MP Materials and USA Rare Earth, escalating critical minerals tensions.

China Trade and Supply Chain Tensions

Chinese Vice Premier Ding Xuexiang warned at the China International Supply Chain Expo that "the risk of global supply chain fragmentation is growing," framing protectionism as the primary threat to global trade architecture. Beijing followed rhetoric with action, adding MP Materials and USA Rare Earth — both central to Washington's effort to build alternative critical minerals supply chains — to China's export control list. Eight additional US companies in drones, robotics, and aerospace were also targeted. Separately, Chinese customs data showed exports of tungsten, dysprosium, and terbium to Japan remained at zero in May, extending a politically driven supply disruption.

Korean Markets and Regulation

Financial Supervisory Service Governor Lee Chan-jin's public admission of regret over single-stock leveraged ETF approvals is notable in its directness. Authorities are now weighing additional stabilization measures to limit contagion from sharp swings in ETFs tracking Samsung and SK Hynix — underscoring the risks that concentrated AI-driven equity positioning poses to a market seen as integral to the global technology supply chain. ---

Europe

  • UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer announced his resignation Monday morning, with Labour leadership nominations opening July 9 and a successor to be determined by September 1; Andy Burnham emerged as the frontrunner.
  • Sterling and gilts recovered quickly from an initial brief selloff, with markets appearing to take the transition in stride — though fiscal uncertainty under a potential Burnham premiership remains a live risk.
  • The Stoxx Europe 600 is up approximately 1.5% in June, outpacing the S&P 500 (down 1% over the same period), as easing energy prices revive the "Buy Europe" trade.
  • Germany's ruling coalition is preparing to back a sweeping pension overhaul introducing a market-based savings component, tighter early-retirement rules, and a gradual increase in the retirement age.

UK Political Transition and Fiscal Risk

Starmer's departure, two years after Labour's landslide victory, reflects accumulated pressure from local election losses. The fiscal implications of a Burnham premiership are the more consequential near-term question for markets. While Burnham has offered reassurances that he will respect current fiscal rules — requiring day-to-day spending to be matched by revenues and debt to fall as a share of GDP — several of his key advisers, including former Bank of England Chief Economist Andy Haldane and former Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jim O'Neill, have previously argued for looser constraints. As Mizuho strategist Jordan Rochester noted, any "whiff of fiscal laxity gets punished by the gilt market," a dynamic that likely disciplines the pace of any rule changes rather than preventing them entirely.

European Equity Rerating

The easing of Iran-related energy pressures has meaningfully improved the European growth and inflation outlook. Cyclical sectors — banks, automakers, and luxury goods — appear particularly well-positioned. Germany's KNDS defense deal, valued at €15–18 billion with a 40% government stake, adds further corporate event risk to watch, while Porsche's ongoing cost-restructuring process reflects the continued pressure on European auto margins. ---

LatAm

  • Colombia's presidential election delivered a narrow preliminary victory for Abelardo de la Espriella, a Trump-endorsed, MAGA-aligned candidate, in what is described as the country's tightest-ever presidential contest; the result remains subject to a final official count.
  • De la Espriella's platform includes reopening Colombia to oil exploration and a zero-tolerance approach to drug cartels, representing a sharp policy reversal from outgoing President Gustavo Petro's leftist program.
  • Peru's presidential count continues, with conservative Keiko Fujimori leading among overseas voters while leftist Roberto Sánchez holds a slim 50.1% lead in the domestic tally; Sánchez has moved to challenge all overseas votes.

Colombia and the Regional Political Shift

De la Espriella's apparent win extends a regional swing toward US-aligned, right-leaning governments that already includes Argentina under Milei and Chile under Kast. Trump's "Donroe Doctrine" — asserting US political and economic primacy across the Western Hemisphere — appears to be gaining traction. However, de la Espriella's lack of governing experience and his open contempt for opposition forces raise legitimate questions about his capacity to implement structural reforms, including the promised reopening of oil exploration. The razor-thin margin also creates space for legal challenges that could cloud the transition.

Brazil as the Regional Swing Factor

With Colombia potentially aligned with Washington and Peru's outcome still uncertain, Brazil's Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva — facing his own presidential election in October against Flávio Bolsonaro — may emerge as the last significant bulwark against comprehensive US hemispheric influence. The outcome of that contest will likely determine whether any meaningful counterweight to the regional rightward shift survives into 2027. ---

Global

  • US-Iran talks in Bürgenstock, Switzerland produced what Iran characterized as "major progress," with mediators Qatar and Pakistan confirming "encouraging progress" and the establishment of a high-level committee, working groups on nuclear issues and sanctions, and a de-confliction cell for Lebanon.
  • Oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz reached approximately 17 million barrels on Saturday alone according to US Central Command — close to prewar levels — though AIS-tracked visible flows averaged a lower 6.6 million barrels per day over the past three days due to widespread transponder suppression.
  • Brent crude fell roughly 1.6% to $79.29, extending a decline of nearly 30% over the past month as markets price in reduced re-escalation risk.
  • China's export controls on US rare earth producers and the G7's agreement that no single country should supply more than 60% of members' rare earth imports by 2030 formalize a structural fracture in global critical minerals supply chains.

Energy Markets and Hormuz

The practical reality in the Strait of Hormuz is that significant volumes of oil are transiting despite Iran's periodic claims to the contrary. Iran is simultaneously moving previously blockaded crude from Kharg Island, benefiting from US sanctions waivers secured under last week's memorandum of understanding. Sellers of Iranian crude to China have already cut spot prices to discounts of $2.50–$5 per barrel to Brent, reflecting the rapid increase in available supply. A blast at Qatar's Barzan gas facility — injuring 54 and leaving 18 missing — is a reminder that physical energy infrastructure risk in the region has not fully abated even as diplomatic progress accumulates.

Trade Fragmentation

The convergence of China's export control escalation, the G7 rare earths concentration target, and the Trump administration's reconfiguration of its tariff regime ahead of the July 24 expiration of temporary 10% duties points to an accelerating structural reorganization of global supply chains. Bloomberg Economics estimates more than $2 trillion in G7 GDP could be at risk if Chinese rare earth exports ceased entirely — a figure that underscores the asymmetry of leverage Beijing retains even as Washington works to build alternatives.
