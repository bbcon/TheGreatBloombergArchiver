Super Cycle

Asia is entering an industrial super-cycle.

That’s the bullish call from Chetan Ahya, chief Asia economist at Morgan 
Stanley in Hong Kong. He and his team see a sustained rise in capital 
expenditure driven by the artificial intelligence boom and AI-related 
infrastructure, the energy transition and increased defense spending.

And while the war in Iran and resulting surge in energy costs is denting 
momentum 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/economic-crosscurrents-go-global-as-energy-shock-meets-ai-wave?cmpid=BBD050426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4095>
 in the short term, they also act as a catalyst for ongoing investment that’s 
set to fuel the strongest industrial cycle since the mid-2000s.

In the past week or so, we’ve seen plenty of evidence of that. Just look at 
Taiwan’seye-popping first quarter 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/taiwan-s-economy-surges-as-exports-power-growth-to-39-year-high?cmpid=BBD050426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4095>
 expansion and South Korea’sexport boom 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/south-korea-s-export-growth-stays-strong-on-semiconductor-demand?cmpid=BBD050426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4095>
. That’s been accompanied by much hand-wringing over K-shaped growth, where a 
few on the upward slope benefit as the rest fall behind.
A Samsung memory chip for AI systems.Photographer: SeongJoon Cho/Bloomberg
What makes Morgan Stanley’s call interesting is how they see the capex boom 
broadening out to boost the wider economies.

“The industrial cycle lines up exactly with Asia’s dominant position in 
manufacturing and allows a virtuous cycle to take hold. The structural rise in 
spending on AI and AI-related infrastructure, energy and energy transition, and 
defense will catalyze a broad-based pickup in capex. Moreover, as the rise in 
capex brings about strong job creation and wage growth, it will in turn lift 
consumption growth and sustain the capex cycle.”

Taiwan, South Korea, China and Japan are most exposed to the high-growth AI, 
energy and defense sectors and will benefit from domestic capex and exports, 
they wrote in a note to clients last week. India should also get a lift, while 
commodity exporters Australia and Indonesia can also benefit.

I caught up with Ahya in Hong Kong on Monday to discuss his bullish call and 
the assumptions that underpin it. The main reason he sees the cycle as 
sustainable is that it’s driven by structural forces, not just cyclical swings. 
He also sees favorable debt dynamics, especially among corporates, which gives 
the boom room play out. He assumes tariffs and protectionism won’t get worse 
and that AI won’t be just kill jobs, it’ll create them too.

So how to keep tabs on the boom? Employment indicators may take a while to 
show the broadening, so keep an eye on things like Asia’s non-tech exports, 
corporate profit margins and wage growth. If they continue to tick higher, that 
suggests a broadening may follow for incomes and retail spending.

The cycle should last three to four years, reinforcing Asia’s already pivotal 
role as the production house for the world, Ahya said.

“For Asia, this is a double boost. They are doing the capex themselves and 
producing it onshore. They are also going to feed everybody else’s demand for 
capex in these areas. Asia is a production house. So this is very good for 
Asia.”

Need-to-Know Research

Japan is undergoing a fundamental shift toward sustained 2% inflation as 
companies and workers find it “easier to obtain price and wage increases,” 
according to Louis Kuijs, chief Asia-Pacific economist at S&P Global Ratings.

“After decades of hibernation, Japanese inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/tokyo-inflation-cools-further-backing-boj-caution-on-rate-hikes?cmpid=BBD050426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4095>
 is waking up. We think this shift is becoming entrenched and that it will 
allow the central bank to raise its policy rate toward 1.5%,” from 0.75% now, 
Kuijs wrote in a note April 30. While a weaker yen pushed up imported energy 
and other goods prices, Kuijs said services inflation is a “telling indicator” 
as it’s more driven by wage costs.

“Our baseline forecast envisages headline inflation of around 2% in coming 
years,” he wrote. “Higher yields are back, and that’s ok, in our view. It 
suggests a healthier domestic economy.” Government finances and corporate 
profitability have benefited strongly from higher nominal growth, he 
highlighted. The publicdebt ratio has fallen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-08/japan-s-debt-burden-tumbles-to-a-16-year-low-on-economic-upswing?cmpid=BBD050426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4095>
, while corporate profits rose to 13.8% of GDP last year from 10.8% in 2021.
