Weekly Brief

May 18 – May 24, 2026

US

  • The 30-year Treasury yield touched 5.19% on Tuesday — the highest since June 2007 — as Hormuz-driven inflation pressures overwhelmed the prior easing narrative, with futures markets by week's end pricing roughly a 70–75% probability of a Fed hike by December.
  • Kevin Warsh was sworn in as Federal Reserve Chair on Friday, inheriting an FOMC that had already shifted hawkish: minutes released Wednesday showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" preferred dropping the easing bias entirely.
  • Nvidia reported 85% revenue growth and an $80 billion buyback authorization on Wednesday, though shares dipped after hours on underwhelming guidance; SpaceX filed its S-1 targeting up to $75 billion in proceeds, disclosing $18.7 billion in 2025 revenue.
  • NextEra announced a $67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy on Monday — the largest US utility deal on record — driven by data-center power demand across Florida and Virginia.
  • Senate Republicans rebuffed Trump's $1.8 billion "weaponization" account request on Friday, a notable intra-party setback, while Moody's downgraded Mexico to one notch above junk, introducing North American credit and trade exposure concerns.

Bond Markets and the Rate Outlook

The week's defining story in US markets was the continued repricing of the long end. The 30-year Treasury yield reached 5.19% on Tuesday, a level last seen in June 2007, while 10-year yields touched 4.63% on Monday — the highest since February. The drivers were mutually reinforcing: war-driven inflation, with April headline CPI at 3.8% year-on-year, the firmest since 2023; persistent fiscal supply; AI-related capital expenditure; and continued labor market resilience. Citigroup's Jim McCormick flagged 5.5% as the next technical focal point. Barclays' Ajay Rajadhyaksha argued that only weaker macro data was likely to arrest the move absent a Hormuz reopening, and HSBC characterized a 2026 Fed hike as a tail risk that "can't be ruled out." The effect on equity markets was bifurcated: large-cap AI names remained broadly bid, while options activity in the Russell 2000 showed the first meaningful pickup in hedging demand in six weeks. BofA's fund manager survey, released mid-week, showed a record monthly jump in equity allocations and the sharpest cash drawdown since February 2024 — readings strategist Michael Hartnett characterized as near-complete "bull capitulation," with early June flagged as a potential inflection.

The New Fed Chair

Kevin Warsh's swearing-in on Friday transferred the chairmanship at an awkward moment. Wednesday's FOMC minutes — Powell's final meeting — showed a committee whose consensus had shifted materially beneath the incoming chair, with a majority signaling some firming may be appropriate if inflation runs persistently above 2%. Futures now price fed funds above 4% next summer, against 2.8% pricing in late February. UBS's Jonathan Pingle suggested Warsh may lean on trimmed-mean inflation and unit labor costs to frame a delay in hikes, and ABN Amro analysts floated the possibility of changes to the SEP cadence and press conference schedule within six to twelve months. Jamie Dimon, speaking to Bloomberg Television during the week, warned rates "could be much higher than they are today." The realistic ceiling on Warsh's dovishness appears to be holding, rather than cutting.

Corporate Developments

Nvidia's Wednesday print — 85% revenue growth, 62% gross margins, $78.9 billion in revenue — met but did not exceed elevated expectations, and the stock's after-hours dip illustrated the challenge of sustaining momentum at a Shiller CAPE of 39.6. Hyperscaler capex projections for 2026 and 2027 were revised to $812 billion and $968 billion respectively, up 89% year-to-date. SpaceX's filing, reportedly led by Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, disclosed a $4.94 billion net loss alongside the revenue figure, a super-voting structure, and performance milestones tied to a Mars colony. NextEra's $67 billion Dominion acquisition — announced Monday — positions the combined entity to capture data-center load growth in Florida and Virginia with battery storage central to the strategy. Google and Blackstone announced a $5 billion initial equity commitment for an AI cloud venture, scaling to roughly $25 billion including leverage, which weighed on CoreWeave, Nebius and Applied Digital. Meta reassigned 7,000 employees to AI roles ahead of broader cuts. Separately, Brookings data showed 82% of Los Angeles County businesses reported negative impacts from ICE enforcement operations, with concentrated damage to small firms a potential early indicator of broader policy-driven disruption.

Fiscal and Trade

The EU finalized the text of its US trade deal on Tuesday, capping EU export tariffs at 15% in exchange for removal of EU duties on US industrial goods — averting a threatened rise to 25% on European automobiles ahead of the July 4 deadline. The post-summit US-China framework remains thin: $17 billion in annual agricultural purchases through 2028 is the most concrete commitment, with Section 232-style investigations queued to replace tariffs struck down in February. A fresh round of duties this summer could collide with gasoline prices near $4.50 and affordability concerns ahead of the midterms. Trump reversed course and announced 5,000 additional troops for Poland on Friday, while the Pentagon confirmed a separate permanent reduction of roughly 4,000 troops from Europe announced earlier in the week. ---

Asia

  • Nvidia's earnings and SpaceX's IPO filing triggered a historic Asian tech rally on Thursday: South Korea's Kospi surged 8.4% and Taiwan's Taiex rose 3.4%, with Korean chip exports up 202% year-on-year in the first twenty days of May.
  • Samsung averted a general strike on Wednesday after reaching a deal with its largest union — potentially distributing roughly ₩40 trillion ($26.6 billion) in AI-linked bonuses averaging $340,000 per chip employee — while SK Hynix's market capitalization reached $910 billion.
  • China's April data, released Monday, delivered a broad miss: retail sales rose 0.2% year-on-year, industrial production grew 4.1% (the weakest in nearly three years), and fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% year-to-date, reigniting the stimulus debate.
  • Bank Indonesia surprised with a 50 basis point rate hike to 5.25% on Wednesday, while President Prabowo announced state centralization of palm oil, coal, and nickel exports — a move that blindsided senior officials and extended the Jakarta Composite's year-to-date decline to roughly 26%, the worst among major global benchmarks.
  • Vladimir Putin arrived in Beijing on Monday for a state visit focused on Power of Siberia 2 pricing and energy cooperation, though the structural leverage favors Xi given Russia's isolation and dependence on Chinese demand.

China: Growth Disappointment and Post-Summit Ambiguity

The week opened with April activity data that fell short across every major indicator. Retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year — the softest print since the post-Covid reopening of December 2022 — with auto sales down 15% and jewelry off 21%. Industrial production at 4.1% was the weakest in nearly three years, and fixed-asset investment unexpectedly contracted 1.6% year-to-date, a result no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated. The figures arrived against the backdrop of the post-Trump-Xi summit narrative, which produced a commitment to at least $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchases through 2028 but little operational detail on the broader trade framework. The "placeholder summit" characterization increasingly resonates: Beijing's reluctance to add domestic stimulus is now harder to sustain given the breadth of weakness, and the trade board established by the summit appears to cover only roughly $30 billion in flows. Capital flows nonetheless continue to favor China, with domestic banks receiving $61.9 billion in net inflows on behalf of clients in April — the sixth month of inflows in seven — though exchanges are reportedly scrutinizing AI-driven rallies and the so-called "national team" is cutting ETF holdings by roughly 90% in the first half of the year, suggesting officials are managing froth rather than endorsing the advance.

The AI Rally and Korea's Concentration Risk

Thursday's Nvidia-driven surge crystallized Asia's structural position in the AI supply chain. Samsung and TSMC have together added approximately $1.2 trillion in market value year-to-date, and South Korean export data for May 1–20 showed chip shipments up 202% year-on-year, lifting the broader trade balance into surplus. Japan posted a third consecutive monthly trade surplus as semiconductor exports rose 44%, partly offsetting a 64% collapse in crude import volumes reflecting Hormuz disruptions. The gains, however, sit alongside notable concentration risk: Korea's Kospi year-to-date gain of roughly 73% has been driven almost entirely by Samsung and SK Hynix, and retail margin debt reached a record ₩36.5 trillion ($24 billion) earlier in the week before Samsung's labor settlement provided some relief. Oxford Economics frames the resulting external surpluses as a narrower echo of the 2000s Asian savings glut, with capital recycling indirectly supporting US asset prices even as Treasury yields press higher.

Indonesia's Policy Turn and Regional Stress

Bank Indonesia's 50 basis point hike — double consensus expectations — and President Prabowo's sweeping reassertion of state control over commodity exports together represent the week's most consequential policy shift in emerging Asia. The export centralization through Danantara echoes earlier authoritarian-era practices and is likely to unsettle foreign buyers of Indonesian coal and palm oil, even as Jakarta frames the move as targeting under-invoicing. A Moody's warning over centralization risks followed. India faces analogous pressures: the rupee touched nearly 97 per dollar during the week and the Reserve Bank of India is reportedly weighing its 2013 taper-tantrum toolkit, including rate hikes and expanded FX swaps. The Philippines, Thailand, and Malaysia each face secondary effects from elevated energy costs — Thai and Philippine fishing fleets are idling on diesel prices, and Thai authorities have prepared a 200 billion baht ($6.1 billion) cost-of-living package.

Japan and the BOJ Dilemma

Japan's Q1 GDP expanded an annualized 2.1%, accelerating from a revised 0.8%, on stronger consumption and trade — a positive surprise that nonetheless sat alongside mounting fiscal and monetary tensions. PM Takaichi, after weeks of insisting a supplementary budget was unnecessary, reversed course and instructed the finance ministry to compile one, targeting roughly $19 billion including revived energy subsidies. The move pushed 30-year JGB yields to fresh post-1999 highs, with Finance Minister Katayama publicly flagging markets as a policy risk. Core inflation, meanwhile, slowed to a four-year low — ostensibly providing the BOJ room to pause — though a BOJ official signaled support for a near-term hike, placing June increasingly in play. Pimco has turned constructive on the Japanese long end, though the combination of fiscal expansion and tightening monetary bias remains an uncomfortable juxtaposition for JGB markets. ---

Europe

  • The EU finalized the text of its US trade deal on Tuesday, averting an increase in auto tariffs from 15% to 25% ahead of Trump's July 4 deadline, though the pact formalizes a less favorable arrangement than the pre-tariff status quo; eurozone exports to the US fell 37% year-on-year in March.
  • The eurozone composite PMI fell to 47.5 in May from 48.8, marking the sharpest contraction in two and a half years, with France's reading plunging to its lowest since 2020 while Germany was broadly stable.
  • UK M&A activity has risen more than 250% year-to-date to roughly $150 billion — the strongest pace since 2015 — as overseas acquirers exploit depressed sterling valuations, with Unilever, Schroders, Beazley, Intertek and Tate & Lyle among the names in play.
  • ECB Governor Pierre Wunsch signaled a likely June rate hike as Iran-driven energy costs reshape the inflation trajectory, while Emmanuel Moulin was confirmed as Banque de France governor, removing one source of policy uncertainty.
  • Standard Chartered announced plans to eliminate roughly 8,000 support roles over four years through AI integration, while Germany formally launched the privatization of Uniper, potentially Europe's largest utility transaction this year.

ECB Policy and the Inflation Dilemma

The week's most consequential European policy development was the crystallization of an ECB tightening bias. Belgian Governor Wunsch indicated a June hike was likely, Emmanuel Moulin reinforced that the ECB must be "ready to act," and at least one additional Governing Council member kept the door open to further moves. ECB President Lagarde maintained that medium-term inflation expectations remain anchored, though her messaging sat uneasily against G7 long-bond yields touching two-decade highs and the EU's economic affairs chief stating the ECB "will have to respond." The shift marks a notable repricing from the easing trajectory priced earlier in the cycle, and leaves the ECB navigating a deteriorating growth backdrop simultaneously — the composite PMI's fall to 47.5 on Thursday confirmed the bloc is contracting at its fastest pace in two and a half years. France's reading was particularly weak, falling to levels last seen in 2020, while Germany showed more resilience. Rapidan Energy's assessment that a Hormuz disruption persisting through August could push Brent toward $130 and deliver a stagflationary impulse comparable in scale to past oil downturns represents the tail scenario the ECB appears least equipped to handle.

UK Politics and Gilts

UK political dynamics remained a persistent source of noise throughout the week. Health secretary Wes Streeting's resignation and subsequent platform-building for a Labour leadership bid, combined with Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham's announcement of a by-election route to challenging Keir Starmer, prompted hedge funds and asset managers to build bearish sterling option positions early in the week. Burnham's mid-week commitment to preserve existing fiscal borrowing limits offered some reassurance to gilt markets, and UK CPI data released on Wednesday fell to its lowest reading in more than a year — a divergence from euro-area inflation dynamics that prompted traders to pare Bank of England hike expectations. Chancellor Rachel Reeves moved separately on multiple fronts: abandoning a planned fuel-duty increase (complicating the fiscal arithmetic at a sensitive moment for gilts), preparing cost-of-living measures including free summer bus travel for children, concluding a free-trade agreement with the Gulf Cooperation Council, and closing a corporate tax loophole used by energy producers projected to yield roughly £300 million annually. The Foreign Office simultaneously announced job cuts that would roughly halve its Iran-related team — a curious resource decision given the conflict's third month of disruption. Reform UK's overtaking of the Conservatives as the principal party of the British Right, with deputy leader Richard Tice warning investors against participating in the offshore wind AR7 auction, introduces a meaningful policy risk premium for UK green infrastructure assets that warrants reassessment.

EU-US Trade and Corporate News

EU lawmakers and member states reached agreement on the US trade deal text on Tuesday, clearing a procedural hurdle that averts auto tariffs rising to 25%. The outcome offers near-term relief but formalizes a less favorable arrangement than the pre-tariff baseline — eurozone export data underscoring the stakes, with March shipments to the US down 37% year-on-year. Separately, the EU is weighing defensive measures against Chinese import volumes ahead of a June leaders' summit, with Beijing signaling retaliation against the pending Nuctech subsidy probe. In corporate news, Publicis rose 2.4% on Monday after agreeing to acquire LiveRamp for approximately $2.5 billion; Marks & Spencer guided full-year profit above pre-cyberattack levels and gained 4.7% on Wednesday; Ubisoft fell 14% on Thursday after guiding to declining sales; and the Estée Lauder–Puig tie-up around Charlotte Tilbury collapsed over compensation terms on Friday, sending Puig shares sharply lower. Germany's climate advisory council warned the country is likely to miss its 2030 emissions targets by up to 100 million tons of CO2, while Germany formally launched Uniper's privatization. Chancellor Merz proposed associate EU membership for Ukraine, a formulation Kyiv has previously received with limited enthusiasm. ---

Global

  • Brent crude traded in a wide range through the week — from above $111 on Monday to near $105 by Friday — as US-Iran diplomatic signals alternated between cautious optimism and renewed threat, with the Strait of Hormuz remaining largely closed for a third consecutive month.
  • G7 long-bond yields reached two-decade highs, with the 30-year US Treasury touching 5.19% on Tuesday and Japan's super-long yields hitting post-1999 records, as the energy shock overwhelmed the disinflationary forces central banks had been counting on.
  • Vladimir Putin arrived in Beijing on Monday seeking better terms on Power of Siberia 2, but structural leverage favors Xi: Russia's economy is heavily dependent on Chinese demand, Sunday's record Ukrainian drone strike on Moscow underscored vulnerabilities, and Xi had just completed his own summit with Trump.
  • Gulf sovereign wealth funds are turning materially inward — Saudi Arabia's PIF cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from near 50%, and war-related costs equivalent to roughly 1.5% of Saudi GDP in the first month are compressing the petrodollar recycling pool that has historically supported global asset prices.
  • Scientists formally retired the RCP8.5 worst-case warming scenario as implausible; BloombergNEF's New Energy Outlook projects 2.4°C warming by 2050 under current trajectories, with solar set to become the largest single power source by 2032.

The Hormuz Stalemate and Its Macro Consequences

The US-Iran standoff entered its third month without diplomatic resolution, though the week produced more movement than most. President Trump called off planned strikes Monday after Saudi Arabia and Gulf allies requested additional time for diplomacy, sending Brent 1.8% lower to around $110. By Wednesday, Iran's Foreign Ministry confirmed it was reviewing a US response to Tehran's 14-point proposal — which envisions a short-term arrangement reopening the strait in exchange for lifting the port blockade, followed by deeper nuclear negotiations — and Trump told reporters Washington was in the "final stages." Brent fell 5.6% on Wednesday on those signals. The relief proved partial: Supreme Leader comments insisting Tehran retain its uranium stockpile and a dispute over a proposed Iran-Oman toll regime in the strait complicated the picture, and by Friday Brent had recovered to above $105 with Trump reiterating the threat of renewed strikes. Goldman Sachs estimates visible global crude and product inventories drew down at a record 8.7 million barrels per day in May, nearly double the conflict's running average — a rate that, if sustained, points to demand destruction rather than supply restoration as the rebalancing mechanism. Rapidan Energy's assessment that a disruption persisting through August could push Brent toward $130 with macroeconomic consequences comparable to 2008 represents a tail scenario the diplomatic track has not yet foreclosed. Physical workarounds are advancing: the UAE is accelerating pipeline construction to Fujairah to double bypass capacity, Singapore's state LNG buyer has secured non-Middle East cargoes through year-end, and NATO is reportedly discussing vessel escort options if the strait remains closed into July.

The Bond Market as Policy Signal

The global sovereign bond selloff deepened through the week before partially stabilizing on Iran diplomatic signals. Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields reached its highest level since July 2008, with US 30-year yields touching 5.19% on Tuesday, Japanese super-long yields hitting post-1999 records following PM Takaichi's fiscal reversal, and UK gilts pricing political risk around a potential Burnham premiership. The drivers extend beyond oil: sticky underlying inflation, AI-related capital expenditure, deteriorating fiscal trajectories, and demographic pressures are reinforcing one another in a way that distinguishes this episode from a simple energy shock. The FOMC minutes released Wednesday showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" preferred dropping the easing bias. Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike on Wednesday to defend the rupiah. The ECB signaled a likely June hike. The RBA has already delivered three hikes this year. TS Lombard's Dario Perkins characterized the environment as potentially heralding "rate hikes everywhere," with 2% targets functioning increasingly as a floor rather than a ceiling. The tension between that tightening bias and Pimco's observation that prior energy shocks have historically culminated in recession remains the defining macro ambiguity heading into summer.

Gulf Capital Flows and Petrodollar Recycling

A slower-moving but potentially consequential structural shift emerged more clearly through the week's analysis: the contraction of the petrodollar recycling mechanism. Saudi Arabia's first-quarter budget reflected roughly $17 billion in war-related costs, equivalent to 1.5% of GDP. Qatar faces potential losses of $20 billion from damaged gas infrastructure that could take five years to restore. The PIF had already cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from a prior ambition near 50% before the conflict began; the war is accelerating the inward turn, with LIV Golf preparing for possible bankruptcy after PIF withdrew funding and a $200 million Metropolitan Opera commitment abandoned. Saudi Arabia is now pursuing a $35 billion Aramco privatization to court domestic capital rather than export it. The multi-trillion-dollar investment pledges made to the Trump administration in 2025 look progressively less deliverable. For US markets, the implication is incremental at present: a structurally smaller Gulf bid for Treasuries and US equities at the margin, at a moment when Treasury supply is already elevated. Sovereigns historically supported by Gulf bilateral financing — Egypt, Pakistan, Jordan — face a tighter external environment, and Asian recipients of GCC foreign direct investment, including Indian infrastructure platforms and Southeast Asian data-center projects, may face delayed commitments depending on the conflict's duration.

Geopolitics and the Russia-China Axis

Putin's Beijing visit clarified the asymmetry of the relationship. Russia arrived weakened by Sunday's record Ukrainian drone strike on Moscow, with its economy heavily dependent on Chinese demand for hydrocarbons and on Beijing for over 90% of sanctioned technology imports. Xi, fresh from his Trump summit and able to offer or withhold leverage on Power of Siberia 2 pricing, retains the structural upper hand. Both sides will emphasize their "no-limits" partnership publicly; the substantive question of gas pricing terms — the core of Russia's leverage pitch, premised on Middle East supply disruption — remains unconfirmed. The September expiry of the US-China trade truce, China's dominance over rare earth separation underpinning roughly $1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity, and Trump's July 24 tariff deadline represent the principal near-term flashpoints for the bilateral relationship. Whether the Beijing détente forged at last week's Trump-Xi summit proves durable against those pressures remains to be seen.
