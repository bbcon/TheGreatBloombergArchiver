The 3% Solution

A main element of the spring and fall IMF-World Bank gatherings sees finance 
chiefs from around the world give updates on their own economies’ trajectories. 
The US on Monday kicked the week off with its perception of how things are 
going for the world’s No. 1.

The annual Economic Report of the President assured the public 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2026-power-bills-midterm-election-issue/?cmpid=BBD041426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 that weaker American job growth shouldn’t be a source of concern, while 
deregulatory efforts under way will lift constraints on GDP gains and 
contribute to a boom in productivity.

Growth is projected at 3.5% this year, and is seen averaging 3% through 2036. 
Don’t expect to see such “3 handles” when the IMF on Tuesday morning in 
Washington releases its latest projections in the World Economic Outlook. As of 
last fall, the IMF was projectingsub-2% rates 
<https://data.imf.org/en/Data-Explorer?datasetUrn=IMF.RES:WEO(9.0.0)> for the 
US at end of this decade. (Federal Reserve 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/fed-s-miran-sees-inflation-close-to-target-a-year-from-now?cmpid=BBD041426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 policymakers see 2% as the longer-run trend.)

The White House is also predicting a drop in interest rates, with 10-year 
Treasury yields coming down to 3.7% this year — from about 4.30% now — and 
dropping further to 3.3% over the 2030-36 period. By contrast, the nonpartisan 
Congressional Budget Office waspenciling in 4.4% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/cbo-boosts-us-deficit-call-by-1-4-trillion-on-trump-policies?cmpid=BBD041426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 for 2031-36.

Among other takeaways from the White House report 
<https://www.govinfo.gov/app/details/ERP-2026>:

ESG Principles

Investment strategies aimed at promoting environmental, social and governance 
practices have led to “capital misallocation” and cost the US as much as $196 
billion over the 2016-23 period.

DEI Policies

US employers’ diversity, equity and inclusion policies have been another 
damper. “Promotion into management on the basis of race — a key consequence of 
DEI initiatives — cost the US economy roughly $94 billion in 2023 alone, based 
on one frame of analysis. Now that firms are curtailing their programs, that 
should be stem economic losses, the report said.

Immigration

“Uncontrolled illegal immigration inflated the price of housing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/housing-shortage-is-at-least-10-million-homes-white-house-says?cmpid=BBD041426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 and lowered real wages for Americans—all while burdening taxpayers with 
hundreds of billions of dollars in benefits.” Trump’s crackdown has contributed 
to a drop in job growth, the White House recognized. But the upside is that 
only about 16,000payroll gains 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-03/us-adds-178-000-jobs-unemployment-rate-drops-to-4-3?cmpid=BBD041426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260414>
 were needed per month by late last year to keep the unemployment rate stable, 
the report said.

Car Seats

One illustration of the administration’s argument that overbearing regulation 
holds the economy back: mandated use of car seats for older children, which one 
study says have prevented thousands of births each year. The thinking is, as 
many standard vehicles cannot safely fit three car seats, parents are dissuaded 
from having a third child.

“Thoughtful deregulation can simultaneously boost growth, reduce inflation, 
and improve fiscal sustainability,” the White House said. Whether that’s a 
compelling message to the rest of the world remains to be seen.

Need-to-Know Research

Back on Feb. 23, economists at Goldman Sachs listed losses from private credit 
loans among its top five risks for its 2026, along with a stock market 
correction, disruption from AI, tariffs and geopolitical tensions that spark a 
spike in oil prices. Less than a week later, US and Israeli strikes on Iran 
began.

Of course, the Iran war has vaulted to the top of economists lists ever since. 
But that’s not to say the other risks have disappeared. Now, fresh research 
from Oxford Economics says recent stresses in the private credit market — while 
worrying — are unlikely to pose a major risk to financial and economic 
stability.
Sources: Oxford Economics, Haver Analytics, Macrobond
* Average of Blackstone, KKR, Apollo and Ares ** Dow Jones composite
Unlike ahead of the global financial crisis, there’s no major credit boom 
right now, with private sector leverage declining in recent years. “That said, 
there is scope for some unpleasant repercussions,” economist Adam Slater wrote.

Banks’ exposure to private credit looks moderate at around $300 billion, but 
exposure to other non-bank financial institutions is an additional $2 trillion 
and has been rising quickly. Insurers, whose exposure to private credit is 
around $1trillion, could also suffer negative consequences, according to the 
note.
