**Climate and Inflation Risks**
Oxford Economics published analysis this week quantifying the inflationary pass-through from climate-driven food supply disruptions. Under a severe shock scenario, eurozone food prices could rise by 1.6 percentage points annually, translating into a 0.6 percentage point increase in headline inflation — a materially larger effect than for other G7 economies. The asymmetry likely reflects the eurozone's greater agricultural exposure and the structure of food price indexation within the bloc. For the ECB, this represents a medium-term upside risk to the inflation path that is difficult to address through conventional monetary policy tools, given its supply-side origin. The finding arrives as Europe experiences an active heat wave, lending immediate salience to what might otherwise appear an abstract modeling exercise.

**UK Political Economy**
Reform UK's position as Britain's most popular party carries concrete policy implications. The party's deputy leader has outlined plans to end net-zero commitments — a posture described internally as opposition to what it calls "net stupid zero." Were Reform to translate polling strength into electoral power, the UK's legally binding climate targets and associated green investment frameworks would face material revision. For investors in UK renewable energy, carbon markets, and ESG-linked instruments, the political trajectory warrants monitoring. The tension between Reform's platform and existing statutory obligations under the Climate Change Act would likely generate significant legal and regulatory uncertainty before any legislative change could take effect.
