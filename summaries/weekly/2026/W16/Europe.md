**Hungary: A Structural Shift in European Politics**
Viktor Orbán's defeat on Sunday, April 13, represents the most consequential shift in European politics in several years. Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured what appears to be a parliamentary supermajority, reducing Fidesz to a rump opposition after 16 years of what one Macquarie strategist characterized as "unchecked executive power through erosion of institutional pillars." The immediate European dividend is substantial: Orbán's Hungary had repeatedly exercised veto power to block EU measures requiring unanimity, holding up approximately €90 billion in support for Ukraine and repeatedly fracturing EU sanctions consensus. Magyar has pledged to dismantle the illiberal governance structures, reorient toward Brussels and NATO, and supports eventual euro adoption. German Chancellor Merz moved quickly to press for rapid disbursement of the Ukraine loan package, meeting with President Zelensky in Berlin on Wednesday. The forint surged to a four-year high on Monday, April 14, triggering what markets have described as a "euro convergence trade." Orbán called a Fidesz party congress for April 28 to review the defeat. Complications remain: Magyar inherits an economy that has consistently underperformed regional peers Poland and Czechia, is dependent on Russian energy imports, and carries commercial ties to Asian battery manufacturers that will constrain the pace of reorientation. Veto rights over EU decisions may also prove too useful a tool to abandon entirely even under new leadership.

**ECB Policy: Genuine Uncertainty**
ECB President Lagarde's comments at the IMF Spring Meetings on Tuesday and Wednesday placed the eurozone explicitly between two scenarios without resolving them. The March baseline incorporated two rate increases in 2026; the adverse scenario projects 3.5% inflation this year. Lagarde stated the ECB has no tightening bias while simultaneously acknowledging that "looking through" the energy shock risks undermining price stability credibility — positions in tension that she did not reconcile. ECB policymaker Madis Müller separately called for vigilance on inflation risks. A Bloomberg survey published later in the week pointed to a June hike as increasingly plausible given inflation tracking above earlier projections. Bank of England Governor Bailey struck a similar note of cautious ambiguity, signaling no urgency to move while acknowledging the difficulty of assessing the war's full economic impact. Pimco indicated it sees value in European bonds following the war-driven selloff, suggesting institutional buyers view current yield levels as attractive entry points.

**Luxury Shock and Industrial Stress**
The week delivered a sharp earnings reality check for European luxury. Hermès shares fell as much as 14% on Wednesday, April 15 — its largest single-day decline on record — after first-quarter sales growth disappointed, with management attributing weakness to the confidence effects of the war alongside broader consumer caution. Kering also reported disappointing figures. LVMH's fashion and leather goods division, home to Louis Vuitton, reported sales declining in the first quarter when it reported on Monday, April 14. The contrast with ASML could hardly be more pronounced: Europe's most valuable company raised its full-year guidance to €36–40 billion on Wednesday, with its CEO noting AI demand means supply cannot meet orders "for the foreseeable future." European chemical producers face acute input cost pressures from surging fossil-fuel feedstock prices, with SKW Stickstoffwerke Piesteritz's COO warning that loss of basic chemical supply chains could trigger broader economic damage. The Port of Antwerp-Bruges, home to major operations for TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil, and BASF, is managing additional disruption from a fuel spill. Alstom fell 36% in Paris on Thursday, April 17 — its steepest single-day decline since 2023 — after withdrawing full-year free cash flow guidance. Lufthansa shares fell 4.4% on Monday following a two-day pilot strike over pensions.

**UK Political Turbulence**
Prime Minister Starmer's position deteriorated materially on Thursday, April 17, when it emerged that Washington Ambassador Peter Mandelson had received security clearance despite objections from vetting officials — a decision complicated by Mandelson's past associations with Jeffrey Epstein. Starmer's response, dismissing Foreign Office permanent secretary Olly Robbins, has been widely characterized as managing the political optics rather than addressing the underlying judgment question. Calls for resignation are growing. The episode arrives as Starmer was attempting to position the UK as a constructive international actor, co-hosting with France a summit on establishing a naval force to ensure freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz — a project whose European support base remains fractured. Separately, UK household energy costs are tracking roughly £500 higher for a typical household, the RICS homebuyer appetite gauge fell to its lowest since August 2023, and Moody's warned that French elections risk stalling budget consolidation. In French telecoms, three major operators entered exclusive talks to acquire Patrick Drahi's SFR at approximately $24 billion on Thursday, a transaction that would reshape the country's mobile landscape. Italy's Meloni called Trump's remarks about Pope Leo XIV "unacceptable," a signal of growing distance from Washington that reflects wider European discomfort with the conflict's management.
