The week's most consequential European development may be the ECB's unmistakable pivot toward a June rate hike. President Christine Lagarde disclosed that policymakers debated an immediate increase at Thursday's meeting but concluded they had "insufficient information" to act — language that, according to people familiar with deliberations, implies only a narrow path to avoiding a hike in June if hostilities at the Strait of Hormuz persist. Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel separately indicated he favors a June move absent a material improvement in the outlook. The Bank of England delivered a similarly hawkish signal: Chief Economist Huw Pill voted for an immediate increase, and Governor Andrew Bailey warned that waiting for second-round inflation effects to materialize before acting would mean responding "too late." Markets are pricing a June hike as the base case for both institutions. The White House's Kevin Hassett publicly characterized potential ECB or Fed rate hikes as "policy errors," an unusual intervention in the affairs of independent central banks.

On UK trade, President Trump announced the removal of some Scotch whisky tariffs following King Charles III's visit to Washington — framed by Trump as a personal gesture to the monarch. Buckingham Palace expressed gratitude, and the concession represents a tangible, if narrowly scoped, trade gain for London. British officials privately expressed skepticism, however, that the royal visit would meaningfully alter Trump's underlying disposition toward Prime Minister Starmer or accelerate a broader UK-US trade agreement.

The UK also raised its national terror threat level to "severe" following a stabbing in London's Golders Green district that left two Jewish men hospitalized. Prime Minister Starmer pledged to deploy the "full power of the state" against antisemitism. Separately, Italy's Meloni government announced housing-access measures ahead of next year's general election, while Spain's persistent child poverty — affecting roughly one-third of children, a share exceeded in the EU only by Bulgaria — drew renewed attention despite years of above-average economic growth.
