 Anywhere but the US 



In stock markets, US exceptionalism is under pressure.

With an increase of just 1.4% this year, the S&P 500 Index is trailing an MSCI 
index of world stocks excluding the US by 7.7 percentage points. A gauge of 
Asian stocks is outpacing the US by 10 points. That sounds huge until you look 
at Latin America — the MSCI index for the region has advanced 21% in 2026.

In fact, the US gauge ranks a rather sad 69th among the 92 stock indexes 
around the world tracked by Bloomberg. South Korea is the world’s 
best-performing market with a 30% surge -- and it built on that today with 
another 3.1% jump.

It's shaping up to be a golden year for Asia’s markets. The region's biggest 
tech companies are indispensable to the AI supply chain, while countries from 
South Korea to Japan are taking steps to boost shareholder returns. Relatively 
cheaper valuations and firmer growth prospects (AI is a big part of that 
<https://bloom.bg/4cqzuvv>) also are luring stock buyers.

A robust economic backdrop -- reinforced by a weakening dollar -- is adding to 
the allure, with the International Monetary Fund projecting that Asia will 
contribute around 60% of global growth this year. Meanwhile, in the US, 
investors are fleeing companies whose businesses areat risk from AI 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZAH7YL>.

“That is what 2026 will be about: diversification across regions but also 
across sectors,” says Elfreda Jonker at Alphinity Investment Management.

The end of American exceptionalism doesn't have to mean the US market will 
tank. But a little of the wealth invested in US markets can go along way if 
it's spread around the rest of the world. —Paul Dobson 
<https://bloom.bg/3DbGBZA>

 Revenge of the old economy 



One thing that is working in the US stock market: some titans of the old 
economy <https://bloom.bg/4co75Gj>. The once-sleepy companies that move goods 
and people through America’s roads, rails, skies and water are mobilizing in a 
rapid rally.

After years of lagging the major US equities benchmarks, the Dow Jones 
Transportation Average is in the lead, outperforming the S&P 500 by 13 
percentage points in the past month and a half, near the most since the 
financial crisis.

The gauge — which contains industry stalwarts such as CSX, FedEx, Old Dominion 
Freight Line and even United Airlines — has been catapulted higher by strong 
data and a pivot away from the big tech winners of the past few years.

“The sector is one of the most economically sensitive, as higher activity 
levels correspond with items needing to move around both the country (and the 
world),” said Sameer Samana of the Wells Fargo Investment Institute. —Joel Leon 
<https://bloom.bg/3H6WMZZ> and Matthew Griffin <https://bloom.bg/46O4jXl>
