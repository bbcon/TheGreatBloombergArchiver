**Regional Tariff Arithmetic and the Shifting Trade Calculus**
The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariffs materially reconfigured Asia's trade exposure early in the week. Morgan Stanley estimated average US levies on Chinese goods fell to 24% from 32%, with the Asia-wide weighted average dropping to approximately 17% from 20%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng China Enterprises index rallied 2.65% on Monday, though mainland China and Japan markets were closed for holidays, limiting broader price discovery. The subsequent activation of a 10% global Section 122 tariff on Tuesday created a more complex winners-and-losers map: some adversarial nations fared relatively better while prior beneficiaries lost preferential arrangements. India, which had been advancing toward an interim deal with Washington, postponed its trade mission following the ruling, citing uncertainty over the new framework's legal durability.

**China: Currency, Consumption, and Diplomatic Positioning**
China's yuan ended its longest winning streak since 2010 on Friday after the People's Bank of China moved to temper appreciation by removing the 20% reserve requirement on foreign-currency forward contracts, effective March 2. The adjustment signals that authorities regard excessive yuan strength as a risk to exporters at a time of entrenched deflationary pressure — China's record $1.2 trillion trade surplus continues to generate currency inflows that complicate domestic policy. Lunar New Year tourism data offered a mixed read: 596 million trips were recorded over the nine-day break, 95 million more than a year earlier, but per-trip spending of 1,348 yuan fell marginally short of the prior year, consistent with a consumer that is mobile but cautious. Diplomatically, Beijing warned against further tariff increases ahead of the planned Trump-Xi summit in Beijing on March 31, while a record $112 billion discrepancy between Chinese export figures and US Customs arrival data for 2025 suggested that up to a quarter of China-origin shipments may have bypassed tariff controls, complicating both revenue collection and bilateral trade assessment.

**Japan: BOJ Constraints and Cross-Shareholding Reform**
Japan's week was shaped by two political economy developments with opposing implications. The yen fell approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar on Tuesday following a Mainichi newspaper report that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi expressed apprehension over further Bank of Japan rate increases at a recent meeting with Governor Ueda — a signal that political resistance to normalization may be more entrenched than markets had assumed following her election victory. Later in the week, Takaichi nominated two reflationist academics to forthcoming BOJ board openings, reinforcing those concerns and pressuring both the yen and JGBs. Notably, former BOJ Governor Haruhiko Kuroda publicly called for rate hikes and warned the yen may be "somewhat too weak" — a striking inversion of roles. On the structural reform side, Reuters reported Toyota is preparing to unwind approximately ¥3 trillion ($19 billion) in cross-shareholdings with banks and financial institutions, a meaningful step in Japan's multi-year effort to improve capital efficiency.

**South Korea and the AI Hardware Rotation**
South Korea emerged as one of the week's clearest beneficiaries of the AI infrastructure rotation. The Kospi advanced 1.91% on Wednesday to 6,084, with Samsung Electronics up 70% year-to-date and SK Hynix gaining 57%. The Bank of Korea held its benchmark rate at 2.5% while upgrading both growth and inflation forecasts, having previously told parliament that the semiconductor cycle is exhibiting unusual strength and exports jumped 47% in the first 20 days of February on a working-day-adjusted basis. South Korea's total equity market capitalization reached $3.76 trillion, surpassing France to rank ninth globally. Foreign funds nonetheless sold a record net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks on Friday after the benchmark's near-50% year-to-date run, a sign that profit-taking pressure is building at elevated levels. The National Pension Service posted an 18.82% return for 2025, its strongest on record.

**Iran Shock: Asian Energy Exposure**
Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iran introduced the week's most consequential late development for Asian economies. China, Japan, South Korea, and India are all major importers of Middle Eastern crude, much of which transits the Strait of Hormuz. Bloomberg Economics estimated a full Hormuz closure could push oil to $108 per barrel — representing a significant terms-of-trade deterioration for energy-import-dependent economies. China's position is particularly complex: Beijing is Iran's largest oil trading partner, and the strikes place it in a diplomatically awkward position vis-à-vis Washington, with whom trade relations are already strained ahead of the March 31 summit.
