The dominant cross-cutting theme remains the US-Iran conflict and its effects on oil markets, supply chains, and the global growth outlook. Brent crude's intraday range on Monday — a swing of approximately $36, the widest on record and surpassing even the moves seen at the onset of the Ukraine invasion — illustrated the degree of market stress, even as prices ultimately settled near $90. The swift reversal from near-$120 to below $90 in a single session reflects both genuine residual optimism about conflict duration and the extreme sensitivity of sentiment to any signal from Trump or the White House. Six-month Brent futures are pricing approximately $80 per barrel, roughly 10% above pre-conflict levels — meaningful, but not yet the sustained $110-plus scenario that historical analysis suggests is necessary to generate a full macro regime shift.

The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively at a near-standstill, with at least twelve vessel clusters identified near the waterway, indicating elevated electronic interference and GPS jamming consistent with active military operations. Oxford Economics data show trade through the strait has dried up materially. Deepening Middle East production cuts are estimated to have removed approximately 6% of global supply. Qatar's LNG export facility, the world's largest, has suffered a war-related outage, tightening global gas markets and leaving some Asian buyers struggling to source prompt cargoes.

G-7 energy ministers are meeting virtually on Tuesday to coordinate responses. Finance ministers have stated readiness to release strategic petroleum reserves, though that threshold has not yet been reached. French President Macron is separately pursuing a joint maritime escort mission for container vessels once initial hostilities subside. Russia's posture adds complexity: President Putin has instructed domestic energy companies to exploit elevated prices to pay down debt, and Moscow is reportedly considering ending most gas sales to the EU, while also holding what the Kremlin described as constructive discussions with Trump on both Iran and Ukraine.

The broader macro framework for assessing duration and damage is reasonably clear among analysts: Societe Generale and Deutsche Bank both identify the shock's persistence and central bank reaction functions — particularly the Fed's — as the two decisive variables. A hawkish Fed pivot in response to oil-driven inflation, layered on an already softening US labor market and strained credit conditions, would constitute a materially more adverse outcome than current market pricing implies. That question is unlikely to be resolved before next week's FOMC meeting.
