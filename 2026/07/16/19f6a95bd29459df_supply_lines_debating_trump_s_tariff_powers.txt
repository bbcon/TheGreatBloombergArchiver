A group of bipartisan lawmakers and the White House may have reached a rare 
deal on a bill to authorize sanctions on countries that buy Russian oil, a 
measure that some observers worry may hand over more unprecedented tariff 
authority to President Donald Trump.

The new Russian sanctions legislation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/trump-to-support-graham-led-russia-sanctions-bill?cmpid=BBD071626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6485>
, which was driven by thelate Senator Lindsey Graham 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-12/us-senator-lindsey-graham-dies-from-sudden-illness?cmpid=BBD071626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6485>
, targets the top five purchasers of Russian crude oil and natural gas, most 
notably China and India.

The bill has prompted criticism from trade experts who say the text is too lax 
in its delegation of tariff authority. The top two Democrats on committees that 
handle trade and tax matters said they did not support the legislation.

Related: Russia Sanctions Threat May Complicate US-India Trade Talks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/russia-oil-sanctions-threat-may-complicate-us-india-trade-talks?cmpid=BBD071626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6485>

The “latest draft of the Sanctioning Russia Act is a prescription for bedlam 
and higher tariffs,” Representative Richard Neal and Senator Ron Wyden said in 
a statement. “It grants Trump authority to impose tariffs up to 100% on major 
trading partners and allies.”

Several Democrats are concerned that parts of the bill are written too 
vaguely, and could be used by the White House to abuse tariff powers, according 
to a person familiar with the matter.

Lacking Specifics

The bill doesn’t specify where the data to determine the top five importers of 
Russian oil should come from, for example, so some fear it could be manipulated 
by members of the administration, the person said. Another concern is that is 
doesn’t have a sunset clause, allowing the duties to potentially be in place in 
perpetuity.

Senator Richard Blumenthal, a Connecticut Democrat, said that he disagreed 
with the interpretation from his Democratic colleagues and that he is talking 
with Wyden about his concerns. Still, he acknowledged that the chamber probably 
would not have unanimous Democrat support.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/eu-freezes-russian-oil-price-cap-for-a-week-as-sanctions-stall?cmpid=BBD071626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6485>

Peter Harrell, who served as senior director for international economics for 
former President Joe Biden, said he is “deeply opposed” to the tariff 
provisions in the bill.

“Let’s be direct: Trump would love this as a tool to threaten 100% tariffs on 
these countries for reasons completely unrelated to Russia,” Harrell said in a
post on X <https://x.com/petereharrell/status/2077406467485757601?s=20>. “And 
the bill would let him do that, so long as these countries had, in fact, 
imported some Russian oil/gas or facilitated evasion.”

Asked about the Russia sanctions bill, US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer 
said Trump “wants to be careful about how he deploys this kind of leverage” 
partly because “geopolitics are very fragile.”
