**Monetary Policy and Inflation**
The week's most consequential domestic development was Tuesday's CPI print, which showed headline inflation at 3.8% year-on-year — the highest reading since 2023 and above the Federal Reserve's upper tolerance band. The Strait of Hormuz closure remains the primary driver through elevated energy costs, but the composition of the report was more troubling than the headline: core inflation ex-energy ran at 2.8%, sticky prices re-crossed 3%, and the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply, suggesting the oil shock is generating second-order wage and price effects rather than operating as a purely relative-price disturbance. Consumer prices rose faster than average hourly earnings in April, the first such occurrence since 2022. Wednesday's PPI report reinforced the picture, rising 6% year-on-year — its highest reading since 2022 on an ex-pandemic basis — led by energy and freight, with electronics components inflating 27% year-on-year as AI-driven memory shortages ended decades of technology-sector disinflation. Oxford Economics now projects the PCE deflator reaching 3.8%, effectively foreclosing near-term rate cuts. The Fed's policy horizon shifted accordingly: Bank of America explicitly recommended short positions in two-year Treasuries, characterizing rate-hike risk as underpriced, while Fed Governor Barr described inflation as "the overwhelming risk facing the economy."

**Federal Reserve Leadership Transition**
Kevin Warsh's Senate confirmation on Wednesday — by the narrowest margin on record — represents one of the more consequential central bank leadership changes in decades, delivered at a particularly difficult moment. Rather than the rate-cutting cycle that markets initially priced around his appointment, Warsh inherits a policy environment where the probability distribution for the fed funds rate has shifted decisively toward tightening. Whether he will defend institutional independence against an administration that has historically expressed preferences on monetary policy remains to be seen.

**Equity Markets and AI Concentration**
US equity markets spent most of the week threading a narrow path between AI-driven earnings momentum and rising rate anxiety. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 set fresh records through Thursday before retreating Friday on PPI data and oil price pressure. The concentration dynamic sharpened further: the top ten S&P 500 constituents account for approximately 40% of index market capitalization, an all-time high, while the SOX semiconductor index has outperformed the Magnificent Seven since the ChatGPT launch. Alphabet closed the week with a market cap of $4.8 trillion against Nvidia's near-$6 trillion, having gained 34% in April alone. The simultaneous rise in the VIX alongside equity prices — a historically uncommon configuration — may signal that investors are hedging aggressively into the rally rather than expressing unqualified conviction. Critics including Jeremy Grantham and Michael Burry publicly flagged valuation concerns, though earnings revisions at semiconductor names continue to outpace price appreciation, with Sandisk and Micron trading at roughly nine times forward earnings despite gains of 482% and 172% respectively year-to-date.

**Fiscal and Policy Developments**
The administration's legal exposure on trade policy intensified this week. A federal trade court declared the president's 10% global tariffs unlawful, and the government responded by requesting a pause pending appeals while processing over $35.5 billion in tariff refunds from a related Supreme Court ruling. The FEMA leadership situation drew scrutiny ahead of the June 1 hurricane season: newly nominated director Cameron Hamilton previously authored a memo advocating the agency's abolition, and roughly 5,000 staffers have departed since January. Trump's financial disclosures separately revealed personal equity purchases in Q1 including Nvidia, Oracle, Microsoft, Boeing, and Costco — a detail unlikely to recede from political circulation with midterms less than six months away.
