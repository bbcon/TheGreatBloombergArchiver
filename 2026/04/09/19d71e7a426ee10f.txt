Warfare Economics

My first job as a journalist on one occasion called for me to tour a 
defense-industry expo. I quickly observed this was a sector that generated its 
own particular vocabulary, as I perused a poster touting “Newly Enhanced 
Lethality!”

The Ukraine and Iran wars have put focus on the term “attritable.” Derived 
from the word “attrition,” it refers to a weapon that’s inexpensive enough that 
you don’t mind losing it in a battle of attrition.

Cheap drones have been a key feature of the Russia-Ukraine war for years now. 
And, as Bloomberg reported three days into the Iran conflict, it had “become 
attritional 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/iran-strikes-missile-math-20-000-iranian-drones-take-on-4-million-patriots?cmpid=BBD040926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
.” Iran’s bombardment of American partners in the Gulf featured utterly 
“attritable” $20,000 drones, and they were being shot down with $4 million 
missiles — which don’t quite seem to fit the definition.

That munitions-cost mismatch has raised big questions about US capacity to 
continue projecting power around the world, as Washington pulls sophisticated, 
costly missilesout of other theaters 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-04/us-deploys-bulk-of-stealthy-long-range-missile-for-iran-war?cmpid=BBD040926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 for use in the Gulf. Japan’s defense minister, Shinjiro Koizumi, pointed to 
concerns about the potential for a security vacuum in Asia as a consequence of 
what’s happening in the Middle East.

The first Cold War helped to reshape the US economy as the Pentagon 
prioritized building ballistic missiles and strategic bombers. A recent 
National Bureau of Economic Research paper,flagged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-31/war-fallout-inflation-surge-seen-in-euro-zone-amid-energy-crunch?cmpid=BBD040926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 in this newsletter last week, referenced how Western and Mountain states 
benefited from these new industries, which provided sustained demand for
low-skill workers <http://www.nber.org/papers/w35008>.

It also saw major investments in naval production, churning out the kind of 
warships that are now looking dangerously vulnerable to attritable weapons — 
and have proved unable to reopen theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/hormuz-stays-blocked-for-now-as-hundreds-of-ships-seek-escape?cmpid=BBD040926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
.

The new era of US confrontation against Russia, China, Iran and others seems 
unlikely to produce the same economic effects as seen decades ago.

“Defense procurement is undergoing a transformation as scalable weaponry and 
next-generation technologies redefine battlefield capabilities,” Noah Ramos, 
chief innovation strategist at Alpine Macro, wrote in a note this week.

The Iran war serves “as the most recent ‘stress test’ of this thesis since 
Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. 38 days into the conflict has solidified our 
conviction that the economics of warfare are shifting irreversibly,” Ramos 
wrote.

Need-to-Know Research

Covid cast a long shadow over the global economy, in countless ways, from the 
death toll to the disrupted education of millions of people. Economists at the 
OECD recently took a look specifically at the impact of so-called long Covid.

The global forum’s analysis 
<https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/addressing-the-costs-and-care-for-long-covid_87a0c171-en.html>
 shows the direct health-care costs of long Covid combined with the wider 
effect of people leaving the workforce and lower productivity will have a 
lasting impact. Economic growth could be reduced by as much as 0.2%, according 
to the OECD.
Source: Organization for Economic Cooperation and Development via Bloomberg
That would amount to $135 billion a year over the next decade, in an 
assumption that incorporates “low or moderate residual transmission of the 
virus leading to new cases of long COVID. Such a figure is “comparable to the 
entire annual health budget of the Netherlands or Spain,” the economists wrote.
