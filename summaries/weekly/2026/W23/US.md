• May nonfarm payrolls — the week's defining macro release — came in at 89,000 on Friday, roughly in line with the 88,000 consensus, with unemployment steady at 4.3%; the print confirmed the labor market is cooling without yet breaking.
• ISM Manufacturing PMI beat expectations on Monday, hitting its highest level in four years, while ISM Services input costs rose at the fastest pace in nearly four years on Wednesday — a combination that complicates the Fed's policy calculus materially.
• PCE inflation has converged with CPI near 4% YoY, well above the Fed's 3% upper band, while real personal income has declined 0.6% over the past year — a pace Goldman Sachs associates with recession conditions.
• The S&P 500 extended a nine-session winning streak driven almost entirely by AI-related technology names before pulling back Thursday–Friday as the Broadcom guidance miss weighed on the complex.
• The Trump administration proposed new tariffs of at least 10% on roughly 60 trading partners on Tuesday, reviving protectionist measures under a Section 301 forced-labor framework after earlier duties were struck down by the Supreme Court.

**Labor Market**
April JOLTS data released Tuesday showed job openings surging to their highest pre-pandemic level, with vacancies once again exceeding unemployed workers — a clear beat relative to recession fears. Friday's May payrolls print of 89,000 confirmed the directional trend: the labor market is softening but not collapsing, with average hourly earnings growth tracking at 3.4% year-over-year, below prevailing inflation. The tension between resilient headline employment and deteriorating real income is the central domestic macro puzzle. Gasoline at a national average of approximately $5.70 per gallon has added roughly $200 per month to household fuel costs since late February, with lower-income cohorts substituting toward public transit — LA Metro weekday ridership is up approximately 8% year-to-date, Boston rail up 13%, Caltrain up 21% in April. The New York Fed corroborated reduced gasoline consumption among lower-income households in its May report.

**Inflation and Monetary Policy**
The ISM Services survey on Wednesday showed input costs accelerating to their fastest pace since 2022, with the Beige Book the same day describing prices rising "at a moderate to strong pace" — energy costs from the Iran conflict transmitting through freight surcharges of up to 50%, packaging, fertilizers, and groceries. The Logistics Managers' Index transport price gauge reached 96, the highest in the report's ten-year history. Fed Governor Logan flagged rising concern that rates may need to move higher; a Brookings paper by Christina and David Romer warned the Fed risks losing credibility by tolerating inflation persistently above target. New Chair Kevin Warsh's focus on trimmed-mean PCE — which is declining — provides some optionality, but with UCLA's Anderson School projecting core CPI peaking at 4.5% by year-end, the easing case has materially weakened. US money fund assets hit a record $8.3 trillion, consistent with a market pricing extended higher-for-longer.

**Trade Policy**
The administration's new tariff proposal — structured as a 10% baseline on most partners and 12.5% on China, Japan, South Korea, Brazil, and Switzerland — adds complexity to an already fragmented trade landscape. Bloomberg Economics estimates the effective US tariff rate rises by only approximately 0.5 percentage points from its current 10.7% level, assuming the expiring Section 122 duties lapse in late July without replacement. A second Section 301 probe targeting industrial overcapacity is expected shortly; analysts identify the potential stacking of these levies as the primary escalation risk. Separately, targeted reductions in steel and aluminum tariffs on select goods — cut to 15% from 25% — were announced to incentivize domestic investment, creating a bifurcated trade posture that will require careful monitoring.

**Equity and Capital Markets**
The AI fundraising cycle reached exceptional scale this week. Alphabet announced an $80 billion equity offering on Monday — including a $10 billion Berkshire Hathaway anchor — representing one of the largest equity raises in corporate history, with Goldman Sachs estimating total 2026 corporate buybacks at $1.3 trillion and new equity supply from IPOs and follow-ons at approximately $675 billion. SpaceX launched investor briefings ahead of a $75 billion IPO targeting June 12 at a $1.8 trillion valuation; Anthropic filed confidentially for a fall listing. The S&P 500's technology sector has outperformed the ex-tech index by 33% over the past three months — the strongest relative quarter on record. The Shiller CAPE ratio has exceeded its September 2000 peak, and a majority of global allocators now expect multiples to expand further. Broadcom's Thursday guidance of $16 billion in AI chip revenue for the current quarter, below consensus, triggered a roughly 14% premarket decline and pulled semiconductor names broadly lower into Friday. Bitcoin fell below $70,000, extending a decline of nearly 45% from its peak, as capital rotated into semiconductor equities.
