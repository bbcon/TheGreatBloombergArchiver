Weekly Brief

Jun 22 – Jun 28, 2026

US

  • Micron Technology's Q3 EPS of $25.11 (vs. $1.91 a year ago) and Q4 revenue guidance of ~$50 billion, roughly 16% above the $43.2 billion consensus, drove a 15–17% after-hours rally Wednesday and anchored a volatile week for tech.
  • Thursday's PCE print (the week's primary macro event) is expected to show core inflation well above the 2% target; Goldman Sachs projects core PCE at 3.2% YoY by December, pushing rate-cut expectations firmly into 2027.
  • The AI trade fractured visibly: memory and infrastructure names surged while the Nasdaq 100 fell more than 1% Friday after Apple raised prices across its device lineup citing chip shortages, and OpenAI's IPO was reported to face delays.
  • Fed stress tests cleared all major banks, prompting dividend increases, while SpaceX's debut $25 billion investment-grade bond offering drew $89 billion in orders despite sustained negative free cash flow.

Monetary Policy

Chicago Fed President Goolsbee stated Monday that above-target inflation is "going the wrong way," reinforcing the hawkish signal from last week's FOMC, where half the committee penciled in at least one 2026 rate hike. Treasury Secretary Bessent invoked Alan Greenspan — who died Monday at age 100 — to argue for Fed forbearance, drawing parallels between the mid-1990s technology productivity boom and the current AI cycle; Chair Warsh has cited the same analogy, though the comparison remains contested. Market-implied pricing shifted rate cuts well into 2027, and JPMorgan, Bank of America, and Goldman strategists coalesced around renewed dollar bullishness, citing the Fed's explicit prioritization of price stability. The 10-year yield edged lower to 4.48% mid-week following Bessent's remarks before retracing.

Corporate Earnings and Markets

Tuesday's global tech selloff — originating in Seoul — arrived on Wall Street with force: the Philadelphia Semiconductor Index recorded its largest single-day decline since June 5, with Micron falling 13% intraday. Micron's Wednesday after-close results reversed the narrative. Twelve-month trailing earnings have quadrupled in two quarters, and the company disclosed 16 multi-year customer agreements averaging three years — a structural signal that may reduce the cyclicality historically associated with memory demand. Goldman partner Bobby Molavi flagged co-correlation risk explicitly, noting that active, passive, hedge fund, and quant strategies are "effectively getting longer AI by the day," creating a dynamic that "feels great on the way up, but can be extremely dangerous on the unwind." Friday's fragmentation — Apple's component-cost-driven price hikes compressing downstream margins — crystallized the divergence: memory names are up over 200% year-to-date while the Magnificent Seven cohort is negative for 2026. FedEx fell 6.7% Friday premarket after earnings beat on profit but flagged trade-policy headwinds. Apogee Therapeutics surged 54% on reports of an ~$11 billion AbbVie acquisition. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi fell 10% Tuesday — triggering a circuit-breaker halt, its largest single-session decline since March — and suffered a second halt Thursday, as Samsung and SK Hynix each fell more than 12% on AI-memory demand concerns before recovering sharply Friday following Micron's blowout results.
  • China's May retail sales fell 0.6% YoY, the first contraction outside the pandemic since 1990, missing the trajectory implied by a successfully developing economy and deepening concerns about structural consumer stagnation.
  • The Bank of Japan reiterated that inflation risks remain tilted to the upside and further tightening is warranted, with Tokyo's key CPI gauge picking up for the first time in eight months; Tokyo's June CPI acceleration reinforces the trajectory.
  • China extended rare earth and tungsten export controls against Japan and the US, with flows of key minerals to Japan at zero since January, in apparent retaliation for Prime Minister Takaichi's Taiwan-related statements.

China: Consumer Stagnation

The May retail sales contraction — 0.6% YoY — is difficult to attribute solely to the expiration of government trade-in subsidy programs; underlying growth averaged 8% annually in 2019 and has run at roughly 4% over the past three years, barely matching the vastly wealthier United States. Macquarie analysts highlight structural parallels with 1990s Japan: a burst asset bubble, subdued consumption, and an aging population, with home prices down approximately 30% from their 2021 peak. The comparison has limits — China's manufacturing sector continues gaining global market share, supported by deliberate currency weakness — but the combination of industrial strength alongside consumer stagnation creates an internally contradictory growth picture with significant implications for commodity-exporting economies.

Korean Semiconductors: Leverage and Volatility

Tuesday's 10% Kospi decline was amplified materially by leveraged single-stock ETFs tracking Samsung and SK Hynix — instruments Korean regulators approved and publicly expressed regret over during the week. An SK Hynix ETF listed in Hong Kong had swelled to $17 billion in assets, translating a 13% single-name decline into roughly a 26% loss for double-leveraged holders. SK Hynix's concurrent announcement of a $29 billion US depositary receipt offering — which would rank among the five largest share sales on record — was absorbed constructively following Micron's demand signal. Alibaba fell to a 16-month low in Hong Kong after Anthropic accused it of orchestrating the largest known illicit access attempt on a US AI model, with Baidu and Xiaomi falling more than 3% in sympathy. ---

Europe

  • Eurozone composite PMI rose to 49.5 in June, above the 48.5 consensus and up from 48.5 in May, though remaining below the expansion threshold for a third consecutive month.
  • UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer resigned Monday, with Andy Burnham emerging as near-certain successor after winning the Makerfield by-election and rivals withdrawing; gilts and sterling showed no acute stress.
  • Europe recorded its worst heatwave on record, with temperatures 5°C–12°C above seasonal averages across France, Germany, Italy, and Spain; French nuclear curtailments pushed evening electricity prices to their highest since the 2022 energy crisis.
  • Rheinmetall shares fell 17% — their sharpest single-session decline in a year — after Germany signaled withdrawal of a major warships contract.

Economic Data

The June eurozone composite PMI beat consensus at 49.5, offering tentative evidence that the region is absorbing the dual headwinds of Iran-related inflation and broader confidence deterioration. The data does not signal recovery — contraction territory persists — but the direction provides modest reassurance. Germany's Ifo business climate brightened slightly in June. Rhine water levels fell sharply during the heatwave, forcing barges below half capacity and disrupting fuel shipments to western Germany and the Netherlands — a supply-side risk that could weigh on July PMIs. French nuclear curtailments and near-record UK and German electricity prices are generating a demand shock that partially offsets the disinflationary impulse from lower oil prices.

UK Political Transition

Starmer's departure — on the tenth anniversary of the Brexit referendum — marks the sixth prime ministerial change since 2016. Burnham inherits constrained fiscal space: the gilt market's 2022 reaction to Liz Truss remains a binding constraint on any new government's ambitions, and several of Burnham's advisers have previously argued for looser fiscal rules. Mizuho's Jordan Rochester noted that any "whiff of fiscal laxity gets punished by the gilt market," a dynamic that likely disciplines pace rather than preventing change. The absence of a drawn-out leadership contest has reduced the risk of unfunded commitments accumulating during a vacuum period. Bank of England rate-cut expectations are firming as falling oil prices and declining short-term inflation expectations reduce pressure for further tightening, though the heatwave-related energy price surge introduces a near-term complication. ---

LatAm

  • Colombia's presidential election delivered a victory for Trump-endorsed conservative Abelardo de la Espriella, marking a sharp policy reversal from outgoing President Petro's leftist program and extending the regional rightward political shift.
  • Peru's presidential count showed leftist Roberto Sánchez holding a slim 50.1% domestic lead, with conservative Keiko Fujimori ahead among overseas voters; Sánchez has moved to challenge all overseas ballots.
  • Venezuela's twin magnitude-7.5 earthquakes killed at least 235 people, devastated the main international airport, and forced the government to deploy IMF Special Drawing Rights for reconstruction amid fiscal exhaustion.
  • Mexico's central bank held interest rates steady, in line with consensus expectations, against a backdrop of continued peso pressure and slowing growth.

Colombia and the Regional Shift

De la Espriella's win extends a regional realignment toward US-aligned governments that already includes Argentina under Milei and Chile under Kast. His platform — reopening Colombia to oil and gas exploration including fracking, zero-tolerance on cartels — reverses Petro's direction and raises the country's attractiveness as an energy investment destination while complicating its ESG positioning. The razor-thin margin creates space for legal challenges, and de la Espriella's limited governing experience raises legitimate questions about implementation capacity. Brazil's Lula, facing reelection in October against Flávio Bolsonaro, may emerge as the last significant counterweight to comprehensive US hemispheric influence — a contest whose outcome will shape regional political economy into 2027. Intra-family tensions surfaced in the Bolsonaro camp after Michelle Bolsonaro publicly distanced herself from Flávio's campaign, a coalition signal worth monitoring.

Venezuela and Brazil

Venezuela's earthquake response illustrates the intersection of humanitarian crisis and fiscal exhaustion: deploying SDRs for reconstruction underscores the absence of conventional fiscal capacity. The Brazilian Senate leader from Lula's party resigned following a police investigation linking him to failed lender Banco Master, narrowing the administration's legislative coalition credibility on financial oversight at a sensitive moment. Chile's senate gave initial approval to President Kast's economic omnibus bill on a narrow margin, introducing modest reform momentum with elevated implementation risk. The Petrobras-Pemex non-binding cooperation agreement — covering exploration, production, and refining — reflects genuine strategic logic around pre-salt deepwater expertise but carries substantial execution risk given Pemex's structural liabilities. ---

Global

  • Brent crude fell below $72.48 per barrel by Thursday, erasing all wartime gains, as tanker flows through the Strait of Hormuz recovered to approximately 75% of pre-conflict levels; oil is on track for a significant weekly decline.
  • The US issued a 60-day sanctions waiver permitting Iran to sell oil internationally — the most significant shift in Iran sanctions policy in nearly a decade — though the thorniest issues (nuclear inspections, enrichment limits, ballistic missiles) remain unresolved and the two sides contradict each other on key terms.
  • Gold broke below $4,000 per ounce (down ~29% from January peak) and Bitcoin fell to $59,023 — its lowest since October 2024 — as the debasement trade unwound under Fed Chair Warsh's hawkish posture and the DXY dollar index closed at a 14-month high.
  • China's export controls on US rare earth producers and the G7's agreement that no single country should supply more than 60% of members' rare earth imports by 2030 formalize a structural fracture in critical minerals supply chains; Bloomberg Economics estimates more than $2 trillion in G7 GDP could be at risk if Chinese rare earth exports ceased entirely.

Energy Markets and Hormuz

The speed of oil's retreat — Brent retracing its entire wartime premium in roughly four sessions — reflects rapidly normalizing tanker routing and an inundation of Middle Eastern and African supply offers reaching buyers simultaneously. Iranian sellers have already cut spot prices to discounts of $2.50–$5 per barrel to Brent. A container ship was struck by an unidentified projectile in the Strait on Friday — the first attack since the interim peace deal — though two-directional traffic continued, and Iran stated navigation would be managed "in the spirit" of the agreement. Market interpretation of the episode as isolated rather than structural appears correct for now, but confidence among shipowners remains fragile. The macroeconomic read-through is complicated: Apollo's Torsten Slok raises the possibility that rapidly falling oil prices function as an effective demand stimulus in an already-tight US labor market — potentially net-inflationary rather than disinflationary. Lagged pass-through in natural gas bills, jet fuel surcharges, and fertilizer costs (a fifth of global ammonia exports originate in the Gulf) means the energy-price relief is not yet fully in the system.

Monetary Policy Divergence and the Debasement Trade

The structural unwinding of the debasement trade is the most consequential cross-asset shift of the period. Fed Chair Warsh's explicit prioritization of price stability has anchored the dollar while compressing gold and crypto simultaneously. Standard Chartered's Steven Englander attributes dollar strength primarily to real and nominal rate differentials, expecting the Fed on hold while the ECB cuts once in H1 2027. Emerging market central banks face the familiar dilemma: domestic inflation elevated by the energy shock, a stronger dollar environment, and limited easing room. Whether price pressures prove transitory post-Hormuz or entrench in non-energy categories — StoneX's Vincent Deluard estimates a 3.5–4% CPI plateau even as oil retreats — will determine the durability of the current repricing. That question remains genuinely open.
