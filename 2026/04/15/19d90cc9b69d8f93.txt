Undecided in DC

Tuesday at the IMF kicked off with a series of bleak warnings from the 
Washington-based lender. With war raging in the Middle East, officials cut their
forecast for global growth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/imf-cut-global-growth-to-3-1-flags-downturn-risk-if-war-lasts?cmpid=BBD041526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 this year, and outlined a more severe scenario where the world could come 
close to experiencing a recession.

Concurrently, the fund also observed that the Iran conflict could yet trigger 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/imf-urges-nations-to-stay-at-frontier-of-mounting-ai-risks?cmpid=BBD041526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 the kind of turmoil that markets have so far avoided, while adding separately 
that governments and regulators should “stay at the frontier” of rising threats 
fromartificial intelligence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/imf-urges-nations-to-stay-at-frontier-of-mounting-ai-risks?cmpid=BBD041526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
.

So how should policymakers respond to that panoply of dangers? European 
Central Bank President Christine Lagarde sentsomewhat mixed signals 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/lagarde-says-europe-between-ecb-s-baseline-and-adverse-scenarios?cmpid=BBD041526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 about that when questioned on Bloomberg Television.
Christine Lagarde interviewed in Washington on Tuesday.Photographer: Al 
Drago/Bloomberg
She responded that the ECB now finds itself somewhere between its baseline 
scenario that it had outlined in its March projections, and its own so-called 
adverse scenario that foresees 3.5% inflation in 2026.

She also emphasized that it could be difficult to simply “look through” that 
shock, because doing so could give rise to doubts about the central bank’s 
commitment to price stability. That’s defined by officials as reaching 2% 
inflation over the medium term.

But while both those points would tend to argue in favor of interest-rate 
hikes, Lagarde didn’t really concur, declining to signal any such imminent move.

She even stated that the ECB has no tightening bias – even though its baseline 
scenario itself assumes two increases in 2026 that were already perceived by 
investors in March.

The bottom line is that, with two weeks to go before the next monetary-policy 
decision on April 30, it’s still completely unclear what the outcome will 
ultimately be. A rate hike is one option, but it’s also possible that the ECB 
will wait until at least June – or take no action at all.

Need-to-Know Research

Among the selling points for Trump’s signature tax legislation enacted last 
July has been a boom in capital spending thanks to provisions for companies in 
the so-called One Big Beautiful Bill (OBBB) act.Surveys 
<https://www.atlantafed.org/research-and-data/publications/policy-hub-macroblog/2026/04/13/did-obbb-affect-firms-plans-for-2026>
 by the Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta raise some questions about that.

The Atlanta Fed found just 20% of respondents in a survey conducted in 
February factored the OBBB into their planning for “outcomes such as capital 
expenditures, employment and sales revenue forecasts.” The remaining ones 
didn’t. While the OBBB was in many ways an extension of previous Trump tax 
cuts, previous surveys in 2017-18 by the Atlanta Fed also found most firms 
didn’t change hiring or capital spending plans due to those reductions.

“Our findings suggest that a broad-based and sizeable future surge in business 
activity stemming from the policy change may not be likely,” the Atlanta Fed 
said. “Although some of the provisions in the OBBB — such as tax changes 
regarding expensing, deductions, and depreciation — might provide a tailwind 
for growth during the coming year, business execs aren’t yet forecasting much 
of a direct boost to their own-firm prospects this year.”
