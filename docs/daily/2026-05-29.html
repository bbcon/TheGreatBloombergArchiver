US

Equity Markets

US equities held at all-time highs heading into the final session of May, with the S&P 500 up 4.9% for the month and the Nasdaq 100 up 10%, following a 16% gain in April. The rally is being led by mega-cap technology, with Dell's premarket surge of 40% — after its AI server revenue outlook far exceeded consensus — lifting Hewlett Packard Enterprise (+20%) and Super Micro Computer (+12%) simultaneously. The AI infrastructure theme continues to dominate earnings surprises, and strategists at Citadel Securities note the market conversation has shifted from hedging downside to identifying remaining sidelined cash as the next marginal buyer.

Monetary Policy

The first Bessent-Warsh breakfast yielded a carefully calibrated public signal: the Treasury secretary did not request rate cuts, framing his expectations around Warsh doing "the right thing for inflation and growth" — language that pointedly invokes the Fed's statutory dual mandate rather than political objectives. Minneapolis Fed President Kashkari, speaking in Seoul, reinforced the cautious stance, warning that elevated energy prices and the risk of unanchored inflation expectations preclude declaring victory. Markets have incrementally repriced toward a Fed hike by early 2027, providing modest support to the dollar, though the Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index's monthly gain of 0.7% is attracting skepticism about follow-through.

In Texas, a little-known hard-right candidate defeated an oil-industry-backed incumbent in the Railroad Commission primary, unsettling Permian Basin operators who had mobilized backing from Occidental, ExxonMobil, Chevron, and ConocoPhillips — a result that introduces genuine regulatory uncertainty into the US's most prolific producing basin. ---

Huawei Semiconductor Breakthrough

The most consequential technology development of the period is Huawei's public disclosure of its LogicFolding architecture at a chip conference in China. Presented by semiconductor chief He Tingbo, the technology seeks to improve chip performance by shortening internal data-transmission distances rather than shrinking transistors — an approach designed explicitly to circumvent China's inability to procure ASML's EUV machines. The company targets 1.4nm-equivalent chip production by 2031, roughly three years behind TSMC's current roadmap. Bloomberg Intelligence notes that if commercially viable, this would help Chinese AI firms maintain competitiveness despite Nvidia export restrictions. Material engineering hurdles remain — heat dissipation, manufacturing complexity, and economic scale — but the market reaction was immediate, with SMIC and Hua Hong shares soaring. The disclosure matters geopolitically as well: it bolsters the argument, previously advanced by Nvidia's Jensen Huang, that export controls may accelerate rather than prevent Chinese semiconductor self-sufficiency.

Korea and Taiwan Equity Surge

Samsung and SK Hynix have driven South Korea's Kospi to a 24% monthly gain — an extraordinary move for a major index — on the back of the global AI infrastructure buildout. The rally has become self-reinforcing for MSCI's emerging-markets index, which gained 7.8% in May to an all-time high, with AI component makers as the primary engine. Taiwan's upward GDP revision to 9.6% for 2026 reinforces that the semiconductor cycle remains in full expansion, and Lenovo's Hong Kong-listed shares gained 105% in May on AI server growth prospects.

China Capital Controls and Geopolitics

Beijing launched what sources describe as its biggest crackdown on offshore capital flows in decades, which directly hit online brokerage Futu: its founder lost $1.7 billion in a single day, representing a quarter of his net worth. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi visited Ottawa — the first such visit in a decade — reflecting a deliberate diplomatic realignment as Canada diversifies away from US dependency, exchanging EV tariff reductions for lower Canadian agricultural levies. In the South China Sea, ship-tracking data reveal intensifying Chinese coast guard activity around the Spratly Islands, raising the risk of confrontation with Vietnam. ---

Inflation and Growth Divergence

The most significant European data of the period presents a genuine policy tension: CPI accelerated in France, Italy, and Spain to the fastest rates since 2024, providing clear support for ECB rate tightening, while France's economy simultaneously contracted in Q1. This combination — inflation overshoot alongside weak growth — is the precise scenario that most complicates the ECB's task. The inflation data appears to reflect energy price pass-through from the Middle East conflict, which means any ceasefire extension that reduces oil prices could ease pressure more quickly than the underlying demand picture would suggest. Sweden's contraction, in line with expectations, adds to the picture of uneven European growth momentum; Riksbank officials may interpret it as justification to pause before moving rates higher.

EU Industrial Policy and Security

The European Commission's rebooted Chips Act, targeting €120 billion in investment by 2035, reflects a strategic response to the semiconductor race being driven by US and Chinese capital — and by the Huawei LogicFolding disclosure specifically. Separately, EU officials are seeking access to Anthropic's Mythos AI model over cybersecurity concerns, placing Brussels at the frontier of AI governance debates. The Romanian drone incident underscores that NATO's eastern flank remains under direct kinetic pressure, with Bucharest calling the intrusion a "serious and irresponsible escalation." Russia-Armenia tensions were on display at the Eurasian Economic Union summit, where Armenian Prime Minister Pashinyan declined to attend as Yerevan pursues EU membership aspirations — a geopolitical fracture within Moscow's own institutional architecture.

Brazil-US Political Friction

The US designation of Brazil's two largest criminal organizations as terrorist entities is the most consequential LatAm development of the period. The move gives US law enforcement expanded tools to pursue international financial flows linked to PCC and CV, but carries significant diplomatic cost: Lula has consistently resisted framing Brazilian organized crime through a counterterrorism lens, and any perception of US interference in domestic security matters is politically toxic in Brasília. The relationship between Trump and Lula was already strained by trade policy and ideological divergence; this designation adds a new source of bilateral friction at a moment when Brazil is deepening its own commodity export relationships with China. The macro implications are indirect but real — political turbulence in Brazil tends to widen sovereign spreads and weaken the real, though neither move has been confirmed in available data for this period.

Venezuela and Regional Politics

Machado's signal of willingness to negotiate on a path to elections is a meaningful shift in tone, suggesting Washington's pressure campaign may be producing some movement at the margins. The broader regional picture is shaped by the commodity cycle: industrial metals' best monthly performance since January benefits Chile, Peru, and Colombia through terms-of-trade improvement, providing some offset to the tighter global financial conditions implied by higher US rates. The China-Canada rapprochement, including reduced EV tariffs, is a reminder that Beijing's commercial outreach in the Western Hemisphere is broadening, with potential implications for trade competition in agricultural and resource markets where LatAm producers compete. ---

Oil Markets and the Hormuz Truce

The tentative 60-day US-Iran ceasefire extension is the dominant macro event of the period, and its impact on energy markets is stark: Brent crude has fallen approximately 19% in May, its steepest monthly decline since 2020, as traders price in the potential resumption of flows through the Strait of Hormuz. The reversal matters globally — elevated energy prices since the conflict began have been a primary driver of inflation reacceleration across the G7, and a sustained reduction in oil prices would reduce pressure on central banks currently contemplating hikes rather than cuts. However, Trump has not yet formally agreed to the terms, and the deal remains tentative; premature dismissal of Hormuz risk appears unwarranted. Meanwhile, the US oil system shows early strain from export demand that has helped plug the Hormuz supply gap, with American crude shipments falling to a near two-month low.

AI Capital Flows

The scale of capital mobilizing around artificial intelligence infrastructure is approaching macro-relevant magnitudes. Anthropic's $65 billion fundraise at a $965 billion valuation — eclipsing OpenAI for the first time — was accompanied by a $36 billion private credit facility assembled by Apollo and Blackstone to finance AI chip procurement, effectively transforming advanced semiconductors into a financeable asset class. Taiwan's tech firms are separately borrowing a record $14.5 billion. These flows are compressing risk premia in private credit, concentrating equity gains in AI-adjacent equities globally, and creating feedback loops between chip demand, energy infrastructure, and sovereign growth forecasts.

Geopolitics and Maritime Risk

The Pentagon's 121-page Indo-Pacific command report to Congress strikes a notably harder tone on China than the broader Trump administration, warning that the PLA is targeting Taiwan readiness by 2027 and requesting billions for asymmetric sea-denial capabilities. The Hormuz conflict has sharpened Washington's focus on maritime chokepoint control, with the Taiwan Strait now explicitly framed through the same lens. Separately, Russia's drone strike into Romanian territory — a NATO member — represents a qualitative escalation on Europe's eastern flank, while the China-Canada diplomatic rapprochement and Beijing's intensifying South China Sea operations reflect a global order in active realignment.
