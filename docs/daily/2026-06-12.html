Friday, June 12, 2026

US

  • SpaceX raises $75 billion in the largest IPO in history, with shadow-market pricing implying a first-day gain of at least 35%.
  • US inflation data beat consensus, rising to its highest level since 2023 amid Iran war energy cost pass-through.

Monetary Policy and Macro Backdrop

Economists revised Federal Reserve rate-cut expectations materially this week, with consensus now anticipating the Fed on hold well into 2026 and beyond — a notable shift from earlier projections. The catalyst is a CPI print that came in above consensus, reaching its highest reading since 2023, a development attributed to energy cost pass-through from the ongoing US-Iran conflict. Incoming Fed Chair Kevin Warsh faces his first policy meeting next week amid an environment of elevated inflation and unresolved geopolitical risk, limiting his room to signal near-term easing.

Equity and Corporate Markets

US equity futures rose approximately 0.5% ahead of the open on Iran ceasefire optimism, while energy sector names — Chevron, ExxonMobil, ConocoPhillips, and Occidental — declined as Brent crude fell more than 4%. SpaceX's Nasdaq debut under ticker SPCX is the dominant market event; retail investors alone placed over $100 billion in orders. Adobe fell nearly 6% in premarket after announcing the departure of its CFO, compounding leadership uncertainty. Lennar's miss on forward guidance added to existing softness in the homebuilder segment. ---

Asia

  • China's CPI rose 1.2% YoY in May, just missing consensus, signaling persistent margin pressure on consumer-facing firms.
  • Beijing instructs major state-owned banks to reduce interbank lending to prevent money-market rates from drifting too far below policy rates.
  • China is considering a $295 billion sovereign-funded data center buildout over five years as part of a broader AI infrastructure push.
  • South Korean chipmaker valuations prompt global banks to curb hedge fund leverage on SK Hynix and Samsung, which have gained nearly six times in market cap over 12 months.

China: Macro Pressure and Strategic Investment

China's May CPI of 1.2% YoY, fractionally below consensus, underscores a demand environment where firms are absorbing higher raw material costs — partly driven by the Iran war's energy price shock — rather than passing them through. The resulting margin compression is a meaningful headwind for corporate earnings. Separately, Beijing's directive to state banks to reduce interbank lending reflects official concern that excessive liquidity is fueling asset bubbles, particularly in government bonds, even as the broader economy remains soft.

AI Ambitions and Market Distortions

Beijing's reported $295 billion data center plan, funded via sovereign debt and state investment vehicles, reflects the leadership's determination to remain competitive with the US in AI infrastructure, despite access restrictions on leading-edge Nvidia GPUs and ASML equipment. Meanwhile, India is preparing to allow its fiscal deficit to widen to approximately 4.8% of GDP against a 4.3% target — the first miss since the pandemic — as surging energy import costs inflate subsidy bills. In Indonesia, Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise rate increase to defend the rupiah, the worst-performing Asian currency this year, while student protests over the cost of living added political pressure on President Prabowo's government. ---

Europe

  • The ECB raised its deposit rate on Thursday — the first G7 rate hike since the Iran war began — with Governing Council members signaling a potential second increase in July.
  • UK GDP data showed Britain's strong early-2026 momentum ended in April, adding to political pressure on Prime Minister Starmer.
  • UK Defence Secretary John Healey resigned over Starmer's failure to accelerate defence spending commitments, significantly weakening the government.

ECB Policy: Divergence and Risk

The ECB's decision to raise rates stands as the most consequential tightening move among major central banks since the conflict in the Middle East began. The Bank of Canada held this week; the Fed and Bank of England are expected to do the same next week. ECB Governing Council member Joachim Nagel explicitly acknowledged the possibility of a second consecutive hike in July, while ECB President Lagarde defended the move by arguing that the eurozone economy retains sufficient resilience to absorb tighter financial conditions. Critics, including Berenberg economist Holger Schmieding, contend the ECB is overreacting — invoking its record of policy mis-steps in 2008, 2011, and 2022.

UK: Growth and Political Deterioration

April GDP data showed UK economic momentum fading after a solid start to 2026 — a result that arrived alongside a politically damaging cabinet resignation. Healey's departure over defence spending credibility leaves Starmer vulnerable to leadership challenges and diminishes the UK's standing in NATO discussions ahead of next week's G7 summit. Spain, by contrast, is emerging as the eurozone's fastest-growing major economy, supported by strong immigration-driven labour supply. ---

LatAm

  • Venezuela's oil exports to Asia are rising as commodity traders redirect flows disrupted by the Iran war, with India now absorbing volumes previously taken by China.
  • US sanctions on Cuba's state energy company threaten to derail a fuel supply arrangement for the island's private sector amid a severe energy crisis.

Venezuela and Regional Energy Dynamics

The disruption to Iranian oil supplies — down to approximately 160,000 barrels per day reaching China in May from 1.8 million barrels per day in February — has created meaningful trade realignment. Venezuelan crude is filling a portion of the gap, with traders redirecting shipments toward Asia and India in particular. This represents a structural shift in regional oil flows that may persist beyond any near-term Hormuz resolution, given the logistical and commercial relationships being established. The dynamics provide some fiscal support to Caracas, though the Trump administration's tightened sanctions posture introduces ongoing uncertainty around the sustainability of those flows.

Cuba

US sanctions on Cuba's state oil company, announced this week, threaten to block a fuel supply deal by a Florida-based trading firm that would have directed energy to Cuba's private sector. The timing is particularly acute given the island's deepening energy crisis, and highlights the extent to which US sanctions policy continues to shape economic outcomes across the Caribbean basin. ---

Global

  • President Trump claimed a US-Iran deal is imminent, stating the US "ended the war with Iran today," though Iran has not confirmed agreement and a complex intermediary network is slowing progress.
  • Brent crude fell more than 4% on ceasefire optimism, extending a decline that has emerged as the clearest market signal of shifting war risk premium.
  • A potentially historic El Niño, declared by the US Climate Prediction Center as emerging in May, threatens to compound existing inflation pressures across agricultural and energy markets.

US-Iran: Deal Proximity and Market Implications

Trump's claim of an imminent agreement — which he asserted would reopen the Strait of Hormuz — drove the most significant single-day crude move of the week, with WTI falling toward $84 per barrel. Iran's semi-official Mehr news agency acknowledged a draft deal is under discussion, and UAE-Iran security officials reportedly held a direct meeting for the first time since the war began. Nevertheless, material uncertainty remains: prior diplomatic advances have failed to produce breakthroughs, Iran has yet to formally confirm readiness to sign, and a layered network of intermediaries continues to slow progress. The naval blockade has reduced Iranian crude flows to China by over 90% since February, creating acute fiscal pressure on Tehran that may be accelerating its willingness to negotiate.

El Niño and Structural Inflation Risk

The US Climate Prediction Center's confirmation of a strengthening El Niño introduces a significant additional upside risk to global inflation that is difficult to price with precision. Agricultural commodities most exposed — sugar and cocoa — face USDA price forecasts of up to 8.4% increases in 2026. Combined with energy price pressures from the Iran war, this represents a dual supply-side inflation shock facing policymakers across multiple regions simultaneously, complicating the path to rate normalization for central banks that have not yet moved.
