European markets were constructive on March 18, with the Stoxx Europe 600 gaining 0.6% as oil prices eased and investor sentiment stabilized ahead of a week dense with central bank decisions. Iraq's agreement to resume pipeline exports via Turkey — bypassing the Strait of Hormuz — contributed to the softer oil tone, though the underlying supply disruption remains unresolved. Germany took targeted domestic action in response to war-driven fuel price increases, restricting petrol stations to one price hike per day and tightening antitrust oversight — a measure reflecting political sensitivity to consumer cost pressures rather than a substantive market intervention.

The Bank of England's positioning is drawing increasing scrutiny. According to Bloomberg Economics analysis, the BoE's internal models may lead it to respond more aggressively than markets currently expect to the energy price shock, given assumptions about the large and persistent inflationary pass-through from oil. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer navigated continued diplomatic pressure, with Irish Taoiseach Micheál Martin defending him after Trump criticized the UK for declining to join the war effort. Commentary from Rosa Prince in Bloomberg Opinion argued Starmer's refusal was justified but that the harder challenge will be managing the economic damage attributable to the conflict — a framing likely to define UK political discourse through the autumn.

On the corporate side, Unilever confirmed it is in the early stages of evaluating a separation of its food assets, a move consistent with the ongoing portfolio simplification strategy the company has pursued since divesting its tea business. Prudential Plc reported an increase in new business profit and announced an additional $1.2 billion share buyback. Logitech fell 5.7% in Zurich after UBS downgraded the stock on weaker gaming market signals, while HelloFresh dropped 9.6% in Frankfurt after issuing guidance materially below analyst expectations. Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel offered a relatively sanguine view on AI's labor market implications, suggesting the technology may prove beneficial for European companies rather than destructive for employment.
