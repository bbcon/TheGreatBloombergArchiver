**China: Resilience Under Pressure**
China entered the week with the most encouraging domestic data of any major Asian economy. Industrial production expanded 6.3% year-on-year in January–February, retail sales exceeded forecasts, and fixed-asset investment turned positive after a period of contraction. Chinese equities regained some footing, and Jefferies' Christopher Wood argued that mainland equities are the most defensible in the current environment, citing robust domestic renewable capacity, battery storage, and reserve buffers as partial insulation from the energy shock. Beijing also began 2026 with its fastest pace of government spending growth since 2022, suggesting fiscal stimulus is being deployed to sustain momentum. The risk, as Gavekal Research flagged, is that rising commodity prices compress manufacturer margins — particularly where cost pass-through to consumers is limited — potentially triggering further investment pullback and necessitating additional easing. China's 2026 growth target of approximately 5% relies heavily on exports to non-US markets, making an oil-driven global slowdown a material threat.

**China: Geopolitical Positioning**
China remains the central geopolitical pivot of the Iran conflict. Chinese state media responded with undisguised scorn to US appeals for help reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and the structural leverage Washington once held through tariff threats has diminished, partly via Supreme Court rulings and partly due to China's commanding position in rare earth supply chains. Beijing appears likely to engage on its own terms and timetable, if at all. The Trump-Xi summit, originally planned for late March, was postponed to approximately mid-May — a delay Beijing reportedly views with quiet relief, given the diplomatic complications of hosting Trump while Iran war operations continue. A Paris meeting between senior officials appeared constructive, and the trade truce remains intact, though a pending $14 billion US arms package for Taiwan represents a significant latent irritant. Industry consultant FGE NexantECA indicated Beijing is close to drawing on its estimated 1.4 billion barrels of commercial oil reserves as the Hormuz disruption persists. Alibaba and Tencent combined lost approximately $66 billion in market value after results from both disappointed investors seeking a clearer path to AI monetization; Alibaba's quarterly net income fell 67%.

**Japan and South Korea**
Japan faces a specific supply chain vulnerability from tightening naphtha supplies, with multiple petrochemical firms announcing production cuts. The Bank of Japan held rates at its Thursday meeting but maintained a conditional tightening bias, with analysts noting a potential move as soon as April if inflation data cooperate. Prime Minister Takaichi characterized her White House meeting as "extremely difficult": Japan's pacifist constitution forecloses overseas military deployments, February exports to the US fell 8% year-on-year, and car exports by value declined 14.8%. Takaichi is expected to offer pledges of additional US investment, potentially in nuclear energy, as a diplomatic offset. South Korea's governance reform story was the week's most constructive equity narrative — the Kospi surged 5% on Wednesday and now trades above 5,900, with Samsung's multi-year contract signaling a potential structural improvement in memory market dynamics.

**Emerging Asia**
India occupies a particularly exposed position. The rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to a record low, Prime Minister Modi secured safe passage for LPG tankers through Hormuz — placing India alongside China and Russia — while managing US relations and preparing for April state elections that will test his handling of the energy crisis. Indonesia's central bank delivered a hawkish hold while tightening foreign-exchange regulations to defend the rupiah, which is down approximately 14% in March. The Philippine peso fell past 60 per dollar. Pakistan's airstrikes on Kabul, reported to have killed at least 400 people at a drug treatment facility, represent a separate and serious escalation risk that global markets have not yet fully priced.
