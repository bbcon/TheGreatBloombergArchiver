**UK Political Crisis Intensifies, Then Stabilizes Partially**

The week opened with Prime Minister Keir Starmer under acute pressure following the resignation of chief of staff Morgan McSweeney and communications head, with Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar publicly calling for Starmer to stand down by Monday. Gilt yields moved higher following Sarwar's intervention, though the reaction remained an order of magnitude below the 2022 Truss episode — sterling held up and the curve did not dislocate. Prediction markets priced Starmer's departure by end-June. By midweek, Starmer had secured a temporary reprieve, winning public backing from his full cabinet including Wes Streeting and Ed Miliband, though the underlying pressures linked to the Epstein files — which reference a key Starmer appointee extensively — continued to weigh. The succession question is the more durable concern for fixed income: the most plausible candidates sit to Starmer's left and are perceived as more fiscally expansive, a dynamic Mizuho's Jordan Rochester described as a "Damocles sword" over gilt traders. Fourth-quarter GDP, released Thursday, barely expanded, with services stagnating — adding to political pressure. Elliott Investment Management disclosed a stake in London Stock Exchange Group as the exchange contends with AI-related disruption and a sustained decline in listings.

**European Corporate News Dominates**

The week's heaviest corporate newsflow was European. BP fell 5% on Tuesday after suspending its $750 million quarterly buyback and withdrawing capital return guidance, then extended declines as much as 7.7% on Wednesday after executives offered no timeline for restoration — leaving the UK major increasingly isolated from peers TotalEnergies, Chevron, Exxon, and Shell, all of which maintained shareholder returns. Incoming CEO Meg O'Neill inherits roughly $60 billion in total debt and financial obligations. Nuveen agreed to acquire Schroders for $13.5 billion in cash at a roughly 29% premium on Wednesday, ending more than two centuries of independence for the UK's largest standalone asset manager while preserving the brand and London headquarters. Kering rose 10% in Paris on a smaller-than-expected Gucci sales decline and a special dividend. Adyen sank 20% in Amsterdam on a softer revenue outlook. Dassault Systèmes fell around 20% in Paris following disappointing results, becoming the latest European software name caught in the broader AI-displacement selloff. Sanofi fell 6% in Paris after abruptly replacing CEO Paul Hudson. Bank of France Governor François Villeroy de Galhau unexpectedly announced his June departure, roughly 16 months ahead of schedule, handing Macron the opportunity to make a six-year appointment ahead of presidential elections in which Le Pen or Bardella poll competitively.

**Macro and Policy**

Norwegian January CPI surprised sharply to the upside on Tuesday, driving 10-year yields to levels last seen in early 2009 and shifting OIS pricing toward a March hike from Norges Bank — a notable divergence from the broader developed-market easing trajectory. At the EU level, the European Parliament moved closer to ratifying the US trade deal, agreeing a 2028 sunset clause that gives Washington six months to reduce steel and aluminium tariffs from 50% to 15%. The Franco-German split over industrial policy sharpened, with Macron pressing his "Made in Europe" agenda while Merz warned against excessive protectionism. Gas inventories are on track to exit winter more depleted than in 2025, with German storage roughly a quarter full, and little financial incentive via summer-winter price spreads to refill. EU carbon prices fell sharply after Merz indicated openness to revising or delaying the bloc's emissions market — the steepest drop since 2022. At the Munich Security Conference, European officials were reportedly reassessing the continent's nuclear deterrent posture following what attendees described as a sobering recalibration of US security commitments.
