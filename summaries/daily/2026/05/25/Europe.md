**ECB Policy**
Two Governing Council members indicated the ECB is moving toward a rate increase at its June meeting, conditional on the Strait of Hormuz situation remaining unresolved. President Lagarde separately confirmed the bank will revise its inflation projections upward next month. The policy pivot is notable given the ECB's recent easing cycle: the energy shock from Middle East hostilities has effectively overridden the disinflationary forces that had been guiding the institution toward neutral. The conditionality on a "lasting peace deal" introduces genuine two-way risk to the rate path in coming weeks.

**UK Political Risk**
The UK's political situation continues to deteriorate. Commentary from Westminster suggests Starmer's administration is entering a period of effective policymaking paralysis as the leadership question remains unresolved. Market participants appear to be watching whether fiscal policy credibility is maintained during the transition — bond markets have thus far been relatively stable, though the risk of a leftward policy shift during any leadership contest is a live consideration. Andy Burnham's potential candidacy is drawing both attention and some skepticism from fixed-income investors.

**Carbon Market Integrity**
A Bloomberg investigation found that companies in at least nine European countries — including BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, EDF, ExxonMobil, and OMV — purchased carbon credits from Chinese projects in the EU's upstream emissions reduction scheme that appear to have been fraudulent or non-existent. German authorities have already voided credits from 30 projects claiming 2.1 million tons of CO₂ savings. No criminal penalties are expected for the purchasing companies, as acquisitions were made in good faith, but the episode raises fundamental questions about the governance architecture of European carbon markets ahead of the EU's planned expansion of credit-based mechanisms toward its 90% emissions reduction target for 2040.
