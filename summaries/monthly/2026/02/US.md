• The Supreme Court struck down "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs in a 6-3 Friday ruling, potentially halving the effective US tariff rate and marking the most consequential domestic policy development of the Trump presidency to date.
• Kevin Warsh's nomination as Fed Chair crystallized a durable tension: an incoming chair with a hawkish institutional record nominated by an administration that explicitly demands lower rates, with FOMC minutes later in the month revealing several members had considered rate increases.
• AI capital expenditure concerns dominated equity markets, with the S&P 500 ranking 69th among 92 global benchmarks and trailing world ex-US equities by approximately 7.7 percentage points year-to-date by late February.
• January payrolls of 130,000 — the strongest print in over a year — pushed the expected first Fed cut from June to July, while trimmed-mean and median CPI fell to their lowest since 2021, creating a genuinely mixed inflation picture.
• New CBO projections showed net interest payments rising from $1 trillion currently to $2.1 trillion by 2036, reaching 4.6% of GDP — a figure that alone exceeds Treasury Secretary Bessent's stated 3% total deficit target for 2029.

**Federal Reserve Transition**

February's defining US macro theme was the transition at the Federal Reserve. President Trump's nomination of Kevin Warsh to succeed Jerome Powell in May generated immediate interpretive tension: Warsh's institutional record is characterized by an orthodox, inflation-conscious disposition that Wall Street broadly reads as hawkish, yet Trump indicated Monday of the final week that he would not have nominated Warsh had he signaled a preference for rate increases. The resolution of that contradiction remains genuinely open. Warsh would inherit a committee whose median view he cannot unilaterally redirect, and the confirmation path — while navigable through the Republican Senate — faces at least one hold pending resolution of a criminal investigation involving Chair Powell. Mid-month FOMC minutes amplified the uncertainty: several members flagged the possibility of rate increases rather than cuts if inflation remains elevated, a posture markets had not fully priced. The dollar posted its best week in four months following the minutes' release. Governor Lisa Cook reiterated the importance of central bank credibility, while Judy Shelton resurfaced in policy circles in conversations with Treasury Secretary Bessent about redeploying $2.9 trillion in bank reserves parked at the Fed. The MOVE index continued declining from its April Liberation Day peak, suggesting bond markets are treating the institutional noise as manageable — an equanimity that may face its first real test once Warsh arrives in May and faces a live inflation decision.

**Equity Markets and the AI Rotation**

Beneath headline index stability, February produced a significant factor rotation. The equal-weighted S&P 500 and small-cap indices hit all-time highs while the Magnificent Seven remained below recent peaks; the Russell 1000 Value extended a meaningful lead over Growth; and the Dow Jones Transportation Average outpaced the S&P 500 by 13 percentage points over six weeks. The catalyst appears to have been a sequence of AI capability releases from Anthropic, Google, and others that investors interpreted not as demand drivers but as displacement threats to incumbent software, insurance-broker, logistics, and financial-data business models. The S&P 1500 Software index fell more than 17% year-to-date by mid-month, with Thomson Reuters falling as much as 20% on concerns that Anthropic's legal-workflow tool threatens its core franchise, AMD extending losses to 17% post-earnings, and Alphabet shares falling despite a revenue beat after guiding 2026 capex to as much as $185 billion versus consensus near $120 billion. Amazon fell approximately 8% in pre-market trading after guiding to $200 billion in AI-related capital expenditure for 2026. A BofA global fund manager survey showed for the first time in a generation that a majority of money managers fear corporate overinvestment rather than underinvestment — a direct consequence of the $600 billion-plus in hyperscaler capex projected for the year. Single-name CDS on Meta and Alphabet became actively traded contracts. Apple's 40-day correlation with the Nasdaq 100 fell to roughly 0.2, its lowest since 2006, effectively functioning as a tech-sector haven. Q4 blended earnings growth reached roughly 14.5%, a four-year high, providing a fundamental floor that limited downside without arresting the rotation.

**Trade Architecture and the Tariff Ruling**

The Supreme Court's 6-3 Friday ruling invalidating the emergency-powers statutory basis for reciprocal tariffs altered the near-term trade architecture more sharply than any executive action this year. Bloomberg Economics estimated potential importer refunds of up to $170 billion, remanded to lower courts. The White House signaled alternative statutory authorities remain available, and the administration moved concurrently to construct bilateral arrangements: a US-Indonesia deal committed Jakarta to facilitate roughly $33 billion in US goods purchases in exchange for lower tariffs, and a formal trade pact with Taiwan featured $44 billion in LNG and crude purchases and $15 billion in civil aircraft orders. The Republican House's vote to terminate Canadian tariffs — passed with six GOP crossovers — exposed meaningful fissures within the party heading into midterm primaries, with swing-district members increasingly sensitive to affordability pressures. French customs data provided concrete evidence of the tariff regime's bilateral costs: Q4 2025 spirits exports to the US fell 47%, wine 39%, and cosmetics 25%. Trump's economic approval ratings softened as Democrats outperformed in recent special elections.

**Macro Backdrop and Structural Constraints**

The macro underpinning remained firmer in aggregate than the equity tape implied. Q3 GDP was revised to 4.4% annualized — the strongest in two years — and Bank of America flagged potential for front-loaded 2026 growth, citing roughly $135–$140 billion in fiscal stimulus and a normalization boost from the partial government shutdown's resolution. January industrial production rose 0.7%, the largest monthly gain in nearly a year, though the data predate the full effect of tariff escalation. Against these constructive aggregates, distributional stress is mounting: consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in more than a decade, the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160–200% of income from 90–120% in 2000. Goldman Sachs economists projected data centers will account for 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation near 6% through 2027 — a figure that would complicate the Fed's disinflation narrative and sharpen the political stakes around November's midterms. The EPA's rescission of the 2009 endangerment finding sets up litigation likely to reach the Supreme Court and constrains future climate rulemaking regardless of political control. Molina Healthcare's guidance — below half of consensus — triggered a sharp managed-care de-rating. Stellantis's $26 billion in EV-related charges carried direct implications for North American manufacturing given its Jeep, Ram, and Chrysler exposure. Private equity, with roughly $7 trillion in AUM and holding periods at a 20-year high of 4.4 years, faces LP pressure over distribution shortfalls that a modest pickup in IPO activity has not meaningfully relieved.
