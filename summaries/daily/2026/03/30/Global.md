The dominant global theme is whether the Iran-US war, now in its second month, will prove more enduring and economically destructive than financial markets have priced. Brent crude extended its advance above $116 per barrel on Monday — heading for its largest-ever monthly gain — after Houthi forces formally entered the conflict over the weekend and Iran struck aluminum production facilities in the UAE and Bahrain. The latter sent aluminum prices up as much as 6%, highlighting how the energy shock is beginning to transmit into industrial metals and broader supply chains.

The strategic picture has shifted materially. President Trump's earlier five-day and ten-day extensions of his ultimatum on Iranian energy infrastructure — both of which prompted no discernible Iranian concession — appear to have revealed the limits of rhetorical escalation as a market management tool. Iran's new leadership, having already absorbed significant military damage, retains low incentive to capitulate: the ability to selectively restrict tanker passage through the Strait of Hormuz imposes severe costs on others while leaving Iranian exports less impaired than an outright mining campaign would. Approximately 20,000 seafarers remain stranded in and around the Strait.

The nuclear proliferation implications deserve attention. From Germany and Poland exploring deeper integration with France's nuclear umbrella, to rising public support in South Korea and renewed debate in Japan, US allies are reassessing deterrence postures in ways that may prove structurally significant regardless of how the current conflict concludes. The Trump administration is simultaneously considering resuming nuclear testing after more than three decades and potentially sharing enrichment technology with Saudi Arabia, according to documents reviewed by Bloomberg.

On the anniversary of Liberation Day tariffs, the global trading system remains in flux. China has initiated two fresh investigations into US trade practices ahead of an expected May presidential summit. The WTO failed to extend its 28-year moratorium on e-commerce tariffs. Fertilizer prices are rising sharply — urea and ammonia particularly — with near half of global urea supply originating in the Persian Gulf region, raising concerns about food security in sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia.
