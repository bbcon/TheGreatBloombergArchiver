Wall Street’s favorite trades are collapsing. Day by day, the headlong rush 
into the most popular trades, from tech stocks to gold to cryptocurrencies, has 
given way to a sudden retreat from risk.Here’s a look <https://bloom.bg/3M8TdFs>
 at the slow drumbeat of news sowing anxiety about valuations that many 
suspected had already run up too far — and causing investors topull back all at 
once <https://bloom.bg/3O1sk6O>.

To be sure, the selloff in equities showed signs of easing 
<https://bloom.bg/45Nn7FR> as Friday’s session progressed during the Asian day, 
suggesting selling pressure was starting to abate after steep declines in US 
tech stocks. That came after Wall Street gauges declined on Thursday, with the 
Nasdaq 100 suffering its worst three-day rout since April’s meltdown. Precious 
metals also stabilized after a volatile start.Bitcoin made a similar move 
<https://bloom.bg/4tjALKL>, rising more than 3% after having earlier tumbled to 
nearly $60,000 — less than half its peak in October.

Investors remained wary of the technology sector with Amazon shares tumbling 
11% in extended trading. The company plans tospend $200 billion 
<https://bloom.bg/4tkKa4N> on artificial intelligence this year, fueling 
concern that its colossal AI bet may fail to pay off in the long run.

“Huge volatility across all assets is still hitting sentiment,” said Nick 
Twidale, chief market analyst at AT Global Markets. “It’s going to be a very 
long day for traders.”—Stephanie Phang <https://bloom.bg/4gs6Wkp>



 What You Need to Know Today 



It’s not just Amazon’s spending that’s causing investors to fret. Four of the 
biggest US technology companies together have forecast capital expenditures 
that will reach about$650 billion <https://bloom.bg/46mqxQ0> in 2026 — a 
mind-boggling tide of cash <https://bloom.bg/45Rk3Zm> earmarked for new data 
centers and the long list of equipment needed to make them tick, including 
artificial intelligence chips, networking cables and backup generators.




Speaking of market angst, Indonesia’s had its fair share. As Indonesia’s 
stocks plummeted last week, a group of executives thousands of miles away were 
starting to panic for a different reason. Read about thequandary facing 
investors <https://bloom.bg/46rm3HV> looking at Southeast Asia’s biggest 
economy: Is the “New Indonesia” envisioned by former leader Joko Widodo — one 
that would propel the nation into the ranks of the world’s top five economies 
by 2045 — still on track?Indonesia’s assets slid <https://bloom.bg/4qj2loI> on 
Friday after Moody’s Ratings cut the country’s credit outlook to negative, 
adding to investor unease over fiscal risks and market liquidity. The country’s
finance minister pushed back <https://bloom.bg/4tip47e> at the decision. 




Meanwhile, Chinese speculators have fanned a rally in global metals prices, 
thanks toample cash but fewer investment options <https://bloom.bg/3Zlpr3a>, 
underscoring just how difficult it is for authorities to channel capital into 
the real economy. International prices of base and precious metals, which are 
heavily keyed to demand in the world’s biggest buyer, went stratospheric last 
month.




A relentless selloff in BYD shares is laying bare investor anxiety over the 
profit outlook forChina’s electric-vehicle sector <https://bloom.bg/3MrKOwQ>, 
as cooling demand at home and surging raw material costs trigger a brutal reset 
of expectations. BYD’s Hong Kong-listed shares are down this week after 
disappointing sales data, extending a selloff that has shaved off more than $60 
billion in market value since May. The slump reverberated across EV peers, 
compounding woes for a stock market also grappling with fresh concerns overtaxes
 <https://bloom.bg/3ZRrgVE> and business disruption <https://bloom.bg/4tjZ8YZ> 
from AI.




Toyota appointed Kenta Kon as its new CEO, a surprise leadership reshuffle 
<https://bloom.bg/4qkZhc0> in the midst of a high-profile bid to privatize a 
key unit. Kon, who is currently the carmaker’s chief financial officer, will 
assume the top job on April 1, replacing Koji Sato who was appointed CEO in 
2023 and will become vice chairman and take on a newly established role as 
chief industry officer. Read more about Konhere <https://bloom.bg/4tlq6zo>. 
Kenta Kon. Photographer: Soichiro Koriyama/Bloomberg 



Thailand’s two decades of chronic political instability have seen the nation 
go from an aspiring economy on track to follow South Korea and Singapore into 
the ranks of rich nations, to a regional laggard beset by stagnant growth, 
soaring debt, widening inequality and a shrinking workforce. That’s thebackdrop 
for this weekend’s election <https://bloom.bg/4axZnIl> that pits a party 
campaigning on sweeping reform against two blocs pledging more populist 
policies. Follow our election coveragehere <https://bloom.bg/3P5Wao3>, and 
watch ourdocumentary here <https://bloom.bg/4ac5i4x>.

Lest we forget, there’s another closely watched election this weekend in Japan 
<https://bloom.bg/4a02RmS>. Join us on Feb. 8 from 7:45 p.m. JST (6:45 p.m. 
HKT) for our live coverage of Japan’s election, where we’ll be tracking the 
results in real time. Will PM Sanae Takaichi secure a majority in the lower 
house? What will the impact on markets be? Bloomberg reporters will answer your 
questions on Japan’s election and whether it will unsettle the bond market and 
impact the yenhere <https://bloom.bg/45Rk4fS>.




Last year, Philippine President Ferdinand Marcos Jr. bet he could boost his 
political fortunes bytaking aim at a brewing graft scandal 
<https://bloom.bg/4r0W0j0> that had stirred public outrage. Building on his 
2022 landslide election victory, tackling corruption would cement his 
reputation and fend off rivals. Itbackfired <https://bloom.bg/4tfruU5>.

Minal Bathwal has quietly become one of the world’s most successful macro 
hedge fund managers, making billions of dollars at Brevan Howard Asset 
Management. Bathwal hasn’t had a single down year since he started managing 
money during the 2008 financial crisis — making him a standout in the global 
macro style of investing that has recently surged in popularity. Now running 
around $5.5 billion, the Singapore-based fund manager has scored wins on 
everything from the trajectory of Korean interest rates to the swings of Asian 
currencies. Readhis story here <https://bloom.bg/4cdS6yL>.
