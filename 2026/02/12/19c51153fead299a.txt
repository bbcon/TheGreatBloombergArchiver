US President Donald Trump’s tariff agenda took a hit after the Republican-led 
House passed legislation <https://bloom.bg/4aaEdjy> aimed at ending his levies 
on Canadian imports. The measure is unlikely to become law (Trump has signaled 
he would veto it), but the vote forced swing-district Republicans to go on 
record and underscored growing unease within the party just months before 
midterm elections are expected to hinge on affordability. Six Republicans 
joined Democrats in backing the bill, highlighting the strain on the GOP’s 
narrow majority.

The rebuke comes as Trump’s approval ratings <https://bloom.bg/4axBvU9> on the 
economy face pressure and Democrats notch a string of stronger-than-expected 
election results. Lawmakers in trade-sensitive districts, particularly those 
with deep ties to Canada, are increasingly vocal about the local costs of the 
tariffs. With primary season looming and Trump warning of political 
consequences for defectors, the fight over trade policy is quickly becoming a 
test of party loyalty and a preview of the midterm battles ahead.—Jui 
Chakravorty



 What You Need to Know Today 



China moved to end its bruising auto price war by banning carmakers 
<https://bloom.bg/3O3V4Mr> from selling vehicles below total production cost, 
closing a loophole that fueled years of aggressive discounting in the world’s 
largest car market.

New guidelines <https://bloom.bg/4bR62yz> from the State Administration for 
Market Regulation also bar price-fixing, crack down on punitive rebate programs 
that push dealers into losses, and tighten oversight of digital sales platforms 
and software features. The campaign underscoresBeijing’s growing concern 
<https://bloom.bg/4cn4igC> about a race to the bottom that has reshaped the 
industry, lifting giants like BYD while squeezing smaller players, even as 
fresh price cuts suggest the battle for market share isn’t over yet.
A BYD Seal 06 DM-i electric vehicle at the Beijing Auto Show. Photographer: 
Bloomberg 



Asian stocks climbed for a fifth straight session <https://bloom.bg/4kthx1o>, 
pushing theMSCI Asia Pacific Index <https://bloom.bg/4axjKo4> to a record and 
delivering itsstrongest start <https://bloom.bg/4bPcoyz> versus the S&P 500 
this century: up about 13% year to date, compared with just 1.4% for US 
equities. Investors are rotating toward relatively cheaper valuations and 
positioning for the next phase of the AI buildout, where many Asian firms 
dominate key parts of the supply chain.

Meanwhile, stronger US jobs data nudged Treasury yields higher and tempered 
expectations fornear-term Fed rate cuts <https://bloom.bg/4bLM3kT>, even as the 
dollar slipped and global markets braced for the next inflation reading.




The US and Japan are said to be narrowing in on the first projects 
<https://bloom.bg/4cn4n3U> to tap Tokyo’s $550 billion investment vehicle, a 
centerpiece oflast year’s trade deal <https://bloom.bg/4coBfJC> that paired 
fresh Japanese spending with lower US tariffs.

The leading candidates span data center infrastructure led by SoftBank, a 
deepwater oil terminal in the Gulf of Mexico and synthetic diamonds for 
semiconductors, said people familiar with the matter. With Commerce Secretary 
Howard Lutnick and Trade Minister Ryosei Akazawa set to meet in Washington, the 
stakes are high: once a project is approved, Japan has 45 days to fund it or 
risk reopening the tariff fight that brought the two sides to the table in the 
first place.




Meanwhile, Japanese markets rallied after Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s  
decisive election win <https://bloom.bg/4axKULD> eased fears of runaway fiscal 
spending, reviving the “buy Japan” trade. Super-long bond yields fell sharply, 
the Topix extended gains and the yen strengthened to its best level this week 
<https://bloom.bg/4ay4F5F> as investors bet political clarity and a firm hand 
on fiscal policy will steady markets after January’s volatility. While 
questions remain over how Takaichi will fund tax cuts and higher defense 
spending without adding debt, traders appear willing to give her the benefit of 
the doubt (at least for now) with money flowing back into Japanese stocks, 
bonds and the currency in tandem.
Sanae Takaichi Photographer: Kiyoshi Ota/Bloomberg 



GQG Partners is doubling down on India even after one of the market’s worst 
stretches of underperformance in decades, holding more than $24 billion in the 
countryas it bets <https://bloom.bg/4au8t7M> the recent “perfect storm” of 
tariff fears, currency weakness and soft earnings is fading.

Portfolio manager Sudarshan Murthy says profit growth should rebound to the 
mid-teens, with banks, along with infrastructure and telecom plays, poised to 
lead a recovery as loan growth picks up and valuations look reasonable. While
foreign investors have been pulling billions <https://bloom.bg/4aaEenC>from 
Indian stocks, GQG is leaning the other way, arguing that long-term earnings 
visibility and economic expansion still make India one of the most compelling 
growth stories in global markets.




Thousands of Indian farmers are gearing up to protest 
<https://bloom.bg/4bQoxmQ> Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s surprise trade deal 
with the US, warning that lower tariffs and new import openings could undercut 
domestic agriculture and reopen wounds from themass farm uprisings 
<https://bloom.bg/3M9vR2x> that once brought New Delhi to a standstill.

While Washington has softened some language and Indian officials insist 
sensitive sectors like dairy remain protected, concessions on products such as 
DDGS (distillers dried grains, an ethanol byproduct used in animal feed) and 
soybean oil are fueling fears that American supplies will flood the market. The 
unrest underscores the tightrope Modi faces: easing trade tensions with Trump 
without provoking the powerful rural constituency that has, in the past, posed 
his most serious political challenge.
Farmers and local residents of floodplain areas protest in Noida on Feb. 11. 
Photographer: Hindustan Times 



US asset manager Nuveen agreed to buy Schroders in a $13.5 billion all-cash 
deal <https://bloom.bg/4kwVwyB> that will bring one of London’s oldest 
investment firms into its fold and create a global asset-management powerhouse 
with nearly $2.5 trillion in combined assets. The deal,offering shareholders 
<https://bloom.bg/4au8ybA> a roughly a 29% premium, will preserve the Schroders 
brand and keeps London as the enlarged group’s non-US headquarters. Still, it 
ends more than two centuries of independence for the UK’s largest standalone 
asset manager.

 For Your Commute 



Bad Bunny’s Super Bowl halftime show was analyzed down to the smallest 
details, from his Zara wardrobe to the $72,000 Audemars Piguet on his wrist. 
Yet the most understated element carried particular meaning: a simple white 
rope belt, knotted at the waist of his cream trousers. The accessory referenced 
Puerto Rican jíbaros, or farmers, who wear rope belts in sugarcane fields. It 
was an homage to the island’s agricultural heritage, woven into a performance 
filled with cultural symbolism.

The moment arrives as belts have become increasingly rare in modern menswear, 
replaced by elastic waists, side adjusters and precisely tailored trousers 
designed to stand on their own. Still, recent runway collections from Ralph 
Lauren, Prada and Louis Vuitton suggest the accessory may be poised for a 
return, reimagined as a point of personal expression, writes my colleague Chris 
Rovzar <https://bloom.bg/4b4TD7G>. If so, Bad Bunny’s choice offered a reminder 
that even the humblest detail can carry weight. Read more here 
<https://bloom.bg/4bPcczl>.
