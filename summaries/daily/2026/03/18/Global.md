The central macro reality on March 18 remains the Strait of Hormuz, now effectively shut for three weeks since the US-Israeli strikes on Iran began February 28. Iran continued missile and drone attacks across Israel and Gulf states following the killing of senior security figure Ali Larijani — a pragmatist whose death leaves Iran's wartime leadership increasingly concentrated among hardliners, materially reducing the probability of a negotiated off-ramp in the near term. The USS Gerald R. Ford aircraft carrier withdrew from operations after an onboard fire, a logistical complication for US force projection. Trump, who called the war's end imminent, simultaneously requested NATO allied military support and was rebuffed — a sequence that led him to publicly abandon the request and criticize allies. The episode has exposed meaningful fissures in the Western alliance architecture.

Oil markets are beginning to show modest differentiation. Brent edged toward $100 on the session, easing slightly from recent highs, as Iraq's Turkey pipeline deal offered one workaround to Hormuz closure. Notably, Iran itself has been transiting its own crude through the strait at broadly pre-war volumes, exploiting its control of the waterway. BofA's global fund manager survey found institutional investors rotating overweight commodities — the biggest such shift since the 2022 Ukraine invasion — while projecting Brent at approximately $76 by year-end, close to futures pricing of just over $80 nine months out. The implied scenario is a meaningful but not catastrophic supply disruption: inflation modestly higher, growth modestly lower, with full-scale stagflation not yet the base case though increasingly prominent as a tail risk.

The Reserve Bank of Australia became the first of eighteen central banks meeting this week to move, hiking rates in response to the inflationary impulse from energy prices — a signal that at least some inflation-targeting frameworks are treating the oil shock as a demand-side tightening event rather than a supply disruption to be looked through. The Fed and Bank of Japan decisions follow, with markets watching closely for any shift in forward guidance. Meanwhile, BloombergNEF data showed oil and gas majors cut low-carbon energy investment by 33% in 2025 to just over $25 billion, the lowest since 2020 — a retrenchment that preceded the current crisis but may complicate medium-term energy transition trajectories if elevated oil prices simultaneously incentivize fossil fuel investment and depress clean energy capital allocation.
