Daily Brief

Monday, June 08, 2026

US

  • US non-farm payrolls rose 170,000 in May, the third consecutive month of solid gains, significantly above expectations against a backdrop of ongoing military conflict in Iran — effectively removing recession risk signaled by the Sahm Rule in 2024–2025.
  • Payroll strength, combined with rising oil prices from Middle East escalation, has hardened market expectations: rate hike bets firmed materially, and Goldman Sachs no longer expects a Fed rate cut in 2026.
  • The Nasdaq 100 suffered a sharp Friday selloff, having traded further above its 50-day moving average than at any point since 2002; momentum strategies posted their worst day since Liberation Day.
  • President Trump publicly opposed Fed rate hikes, telling NBC that raising rates would be "the wrong thing to do," creating political tension ahead of new Chair Kevin Warsh's first FOMC meeting.

Labor Market

May non-farm payrolls printed at 170,000, beating consensus and marking the third straight month of meaningful job creation. This effectively negates the recession signal that the Sahm Rule briefly triggered during 2024–2025, when unemployment was rising rapidly enough to warrant concern. The print is broadly constructive for the economic outlook, though it removes the Fed's primary justification for easing.

Monetary Policy

Markets repriced aggressively following the payroll release. Overnight index swaps shifted from pricing no cuts to pricing meaningful probability of hikes, with Morgan Stanley revising its r-star estimate to 1.34% — implying the current 3.50–3.75% fed funds rate may already be near neutral. Goldman Sachs formally dropped its 2026 rate-cut call. Trump's public pressure on Warsh not to hike adds political complexity ahead of next week's CPI print, which may prove decisive.

Equity Markets

The Nasdaq 100's selloff was both substantial and arguably overdue. Semiconductor valuations, measured as a multiple of sales, had reached levels consistent with prior bubble episodes. Momentum strategies — which had concentrated exposure in the highest-flying AI names — bore the brunt of selling. S&P 500 futures recovered modestly in early Monday trading, with bulls citing the selloff as healthy consolidation rather than trend reversal. Bitcoin fell below $60,000, down more than 50% from its 2025 peak, with crypto winter dynamics appearing to reassert. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi declined more than 8% Monday, its sharpest single-day drop in recent memory, as AI-driven momentum reversed sharply following Friday's Wall Street selloff.
  • Indonesia's financial markets intensified their deterioration: the 10-year bond yield surged 36 basis points to a multi-year high, the rupiah weakened 0.9%, and the equity benchmark fell over 4%, extending year-to-date losses to nearly 40%.
  • Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang, visiting Seoul, called the tech selloff "a buying opportunity," characterizing the AI infrastructure buildout as still in its earliest stages.
  • Xi Jinping arrived in Pyongyang for his first North Korea visit in seven years, signaling Beijing's intent to reassert influence over a neighbor whose deepening Russian alliance has created new strategic room.

Regional Equity Markets

The MSCI Asia-Pacific benchmark fell 3.2%, with 100-day volatility rising to a six-year high. The Kospi's 8%-plus decline was particularly severe given South Korea's position as a central beneficiary of the AI hardware cycle — SK Hynix, the primary memory chip supplier to Nvidia, was among affected names, even as Nvidia and SK Hynix separately announced a partnership on next-generation AI memory architecture. Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 4.2%. The breadth of regional losses reflects both spillover from US momentum unwinding and rising geopolitical risk premium.

Indonesia

Indonesia's market instability warrants specific attention. Recent legislative moves expanding supervisory authority over the central bank, an active corruption probe, and new commodity export rules have collectively destabilized investor confidence. The rupiah continues to weaken despite weekend pledges from finance and central bank officials to step up stabilization efforts. The combination of institutional uncertainty and currency pressure creates a difficult macro environment for an economy already navigating the global energy shock. ---

Europe

  • The ECB is widely expected to raise interest rates by 25 basis points on Thursday — a move that would place it at the vanguard of developed-market monetary tightening driven by the Iran war oil shock.
  • German factory orders fell more than expected in the latest reading, adding to evidence that Europe's largest economy may contract in the second quarter.
  • Intesa Sanpaolo bid €30.6 billion for Banca Monte dei Paschi di Siena, potentially opening a new phase of consolidation in Italian banking.

Monetary Policy

The ECB faces a difficult policy calculus. Energy-driven inflation is accelerating, and officials appear committed to demonstrating anti-inflation credibility with a Thursday hike. However, economists are explicitly drawing parallels to the ECB's 2011 error, when two rate hikes ahead of the eurozone debt crisis deepened a double-dip recession. JPMorgan Asset Management argues sluggish growth will make Thursday's move "one and done." Pictet and Carmignac contend the case for staying on hold remains legitimate. Markets are pricing approximately 75 basis points of total tightening by year-end, a path the consensus views as likely too aggressive given current growth dynamics. Austria separately lost its final AAA credit rating from a major assessor.

Corporate and Political

Intesa Sanpaolo's €30.6 billion offer for Monte dei Paschi, coming after a rival approach from BPM, signals renewed appetite for Italian financial sector consolidation. European leaders — Starmer, Merz, and Macron — convened in London with Zelenskyy and called on Putin to accept an immediate ceasefire in Ukraine, though Russia has thus far rejected a direct talks proposal. A UK poll showed 52% of Britons now favor rejoining the EU, reversing the 2016 result. ---

LatAm

  • Peru's presidential election remained unresolved with approximately 89% of votes counted, with conservative Keiko Fujimori holding a narrow lead over leftist Roberto Sánchez; a final official count is not expected until mid-July.
  • US tariff proposals targeting Brazil cited deforestation and corruption, with the administration also proposing duties on 60 economies for alleged failure to enforce forced-labor prohibitions — framing that may make tariffs politically durable against future Democratic opposition.
  • Argentina's Milei administration awarded a 25-year contract to upgrade a key trade artery to a venture with Chinese ties, despite Washington pressure, highlighting the persistent competition for regional influence.

Trade and Tariffs

The Trump administration's use of environmental and labor standards as tariff justifications represents a strategically notable development for the region. Brazil faces proposed duties linked explicitly to Amazon deforestation and corruption — charges that, whatever their sincerity, are difficult for any future US administration to unilaterally abandon. The US-Mexico-Canada trade agreement appears set to blow past its July 1 renewal milestone, with auto manufacturing rules remaining a central unresolved issue. Mexico awarded 37 renewable energy projects in a recent tender, adding 7,411 megawatts of expected capacity — a constructive signal for the energy transition even amid broader political turbulence.

Peru Election

The tight presidential race between Fujimori and Sánchez carries significant implications for Peru's economic policy trajectory. Both campaigns are expected to contest ballots aggressively, with the final adjudicated result unlikely before mid-July. The outcome will shape copper sector policy, fiscal management, and relations with multilateral lenders in one of the region's key commodity exporters. ---

Global

  • Israel struck military targets in Iran on Monday, retaliating against Iranian ballistic missile attacks, representing the most serious challenge yet to the April 8 ceasefire; Iranian state media later reported Tehran had declared an end to its military operation.
  • Brent crude rose above $97 per barrel and WTI advanced approximately 4.5% on escalation fears, though prices pared gains after the Iranian ceasefire declaration; the Strait of Hormuz closure, now past 100 days, continues to reshape global energy flows.
  • The Houthis declared a "complete ban" on Israeli shipping in the Red Sea, though the practical impact appears limited given already-depressed traffic volumes and ambiguity over target definitions.

Energy Markets

The Iran war has now persisted beyond 100 days, and the economic modeling of sustained elevated oil prices is becoming more concrete. PIIE analysis of a $120/barrel scenario over one year projects global growth slowing materially, with the Middle East and North Africa suffering GDP losses exceeding 12% below baseline. China faces a 1.8% GDP reduction; South Korea 2.8%; Western Europe 1.6%. The US remains relatively insulated at approximately 1.2% below baseline. A Qatari LNG tanker was tracked transiting the Strait of Hormuz over the weekend, suggesting limited but ongoing flows. Iranian crude is being offered to Chinese independent refiners at discounts exceeding $1/barrel to Brent, a sign of pressure on Iranian export revenues.

Geopolitics

Xi Jinping's Pyongyang visit — his first in seven years — underscores Beijing's intent to retain leverage over a North Korean government whose Russian alliance has provided new strategic autonomy. The IMF's Georgieva warned separately that the global economy must build resilience against shocks that are becoming more frequent, a judgment that applies equally to energy, geopolitical, and financial system stresses. The FIFA World Cup opening this week in the US adds symbolic geopolitical texture, including the theoretical possibility of a US-Iran knockout stage encounter, though its macro relevance is limited.
