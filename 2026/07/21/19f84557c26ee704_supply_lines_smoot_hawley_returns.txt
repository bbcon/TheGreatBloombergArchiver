It’s all about pressure and leverage. That’s Donald Trump’s ultimate theory of 
negotiation, as described inThe Art of the Deal and as practiced during his 
trade wars.

The US president believes he has a natural advantage over Canada, a country 
whose economy is less than a tenth as large and whose exporters are 
overwhelmingly reliant on the American market. The two nations have been 
democratic allies for well over a century, fought alongside each other in two 
world wars and have had some form of free-trade deal since the 1980s. The 
latest version is the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement, negotiated and signed by 
Trump himself.

Trump’s latest tactic 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/us-sets-50-tariff-on-some-canadian-goods-over-retaliation-claim?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>
 is to use a provision of the Trade Act of 1930 — the infamous Smoot-Hawley law 
— to threaten more taxes on imports of Canadian goods. In 30 days, the US would 
put 50% tariffs on a range of items (beer, hockey equipment, glassware, 
cement), with no loophole this time for “USMCA-compliant” goods. Stuff the US 
needs from Canada in large quantities, like oil and potash, are exempted, as 
are items covered by prior Section 232 levies like autos and metals.

Related: US Decides Against Renewing USMCA, Shifting to Rolling Talks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/us-decides-against-renewing-usmca-shifting-to-rolling-talks?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>

“This is definitely a big escalation. We always knew that things would 
probably get worse before they got better,” William Pellerin, a Canada-based 
international trade lawyer with McMillan, told Bloomberg’s Thomas Seal. “It 
certainly forces Canada back to the negotiation table, already more than we 
were already forced.”

On the surface, Washington’s latest move is retaliation for retaliation.

When Trump put his tariffs on steel, aluminum, autos and other items last 
year, Canada called it a violation of USMCA and hit back in a variety of ways. 
Prime Minister Mark Carney backed down on some of those measures, but others 
remain in place.

Most Canadian provinces banned US booze from retail sales (yes, they have that 
power). For more than a year, it has been impossible to find a drop of Kentucky 
bourbon or California wine in the government-run liquor stores of Ontario, the 
largest province.

And Carney, desperate to save some of Canada’s dwindling automotive jobs, 
decided to match Trump’s auto tariffs, with a twist. A company like Ford Motor, 
which still employs thousands of Canadians making trucks and parts, is allowed 
to sell US-made vehicles in Canada tariff-free. But a company like BMW, which 
manufactures cars in South Carolina but not in Canada, pays a duty to ship from 
the US.

So Canadian imports of US alcoholic beverages plunged 81%, while imports of US 
vehicles tumbled 22%,according to 
<https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/07/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-imposes-additional-tariffs-on-canada/>
 the White House, which is why it’s taking action using so-calledSection 338 
<https://www.cov.com/-/media/files/corporate/publications/2016/12/law360_the_presidents_long_forgotten_power_to_raise_tariffs.pdf>
.

Context: USTR Greer Threatens Canada Over Its Boycott of Booze 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-22/us-trade-rep-jamieson-greer-threatens-canada-over-booze-boycott?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>

What will Carney do? His statement Monday night said the government is ready 
to “intensify” discussions aimed at resolving trade irritants with the US. And 
he may have no choice but to try to reach some kind of deal.

But past efforts to strike a bargain with Trump have been fruitless. The 
Canadian leader remains quite popular, but every time he gives the White House 
a concession without getting something in return, he loses a bit of political 
capital.
In a research note <>, Chris Kennedy of Bloomberg Economics said “we expect 
Washington and Ottawa to work toward a resolution to delay or cancel at least 
some of the planned tariffs between now and Aug. 19.” Still, “going forward, we 
expect Trump will deploy Section 338 in future disputes,” he wrote.
Canada’s Concessions

Last year, Carney backed down on a digital services tax 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-26/digital-services-taxes-why-trump-is-threatening-tariffs-over-tech-levies?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>
 — one that had been planned and discussed in Canada for years — to mollify 
Trump and the US tech industry. Within weeks, Trump sent him a letter 
increasing US levies against Canadian goods. Then, last August, the prime 
minister stunned some observers by unilaterally scrapping many of the 
retaliatory tariffs that had been put in place by his predecessor, Justin 
Trudeau.

Carney’s idea was that concessions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/canada-greenlights-all-gulfstream-jets-after-trump-threats?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>
 offered the best path to a deal on tariff relief — wishful thinking, in 
retrospect.

Related: Ford Commits $499 Million to Canada Plant, Defends Trade Deal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-19/ford-commits-499-million-to-canada-engine-plant-defends-trade-deal?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>

This month, Carney agreed to give a slice of toll revenue from a new bridge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/us-canada-contradict-each-other-on-financial-terms-of-detroit-bridge-deal?cmpid=BBD072126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6696>
 that Canada paid for, all because Trump and his people refused to let it open. 
The giveaway was small in financial terms, but the prime minister still took 
plenty of criticism. Canadians would like to know: Will anything ever be enough 
for Trump?

“We’ve said as the business community to the government, ‘No more concessions, 
get to the negotiation table and address all of this together,’” said Matthew 
Holmes, chief of public policy at the Canadian Chamber of Commerce.

That’s easy to say. Can it actually be done?
