• S&P 500 closed above 7,000 for the first time last week, with the index up approximately 9% in April, but the rally showed signs of fragility: fewer than half of members traded above their 50-day moving averages at the record, and volume ran 11% below the six-month average.
• Kevin Warsh's Senate Banking Committee confirmation hearing on Tuesday and Wednesday dominated monetary policy attention, with the nominee pledging Fed independence while signaling comfort with the administration's "economic statecraft" framework and interest in overhauling inflation measurement toward trimmed-mean gauges.
• Tesla beat first-quarter earnings estimates on Wednesday but gave back after-hours gains after announcing a $25 billion capex plan; Intel surged approximately 20% on Friday on a blockbuster AI-driven revenue forecast.
• Meta and Microsoft announced workforce reductions totaling as many as 23,000 positions, framed by markets as AI cost discipline; Alaska Air suspended profit guidance, citing Hormuz-driven fuel costs.
• Consumer sentiment fell to an all-time low, with household concerns about war-driven inflation and gasoline prices approaching four-year highs diverging sharply from equity market performance.

**Equity Markets**
US equity markets delivered a week of broadly constructive performance against a backdrop of persistent geopolitical uncertainty, with the S&P 500 sustaining its April advance and the Nasdaq 100 logging a 13-session winning streak — its longest since 2013 — before pausing Monday. JPMorgan raised its year-end S&P 500 target to 7,600 following the index's breach of 7,000. The rally's technical characteristics, however, warrant scrutiny: the combination of below-average volume, narrow market breadth, and declining investor sentiment during an advance to all-time highs suggests the move is being driven by a relatively small cohort of large-cap names rather than broad-based conviction. Analysts at Barclays drew a parallel to the low-volatility conditions preceding the February 2018 "Volmageddon" correction, a precedent that merits monitoring even if it does not constitute a near-term forecast.

**Corporate Earnings**
First-quarter earnings provided genuine support to the market's constructive positioning, with nearly 80% of S&P 500 companies reporting beats — a rate above the historical average. Technology remained the dominant earnings driver. Intel's Friday surge of approximately 20% in premarket trading reflected a blockbuster AI infrastructure forecast and placed the stock on course to eclipse its year-2000 record. Texas Instruments surged 12% Thursday after a stronger-than-expected outlook driven by data-center and industrial demand. The contrast with ServiceNow, which fell 13% after cutting its margin outlook and citing deal delays attributable to the Middle East conflict, illustrates the differentiation within technology: companies with direct infrastructure exposure are outperforming those dependent on discretionary enterprise software spend. Tesla's Wednesday report — $0.41 per share against a $0.34 consensus — generated a muted response once the $25 billion capex commitment became clear, as investors signaled a preference for automotive clarity over unquantified robotics ambitions.

**Monetary Policy and the Warsh Hearing**
The Warsh confirmation hearing was the week's most consequential domestic policy event, though it generated more questions than answers. The nominee's preference for trimmed-mean inflation gauges — where the Dallas Fed's measure currently reads 2.33% against core PCE's 3.0% — carries material implications for perceived distance from the 2% target and, if adopted as the policy anchor, would shift the rate path considerably. Warsh's alignment with the Bessent-Rubio "economic statecraft" framework on dollar stability also drew attention, though he was careful not to commit to specifics. Senator Thom Tillis's hold on the nomination, tied to the Justice Department's probe of Jerome Powell over Fed renovation expenditures, creates a genuine possibility that Powell remains in post beyond his May 15 term expiry — an awkward institutional interregnum that bond markets have not yet fully priced.

**Fiscal and Energy Policy**
Trump invoked the Defense Production Act to direct federal funds toward energy infrastructure, including coal, LNG, petroleum, and grid projects, as the administration faces political pressure over elevated fuel prices. The internal divergence between Energy Secretary Wright — who said gasoline prices are unlikely to fall below $3 per gallon until next year — and Treasury Secretary Bessent on the energy price outlook is a notable crack in the administration's narrative. Goldman Sachs estimated US potential GDP growth at approximately 2.3% annually through 2028, driven by roughly 2% annual AI productivity gains, a constructive structural backdrop that sits in tension with the cyclical headwinds generated by sustained energy price elevation and consumer confidence at all-time lows.
