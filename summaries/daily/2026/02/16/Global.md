# Global Daily Briefing — February 16, 2026

**Oil markets in suspended animation ahead of US-Iran talks.** WTI traded narrowly around $63 as participants awaited the resumption of US-Iran negotiations Tuesday, with directional conviction notably absent. Javier Blas characterized last week's International Energy Week debate as a narrow win for bears, though "whispers about an actual surplus are getting louder." More striking was activity in tanker markets: South Korea's Sinokor group has accumulated roughly 120 VLCCs over the past two months, helping push benchmark VLCC earnings above $120,000 per day—a more than fourfold increase in a month. Separately, the US military boarded a tanker in the Indian Ocean over the weekend, alleging it sought to evade Trump's Caribbean energy blockade against sanctioned vessels, while Netanyahu reiterated that any Iran deal must strip nuclear capabilities—a maximalist position that may complicate the diplomatic track.

**Monetary policy divergence widens, with the ECB making a strategic move.** The European Central Bank announced it stands ready to provide euro liquidity to monetary authorities globally, an effort to mitigate market stress and expand the single currency's international role. The initiative fits a broader European recalibration in response to a shifting US posture. Sweden's tentative reopening of the euro debate—unthinkable a few years ago—reflects the same dynamic, with geopolitical exposure rather than monetary mechanics now driving the conversation. Speculation has also intensified around an accelerated, package appointment of Lagarde's successor at the ECB, partly to insulate the choice from a possible National Rally victory in France in April 2027.

**Europe's carbon market faces its sternest political test in years.** EU allowances fell to their lowest since July as leaders openly clashed over ETS reform at last week's summit. Slovakia's Fico called for suspension; Merz floated revision before walking it back; Macron defended the system. The Commission has committed to a reform proposal in Q3, but JPMorgan notes the uncertainty is already impairing corporate green capex planning—a tension that may persist regardless of where reforms ultimately land.

**Climate tools under pressure elsewhere.** The collapse of Kenya's Koko Networks, after authorities delayed compliance-market authorization, has damaged Kenya's standing as an offsets destination and may trigger a World Bank insurance payout near $180 million.
