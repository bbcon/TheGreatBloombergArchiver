Donald Trump may have backed away from his demand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-belittles-allies-that-rejected-his-iran-war-appeals?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 that US allies help him in Iran. That doesn’t mean Sanae Takaichi is off the 
hook.

The Japanese prime minister is due to meet with Trump at the White House 
tomorrow, one of the first foreign leaders to visit from a country singled out 
by the president since he launched the war.
Trump and Takaichi on board the USS George Washington at the US naval base in 
Yokosuka, Japan, on Oct. 28.Photographer: Andrew Caballero-Reynolds/AFP/Getty 
Images
Japan was among those nations Trump called on to help protect shipping in the 
Strait of Hormuz, a tough proposition for Tokyo given its pacifist constitution 
strictly limits the deployment of the military overseas. Like those others, 
Japan indicated it hadno plans to meet the request 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/europe-is-finally-standing-up-to-donald-trump-s-threats-on-iran?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

Faced with a blanket no, Trump abruptly dropped his demand yesterday, saying 
that the US didn’t need any help. But the famously petulant president may yet 
try to extract a price from Takaichi.

The prospect of Trump pressuring Takaichi in the Oval Office is a worrying 
thought for Japanese officials who want the trip to demonstrate the strength of 
US-Japan ties.

They’re also looking for US support in a damaging standoff with China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-14/china-s-wang-warns-takaichi-over-japan-s-ghosts-of-militarism?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

The timing had looked optimal: getting in Trump’s ear immediately before he 
visited Beijing.

That was before the joint US-Israeli attacks on Iran disrupted oil supplies, 
especially to Asia, and dragged on the global economy, prompting Trump to
postpone his China trip 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-s-delay-of-xi-summit-buys-china-time-to-game-out-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

Addressing parliament in Tokyo today, Takaichi said she’s facing an “extremely 
difficult 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/japan-s-takaichi-visits-trump-as-hormuz-warship-standoff-simmers?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
” meeting. She did, though, hint at her possible approach of pledgingmore US 
investments 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/how-tariff-threats-got-japan-south-korea-to-promise-billions-in-us-investments?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
.

While avoiding expressing support 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/japan-condemns-iran-nuclear-program-stays-quiet-on-us-attack?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 for Trump’s war, Takaichi has often repeated a guiding principle of Tokyo’s 
foreign and security policy: Countries shouldn’t seek to alter the geopolitical 
status quo through force.

It’s a message usually directed at revisionist powers like China and Russia. 
With its attacks on Iran, the US may now be in that bracket, too.— Alastair Gale
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AW8LqVZ6g3Q/alastair-gale>

the Cuban economy to the diaspora as falling short,suggesting no letup

The UN warned that as many as 45 million more people could face acute hunger 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/un-warns-prolonged-iran-war-could-trigger-record-global-hunger?cmpid=BBD031826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 if the conflict in Iran doesn’t ease by the middle of the year. The near 
shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz and mounting risks to traffic in the Red Sea 
are already increasing energy, fuel and fertilizer costs, deepening hunger 
beyond the Middle East, the UN World Food Programme said yesterday. That could 
push the number of people in acute food insecurity to 363 million, eclipsing 
the hunger levels that followed Russia’s invasion of Ukraine in 2022.
