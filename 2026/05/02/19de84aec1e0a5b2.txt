India this week signed the latest in what’s been a run of rapid-fire free 
trade agreements 
<https://www.pib.gov.in/PressReleasePage.aspx?PRID=2236134&reg=3&lang=1>. The 
deal, with New Zealand, is far from the South Asian country’s largest, but like 
others over the past year it marked a swift conclusion to what had been a 
drawn-out process.

Negotiations between Asia’s No. 3 economy and the Australasian nation began in 
2010, but spent much of the past decade in cold storage. The agreement comes on 
the heels of India’s “mother of all deals 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/eu-trade-deal-gives-modi-s-make-in-india-ambitions-a-boost?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
” with the European Union, which ended two decades of stop-start talks. Last 
July, New Delhi sealed a free-trade dealwith the UK 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-24/uk-india-seal-free-trade-agreement-slashing-tariffs-barriers?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
, and negotiations continue onfinalizing an interim pact 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/india-in-talks-with-us-as-court-overturns-sweeping-trump-tariffs?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
 with the US.

This all amounts to a striking reversal of Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s 
earlier turn toward protectionism, and a resumption of what had been a 
long-term trend of declining tariffs. Part of the impetus for this turnabout is 
pressure generated by President Donald Trump’sprotectionist moves 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-06/trump-hits-india-with-additional-25-tariff-over-russia-oil-buys?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
 and a broader unraveling of the US-India relationship. The new reality of 
geopolitical uncertainty makes securing new markets and partnerships more 
urgent.

But the shift also reflects a deeper realization for India: There is no 
durable alternative to export-led growth. Modi’s original protectionist pivot 
rested on prioritizing domestic demand and economic self-reliance over exports. 
He set out to build national champions in strategic, technology-intensive 
sectors through a mix of protection, production incentives and regulatory 
support.

The bet was that higher trade barriers also would lure global firms to 
relocate production 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-15/trump-wants-apple-to-stop-moving-iphone-production-to-india?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
 to India. That template now is being cast aside.

Between 2017 and 2020, India’s average tariff rate rose from roughly 13% to 
roughly 18% <https://pages.jh.edu/schatt20/papers/SC_AS_TradePolicy.pdf>. 
Modi’s protectionist tilt included an increase inquality control orders 
<https://www.grantthornton.in/insights/articles/quality-control-orders-disruption-or-advantage-india/>
 (QCOs), which effectively served as non-tariff barriers aimed at low-cost 
imports, primarily from China.

The idea was that India’s producers could supply the nation’s own consumer 
market. But India’s domestic demand, although large in absolute terms, isn’t 
deep enough to sustain the high growth needed to match the ambition of 
achieving developed-nation status by 2047. The approach shielded large Indian 
conglomerates from foreign competition, but also reduced their capacity to 
become globally competitive.

The numbers tell the story: Manufacturing’s share of India GDP has slipped 
from 16% to 13%in the last decade 
<https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/NV.IND.MANF.ZS> and job creation remains 
Modi’s Achilles heel. High tariffs also hurt India’s exports, which are import 
intensive — in other words, they depend on intermediate items made elsewhere. 
The burden of protection fell disproportionately on small and medium-sized 
firms, which are responsible for the majority of job creation.

Policymakers are now beginning to adjust. To expand labor-intensive exports 
and attract the investment and technology needed to upgrade manufacturing and 
create jobs, India is having to lower its own trade barriers.

In its deals with the EU and the US (although US deal terms are still 
unclear), India has committed to eliminating or drastically reducing tariffs 
over the next decade, except in politically sensitive sectors like agriculture 
and dairy. Some duties in machinery, textiles and chemicals will be removed 
immediately. Combined with India’s expanding number of free trade agreements, 
this amounts to an unprecedented opening of the economy.

India also has unilaterally cut tariffs on some key inputs and rolled back 
QCOs in sectors including steel, textiles and chemicals. Additionally, 
subsidies are being redesigned to promote exports rather than just production.

Still, India’s change in tone isn’t a wholesale embrace of trade 
liberalization, but a recalibration.

India remains wary of slashing its baseline tariffs, known as 
most-favored-nation rates, given concerns about Chinese overcapacity and a 
wideningbilateral trade deficit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-01/china-ready-to-buy-more-products-from-india-as-us-tariffs-loom?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
 with its giant neighbor. And while QCOs are being scaled back, they haven’t 
been dismantled to the extent recommended by the government’s own advisory 
committee. Trade negotiators continue to view high tariffs and QCOs as 
necessary barriers against China, and leverage to secure better terms in 
bilateral deals.

It’s also unclear if the recent trade deals go far enough. Without strong 
safeguards for foreign investments, and greater alignment of intellectual 
property protection and regulatory standards, they may leave India falling 
short of meaningful integration into theglobal supply chain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-04-28/trump-tariffs-india-s-strengthening-supply-chain-position-lures-even-china?cmpid=BBD050226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=3899>
.

And from a broader economic perspective, the commercial agreements cannot 
compensate for New Delhi’s unpredictable tax enforcement and slow progress on 
land and labor reforms and cutting regulatory red tape.

Even so, Modi has been able to use a challenging external environment to 
catalyze a new push toward liberalization. The rest of the world would be wrong 
to shrug off the consequences of the changes underway.

India presents major opportunities in a world where the largest economy — the 
US — is no longer the market-for-all it once appeared to be. The Modi 
government’s trade-policy revamp is incentivizing advanced economies to show 
more flexibility in reaching deals with India.

Paradoxically, India’s past underperformance also serves as an advantage: its 
exports can expand significantly without overwhelming global markets. And 
history shows that India’s highest growth rates were achieved alongside robust 
export performance.

Regardless of how US trade policy evolves, Modi’s turn toward openness is a 
course correction that’s arguably been long overdue. —Chetna Kumar 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AXMrXSEbA9A/chetna-kumar>
