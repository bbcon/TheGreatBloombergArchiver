Asia’s oil-importing economies are caught in a deepening bind as the 
intensifying US-Iran conflict drives crude prices higher.

Given these countries pay for oil in dollars, rising prices pressure their 
currencies by widening trade and current account deficits. And the more their 
currencies fall, the more their central banks are faced withthe difficult choice
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/php-usd-philippine-peso-declines-to-match-record-low-on-higher-oil-prices?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6826>
 between defending their exchange rates and supporting growth.

For now, the balance is tilted toward defense. The Philippine central bank 
intervened in the currency market on Wednesday after the peso fell to a record 
low. On Monday, the Reserve Bank of India sold dollars tosupport the rupee 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/rbi-intervenes-to-support-rupee-as-currency-nears-record-low?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6826>
 as it dropped toward its weakest level ever.

Brent crude has jumped 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-21/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-july-22?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6826>
 30% this month to top $95 a barrel as the US and Iran played down the prospect 
of talks and supply disruptions continued to mount.

While Japan runs a current account surplus, the country still imports almost 
all of its oil, leaving its trade balance sensitive to crude prices. After the 
yen slid past 163 per dollar overnight for the first time since 1986, Minister 
of Finance Satsuki Katayama warned that authorities areready to take action 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/japan-s-katayama-says-ready-to-take-bold-steps-on-fx-as-needed?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6826>
 in the currency market if necessary, saying the US-Iran situation was creating 
a “very difficult” environment. One possible step israising interest rates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/boj-is-said-open-to-faster-rate-hike-pace-as-yen-adds-price-risk?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6826>
 at a faster pace than consensus.

Indonesia, which posted its first trade deficit in six years <> in May as 
energy costs surged, has already taken aggressive action: its central bank 
lifted rates by 100 basis points in recent months after its currency fell to a 
record low. It leftrates unchanged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/indonesia-keeps-rate-steady-at-5-75-after-rapid-tightening?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6826>
 on Wednesday.

With oil prices likely to remain elevated, Asian central bankers may need to 
rethink summer vacation plans.
