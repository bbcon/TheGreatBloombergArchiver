US equity futures recovered partial ground in early Monday trade, with S&P 500 futures rising 0.3% after an earlier selloff, while the Nasdaq 100 sits roughly 11.5% below its October all-time high and remains under sustained pressure. The slow-moving nature of this correction — quite unlike the sharp sell-off that followed Liberation Day tariffs a year ago — reflects lingering market conviction that either a rapid diplomatic resolution or a decisive US military outcome remains plausible. That conviction is fraying.

On the monetary policy front, Federal Reserve Chair Powell is scheduled to speak Monday, with markets attentive to any signal on how the Fed is calibrating the competing risks of oil-driven inflation and decelerating growth. Bond managers at Pimco, JPMorgan Asset Management, and Columbia Threadneedle have begun shifting their focus from the inflationary impulse of higher crude to the growth-shock scenario, arguing the surge in yields has run its course. Two- and five-year Treasury yields have risen by more than half a percentage point since the Iran conflict began; the 10-year briefly traded above 4.4% before firming on Monday. The employment report on Friday — with consensus expecting 60,000 jobs added after a 92,000 decline in February — will provide the first clean read on how the real economy is absorbing the shock.

On fiscal and trade policy, this week marks the one-year anniversary of Liberation Day tariffs. The US Supreme Court in February ruled those tariffs illegal, triggering a $170 billion refund process. Bloomberg Economics estimates that 43% of US imports — roughly $1.4 trillion in goods — currently remain tariff-free, a figure frequently lost in the broader narrative. Foreign direct investment into the US totaled $288.4 billion in 2025, a modest decline from 2024 and the lowest of the Biden era, undermining the administration's argument that tariffs are catalyzing a manufacturing investment surge. Aluminum producers Alcoa and Century Aluminum rose 8.7% and 8.5% respectively in premarket trade, following Iran's weekend strikes on Gulf production facilities.
