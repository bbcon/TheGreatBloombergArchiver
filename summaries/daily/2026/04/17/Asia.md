China's first-quarter GDP expanded 5.0% year-on-year, beating expectations, offering a measure of reassurance to policymakers navigating simultaneous headwinds from US tariffs and the Iran war. However, the Canton Fair in Guangzhou provided a more granular and cautionary picture. Middle Eastern buyers — who had increasingly substituted for absent American and European customers since the pandemic and the onset of Trump's tariffs — are now conspicuously absent due to the conflict and flight disruption. Exporters described this year as potentially the worst in memory, facing the sequential blows of tariffs and war simultaneously. High-tech exports surged nearly 30% in the first quarter, offering a partial offset, but the durability of that momentum against deteriorating external conditions remains uncertain.

Xi Jinping had his most diplomatically active week in nearly two years, receiving at least five high-profile delegations including Spain's prime minister, Abu Dhabi's crown prince, Vietnam's president, and Russia's foreign minister. Xi framed China as a source of stability against what he described as a "crumbling" global order, proposing a four-point Middle East peace plan and vowing to deepen ties with Moscow. His positioning is deliberate — building on the 2023 Saudi-Iran rapprochement — but Beijing's concrete contributions to conflict resolution have so far been limited, and state media pushed back against suggestions that China nudged Tehran toward negotiations. A Trump-Xi summit in roughly a month's time looms as the next test.

The yuan's strength has prompted the People's Bank of China to use its daily reference rate to temper further appreciation, as war-driven capital flows fuel bullish sentiment. On trade infrastructure, yuan-denominated crude oil transactions reached a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS. Separately, Australia announced a multibillion-dollar agreement to procure 11 advanced naval frigates from Japan — the first three vessels covered by an A$15–20 billion contract — a transaction that advances both Australian defense modernization and Japan's ambitions in global arms markets.

LNG supply disruptions are reshaping long-term energy strategy across the region. Asian LNG flows have fallen to their lowest since 2020, and with prices still 50% above pre-war levels, Vietnam is reconsidering gas-fired power in favor of renewables, Pakistan is reviving domestic gas production, and China — buffered by domestic gas, pipeline imports, and record coal output — has seen LNG imports fall 25% without facing a supply deficit.
