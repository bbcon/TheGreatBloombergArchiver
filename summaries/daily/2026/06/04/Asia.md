• The yen weakened back through 160 per dollar despite hawkish BOJ signaling and prior Ministry of Finance intervention warnings, highlighting persistent structural pressures on the currency.
• South Korea's KOSPI fell 1.8%, with Broadcom's guidance miss weighing on tech-heavy indexes in Seoul and Taiwan.

**Monetary Policy and Currency Stress**

The BOJ's apparent readiness to raise its benchmark rate toward 1% marks a remarkable inflection for Japanese monetary policy. Yet yen weakness persists near 160 per dollar — a level that previously triggered intervention in April — suggesting markets remain skeptical that rate increases alone can resolve Japan's structural imbalances. Brookings' Robin Brooks attributes the weakness to Japan's debt overhang; others point to growth remaining too fragile to sustain meaningfully higher rates. The yen's depreciation against the Chinese yuan is also deepening bilateral tensions. Across the broader region, currency guardians in Seoul and Jakarta stepped up interventions as dollar strength, rising oil prices, and expectations of higher US rates pressured regional exchange rates. India is reportedly weighing capital inflow measures to support the rupee without tightening borrowing costs.

**Corporate and Political Developments**

TSMC's CEO stated AI chip supply will remain constrained for years despite ongoing capacity expansion, confirming a structural bottleneck in AI infrastructure buildout. Indonesia faces compounding headwinds: the rupiah is at record lows, an MSCI investability review is pending, and a corruption investigation has reached President Prabowo Subianto's flagship $15 billion free-meals program. South Korea's ruling Democratic Party won broadly in local elections, further pressuring the opposition People Power Party following former President Yoon's impeachment.
