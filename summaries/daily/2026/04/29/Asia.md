The Bank of Japan delivered a "hawkish hold" at its April meeting, keeping rates unchanged on a 6-3 vote — a record number of dissents — while signaling its intention to raise rates further, likely in June, contingent on avoiding serious escalation in the Middle East. Despite the explicit forward guidance, the yen remains stubbornly weak near 160 per dollar, reflecting persistent investor skepticism about how far normalization can proceed under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, who has historically favored a weak yen to support exporters and appointed two dovish committee members last month. Takaichi recently criticized a trade minister for suggesting rate hikes could strengthen the currency, keeping political pressure on the BOJ visible. Spring Shunto wage negotiations produced a third consecutive year of approximately 5% wage increases, and five-year inflation breakevens have reached 2.7%, suggesting bond markets believe the deflationary era is genuinely over — yet the yen's weakness persists, creating a tension between the data and market pricing.

In China, regulators have suspended the issuance of new autonomous vehicle licenses after dozens of Baidu Apollo Go robotaxis abruptly stopped operating in Wuhan last month, stranding passengers. Authorities called for a full safety review by local governments, marking a meaningful setback for an industry estimated to reach 83 billion yuan by 2030. Goldman Sachs staff in Hong Kong have separately lost access to Anthropic's Claude AI model, a location-specific restriction affecting primarily software engineers. Hong Kong Exchanges reported record first-quarter net income of HK$5.2 billion, up 27%, driven by a surge in IPOs and robust trading volumes. Hong Kong also ran its widest trade deficit in at least 74 years, a likely spillover from Hormuz-related supply disruptions.

In India, major airlines warned of potential service suspensions unless the government reduces jet fuel prices, with the Federation of Indian Airlines describing the sector as "on the verge of closing down." As the world's third-largest oil consumer, India is absorbing disproportionate pressure from the Hormuz closure, with Brent trading near $111 a barrel following a 2.8% gain on Tuesday.
