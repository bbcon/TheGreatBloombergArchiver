The most consequential cross-cutting development remains the US-Iran conflict and its implications for global energy markets. US forces struck Iranian missile and drone launch sites near the Strait of Hormuz after Iran attacked three American warships transiting the strait — a significant escalation even as President Trump maintained that the broader ceasefire remains in effect. WTI crude moved higher on the clash but retreated intraday, reflecting market uncertainty about whether the engagement represents a contained incident or the beginning of a new escalation cycle. Iran is expected to respond to a US-proposed peace framework via Pakistan within two days; Trump has threatened intensified strikes if Tehran refuses terms.

China's role in the energy disruption is multifaceted and worth disaggregating. Beijing slashed April oil imports by approximately one quarter from pre-war levels, with state-owned companies reselling cargoes to European and Asian buyers — a dynamic that has helped rebalance global supply despite the Hormuz closure. Simultaneously, China's top diplomat Wang Yi urged Iran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz quickly, driven both by Beijing's dependence on Middle Eastern oil (approximately two-fifths of its imports) and the desire to position Xi Jinping as a peacemaker ahead of his summit with Trump. Iran, meanwhile, is expanding rail trade through Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan to China as a partial workaround to the US port blockade, deepening its economic dependence on Beijing.

The Trump-Xi Beijing summit, scheduled for next week, will be the first visit by a sitting US president to China since 2017. Senior business figures from Blackstone, Citigroup, Nvidia, Apple, Exxon, and Boeing are expected to accompany Trump, signaling a commercial dimension to the visit. Expectations for substantive deliverables are low — Taiwan, tech curbs, and Hong Kong remain areas of significant divergence — but both sides appear to have strategic incentives to maintain a stable bilateral appearance. On artificial intelligence and energy infrastructure, the power demands of data centers are increasingly a government-level concern across Australia, the UK, Kenya, and the United States, with BloombergNEF projecting US data center power consumption to exceed 106 gigawatts by 2035, more than double current levels.
