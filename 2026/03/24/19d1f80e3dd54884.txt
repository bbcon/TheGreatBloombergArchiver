Checking on Bessent’s 3%

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent regularly says he got out from behind his 
desk in the private sector to tackle the unsustainable path for US fiscal 
policy. The way things are going, critics might suggest he get back behind — or 
even under — it.

Trump’s Iran war, along with diminished prospects for tariff revenue, mean the 
federal government’s budget gapfaces a widening this year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/bessent-s-longshot-deficit-goal-undercut-by-war-and-tariff-news?cmpid=BBD032426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
, economists warn. That means the Treasury will need to sell more debt, testing 
investors’ appetites and potentially putting upward pressure on longer-term 
interest rates.

The Pentagon has already asked Congress for $200 billion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/hegseth-says-iran-war-not-becoming-a-forever-war-or-quagmire?cmpid=BBD032426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 in extra funding for the Iran conflict. If not offset by new revenue or 
spending cuts, that would take the 2026 deficit near $2 trillion compared with 
the $1.78 trillion for fiscal 2025.

As for tariffs, revenue flowing from those to the US Treasury had already shown
signs of peaking 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/budget-deficit-narrows-at-slower-pace-after-tariff-rate-decrease?cmpid=BBD032426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 before the Supreme Court in February invalidated most of Trump’s levies. While 
the administration aims to rebuild its trade regime via other laws, it will be 
a challenge to bring in as much money.

And by stoking inflation risks with the surging price of oil, the Iran war has 
curbed expectations for Federal Reserveinterest-rate cuts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/fed-s-goolsbee-says-he-could-see-circumstances-for-rate-hike?cmpid=BBD032426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 that might otherwise have helped shrink one of the key deficit drivers.

The Treasury last year paid some $970 billion in net interest on federal debt 
— more than half the entire budget gap. For 2026, the Congressional Budget 
Office last month projected the bill would surpass $1 trillion. But that 
projection assumed tariff rates would remain at last November’s elevated 
levels. It also was based on a Fed rate cut and 10-year Treasury yields 
averaging 4.1% this year. (As of Monday, they were around 4.3%.)

All of that makes it likely the budget gap will surpass 6% of GDP this year. 
That would be double Bessent’s original 3% target. For some months he’s been 
eyeing “something with a 3 in front of it by the time the president leaves 
office.”

That 3% target represents something of a global standard for fiscal 
sustainability — an analogue to the 2% inflation goal for central banks. It’s 
the European Union’slongstanding deficit ceiling 
<https://www.consilium.europa.eu/en/policies/excessive-deficit-procedure/>. 
Indonesia set it as the legal limit after its late-1990s financial crisis.

The US, however, has been averaging around 6% the past three years, a run the 
country hasn’t seen before in the modern era during times of economic growth 
and full employment. Goldman Sachs economists see the gap surpassing 6% through 
2029, when Trump leaves office.

The bottom line for Maya MacGuineas, who heads the nonprofit, nonpartisan 
Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, is that the Supreme Court “ruling 
on the tariffs along with the war both make avery bad fiscal trajectory 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-20/new-us-debt-projections-put-spotlight-on-the-doom-loop-scenario?cmpid=BBD032426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 even worse.”

Need-to-Know Research

Will the energy-bill hike swamp the Trump administration’s tax-cut benefits? 
This newslettertook a look 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-10/iran-war-trump-tax-cut-risks-getting-getting-canceled-out-by-energy-price-surge?cmpid=BBD032426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 at that risk earlier this month. Morgan Stanley economists on Monday said 
things aren’t looking constructive.

Tax refunds this filing season have indeed tracked higher than 2025, thanks to 
Trump’s sweeping fiscal package last July. So far, they’re up 12% in total, 
with the average refund 11% bigger, Morgan Stanley economists including Heather 
Berger wrote in a note. But that’s on the low end of the team’s expectations. 
The boost to 2026 consumer spending may be a little short of the 0.2 percentage 
point the bank had previously penciled in, they said.

The energy-cost shock, meantime, shaves 0.3 percentage point off the 2026 
consumption forecast, the economists wrote, more than offsetting the tax help. 
Consumer spending is now expected to rise 1.7% this year, down from 2.1% in 
2025, Berger and her colleagues wrote.
