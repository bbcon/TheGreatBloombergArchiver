Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asian energy team leader Stephen Stapczynski looks at the ramifications of an 
attack on a Qatari LNG tanker.

For weeks, Qatar had been signaling confidence that it would quickly revive 
liquefied natural gas exports that have been all-but halted since the beginning 
of the Iran war.

QatarEnergy’s trading arm was publicly offering spot shipments for late-August 
delivery to Asia as recently as Monday,reinforcing the impression 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/asian-lng-buyers-expect-qatar-to-let-force-majeure-lapse-in-july?cmpid=BBBXT070726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6294>
 that supply was set to return.

Satellite thermal readings suggested production trains at Ras Laffan were 
preparing for a restart, empty LNG carriers weremassing offshore 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/empty-lng-tankers-mass-outside-qatar-as-exports-tick-higher?cmpid=BBBXT070726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6294>
, and the country’s prime minister had said exports could resumewithin weeks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/qatar-sees-most-lng-output-returning-within-weeks-ft-says?cmpid=BBBXT070726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6294>
.

All of these restart plans, however, depend on one thing: Safe transit through 
the Strait of Hormuz.

The first attack on a Qatari LNG tanker since the war began now threatens that 
entire calculus. The Al Rekayyat, carrying a cargo from Qatar and concealing 
its position as it exited Hormuz via Omani waters, wasstruck by a projectile 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/iranian-missile-hits-qatari-lng-ship-in-strait-testing-us-talks?cmpid=BBBXT070726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6294>
 early on Tuesday.

The impact of the attack is rippling through the market.
A floating storage regasification unit at an LNG terminal in Karachi, Pakistan.
Photographer: Asim Hafeez/Bloomberg
One Qatari LNG carrier in the Persian Gulf appears to have paused its intended 
journey to Pakistan, forcing anxious officials in Islamabad to scramble for 
replacement cargoes. European gas prices climbed as much as 6%.

The longer Qatari supply remains constrained, the greater the risk that Europe 
and Asia will be in direct competition for LNG as they replenish inventories 
ahead of winter, pushing prices — still about 70% above preaskaskwar levels — 
even higher.

Qatar accounted for roughly a fifth of global LNG supply last year, and every 
shipment bound for customers outside the Middle East must pass through the 
Strait of Hormuz. Unlike oil, there is no practical alternative route.

That dependence has hamstrung Doha throughout the war. Qatar has repeatedly 
slowed or suspended tanker movements when security risks intensified, helping 
explain why LNG prices have remained elevated even as oil prices have largely 
returned to pre-war levels.

Now, one of Qatar’s flagship LNG carriers has come under attack despite 
efforts to conceal its location. The incident will force the country to again 
weigh how quickly it can restore exports without exposing crews and vessels to 
greater danger.

Any renewed caution will have global ramifications 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-04/energy-hungry-asia-is-already-drawing-lessons-from-iran-crisis?cmpid=BBBXT070726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6294>
.

—Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News

Arabica coffee futures rocketed higher, clocking the steepest intraday rise in

BNEF today

Europe’s battery boom has already outgrown one of its earliest revenue 
anchors: helping to keep the power grid’s frequency stable. Installed 
utility-scale battery capacity across nine key markets has risen sharply since 
2024, but demand stayed almost flat for services such as Frequency Containment 
Reserve, according to BloombergNEF. The gap is now wide enough to challenge a 
revenue stream that helped launch many of Europe’s first merchant battery 
projects.
