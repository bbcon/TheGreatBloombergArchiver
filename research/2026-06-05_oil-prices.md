# Oil Prices: The Big Story Since January
*June 5, 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

Oil markets have experienced their most violent disruption since the 1970s over the January–May 2026 period. Brent crude moved from approximately $65–70 in January to an intraday peak above $126 in late April — a gain of roughly 80% from pre-conflict levels — before partially retracing on tentative ceasefire signals in late May, closing the month down approximately 19% (the steepest monthly decline since 2020). The Strait of Hormuz closure — effectively shutting down approximately 20% of global oil flows for its third consecutive month — is the proximate cause. The structural changes set in motion by the conflict (UAE OPEC exit, EV adoption acceleration, US shale at record, petrodollar recycling contraction) will outlast the hostilities themselves. The forward curve (~$80 for later 2026) implies a Hormuz resolution that is not operationally confirmed.

## Chronology of the Move

### January: Range-Bound, Glut Thesis Intact

Brent approximately $65–70. The dominant oil narrative was geopolitical optionality rather than a live supply shock. US-Iran diplomatic tensions were building (nuclear talks signaled), but no disruption had materialized. Venezuela's re-entry into the investable universe — Maduro's ouster, Trump committing billions to infrastructure rehabilitation — was January's oil narrative, directionally a medium-term supply *addition* of heavy-sour barrels at the margin. The supply glut thesis was intact: sanctioned Iranian and Russian crude finding alternative buyers (principally Chinese teapots), OPEC+ managing production within targets, US shale near record output. Bloomberg Opinion's John Authers was already arguing markets were *underpricing* a major oil shock — an assessment that proved prescient.

### February: Escalation Without Yet a Shock ($63–70)

Brent oscillated $63–70 with escalating but still-diplomatic US-Iran tensions. The US Navy shot down an Iranian drone heading toward a carrier in the Arabian Sea — Brent moved to approximately $68 before easing as Iran confirmed nuclear talks in Muscat and Geneva. The tone shifted materially when Trump stated Iran had "10–15 days" to reach an agreement and that the US was "ready, willing, and able to rapidly fulfill its mission, with speed and violence, if necessary." Iran responded it would "defend itself and respond like never before." The Wall Street Journal reported the administration was evaluating a limited early strike to pressure rather than preempt. WTI moved from near $63 at month's open toward $66 by the final Friday.

Iranian and Russian sanctioned crude continued accumulating unsold, driven less by enforcement than by ample availability of legitimate alternatives at competitive prices — compressing Iran's fiscal position and the discount structures available to remaining buyers.

### Late February / March: The Shock (from ~$70 to $118+)

US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities were launched in the final days of February. The Strait of Hormuz effectively closed. The binding near-term constraint proved to be the **insurance market**, not a formal naval blockade: more than half of the world's largest maritime insurance clubs withdrew war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels, causing commercial shipping to self-suspend. Only two commercial vessel transits were recorded in a 24-hour period at the closure's peak, with no oil shipments passing at all.

Brent surged approximately 18% in the first full week of conflict, peaked **above $118 intraday** in the third week of March — a gain of roughly 63% from pre-conflict levels. US diesel futures gained more than 40% in a single week at peak. European natural gas rose more than 50% in one week and ultimately more than doubled. Qatar's Ras Laffan facility (approximately 20% of global LNG supply) was struck, with two LNG trains expected offline for up to five years.

The IEA announced a **record emergency release of 400 million barrels** from member-country stockpiles — its largest in history. Japan separately released approximately 80 million barrels. Brent held in the $108–113 range through the final week of March on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990. Urea prices rose 25%, aluminum briefly hit a near four-year high, and the Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index moved meaningfully higher.

The IEA's executive director, speaking in Canberra, characterized the cumulative supply disruption as equivalent to the combined effect of the two 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis. BloombergNEF's base case at peak saw Brent averaging $106.70 in April; a delayed Hormuz reopening until mid-June would push that to $154.40; Macquarie warned of a path toward $200 in a prolonged closure scenario.

### April: Ceasefire Shock, Partial Reversal, New Spike (range $91–$126+)

The ceasefire announced **April 8** — brokered with Pakistan serving as the key intermediary — produced one of the most dramatic single-day commodity moves on record: Brent fell **as much as 16%** to approximately $91.70, WTI tumbled nearly 19%. These are among the largest single-day crude declines on record, comparable only to the Covid demand collapse (2020) and the allied entry into Kuwait (January 1991).

The ceasefire was quickly doubted. Tehran alleged violations, Israeli strikes in Lebanon intensified, and WTI partially recovered toward $98–99 within days. By late April, Brent **surged back above $126** following reports that Trump was scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options and US Central Command was preparing fresh strike plans. Trump confirmed the naval blockade of Iranian ports would not be lifted without a nuclear agreement.

Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura refinery suspended operations following a drone strike. Iraq resumed pipeline exports via Turkey, contributing to price relief in the final days. The UAE's shock exit from OPEC — announced in early May — removed the cartel's primary spare capacity buffer.

Paradoxically, Iran appeared a net financial beneficiary through this period, reportedly earning hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental oil revenue as the only major exporter able to utilize the strait freely.

### May: Peak and Partial Reversal (from $126 to ~$95–100)

Brent briefly exceeded **$126 per barrel** mid-month after Axios reported Trump was to receive a briefing on new military options. Goldman Sachs' inventory analysis estimated visible global crude and product inventories drawing at **8.7 million barrels per day** in May — nearly double the conflict's running average. This rate implies **demand destruction** as the adjustment mechanism rather than supply restoration: rationing is occurring through price, not physical supply restoration.

The final week of May produced the month's most significant diplomatic development. Trump called off planned strikes after Saudi Arabia and Gulf allies requested additional time for diplomacy, sending Brent approximately 7% lower in thin holiday trading. A tentative **60-day ceasefire extension** framework was reported, with Hormuz reopening proposed in exchange for a moratorium on uranium enrichment and lifting of some sanctions.

WTI fell nearly 10% from its weekly peak on the diplomatic signals before partially recovering as US forces struck additional Iranian targets and Iran's Supreme Leader declared that US military bases in the region "will no longer be safe." Republican hawks argued the emerging terms concede too much. Trump had not yet formally approved the terms as of end-May. Iran's Foreign Ministry stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent." Polymarket assigns **below 50% odds** to normal Hormuz traffic by end-June.

May closed with Brent down approximately **19% from its monthly peak** — the steepest monthly decline since 2020 — but still well above pre-conflict levels.

## How the Narrative Shifted Through the Year

The oil narrative evolved through four distinct phases:

**Phase 1 (Jan–early Feb): Diplomatic resolution likely; glut persists.** The market was pricing a negotiated nuclear agreement with the supply overhang from discounted Iranian/Russian barrels intact. Risk premium was modest. The Authers underpricing argument was a minority view.

**Phase 2 (late Feb–early March): Escalation risk underpriced; war risk premium building.** The US military build-up in the region was visible and accelerating. Insurance market dynamics were becoming the key variable — more important than military force in determining transit feasibility.

**Phase 3 (March–April): Supply shock dominance; Hormuz as the mechanism.** The narrative shifted from "diplomatic track" to "structural disruption, duration uncertain." IEA record releases, emergency policy responses globally, Brent in $108–126 range. Markets debating $154 vs $200 tail scenarios.

**Phase 4 (May): Ceasefire optionality priced; duration risk elevated.** The market began pricing a ceasefire probability without certainty. The forward curve — with oil for later 2026 delivery near $80 — implies resolution and normalization. Goldman revised its Q4 Brent forecast to $90 (up ~$30 from pre-conflict projections). The spot/forward spread reflects genuine uncertainty about timing rather than a settled view.

## Structural Changes That Outlast the Conflict

**UAE OPEC exit** after 60 years removes the cartel's primary spare capacity buffer permanently. Abu Dhabi is choosing to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand. ADNOC simultaneously announced $55 billion in accelerated project awards and plans to double Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027 — treating prolonged disruption as a planning assumption. OPEC's ability to defend a price floor through coordinated production restraint is materially diminished going forward.

**EV adoption acceleration** is the most significant demand-side structural consequence. European EV purchases +44% YoY in March, South Korean EV sales more than doubled, Chinese EV/hybrid exports +140%. The conflict made the EV TCO argument at the consumer level decisively — oil prices above $100/bbl validate electrification across every geography. Adoption trajectories, once accelerated by a price shock, rarely revert to the pre-shock baseline.

**US shale at record** (13.6 Mbpd production, 6 Mbpd exports — both records) serves as a structural cap on the oil price through the medium-term horizon. The Permian Basin regulatory uncertainty following the hard-right Texas Railroad Commission upset is a monitoring risk, but the basin's breakeven economics at $50–55/bbl ensure continued production at any plausible forward curve.

**Petrodollar recycling contraction**: Gulf SWFs turning inward (Saudi PIF cutting foreign asset allocation from ~50% to 18%), war-related fiscal costs (~1.5% of Saudi GDP in the first month), damaged Gulf infrastructure, Qatar's Ras Laffan losses. The multi-trillion-dollar investment pledges made to the Trump administration in 2025 look progressively less deliverable. This is a slow-moving but directionally important structural shift for global asset prices: incrementally smaller Gulf bid for Treasuries and US equities at a moment when Treasury supply is already elevated.

**Iran's nuclear status and China's role**: If the diplomatic resolution includes Iran's highly enriched uranium being transferred to China (as reported), Beijing's direct stake in Persian Gulf security architecture deepens materially. China gains leverage over Middle East energy flows that markets have not fully priced.

## Outlook and Risk Scenarios

**Base case — Ceasefire and gradual reopening (~50–55% probability)**

A genuine ceasefire allows gradual Hormuz reopening. Even a partial reopening shifts the insurance market calculus and allows commercial shipping to resume. Brent retraces toward $80–90 by Q3 2026, consistent with Goldman's Q4 forecast. US supply records, UAE maximization, and some recovery of Saudi capacity (halved in May) provide the supply-side rebalancing mechanism. Demand side: the 8.7 Mbpd inventory draw cannot persist — either demand destruction deepens (already partially visible in teapot utilization rates and Indian/SE Asian demand softness) or supply restores.

**Bull oil scenario — Duration risk materializes (~25–30% probability)**

Hormuz remains effectively closed into August or beyond (Rapidan Energy's scenario → $130 with 2008-scale macroeconomic consequences; Macquarie tail → $200). Requirements: collapse of the tentative ceasefire framework, or Iran's domestic politics preventing substantive concessions. The gap between the parties' stated positions remains wide — the US claims Iran agreed to surrender enriched uranium, Iran denies this. Republican hawks are pressuring against concessions; Iran's Supreme Leader's public statements are maximalist. At $130+, global demand destruction accelerates materially, recession probabilities move sharply higher, and central banks face an impossible trade-off. The $200 scenario requires Iranian provocations that draw additional US military escalation — the probability is non-trivial but modest.

**Bear oil scenario — Rapid resolution and supply surge (~20–25% probability)**

A comprehensive nuclear deal reopens Hormuz, insurance resumes, commercial shipping normalizes within 4–6 weeks. Iranian supply partially normalizes (Iran has been the only major exporter using the strait freely during the closure). UAE maximization + US record output + some OPEC+ additions create a supply glut. Brent potentially falls toward $65–70. Rapid disinflationary consequences for global CPI, Fed hike probability collapses, growth outlook improves, dollar weakens, industrial metals rally. This is the scenario the oil forward curve most closely resembles, though the timeline is less certain than futures pricing implies.

## Key Variables to Monitor

1. **Polymarket and prediction market odds** on Hormuz reopening timeline — the most real-time probabilistic read on the diplomatic process.
2. **Insurance market war-risk coverage** — the actual operational gating mechanism for Hormuz transit, more important than diplomatic language.
3. **US Central Command strike authorization** — whether planned strikes proceed or continue to be deferred under Gulf ally pressure.
4. **Iran's Foreign Ministry vs Supreme Leader signals** — persistent divergence between negotiating posture and public maximalism.
5. **US domestic shale production and export data** — the structural price cap mechanism; any Permian regulatory disruption would shift the supply balance.
6. **Global inventory data** — if the 8.7 Mbpd draw rate persists into June, demand destruction is the rebalancing mechanism and will eventually show in PMI and activity data, creating a self-correcting feedback.
7. **Chinese teapot refiner utilization and crude import volumes** — the demand-destruction signal for the largest marginal buyer.
8. **Ceasefire ratification by Trump** — formal approval (not yet given as of end-May) is the next operational gate.
