Even the best laid plans can go awry. It was supposed to be the deal that 
vaulted Mirae Asset Securities into the global big leagues. But SpaceX’s 
record-breaking initial public offering has instead forced South Korea’s 
biggest brokerage to apologize to its clients and have its practices inspected 
by local regulators.

The episode, which left Mirae as the only one of 23 underwriters to receive no 
stock allocation in SpaceX’s IPO, underscores how even themost mundane 
communication snafus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-30/spacex-ipo-left-korea-broker-with-no-shares-on-misunderstanding?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260630&utm_content=6061>
 can have big consequences for financiers working on multibillion-dollar deals.

At the heart of the issue was a misunderstanding over how orders were supposed 
to be submitted for SpaceX’s offering, people familiar with the situation told 
Bloomberg News.

The miscommunication has emerged as one of the few blemishes on an otherwise 
successful deal. The $86 billion offering, the biggest in history, has won 
praise from investors for proceeding smoothly despite its complexity and 
compressed timeline. Ironically, Mirae was an early investor in Elon Musk’s 
rocket, satellite communications and artificial intelligence company, giving it 
an inside track to become one of only a handful of foreign underwriters on the 
IPO.
