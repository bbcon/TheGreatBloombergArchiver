UK gilt markets remain the most acute stress point among developed-economy sovereign bond markets. Ten-year gilt yields briefly breached 5% earlier this week before partially retracing following Trump's temporary delay of strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure. Yields have now returned to levels last seen before the Global Financial Crisis, exceeding even the extremes of the Liz Truss episode in 2022 in duration if not in the immediate velocity of the move. The driver this cycle is less directly fiscal and more about the Bank of England's constrained policy options: markets have pivoted from pricing at least two 25 basis-point cuts by year-end to pricing at least two hikes, as inflation expectations — already elevated relative to European peers — have surged further in response to the oil shock. UK inflation break-even spreads over US equivalents, which had narrowed as the Labour government stabilized its market credibility, have reversed sharply.

Politically, Prime Minister Starmer's position is deteriorating. Prediction markets currently assign a 68% probability that he exits office before year-end, with his most likely successors positioned to his left — a configuration unlikely to reassure gilt investors. The party retains a substantial parliamentary majority, precluding any near-term electoral test, but the political and market pressures are feeding on each other.

The ECB, by contrast, is moving more deliberately. President Lagarde used the ECB Watchers Conference in Frankfurt to signal that the bank will not act until it has "sufficient information on the size and persistence" of the energy shock, while also making clear it will not be "paralyzed by hesitation" if a broader inflationary spiral materializes. The contrast with the Bank of England's position is meaningful: the ECB retains more credibility cushion and faces less acute sovereign spread pressure, though vigilance is explicitly warranted. Germany's business outlook deteriorated as higher energy prices threaten what had been a nascent industrial recovery.
