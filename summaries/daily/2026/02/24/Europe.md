European markets absorbed a modest decline, with the Stoxx 600 off 0.12% as the AI-driven selloff that battered US software and payments stocks rippled through European equities. Teleperformance, among the earliest European casualties of AI disruption fears, saw short sellers renewing bets against the stock, which has shed roughly €20 billion in market value since January 2022. Goldman Sachs offered a partial counternarrative, promoting a basket of capital-intensive companies — utilities, miners, some industrials, and luxury goods — under the label HALO (Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence), arguing these sectors are structurally insulated from AI displacement. The basket has outperformed capital-light peers by 35% since early 2025.

On trade, an EU assessment confirmed that Trump's new 10% global tariff, layered on top of existing levies, will push the cumulative rate on certain EU exports — including cheese and agricultural products — above the 15% ceiling permitted under the EU-US trade agreement. The European Commission, which presented the assessment to lawmakers, faces pressure to respond without triggering further escalation. Separately, the US warned the EU against advancing "Made in Europe" local-content rules, with Brussels caught between the need to rebuild industrial competitiveness following Chinese pressure and Trumpian tariffs on one side, and the risk of retaliatory friction with Washington on the other.

In the UK, the political environment around Prime Minister Keir Starmer grew more complicated as two public figures — Andrew Mountbatten-Windsor and former US ambassador Peter Mandelson — were arrested on alleged misconduct charges. With Starmer registering as the most unpopular UK prime minister in modern times by some polling measures and local elections approaching, the episode adds unwanted turbulence. On a brighter note, retail crime — which surged in the post-pandemic period — appears to be easing, with the British Retail Consortium reporting a fifth reduction in daily incidents through August 2025, reflecting over £5 billion in security investment over five years.
