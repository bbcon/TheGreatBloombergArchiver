‘Board of Trade’?

Asked by Bloomberg in September about a label for his by-then-regular 
negotiating sessions with China, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent saidthere was 
no name 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-09-19/bessent-steers-clear-of-a-name-for-now-regular-us-china-talks?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
. He just called them “trade talks.”

That contrasted with the “Strategic and Economic Dialogue” structure of Obama 
administration vintage, and the slightly simpler “Strategic Economic Dialogue <>
” of President George W. Bush’s time.

But on Monday — just as US President Donald Trump requested 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-s-china-delay-echoes-pattern-of-world-stage-leverage-plays?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 adelay 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/trump-floats-delaying-xi-summit-if-no-help-for-hormuz-ft-says?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 to hisupcoming summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-s-delay-of-xi-summit-buys-china-time-to-game-out-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 with Xi Jinping — a possible name suggestion emerged from the latest round of 
discussions between Bessent and US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer and the 
Chinese delegation led by Vice Premier He Lifeng.

“We talked about potentially having even a mechanism with the US and China, 
almost like what we might call aUS-China Board of Trade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/us-china-mull-board-of-trade-to-manage-bilateral-economy-ties?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
,” Greer told reporters after the two-day meeting in Paris. Li Chenggang, 
China’s vice minister for commerce, earlier flagged the potential to establish 
a “cooperation mechanism” for bilateraltrade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/iran-war-will-be-eclipsed-by-trump-s-tariffs-come-midterms?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 and investment.
Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent and Trade Representative Jamieson Greer.
Photographer: Ludovic Marin/AFP/Getty Images
Greer said that, from the US perspective, a board would serve as a way to 
formalize the kinds of things the US should be importing from and exporting to 
China.

This harkens back to a long tradition of the US coming up with mechanisms to 
put some specific parameters around problematic bilateral commercial ties — not 
least thePhase One trade deal 
<https://www.piie.com/research/piie-charts/us-china-phase-one-tracker-chinas-purchases-us-goods>
 Trump reached with China in his first administration, which set targets for 
Chinese purchases of agricultural and manufactured goods.

Ronald Reagan’s administration had the MOSS, for Market Oriented Sector 
Selective <https://www.usitc.gov/publications/332/pub2120.pdf>, framework with 
Japan, which brought senior American and Japanese officials together with 
technical specialists to address tensions in five main areas, including 
telecoms, pharmaceuticals and auto parts.

What could the US-China board address? Potentially “opportunities for 
expanding ‘managed’ trade innon-sensitive products 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/china-s-party-paper-urges-apple-to-fix-monopolistic-policies?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
,” Wendy Cutler, a former US trade official now at the Asia Society Policy 
Institute, wrote in a brief initial take Monday. There could perhaps even be 
discussions on mutual tariff reduction in non-strategic products, she said.

Need-to-Know Research

Grade inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/harvard-proposes-cap-on-a-s-amid-worries-over-grade-inflation?cmpid=BBD031726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 in American education may be leading to lifetime earningsdeflation, according 
to a new National Bureau of Economic Researchpaper 
<http://www.nber.org/papers/w34952> that examined evidence from the high school 
level — where average grades have increased by nearly half a letter grade over 
the past 40 years.

“A teacher who inflates average grades has a negative effect on students’ 
future performance as measured by test scores, high school graduation, college 
enrollment and earnings,” Jeffrey Denning, Rachel Nesbit, Nolan Pope and 
Merrill Warnick wrote. “A teacher with one standard deviation higher mean grade 
inflation reduces the present discounted value of lifetime earnings of their 
students by $213,872 per year of teaching.”
Source: Jeffrey T. Denning, Rachel L. Nesbit, Nolan G. Pope and Merrill 
Warnick, and data from the National Center for Education Statistics
At the same time, the authors also found evidence suggesting that teachers who 
inflate grades for the purpose of passing a student “may have a small positive 
effect on students’ early labor market outcomes, though those results are 
sensitive to the definition of employment and only appear in the first few 
years post high school.” They also help improve graduation rates.
