US equity futures edged higher on Monday, with S&P 500 contracts up 0.6%, as investors weighed surging energy costs against the prospect that the Federal Reserve and other major central banks will hold rates steady this week. The Fed meets Wednesday in what may be its most consequential policy gathering since the post-pandemic inflation cycle, convening for the first time since US and Israeli strikes on Iran began roughly three weeks ago.

Market expectations have shifted materially. Bets on Fed easing in 2026 have eroded substantially, and while investors still lean marginally toward cuts later in the year — making the US the relative dovish outlier among G7 peers — the near-term path has narrowed. As Bloomberg Economics' Tom Orlik summarized the likely message from policymakers: "Stay on hold, signal vigilance, and hope the Iran war ends before it lands them with another inflation problem they're ill equipped to solve." Goldman Sachs economists offered a somewhat reassuring counterpoint, arguing that the inflation risk remains "mostly confined to energy" and that the conditions for a 2021-22 style broad supply-chain shock are absent.

The political economy of the conflict is becoming increasingly uncomfortable for the administration. US gasoline prices averaged $3.70 per gallon on Sunday, up 26% from a month ago — a level that historical precedent suggests carries meaningful electoral consequences. The administration faces the compounding challenge of simultaneous tariff pressure and energy inflation, with Supreme Court-directed tariff refund processes still unresolved and new tariff investigations ongoing. A parallel domestic story: the Trump administration directed Sable Offshore to restore oil production off California's coast as part of efforts to ease supply tightness. In corporate news, Meta shares rose roughly 3% in premarket trading after reports of a $27 billion cloud infrastructure deal with Nebius and a planned workforce reduction of over 20%, while Nvidia gained ahead of its four-day AI conference.
