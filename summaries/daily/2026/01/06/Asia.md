**Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — January 6, 2026**

Coverage in today's source material is notably thin for the Asia-Pacific region. The single available item is Bloomberg's annual compilation of 2026 Wall Street outlooks, which offers limited but relevant read-throughs for Asian markets and capital flows.

The consensus across more than 60 institutions — including Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan Wealth Management, and the BlackRock Investment Institute — points to modest optimism for global risk assets, supported by continued AI-related capital expenditure and accommodative government policy. For Asia, this framework carries several implications worth flagging, though none are addressed directly in the source material and should be treated as inference rather than reported view.

First, the widely held call for further dollar depreciation, if realized, would be supportive for Asian FX and could ease financial conditions across the region, particularly for economies with dollar-denominated liabilities and import-sensitive inflation profiles such as India, Indonesia, and the Philippines. It would also reduce pressure on the PBOC to defend the yuan's reference rate and may facilitate portfolio inflows into Chinese assets, though structural concerns around China's growth model and property sector remain unresolved.

Second, the view that inflation will prove stickier than markets expect — and that central banks may have less room to ease than priced — is relevant for the BOJ's normalization trajectory, the RBA's policy path, and emerging Asian central banks that have generally been waiting on the Fed. A shallower Fed cutting cycle would constrain regional easing.

Third, the persistence of the AI capex theme remains directly consequential for North Asian technology supply chains, particularly Taiwan, South Korea, and Japan, where semiconductor and equipment names have been the principal beneficiaries.

Bloomberg also flags 2026 as likely another difficult year for global trade — a material concern for export-dependent Asian economies, though specifics are not provided in today's material. We will revisit these themes as more granular regional reporting becomes available.
