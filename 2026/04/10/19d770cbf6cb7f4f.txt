Rate Path Veers Higher

Trump’s second big shock to the world economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/imf-chief-says-oil-shock-tests-a-world-with-little-fiscal-buffer?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 since he returned to the White House looks increasingly likely to force 
central banks into a new round of tightening.

The US president’s war on Iran has already produced an initial bout of 
inflation, with further energy-related pressure in the pipeline, putting global 
policymakers on alert for possible interest-rate hikes. That contrasts with 
this time last year, when hisso-called Liberation Day tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/trump-tariffs-tracker/?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 left officials bracing for asynchronized hit to growth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-20/trump-tariffs-central-banks-stare-into-trade-war-abyss-with-rate-cuts-primed?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
.

A higher trajectory for near-term monetary policy is clear from the aggregate 
measure of advanced-economy interest rates compiled by Bloomberg Economics. The 
gauge shows rates about 35 basis points higher at the end of this year compared 
with the reading from three months ago.

Policymakers around the world are increasingly realizing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-10/the-new-world-order-will-be-more-dangerous-and-more-cooperative?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 that damage to energy production capacity in the Middle East is extensive 
enough to make a lingering supply shock probable. That’s consequential for 
inflation, and monetary policy.

The impact is still set to be asymmetrical, with central banks such as the 
European Central Bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/sleijpen-says-ecb-will-act-if-needed-to-keep-inflation-at-target?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 and theBank of Canada 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/bank-of-canada-agreed-iran-war-shouldn-t-overtake-other-risks?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 seen hiking, while others — such as theBank of England 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/dynamic-prices-may-lift-uk-inflation-fears-bank-of-england-says?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 — now in the hold column instead of cutting.

BE still sees the US Federal Reserve 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/fed-minutes-show-officials-see-dual-sided-risks-from-iran-war?cmpid=BBD041026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260410>
 lowering borrowing costs, though January’s call for four cuts is now down to 
two.

As usual, predicting the path for rates in the era of Trump is harder than it 
once might have been. And the binary nature of the Middle East war, leading to 
different outcomes depending on how long hostilities last, makes it even more 
difficult.

“Sharply higher oil prices, head-spinning uncertainty, and — for the Fed — 
changing leadership and political pressure for low rates, add up to a 
challenging environment for central banks,” BE Global Chief EconomistTom Orlik  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/APScahppEq4/tom-orlik>said. “We think the 
war keeps most in wait-and-see mode in the second quarter. Right now, it’s hard 
to look much further ahead.”

Need-to-Know Research

It used to be that Americans worked notably longer hours than people in most 
other developed nations, but that gap has narrowed considerably since the turn 
of the century, according to a National Bureau of Economic Researchpaper 
<http://www.nber.org/papers/w35020>.

The main factor has been an expansion in US healthcare benefits outside of 
work — and especially via Medicaid — economists Serdar Birinci, Loukas 
Karabarbounis and Kurt See wrote this month.

President Barack Obama’s signature health legislation contributed to enlarging 
Medicaid, which is now received by some 30% of the population, versus roughly 
10% 40 years ago. Many other rich nations have long had comprehensive public 
health systems.
Source: Birinci, Karabarbounis and See. Note: CPS and LIS are separate 
databases. ‘Adjusted’ figures reflect changes for occupational composition.
The trios findings included the observation that “groups with larger changes 
in health benefits experience larger declines in hours than groups with smaller 
changes in health benefits.”
