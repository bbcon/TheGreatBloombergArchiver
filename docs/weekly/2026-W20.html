Weekly Brief

May 11 – May 17, 2026

US

Equity Markets and the AI Rally

US equity markets entered the week at all-time highs, with the S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 propelled by a semiconductor earnings cycle that analysts characterized as qualitatively different from prior beat-and-raise episodes. The SOX index has outperformed the Magnificent Seven since the ChatGPT launch, and brokers projected next year's semiconductor earnings approaching double last year's levels. Alphabet closed in on Nvidia as the world's largest company by market cap, at $4.8 trillion versus Nvidia's $5.2 trillion, following a 34% gain in April alone. The concentration dynamic intensified: the top ten S&P 500 constituents now account for roughly 40% of index market capitalization. Corporate milestones added texture — Cisco surged 19% on Wednesday after results confirmed its AI pivot was gaining commercial traction, Cerebras Systems raised $5.55 billion in the year's largest IPO and surged 68% on debut, and Ford gained 13% after Morgan Stanley argued its CATL battery partnership could underwrite a $10 billion energy storage business. The rally began to stall on Friday, with S&P 500 futures off 1.0% and Nasdaq 100 futures down 1.5%, as the inflation and rate-hike narrative displaced AI optimism at the margin.

Inflation and the Federal Reserve

Tuesday's CPI print delivered a reading of 3.8% year-on-year, the highest in three years, driven primarily by Hormuz-related energy prices but with meaningful breadth underneath: core inflation ran at 2.8%, sticky prices re-crossed 3%, and the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply. Consumer prices rose faster than average hourly earnings in April, the first such occurrence since 2022. Wednesday's PPI data compounded the concern, rising 6% year-on-year and including a 27% surge in electronics components — the latter ending decades of technology deflation. Oxford Economics now projects the PCE deflator reaching 3.8%, effectively foreclosing near-term rate cuts. By Friday, the two-year Treasury yield reached 4.06% and the ten-year 4.54%, its highest level in approximately a year. Fed futures markets priced roughly a two-thirds probability of a rate hike by December, a repricing that gathered momentum throughout the week as Fed Governor Barr characterized inflation as "the overwhelming risk facing the economy."

Federal Reserve Leadership

Kevin Warsh cleared an initial Senate vote Monday and was confirmed as Federal Reserve chair Wednesday by the narrowest margin on record. The timing proved immediately consequential: rather than inheriting the easing cycle markets had anticipated around his appointment, Warsh takes the chair at a moment when the risk profile has shifted toward a harder stagflationary configuration. Bank of America recommended short positions in two-year Treasuries on the basis that rate hikes are underpriced.

Fiscal and Political Developments

A federal trade court declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful early in the week, and the administration subsequently asked the court to pause the ruling pending appeal while processing more than $35.5 billion in tariff refunds following a Supreme Court ruling striking down broad tariff authority. Trump's financial disclosures revealed personal equity purchases in Q1, including positions in Nvidia, Oracle, Microsoft, Boeing, and Costco. FEMA's operational capacity ahead of hurricane season attracted scrutiny after roughly 5,000 staffers departed since January, compounded by the ambiguous record of Cameron Hamilton, nominated to lead the agency. ---

Asia

Trump-Xi Summit

The week's defining regional event was the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing on Thursday and Friday, the first US presidential visit to China in nearly a decade. Xi received Trump with full state ceremony at the Great Hall of the People; both leaders struck cooperative public tones, with Trump calling Xi "a great leader" and extending a White House invitation for September. The US delegation — encompassing Elon Musk, Tim Cook, Jensen Huang, Kelly Ortberg, and David Solomon, representing collectively roughly $20 trillion in market capitalization — signalled the summit's commercial weight. Deliverables included a Chinese commitment to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft, a renewal of the trade truce from the South Korea summit under a "constructive strategic stability" framework, and the outline of a potential board of investment to allow Chinese capital into non-sensitive US industries, with possible tariff relief on roughly $30 billion of non-critical trade. Huang's last-minute inclusion thrust the $50 billion Chinese AI chip market into the agenda. Pre-summit talks between Treasury Secretary Bessent and Vice Premier He Lifeng took place in Seoul on Wednesday. On Iran, both sides expressed a shared desire to reopen the strait but produced no operational mechanism. Xi's pointed warning that mishandling Taiwan could push the relationship into a "highly dangerous situation" — published by state media while the leaders were still in the room — set a firm boundary beneath the cordiality.

China: Economy, Policy, and Markets

April's inflation data confirmed China's exit from its deflationary spiral, with PPI rising 2.8% year-on-year — the fastest pace since July 2022 — and CPI climbing to 1.2%, though the drivers are partly imported via energy costs. April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above consensus, with technology the primary driver. The yuan strengthened to a more than three-year high; Goldman Sachs estimates it remains over 20% undervalued and projects a move to 6.50 within twelve months. Currency accord speculation — a "Beijing Accord" under which yuan appreciation would be formalised — received attention ahead of the summit, though no arrangement emerged. Beneath the headline data, independent analysts estimate true non-performing loan ratios closer to 10% versus the official 1.5%, implying approximately $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt. China's ten-year bond yield fell below Japan's — a striking inversion that reflects subdued domestic demand even as export and inflation data improve. China also confirmed it has not approved purchases of Nvidia H200 chips and is actively developing domestic alternatives.

South Korea: Redistribution Politics and Market Fragility

South Korea's Kospi experienced its sharpest intraday dislocation in recent memory on Tuesday when a presidential policy chief floated a "citizen dividend" funded by AI profits via social media, shedding more than $300 billion in market capitalization within 97 minutes before partially recovering after clarification. On Friday the index fell a further 6.1%, the steepest single-session decline since March, as rate repricing globally hit semiconductor-heavy indices acutely. The underlying tension is genuine: Samsung posted a 755% profit increase in Q1 2026, its market capitalization recently crossed $1 trillion, and Goldman economists flagged that AI-driven chip booms in South Korea and Taiwan are on track to push current-account surpluses to record levels. Samsung's labor union is threatening an 18-day strike from May 21, demanding 15% of operating profit for chip-division employees.

Japan and the Rest of the Region

Japan's 20-year JGB yields touched their highest level since 1997, driven by energy import costs feeding domestic inflation; a Bank of Japan board member called for tightening "as early as possible," and Japanese household spending fell for a fourth consecutive month. Finance Minister Katayama confirmed close FX coordination with Bessent. India more than doubled gold and silver import tariffs to approximately 15% and raised retail fuel prices for the first time in four years as the rupee traded near record lows. The Philippines, Indonesia, and India are each burning foreign-exchange reserves to defend currencies under oil import pressure. Thailand convened an emergency meeting of its prime minister and business leaders to address converging inflation, unemployment, and energy crises. SoftBank reported a surprise jump in quarterly profit driven by valuation gains on its OpenAI stake.

China's Supply Chain Coercion

A report from the Rhodium Group, released Saturday, characterized Beijing's regulatory posture as having shifted from reactive adjustment to proactive economic coercion. New Chinese regulations allow retaliation against foreign governments or firms that assist actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial and supply chain security — a legal architecture explicitly designed to deter sourcing diversification. Xi's latest Five-Year Plan replaced "peace and development" framing with explicit acknowledgment of geopolitical rivalry as the primary driver of global uncertainty, elevating national security as the central organizing principle for economic policy. For industrial communities in Germany, Japan, and Italy, the Rhodium analysis suggests the adjustment pressure is structural rather than cyclical. ---

Europe

UK Political Crisis

The week proved exceptionally turbulent for UK politics. Prime Minister Keir Starmer began the period under acute pressure following Labour's sweeping local election defeats, with Deputy Prime Minister Angela Rayner publicly calling for a change in direction. By Tuesday, four junior ministers had resigned seeking to force his departure, and Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood was reported to be privately urging him to consider a transition. Starmer publicly defied calls to set a departure timetable, telling cabinet that "the country expects us to get on with governing." Health Secretary Wes Streeting was expected to resign to mount a formal leadership challenge by Thursday, and by Friday Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham had secured a credible path to a challenge after a supportive MP stood aside. Markets responded throughout the week with consistent directional pressure: sterling fell 0.5% to $1.35 on Tuesday, thirty-year gilt yields hit 5.80% — the highest since 1998 — and the ten-year gilt reached 5.14% on Friday, up 15 basis points in a single session. Schroders disclosed it was actively avoiding UK bonds, and Jamie Dimon warned that any post-Starmer government raising bank taxes would cause JPMorgan to abandon plans for a multi-billion-pound Canary Wharf headquarters.

UK Economy

Against the political noise, the UK economy delivered its strongest quarterly growth in a year, with GDP rising 0.6% in Q1 2026, with IT contributing meaningfully. The data point is unlikely to prove durable: Bloomberg Economics projects near-stagnant growth for the remainder of the year as energy costs, tighter borrowing conditions, and political uncertainty compound one another. British employers continued shifting toward temporary hiring in April, with permanent-role placement falling at its fastest pace in three months, extending a downturn that began in 2022. The UK also halved its pledge to the Green Climate Fund, reducing its 2024–2027 contribution from £1.6 billion to £815 million. An analysis of London commercial property found that as much as 78% of Westminster offices and 71% of City offices will fail to meet Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards expected in the early 2030s, raising stranded-asset risk across the sector.

Germany and the Eurozone

German industrial production unexpectedly fell in March for a second consecutive month, reinforcing concerns about the eurozone's largest economy under rising energy costs. Chancellor Friedrich Merz's coalition held a six-hour working session during the week that produced few concrete agreements; public support has declined sharply since he took office and the AfD has extended its polling lead. ECB policy shifted in tone: Bundesbank President Nagel told Handelsblatt that rate hikes are increasingly likely — a significant pivot from earlier-year expectations for cuts — while Governing Council member Kazaks warned of stagflation risks and described a June hike as "less obvious" rather than imminent. France reported unemployment rising unexpectedly to its highest level in five years, adding to evidence that the eurozone's second-largest economy was losing momentum before the Iran conflict began. A Bloomberg survey of economists showed the ECB expected to hike twice in 2026.

European Political Landscape

Broader European political fragility was visible across the continent. Romania's government fell, Latvia's prime minister resigned over a drone-related controversy, and Germany's chancellor faced trade union protests. Hungary's borrowing costs fell below Poland's for the first time since 2020, reflecting investor optimism around new Prime Minister Peter Magyar's EU alignment agenda. The EU reached unanimous political agreement to sanction violent settlers in the West Bank. EU-China tensions mounted as Beijing warned Brussels that countermeasures would follow if its probe into Nuctech, the Chinese security-devices manufacturer, proceeds — a pairing with China's broader supply chain coercion framework that places Europe's industrial diversification agenda under direct diplomatic pressure. ---

Global

The Iran Conflict and Strait of Hormuz

The Hormuz closure, entering its tenth to eleventh week, remained the dominant driver of cross-asset volatility throughout the period. Brent crude rose above $107 on Tuesday after Trump rejected Iran's latest peace proposal as "a piece of garbage" — characterising the ceasefire as on "massive life support" — before WTI held near $101 by mid-week and Brent approached $109 by Friday, representing a cumulative rise of more than 60% over three months. BloombergNEF estimates disruption across 27 Middle Eastern oil fields has reached 9 million barrels per day. A commercial vessel was apparently seized near the UAE on Thursday, injecting fresh uncertainty over effective control of the waterway. Qatar directed vessels near its Ras Laffan LNG facility to disable AIS transponders from Monday, with at least nine tankers going dark. A Japanese supertanker and two India-bound LNG cargoes appear to have made covert transits, illustrating that flows are occurring but under conditions of elevated rather than normalised risk. Iran is sustaining partial Hormuz control through asymmetric tactics including midget submarine deployments, despite significant US naval superiority. Trump's comment to Fox News that the US does not need the strait "open at all" complicated diplomatic signalling. The Trump-Xi summit produced shared rhetorical commitment to reopening the strait but no operational mechanism. The UAE announced plans to double Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027, a structural adaptation to prolonged disruption.

Oil Market Dynamics and Price Risk

The buffers that contained prices through the conflict's initial weeks are visibly eroding. US crude and product exports are running above 14 million barrels per day with emergency stockpile drawdowns at record rates — a pace Morgan Stanley and Mercuria both characterise as unsustainable beyond late June or July. Chinese state refiners are drawing on commercial inventories rather than importing, suppressing seaborne demand at a rate that likewise cannot persist. The IEA reported global inventories falling at a record pace and projected an outright contraction of 420,000 barrels per day in 2026 oil demand — a rare and historically stress-associated outcome — against a pre-war forecast of 1.2 million barrels per day growth. TD Securities warned of a potential surge toward $150 per barrel absent a Hormuz resolution; BloombergNEF placed the upside at $160 if closure persists through July. US jet fuel exports surged to a record 455,000 barrels per day as European and Asian airlines scrambled for supply. Iran is also boosting rail freight to China via Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan, with container rates along the route climbing 40% above typical levels.

Global Inflation and Bond Markets

Producer price data from the US, China, and Japan all surprised to the upside this week, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. US PPI accelerated to 6% year-on-year, led by freight costs and energy; electronics components inflated 27% as AI-driven memory chip shortages ended decades of technology deflation. Sovereign bond markets are pricing a coherent inflation narrative across jurisdictions: US ten-year yields reached 4.54% on Friday, UK thirty-year gilts hit 5.80%, and Japan's twenty-year rate touched its highest level since 1996. The correlation of losses across US, UK, eurozone, and Japanese government bond indices this year reflects a common energy-price driver layered over country-specific fiscal and political factors. Investors captured 5% yields on thirty-year Treasuries for the first time in nearly two decades. Fed rate hike expectations for December moved from tail risk toward base-case probability for a meaningful share of market participants — a repricing with implications for equity valuations, credit spreads, and emerging market capital flows that have not yet fully resolved.

China's Clean Technology Opportunity and Supply Chain Coercion

China's green technology firms are explicitly leveraging the energy shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third. Chinese manufacturers are targeting Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia as primary growth markets. Simultaneously, Beijing's new regulatory framework for supply chain coercion — characterised by Rhodium Group as a shift from reactive to proactive strategy — creates a direct tension with Western diversification efforts. The prospect that any US-China investment accommodation reached at the summit could prioritise American companies at European expense drew concern in Brussels.

Infrastructure, Water, and Resource Stress

The conflict is accelerating awareness of concentrated infrastructure vulnerability beyond energy. Centralized desalination plants supplying tens of millions across the Middle East face exposure analogous to centralized energy nodes, driving commercial interest in decentralized atmospheric water harvesting technology. In the American West, record-low Rocky Mountain snowpack is prompting renewed investment in water storage, with the Chimney Hollow Dam — the largest built in the US in twenty-five years — beginning to fill in April, even as 500,000 Corpus Christi residents face potential shortfalls within a year. Data centers are simultaneously placing significant new demand on already-stressed aquifers, creating a tension between AI infrastructure requirements and physical resource constraints that has no near-term resolution.
