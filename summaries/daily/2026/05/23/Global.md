**Global Daily Briefing — May 23, 2026**

**Gulf capital flows reverse as Hormuz disruption enters third month.** Nearly three months into the US-Iran conflict, the largely shuttered Strait of Hormuz is reshaping not only energy markets but the global capital cycle that has long depended on recycled petrodollars. Bloomberg Economics estimates Saudi Arabia's first month of fighting alone cost roughly $17 billion, or 1.5% of GDP, with defense, logistics and economic-support outlays climbing across the six GCC states. Qatar reports that one damaged gas facility could take up to five years to restore, with associated losses potentially reaching $20 billion. The UAE is accelerating construction of pipelines that bypass Hormuz. The combination of compressed export revenues and elevated domestic spending implies a meaningful contraction in the pool of Gulf capital available for deployment abroad — a flow that, over decades, helped absorb US Treasury supply and suppress global borrowing costs. The magnitude and persistence of that effect on US rates remains to be seen, but the directional implication is clear.

**Pledges to Washington increasingly in question.** The trillions in investment commitments made to the Trump administration last year look progressively less deliverable. Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund had already cut its targeted share of foreign assets to 18% from an earlier ambition near 50%, and the kingdom shifted from net lender to net borrower in 2024. The war is accelerating an inward turn: LIV Golf is preparing for possible bankruptcy after PIF withdrew funding, a $200 million Met Opera deal has been abandoned, and Riyadh is now focused on the $35 billion Aramco privatization to court capital domestically rather than export it. Recipients of Gulf largesse — Egypt, Pakistan, Jordan, and prospective reconstruction in Gaza, Lebanon and Syria — face a tighter funding environment.

**Climate policy bifurcation.** With the Trump administration unwinding federal climate action, sub-national actors are filling gaps: Berkeley's new ordinance mandating emissions-reducing upgrades at point of sale is the first US measure of its kind, joining disclosure regimes in Austin, Minneapolis and Portland.
