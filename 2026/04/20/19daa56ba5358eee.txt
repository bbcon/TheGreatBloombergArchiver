Chancellor Friedrich Merz hosts Brazilian President Luiz Inácio Lula da Silva 
in Hanover today, but the real action is to the north on Germany’s Baltic Sea 
coast.

For weeks now, alongside news of war and economic shock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/merz-sets-crisis-talks-to-tackle-energy-price-impact-on-germany?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
, Germans have been gripped by the fate of a stranded whale.

The adult male, a 12-meter (40-foot) humpback, was first spotted in early 
March after straying off course into the relative shallows of the Baltic and 
getting stuck on a sandbank. It has repeatedly refloated only to get into 
trouble again, becoming ever weaker.
A humpback whale in the more natural habitat of Monterey Bay, California.
Photographer: Eva Hambach/AFP/Getty Images
It’s a tempting allegory for the political fate of Merz, the conservative 
leader who justcan’t seem to catch a break 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/germany-unveils-cost-cutting-overhaul-as-healthcare-costs-soar?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
.

After less than a year in office, his ratings are abysmal: 80% of respondents 
in a recent poll said they disapproved of his performance.

The reasons are not hard to fathom.

His pledge to re-energize the economy has yet to yield results, and measures 
announced last week to soften the blow of surging oil prices were denounced as
too timid or unworkable 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/germany-agrees-on-measures-to-cushion-jump-in-energy-prices?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
.

They included a 1,000 euro ($1,180) giveaway to workers to be voluntarily paid 
by employers. Even the federal government said it wouldn’t participate.

Merz’s coalition is reduced to infighting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/merz-drafted-lobbyist-for-german-coalition-now-it-s-costing-him?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 reminiscent of the administration it replaced, helping the far-right 
Alternative for Germany (AfD) lead in polls.

Underlining the sense of drift, voters see Berlin as having little influence 
over the Iran war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/german-growth-forecast-cut-in-half-on-iran-war-advisers-say?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
.

Merz, like other leaders, can do little about US President Donald Trump’s 
global mayhem. It’s a point in common with leftist Lula.

In the Baltic, a rescue operation is under way to take the whale back out to 
the North Sea.

But the chances of success remain slim.

For Merz, state elections in the former Communist east in September threaten 
to bring the AfD to regional power for the first time.

Time is running short to get his floundering chancellorship back on track. — 
Alan Crawford <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AOTqYzZDKuY/alan-crawford>

Chart of the Day

China’s exports of clean technology climbed in March, reinforcing signs that 
manufacturers are benefiting from rising global demand for alternative energy 
sources. The data gives the first comprehensive picture since the war in Iran 
heightened the issue ofenergy security 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-18/china-clean-tech-exports-jump-amid-global-energy-disruption?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 for countries reliant on fuel imports. The most notable growth came in 
shipments of lithium-ion batteries and electric vehicles.

And Finally

Asia’s emerging economies have borne the brunt of the global energy crisis 
stemming from the war in the Gulf. Few, though, havefelt the pain more acutely 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/a-war-in-the-persian-gulf-is-plunging-pakistan-into-darkness?cmpid=BBD042026_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260420>
 than Pakistan. When Mohammad Rizwan leaves for his office in the city of 
Lahore each morning, there is no power. When he gets home, the electricity is 
still out. His kitchen gets piped gas only two hours a day, forcing the family 
to rely on expensive cylinders. The outages have, he says, “taken us back to 
the stone age.”
