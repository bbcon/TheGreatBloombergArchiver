The imminent expiry of the two-week US-Iran ceasefire is the dominant global market risk. President Trump signaled on Tuesday that he is unlikely to extend the truce beyond its Wednesday deadline, intensifying pressure on negotiators attempting to reconvene talks in Islamabad. Iran has yet to formally confirm its participation. Three vessels — two cargo ships and a fuel tanker — appeared to attempt transit of the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday while both US and Iranian blockades nominally remained in place, a high-risk manoeuvre that may test enforcement. Approximately 800 vessels remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, and the International Maritime Organization is developing an evacuation plan. DHL's CEO warned the global economy risks a "tipping point" if the supply shortfall is not resolved. Xi Jinping broke his public silence on the standoff, calling Saudi Crown Prince Mohammed bin Salman to urge restoration of Strait transit — a signal of Beijing's acute vulnerability as a major oil importer, though short of any concrete diplomatic intervention.

Oil markets slipped on Tuesday as the prospect of resumed talks tempered immediate supply fears, with WTI trading near $88 per barrel. Analysts cautioned that prices do not yet fully reflect the scale of the supply disruption. US shale producers face an awkward dilemma: oil above $100 per barrel generates strong cash flows, but memories of the 2014 dash-for-growth bust have instilled discipline that resists White House pressure to ramp up output. Continental Resources remains the only major producer to confirm increased drilling plans. On a structural note, clean power surpassed coal's share of global electricity generation in 2025 for the first time since 1919, with renewables at 34% against coal's 33%, according to Ember — a milestone likely to sharpen policy focus on energy independence. In the long-duration energy storage space, China holds roughly 72% of cumulative installed capacity but its dominance appears less entrenched than in conventional lithium-ion, offering competitive openings for US and European developers.
