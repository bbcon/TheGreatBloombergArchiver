Asia

India's trade policy underwent a notable shift this week, with New Delhi signing a free trade agreement with New Zealand — the latest in a sequence of deals that collectively represent a meaningful departure from the protectionist posture Prime Minister Modi embraced between roughly 2017 and 2020. During that period, India's average tariff rate rose from approximately 13% to 18%, accompanied by an expansion of quality control orders that functioned as non-tariff barriers directed primarily at Chinese imports. The strategic logic at the time rested on nurturing domestic demand and building national champions through protection and production incentives. That template now appears to be under revision. The New Zealand agreement follows what negotiators have described as a landmark deal with the European Union — ending roughly two decades of intermittent talks — as well as a trade agreement with the UK concluded last July. Negotiations on an interim arrangement with the United States remain ongoing. The proximate catalyst for the pivot appears to be the pressure generated by the Trump administration's own protectionist turn, which has made securing alternative market access more urgent. But policymakers also appear to have absorbed a structural lesson: manufacturing's share of Indian GDP has declined from 16% to 13% over the past decade, and export performance has consistently correlated with India's strongest growth periods. The recalibration has practical limits worth noting. India has not moved to reduce most-favored-nation tariff rates broadly, retaining concerns about Chinese overcapacity and a widening bilateral trade deficit with Beijing. Quality control orders are being scaled back selectively rather than dismantled. Agriculture and dairy remain shielded. And structural constraints — unpredictable tax enforcement, slow land and labor reform, regulatory complexity — remain unaddressed by trade agreements alone. Whether the current liberalization momentum is durable or proves contingent on external pressure remains an open question.

Global

Two cross-cutting themes warrant attention this week. First, India's trade policy shift — detailed in the Asia section — carries global implications that extend beyond the bilateral deals themselves. In a world where the United States has retreated from its role as an open, anchor market, advanced economies in Europe, Australasia, and potentially elsewhere face renewed incentive to deepen commercial ties with large emerging markets. India's historical underperformance as an exporter means its export capacity can expand substantially without immediately displacing incumbent suppliers, a feature that makes it a relatively low-friction partner for trade liberalization. The EU deal in particular may signal a broader reorientation of global trade architecture away from the US-centric model that prevailed for much of the post-war era. Second, climate science is producing increasingly granular evidence of how global warming is altering seasonal weather patterns in ways that carry economic consequences beyond headline temperature records. Research published in Nature Communications found that more than 60% of the globe has experienced more frequent, intense, and rapid temperature reversals since 1961. Separate work projects that extreme day-to-day temperature changes could grow in frequency and intensity by approximately 20% this century. The mechanisms identified include earlier seasonal warming, a more thermally active Gulf of Mexico driving warm air northward, and a wobblier jet stream permitting greater intrusion of Arctic air into temperate latitudes. For investors, the relevance extends to agricultural commodity markets — an intense drought in the US Great Plains is currently threatening wheat harvests — as well as to insurance, retail seasonality, and infrastructure resilience. These effects are incremental rather than abrupt, but the cumulative drift in weather volatility appears to be accelerating.
