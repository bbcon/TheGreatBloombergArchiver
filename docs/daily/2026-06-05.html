Macro and Policy

The May payrolls report looms as the session's defining data point. Consensus sits at 88,000 new jobs — the softest reading expected in three months — against an unchanged 4.3% unemployment rate. Bond markets are positioned asymmetrically: a strong print would nudge yields modestly higher, but a weak result carries greater rally potential given that Fed rate hike expectations have been building since the US-Iran conflict drove oil prices sharply higher in late February. Fed Governor Logan reinforced the hawkish baseline this week, saying she is "increasingly concerned" that rates may need to rise. Treasury Secretary Bessent, meanwhile, has quietly retired the "One Big Beautiful Bill" branding in congressional testimony, pivoting to "Working Families Tax Cuts" — a messaging shift that reflects polling showing more than three-quarters of Americans view the economy negatively and 73% disapprove of the administration's handling of living costs.

Lululemon declined 12% premarket after cutting revenue guidance. The 10-year Treasury yield holds at 4.47%. China: Capital Controls and Trade Tensions

Beijing's crackdown on cross-border capital flows is deepening in scope. Regulators have threatened to confiscate "illegal gains" from unlicensed overseas brokers operating on the mainland, while tax authorities are pursuing wealthy individuals on unreported offshore investment income. Citic Securities estimates the campaign could affect up to HK$250 billion ($32 billion) of Hong Kong-domiciled assets. Private banks including UBS have postponed mainland events; non-essential travel by Hong Kong-based private bankers is being discouraged. The backdrop is striking: resident capital outflows reached an estimated record $807 billion in 2025, partly a consequence of the domestic property slump reducing onshore investment appeal. Separately, the US proposed a 12.5% tariff rate on Chinese goods under a forced-labor import investigation — a development Beijing dismissed as "political manipulation" and analysts at Bloomberg Economics say is unlikely to derail China's export engine in the near term, though further stacked duties from a parallel overcapacity probe could test Beijing's patience ahead of an expected Xi-Trump White House meeting in September.

Eurozone: Growth Revision and ECB Path

The downward revision to eurozone Q1 GDP — now showing a contraction rather than stagnation — principally reflects a sharp decline in Irish national accounts, which are prone to distortion from multinational profit-shifting activity. The revision does not appear to have materially altered the ECB's rate trajectory: policymakers appear committed to two further hikes, supported by still-elevated services inflation and wage dynamics across core economies. The tension between a contracting growth print and a tightening bias warrants monitoring; whether Irish distortions genuinely mask underlying weakness or flatter the regional picture in other quarters remains to be seen.

UK

Survey evidence points to firms planning 4% price increases over the next year, suggesting consumer price pressures remain sticky even as the broader disinflation trend continues. The labour market is adjusting visibly: large retailers have cut nearly 18,000 positions in the past 12 months, consistent with the impact of higher employer National Insurance contributions enacted earlier this year. Politically, Andy Burnham's formal entry into the Labour leadership contest introduces a degree of governing uncertainty, though markets have not yet priced a meaningful political risk premium into sterling or gilts. ---

Energy Markets and the Hormuz Conflict

Oil markets are in a holding pattern around $93/barrel, with direction contingent on diplomatic signals from US-Iran negotiations. The talks appear to have stalled materially this week, with Iran conditioning any extension of the April truce on a resolution of the Israel-Hezbollah conflict in Lebanon — a linkage that introduces significant complexity and timeline uncertainty. The economic consequences of prolonged Hormuz closure are accumulating: Pakistan's record LNG purchase illustrates the supply-chain stress cascading through energy-import-dependent economies. The Asia energy shock appears to be accelerating clean energy investment decisions, with governments treating the disruption as a structural prompt to reduce fossil fuel dependence.

Geopolitics and Monetary Divergence

The US-Iran conflict continues to function as the dominant macro variable globally, having suppressed Fed rate-cut expectations, elevated inflation readings, and weighed on consumer sentiment across developed markets. Xi Jinping's planned Pyongyang visit and the US's new forced-labor tariff proposal — targeting dozens of countries with China at 12.5% — suggest the geopolitical backdrop will remain complex into the second half of 2026. The World Cup opening on June 11 across the US, Canada, and Mexico introduces climate stress as a visible theme, with heat exposure now a quantifiable competitive variable and a marker of the broader physical-risk environment facing outdoor mass events.
