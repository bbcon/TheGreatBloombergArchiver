• South Korean exports surged 61% YoY in May on a working-day-adjusted basis, with semiconductor shipments up a record 169.4% — a sharp beat that anchored the region's AI-driven narrative for the week.
• South Korea's Kospi fell 5.5% on Friday as foreign investors sold more than $10 billion of Korean equities during the week, the steepest weekly foreign exit on record, illustrating the volatility inherent in concentration around a single demand theme.
• China's May Caixin manufacturing PMI showed broad-based deceleration in revenues and margins, missing prior momentum, with Bloomberg Economics holding its 2026 China GDP forecast at 4.5%.
• The Bank of Japan signaled a 25 basis point rate increase at its June meeting, yet the yen weakened back through 160 per dollar — a level that previously triggered intervention — underscoring persistent skepticism about Japan's rate trajectory.
• Indonesia's Jakarta Composite fell as much as 5.7% Wednesday to its lowest since May 2021 after anti-corruption prosecutors raided President Prabowo Subianto's flagship free-meals program headquarters.

**China: Deceleration and Capital Controls**
China's macro picture deteriorated through the week. The Caixin PMI miss was reinforced by China Beige Book findings of weaker revenues, declining margins, soft retail sales, and continued housing disappointment — services the sole partial exception. Consumer inflation remains subdued despite producer prices rising at their fastest pace since the pandemic, reflecting demand constraints that limit pricing power. Beijing simultaneously intensified its capital outflow crackdown: regulators threatened to confiscate gains from unlicensed overseas brokers, tax authorities pursued wealthy individuals on offshore income, and reports that some banks had suspended mainland clients' Hong Kong account openings sent HSBC and AIA shares sharply lower on Friday. Citic Securities estimates the campaign could affect up to HK$250 billion of Hong Kong-domiciled assets, against a backdrop of estimated record resident outflows of $807 billion in 2025. The yuan has strengthened to a three-year high, partly reflecting exporter repatriation, though financial institutions are trimming appreciation forecasts on apparent central bank signaling. Xi Jinping is scheduled to visit Pyongyang on June 8–9 for his first trip to North Korea since 2019.

**Semiconductor Boom and Its Limits**
South Korea and Taiwan remain the clearest beneficiaries of the AI capital expenditure cycle. Taiwan revised its full-year growth forecast upward, projecting export performance at a five-decade high; TSMC's CEO stated AI chip supply will remain constrained for years despite ongoing capacity expansion. SK Hynix announced plans to double wafer capacity. Yet Broadcom's Thursday guidance miss demonstrated how quickly sentiment can reverse in a trade this concentrated: the Kospi fell 5.5% on Friday alone, and foreign investors liquidated Korean equities at a record weekly pace. Goldman Sachs nonetheless lifted its targets for both the Kospi and Taiwan's benchmark index, and Nomura frames AI hardware as a structural fourth variable in Asia's growth model alongside labor, capital, and China integration. HSBC's Frederic Neumann cautions that the boom remains narrow and any pullback in AI hardware demand would expose underlying fragilities — a risk difficult to quantify but rising as valuations extend.

**BOJ and Currency Stress**
The BOJ's apparent readiness to raise its benchmark rate toward 1% marks a remarkable inflection for a central bank that spent decades battling deflation. Governor Ueda reiterated the tightening trajectory unambiguously. Yet the yen's persistence near 160 per dollar — a level that previously triggered Ministry of Finance intervention — suggests markets remain skeptical that rate increases alone can resolve Japan's structural imbalances. Brookings' Robin Brooks attributes the weakness to Japan's debt overhang. Overseas investors have directed $73.6 billion into Japanese equities year-to-date, even as foreign selling of Korean equities stands near $70 billion — a flow pattern that reflects Japan's market breadth and corporate governance improvements rather than superior near-term earnings momentum. India coordinated RBI and government action to support the rupee, which fell to a record low mid-week, through eased foreign access to bonds and reduced capital gains taxes on FII bond investments.
