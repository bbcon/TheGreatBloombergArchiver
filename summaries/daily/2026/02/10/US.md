# US Daily Briefing — February 10, 2026

**Markets pause near records ahead of data.** S&P 500 futures were little changed at 6,988, with the index sitting just below an all-time high as traders awaited December retail sales and remarks from Cleveland Fed President Hammack and Dallas Fed President Logan — both 2026 voters viewed as hawkish. Asian indexes set fresh records overnight, led by Japan's Nikkei (+2.3%), but US tech sentiment remains uneven: ON Semiconductor fell 4.3% pre-market on soft guidance, Goodyear dropped 8.8% on a projected 10% Q1 volume decline, and Regenxbio slid 15% after an FDA rejection. Coca-Cola, CVS, Fiserv, S&P Global and Datadog report today, with the latter two under scrutiny following last week's AI-disruption selloff in data and analytics names.

**Alphabet's century bond underscores AI capex scale.** Alphabet launched its first sterling and Swiss franc bond sales, including a rare 100-year tranche — the first century bond from a tech issuer since Motorola in 1997 — following a $20 billion US deal last week. The financing is earmarked for AI infrastructure. While pension and insurance demand for ultra-long duration is real, the structure is unusually sensitive to rate expectations; comparable Austrian century debt trades near 30 cents on the euro after peaking near 140. The issuance highlights how hyperscalers are terming out AI-related capex into the long end of the curve.

**Fed succession rhetoric escalates.** President Trump claimed his Fed chair pick Kevin Warsh could deliver 15% GDP growth — a figure detached from any plausible potential-output estimate, and one that will sharpen questions about institutional independence should Warsh be confirmed. Separately, Fed Governor Waller suggested Trump-induced crypto enthusiasm may be fading; Bitcoin fell 2.2% and ether extended declines as derivatives positioning remained defensive following October's crash.

**Memory chip squeeze bifurcates equity returns.** Sustained DRAM and NAND price increases are compressing margins at PC OEMs and Apple suppliers while lifting memory producers to records. Fidelity International's Vivian Pai expects tightness to persist through 2026, suggesting the dispersion trade has further to run.

**Trade friction.** Trump said he would open negotiations with Canada over the Gordie Howe Bridge, threatening to block its opening absent US compensation and partial ownership — a reminder that USMCA-adjacent frictions remain a recurring tail risk.
