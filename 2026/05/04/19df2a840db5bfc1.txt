Let’s imagine for a moment that you are the leader of a middle power 
contemplating your economic survival in the 21st century. On this Monday in May 
what — or whom — are you worried about most?

You are confronting two shocks from two strongmen of the global economy. The 
most immediate is anenergy one 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-03/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-may-4?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
 tied to US President Donald Trump’s war in Iran and the persisting closure of 
the Strait of Hormuz. The longer-run one gnawing at you is asurge of imports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-24/china-s-112-billion-cargo-gap-shows-record-us-tariff-evasion?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
 from China that are the result of Chinese leader Xi Jinping’s industrial 
policies.

With the leaders of the world’s two economic superpowers scheduled to meet in 
10 days, the growing dilemma facing their peers in the global middle is that 
both are making life increasingly uneasy for the rest of the world.

There will be plenty of pomp but it’s hard to imagine much changing when Trump 
flies to China. The two sides are focused on maintaining the current fragile 
stability in their relationship. The biggest deliverables to come out of the 
summit seem likely to end up being commercial deals and an extension of their 
current economic ceasefire with a framework forfurther discussions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/greer-stresses-china-trade-over-investment-before-trump-xi-meet?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
 attached. Trump and Xi seem unlikely to solve larger structural problems. 
Including the growing imbalances in the global economy that they are both 
contributing to.

US officials continue to attack China for what they portray as its predatory 
exports of EVs and other consumer goods. Behind closed doors at the World Trade 
Organization in Geneva last week, US representatives railed at Beijing’s 
industrial overcapacity and called for more to be done while Chinese officials 
fired back, according to a readout.

Tariff Wall

Trump and his supporters argue the answer to narrowing global imbalances lies 
in his tariffs. They have helped insulate the US economy from some of China’s 
export surge, the argument goes, and the rest of the world should embrace the 
same tools. And also help the US crack down on Chinese companies’ attempts to 
bypass duties by building factories overseas or simply having their products 
make an extra pit stop.

But the rest of the world isn’t buying that argument. A view for which they 
have the backing of the economic referees at the International Monetary Fund, 
which even as itrang the alarm bell 
<https://www.imf.org/en/publications/wp/issues/2026/04/04/global-imbalances-industrial-policy-and-tariffs-575229>
 on imbalances last month declared that tariffs wouldn’t solve the issue.

More importantly, perhaps, while the US-China relationship may be stabilizing, 
America’s bond with everyone else is fraying and China is noticing.

The relationship across the Atlantic is the enduring sign of that. Trump on 
Friday threatened toincrease tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/trump-says-us-to-raise-tariff-rate-on-eu-cars-trucks-to-25?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
 on cars made in the European Union. On the same day the Pentagon announced the
withdrawal <> of 5,000 US troops from Germany. Both moves were the apparent 
result of Trump’s annoyance withcritical comments 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/trump-says-germany-s-merz-doesn-t-know-what-he-s-talking-about?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
 Germany’s Friedrich Merz made to high school students about the US war in Iran.

Merz’s comments, though, were emblematic of the annoyance many leaders in 
middle powers like Germany are feeling right now. And the dilemma they face 
over which shock to take most seriously.

Caught in the Middle

If you are the leader of a middle power, the closure of the Strait of Hormuz 
has sent the price of gasoline, diesel and cooking gassoaring 
<bbg://news/stories/TEBHKWT96OSR> and you can already see inflation creeping up 
and growth slowing. Your central bank is either poised to raise interest rates 
or already has. Your national fuel stockpiles are dwindling. Your farmers are 
telling you they are planting less this season because of the price offertilizer
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/mideast-urea-output-slumps-with-lack-of-fertilizer-ships-to-load?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
, which means you are preparing for a food price shock. Airlines in your region 
are cutting flights because of thecost of jet fuel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/india-holds-domestic-jet-fuel-prices-raises-international-rates?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
, causing your tourism minister to warn that a poor holiday season is 
approaching. The CEO of your largest industrial conglomerate is complaining 
that higher energy costs are squeezing profits.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-europe-chemicals-iran-war/?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>

What’s coming out of China feels less urgent and fuzzier perhaps. You worry 
about the China Shock and the impact of all those imports, sure. But it feels 
like your problem with demographics: It is a slow-burn issue even if it has 
obvious consequences. Plus there’s a Chinese conglomerate offering to build an 
EV factory in one of your provinces and abattery plant 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/byd-plans-20-car-dealerships-in-canada-to-aid-global-expansion?cmpid=BBD050426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260504&utm_content=4048>
 to boot. Which all comes as your energy minister is drafting a plan to reduce 
your dependence on fossil fuels that is already popular with voters. You’ve 
also ridden in a Chinese EV and now understand the hype. Who doesn’t want a 
zero-gravity massage chair and high-speed internet access on a long ride?

One man’s shock is presenting you with an immediate crisis that seems likely 
to get worse before it gets better. The other man’s jolt to your economy is 
potentially existential, and yet also distant and conceivably manageable. The 
two are meeting to sort out their relationship. But no one else seems likely to 
benefit. No wonder the middle powers are starting to stir.
