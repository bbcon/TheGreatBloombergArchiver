US President Donald Trump faced a big problem 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/hormuz-stays-blocked-for-now-as-hundreds-of-ships-seek-escape?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 with the five-week closure of theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-9?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, which fed his frustration that other members of the North Atlantic Treaty 
Organization left him hanging in his time of need.

The president has assailed the other members of the 32-nation alliance, over 
and over again, for balking at calls to take action to help reopen the strait. 
And in his criticisms aimed mainly at Europe, he again raised his desire to see 
the UStake over Greenland 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-06/trump-raises-greenland-dispute-as-he-assails-nato-over-iran-war?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
.

“NATO wasn’t there when we need them, and they won’t be there if we need them 
again,” the presidentwrote in a post 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/trump-will-discuss-leaving-nato-when-he-meets-alliance-chief?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 on his Truth Social platform on Wednesday evening. “Remember Greenland, that 
big, poorly run, piece of ice!!!”

Yet there’s one often-overlooked NATO member with greater economic and trade 
exposure to the US than the others. Canada exported about $450 billion in goods 
and services to its southern neighbor last year, roughly equal to the combined 
total of the UK, Italy, France, Belgium and the Netherlands (all original 
members of the alliance, like Canada).

That trading relationship has weakened 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/canada-s-trade-deficit-widens-as-auto-trade-weakens?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 in the past year, and a potentially difficult review of theUS-Mexico-Canada 
Agreement 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-08/time-is-on-canada-s-side-in-usmca-talks-former-trade-chief-says?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 lies ahead. Some believe the Iran situation offers an opportunity for Prime 
Minister Mark Carney.

“Canada has more leverage than they actually realize they have” in trade 
discussions with the US, says Barry Appleton, an international trade lawyer and 
dual US-Canadian citizen who has given testimony to governments and agencies in 
both countries. The Canadian government isn’t really using that leverage, he 
says, and it has a chance to score points with Trump by being more supportive 
of some of his Middle East goals.

Offering Help

The Iran conflict underscores Canada’s natural resources advantage. Tehran’s 
control of the strait choked off aglobally important conduit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/strait-of-hormuz-how-iran-is-blocking-shipping-route-despite-trump-s-threats?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 for oil and gas, along with the flow of vital materials like aluminum from 
Gulf producers. Canada is rich in crude and critical minerals, a growing power 
in liquefied natural gas, and an important supplier of fertilizer and aluminum 
to the US, though the latter industry has beendisrupted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-27/the-tariff-on-aluminum-is-the-world-s-dumbest?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 by Trump’s tariffs on foreign metals.

“Canada doesn’t need oil from the strait,” Appleton says. But “Canada could 
say, ‘We would help you.’”

Since the conflict started, Carney and his government have tried to walk a 
tightrope alongside other NATO allies. The prime minister said Iran should 
never be allowed to develop a nuclear weapon anddescribed 
<https://www.pm.gc.ca/en/news/statements/2026/02/28/statement-prime-minister-carney-and-minister-anand-situation-middle-east>
 the regime as “the principal source of instability and terror throughout the 
Middle East.” But he quickly turned to calling for a de-escalation of the 
fighting. As oil prices spiked, Canada joined global efforts toease the supply 
crunch 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/canada-to-supply-24-million-barrels-of-oil-as-part-of-iea-effort?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
, but there’s a limit to how much extra crude it can put into the market.

“Canada is not participating in the United States and Israeli offensive and 
will never participate in it,” the prime minister has said.

That doesn’t mean it won’t assist in stabilizing the region, eventually. But 
the government hasn’t made any firm commitments. Carney’s foreign minister, 
Anita Anand,told 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-07/foreign-minister-anand-says-canada-is-prepared-to-help-in-the-strait?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 Bloomberg this week that the government may help with de-mining the strait or 
offer other resources.

Philippe Lagassé, an associate professor of international affairs at Carleton 
University in Ottawa, has some ideas on what that might look like. Canada has a 
history of deploying naval forces to the Middle East, hewrote 
<https://www.readtheline.ca/p/philippe-lagasse-canada-could-help> in a Substack 
post. Canada could contribute a frigate or two to the region, or bring maritime 
patrol aircraft.

None of that is possible unless the fighting is truly over, however.

‘Renewed’ Role

“Why should allies who weren’t consulted, and therefore weren’t able to 
caution the United States, bear the burden of cleaning up this mess?” Lagassé 
wrote. “Alas, the answer may be that this is what a renewed Canadian role in 
the world looks like.”

Appleton is worried about the Canadian position going into this year’s USMCA 
review. The Canadian approach to Trump’s trade war has been a mixture of 
retaliation (includingcounter-tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-17/carney-drafts-new-auto-plan-to-favor-firms-with-canadian-plants?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 on US autos) and concessions (dropping a digital tax that the White House 
didn’t like). Talks between the two countries have been limited since October, 
when Trump ended them. Then Carney irritated the president with hisDavos speech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-20/carney-stands-by-greenland-urges-allies-to-unite-against-coercion?cmpid=BBD040926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260409>
 about middle powers uniting.

Now Trump’s fury at his NATO partners threatens to further complicate the 
relationship between the US and its neighbor at a critical time.

“The issue with the war in Iran is we didn’t play the cards the right way,” 
Appleton says. “Because what the Americans want is somebody who will try to be 
supportive.”

But there’s still an opening. Beyond help in the Strait of Hormuz, Canada also 
could offer to help civilian reconstruction efforts or provide humanitarian 
funding in Iran, as a contribution to a negotiated deal, Appleton says. “Trump 
responds to people who show up with something tangible rather than statements 
of principle.”
