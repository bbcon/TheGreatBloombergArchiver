US equity markets are holding near all-time highs as investor sentiment has rotated decisively toward optimism over a potential end to the US-Iran conflict. The Nasdaq 100 has posted ten consecutive sessions of gains — its longest streak since November 2021 — rising approximately 13% over that stretch, while the S&P 500 futures were trading near 7,000. Software stocks, which had been the most punished by fears of AI-driven disruption, staged a sharp bounce: Oracle surged 18% in two sessions, and an iShares software ETF gained 6.4%, though it remains down 25% year-to-date and bottom-fishing investors are not yet declaring a durable turn.

The Bank of America global fund manager survey is notable for the degree to which sentiment has reoriented. The proportion of respondents viewing US equities as overvalued fell to its lowest level in more than seven years. That finding warrants some skepticism — the Shiller CAPE remains elevated by historical standards and US stocks trade at a substantial premium to the rest of the world — but it does suggest that war-driven selling compressed valuations enough to bring marginal buyers back. The interaction between that valuation re-rating and a weakening dollar may prove self-limiting: if the US is perceived as cheap again, capital inflows could put a floor under the currency, but the structural drivers of dollar weakness — narrowing rate differentials and reduced foreign appetite for US assets — have not been resolved.

On the fiscal and monetary policy front, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent confirmed that tariff rates may be restored to their pre-Supreme Court levels by July, after the court struck down the use of emergency powers for their original imposition. The Atlanta Fed published survey evidence suggesting that the One Big Beautiful Bill's capex provisions have had limited uptake: only 20% of firms incorporated the legislation into their planning, echoing a similar result from the 2017 tax cuts. That finding complicates the bull case for a business investment-led expansion.

US consumer stress continues to accumulate beneath the equity market resilience. BNPL provider Klarna has lost more than 60% of its market value since its September IPO; nearly half of BNPL users report having paid at least one installment late in the past year, and usage is increasingly concentrated in essentials such as groceries — a sign of financial necessity rather than discretionary adoption. Prosecutors also made an unannounced visit to the Federal Reserve in Washington, adding to institutional uncertainty around the central bank.
