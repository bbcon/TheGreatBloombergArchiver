If there is a sick currency of Asia, it’s the yen. In the past five years it’s 
fallen 32% against the dollar, trailing only the Turkish lira and Argentine 
peso among some 30 peers, and trades near its weakest level in four decades.

The currency got a boost on Friday when Japan’s finance minister unexpectedly 
called for 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/japan-seeks-more-domestic-investment-by-gpif-pension-funds?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6412>
 the nation’s massive pension funds to increase investments in domestic assets.

“One priority is to encourage households, as well as pension funds including 
the GPIF, to increase their investment in Japanese financial assets. We intend 
to pursue policies that support that objective,” Finance Minister Satsuki 
Katayama said, referring to the Government Pension Investment Fund. It’s one of 
the world’s largest pensions with ¥293.6 trillion ($1.81 trillion) in assets.

The call 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/japan-s-long-bonds-yen-rise-on-push-for-more-gpif-investment?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6412>
 signals the government’s intention to channel more savings into domestic 
assets as the nation entersa new phase 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-07-10/japan-s-yen-fix-starts-with-its-pension-cash-coming-home?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6412>
 of economic growth accompanied by positive interest rates. Unlike the yen, 
Japanese equities have performed strongly this year, with the Nikkei 225 
recently climbing above the 70,000 mark for the first time.

Japan has considerable firepower. The nation is the largest foreign holder of 
US Treasuries with a $1.2 trillion stockpile, and almost $5 trillion of the 
country’s capital is deployed overseas.

Still, Tokyo faces an uphill struggle 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-10/yen-traders-question-how-japan-pension-money-can-sustain-rally?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6412>
. Global traders have turned themost positive 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/traders-are-most-positive-on-dollar-since-2015-as-fed-hike-looms?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6412>
 on the outlook for the dollar since 2015 on bets that borrowing costs will 
remain elevated for longer, while hedge funds are themost negative 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/hedge-funds-turn-most-negative-on-the-japanese-yen-since-2007?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6412>
 on the yen since 2007. Government intervention totaling $72.6 billion earlier 
this year failed to stop the Japanese currency from falling to its lowest level 
since 1986.

Whether this latest move can be a cure for the yen’s ills remains to be seen.
