European markets were closed Monday for Easter Monday, leaving price discovery to thin futures markets. The week ahead carries several significant data releases and policy decisions for the region. Eurozone services PMI data are due Tuesday, euro-area retail sales and PPI figures follow Wednesday, and Germany's industrial production print arrives Thursday — all of which will be read in the context of Iran-driven energy inflation that is already reshaping the political economy across the continent.

The most substantive European story of the moment is unfolding at the state level rather than in Brussels. Soaring energy prices are generating meaningful political pressure on climate regulation across both sides of the Atlantic. In Europe, the Iran war is accelerating what was already a visible tension between the energy transition agenda and near-term affordability constraints. The broader debate — whether the Iran conflict ultimately accelerates the shift to renewables by demonstrating fossil fuel vulnerability, or temporarily weakens it by making affordability the dominant political priority — remains genuinely unresolved and is being contested in real time across multiple European capitals.

On the corporate side, Unilever's €44.8 billion sale of its food business to McCormick & Co. was among the headline transactions contributing to a record global M&A first quarter. European dealmaking has benefited from the same strategic logic driving activity globally — portfolio transformation and long-term positioning — though the war-related deterioration in earnings multiples over the past month has complicated valuations and may weigh on second-quarter activity. The ECB's path remains contingent on how durably the energy shock passes through to core inflation; rate decisions in Romania this week and New Zealand and India on Wednesday offer secondary reads on how emerging market central banks are navigating the same dilemma.
