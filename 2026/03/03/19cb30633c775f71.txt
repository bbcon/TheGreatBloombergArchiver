The search for clarity over America’s war aims continues as the conflict widens
 <https://bloom.bg/3PcjBiB> and stress deepens in financial markets. 

Washington ordered its citizens <https://bloom.bg/40BvukK> as well as 
non-essential government employees to flee more than a dozen countries in the 
Middle East — including Israel, Saudi Arabia, Qatar and the UAE — after its 
embassy in Riyadh was struck by Iranian drones. Iran said it also attacked a US 
base in Bahrain.

The Israel Defense Forces said it was conducting airstrikes in Tehran, hours 
after it announced a new “wave of strikes” on the Iranian capital. The US 
military said it destroyed installations such as command and control 
facilities, air defense capabilities as well as missile and drone launch sites. 
Iran also acknowledged overnight that its Natanz nuclear facilities had been 
attacked.

US justifications <https://bloom.bg/3PcjIux> for the initial strikes have 
varied, with officials citing regime change, the threat of Iran’s nuclear 
program, Israel’s preparations for an attack and the domestic crackdown on 
protesters.

On Monday, Secretary of State Marco Rubio said the campaign was aimed 
<https://bloom.bg/40EmYBp> at destroying Iran’s ballistic missile program and 
its navy, in order to prevent threats to global shipping — and that it can be 
achieved without using ground forces, adding that “the hardest hits are yet to 
come.” Yet Israeli Prime Minister Benjamin Netanyahu said it was partly meant 
“to create the conditions for the brave Iranian people to cast off the yoke of 
tyranny.”

Trump will have the opportunity to shed more light on his administration’s 
objectives during a scheduled press conference when meeting German Chancellor 
Friedrich Merz at the White House Tuesday morning. Earlier Trumptold NewsNation 
<> the US will hit back after the US embassy in Riyadh was attacked, adding 
that “you’ll find out soon” what form the retaliation would take.

But with nearly all civilian air traffic closed across the Gulf after the 
region’s biggest airport in Dubai was damaged, energyprices surging 
<https://bloom.bg/3NdLAxS> and unease growing in global stock and bond markets, 
pressure is building for the US leader to outline clear goals for its war in 
Iran.—Emma Clark and Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



In such an environment, investors are taking an increasingly pessimistic view 
<https://bloom.bg/4rbp2Mi>. Losses in Asian stocks were much steeper than on 
Monday, with MSCI’s regional benchmark slumping about 3%. South Korea’s Kospi 
was worst hit <https://bloom.bg/40H37BC>, sinking 7.2%, as markets there 
reopened after a long weekend. Korea’s stock market has been one of the biggest 
beneficiaries of the AI boom. S&P 500 futures tumbled 1.5%.

The Bloomberg Dollar index extended gains, taking its two-day advance to 1.2%, 
while Brent crude rose almost 5% to top $80 a barrel.

Government bonds fell, with the yield on the UK’s 10-year gilt rising 12 basis 
points and the 10-year Treasury rate climbing six basis points to 4.10%. 
Concern a prolonged conflict will keep oil prices elevated, supercharge 
inflation and limit room by central banks to lower interest rates is 
undercutting the appeal <https://bloom.bg/4b9Cn1M> of fixed-income assets — 
which are typically a haven in times of trouble.

Oaktree Capital Management’s co-founder Howard Marks  warned investors 
<https://bloom.bg/40EhoPs> to avoid falling victim to emotional reactions to 
the war.

“The main thing to keep in mind is how much we don’t know,” he told the 
Australian Financial Review Business Summit in Sydney via a video call. “Since 
we don’t know what it means, there is probably nothing smart to do.”
Be mindful: Howard Marks, co-founder of Oaktree Capital. Photographer: 
Christopher Pike/Bloomberg 



China called on all sides of the Iran war to ensure the safe passage of ships 
through the Strait of Hormuz, themost direct call <https://bloom.bg/3Ne6qNB> to 
date from the Islamic Republic’s top economic partner for continued trade 
through the waterway.

Tanker traffic through the energy chokepoint effectively halted  
<https://bloom.bg/4b6BuqJ>after the US and Israel began a bombing campaign over 
the weekend that has prompted Iran to respond with strikes across the region. 
As the world’s largest oil and gas importer, China is among the most exposed 
nations — while it has ample reserves, almost half of its crude imports 
transited the strait in December.




European natural gas prices surged more than 20% amid uncertainty over how long
 <https://bloom.bg/407wJrG> exports will be halted from the world’s largest LNG 
export plant in Qatar and the impact it will have on global energy supplies.

Benchmark futures surged for a second day as countries in Asia scramble to 
secure <bbg://news/stories/TB9N1TKJH6V4> alternatives after QatarEnergy’s 
facility was shut when it was targeted in an Iranian drone attack. Several 
buyers are requesting LNG shipments to be delivered early to try to meet demand 
now, hoping that the situation eases before long.




Sam Altman is questioning his firm’s rush to forge a deal with the US Defense 
Department — following a clash between the Pentagon and rival Anthropic. The 
OpenAI CEO said the company’s approach looked “opportunistic and sloppy 
<https://bloom.bg/4cHUpKX>” in a post on the X social media service.

Altman said his company was working with the department to “make some 
additions in our agreement to make our principles very clear.” That includes 
ensuring that AI isn’t used for domestic surveillance of Americans and that 
intelligence agencies like the National Security Agency can’t rely on OpenAI 
services.

His comments come after the Defense Department showdown with Anthropic 
<https://bloom.bg/40broQa>, which had demanded its technology not be used for 
mass surveillance of Americans or autonomous weapons deployment. Anthropic’s 
main app hassince topped <https://bloom.bg/3N0hg9N> Apple’s download charts, 
reflecting a surge of support for the company during its feud with the Pentagon.
Critical of firm: Sam Altman, CEO of OpenAI. Photographer: Kyle 
Grillot/Bloomberg 



The drumbeat of negative news about private credit is not stopping. In the 
latestsign of unease <https://bloom.bg/4r0t8Xj> in an industry that’s faced a 
wave of withdrawals <https://bloom.bg/4b2Arrw>, Blackstone is allowing 
investors to redeem a record 7.9% of shares from its flagship private credit 
fund.

The firm is meeting the requests, which are equivalent to around $3.8 billion, 
by increasing the size of a previously announced tender offer to 7% of the 
fund’s total shares and by stepping in, alongside employees, to offset the 
remaining 0.9%, according to a filing and a spokesperson.

Redemption requests have picked up across several private credit funds in 
recent quarters,amid anxiety <https://bloom.bg/4r60vIe> over the asset class 
and its exposure to software businesses at risk of disruption from AI. Private 
credit has been a key component of Blackstone’s expansion beyond its roots as a 
buyout firm.




US and Chinese trade negotiators are slated to meet in mid-March, according to 
people familiar with the matter, signaling a planned summit between Trump and 
Xi Jinpingis pushing ahead <https://bloom.bg/4b5I4O8> despite American strikes 
against Iran.

US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer 
and China’s Vice Premier He Lifeng are expected to convene in Paris at the end 
of next week to discuss business deals that could stem from the leaders’ 
meeting, said the people who requested anonymity. Both the timing and location 
of the meeting could still shift, the people added.
Still keen to meet: Xi Jinping, China’s president. Photographer: Manuel 
Orbegozo/Bloomberg

freeze on assets blocked over President Vladimir Putin’s invasion of Ukraine, 
arguing that it’sbeing deprived <https://bloom.bg/4rhQt7i> of legal protections 
in a first challenge of its kind since the war erupted. The claim filed with 
the EU’s General Court in Luxembourg contests the European Council’s regulation 
of Dec. 12, 2025, according to a Russian central bank statemen.

 For Your Commute 



The city of Muscat in Oman is typically targeted by travelers as the cultural 
counterpoint to nearby Dubai and its glitzy skyscrapers. But now the city is 
becoming a major draw for a very different kind of visitor: people stranded by 
airspace closings who are willing to pay a premium to evacuate.

The drive from Dubai to Muscat International Airport, where operations are 
normal, can be made in just under five hours if you don’t take the scenic route 
along the Persian Gulf. It’s becoming a popular trip for people worried about 
spending a lengthy stretch of time in Dubai, where debris from Iranian missiles 
has rained down on the local airport and the man-made island Palm Jumeirah, and 
flights out of the city remain elusive.

Oman’s national carrier, by contrast, has increased flights into Europe and 
Saudi Arabia to accommodate fleeing travelers. It’s also running buses from the
