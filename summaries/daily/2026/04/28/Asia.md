The Bank of Japan delivered what markets are characterizing as a hawkish hold, keeping its benchmark rate unchanged at 0.75% in a 6-3 vote — the most divided outcome under Governor Ueda's tenure. Three board members, including the usually consensus-leaning Junko Nakagawa, voted to raise rates immediately, and Bloomberg Economics now assigns high probability to a hike at the June meeting. The yen briefly strengthened through 159 per dollar before reversing as Ueda's press conference introduced caveats around the bank's baseline outlook and dollar buying resumed alongside rising oil prices. The currency's near-term trajectory now depends substantially on the tone of Powell's Wednesday remarks; a hawkish signal could push the yen through the psychologically significant 160 level.

Political constraints on the BOJ remain meaningful. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's administration — aligned with the late Shinzo Abe's reflation legacy — has appointed two dovish board members, with a second joining in June, complicating the central bank's normalization path even as inflationary pressures from energy costs and a weak exchange rate accumulate.

South Korea's equity market has emerged as the year's strongest performer among major indices, with the Kospi rising approximately 60% and total market capitalization surpassing the UK's at $4.04 trillion. Samsung Electronics reported a 755% year-on-year profit increase in the March quarter, and Samsung and SK Hynix together now represent 42% of Kospi capitalization, up from 28% two years ago — a concentration that amplifies both momentum and tail risk. Labor tensions are building: thousands of Samsung employees rallied over the weekend demanding higher wages, with the union threatening an 18-day strike beginning May 21.

China's Politburo pledged to counter external shocks and enhance energy security, citing better-than-expected first-quarter growth. Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable. Separately, Beijing is pressing Meta to unwind its $2 billion acquisition of AI startup Manus, a degree of extraterritorial commercial intervention that, if executed, would represent a significant escalation in China's assertiveness over cross-border technology deals.
