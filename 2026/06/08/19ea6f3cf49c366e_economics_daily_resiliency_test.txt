Resiliency Test

The world economy has proven more resilient than expected to the Iran war 
triggered oil shock.

But that may change.

New analysis from the Peterson Institute for International Economics warns 
that emerging economies are especially vulnerable to a new slowdown if the 
conflict continues.

The analysis is based on two scenarios. One sees oil jumping to around $120 a 
barrel for a year, with prices of LNG and refined petroleum soaring and 
agriculture taking a hit from shortages and expensive fertilizer. A second 
scenario sees those high prices persist for three years.

The PIIE isnтАЩt the only one contemplating higher-for-longer тАФ BritainтАЩs 
government has assessed crude could stay around $100until 2028 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/uk-sees-risk-of-100-oil-until-2028-in-worsening-iran-outlook?cmpid=BBD060826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5250>
. Both of the instituteтАЩs scenarios spell bad news for global growth, with the 
impact pronounced for developing economies that rely on the Middle East for 
oil, petroleum, gas and fertilizer.
PIIE, working paper, Global economic implications of the 2026 Middle East war
тАЬWe find in both scenarios, global growth slows relative to our baseline 
projection, but the effects are felt very unevenly,тАЭ wrote Warwick McKibbin, 
Marcus Noland and Geoffrey Shuetrim, co-authors of the PIIE report.

The warning from the PIIE is similar to one issued by the Paris-based OECD on 
June 3, when it revised up its inflation expectations,tweaked 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/global-economy-risks-slump-on-prolonged-iran-conflict-oecd-says?cmpid=BBD060826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5250>
 already weak growth projections and said the outlook for the world economy 
continues to rest on how the Iran conflict plays out.

In its analysis, the PIIE found that the Middle East and North Africa would 
suffer the biggest hit, with surging inflation and GDP at least 12% lower than 
the regionтАЩs baseline.

ChinaтАЩs GDP would be around 1.8% lower than it otherwise would have been 
without the war, while Vietnam, India and Thailand would see economic growth 
reduced by about 3% GDP .

Some advanced economies would also take a knock, with Western Europe losing 
1.6% of GDP and South Korea seeing 2.8% shaved off.

But the US economy would remain somewhat shielded, with 2026 GDP around 1.2% 
lower, with farming and transport among sectors that see bigger impact.

тАЬThe longer the disruption to global energy markets persists, the greater the 
economic costs over time,тАЭ the PIIE authors wrote.

Need-to-Know Research

Morgan Stanley economists have hiked their estimates of whatтАЩs known as 
r-star, or the inflation-adjusted neutral rate, for the Fed. ThatтАЩs the level 
of the US central bankтАЩs policy benchmark thatтАЩs thought to be where 
policymakers are neither stimulating nor restraining the economy.

A little over a year ago, the teamтАЩs estimate was 0.8%. Assuming 2% inflation 
тАФ the FedтАЩs target тАФ that would mean 2.8% for the nominal setting. Late last 
year, the bank already boosted its reckoning to 1%. Now, its analysis suggests 
1.34%. Thesurge in spending 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/fed-s-logan-finds-more-inflation-drivers-in-west-texas?cmpid=BBD060826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260608&utm_content=5250>
 on business investment, led by the gargantuan artificial intelligence 
build-out, is putting upward pressure on rates, Morgan StanleyтАЩs US economists 
led by Michael Gapen wrote in a note Friday.

тАЬWe do think itтАЩs reasonable to conclude the neutral rate of interest is 
higher given inelastic AI-driven spending, with an open question as to just how 
much higher,тАЭ Gapen and his colleagues wrote. тАЬTaken at face value,тАЭ the new 
1.34% estimate тАЬwould suggest the FedтАЩs current 3.50-3.75% policy setting is 
neutral in the context of year-ahead forecasts for inflation,тАЭ they wrote. That 
analysis would undermine the case for any Fed rate cuts.
