Three weeks into the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran, the conflict has entered a potentially pivotal moment. Israel's strike on Iran's South Pars gas field — the largest natural gas installation in the world — prompted Iran to inflict significant damage on a Qatari LNG hub, with Qatar indicating two affected trains could remain offline for up to five years. That escalation appears to have prompted a degree of recalibration on all sides. Prime Minister Netanyahu stated Israel would no longer target energy infrastructure and said the war would end faster than people expect; Iran's foreign minister signaled that recent restraint was deliberate. Whether this constitutes a durable de-escalation or tactical repositioning remains to be seen.

Brent crude, which surged to $119 per barrel on Thursday, pulled back to approximately $108-110 on Friday following Netanyahu's remarks, still well above $100. The Strait of Hormuz remains closed to commercial shipping, and Europe's largest naval powers stated that reopening the waterway during active hostilities would require a deeply complex multinational capability that is not currently feasible. The Pentagon is reported to have requested an additional $200 billion from Congress to fund ongoing operations.

Gold fell approximately 7% on the week — the largest weekly decline since March 2020 — and is on pace for its worst monthly performance since 2008. The move reflects the global monetary policy repricing, as higher-for-longer rates erode the appeal of non-yielding assets. ETF outflows have exceeded 60 tons over three weeks. In Southeast Asia, the oil shock is accelerating EV adoption, with BYD and VinFast reporting surging demand in the Philippines and Vietnam respectively. Asian buyers are also absorbing record volumes of US crude, with April loadings reaching approximately 60 million barrels — the most since 2023 — as buyers seek alternatives to disrupted Persian Gulf supply. US officials have ruled out banning oil and gas exports, and the administration's Iran sanctions relief proposal is widely viewed as operationally complex and unlikely to provide near-term price relief at scale.
