Wall Street reckons with 5% yields

In some parts of Wall Street, the hottest debate isn’t about a tech-stock 
bubble or $100-a-barrel oil. It’s whether 30-year Treasury yields will mount a
sustained push over 5%. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/us-yields-at-5-tug-traders-between-dip-buying-greed-and-fear?cmpid=050726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260507&utm_content=4246>

The US government’s long-term borrowing costs briefly topped that level this 
week. The move has been driven, in part, by fears that consumer prices will 
spiral again due to the Middle East war.

Elevated bond yields have repercussions for global markets, the economy and 
politics because they lift the cost of credit cards, mortgages and business 
loans.

A yield of 5% is a “psychological threshold that tends to reignite worries of 
bond vigilantes and higher interest rates,” said Gennadiy Goldberg of TD 
Securities.

A key question for traders is how long the rate hangs around 5%. If a US-Iran 
peace deal causes the oil shock to fade, or if the economy stalls, a revival of 
Fed rate cuts bets would push yields lower.

“We think market pricing remains too hawkish,” said Krishna Guha of Evercore 
ISI. “A deal with oil moving lower is consistent with Fed cuts delayed not 
derailed.” —Michael MacKenzie

AI trading bots are mostly losing

AI isn’t ready to replace your fund manager. Public experiments show why 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-06/ai-bots-auditioning-for-wall-street-trading-are-mostly-losing?cmpid=050726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260507&utm_content=4246>
.

Across a series of new trading contests between the world’s leading AI models, 
the verdict so far is unflattering: Most of the systems lose money. They trade 
too much. They make wildly different decisions when given identical 
instructions. And no one knows if they can improve.

One example is Alpha Arena, a contest run by tech startup Nof1. It pitted 
eight AI systems — including Claude, ChatGPT and Grok — against each other in 
four separate competitions. Each was handed $10,000 per contest.

The portfolio as a whole lost about a third of its capital. Across all 32 sets 
of results, a model finished in profit only six times.

For now, “human in the loop” remains the motto when it comes to trading real 
money. —Justina Lee <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQ0Te4IePFE/justina-lee>
