The Song Remains the Same

The International Monetary Fund has just released its latest yearly report on 
China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-18/imf-warns-china-s-economic-policies-are-causing-damage-to-others?cmpid=BBD021926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
, calling on authorities to rebalance the economy toward consumption as the old 
investment and export-led growth model “now faces challenges.”

With domestic demand in a funk due to the ongoing property slump, the Fund 
notes that China has relied on exports to drive growth, “with adverse 
spillovers to trading partners” — a rather more polite critique than US 
President Donald Trump has leveled against China’s trade practices.
“China’s large economic size and heightened global trade tensions make 
reliance on exports less viable for sustaining robust growth going forward,” 
the report warns. It goes on to list various prescriptions — from scaling back 
industrial policies to raising social spending — to help rebalance growth 
toward domestic demand.
It’s a message that the IMF has been peddling for two decades now. Way back in 
its2006 Article IV report 
<https://www.imf.org/external/pubs/ft/scr/2006/cr06394.pdf> — when George W. 
Bush was president and the phrase “Chimerica” was coined to emphasize the 
interconnectedness of the US and China — the Fund’s staffers had the following 
suggestions for Beijing:
“To facilitate rebalancing of the economy, financial sector reforms are needed 
to improve the intermediation of China’s large private savings. The government 
also needs to raise social spending in the areas of education, health care, and 
pensions, which will serve to reduce precautionary saving and boost consumption 
over time.”
Chinese households consumed less than 40% of GDP in 2005, the report noted. 
Today’s level? You guessed it: Just under 40%.

Over the intervening two decades — spanning financial crises, property slumps, 
trade wars and a pandemic — China’s leaders have paid lip service to the need 
to spur spending. It’s just that they’re not prepared to do so at the expense 
of the other big growth drivers — investment and exports. To Beijing, 
consumption is a follow on from development, not a driver of it.

Which means China’s growth balance is looking as uneven as ever, if its 
whopping capital account surplus is any guide.

So will IMF staffers in 20 years still be singing the same tune? Maybe not.

Amid Trump’s tariff campaign and rising trade restrictions elsewhere, Chinese 
officials from President Xi Jinping down have recently been calling for 
domestic demand to make up a bigger share of growth. They’ll gather in Beijing 
next month to announce goals for 2026 and to layout their 15th five-year-plan.

In late October, leaders gave a broad outline of the blueprint, saying it 
would seek to “significantly” boost the share of consumption while keeping 
manufacturing’s share of the economy at a “reasonable” level.

If it finally happens, it’s a rebalancing that’ll reshape the world economy, 
not just China’s.

Need-to-Know Research

In a working paper titled “The Politics of AI 
<https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w34813/w34813.pdf>,” 
researchers Nicholas Bloom and Christos Makridis found that Democrats in the US 
are more likely to use artificial intelligence frequently than Republicans.

In polling data from Gallup, 27.8% of Democrats reported using the technology 
weekly or daily last quarter, versus 22.5% of Republicans, a gap that persisted 
as the share for both parties grew over the past year.

But that doesn’t necessarily mean that AI adoption is driven by political 
motivations, the paper shows. The partisan gap is mostly explained by the type 
of jobs people are holding and their skills or level of education — with 
Democrats more likely to be employed in occupations with higher AI exposure.
The Politics of AI. NBER working paper 34813 
https://www.nber.org/system/files/working_papers/w34813/w34813.pdf
This has important implications from a political economic perspective, the 
authors said.

“If AI complements high-education and professional occupations, and these 
remain unevenly distributed across political constituencies, then the economic 
gains from AI may reinforce existing partisan cleavages in income growth, skill 
accumulation, and workplace opportunity,” Bloom and Makridis wrote in the 
paper, published by the National Bureau of Economic Research.
