China's financial regulators have quietly directed the country's largest banks to halt new lending to five refiners recently sanctioned by the US for their involvement in Iranian oil trade. The directive leaves existing loans intact, stopping short of a full clampdown, but the signal is deliberate and well-timed: President Trump is expected to press President Xi Jinping on China's continued purchases of Iranian crude at their upcoming Beijing summit. Beijing is simultaneously invoking its "blocking" rules to project defiance against US secondary sanctions while shielding its major banks from the dollar-access risks that aggressive enforcement would entail. The financial retreat is cautious but unmistakable.

The legal exposure for global banks operating between the two systems is crystallizing separately. JPMorgan and Citigroup are being sued in China after freezing tens of millions of dollars in payments tied to a firm subsequently sanctioned by the US — with the plaintiff alleging funds were blocked and routed to US authorities before sanctions formally applied. The case illustrates the narrowing corridor available to institutions caught between competing legal jurisdictions.

Japan's Nikkei 225 surged 5.6% to a record high as deal optimism surrounding the Strait of Hormuz lifted risk sentiment broadly across the region. The yen, however, continues to struggle near 155 per dollar despite repeated suspected interventions by Japanese authorities estimated to have deployed around $30 billion in recent days. Analysts broadly characterize intervention as increasingly ineffective level-targeting absent a meaningful shift in Bank of Japan policy, given persistent structural headwinds from elevated energy import costs and still-accommodative monetary settings.

In Southeast Asia, the Philippines faces its most severe economic stress since the pandemic, with growth slowing sharply, inflation surging, and consumer spending at its weakest in over a decade — a direct consequence of the country's heavy reliance on Middle Eastern crude. Malaysia's central bank kept its benchmark rate unchanged for a fifth straight meeting, judging that contained inflation and a relatively resilient ringgit provide sufficient buffer for now.
