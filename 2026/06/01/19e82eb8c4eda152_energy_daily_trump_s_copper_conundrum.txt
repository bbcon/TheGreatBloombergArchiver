Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Metals and Mining Team Leader Mark Burton looks at the implications of TrumpтАЩs 
upcoming decision on copper tariffs.

Since Donald Trump came to power, the copper market has been gripped by the 
question of whether heтАЩll impose tariffs on the metal.

The answer that best serves the presidentтАЩs economic goals might be: Maybe. 

For more than a year, traders have been shipping huge volumes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/copper-s-giant-tariff-trade-is-back-and-squeezing-global-market?cmpid=BBBXT060126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5049>
 of copper to America, capitalizing on a surge in US copper prices built partly 
on bets that Trump will push ahead with tariffs.

That trade has been gaining momentum ahead of the June 30 deadline for the 
Commerce Department to deliver a market update to Trump, so he can decide 
whether tariffs are needed.

If he imposes levies, the once-in-a-generation profits traders have made 
shipping copper to the US are likely to dry up. There may be a final spurt of 
activity if he follows Commerce Department advice to introduce tariffs from 
2027, but imports would eventually slow to a trickle.

Manufacturers would begin to work through huge stockpiles of metal that have 
accumulated in US ports. However, they could ultimately face higher costs as 
they wait тАФ for years or even decades тАФ fornew mines 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-10/freeport-ceo-says-tariffs-threaten-to-hinder-us-copper-mining?cmpid=BBBXT060126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5049>
 and smelters to be built.

If Trump says no to tariffs, US copper prices are likely to plunge back into 
line with futures markets in London and Shanghai. The incentive to ship the 
metal to the US would vanish, and American inventories could start being 
transferred back out into international markets.

The US would remain heavily dependent on imports of copper at a time when 
China is strengthening its grip on global supply.

From an industrial-policy standpoint, both options have drawbacks. As a 
result, traders and analysts are increasingly betting on a third outcome: Trump 
will hold off on tariffs for now, but keep the door open to imposing them in 
future.

ThatтАЩs what he did in July 2025, when the Commerce Department first laid out a 
proposal for tariffs. The movewhipsawed markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-31/traders-brace-for-wave-of-copper-to-hit-lme-after-tariff-shock?cmpid=BBBXT060126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5049>
, but they never stopped pricing tariffs in as a possibility. That meant 
imports kept pouring into the US, creating a massive stockpile for 
manufacturers at zero cost to the government.

Some in the industry see that as a strategic fluke, but a growing number are 
betting that keeping the market guessing тАФ what Citigroup Inc. calls тАЬstrategic 
ambiguityтАЭ тАФ will become an intentional part of TrumpтАЩs trade policy.
