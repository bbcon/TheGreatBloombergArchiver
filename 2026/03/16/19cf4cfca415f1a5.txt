It’s No Game <https://bloom.bg/4bs9P3x> 

The game theory of the Persian Gulf is no longer comforting. For two weeks, 
the logic was that President Donald Trump wanted a short, sharp action, as he 
was elected on an anti-war platform, and didn’t wish prices to rise ahead of 
the midterm elections. Thus he would likely TACO (for “Trump Always Chickens 
Out”), declare a victory and move on.

With Iran putting up stiffer resistance and inflicting more economic pain than 
had been expected, such logic now points toward escalation, for all that Trump 
needs a brief conflict. TheStrait of Hormuz <https://bloom.bg/4blDVWo> is 
closed, Iran evidently intends to keep it that way, and there is no credible 
means to claim US victory unless it reopens.

Thus the window for a TACO outcome is closed, according to David Woo of David 
Woo Unbound, because Trump now “needs a clear win to get a face-saving 
off-ramp.” The Strait currently matters to world markets more than anything 
else, and the closure is total (for those not blessed by the Iranian 
government):

The level of fascination with the neck of water between Iran and Oman only 
intensifies. The number of stories mentioning it from all sources on the 
Bloomberg terminal has suddenly eclipsed even ChatGPT, whose advent transformed 
the financial world:

While the Strait remains closed to traffic, virtually all agree that pressure 
on oil and other vital commodity prices will remain intense. Iran appears now 
to be committed to keeping the passage closed as its sole but very powerful 
item of leverage, and that’s fairly easy to do; large arsenals of the most 
advanced weaponry aren’t required. The corollary is that forcibly reopening it 
could require very major escalation, and therefore taking major risks.

Andrew Bishop of Signum Global Advisors sums this up as follows:

President Trump’s instinct/inclination at a juncture like this one is likely 
to be to try to regain the upper hand over Iran by “taking” something from it — 
with the two most plausible options being invading Kharg Island and/or 
forcefully reopening the Strait of Hormuz. The problem for him is that both of 
those options carry extremely poor risk/reward prospects.

The fact that the US president has belatedly called for western allies to send 
ships to help reopen the Strait, having embarked on the war without them, 
demonstrates that the risks have increased and that the logic of the situation 
tends toward further escalation. Prediction market bettors, for what it’s 
worth, are pushing the likely date of a ceasefire ever further into the future. 
The odds of a ceasefire even by May 31 have now dropped below 50%, according to 
Polymarket <https://bloom.bg/3N8tpK8>:

How Bad Is This?

The closing of the Strait has already widely been described by the 
International Energy Authority as the“largest oil supply shock in history.” 
<https://bloom.bg/4bLncNq> This is true in the sense that the amount and 
percentage by which the regular flow of oil has fallen is greater even than the 
disruption caused by the oil embargos that followed the Yom Kippur War in 1973 
and the Iranian Revolution of 1979. However, the world is less dependent on oil 
now than it was then, and there is also underlying confidence that the supply 
interruption won’t last long.

Brent crude futures for one month hence are over $100. For five years hence, 
however, they’re still just below $70, lower than for much of 2024, and well 
under the levels recorded during the first year of the war in Ukraine. This can 
change, but the market is definitely not yet pricing that this will be the 
biggest ever financial shock from restricted oil supply:

Even the rise in the spot price isn’t exceptional. This is how Bloomberg’s 
measure of historic crude, deflated by the US consumer price index, has varied 
since 1970. It’s nothing like the two biggest shocks of the 1970s, or the 1990 
response to Saddam Hussein’s invasion of Kuwait. And the biggest price shock on 
record, in the early stages of the Global Financial Crisis, was driven by 
factors other than supply:

This might denote complacency. It clearly reflects the belief that Hormuz will 
not stay closed for long, and that, to quote John Cochrane’s Grumpy Economist 
blog, the US and others will not “effectively decide to lose the war by handing 
Iran this perpetual trump card.” Both supply and demand will find ways to adapt:

There is a lot one can do to use less oil when the price rises. And there is a 
lot of production capacity that is not worth tapping at $65 but very worth 
turning on at $100.

The longer the war continues, or escalates, the harder this view will be to 
sustain. For now, it’s reasonable; this is not, yet, a financial shock to rank 
with history’s greatest.

The Financial Aftershocks

Even if the blow is less than catastrophic, it is startling how few benefit. 
The direction of travel is inimical for the Trump administration. Treasury 
Secretary Scott Bessent’s strategy has involved a weak dollar, lower yields and 
cheaper oil, combined with a strong stock market, and it was going very well 
until the last few weeks. The war has thrown that into reverse:

The US is far less affected by the Strait closure than other developed 
nations, and its stock market has therefore remained far more resilient than 
most over the last two weeks. As Eric Robertsen of Standard Chartered points 
out, Asia is most vulnerable, with China, India, Japan and South Korea combined 
accounting for about 70% of Hormuz crude shipments. “The biggest risk for 
Asia’s economies, in our view, is not inflation but stagflation.”

Thus Japan has seen renewed pressure on the yen. It is now almost back to the 
all-time low it hit in 2024, despite radically higher interest rates since then:

And Europe, whose vulnerability was cruelly revealed by the energy crisis that 
followed the Ukraine invasion in 2022, has seen rising bond yields across the 
board. This reverses the previous trend, and shows markets are worried about 
both inflation and a hawkish response from central banks:

Emerging Pain Points

The greatest difficulty will be for countries with high energy imports that 
take up a large part of gross domestic product. As this chart from BNY shows, 
the oil-importing countries of Europe have generally managed to grow more 
energy efficient. The big outliers, standing to be hurt the most, are China and 
particularly India:
Source: BNY, Bloomberg, International Energy Agency 
In emerging markets, central bankers <https://bloom.bg/4uwVWtE>, who were 
quick to react to the post-pandemic inflation surge, are likely standing ready 
to act again. A case for a swift policy response is made stronger given the 
need to ring-fence the gains and prevent a relapse into a cycle oflackluster 
growth <https://bloom.bg/4bKbgeV> like the prior decade. 

Every day that passes without reopening the Strait <https://bloom.bg/4rA9QZh> 
increases the likelihood that meaningful measures are taken to mitigate the 
economic damage. For now, investors unwinding their positioning in emerging 
market forex is reminiscent of theLiberation Day plunge 
<https://bloom.bg/4rCcY75>, as this chart from Bank of America shows:

This decline, BofA’s Ralf Preusser points out, makes EM external debt most 
vulnerable to further corrections, especially given its correlation with US 
real rates that have yet to see a meaningful jump. This fragility is evident in 
cross-border flows. TheInstitute of International Finance 
<https://bloom.bg/3P2qNOB> reports that foreign investors sharply slowed 
purchases of EM assets in February — before the war broke out — to below $22 
billion, far under January’s record $100.5 billion and below the $45.5 billion 
recorded a year ago:

Flows remain positive. But the experience after Liberation Day — when money 
flowed out of EM assets — suggests a renewed flight to safe havens could 
happen. That would be a gut punch for investors who had just begun to buy into 
emerging markets’ resurgence. While the conflict hasweighed 
<https://bloom.bg/4rCfBFU>, EM’s outperformance in January and February 
provides some insulation:

An Oxford Economics prewar survey also suggests resilience. Around 44% of 
respondents see upside risks to EM GDP projections above the historical average:

This forecast is premised on domestic policy support and resilient demand, 
factors that, Oxford Economics’ Joshua Fisher argues, may offer some buffer 
against higher oil prices and financial spillovers:

Inflation risks were skewed to the upside before the Iran conflict, but less 
than historical averages because of strong forex and benign global inflationary 
pressures. Higher oil pressures undoubtedly tip risks toward the upside. Our 
severe disruption oil-shock simulation suggests second-quarter inflation could 
rise by 0.4 percentage points to 1.7 percentage points.

Idiosyncratic factors mean that any escalation will likely hit each country 
differently. Even before the war,EM whales like India <https://bloom.bg/4737jPR>
 were in shallow waters. A sustained oil price surge would be a windfall for 
energy exporters outside the Gulf region, like Brazil and Russia. Does that 
move the needle on GDP projections for the year? Not much, William Jackson of 
Capital Economics argues:

Inflation will rise from current low rates. If the conflict is protracted, 
policymakers could turn to monetary tightening. But even if it is short-lived, 
officials are less likely to deliver the cuts we had previously penciled in.

Over the next two weeks, more than 18 central banks <https://bloom.bg/4btwtsm> 
will deliberate on monetary policy, which should provide greater clarity on the 
growth outlook amid the crisis. For now, caution is justified: If thelessons 
from the post-pandemic inflation surge <https://bloom.bg/4rB4X2e> are any 
guide, policymakers have learned the value of erring on the side of caution 
rather than risking the painful consequences of inflation months down the line.

—Richard Abbey
