Monetary Policy

Kevin Warsh begins his tenure as Federal Reserve chair with Wednesday's FOMC meeting widely expected to produce a hold, leaving the policy rate at 3.5%–3.75%. The decision is straightforward; the press conference is not. Two-year Treasury yields at approximately 4.15% sit materially above the current policy range, a gap that first opened in March and reflects market conviction that today's settings may prove insufficient. May's PPI print — the fastest annual gain in three years — reinforces that view. Consensus expects Warsh to avoid an overtly hawkish tone at the outset, instead seeking cover in the data while leaving optionality for a hike later in the year should energy-driven inflation persist. Oxford Economics argues the bar for an immediate hike remains high, and Warsh may emphasize productivity-driven disinflation to justify an eventual easing bias — though that case has become harder to make after three consecutive upside surprises in non-farm payrolls.

Economic Conditions

China's domestic demand picture deteriorated materially heading into this week's data releases — retail sales, industrial production, and fixed asset investment figures are due Tuesday — with early indications suggesting consumer spending may have contracted on a quarterly basis for the first time since the pandemic. Should Tuesday's prints confirm this weakness, it would represent a meaningful escalation of China's demand-side challenge, compounding deflationary pressures already visible in producer prices. The Hormuz deal offers some relief for import-dependent Asian economies; Barclays economists noted the interim agreement "could strengthen the hands of doves" at upcoming central bank meetings in the Philippines and Indonesia, both of which face decisions this week.

Monetary Policy and Macro Outlook

The ECB's rate increase last week placed it at the frontier of developed-market policy tightening, a position reflecting an uncomfortable combination of above-target inflation and deteriorating growth. The bank's updated projections expect energy price pass-through into core inflation to keep both headline and underlying measures above target through next year. ECB President Lagarde has explicitly avoided forward guidance, but overnight index swap markets have repriced significantly since the Iran conflict began, implying more than one additional hike in the base case. The US-Iran interim deal complicates this calculus: a durable fall in oil prices would give the ECB room to pause, but Oxford Economics cautions that last week's move is unlikely to be the last if inflation expectations remain unanchored. The stagflationary bind — where further hikes risk amplifying the growth slowdown — leaves policymakers with limited room to maneuver.

Regional Macro and Policy

Latin America's near-term macro narrative is shaped primarily by external forces rather than domestic data today. The US-Iran interim deal, if sustained, would reduce energy import costs for oil-importing economies including Chile and Brazil, providing modest disinflationary relief and potentially widening the policy space for central banks already navigating a complex mix of slowing growth and sticky price pressures. Brazil's Copom decision Wednesday is the principal domestic event of the week. The World Bank's revised global growth forecast — cut on the back of Hormuz disruption and commodity-flow dislocations — carries direct implications for commodity exporters across the region, though lower oil prices represent a terms-of-trade headwind for producers including Colombia and Ecuador.

Oil Markets and the Hormuz Deal

The interim US-Iran agreement announced Sunday represents a meaningful de-escalation of a conflict that has disrupted roughly 20% of global seaborne oil flows for nearly four months. Markets responded decisively — Brent futures down more than 5% in a single session — but analysts urge caution on the pace of normalization. Nearly 600 vessels remain stranded in the Persian Gulf awaiting departure, mine-clearance operations led by the UK and France have not been completed, and shipowners including Mitsui OSK Lines and Nippon Yusen have signaled they will wait for greater clarity before transiting the waterway. A mid-April precedent, when a brief apparent opening collapsed within days, adds grounds for skepticism. Trump has stated the strait will open June 19; Iran's framing — suggesting continued Iranian-Omani oversight of vessel passage — implies the two sides may still interpret the arrangement differently.

Global Monetary Policy

The deal's most immediate macro consequence may be felt in central bank meeting rooms. Seven major central banks convene this week, most widely expected to hold. The ECB's hike last week made it the first developed-market central bank to tighten in response to war-driven inflation; a sustained oil price decline would give it — and peers from the Fed to the Bank of England to Southeast Asian central banks — more room to assess without acting. A Bloomberg diffusion index of 52 central banks shows tightening has again become the modal outcome globally, underscoring how deeply the conflict has reshaped the rate cycle. Whether the interim deal proves durable enough to shift that trajectory remains the central question for global monetary policy into the second half of 2026.
