Daily Brief

Thursday, March 26, 2026

US

US equity markets remained defensive, with S&P 500 futures falling roughly 0.7% as conflicting signals over US-Iran ceasefire prospects sapped risk appetite. The S&P 500 has now surrendered less than 5% since hostilities began nearly a month ago, a relative resilience that many on Wall Street attribute less to fundamental optimism than to the memory of April 9, 2025, when the index surged 9.5% on tariff relief — keeping would-be bears cautious about committing to the short side. The dominant posture across institutional desks is cash accumulation rather than directional conviction: Bank of America's latest fund manager survey recorded the largest jump in cash allocations since March 2020, with holdings at 4.3%, while Nomura's macro traders report having been instructed to stand down. Goldman Sachs has raised its 12-month US recession probability to 30%, citing surging oil prices, and projects unemployment climbing to 4.6% by year-end from 4.4% in February. The OECD now sees US inflation reaching 4.2% in 2026, up from 2.6% last year and 1.2 percentage points above its December projection. Trump administration officials are separately stress-testing scenarios involving oil at $200 a barrel, an exercise framed as contingency planning rather than a forecast. The cost of US imports rose in February by the most in nearly four years, suggesting inflationary pressure was already building before the Middle East conflict began. On the corporate side, a jury found Meta Platforms and Alphabet liable for damages in a social media addiction case — a ruling that may signal broader litigation risk — though both stocks edged higher, consistent with markets treating the decision as bounded rather than existential. Memory and storage stocks fell in premarket trading after Google researchers disclosed a compression technique that could reduce AI memory requirements, with Micron down 2.8% and Sandisk off 4.1%. Federal Reserve nominee Kevin Warsh's ambitions to shrink the Fed's $6.6 trillion balance sheet face a structural constraint: analysts note the task would require more than a single term to execute meaningfully.

Asia

Asia is bearing the sharpest economic costs of the Strait of Hormuz disruption. Saudi Aramco has been forced to reroute some flows through its Red Sea pipeline, a partial workaround that still leaves China and India facing reduced crude shipments from the kingdom next month. India, where over 330 million households rely on LPG for cooking and roughly 90% of supply transits the Middle East, has seen delivery times stretch from five to 25 days in some regions, triggering a run on electric induction stoves with online sales rising as much as 30-fold. Across Southeast Asia, consumers are shifting toward BYD electric vehicles, while Pakistan stands to save an estimated $6.3 billion this year by substituting solar for oil and gas at elevated prices — a structural response that the war may be accelerating beyond what the energy transition alone would have achieved. Governments across the region have shifted to emergency energy footing, cutting work weeks, switching off street lighting, and limiting petrol station hours. The IMF is actively modelling which economies may require fresh financing if the conflict extends, with energy-dependent emerging Asia the primary area of concern. China's small and medium-sized enterprises are already reporting surging input costs, with some reducing headcount. BlackRock President Rob Kapito warned that oil could still spike toward $150 a barrel even if the fighting stopped tomorrow, while Apollo's Jim Zelter flagged rising recession risk and credit cycle strain from a prolonged conflict. On a more constructive note, the Trump-Xi summit has been confirmed for May 14-15 in Beijing, a development that provides some stabilization to US-China relations after weeks of additional uncertainty. Meanwhile, China's semiconductor ambitions advanced: ChangXin Memory Technologies reportedly more than doubled revenue to approximately $8 billion in 2025, driven by AI chip demand, as Beijing continues its push toward domestic self-sufficiency in advanced semiconductors. Moonshot AI, maker of the Kimi chatbot, is exploring a Hong Kong IPO alongside up to $1 billion in private funding, reflecting continued investor appetite for Chinese AI assets.

Europe

Europe is absorbing a simultaneous growth and inflation shock from the Iran war, with Italy now preparing to cut its 2026 GDP forecast to as low as 0.5%. UK consumer confidence fell to its weakest reading since the British Retail Consortium's tracker began in March 2024, as households anticipate an inflation surge. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 1.0%, tracking the broader deterioration in risk sentiment. Airfare disruption adds a further consumer cost: ticket prices on major Asia-Europe routes have risen as much as 560% this month and are likely to remain elevated through summer, according to Alton Aviation. British Airways is offering pilots financial incentives to reduce fuel consumption, reflecting the pressure on airline economics. Germany is advancing contingency planning against potential US coercion, with officials mapping vulnerabilities in American supply chains — spanning Big Tech, AI infrastructure, and pharmaceutical flows — that the EU could exploit in a future confrontation with the Trump administration. The exercise reflects a broader shift in Berlin's strategic posture, treating economic leverage as genuinely bilateral rather than asymmetric. Separately, EU lawmakers approved a long-delayed trade deal with the United States, a constructive development at the margins of the WTO's 14th ministerial conference opening in Yaoundé, Cameroon. In the UK, the Green Party under new leader Zack Polanski has moved ahead of both Labour and the Conservatives in some polls, a development worth monitoring as energy costs continue to drive voter behavior. On the pharmaceutical side, Novo Nordisk's market capitalization has declined by nearly $500 billion over 21 months, with patent expirations in India and China — markets representing roughly 40% of the world's population — enabling generic GLP-1 drugs at prices as low as $14. Henkel reached a deal to acquire hair-care brand Olaplex for $1.4 billion, with Olaplex shares surging nearly 50%.

Global

The defining macro tension of the moment is the simultaneity of an acute energy supply shock and unresolved geopolitical uncertainty. Brent crude traded near $108 a barrel on March 26 and is on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990, with the near-total shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz — through which roughly one-fifth of global oil supply transits — driving what the International Chamber of Commerce described as potentially "the worst industrial crisis in living memory." BloombergNEF's base case sees Brent averaging $106.70 in April; a delayed Hormuz reopening until mid-June would push that average to $154.40. Iran, meanwhile, appears to be a net financial beneficiary: as the only major exporter currently able to utilize the strait freely, Tehran has reportedly earned hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental oil revenue since hostilities began, effectively converting the disruption into strategic and fiscal leverage. Diplomatically, the gap between Washington and Tehran remains wide. The White House describes Iran as seeking an exit ramp; Iran publicly rejects US overtures and is drafting legislation to impose passage fees on vessels transiting the strait. Former MI6 chief Alex Young assessed that the US "lost the initiative to Iran about two weeks ago." Prediction markets place the probability of a ceasefire by end of April at no better than 50-50. A suspected sea-drone attack on a crude tanker in the Black Sea added a further dimension to energy market risk. The OECD, the first major institution to formally revise its forecasts, left its 2026 global growth projection unchanged at 2.9% while trimming 2027 by 0.1 percentage point to 3.0%, noting the conflict arrived just as AI investment, tariff normalization, and accommodative policy were generating genuine upside. G20 average inflation is now expected at 4.0% versus 2.8% in December. Central banks are pivoting away from rate cuts, and the OECD warned that "monetary policy adjustments may be needed if price pressures broaden." The structural response from consumers — accelerating EV adoption, rooftop solar, and electrification of cooking in India — may prove durable, though it is unlikely to offset the near-term coal reversion across energy-stressed Asia.
