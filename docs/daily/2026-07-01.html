Daily Brief

Wednesday, July 01, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 capped its best quarter since 2020, gaining 9.6% YTD, while the SOX semiconductor index outperformed dramatically; S&P 500 futures are down 0.3% to start Q3.
  • JOLTS data showed job vacancies returning to pre-pandemic peaks, a data point that may reinforce the Fed's cautious posture on rate cuts.
  • Bitcoin fell to a 21-month low, down 33.3% for H1, as Fed hawkishness eroded the "debasement trade"; gold dropped 14% in Q2, its worst quarterly performance since 2013.
  • Trump disclosed at least $1.4 billion in 2025 crypto earnings, reigniting concerns over presidential conflicts of interest.

Equity Markets

Markets entered Q3 on a cautious footing, with attention focused on Fed Chair Kevin Warsh speaking at the ECB's Sintra conference. The broader S&P 500 finished H1 up 9.6%, but beneath the surface the Magnificent 7 declined 1.9% YTD as investors rotated from hyperscale AI spenders toward semiconductor hardware beneficiaries. Alphabet, despite a stock that has more than doubled over the past year and a $4.3 trillion market cap, shed 6% in June alone — a consequence of its $85 billion equity raise and a series of high-profile AI researcher departures. Nike fell 4.3% in premarket after management flagged elevated consumer anxiety and offered no near-term improvement in the demand outlook. Constellation Brands rose 3.2% on an earnings beat.

Monetary Policy and Hard Assets

Gold opened Q3 below $3,980, extending a 14% Q2 decline — its worst since 2013 — as speculation that Warsh's Fed may hike rates weighs on non-yielding metals. A technical "death cross" has formed on gold charts, adding downside momentum. Bitcoin stabilized near $58,700 after hitting a 21-month low, with concerns that Strategy Inc. may reduce purchases amplifying rate-driven selling pressure. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi fell 2.0% to 8,303, with foreign investors extending an equity exodus to an eighth consecutive session; the won weakened toward its softest level since 2009.
  • China's manufacturing PMI capped its strongest quarter in nearly six years, though the private gauge showed growth easing in June and underlying domestic demand remains fragile.
  • Sentiment on the yuan reversed sharply as the Fed's hawkish tone revived dollar momentum, prompting investors to unwind bullish yuan positions.

China: Macro and Trade Dynamics

China's official and private manufacturing PMIs pointed to the strongest quarterly performance in almost six years, a print that modestly beat expectations on the headline but masked fragility in domestic demand. A more consequential development may be structural: the Iran conflict has redirected petrochemical trade flows across Southeast Asia toward Chinese producers, who leveraged excess capacity and alternative feedstocks to capture market share previously held by Persian Gulf suppliers. Whether these trade routes persist beyond the conflict remains uncertain, but the near-term export benefit is measurable.

Korea and Regional Currency Pressure

The Kospi's near-doubling in H1 — driven overwhelmingly by Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix on AI-driven memory demand — appears increasingly fragile. Foreign capital has been exiting for eight consecutive sessions, and the rally has been sustained primarily by domestic retail investors, a composition that historically precedes sharp reversals. The won's slide toward 2009 lows is spilling across regional FX markets, reinforcing a broader dollar reassertion. Japan's currency chief acknowledged yen intervention two months ago was effective; the yen has since fallen to a fresh 40-year low regardless. ---

Europe

  • Euro-area CPI came in below consensus, reducing near-term pressure on the ECB to resume tightening; ECB Governing Council member Kazaks stated the bank is "in no rush" to hike next month.
  • At the Sintra symposium, ECB President Lagarde, Fed Chair Warsh, and BoE Governor Bailey spoke alongside peers, with Bailey striking a dovish tone emphasizing weak growth over above-target inflation.

ECB and Monetary Policy

The softer-than-expected eurozone inflation print, combined with easing oil prices as Middle East peace talks progressed, has reduced the probability of an ECB rate increase at its next meeting. Kazaks explicitly confirmed this posture. However, Bundesbank President Nagel and Austria's Kocher indicated they are still considering hikes, reflecting a governing council that remains divided. ECB official Schnabel raised concerns about an AI-driven financial bubble, echoing BIS warnings earlier in the week, while IMF official Adrian flagged potential financial stability risks from rising leverage among major tech firms.

UK and Trade Policy

Bank of England Governor Bailey described the energy shock as "frustrating" for policymakers and emphasized growth weakness rather than inflation persistence — a notably dovish framing given that UK inflation remains above target. Separately, the Supreme Court's unwinding of US tariffs was flagged as a potential tailwind for corporate earnings that markets may not yet have fully priced. The USMCA review, triggered on its six-year anniversary, carries indirect implications for European competitiveness as North American trade uncertainty may redirect supply chain investment. ---

LatAm

  • The US raised Argentina's tariff-free beef export quota to 100,000 tons annually, a development that may support Argentine export revenues even as domestic beef consumption has fallen to record lows — 47.5 kg per capita over the past year — due to rising local prices.
  • Venezuelan opposition leader María Corina Machado remains stranded in Panama following twin earthquakes last week that killed nearly 2,000 people, with her attempts to reenter Venezuela unsuccessful amid ongoing political repression.

Argentina Trade and USMCA Dynamics

The expanded beef quota is a modest but concrete benefit for Argentina at a moment when domestic consumption has been structurally compressed by inflation. The bilateral arrangement with Washington fits a broader pattern of the Trump administration deploying market access as diplomatic currency. Meanwhile, the USMCA review process — launched on the agreement's sixth anniversary — threatens to impose a meaningful drag on confidence and investment across Canada and Mexico, with Oxford Economics explicitly revising down its forecasts for all three North American economies. The spillover for Latin American exporters competing with or supplying into the North American value chain warrants monitoring. ---

Global

  • Global M&A reached $2.6 trillion in H1, up approximately 30% year-over-year, with 38 deals above $10 billion — the most in any six-month period on record.
  • Goldman Sachs projects the global oil market will swing back into oversupply as Strait of Hormuz traffic recovers and the Iran war's supply disruption fades; Brent crude is still up 19.8% YTD.
  • US-Iran technical talks are advancing, with Kushner and Witkoff reporting positive discussions in Qatar; a durable ceasefire remains uncertain.

Energy and Geopolitics

The US-Iran negotiations represent the most consequential near-term variable for global energy markets. Brent crude's 19.8% YTD gain reflects the supply shock premium from Hormuz disruption; Goldman's oversupply forecast suggests significant downside to oil prices if a lasting agreement is reached. Simultaneously, the EU methane regulation dispute — with the US, Qatar, Algeria, and Nigeria demanding revisions before January 1 compliance kicks in — introduces a new fault line in transatlantic energy relations. Given the EU's dependence on US LNG for nearly two-thirds of its imports and a $750 billion committed energy purchasing agreement with Washington, energy security considerations are likely to force a European compromise on the methane rules.

AI Capital Concentration

The cross-regional pattern most defining H1 2026 is the extreme concentration of capital flows into AI semiconductor hardware — at measurable expense to software, broader emerging markets ex-Korea and Taiwan, and non-tech fixed investment. BCA Research estimates US tech capex now exceeds 7% of GDP in real terms, above the 2000 dot-com peak of 5% in nominal terms and comparable to housing-bubble shares of GDP that historically preceded sharp corrections. Whether this represents productive investment or malinvestment remains the central unresolved question for global markets in H2.
