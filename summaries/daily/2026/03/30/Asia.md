Asian equity markets opened the week sharply lower. Japan's Nikkei fell as much as 4.5% at the open, prompting Japan's top currency official to deliver his strongest warning yet against yen weakness and signal potential bold intervention — remarks that helped stabilize the currency. The broader MSCI Asia gauge declined as investors absorbed the entry of Houthi forces into the conflict and digested the prospect of a prolonged Strait of Hormuz disruption.

India remains among the most acutely exposed economies in the region. The Reserve Bank of India's move to cap lenders' open foreign-exchange positions at $100 million per day delivered only a fleeting lift to the rupee, which subsequently weakened to fresh lows. Global funds have withdrawn approximately $12 billion from Indian equities since the conflict began, while index-eligible bonds recorded $1.6 billion in outflows in March alone, a record. India's combination of heavy energy import dependence and proximity to the war zone leaves it with limited natural buffers.

South Korea signaled it is considering extending driving restrictions to the general public should Brent crude breach $120 per barrel — a threshold nearly reached on Monday at $117 — the first such potential measure since the 1991 Gulf War. The government has already asked citizens to reduce power consumption and favor public transport. Australia announced a temporary halving of fuel taxes at an estimated cost of A$2.55 billion. BYD offered a more constructive datapoint: management told analysts the company is confident 2026 exports will reach 1.5 million vehicles, 15% above its prior guidance, providing some relief after a disappointing earnings release.

On the geopolitical side, President Xi Jinping invited Kuomintang Chairwoman Cheng Li-wun — leader of Taiwan's largest opposition party — to visit China from April 7-12, the first such visit by a sitting KMT leader in a decade. The invitation comes one month before Trump's scheduled summit in Beijing and gives Xi a platform to discuss Taiwan's US weapons purchases directly with a Taiwanese political figure who has publicly questioned the scale of her island's defense spending.
