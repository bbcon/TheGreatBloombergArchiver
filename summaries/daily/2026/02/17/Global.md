# Global Daily Briefing — February 17, 2026

**US-Iran talks reopen as Hormuz sees scheduled disruption.** US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Iranian Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi are holding indirect nuclear talks in Geneva, with President Trump saying Tehran "wants to make a deal" and that he will be involved indirectly. Secretary of State Marco Rubio struck a cautiously optimistic tone, expressing hope that years of hostility can be bridged. The diplomatic opening coincides, however, with Iranian naval exercises that will close parts of the Strait of Hormuz for "several hours" today, according to Iranian state media — a calibrated reminder of Tehran's leverage over the world's most important oil chokepoint. Crude prices initially declined before recovering as the drills drew attention, leaving the market to weigh the prospect of de-escalation against the continued risk premium embedded in any Hormuz disruption. The combination of talks and exercises suggests both sides are negotiating from positions they wish to reinforce militarily; an agreement remains far from assured.

**Monetary policy divergence sharpens.** UK unemployment reached its highest level since the pandemic alongside easing wage growth, prompting traders to fully price half a point of Bank of England cuts by year-end. In contrast, the Reserve Bank of Australia's February minutes confirmed its rate hike was driven by a downgrade to productivity and potential output, with policymakers concluding the neutral rate is higher than previously assumed. The RBA's Andrew Hauser openly questioned whether Australia is a "first mover" on a constraint other advanced economies will face, or an idiosyncratic case. If productivity-constrained potential growth proves a broader phenomenon, markets pricing aggressive global easing may be underestimating where neutral now sits. US disinflation, by contrast, continues gradually, with markets discounting two Fed cuts in the second half of 2026.

**Russia's African pivot.** Lacking the fiscal capacity of China or the US, Moscow is leveraging the Russian Orthodox Church, now present in at least 34 African countries, alongside cultural centers and scholarships. Reporting suggests the soft-power push is increasingly tied to military and industrial recruitment as Ukraine attrition continues — an inexpensive substitute for the economic statecraft Russia can no longer afford.
