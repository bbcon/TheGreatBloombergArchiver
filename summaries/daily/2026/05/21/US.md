# US Daily Briefing — May 21, 2026

**A new Fed era begins amid a hawkish pivot.** Kevin Warsh will be sworn in as Federal Reserve Chair on Friday, inheriting an FOMC that has grown markedly more hawkish since the outbreak of the Iran war redrew the inflation landscape. Minutes from Jerome Powell's final meeting indicate "almost all participants" expect upward pressure on inflation from energy and supply-chain disruptions to persist, while "many" preferred dropping the easing bias from the statement altogether. A majority signaled some firming may be appropriate if inflation runs persistently above 2%. Markets have repriced accordingly: fed funds futures now imply rates topping 4% next summer, versus 2.8% priced in late February, with traders assigning roughly 70% odds to a quarter-point hike by December. Warsh, nominated as a dove, appears positioned to cap rates rather than cut them — Standard Chartered's Steve Englander notes he may lean on trimmed-mean inflation and unit labor costs to delay hikes, though a credible case for cuts remains elusive given the energy shock. Jamie Dimon, speaking to Bloomberg Television, warned rates "could be much higher than they are today."

**Nvidia delivers, but SpaceX steals the narrative.** Nvidia reported 85% revenue growth, 62% gross margins, and authorized an additional $80 billion buyback — among the largest capital return commitments on record. The stock nonetheless dipped in extended trading as the forward guidance underwhelmed elevated expectations. Attention shifted to SpaceX's public IPO filing, targeting up to $75 billion in proceeds via Nasdaq under ticker SPCX. The prospectus disclosed $18.7 billion in 2025 revenue and a $4.94 billion loss, alongside a super-voting structure preserving Elon Musk's control and performance milestones tied to a Mars colony. OpenAI is reportedly preparing its own filing within weeks, with Anthropic potentially following in October. The 30-year Treasury yield touched levels last seen in 2007, and the 2-year reached its highest since February 2025, complicating the equity backdrop.

**Microeconomic strain from immigration enforcement.** Brookings data show 82% of Los Angeles County businesses report negative impacts from ICE operations, with 44% losing more than half their revenue; in Chicago, 95% of businesses in affected corridors report declining sales. While the aggregate GDP effect remains modest, the concentrated damage to small firms may serve as an early indicator of broader policy-driven disruption. Separately, Moody's downgraded Mexico to one notch above junk, citing fiscal deterioration — a development with implications for North American credit and trade exposures.
