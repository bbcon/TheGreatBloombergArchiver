**Wall Street's 2026 Consensus: Structural Tensions Beneath Constructive Surface**

The sell-side's aggregated 2026 outlooks, synthesized across more than 60 institutions, establish a cautiously constructive baseline for global risk assets — but one containing genuine internal tensions that are likely to generate recurring volatility rather than a smooth directional trend. The analytical centerpiece across most house views is the unusual configuration of AI-driven secular investment and government fiscal support extending an aging expansion. This dynamic supplies growth impulse that, by conventional late-cycle metrics, should be moderating. The corollary is that disinflation stalls short of central bank targets, leaving monetary policy less accommodative than rates markets price. The dollar-bearish consensus — grounded in narrowing rate differentials and reserve diversification — sits in partial contradiction with this sticky-inflation view; if the Fed has less room to cut than expected, the case for rapid dollar depreciation becomes less clean. Investors should treat these narratives as competing rather than mutually reinforcing, with the data flow determining which dominates in any given quarter. Global trade is flagged as likely to remain difficult in 2026, with unresolved tariff regimes and supply-chain reconfiguration sustaining policy-shock risk alongside cyclical data.

**Venezuela: Energy Supply and EM Restructuring Dynamics**

The most consequential cross-regional development of the week was Venezuela's abrupt return to investable status following Maduro's ouster. Sovereign and PDVSA bondholders captured roughly $4 billion in single-day mark-to-market gains, underpinned by restructuring expectations rather than near-term fundamentals. President Trump's pledge of US capital deployment into Venezuelan oil infrastructure — with energy majors and private capital beginning preliminary assessments — represents a potential multi-year supply-side development of modest but directional global significance. Venezuela holds the world's largest proved crude reserves, though current output is a fraction of its early-2000s peak. A credible reinvestment program would, over time, add incremental heavy-sour barrels, applying marginal pressure to the medium-term oil curve and benefiting refiners configured for that grade. The near-term price impact appears limited given the lead times involved. Material uncertainties include the political transition's durability, the mechanics of sanctions relief, layered creditor claims from defaulted sovereign and PDVSA debt, and the competing positions of Chinese and Russian creditors whose cooperation — or lack thereof — will shape any restructuring outcome. The interaction between a US-backed reconstruction framework and OPEC+ supply discipline is a dynamic worth monitoring as the year progresses.
