Released From Geography

One of the most popular and readable non-fiction books of recent times was  
Tim Marshall <https://bloom.bg/3PTyQxU>’s  Prisoners of Geography: Ten Maps 
That Tell You Everything You Need to Know About the World 
<https://bloom.bg/4dJGEeK>. The title is self-explanatory. Beautifully clear 
and concise prose, accompanied by maps, it lays out just how many of the 
world’s most intractable political and economic problems are imposed by the 
immutable facts of geography (asNapoleon <https://bloom.bg/4o1oxnT> so aptly 
observed but failed to take to heart). If anyone thought they could ignore 
this, the global fascination with the Strait of Hormuz over the last three 
months is a potent argument otherwise.

This is a track of the number of stories crossing the Bloomberg terminal, from 
all sources, to mention the Strait <https://bloom.bg/4dUD9Cp> each day:

On the face of it, events there have degenerated into a disaster, with both 
sides blockading the other. Bloomberg’s analysis of shipping through the 
waterway (which you can find in the fascinating terminal function MAP SHIP 
<GO>) suggests that it’s to all intents and purposes closed, and has been for 
three months. Crude and gasoline prices are rising accordingly, and hopes for a 
reopening in June are dwindling on prediction markets. A dashboard basically 
describes what strategists would have called a nightmare scenario in early 
March:

And yet the  US stock market <https://bloom.bg/4x3YwZh> is on quite a tear. 
The S&P 500 was up on Tuesday, for the ninth trading day in succession. That’s 
because advances in technology have effectively freed us from geography. 
They’ve also, fascinatingly, rendered geography almost obsolete when it comes 
to placing investments. Where a company is based is ever less important.

The driving force is technology, which is concentrated — although not 
exclusively — in the US. The IT sector has outpaced the rest of the market for 
most of the time since the excesses of the dot-com bubble were finally 
extinguished about two decades ago. But while tech outperformance has become 
the norm, what’s happening now is completely abnormal:

Those reading this on the terminal might want to open the chart in GP, so that 
you can then view it on a log scale <https://bloom.bg/4eiD3Et>. The linear 
scale tends to exaggerate the recent movements, but this is still the strongest 
relative period for tech this century so far. This shows tech’s rolling 
three-month outperformance of the S&P 500 excluding tech. At 33%, the latest 
quarter is the best on record:

Its previous best quarter came in the three months after the 9/11 terrorist 
attacks in 2001, as tech was relatively immune to the geopolitical pain of the 
time. Something similar is at work now, as the closure of Hormuz endangers 
supply of materials necessary for making semiconductors. Add possible 
restricted supply to massive increases in demand and, investors are 
calculating, prices should go through the roof.

A week ago, Points of Return concentrated on the remarkable fact that Micron 
Technology Inc. had just topped a trillion dollars in market cap. Seven days is 
a long time. It’s now topped $1.2 trillion. The last week’s growth is 
equivalent to the entire market valuation of companies like McDonald’s Corp. or 
Verizon Communications Inc.

What’s interesting is that this effect doesn’t respect geography. Since the 
2024 presidential election, the IT sector for the world as a whole has done far 
better than the US IT index alone, according to MSCI:

This is because the US is particularly dominant in software groups, which are 
perceived to be likely victims of AI and have endured a major selloff. Tech 
sectors elsewhere can luxuriate in the demand for chips.

Focusing on the semiconductor sector itself, there are other big players, 
particularly in South Korea and Taiwan. The US industry has done no better than 
global semiconductors as a whole:

The US stock market has emerged from the war’s first three months in far 
better shape than the rest of the developed world. There appears to be a clear 
geographical reason for this: The US isn’t dependent on imported oil via the 
Strait while Europe and the Far East are deeply exposed. But US outperformance 
during that time proves to be entirely attributable to its tech sector. Exclude 
it, and the S&P is below its level from three months ago and has barely made up 
any ground on the EAFE (Europe, Australia and the Far East) index:

As the German commercials used to say <https://bloom.bg/4o1iShG>, progress is 
through technology <https://bloom.bg/4fX9SrT> — and geography can’t hold it 
back. No amount of technical advance can keep any market advancing as quickly 
as this forever. But for now, the strength is overwhelming and helping to tide 
capital markets through some dire straits.

Of JOLTS and Jobpocalypses

Fear of a “jobpocalypse” driven by artificial intelligence is pervasive; just  
ask any graduating student <https://bloom.bg/3RFC0G5>. Virtually every report 
on the labor market is now scrutinized for signs of job displacement. But even 
though we’re now halfway through the fourth year since the launch of ChatGPT 
marked the unofficial beginning of the generative AI era, a meltdown in 
employment isn’t showing up in the official data.

It could just be a matter of time. A Bloomberg Economics analysis based on AI 
exposures to about 20,000 tasks across occupations suggests that 27% of workers 
in advanced economies, or more than 120 million in over 30 countries covered, 
are likely to be meaningfully affected by AI:

Reports of layoffs, particularly in the tech space, appear to reinforce the 
job-displacement hypothesis. Bloomberg Intelligence’s analysis shows that 
AI-driven disruption drove about 55,000 planned layoffs in 2025, representing 
approximately 5% of total job cuts last year. It ranked fifth among all cited 
reasons.

But this is inconclusive. Anecdotal evidence cited as proof of disruption is, 
after scrutiny, often dismissed as possibleAI-washing <https://bloom.bg/3RBBNUn>
. And for all the anxiety surrounding artificial intelligence, broad labor 
measures show no meaningful signs of displacement. If anything, the latest Job 
Openings and Labor Turnover Survey (JOLTS), for April, continues topaint a 
picture of a remarkably healthy jobs market <https://bloom.bg/4x1jjwJ>. 
Tuesday’s print shows vacancies surging back to their highest pre-pandemic 
level. That doesn’t support any notion of an AI takeover:

Even taking population growth since the turn of the century into account, the 
latest JOLTS show the US once again has more than one vacancy for each 
unemployed worker. Whatever the threats from AI and the discontent of 
graduating students, the labor market remains firmly tilted in favor of job 
seekers:

For all this resilience, however, it’s far too early to rule out meaningful 
displacement. Previous industrial revolutions took decades before their full 
employment impact was felt. That lag between technological innovation and an 
eventual hit to jobs has shortened, with each successive upheaval accelerating 
the pace at which certain occupations are transformed or rendered obsolete — 
but there’s still a lag.

As Points of Return noted here <>, roles like software engineering have been 
transformed by agentic AI systems that can perform many entry-level roles with 
little to no supervision. But such changes are likely concentrated in specific 
sectors and unlikely to shift the overall aggregate.

Humanoids and robotics remain far from their full potential, and it remains 
unclear whether they will ultimately complement human workers or compete with 
them. Over history, human ingenuity tends to prevail; the technological forces 
that render some jobs obsolete often give rise to new occupations that were 
previously inconceivable.

BCA Research’s Dhaval Joshi channels Schumpeter’s notion of  creative 
destruction <https://bloom.bg/3RM5LVF> to argue that while every generation 
believes it is living through exceptional change, humans repeatedly adapt. Jobs 
like lamplighters,  ostlers <https://bloom.bg/4xnCIIv>, livery-stable keepers, 
newspaper criers and boiler firemen once dominated.

In 1910, one-third of workers were employed on farms, and by the end of the 
century, that proportion was near zero. In 1950, one-fifth comprised machine or 
vehicle operatives; by the end of the century, the proportion was less than a 
10th. In 1970, over 5% of the workforce were stenographers, typists or 
secretaries; yet these jobs are almost nonexistent today:

Green shoots of the job market’s next evolution are emerging. Revelio Labs, a 
work intelligence platform, shows that the need for core data-center jobs has 
grown sharply since the launch of ChatGPT, with demand for the construction 
workers building them (a segment that will presumably decline again once the 
infrastructure has been completed) rising over 430%.

Wall Street’s greatest powers are lined up behind the notion that AI will 
displace a huge number of jobs — but also, conveniently, create new posts to 
replace all of them. Goldman Sachs researchers estimate that AI could automate 
25% of current work hours over the next decade, with white-collar occupations 
facing the greatest exposure, butCEO David Solomon argues in the New York Times 
<https://bloom.bg/4ektsgs> that this will be offset by new jobs that the 
technology will enable:

When I was a first-year banking analyst, something as simple as making a graph 
of a stock’s performance took six hours of looking up prices in back issues of 
The Wall Street Journal on microfiche. Today, a first-year analyst can do it in 
seconds, and we have employed more people than ever in recent years. With more 
sophisticated tools, the complexity of our work naturally expands. Do any of us 
feel like we have less to do these days despite the convenience of Excel, email 
or Zoom?

It’s possible that AI will have to wait for a recession before employers feel 
determined to use it to eliminate jobs — and as Solomon says, human ingenuity 
will probably use the new technology to create new roles. The jobpocalypse 
certainly hasn’t started yet. But all these points, valid though they are, 
still have to contend with genuine anxiety that spreads from graduating 
students all the way tothe Pope <https://bloom.bg/49xEPiC>. 

—Richard Abbey
