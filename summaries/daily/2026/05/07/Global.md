The central geopolitical and market-moving development of the day is the tentative US-Iran diplomatic framework that has circulated in the form of a one-page memorandum of understanding. The proposal calls for Tehran to reopen the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for Washington ending its blockade of Iranian ports and lifting some sanctions, with Iran agreeing to a moratorium on uranium enrichment. Broader nuclear questions — the duration of the moratorium and disposition of Iran's highly-enriched uranium stockpile — would be negotiated subsequently. Brent crude fell sharply on the news before paring losses after Tehran indicated a deal was not imminent; the commodity remains above $99 per barrel, having declined roughly 12% over the prior two sessions but still elevated relative to pre-conflict levels.

Market participants appear to be pricing something between a near-term transit reopening and a comprehensive settlement — a distinction that matters considerably for the path of energy prices, inflation expectations, and central bank policy globally. Analysts note that even a narrow deal restoring shipping flows would meaningfully relieve pressure on fuel, fertilizers, plastics, and a range of intermediate goods now experiencing shortages. European industrial sectors — aerospace, autos, broader manufacturing — stand to benefit disproportionately given the continent has borne the heaviest economic burden from the disruption.

Supply chain infrastructure is adapting in parallel with diplomacy. DP World has introduced end-to-end war risk insurance covering ocean freight, air freight, inland haulage, and storage for cargo traversing the region — an acknowledgment that perceived risk will persist even if a deal materializes. The New York Fed's global supply chain pressure index reached its highest reading since July 2022 in April. Meanwhile, the Iran conflict is accelerating green bond issuance in emerging markets, with first-quarter volumes rising 53% year-on-year to $24 billion as energy-import-dependent economies seek to accelerate renewables deployment — a structural consequence of the shock that may outlast any near-term diplomatic resolution.
