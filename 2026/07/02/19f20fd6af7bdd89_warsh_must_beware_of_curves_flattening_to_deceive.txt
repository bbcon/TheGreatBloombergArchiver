Scintillating Sintra

Kevin Warsh certainly isn’t taking anything for granted, but he was justified 
in telling thecentral banking conference in Sintra, Portugal 
<https://bloom.bg/4v61VVe>, that his chairmanship of the Federal Reserve is 
going according to plan so far. “Expectations of inflation over the first four 
weeks of this period have come down,” he said, and “inflation risks have come 
down.”

This is accurate, and owes at least as much to the fall in oil prices as to 
market surprise that he is setting himself up as an inflation hawk. He also did 
a good job ofsounding markedly different <https://bloom.bg/3Tess5m> from his 
predecessors, and without in any way coming over as rude to his European hosts 
— a trick that American politicians who have visited recently havenotably 
failed to manage <https://bloom.bg/4v84032>.

But while he’s right that the bond market <https://bloom.bg/44HZwoW> is taking 
him seriously and dialing down inflation expectations, that’s not necessarily 
great news. Theyield curve <https://bloom.bg/4gLKZzS> — the amount by which 
yields on longer-dated Treasuries exceed shorter-dated bonds — is reducing, in 
what is known on markets as a “bear flattening.”

To explain this, Mike Howell of Global Liquidity Indexes in London argues as 
follows:

The US yield curve is flattening, but not in a benign way. In liquidity terms, 
a flatter curve is consistent with tighter conditions. Less liquidity raises 
systemic risks and so increases the demand for longer-dated ‘safe’ government 
debt. The problem is that this implies a bearish flattening... It points to a 
market increasingly focused on ‘higher-for-longer’ policy risk rather than on a 
straightforward growth and inflation slowdown.

The rise in two-year yields since inflation fears were revived by the Iran war 
has been much sharper than in 10-year yields:

A further reason for rising bond yields is the strength of the incoming data, 
which continue to offer scant reason for rate cuts. The latest JOLTS (Job 
Openings and Labor Turnover Survey) numbers, for May, show vacancies now above 
their pre-pandemic high from 2019:

AI may well eventually have a profoundly negative effect on the demand for 
human labor, but for the time being, workers are in a strong position in wage 
negotiations (meaning inflationary risks) and the economy seems buoyant. That’s 
good news, and suggests that all the Fed needs to do, as Warsh said repeatedly 
at Sintra, is keep a lid on inflation. Which, all else equal, means rates are 
more likely to be higher for longer. It could also lead to a stronger dollar 
than currently expected, particularly as Warsh’s counterparts from other 
central banks sound less likely to raise rates.

Warsh may well be right that the current odd combination of falling inflation 
expectations and rising rate forecasts shows confidence in the Fed. But it also 
suggests that rates will indeed be higher for longer. That would tighten 
financial conditions and create its own risks.

Barring a sudden easing of inflation pressure, Howell suggests that the US 
might be tempted to concentrate Treasury issuance in short-dated bonds (a 
policy they’ve already been following). That would lean downward on longer 
yields “but it would also risk accelerating monetary growth, as bank purchases 
of debt are a form of monetization.” And Warsh has made plain hisopposition to 
conducting monetary policy through bond purchases <https://bloom.bg/4v60Bl6>, 
rather than through setting interest rates.

Alternatively, the Treasury could step up its program of buying back bonds, 
which has had the effect of reducing bond market volatility (and thus easing 
financial conditions by making it easier to use bonds for collateral). Howell 
has long suggested that the US is deliberately trying to keep down the MOVE 
index of bond volatility. He has circumstantial evidence on his side from its 
remarkably steady decline following the Liberation Day tariffs last year:

But such policies are extremely vulnerable to shocks. Warsh has done a good 
job of establishing his credentials so far, but he now needs to show that he’s 
prepared to let conditions tighten gradually (whatever the president who 
nominated him thinks of this) to avoid the risk of a major bond dislocation 
later. Freya Beamish and Davide Oneglia of TSLombard argued:

The key question is whether Warsh is going to fight the trends of the new 
macro regime or flow with them. If Warsh chooses to fight, delaying necessary 
rate adjustments will likely force the Fed to hike a lot more down the line. 
This would have major implications for our views on the curve flattening we 
expect, it could break the back of the AI equity bull next year with potential 
negative spillovers on the economy, and it would adversely impact the extent of 
the dollar rally. 

Howell agrees, arguing that suppressing the curve today might merely store up 
a larger bond-market shock tomorrow: “Policymakers should allow the US 
liquidity cycle to contract naturally. Fed, don’t fight the curve.”

So-So <https://bloom.bg/4vK0zAP> Beijing

Chinese equities have conspicuously lacked resilience in recent weeks as the 
Iran war exposed the fragility of a rally that once looked difficult to derail. 
The advance sparked by the Communist Party’s“whatever it takes” moment 
<https://bloom.bg/4wlMbhN> nearly two years ago is now giving way to a renewed 
selloff, just as consumer sentiment sinks to record lows and demand remains 
stubbornly weak. The slump has the trappings of the broader selloff in global 
tech stocks but goes beyond that.

The offshore Hang Seng China Enterprises Index (or “red chips”) fell into a 
technical bear market last week after tumbling more than 20% from its October 
peak. This might be linked to Beijing's late-May crackdown on several Hong 
Kong-based online brokers, which reinforced concerns that policymakers are 
increasingly determined to keep Chinese savings at home. Gavekal Research’s 
Louis-Vincent Gave argues that the move possibly reflected growing fears of 
capital flight as domestic interest rates continue to grind lower — an issue 
that for many years has bedeviled Japan, which these days has higher yields. 
The risk of this happening is exacerbated by the upward turn in yields 
elsewhere.

But China’s stock market downturn also reflects concerns about the pace of 
Beijing’s economic recovery and whether it has enough momentum to translate 
into earnings growth, particularly for technology and consumer-oriented 
sectors. With tech stocks leading the losses, investors are rotating into 
defensive sectors:

In the two weeks since the Fed’s hawkish hold, the Penghua CSI 800 Financials 
ex Banks Exchange-Traded Fund has nudged double-digit growth. Whether this 
upside continues largely depends on external factors as much as domestic ones, 
especially given structurally weak consumption. Oxford Economics’ Cassidy 
Ainsworth-Grace projects the depressed demand to lag the export recovery, 
weighing on overall growth. Still, she doesn’t expect consumption to become the 
focus of policymaking anytime soon:

Policymakers will likely continue to focus on supply-side reform in a narrow 
set of advanced manufacturing industries that deepen industrial capabilities. 
Productivity is being generated through these investments, but this narrow lens 
means it isn’t diffusing through the household sector at a sufficient scale; in 
other words, it isn’t creating enough jobs.

Despite this subdued backdrop <https://bloom.bg/4v6inoA>, capital flows into 
China remain surprisingly robust. BNY's data show institutional investors 
continue to accumulate Chinese equities, with inflows outperforming the rest of 
Asia, where investors have been taking some of the profits from the massive 
rally in South Korean and Taiwanese computer hardware companies:
Source: BNY 
With Chinese equities down by about 15% for the year, it’s plausible that 
losses on existing holdings have more than offset the value of new purchases. 
BNY’s Geoff Yu argues that there is now a more attractive entry point, with 
stocks looking oversold while valuations remain undemanding:

Yu argued that attractive valuations, better export data, and the potential 
for further policy support if stocks sell off much more explained why foreign 
investors were still adding to Chinese holdings as share prices fell.

One reason Beijing might maintain a tight fist and not resort to widespread 
fiscal stimulus is the continued outperformance of exports, which have propped 
up growth in the wake of the housing sector’s spectacular collapse. Bank of 
America’s Helen Qiao argues that if exports lose momentum, policymakers would 
rely more actively on fiscal and quasi-fiscal tools. For now, strong exports 
have given them “room to respond” later. “This could be part of the reason we 
see weaker infrastructure-related spending in the general public expenditure.”

Meanwhile, the wait for the elusive fiscal stimulus continues. China’s 
reluctance to unleash widespread support for domestic consumption, even as it 
relies heavily on exports, leaves investors betting more on attractive 
valuations than on an imminent economic turnaround.

If there’s any lesson from the rally that followed the 2024 whatever-it-takes 
pivot <https://bloom.bg/449g355>, it’s that until policymakers deliver a 
stronger demand-side response, the equity market will likely continue appealing 
to bargain hunters without sustaining a durable rally.

—Richard Abbey
