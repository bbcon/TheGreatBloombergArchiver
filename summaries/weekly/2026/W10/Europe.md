**Equity Markets and Energy Shock**
European equity markets experienced one of their most volatile weeks of the year. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 1.45% Monday, declined a further 3.16% Tuesday as banks and insurers led the selloff, then partially recovered Wednesday — rising 1.3% on reports of indirect Iran-US ceasefire contact — before ending the week under mild pressure, down 0.28% Friday. The underlying driver was energy: European natural gas futures surged as much as 52% in a single session Monday — the largest intraday gain since the 2022 crisis — following Iranian drone attacks on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility, the world's largest export plant. By week's end, European gas prices had risen more than 60% from pre-conflict levels, breaching €50 per megawatt-hour. Bloomberg Economics estimated sustained high energy prices could push Europe to the brink of recession, while Goldman Sachs modeled a month-long Hormuz closure as potentially more than doubling European gas prices.

**UK Politics and Fiscal Policy**
The UK navigated a particularly complex week. Chancellor Rachel Reeves delivered her spring fiscal statement against an unusually turbulent backdrop; tax receipts came in approximately £22 billion ahead of Office for Budget Responsibility forecasts, driven by stronger-than-expected income tax and VAT revenues, reducing gilt issuance pressure. However, the statement was overshadowed by geopolitical friction. Prime Minister Starmer sharpened his distancing from US military action, stating Britain does not support "regime change from the skies" and questioning the legal basis for the strikes. President Trump publicly criticized Starmer for refusing a US request to use British military bases for Iran operations, adding bilateral friction to an already complicated alliance. Despite the political difficulty, Citigroup upgraded UK equities to overweight from underweight — citing its structural tilt toward energy, commodities, aerospace and defense, and defensive sectors as an effective geopolitical hedge — and the FTSE 100 outperformed materially on Monday, falling less than 1% versus broader European declines. Separately, the OBR indicated meeting Labour's commitment to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035 would require approximately £40 billion in additional outlays.

**ECB Policy Dilemma and Individual Economies**
The European Central Bank faces an increasingly uncomfortable stagflationary tension. Bundesbank President Nagel stated Thursday that the inflation impact of the Iran war currently outweighs the growth drag — a meaningful signal given the ECB's recent easing posture — and traders moved to fully price in a 2026 rate hike. Euro-area fourth-quarter GDP was simultaneously revised lower, with trade weighing on output. The euro declined roughly 2% against the dollar over the week. Spain emerged as a diplomatic flashpoint after Prime Minister Sánchez denied Washington access to military bases; Trump threatened to cut off all trade with Spain and to terminate LNG exports, with Spanish equities falling to their lowest in nearly three months. Italian fiscal credibility received a modest setback after data showed it failed to meet the EU's 3% deficit ceiling last year. Germany provided a rare constructive datapoint: strong solar generation fed over 40 gigawatts into the grid for five consecutive days in March, helping cap domestic electricity prices and reinforcing policy arguments for accelerated energy transition investment. In corporate news, Adidas fell 7.6% in Frankfurt after its profit forecast missed expectations on US tariffs and currency headwinds, while Novo Nordisk rose 3.5% after US regulators moved against copycat GLP-1 drugs.
