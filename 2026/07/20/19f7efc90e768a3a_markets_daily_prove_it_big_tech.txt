Time to prove it, Big Tech

Earnings reports over the next two weeks from the biggest AI spenders will be 
scoured for evidence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-19/big-tech-needs-to-justify-ai-spending-as-investors-dump-stocks?cmpid=072026_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6734>
 that the investments are generating bigger returns.

Alphabet and Tesla kick off Big Tech’s reporting season on Wednesday. 
Microsoft, Meta, Apple and Amazon hit the following week. Together, the stocks 
account for a quarter of the market capitalization-weighted S&P 500.

The stakes are high. An index tracking the Magnificent Seven tech giants is 
underperforming the S&P 500 this year. At the same time, concerns about 
spending are hitting semiconductor stocks, which have benefited most from the 
outlays.

“Investors are getting to the point where they’re uncomfortable with how much 
money is being spent and they’re worried about a bubble,” said Jake Seltz of 
Allspring Global Investments.

The first big test is Alphabet. The Google parent is widely seen as an AI 
winner owing to the popularity of its Gemini chatbot, homegrown data-center 
chips and expansion in its cloud-computing business.

But growth has come at a cost. Alphabet’s capital spending is projected to 
more than double this year to $187 billion, and like many of its peers it’s 
increasingly turning to debt and equity markets to fund it. The stock is up 11% 
this year, but has fallen 14% from a May peak. —Jeran Wittenstein

Wall Street alchemy

Creative bankers and advisers are devising all sorts of ways to unlock cash 
tied up in private assets.

One product, pitched by UBS, is emblematic of the rapidly evolving realm of 
fund finance. It bundles stakes in private credit funds into a bond, stamped 
with a target grade of A2 from Moody’s, analogous to the level of risk awarded 
to multibillion-dollar companies such as Nike and Home Depot.

What makes it look so solid? A rare kind of insurance provided by Nationwide 
Mutual Insurance, known to Americans for its “on your side” jingle.

In just a few years, fund finance has roared to life as Wall Street’s most 
active laboratory — bundling up assets, slicing them into tranches of varying 
size and risk, and weaving in other innovations. Often, the goal is to create 
investment-grade debt that can be sold to insurers, annuity providers and 
pension systems.
