Becoming a grandfather is reinvigorating because your focus again turns to the 
future and all the hope you have for the new life just begun. That’s the theory 
I’m embracing at least, having welcomed my first grandchild into the world last 
week. Alongside, of course, a new boyish desire to dive once more into all the 
things from my own childhood that my grandson will soon enjoy, from fishing and 
sailing to beach cricket and backyard wiffle ball.

Though I’m far from the only one thinking about the future these days. Even as 
the new chair of the Federal Reserve is dismissing the need for “forward 
guidance” on monetary policy, a lot of eminent folks have offered their vision 
of the future shape of the global economy recently.

In a summer flurry of op-eds, speeches, academic papers and Substack posts, 
Trump Cabinet members, economists and former presidential advisers have offered 
not just a justification for where we are but some confident predictions of 
what comes next.

In a June 23rd speech <https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0539> 
at the Economic Club of New York and accompanying Wall Street Journalop-ed 
<https://www.wsj.com/opinion/scott-bessent-hamilton-inspires-trumps-economic-statecraft-4a2786dd?st=Sdx3Sv&reflink=article_copyURL_share)>
, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent laid out five principles his Treasury 
adhered to in its pursuit of the Trump America First doctrine, invoking 
Alexander Hamilton liberally throughout.

The first is what you might call strategic autarky, though Bessent calls it a 
pursuit of national capacity.
Scott Bessent, US Treasury secretary, during an Economic Club of New York 
event on June 23.Photographer: Krisanne Johnson/Bloomberg
“Our strength is derived from what we can build, for the nation that can’t 
produce what it needs isn’t truly secure. The nation that depends on its 
adversaries for critical inputs isn’t truly sovereign. And the nation that 
reduces its economics to consumption isn’t truly prosperous.”

Second, third and fourth are principles related to America’s place in the 
world. Economic relationships must be “reciprocal,” “America must write the 
rules of the next economy,” and the dollar and the power that comes with its 
dominant status are essential instruments of statecraft.

Fifth is what reads like a political assertion that “economic statecraft must 
serve the American people.” By which he also means the US depends on its human 
capital.

Security First

All are worthy principles and might have been laid out by any Treasury 
secretary from either political party at any point in recent history. And yet 
if you are pondering the future it’s also fair to ask just how genuine the 
Trump administration’s actions have been in pursuing them.

In an op-ed 
<https://www.nytimes.com/2026/07/07/opinion/scott-bessent-trade-economy.html?smid=nytcore-ios-share>
 last week in the New York Times, former PIMCO CEO and now Wharton professor 
Mohamed El-Erian called Bessent’s speech “remarkably important” and dubbed it 
the Bessent Doctrine. It marked, he wrote, a turn from economic policy driven 
by business interests to “one in which considerations of national security, 
domestic politics and geopolitics no longer play second fiddle to traditional 
business interests in determining corporate and economic outcomes. Those 
business interests are now being actively sidelined.”

“Whether you are a country, a company, a household or an investor, your 
well-being is increasingly dictated by a fundamentally different calculus,” 
El-Erian, who is also a Bloomberg Opinioncolumnist 
<https://bloomberg.com/authors/ABsGWPqZF9s/mohamed-elerian?cmpid=BBD071326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6475>
, added.

So what follows the vision we are living through now? In a July 10 Substack 
post 
<https://open.substack.com/pub/peterharrell/p/donald-trump-trade-order-builder?r=zlx1&utm_medium=ios>
, Peter Harrell, who served as the lead international economics mind on 
President Joe Biden’s National Security Council, offers his prediction for what 
will come after Trump.

Placing Bets

Which, he argues, is likely to be a version of Trump’s approach to economic 
relationships and its dependence on tariffs and bilateral deals.

“If I had a Kalshi account and wanted to gamble, I’d bet on adjusting Trump’s 
architecture,” he writes. Though Harrell also thinks the next president will 
lower Trump’s unpopular tariffs.

Is this future world we’re contemplating actually going to turn out to be good 
for the people who live in it?

Time will tell. But in a new paper <https://www.nber.org/papers/w35416> three 
Stanford economists and a Princeton one use data from 106 countries from 1993 
to 2017 to model the effects of nations responding to growing global 
uncertainty by investing in “resilience” and domestic capacity much in the way 
Bessent declares the US has to.

Their findings are predictably nuanced. The broad takeaway, though, is that 
countries that bet on global links to improve their resilience improve the 
welfare of their people and that those who don’t see the opposite effect. And 
that countries less dependent on the US might actually do better in the future.

In other words: This new world might end up being worse for its people than 
the one it’s setting out to replace.

It’s already been a long hot summer in the US and Europe. Which means the 
immediate priority for many is air conditioning rather than a new global order. 
And keeping cool might be a healthier pre-occupation. The beach reading this 
summer points pretty clearly to a future as unsettled as the age we are living 
in now.

Oh well. At least my new grandson and I still have baseball.
