King Dollar

In a  conflict with few winners <https://bloom.bg/3O2pSxo>, the dollar’s 
surviving hegemony stands out. The greenback has risen 2.2% since the US and 
Israel began their coordinated attacks on Iran. That’s consequential after the 
world’s reserve currency dropped more than 8% last year — but despite 
appearances, it’s premature to declare this a turnaround.

First, the dollar’s weakness going into the war broadly aligned with 
Washington’s desire to boost export competitiveness. Treasury Secretary Scott 
Bessent had made a weaker dollar an explicit aim. The dollar’s uptick has been 
an unintended consequence, just as rising oil prices have dented the 
affordability drive. For now, it’s not clear that this is a paradigm shift:

Second, the six-week rally followed an awful start to 2026. The dollar is up 
only about 0.9% year-to-date. The question is whether the recent resurgence — 
not surprising at a time when heightened risk aversion prompted investors to 
seek shelter — has enough strength to carry through the rest of the year.

The wartime rally has at least put to rest any doubts about the dollar’s 
“safe-haven” status. Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution illustrates this 
vote of confidence with a chart of foreign capital flows into Treasuries, 
corporate debt and equities, which are at their strongest in recent years:
Source: Institute of International Finance 
Regardless, Brooks maintains a bearish view of the dollar, projecting a 10% 
fall in trade-weighted terms this year. This isn’t about any loss of reserve 
currency status, but is driven by an increasingly politicized Federal Reserve, 
which will cut rates in the face of high and rising inflation:

…That means a regime <https://bloom.bg/3QoD5Bd> change for the dollar, whereby 
strong growth will coincide with a falling greenback. That’s similar to the 
years after the global financial crisis, when Fed quantitative easing capped 
nominal yields in the face of strong data, pushing real yields down and 
weighing on the dollar. US reserve currency status is just fine, but the dollar 
will fall anyway.

Ultimately, concerns about whether a prolonged Middle East conflict or other 
factors act as a tailwind to the dollar are legitimate. However, as rosy as the 
greenback’s performance against its peers appears to be, Steve Englander of 
Standard Chartered Bank notes that it is nothing short of extraordinary if 
measured through the lens of implied volatility:

That relative calm is also showing up in the euro, which came under pressure 
when the conflict broke out. It now appears to have bottomed out at least two 
weeks ago, even without an off-ramp for the war and with oil prices largely 
staying above $100 per barrel:

This muted volatility mirrors experience of the 2022 energy shock after 
Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Volatility shot up as energy concerns mounted 
ahead of winter, and subsided when supplies proved just enough. As things 
stand, Englander argues that the calm reflects the energy markets’ expectations 
for a return to normalcy by the end of the year.

If the Brent futures market projection — which expects oil to trade just over 
$80 per barrel in the next 6 to 12 months — holds, he argues that the euro 
should stay within its current range. If oil prices stay near current levels 
for a while, that would renew pressure on the euro.

None of this means that the dollar can avoid some long-term damage. Far from 
that. My Bloomberg Opinion colleague Aaron Brownpoints to 
<https://bloom.bg/4cuPzzO> several ways that the Iran conflict has punctured 
the decades-old, reliable petrodollar system. Typically during an oil shock, 
the Gulf states benefit from the surge in prices, and pour their windfall into 
US Treasury bonds.

This time, Iran’s closure of the Strait of Hormuz and subsequent attacks on 
oil infrastructure mean that these oil producers are not only cutting back on 
production, but also selling US Treasuries to generate the funds to defend 
their under-pressure domestic currencies and underwrite other wartime 
expenditures. So far, foreign central banks’ holdings at the Federal Reserve 
Bank of New York have dropped by roughly $82 billion <https://bloom.bg/4dnYSma> 
to $2.7 trillion since late February, the lowest level since 2012. Reserve 
currency status remains safe, thanks to the lack of competitors, but the war is 
taking further chips out of its foundations.

—Richard Abbey

Jamie & the Cockroaches — Part II

JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon has a way with words. He singlehandedly added the 
cockroach to the financial menagerie six months ago, when he reacted to a 
couple of alarming events in private credit by warning that “when you see one 
cockroach, there are probably more <https://bloom.bg/4vfW6WG>.”

That provoked an angry response from Marc Lipschultz, CEO of Blue Owl Capital, 
who suggested that Dimon was talking his own book. “I guess he’s saying there 
might bea lot more cockroaches at JPMorgan <https://bloom.bg/4vuC5fc>, I’m not 
sure I know what he’s saying,” he said at the time. “It’s not a private credit 
issue. It’s a liquid credit market.”

Now Dimon has published his annual letter to shareholders 
<https://bloom.bg/3NQRW6R>, with more forceful thoughts on private credit, on 
the same day thatBlue Owl’s shares dropped back to the level of their IPO 
<https://bloom.bg/4vcNe3U> in 2020. The company’s stock is down 49% since 
Lipschultz’s retort to Dimon:

This is happening as economic growth continues unabated, with relatively easy 
financial conditions. Credit spreads in the public markets are tight. But 
investors want to pull their money from private credit. The asset class is 
designed to be relatively illiquid, and that’s forced a crisis for lenders as 
they decide whether to honor redemptions. In this respect, investors are 
behaving as though they’re stamping everywhere on the kitchen floor to 
eradicate cockroaches.

Blue Owl also has the particular problem of a lot of loans to software groups 
on its books. That sector suffered a brutal rerating in the month before war 
broke out, as investors bet that artificial intelligence would put a serious 
dent in software companies’ profit margins. The fall in their premium to the 
market was dramatic:

That’s bad for shareholders, but it’s not clear lenders, who have seniority, 
should take a similar hit, unless software businesses are driven into outright 
default. This isn’t an issue of solvency so much as liquidity. True crises 
happen when the two combine. For now, this looks like an incipient bank run, 
when everyone wants their money at once.

Dimon managed to be reassuring on this score while continuing to talk down 
private credit, which “in the great scheme of things, probably does not present 
a systemic risk.” That’s mostly because of size. Both the markets for 
securitized mortgages and investment-grade bonds are some 10 times the size of 
leveraged private credit. However, he continued:

I do believe that when we have a credit cycle, which will happen one day, 
losses on all leveraged lending in general will be higher than expected, 
relative to the environment. This is because credit standards have been 
modestly weakening pretty much across the board.

So he still thinks banks are more trustworthy than private credit lenders, but 
he doesn’t think we’re on the brink of a cockroach-riddled disaster. Dimon, who 
devoted much of his letter to his continuing complaint at post-GFC banking 
regulations, also suggested that a key bull point for private credit — that 
large 401(k) pension funds will soon be allowed to hold it — could actually be 
a problem:

Anything that gets sold to retail investors as opposed to institutional 
investors requires greater transparency, higher standards and fewer potential 
conflicts. If anything ever goes wrong, you should assume that retail 
investors, even though they were told about some of the risks, will seek remedy 
in the courts.

He might have added: If you’re a shareholder, you’re going to be the one 
paying for those lawsuits.


Guns and Butter

War continues in Iran with little sign of a ceasefire. The sky continues to be 
the only limit to increasing arms spending across the world. Last week the US 
administrationrequested $1.5 trillion <https://bloom.bg/3OleSey> for the 
Pentagon’s budget.

As the year started, the defense sector was a popular investment pick. Morgan 
Stanley this week reiterated rearmament as one of its main themes for the year, 
saying:

It is clear that this dynamic will “go global” in a significant way. One of 
the big drivers of alpha in global markets for the remainder of 2026, and 
likely 2027 and beyond, is a major push among many nations to eliminate 
dependence on other nations for the most important drivers of national economic 
health and prosperity, ranging from energy to national security to 
technological competitiveness.

And yet the attack on Iran provided the cue for a pause and reverse in the 
secular rise in defense stocks which started with Russia’s invasion of Ukraine 
four years ago, and became overwhelming after US vice president’saggressively 
anti-European speech <https://bloom.bg/4milTtd> at Munich in February 2025. 
Even though markets overall have been falling, aerospace and defense stocks 
have fallen even more since the bombardment of Iran began on Feb. 27:

This is driven almost entirely by sentiment, rather than fundamentals. We can 
look at this in the context of the eternal “guns versus butter 
<https://bloom.bg/48ySCoj>” debate, with the food retailing sector proxying 
butter. The US defense sector, although hampered by the idiosyncratic 
difficulties of Boeing, has climbed to a steep premium over food retailers in 
the last few years, which suffered a big reverse in the last month:

If there’s any good reason for this, beyond the ghastly cliche that we should 
“buy on the rumor and sell on the news,” it lies inasymmetry 
<https://bloom.bg/4sdXcPT>. Iran, armed primarily with cheap drones 
<https://bloom.bg/4skXIM9>, appears to be holding its own, or even winning in 
strategic terms, despite the massive US edge in weaponry. 
<https://bloom.bg/4skXIM9>Spending massively on the state-of-the-art powerful 
weapons made by the biggest contractors might not be the best way for the world 
to re-arm.

The forces behind greater arms spending will not, regrettably, go away. But it 
might make sense for investors to pause to try to learn the lessons from Iran 
and work out whether it will be the incumbents in the sector who reap the 
rewards.
