Asian markets registered broad declines on Monday, with the MSCI Asia benchmark retreating 1.6% as the implications of the US-Israeli strikes on Iran reverberated across regional financial centers. The session's most acute stress emerged in Pakistan, where the KSE-30 Index plunged 9.6% in early trade to trigger an hour-long trading halt — a reflection of Pakistan's geographic proximity to the conflict and domestic instability, with at least ten killed in clashes linked to pro-Iran protests and a concurrent declaration of "open war" against Afghanistan following cross-border strikes.

Dubai's status as the pre-eminent financial hub for high-net-worth individuals fleeing earlier geopolitical episodes made the Gulf city a focal point of risk. Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Citigroup, and Nomura instructed regional employees to work from home. The Fairmont The Palm Hotel in Dubai sustained damage in an explosion. Emirates halted all operations until Tuesday afternoon with disruption warnings extending through Thursday; Etihad and Qatar Airways followed suit. Private jet charters to Europe were reportedly reaching $350,000 as affluent travelers sought overland routes to Riyadh.

China's position warrants particular attention. Beijing called for an immediate ceasefire and is assessed to be among the most economically exposed nations given that it purchases an estimated 99% of Iranian crude exports. A complete Iranian supply outage could lift oil prices by around 20%, while a Strait of Hormuz closure poses broader LNG supply risks. Asian buyers were already contacting non-Middle Eastern suppliers for early LNG deliveries to cover potential March gaps. China's annual National People's Congress is due to convene later this week, where the economic consequences of the conflict are likely to feature prominently in policy discussions.

In Japan, the Toyota group reached a landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over the privatization of Toyota Industries, with the group offering ¥20,600 per share — a 9.6% increase from the prior bid — in a deal valued at approximately ¥6.7 trillion ($42.8 billion). A Bank of Japan deputy governor declined to signal any imminent rate increase. Separately, Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney's visit to India produced a C$2.6 billion uranium supply agreement with India and agreement on terms of reference for a bilateral trade deal, reflecting both nations' effort to reduce US dependency.
