Weekly Brief

Mar 30 – Apr 05, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 posted its worst monthly performance since September 2022, declining approximately 7.8% in March, with the index sitting roughly 8–9% below its pre-war peak entering April.
  • Average US gasoline prices crossed $4 per gallon for the first time since August 2022, prompting Georgia to implement a 60-day gas tax holiday and several other states to explore similar measures.
  • Iranian President Pezeshkian's overture toward negotiations on Tuesday triggered the S&P 500's best session in nearly a year, though the move reflected short-covering as much as fundamental reassessment.
  • Fed Chair Powell and NY Fed President Williams signaled policy is well-positioned, while bond managers at Pimco, JPMorgan Asset Management, and Columbia Threadneedle began shifting emphasis from inflation risk toward the growth-shock scenario.
  • The week marked the one-year anniversary of Liberation Day tariffs, with the administration preparing to announce a tiered steel and aluminum tariff restructuring and 100% tariffs on pharmaceutical imports reportedly imminent.

Equity Markets

US equity markets spent the week oscillating between geopolitically driven relief rallies and late-week risk reduction, a pattern that has defined trading since the Iran conflict began five weeks ago. The S&P 500 ended March down approximately 5% on the month — its worst since September 2022 — and the Nasdaq 100 sat roughly 11.5% below its October all-time high entering the week. Tuesday produced the index's best single session in nearly a year after Iranian President Pezeshkian indicated to EU officials that Tehran was open to ending hostilities, but trading desks were quick to note that the magnitude of the move owed more to a short squeeze against heavily bearish end-of-quarter positioning than to a durable shift in conviction. By Thursday, S&P 500 futures had given back roughly 1.1%, continuing the pattern of cumulative gains in the first half of each trading week followed by net losses on Thursdays and Fridays — a dynamic reflecting investor reluctance to carry risk through weekends when major geopolitical announcements have consistently occurred. Corporate earnings added modest color: Nike fell nearly 10% in premarket trading after issuing a weaker-than-expected full-year outlook, while aluminum producers Alcoa and Century Aluminum each rose approximately 8.5–8.7% in premarket Monday trade following Iran's weekend strikes on Gulf production facilities.

Monetary Policy and Fixed Income

The Federal Reserve's calibration challenge intensified through the week. Fed Chair Powell, speaking Monday, noted that longer-term inflation expectations appear anchored, and New York Fed President Williams described policy as well-positioned — language that markets read as signaling no imminent move in either direction. That posture is being tested: Treasury yields rose across the curve after Trump's Thursday address, with traders rapidly paring expectations for rate cuts as oil surged above $107. The competing pressures are genuine and unresolved — the US manufacturing ISM prices-paid subindex climbed to 78.3 in March, its highest since mid-2022, while the growth outlook is deteriorating. Bond managers at several major asset managers have begun shifting their analytical emphasis toward the demand-destruction scenario, arguing the surge in yields has run its course. The Friday nonfarm payrolls report — consensus expecting 60,000 jobs added after a 92,000 decline in February — was released when equity markets were closed, leaving the first clean read on the real economy's absorption of the shock to land into an asymmetric market open.

Fiscal and Trade Policy

This week's Liberation Day anniversary arrived against a backdrop that complicates the administration's industrial policy narrative. Foreign direct investment into the US totaled $288.4 billion in 2025, a modest decline from 2024 and the lowest of the Biden era, providing limited evidence that tariffs have catalyzed a manufacturing investment surge as claimed. Bloomberg Economics estimates that 43% of US imports — roughly $1.4 trillion in goods — currently remain tariff-free, a proportion frequently overlooked in broader commentary. The Supreme Court's February ruling that the original tariffs were illegal triggered a $170 billion refund process that adds fiscal complexity. Looking ahead, the administration's preparation of a tiered steel and aluminum tariff restructuring and reportedly imminent 100% pharmaceutical tariffs suggests the trade policy agenda remains active even as the Middle East crisis consumes political bandwidth. State-level fiscal responses to the oil shock — led by Georgia's 60-day gas tax holiday — are providing near-term consumer relief but carry revenue implications for already-stretched state budgets.

Consumer Backdrop

The household sector is entering the shock with a narrowed buffer. The savings rate declined to 4.5% from 6.4% in January 2024, and while fuel expenditure as a share of disposable income remains near historic lows at approximately 1.6%, the $4-per-gallon threshold carries significant political resonance in a midterm election year. Potentially more consequential than pump prices is the equity wealth channel: corporate equities now represent over a quarter of household net worth — a record in eight decades of Federal Reserve data — meaning that equity market performance may prove the more potent transmission mechanism to consumer spending. Public transit agencies are reporting early ridership upticks consistent with prior oil shocks, though the durability of any behavioral shift at current price levels remains uncertain. ---

Asia

  • Asian benchmark indices fell approximately 13% in March — the steepest monthly decline since October 2008 — with South Korea, Japan, and India each recording drawdowns of roughly 10%, while Chinese equities declined a comparatively contained 5.5%.
  • China's official manufacturing PMI returned to expansionary territory in March for the first time this year, though input costs surged sharply and smaller export-oriented firms reported deteriorating conditions in the private Caixin survey.
  • India faced compounding pressures: the RBI capped lenders' daily FX positions at $100 million, global funds withdrew approximately $12 billion from Indian equities since the conflict began, and LPG shortages began disrupting the food service sector.
  • Japan's spring Shunto wage negotiations secured average increases exceeding 5% for a third consecutive year, while the Nikkei surged approximately 5% on Wednesday before partially reversing after Trump's Thursday address.
  • BYD reported overseas sales of 120,083 vehicles in March, a three-month high, and raised its 2026 export target to 1.5 million units, as elevated fuel prices appear to be accelerating EV adoption globally.

China: Relative Resilience Under Pressure

China entered the week as Asia's relative outperformer, a position it largely maintained. The official manufacturing PMI returned to expansion in March, supported by ramped-up fiscal spending and AI infrastructure demand. Chinese equities declined approximately 5.5% since hostilities began — meaningfully less than the roughly 10% drawdowns recorded in South Korea, Japan, and India — a divergence that appears to reflect structural investments in renewable energy, diversified export markets, and strategic oil reserves. Beijing's policy response revealed its priorities clearly: the People's Bank of China withdrew cash from the financial system for the first time in approximately a year — a cautious move that keeps options open without signaling a pivot — while authorities instructed independent "teapot" refiners to maintain 2025 output levels or risk losing future import quotas, effectively socializing margin losses among smaller processors to ensure downstream fuel availability. On the diplomatic front, Beijing and Pakistan issued a five-point joint peace proposal calling for the restoration of Hormuz shipping, signaling that China's calculus is shifting from passive beneficiary toward active mediator as economic costs accumulate. The China-Pakistan proposal coincided with the PBOC's unusual financial system tightening, illustrating the competing demands on Chinese policymakers navigating energy security, growth support, and diplomatic positioning simultaneously.

Corporate and Technology Developments

Several significant corporate developments emerged from the region. Alibaba released its Qwen3.5-Omni multimodal model on a closed-source basis — a departure from its usual open-source practice that may signal a shift toward commercialization. Zhongji Innolight, an Nvidia optical components supplier, confidentially filed for a Hong Kong IPO targeting roughly $3 billion, illustrating continued momentum in AI-adjacent hardware even as broader sentiment remains pressured. TCL Electronics announced a $475 million acquisition of a 51% stake in a joint venture absorbing Sony's home entertainment business — a transaction that would position the combined entity above Samsung in global TV market share at approximately 16.7%, exemplifying Chinese manufacturers' strategy of acquiring premium Western brand equity to penetrate higher-margin consumer segments. President Xi Jinping also invited Kuomintang Chairwoman Cheng Li-wun to visit China from April 7–12, the first such visit by a sitting KMT leader in a decade, providing Beijing a platform to discuss Taiwan's US weapons purchases with an interlocutor who has publicly questioned her island's defense spending levels, one month ahead of Trump's scheduled Beijing summit.

Korea, Japan, and Southeast Asia

South Korea experienced the most dramatic volatility of the week. Having entered the year as the world's best-performing market on AI optimism, Seoul approached bear market territory by end-March before surging to its strongest single session since 2001 on Wednesday following Trump's negotiation signals — a move led by Samsung and SK Hynix on revived semiconductor demand sentiment. The government announced a supplementary budget to cushion households and businesses while President Lee Jae Myung deployed a $17 billion support package, as inflation nudged above the 2% target. South Korea also signaled it is considering extending driving restrictions to the general public should Brent breach $120 per barrel. Japan's spring Shunto wage negotiations secured average increases exceeding 5% for a third consecutive year, with base pay recording its largest year-on-year gain in more than 33 years, while total employment is rising despite population contraction as female participation and immigration liberalization expand the labor pool. The Nikkei fell as much as 4.5% at Monday's open before Japan's top currency official delivered his strongest intervention warning yet. Across Southeast Asia, Vietnam is emerging as a structural beneficiary of trade diversion, surpassing China as the leading US supplier of laptops and gaming consoles last year, though its exposure to the tariff policies driving that shift represents a meaningful vulnerability. The broader ASEAN manufacturing bloc saw PMI readings fall to their weakest since September, with the Philippines declaring a national emergency over energy supply. India's rupee staged its largest single-day gain in over a decade on Thursday after the RBI banned key offshore derivatives and forced the unwind of speculative positions, though structural pressure from elevated energy import costs persists. ---

Europe

  • Euro-area CPI rose to 2.5% in March — the steepest monthly acceleration since March 2022 — though the reading came in below the 2.6% consensus, and core and services inflation actually softened relative to February.
  • European equities fell 7.6% in March, the worst monthly performance in nearly four years, with LVMH, SAP, and Novo Nordisk collectively accounting for more than half of the €420 billion in market capitalization erased in Q1.
  • ICE Gasoil diesel futures broke above $200 per barrel on Thursday — the highest since 2022 — as tankers divert refined product flows toward higher-bidding Asian buyers.
  • Norway is emerging from its driest winter in decades, with snow reserves at two-decade lows and an estimated 25 terawatt-hour energy deficit, reducing electricity exports to the UK by approximately 50% year-on-year and compounding the imported energy shock.
  • Germany's leading economic institutes cut their 2026 growth forecast to less than half their prior projection, warning that potential growth may approach zero in the medium term due to demographic decline and declining hours worked.

Inflation and Monetary Policy

The March euro-area CPI print of 2.5% — below the 2.6% consensus — provided the ECB's doves with modest cover, given that the overshoot relative to February was concentrated in energy rather than core or services categories. The divergence between headline acceleration and underlying softness is the central analytical tension for the Governing Council. Bank of Italy Governor Fabio Panetta emphasized the need to prevent a wage-price spiral while keeping policy proportionate to the mandate; ECB Governing Council member François Villeroy de Galhau signaled readiness to act against unanchored inflation expectations but stopped short of indicating specific action; and ECB Executive Board member Cipollone spoke Wednesday without materially shifting the forward guidance calculus. Bloomberg Economics assessed April rate hike odds as low given the data undershoot, but the ECB's institutional memory of its slow response to the 2022 shock is clearly shaping communications toward vigilance regardless of near-term data. The Bank of England faces a structurally different exposure: regulated energy pricing and inflation-linked wages leave the UK comparatively vulnerable to energy shocks relative to euro-area peers, and a Bank of England survey showed businesses planning larger-than-normal price increases. UK house prices unexpectedly rose, suggesting consumer demand held up at the onset of the conflict, though the durability of that resilience against sustained energy cost pressure remains to be seen.

Energy Stress and Supply Disruption

The diesel market emerged as the week's most acute European stress point. ICE Gasoil futures breaking above $200 per barrel on Thursday reflected not just the Hormuz disruption but a global arbitrage dynamic: tankers loading diesel in UK waters are diverting to Australia and other high-bidding destinations rather than discharging locally, while Atlantic-crossing cargoes originally bound for Europe are rerouting to Africa. The market appears to be signaling that European buyers will need to pay more to attract supply. Norway's energy deficit compounds the picture — an estimated 25 terawatt-hour hydropower shortfall from the driest winter in two decades has reduced electricity exports to the UK by approximately 50% year-on-year and to Germany by around 40%, while Nordic prices have surged to more than four times 2025 levels in parts of the region. The dual squeeze — imported energy shock plus domestic hydropower deficit — is arriving precisely as European gas markets remain under Persian Gulf supply pressure. The EU is actively considering reactivating emergency energy measures last used after Russia's 2022 invasion, and the European Commission has proposed limited ETS adjustments to cushion industry competitiveness.

Corporate and Political Developments

LVMH shares fell 28% in the first quarter — the worst start to a year in the company's history — serving as a proxy for deteriorating global consumer confidence among high-net-worth cohorts. Unilever announced advanced talks to sell most of its food business to McCormick, with a final announcement potentially imminent. Meg O'Neill assumed the role of CEO at BP on Wednesday, becoming the first woman to lead a major oil company, inheriting a business that receives near-term revenue support from elevated prices but faces deepened long-term questions about capital allocation and transition strategy. Greece received a notable upgrade from MSCI to developed-market status, effective during the May 2027 index review — a milestone in its recovery from the sovereign debt crisis that saw it demoted in 2013. In the UK, London's youth unemployment reached approximately one in four 16-to-24 year-olds seeking work, with graduate job postings falling from roughly 13,000 in 2019 to just over 2,000 at the start of 2026 — a political risk for Keir Starmer's Labour government ahead of municipal elections. French authorities detained two additional suspects linked to a foiled bomb plot near a Bank of America office in Paris, which officials believe is connected to the Middle East conflict. Chinese brands' share of fully electric car sales in Europe rose approximately two percentage points to 8% of the EV market, nearly double their share from a year prior, as elevated fuel prices accelerate the adoption of alternatives to internal combustion vehicles. ---

Global

  • Brent crude advanced above $107 per barrel by Thursday — a gain of more than 40% since the Iran conflict began approximately five weeks ago — with WTI closing above $100 for the first time since hostilities started on Monday.
  • The Strait of Hormuz, which under normal conditions sees approximately 135 vessel transits daily, was averaging fewer than six commercial transits per day; the IRGC is reported to be demanding payment in yuan or cryptocurrency for safe passage under re-flagged registries.
  • Fertilizer prices — particularly urea and ammonia, with nearly half of global urea supply originating in the Persian Gulf region — are rising sharply, raising concerns about food security in sub-Saharan Africa and parts of South Asia.
  • The WTO's 14th ministerial conference ended without agreement on renewing the 28-year e-commerce tariff moratorium, with the US Trade Representative declaring the organization's role "limited" and pivoting toward bilateral agreements.
  • Nuclear proliferation debates are intensifying across US allies, from Germany and Poland exploring deeper integration with France's nuclear umbrella to rising public support in South Korea and renewed discussion in Japan.

The Hormuz Chokepoint and Oil Markets

The Strait of Hormuz remains the defining macro variable for global markets. An Iranian drone struck a fully laden Kuwaiti oil tanker at anchor near Dubai on Tuesday, one of the most significant vessel attacks of the conflict, temporarily pushing WTI toward $107. WTI recorded its largest monthly gain since May 2020, rising more than 50% in March and closing above $100 for the first time since hostilities began. Brent, reflecting non-US supply dynamics, broke above $107 following Trump's Thursday address. The divergence between crude prices and refined products is notable: jet fuel in Europe surged to all-time highs equivalent to over $215 per barrel equivalent, while benchmark crude remains in the low triple digits — a gap explained by record IEA emergency reserve releases, a well-supplied market entering the conflict, and traders' reluctance to hold leveraged positions into weekends. Options markets span downside hedges to speculative bets on $450 per barrel oil, reflecting the absence of directional conviction rather than any particular scenario. Energy consultancy FGE NexantECA warns that if near-closure of Hormuz persists for six to eight weeks, Brent could reach $150–$200; Bloomberg Intelligence estimates an additional surge to $140 if Houthi strikes on Red Sea shipping resume. Approximately 20,000 seafarers remain stranded in and around the Strait, and Emirates Global Aluminium has halted operations at its Al Taweelah smelter following missile and drone strikes on Gulf production facilities.

Strategic and Diplomatic Landscape

Trump's Tuesday signals and Thursday address encapsulate the week's central tension: the president simultaneously claimed the war is "very close" to completion and pledged to hit Iran "extremely hard" over the next two to three weeks, while declining to define exit conditions. The Wall Street Journal reported Monday that the administration is considering ending the campaign without requiring the Strait to reopen — a significant departure from US policy documents published four months prior that listed Hormuz access as a core national interest. Iran's President Pezeshkian published an open letter addressed directly to the American public on Thursday, a move that appears calibrated to exploit divisions within Trump's political base rather than advance formal diplomatic channels; the IRGC, which retains effective strategic control, has shown no inclination toward ceasefire discussions. China and Pakistan issued a five-point joint peace proposal calling for Hormuz shipping restoration. Polymarket probabilities assign a 60% chance that the war concludes in April and only 20% that it extends beyond June — a distribution that has shifted sharply following Pezeshkian's remarks but rests on diplomatic conditions that may prove difficult to formalize. Secretary of State Rubio declared NATO's response "very disappointing," calling out allies for denying base access — a statement that may presage a formal reassessment of the US-NATO relationship once the conflict concludes.

Second-Order Effects: Trade, Food Security, and Proliferation

The conflict's cascading effects are spreading beyond energy markets into commodity supply chains, institutional architecture, and security postures. UNCTAD projects global merchandise trade growth of 1.5–2.5% for 2026, a sharp deceleration from 4.7% last year. US naphtha exports reached a single-month record of approximately 15 million barrels in March as Japanese buyers substituted US Gulf Coast supply for unavailable Middle Eastern cargoes. Fertilizer prices — urea and ammonia in particular — are rising sharply given the Persian Gulf's roughly 50% share of global urea supply, with fuel shortages already spreading to sub-Saharan Africa: Kenya, Ethiopia, and Zambia are reporting station outages. Nuclear proliferation debates are intensifying with a breadth and speed that may prove structurally significant regardless of how the current conflict resolves, with Germany and Poland exploring deeper integration with France's nuclear umbrella, and the Trump administration reportedly considering both resuming nuclear testing after more than three decades and sharing enrichment technology with Saudi Arabia. The Russia-Ukraine conflict is re-intensifying as spring conditions return to the battlefield, with diplomatic attention consumed by the Gulf crisis reducing pressure on Moscow. The voluntary carbon market suffered a setback as Koko Networks — a World Bank-backed clean cookstove operator serving approximately 1.3 million Kenyan households — filed for bankruptcy in January, depressing Corsia aviation offset prices from approximately $15 to $12.85 and reinforcing methodological credibility concerns ahead of mandatory airline compliance beginning in 2027.
