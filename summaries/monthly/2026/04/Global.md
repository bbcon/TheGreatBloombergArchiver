**Strait of Hormuz: The Defining Variable**

The near-closure of the Strait of Hormuz remained the single most consequential variable across all asset classes and regional economies throughout April. Under normal conditions approximately 135 vessels transited daily; that figure fell to fewer than six per day on average in early April, and by late April only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker had completed an exit with crude cargo since the blockade's formal imposition. Brent crude surpassed $107 per barrel following Trump's address in early April, briefly reaching $126 in late April after Axios reported Trump was scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options, before settling near $112 per barrel by month-end — representing a peak-to-trough range that reflects the extraordinary sensitivity of price discovery to diplomatic signals. WTI closed above $100 per barrel for the first time since hostilities began in early April, and Dated Brent briefly surged above $140 on Tuesday of the penultimate week before falling toward $120 as ceasefire optimism briefly prevailed. By the final week, Goldman Sachs estimated Persian Gulf oil output was running approximately 14.5 million barrels per day below pre-war levels — roughly 57% below pre-conflict volumes — and approximately 800 vessels remained stranded in the Persian Gulf.

**Diplomatic Process and Its Limits**

The diplomatic process generated intermittent market-moving signals without approaching durable resolution. A two-week US-Iran ceasefire brokered with Pakistan as key intermediary was announced on April 8, producing one of the largest single-day crude declines on record — Brent fell as much as 16% to approximately $91.70 and WTI tumbled nearly 19% in a session comparable only to the Covid demand collapse and the allied entry into Kuwait in January 1991. By Thursday of that same week, WTI had partially recovered toward $98–99 as Tehran alleged ceasefire violations. The ceasefire framework subsequently collapsed following the Islamabad talks, and Trump announced a full naval blockade of Iranian ports, removing near-term ambiguity by confirming the Strait would not reopen without a nuclear agreement. Iran submitted a peace proposal through Pakistani mediation calling for reopening the strait in exchange for lifting the port blockade, with nuclear negotiations deferred to a separate track — a sequencing Washington has not accepted. The gap between the two parties' stated positions remains wide, and the anticipated Trump-Xi summit in mid-May looms as the next significant diplomatic test. Polymarket assigned only approximately 52% odds to the strait reopening by end-June by late April, making a protracted closure the probabilistic base case.

**Energy Markets: Structure and Second-Order Effects**

The EIA estimates the conflict removed approximately 314 million barrels from global supply through April — roughly 80% of the IEA's record emergency reserve release. The UAE's shock exit from OPEC after 60 years, announced in late April, added a structural dimension extending beyond near-term price: the departure reflects Abu Dhabi's strategic imperative to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand, removing meaningful spare capacity from the cartel's toolkit. OPEC+ warned of prolonged output impacts extending well beyond any eventual ceasefire. The Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index rose meaningfully since hostilities began, with wheat and rice moving higher. Fertilizer prices — urea and ammonia particularly — rose sharply, with nearly half of global urea supply originating in the Persian Gulf, raising food security concerns in sub-Saharan Africa and South Asia; a Farm Bureau survey found more than 70% of US farmers cannot currently afford sufficient fertilizer at prevailing prices. Industrial metals diverged from short-term oil price moves: aluminum reached four-year highs above $3,600 per ton, with JPMorgan forecasting prices could approach $4,000; copper and aluminum were each up more than 11% since mid-March; and the copper-to-gold ratio touched four-decade lows, a level historically associated with commodity supercycle beginnings.

**Geopolitical Architecture Under Strain**

The conflict is accelerating structural shifts in the geopolitical order that may outlast the hostilities. Not a single NATO member or Gulf ally directly joined the US-Israeli campaign, and French President Macron publicly called on medium powers to assert greater independence from Washington. The US formally requested European NATO allies submit concrete plans within days to help secure the Strait of Hormuz, shifting from rhetorical commitment to operational demand. Pakistan's emergence as the key mediating channel — leveraging ties with Saudi Arabia, Iran, the US, and China simultaneously — reflects a meaningful realignment of diplomatic influence in the region. China's role in persuading Iran to accept the initial ceasefire, credited by Iranian officials to a last-minute Beijing push, illustrates the growing influence Beijing derives from its $270 billion financial footprint across the Gulf. Trump's cryptic remarks about a vessel carrying a "gift from China" — implying potential lethal aid to Iran — introduce a significant variable Beijing has not publicly addressed. Secretary of State Rubio declared NATO's response "very disappointing," calling out allies for denying base access; the WTO's failure to renew its 28-year e-commerce tariff moratorium prompted the USTR to declare the organization's role in global trade "limited." The IMF cut its global growth forecast, with Bloomberg Economics' base case now at 2.9% for 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — and a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 a barrel.

**Structural Acceleration: Energy Transition and AI Risk**

The crisis is simultaneously acting as a catalyst for energy transition while introducing new systemic concerns. Clean power surpassed coal's share of global electricity generation in 2025 for the first time since 1919, with renewables at 34% against coal's 33%. European consumers purchased 206,200 electric vehicles in March, a 44% year-on-year increase; South Korean EV sales more than doubled; and Chinese EV and hybrid exports reached a record. Battery installations are expected to jump roughly a third this year. China holds approximately 72% of cumulative installed long-duration energy storage capacity. Brazil's program to electrify isolated Amazon communities via solar and lithium storage illustrates how the conflict's upward pressure on diesel prices has counterintuitively accelerated the economics of off-grid electrification. Against this accelerating transition, Anthropic's Mythos AI model — described as capable of identifying system vulnerabilities and orchestrating cyberattacks — emerged as an unexpected focal point at the IMF-World Bank Spring Meetings, with central bank governors from the ECB, Bank of England, and New York Fed all raising the issue publicly. Fed Chair Powell and Treasury Secretary Bessent jointly convened Wall Street bank CEOs over these concerns, an indication that financial regulators are treating advanced AI as an emerging systemic risk at precisely the moment when other institutional coordination mechanisms are under strain. BlackRock Investment Institute warned that elevated oil-driven inflation means long-term government bond yields are likely to remain structurally higher, and that traditional equity-bond diversification relationships may no longer hold reliably — a caution that applies as much to the months ahead as to April itself.
