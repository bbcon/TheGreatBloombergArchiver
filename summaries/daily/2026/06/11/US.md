• US CPI rose to 4.0%+ YoY in May — a three-year high — though core inflation (ex-food and energy) came in below consensus, limiting the immediate policy implications.
• NFIB small business optimism slipped below expectations in May, giving up all post-election gains; the NFIB prices index reached its highest reading since 1981.
• The Fed is widely expected to hold rates at next week's FOMC, Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair; market-based inflation expectations continued to decline, signaling perceived transience.
• Oracle shares fell approximately 10% in after-hours trading after reporting higher-than-expected data center capex; the Nasdaq 100 remains down roughly 7% from last week's peak but is still up 12% year-to-date.

**Inflation and Monetary Policy**

May CPI topped 4% year-over-year for the first time in approximately three years, driven heavily by energy. However, core inflation — excluding food and energy — came in below consensus, and markets adjudged the print insufficiently alarming to force a rate hike at next week's FOMC. That conclusion appears well-founded in the near term but may be premature on a longer horizon. The Fed's preferred "supercore" measure (services ex-housing) is rising sharply, the Atlanta Fed's sticky-price index has moved above 3%, and both trimmed-mean and median CPI exceeded headline core. CPI excluding shelter now exceeds 4%, making it difficult to attribute current inflation primarily to housing's well-understood lag dynamics.

**Small Business and Corporate Divergence**

The NFIB May optimism index declined below expectations, erasing post-election gains entirely. More than 70% of small business owners cited supply-chain disruptions; capital expenditure fell to its lowest since 2009; and hiring plans deteriorated. The NFIB prices survey — which has historically led inflation inflection points by nine months — currently signals reacceleration. By contrast, larger firms demonstrate greater resilience, aided by scale, capital access, and AI deployment capacity. SpaceX's IPO, set for Friday with demand exceeding four times available supply, exemplifies continued investor enthusiasm at the large-cap end, even as PIMCO warned that a "credit loss cycle" may be underway among more leveraged borrowers.
