Utility bills are emerging as a defining issue heading into the 2026 midterm elections, with electricity costs having nearly doubled for many American households over the past several years. The confluence of AI data center buildout, grid upgrade expenditures, tariff pass-throughs, and energy market disruption linked to the Iran conflict has driven power prices higher at rates not seen in decades. Democrats who campaigned explicitly on household energy affordability swept key races in New Jersey, Virginia, and Georgia in late 2025, providing a template that challengers are now deploying against incumbent Republicans defending a narrow House majority. Pennsylvania's 7th District, where a former utility employee is running against Republican Representative Ryan Mackenzie, illustrates the dynamic: bipartisan agreement on the problem dissolves into sharp disagreement over solutions. President Trump acknowledged the issue in his February State of the Union address — the first time a president has explicitly framed rising electricity bills as a consumer concern in that forum — though his proposed remedy, requiring tech companies to build their own power plants, remains more aspiration than policy. The midterm environment appears to be shaping up around kitchen-table economics rather than ideological abstraction, a terrain historically more favorable to the opposition party.

On defense and climate adaptation, a notable institutional divergence is apparent within the Trump administration. Secretary Hegseth has moved aggressively to eliminate explicit climate language from Pentagon mission statements and cancel climate-related research, yet the Defense Department continues to advance large-scale infrastructure hardening at military installations. Tyndall Air Force Base in Florida — damaged to the tune of nearly $5 billion by Hurricane Michael in 2018 — is being rebuilt with flood-resistant structures, reinforced roofing, and coastal protection features. The framing has shifted from "climate resilience" to "operational readiness," a semantic adjustment that has proved sufficient to preserve funding. Close to $400 billion in federal assets, predominantly Defense Department holdings, face elevated coastal flood risk, providing a practical rationale that transcends the political debate.
