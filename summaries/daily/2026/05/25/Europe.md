# European Morning Briefing — May 25, 2026

**ECB tilts hawkish as Iran diplomacy hangs in balance.** Two ECB Governing Council members indicated the central bank is moving toward a rate hike at its June meeting unless a durable US-Iran peace agreement materializes, with President Lagarde signaling an upward revision to the inflation outlook. The hawkish lean reflects the pass-through from elevated crude prices stemming from the Hormuz blockade, though the calculus could shift quickly: Brent fell below $100 and WTI dropped nearly 6% to around $91 after Washington indicated it was closing in on an interim deal with Tehran that would reopen the strait in exchange for lifting the US port blockade and potentially unfreezing Iranian assets. Both President Trump and Iranian officials cautioned against assuming an imminent signing, and Iran's new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei — not seen publicly since the strikes that killed his father — must weigh whether sustained energy-price pressure on the West outweighs the economic toll of the blockade on Iran. The two-way risk for European rates is material: a deal removes the principal upside inflation impulse, while a breakdown likely cements a June hike.

**UK politics drifts into paralysis.** The leadership crisis surrounding Keir Starmer has slowed the machinery of government, with confirmation of a successor potentially weeks or months away. The vacuum has reopened debate over fiscal strategy, emboldening Labour's left flank to push wealth taxes. Bloomberg Opinion's Martin Ivens argues markets may be underestimating Andy Burnham, while gilt investors face genuine uncertainty over the direction of revenue policy.

**Corporate.** Uber offered €33 per share for Delivery Hero, valuing the German group at roughly €10 billion and building on Uber's existing 20% stake — a meaningful consolidation move in European food delivery. Separately, Mango co-owner Jonathan Andic was bailed after questioning over his father Isak Andic's 2024 death, with a judge citing indications of "non-accidental" circumstances; Andic denies wrongdoing, but the case has renewed scrutiny of succession at one of Spain's flagship retailers.

**Carbon-market integrity.** A Bloomberg investigation found buyers across at least nine European countries — including BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, EDF and OMV — purchased credits from Chinese projects that, in some cases, appear not to exist. German authorities have voided credits from roughly two-thirds of 45 "suspicious" projects, raising governance questions ahead of the EU's 2040 climate framework.
