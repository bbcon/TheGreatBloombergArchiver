Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, Oil 
Europe Team Leader Alaric Nightingale looks at prospects for a resumption of 
Hormuz ship traffic as military hostilities continue for a fifth day.

The oil and gas market needs US military escorts in the Strait of Hormuz right 
now if Persian Gulf energy flows to global markets are to start coming back.

President Donald Trump has said his country will ensure safe passage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/trump-pledges-safe-oil-transit-from-mideast-as-iran-war-widens?cmpid=BBBXT030426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 of tankers from the Middle East. But even if the shipping industry gets its 
wish, the crisis at the critical chokepoint won’t resolve itself overnight.

Oil and gas prices show that the need for protection is pressing.

Brent crude rose again today, taking its increase since the war began to 13%. 
Diesel and jet fuel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/global-diesel-prices-surge-higher-as-iran-war-disrupts-supplies?cmpid=BBBXT030426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 are gaining even more sharply, a reminder that this situation will quickly rip 
into retail prices that Trump has vowed to tame. European natural gas is up 
more than 50% this week.

The fate of Hormuz transits is key to both markets because the waterway is the 
route for one in five barrels produced worldwide and a similar proportion of 
liquefied natural gas.

For now, there’s no sign 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/trump-s-hormuz-assurances-would-be-a-partial-fix-shippers-say?cmpid=BBBXT030426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 of shipping resuming in a material way, and the interruption to exports is 
starting to affect both commodities at the point of production.

Iraq has had to slash oil output 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/iraq-starts-shutting-oil-output-at-rumaila-as-storage-fills-up?cmpid=BBBXT030426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 because storage is already filling up due to a lack of tankers to collect 
crude. At Qatar’s Ras Laffan LNG plant, the world’s largest, tanks had been 
almost topping out before a drone attack shut the site.
Ras Laffan Industrial City in Qatar, site of the world’s largest LNG plant.
Photographer: KARIM JAAFAR/AFP via Getty Images
But even with convoys and escorts, the risk tolerance of owners and crews will 
vary. Some vessels are already going elsewhere for work and long-planned export 
programs have been snarled.

Every day of the war that passes, more disruption is likely. And even if the 
security situation is fixed fast, resolving theshipping havoc 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/oil-supertanker-costs-near-500-000-a-day-as-war-disrupts-flows?cmpid=BBBXT030426_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 will take time.

Remember, it’s ultimately the captain of a ship — not somebody sitting behind 
a desk in Athens, Oslo or Beijing — who decides whether it’s safe to sail.

Many will choose not to do so if it’s too risky. Escorts are a first urgent 
step, but they won’t bring an immediate end to the chaos.

—Alaric Nightingale, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

China is dominating yet another area of the global energy transition: 
long-duration energy storage, or LDES. Unlike conventional lithium-ion 
batteries that typically store power for a few hours, LDES can hold and release 
electricity for days. As intermittent renewables become a bigger part of the 
global power mix, LDES is badly needed to help balance supply and demand, and 
China is racing ahead of the rest of the world,BloombergNEF <> said.
