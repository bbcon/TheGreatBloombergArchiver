# Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — May 25, 2026

**Risk-on tone as Hormuz diplomacy advances.** Asian markets opened the week firmer alongside a sharp decline in crude, with Brent slipping below $100 and WTI down nearly 6% to roughly $91, as Washington signaled progress toward an interim agreement with Tehran that would reopen the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for lifting the US blockade of Iranian ports. Secretary of State Rubio, speaking in New Delhi, struck a cautiously upbeat tone, though both sides cautioned against assuming imminent closure. JGBs rallied and the dollar softened. A supertanker carrying Iraqi crude bound for China cleared the US blockade line into the Arabian Sea, providing the first tangible signs of restored flows, though the durability of any de-escalation remains contingent on decisions by Iran's new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei and final terms still under negotiation.

**Beijing intensifies capital-outflow controls.** China's most forceful crackdown to date on illicit cross-border equity trading is reshaping flows, with onshore investors scrambling for alternative routes to access overseas markets. Futu founder Leaf Li's net worth fell $1.7 billion in a single session to $4.7 billion, less than half its late-October peak, underscoring the immediate hit to the offshore brokerage channel. The crackdown follows an estimated $1 trillion in hot-money outflows last year, the largest since records began in 2006, and signals authorities' prioritization of capital-account control over financial market liberalization.

**Regional spillovers from the Iran war persist despite the diplomatic opening.** Singapore downgraded its 2026 outlook, citing energy-supply disruption, tariff risks, and potential AI capex pullback, even as Q1 GDP rose 1% q/q, well above consensus. Malaysia reported industrial fallout, and India raised retail fuel prices for the fourth time this month. RBI Governor Malhotra's unusual public remark that the rupee "may now be undervalued" — ahead of the June 5 rate decision — suggests heightened official discomfort with currency weakness amid balance-of-payments strain.

**Other notable items.** The Shanxi coal-mine explosion, China's deadliest since 2009, killed 82 and lifted domestic coal prices; Xi reportedly grew agitated criticizing PM Takaichi's defense posture during his Beijing summit with Trump (FT); Huawei's chip chief outlined a pathway to narrow the gap with TSMC without leading-edge equipment; and SoftBank shares hit a record on OpenAI IPO speculation.
