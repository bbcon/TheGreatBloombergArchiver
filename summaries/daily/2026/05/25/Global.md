**Hormuz Diplomacy**
The most consequential development across global markets remains the trajectory of US-Iran negotiations. Secretary of State Rubio struck an upbeat tone in New Delhi, and some vessel movement — including an Iraqi crude supertanker crossing the US blockade line into the Arabian Sea — has added practical weight to diplomatic optimism. ADNOC has reportedly been moving oil and gas shipments through the Gulf using its own fleet, suggesting informal clearances on both sides. However, Iran's Foreign Ministry explicitly stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent," and Tehran's demand to unfreeze assets remains a blocking issue. The gap between market pricing and diplomatic reality appears to be narrowing but not yet closed.

**Geopolitical Crosscurrents**
Beyond Hormuz, the geopolitical backdrop remains unsettled. Russia's use of a hypersonic Oreshnik missile in a mass strike involving 90 missiles and 600 drones against Ukrainian cities represents a continued escalation in the quality of munitions deployed, even as the front lines remain relatively static. In China, Xi Jinping reportedly expressed sharp criticism of Japan's Prime Minister Takaichi over Tokyo's defense buildup during the Beijing summit with Trump, a signal of persistent Sino-Japanese friction. In Turkey, the forced entry into CHP opposition headquarters marks a further erosion of political norms under the current government. These developments collectively suggest the global geopolitical risk premium, while partly priced through energy markets, has additional dimensions that remain underweighted in equity valuations.
