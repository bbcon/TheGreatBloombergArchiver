**The Hormuz Conflict: Escalation, Costs, and Tentative Diplomacy**

The Strait of Hormuz closure — entering its third consecutive month — remained the single most consequential variable in global markets throughout May, with the diplomatic and military situation oscillating in ways that produced sharp intraday moves across oil, equities, and fixed income. Brent briefly surpassed $126 per barrel mid-month after Axios reported President Trump was scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options against Iran and US Central Command was preparing fresh strike plans — a level representing a rise of more than 60% since the conflict began in late February. The conflict's military dimension intensified through the month: Iran attacked three American warships and struck the UAE's Fujairah oil terminal; US forces struck Iranian missile and drone launch sites near the strait on multiple occasions; and Iran deployed midget submarines that have sustained the stalemate despite significant US naval superiority. The cumulative supply loss since the conflict began approached one billion barrels.

The final week of May produced the month's most significant diplomatic development. President Trump called off planned strikes after Saudi Arabia and Gulf allies requested additional time for diplomacy, sending Brent approximately 7% lower in thin holiday trading. By Thursday, a tentative 60-day ceasefire extension framework had been reported, with a Hormuz reopening proposed in exchange for a moratorium on uranium enrichment and lifting of some sanctions. WTI fell nearly 10% from its weekly peak on Wednesday following the diplomatic signals before recovering partially as US forces struck additional Iranian targets and Iran's Supreme Leader declared that US military bases in the region "will no longer be safe." Republican hawks argued the emerging terms concede too much. Secretary Rubio acknowledged talks could take several more days. Trump has not yet formally approved the terms, and Iran's Foreign Ministry earlier in the week stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent." Polymarket assigns below 50% odds to normal Hormuz traffic by end-June. The IEA reported global oil inventories falling at a record pace — Goldman Sachs estimated visible crude and product inventories drew down at 8.7 million barrels per day in May, nearly double the conflict's running average — a rate that, if sustained, points toward demand destruction rather than supply restoration as the rebalancing mechanism.

**Energy Architecture in Transition**

The UAE's exit from OPEC after 60 years, announced in early May, adds a structural dimension to the energy disruption that extends beyond near-term price dynamics. The departure reflects Abu Dhabi's strategic imperative to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand and removes from the cartel's toolkit the spare production capacity that served as its primary price-defense mechanism in 2014 and 2020. Reports that the UAE is reconsidering participation in the Arab League, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation, and potentially the Gulf Cooperation Council suggest a durable strategic reorientation. OPEC+ agreed a modest symbolic increase of 188,000 barrels per day for June — largely a signaling exercise — while ADNOC simultaneously announced $55 billion in accelerated project awards and plans to double Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027, treating a prolonged disruption as a planning assumption rather than an outlier. US crude exports reached a record above 6 million barrels per day as importing nations sought alternatives, with domestic shale production near-record at 13.6 million barrels per day. Goldman Sachs raised its fourth-quarter Brent forecast to $90 per barrel — up nearly $30 from pre-conflict projections — though spot prices remained well above that level through most of the month.

The oil shock is simultaneously accelerating structural energy transitions. European EV purchases rose 44% year-on-year in March, South Korean EV sales more than doubled, and Chinese EV and hybrid exports reached a record, rising 140% from the prior year. China's clean technology manufacturers explicitly leveraged the shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third. Green bond issuance in emerging markets rose 53% year-on-year to $24 billion in Q1 as energy-import-dependent economies accelerated renewables deployment. The strategic logic is clear — sustained high fossil fuel prices simultaneously improve the economics of electrification across every geography and reinforce the security case for domestic clean energy investment.

**Global Bond Markets and the Synchronized Tightening Impulse**

The synchronized selloff in sovereign bond markets across the US, UK, eurozone, and Japan through May represents a meaningful departure from the post-GFC environment of divergent monetary cycles. US 30-year yields touched 5.19% on the third Tuesday of the month — a level last seen in June 2007; UK 10-year gilts reached 5.14%; and Japan's 20-year rate hit its highest level since 1996. Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields reached its highest since July 2008 at mid-month before partially stabilizing on Iran diplomatic signals in the final days. Producer price indices in the US, China, and Japan all surprised to the upside through the month, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. The common driver is the energy shock, but reinforcing factors — AI-related capital expenditure, deteriorating fiscal trajectories, demographic pressures, and sticky underlying inflation — distinguish this episode from a simple commodity price impulse. All major central banks simultaneously face inflationary pressure that limits their ability to buffer growth slowdowns with easing. The Reserve Bank of Australia has already delivered three hikes, unwinding all of last year's easing. The ECB and Bank of England are converging toward June increases. Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike. TS Lombard's Dario Perkins characterized the environment as potentially heralding "rate hikes everywhere," with 2% targets functioning increasingly as a floor rather than a ceiling. The tension between that tightening bias and the historical precedent of energy shocks culminating in recession remains the defining macro ambiguity as markets enter summer.

**Dollar Architecture, Geopolitical Realignment, and the AI Supercycle**

A slower-moving but potentially consequential structural development accumulated visibility through May: observable stress points in dollar-denominated financial architecture. Iran's collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin represents a de minimis but symbolically notable bypass of dollar payment infrastructure. China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US secondary sanctions directly challenges the enforcement architecture that gives dollar primacy its coercive utility. Gulf sovereign wealth funds — led by Saudi Arabia's PIF, which cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from near 50% — are turning materially inward, with war-related costs equivalent to roughly 1.5% of Saudi GDP in the first month compressing the petrodollar recycling pool that has historically supported global asset prices. Central banks across emerging markets are increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves; gold peaked above $4,490 in the early weeks of the month before shedding nearly 20% from its late-January peak as rising real yields and speculative position unwinds dominated.
