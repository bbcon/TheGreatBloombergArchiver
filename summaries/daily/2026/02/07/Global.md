**Global Macro Briefing — February 7, 2026**

**Middle East emerges as globalization's bright spot.** While protectionist currents dominate the broader global trade narrative, Gulf Cooperation Council economies expanded trade at a 15% annual clip from 2021-24, outpacing the 9% global rate, according to Standard Chartered. The Asia-Middle East corridor is projected to reach $1.5 trillion by 2030 from $905 billion in 2024, with Middle East-Africa trade expected to roughly double to $260 billion. The UAE, in particular, has positioned itself as an intercontinental logistics nexus: it imported approximately 560,000 Chinese passenger vehicles in 2025—third globally behind Mexico and Russia despite a population of only 11 million—reflecting its role as a transshipment hub rather than purely an end market. Britain signaled a GCC trade deal is "imminent," and competition between DP World's Jebel Ali and ADQ-owned Khalifa Port underscores the depth of regional logistics capacity. For investors, the Gulf increasingly offers diversification optionality as the Trump tariff regime reshapes traditional corridors, though the durability of this trade momentum will depend on whether energy-funded fiscal expansion can be sustained—Saudi Arabia has already scaled back its more ambitious Vision 2030 projects.

**Climate-energy nexus pressures Western utilities.** A La Niña-driven dry winter across the US Mountain West has forced Xcel Energy to implement multiple preventative power shutoffs across Colorado, affecting more than 50,000 customers. The pattern, projected to persist into spring, illustrates the operating cost utilities now bear from elevated wildfire liability—a tail risk made tangible by Edison International's mounting litigation over the Eaton fire and PG&E's 2019 bankruptcy precedent. Capital allocation toward weather monitoring infrastructure, AI-driven risk modeling, and vegetation management is becoming a structural feature of utility cost bases in fire-prone jurisdictions.

**Climate transition meets symbolism.** The Cortina Winter Olympics opened with regional temperatures 7°C above 1956 levels, with athletes increasingly competing on manufactured snow. While anecdotal, the trajectory has implications for tourism-dependent Alpine economies and reinforces the secular case for adaptation-related capex.
