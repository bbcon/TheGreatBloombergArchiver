The Houthis opened another front in the Iran war, targeting two oil tankers in 
the Red Sea in a move that threatens to disruptmaritime trade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/containers-surge-into-top-us-ports-amid-tariff-iran-uncertainty?cmpid=BBD072326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6694>
 more broadly.

Just before the Houthi statement on Wednesday, the UK Maritime Trade 
Operations Centersaid <https://x.com/UK_MTO/status/2080043376351535532> the 
master of a tanker reported that the ship had been struck southwest of Al 
Shuqaiq on the western Saudi coast.

The Latest: Houthis Say They Hit Tankers in Red Sea, Widening Iran War 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/us-iran-suggest-peace-talks-aren-t-imminent-as-attacks-widen?cmpid=BBD072326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6694>

Since the US and Israel started bombing Iran in late February, the waterway 
emerged as a vital workaround for crude exports, particularly from Saudi 
Arabia, as the Strait of Hormuz became too dangerous to navigate.

Oil approached $100 a barrel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-july-23?cmpid=BBD072326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6694>
 in London, and Bloomberg Intelligencesaid <> a sustained blockade of the Red 
Sea “could boost energy-shipping rates by at least 30%.”

Given the access it provides to the Suez Canal, the body of water is also 
vital for container vessels hauling goods between Asia and markets in Europe, 
Africa and North America. The Houthis started attacking cargo carriers inlate 
2023 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-12-18/houthi-attacks-start-shutting-down-red-sea-merchant-shipping?cmpid=BBD072326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6694>
, forcing many operators to divert south of Africa on routes that remain in 
effect.

Cargo Diversions

Before this week’s flareup in Houthi violence against the commercial fleet, 
Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd and other container lines were taking steps toshift some 
service 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/maersk-hapag-lloyd-eyeing-return-to-red-sea-route-shares-fall?cmpid=BBD072326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6694>
 back through the Red Sea.

Lars Jensen, a shipping analyst currently on a road trip 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-05/shipping-s-biggest-geek-charts-40-000-mile-africa-road-trip?cmpid=BBD072326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6694>
 co-sponsored by Hapag-Lloyd and DSV across Europe and Africa, said in a
LinkedIn post 
<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/larsjensenvespuccimaritime_houthies-have-attacked-a-tanker-for-the-first-share-7485922194271903744-lYsG/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_ios&rcm=ACoAAAQhrDgB0bYwZGt1gaWdmMK9EIR1HhRFMEU>
 Thursday that cargo owners might want to start planning for disruptions such 
as diverted shipments.

“Shippers might want to be prepared that if Bab el-Mandeb genuinely is closed 
off,” Jensen wrote. “Cargo already on vessels from Asia to Red Sea is likely to 
suddenly being discharged in the ‘wrong’ ports across Asia, similar to what we 
saw when Hormuz was initially closed.”
