Asian equity markets followed Wall Street higher on Tuesday, with the Nikkei 225 gaining 2.4% and South Korea's Kospi rising 2.7%, as investors focused on the possibility of resumed US-Iran negotiations rather than the formal initiation of a US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz. Taiwan's Taiex closed at a record high, posting a year-to-date gain of 25%, driven heavily by Taiwan Semiconductor as investor enthusiasm for AI supply-chain names reasserted itself.

China faces a complex set of pressures. March trade data showed export growth slowing sharply while imports surged at the fastest pace since late 2021, as elevated energy commodity purchases reflected the war's disruption to global supply. The divergence underscores the dual squeeze: higher input costs weighing on margins while external demand softens. Chinese President Xi Jinping, meeting with Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez in Beijing, described the international order as "crumbling into disarray" — language observers noted was notably stronger than his customary characterizations of global turbulence.

China's independent oil refiners — the so-called teapots, accounting for roughly a fifth of national refining capacity — remain under acute pressure. Heavily reliant on discounted Iranian crude, the sector is now exposed to both US sanctions enforcement and the blockade. Floating stockpiles of approximately 38 million barrels of Iranian crude in Asian waters provide a temporary buffer of perhaps two months, and Beijing has released strategic reserves and granted additional import quotas to sustain output. Nevertheless, if the blockade persists, the cushion will narrow. China's accelerating shift toward domestically-generated electricity from coal and renewables provides some structural insulation, but not enough to offset near-term refining stress.

Singapore became the first Asian central bank to tighten monetary policy settings in response to rising inflation driven by higher energy prices — a move that may signal broader policy divergence across the region if crude remains elevated. Meanwhile, China Evergrande founder Hui Ka Yan pleaded guilty to fundraising fraud and bribery in a Chinese court, closing a significant chapter in the country's property sector restructuring.
