**Oil and the Strait of Hormuz**
Oil markets are pricing a meaningful easing of supply disruption risk as tanker transits through the Strait of Hormuz gradually resume. WTI at $70.97 reflects a combination of recovering Gulf flows, strong US supply, and weak Chinese demand — a configuration that Morgan Stanley warns could produce a supply glut. However, Iran's simultaneous assertion of sovereign control over the waterway introduces considerable uncertainty: the ceasefire remains fragile, peace talks in Qatar have yet to produce a formal settlement, and any re-escalation could rapidly reverse the recent easing in energy prices. The ECB has explicitly flagged residual pipeline inflation from four months of elevated energy costs.

**Global Monetary Policy and Trade**
Global monetary policy divergence is narrowing at the margin. The ECB has hiked; the Fed under Chair Kevin Warsh has adopted a hawkish stance; and the Bank of Japan has raised rates to a 31-year high — though not fast enough to arrest yen weakness. The USMCA faces its first renewal deadline on July 1, likely to be missed, with President Trump's stated preference to exit the agreement representing a tail risk to North American supply chains. Meanwhile, a US Commerce Department report on copper tariffs was due Tuesday, with a potential 15% levy on refined copper in January 2027 creating fresh uncertainty for industrial metals markets.
