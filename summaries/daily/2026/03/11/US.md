US equity futures erased early gains on Wednesday as oil prices climbed anew after UK naval authorities reported three vessels struck in the Strait of Hormuz and the Persian Gulf, underscoring the fragility of any market optimism around a near-term resolution to the US-Iran conflict. S&P 500 futures settled around 6,775, down modestly on the session, while Treasuries held broadly steady ahead of February CPI data — a release that retains significance even as the oil shock increasingly dominates the inflation conversation.

On the policy front, Kevin Warsh met with Senator Thom Tillis to discuss his Federal Reserve chair nomination but failed to secure a clear path forward, leaving the succession question unresolved at a moment when the central bank's credibility and independence are under considerable scrutiny. A separate Bloomberg Opinion piece warned that a compromised Fed risks precipitating a fiscal crisis — a concern that may gain traction should energy-driven inflation force difficult monetary decisions in the months ahead.

In corporate news, Amazon raised $37 billion in what ranks as the fourth-largest US corporate bond sale on record, with a further $11.6 billion sought in European markets, as the company funds accelerating AI infrastructure investment. Oracle shares surged approximately 10% in extended trading after reporting strong AI cloud demand, reinforcing the thesis that hyperscaler capital expenditure remains robust. On the credit side, JPMorgan Chase moved to restrict lending to certain private credit funds after marking down the value of loans to software companies — a development PIMCO's Christian Stracke described plainly as "a crisis of really bad underwriting." The $1.8 trillion private credit market is showing mounting stress as high-profile corporate blowups surface.

Pre-conflict consumer data offered a counterpoint to the gathering macro gloom: Bank of America card data showed spending rose 3.3% in February year-on-year, the strongest growth in over three years, with tax refunds providing a visible boost across income cohorts, though the K-shaped divergence between higher and lower earners remains pronounced.
