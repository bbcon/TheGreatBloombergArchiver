European markets reflected the strain of the energy shock on Monday, with the Stoxx Europe 600 slipping 0.2% as bond yields continued rising across the eurozone and UK — reversing the prior trend and signaling market concern about both inflation and potential monetary policy tightening. The Bank of England and ECB both meet this week, with traders now pricing in possible rate hikes later in the year, a marked shift from the easing trajectory that prevailed just weeks ago. The more likely near-term outcome is a hold accompanied by cautious language, but the direction of risk is clearly shifting. UK inflation dynamics are being assessed against the 2011 energy shock precedent rather than 2022, which analysts suggest may imply a more measured BoE response.

The conflict is also exposing tensions within European political structures. Trump explicitly warned that the US "will remember" whether NATO allies support efforts to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, adding that insufficient response would be "very bad for the future of NATO." European governments are weighing these demands cautiously, with several years of post-Greenland skepticism about Washington's reliability. In UK domestic politics, Nigel Farage's Reform UK appears to be losing ground to two newer far-right parties — Restore UK (backed by Elon Musk) and Advance UK — suggesting fragmentation on the right fringe may complicate the political landscape ahead of future elections.

In European corporate news, UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank that would allow the Italian lender to increase its shareholding beyond the 30% threshold, easing the path toward a potential full acquisition. In Italy, Giorgia Meloni's government is consulting on restarting nuclear energy production, positioning atomic power as a long-term solution to Italy's elevated energy prices — among the highest in Europe.
