Weekly Brief

Apr 13 – Apr 19, 2026

US

Market Recovery and Valuation Concerns

US equities mounted a striking recovery across the week, with the S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 reaching all-time highs on Wednesday and the S&P closing above 7,000 for the first time since January by Thursday. The Nasdaq 100 extended its winning streak to 12 consecutive sessions, rising approximately 14% from late March lows in one of the fastest recoveries recorded since 1950. Analysts drew the closest historical parallel to March 2000 in terms of earnings multiple and timing — a comparison that should give pause. The Bank of America global fund manager survey showed the proportion viewing US equities as overvalued at a seven-year low, though the Shiller CAPE remains elevated and first-quarter earnings estimates declined during the rally rather than rising, making the multiple expansion difficult to justify on fundamentals alone. The behavioral explanation may be more compelling: investors conditioned by 2025's Liberation Day episode, when early sellers missed a rapid rebound, appear reluctant to hold short positions through stress events.

Earnings Season Opens with Mixed Results

Goldman Sachs kicked off first-quarter reporting season on Monday against a deteriorating macro backdrop. The season's early returns were mixed: JPMorgan, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo reported before Tuesday's open, while Goldman and industrial bellwether Fastenal disappointed. Netflix shares fell approximately 10% in premarket trading on Thursday after missing earnings estimates, with co-founder Reed Hastings simultaneously announcing his departure after 29 years. Alcoa also disappointed despite elevated aluminum prices. QVC filed for bankruptcy to restructure more than $5 billion in debt. Against these disappointments, TSMC's 58% profit surge and raised guidance provided a reminder that AI-infrastructure earnings remain in a different cycle. The Roundhill Meme Stock ETF gained 54% since late March — a sentiment indicator more than a fundamental one.

Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy

The Fed remains firmly on hold, and the situation around its leadership has grown politically charged. Trump reiterated his threat to dismiss Jerome Powell if he remains past his May term expiration, while Senator Thom Tillis blocked Kevin Warsh's confirmation pending resolution of a Justice Department building-renovation probe that a court characterized as without merit. Treasury Secretary Bessent signaled twice in consecutive days that he understands the case for holding rates steady while the war's inflationary impact remains unclear — a marked shift from earlier advocacy for cuts that implicitly provides political cover for Warsh. The Fed's Beige Book described an economy in suspension. Markets are pricing near-zero probability of a June rate change.

Consumer Stress and Political Economy

Beneath the equity market resilience, consumer stress is accumulating. The University of Michigan's preliminary April sentiment index fell to 47.6 on Friday, the weakest in the survey's history dating to 1978 — surpassing troughs from the 1980 hostage crisis and the Covid pandemic — with year-ahead inflation expectations rising a full percentage point to 4.8%. Mortgage applications fell more than 10% through March. BNPL provider Klarna has lost more than 60% of its value since its September IPO, with usage increasingly concentrated in essentials such as groceries. Electricity costs are emerging as a defining issue ahead of November midterms, with utility bills having nearly doubled for many households under the combined pressure of AI data center buildout, grid upgrade spending, tariff pass-throughs, and Iran-related energy disruption. Democrats who campaigned on affordability swept key races in late 2025, providing a template challengers are deploying against Republicans defending a narrow House majority.

Fiscal Policy and Other Developments

Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed tariff rates may be restored to pre-Supreme Court levels by July after the court struck down emergency-powers authority for their original imposition. The Atlanta Fed found only 20% of firms incorporated the One Big Beautiful Bill's capex provisions into planning — echoing the limited uptake seen after the 2017 tax cuts and complicating the bull case for investment-led expansion. A federal antitrust jury found Live Nation illegally monopolized the live events industry, setting the stage for a potential structural breakup. Former Treasury Secretary Paulson warned that a collapse in Treasury demand remains a tail risk, a sobering note against an ebullient backdrop. ---

Asia

China: Growth Beats, but Structural Imbalances Persist

China's first-quarter GDP expanded 5.0% year-on-year — the strongest pace in three quarters — beating consensus expectations and suggesting limited direct spillover from the Iran conflict thus far. However, the composition of growth warrants scrutiny. Industrial output grew 5.7% in March while retail sales rose only 1.7%, missing forecasts. High-tech exports surged nearly 30% in the first quarter, partly reflecting manufacturers front-running US tariff escalation, but the Canton Fair in Guangzhou offered a more sobering ground-level view: Middle Eastern buyers — who had increasingly substituted for absent American customers since the pandemic — were conspicuously absent due to the conflict and flight disruption. Exporters described 2026 as potentially the worst in memory, facing the sequential blows of tariffs and war simultaneously.

China: Energy Exposure and Strategic Positioning

Iran is a critical oil supplier to China, and the Hormuz disruption has exposed the country's refining sector to acute input cost pressure. Saudi Arabia's crude shipments to Chinese customers are set to halve in May, to approximately 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April, as Aramco raised official selling prices to a record. China's independent teapot refiners — accounting for roughly a fifth of national capacity — are heavily reliant on discounted Iranian crude, with floating stockpiles of approximately 38 million barrels providing a buffer of perhaps two months. Beijing has released strategic reserves and granted additional import quotas to sustain output. Separately, yuan-denominated crude oil transactions reached a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS on Thursday, and China's lithium battery exports rose 50% year-on-year in the first quarter as buyers sought alternatives to oil-dependent energy.

China: Diplomatic Signaling and Xi's Strategic Posture

Xi Jinping had his most diplomatically active week in nearly two years, receiving delegations from Spain, Abu Dhabi, Vietnam, and Russia, among others. His meeting with Russian Foreign Minister Lavrov was the most consequential: Xi emphasized that "the stability and certainty of China-Russia relations are particularly valuable," signaling strategic alignment at a moment when Russian energy has become more valuable to China as Middle East flows tighten. Xi framed China as a stabilizing force against a "crumbling" global order and proposed a four-point Middle East peace plan. His positioning appears deliberate and builds on the 2023 Saudi-Iran rapprochement, but Beijing's concrete contributions to conflict resolution have so far remained limited. A Trump-Xi summit in approximately a month looms as the next significant test.

Taiwan and South Korea: AI-Driven Outperformance

Taiwan Semiconductor delivered a first-quarter profit surge of 58% on Thursday and raised full-year revenue growth guidance to above 30%, reinforcing its status as the primary proxy for global AI infrastructure investment. TSMC's market capitalization now exceeds $1.7 trillion, and Taiwan's total equity market has overtaken the UK to become the seventh largest globally at $4.14 trillion. The Taiex ended the week with a year-to-date gain of approximately 25% and posted a record close on Tuesday. South Korea's Kospi gained approximately 23% since its sharp two-day decline at the onset of the war, with BlackRock flagging Korean equities as a preferred position. Taiwan's export data surged to an all-time high as AI chip demand more than offset supply chain disruptions.

Japan and Regional Central Banks

BOJ Governor Ueda signaled a more cautious stance on rate hikes ahead of the next policy meeting — a notable pullback from earlier market expectations — while Japan's 10-year government bond yield climbed to 2.49% on Monday, its highest since 1997. Finance Minister Katayama warned she was prepared to take "bold action" to support the yen, a statement widely interpreted as an intervention threat; the absence of pushback from Treasury Secretary Bessent was read as implicit US tolerance. Singapore became the first Asian central bank to tighten monetary settings in response to rising energy-driven inflation, a move that may signal broader regional policy divergence if crude remains elevated. Chinese macro hedge funds suffered notable losses, with Bridgewater's All Weather Plus strategy falling 5.6% between late February and early April and Shanghai Longlife's top-performing fund declining 25% in March alone. ---

Europe

Hungary: A Political Inflection Point

Viktor Orbán's 16-year tenure ended on Sunday when opposition leader Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured a supermajority in parliament — the most significant shift in Hungarian politics in over a generation and, by extension, one of the more consequential developments for EU cohesion in recent years. The forint surged to a four-year high following the result. The practical European implications are immediate: Orbán's Hungary had held up approximately €90 billion in EU support for Ukraine and repeatedly exercised veto power to fracture sanctions consensus. Magyar has pledged to reorient Hungary toward Brussels, supports eventual euro adoption, and has signaled a swift transition of power. German Chancellor Merz moved quickly to press for disbursement of a €90 billion EU loan for Ukraine. The result simultaneously set back both Trump, who had endorsed Orbán, and Putin, who had relied on Budapest as a consistent obstruction within EU deliberations. That said, Magyar inherits an economy that has consistently underperformed regional peers Poland and the Czech Republic, and Hungary's dependence on Russian energy and existing commercial ties to Asian battery manufacturers will constrain the pace of reorientation.

ECB Policy: Deliberate Ambiguity

ECB President Lagarde declined to signal either a hike or a hold at the IMF Spring Meetings in Washington, placing the bank explicitly between its March baseline — which incorporated two rate increases in 2026 — and its adverse scenario projecting 3.5% inflation this year. She stated the ECB has no tightening bias while simultaneously acknowledging that "looking through" the energy shock risks undermining price stability credibility. Those positions are in tension, and Lagarde did not resolve them. People familiar with internal deliberations suggest the April 30 meeting is likely to result in a hold, with policymakers choosing to defer judgment on the conflict's full economic impact. A Bloomberg survey nonetheless points to a June hike as 2026 inflation runs above earlier projections. The Bank of England's Bailey struck a similar note of deliberate patience.

Corporate Earnings and Economic Stress

European luxury goods suffered a meaningful earnings shock this week. Hermès shares fell as much as 14% on Wednesday — its largest single-day decline on record — after first-quarter sales growth disappointed, with the war's confidence effects cited alongside broader consumer caution. LVMH reported that its fashion and leather goods division saw sales fall in the first quarter, with Middle East uncertainty compounding structural demand headwinds. Kering also disappointed. The counterpoint came from ASML, Europe's most valuable company, which raised its full-year sales forecast to €36–40 billion from €34–39 billion, driven by AI-related semiconductor demand, with its CEO noting supply cannot meet demand "for the foreseeable future." easyJet shares fell steeply after warning of a first-half loss attributable to travel disruptions. Alstom fell 36% on Thursday after withdrawing its free cash flow guidance for the full year. Lufthansa fell 4.4% after pilots announced a two-day strike over pensions.

UK Politics and Macro

UK Prime Minister Starmer faces a deepening political crisis after it emerged that Ambassador Mandelson received security clearance despite objections from vetting officials, complicated by his past associations with Jeffrey Epstein. Starmer dismissed Foreign Office civil servant Olly Robbins in response, but calls for his resignation are growing. The episode arrives as Starmer was attempting to co-host with France a summit on establishing a naval force to ensure freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz. UK household energy costs are tracking roughly £500 higher for a typical household, and the RICS homebuyer appetite gauge fell to its lowest since August 2023. ---

Global

US-Iran Conflict: The Gap Between Diplomacy and Operational Reality

The week opened with the aftermath of collapsed US-Iran talks in Islamabad and President Trump's announcement of a full naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz — a significant escalation following US and Israeli strikes on Iran on February 28. By the week's end, Trump expressed optimism about a permanent ceasefire, claiming Iran had conceded on nuclear ambitions and Hormuz access, though Tehran has not publicly confirmed those terms. The ceasefire entered its second week, a second round of talks is being arranged, and a two-week extension is reportedly under consideration. Yet the gap between diplomatic tone and operational reality remains substantial: the Strait remains effectively closed, with only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker completing an exit with crude cargo since the blockade's formal imposition. A US-sanctioned Chinese-linked tanker transited the Strait on Tuesday, directly testing enforcement credibility. Iran warned that "no port in the Persian Gulf and Sea of Oman will be safe" if its own facilities are targeted. Gulf Arab and European officials believe a durable agreement is more likely six months away than within weeks.

Energy Markets: Volatility Moderating, Structural Damage Accumulating

Brent crude rose as much as 9.1% to approach $104 on Monday's Asian open before retreating toward $95–98 as peace signals accumulated through the week. WTI traded near $92–93 by week's end. Price volatility has cooled markedly from the record $38 intraday range seen in mid-March, with Brent trading in approximately a $10 band this week. European natural gas futures spiked nearly 18% at one point on Monday before partially recovering. The IEA issued a striking forecast: global oil demand is expected to decline in 2026 for the first time since the pandemic, as the price surge offsets underlying demand growth. Industrial metals diverged from the calming oil picture — aluminum reached four-year highs above $3,600 per ton on Thursday, with JPMorgan characterizing the market as entering a structural supply "black hole" following Iranian strikes on Gulf smelters and forecasting prices could approach $4,000 per ton.

Global Growth: Deteriorating Baseline

The IMF-World Bank Spring Meetings in Washington provided a sobering macro backdrop. The Fund cut its global growth forecast, with Bloomberg Economics' base case now at 2.9% for 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — and a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 a barrel. Global trade volume growth is projected at 2.8%, below global GDP growth of 3.1%. Qatar's Finance Minister described current market conditions as "the tip of the iceberg," and participants broadly expect structural consequences — higher costs, longer trade routes, elevated geopolitical risk premiums — to persist well beyond any ceasefire. Economist Carmen Reinhart argued the world is less prepared to respond to a crisis than at any point in the past two decades, constrained by a $348 trillion global debt burden, central banks still contending with inflation, and a fragmented multilateral order. Panama Canal congestion reached its worst since the 2023–2024 drought, with vessels facing 3.5-day waits. The Port of Los Angeles chief estimated full shipping normalization will take approximately four and a half months even after the Strait reopens.

Food Security and Fertilizer

Food security represents a slower-moving but potentially consequential transmission channel. Gulf-region countries account for at least 30% of global fertilizer supply, including urea, ammonia, and diammonium phosphate. A Farm Bureau survey found more than 70% of US farmers cannot currently afford sufficient fertilizer at prevailing prices. Price effects will emerge with a harvest-cycle lag, and a prolonged conflict risks compounding input cost inflation with yield reductions. The US Justice Department has opened a probe into fertilizer and farm input pricing practices. Global Trade Alert has tracked more than 250 distinct government policy responses since hostilities began, the large majority being liberalizing import measures, energy subsidies, or demand-reduction programs — a notably broad-based response reflecting the diffuse nature of the supply disruption.

Longer-Run Structural Shifts

The conflict is accelerating several structural transitions that were already underway. LNG's credibility as a long-term energy solution for emerging Asia has been materially set back — a second price shock in four years is accelerating diversification toward renewables, domestic gas, and coal across Vietnam, Pakistan, and elsewhere, with implications for Big Oil's strategy in developing markets. The geothermal case in Europe is gaining institutional momentum, with more than 60 companies urging EU leaders to strengthen support for a domestic, dispatchable, and infrastructure-resilient technology. Delta Air Lines quietly downgraded its net-zero emissions goal to an "aspiration" and removed its 2030 sustainable aviation fuel target. Anthropic's Mythos AI model — described as capable of identifying system vulnerabilities and orchestrating cyberattacks — emerged as an unexpected focal point at the Spring Meetings, with central bank governors from the ECB, Bank of England, and New York Fed all raising the issue publicly, reflecting institutional alarm at a moment of already-strained coordination capacity.
