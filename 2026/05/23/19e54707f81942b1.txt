The Middle East supplies the world with three inputs: energy, trade routes and 
capital. The Iran war isdisrupting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-19/economic-outlook-at-risk-of-further-deterioration-oecd-says?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 all of them at once. Oil and gas are facing their biggest shock in history. 
Constricted trade arteries are choking flows of fertilizers, helium and sulfur 
as well as energy. And the supply of petrodollars from the region to the world 
is under threat.

In normal times, the petrodollar machine is simple. Gulf states sell oil and 
gas, spend part of the proceeds at home then invest the surplus abroad. Over 
decades, that flow has built some of the worldтАЩs largestsovereign wealth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/saudi-1-trillion-wealth-fund-weighs-creating-a-logistics-giant?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 funds. Several now manage assets approaching a trillion dollars.

Their portfolios reach across continents and asset classes. They own American 
tech stocks, English football clubs, Egyptian real estate, African mines and 
Turkish deposits. For years, Gulf capital has helped absorb US debt andsuppress 
borrowing costs <>. If that flow changes, even the worldтАЩs deepest financial 
market will feel it.

Gulf wealth isnтАЩt just an economic tool, itтАЩs also a geopolitical weapon: it 
buys influence and allies. It can steady economies, sway bailouts and deepen 
ties with Washington. If the war drains that cash, the Gulf loses power, not 
just money, as detailed below.

The petrodollar machine, as I outlined in a recent paper 
<https://www.belfercenter.org/research-analysis/theory-everything-middle-east-political-economy>
, let Gulf states diversify their incomes without remaking their political 
economies. By building giant sovereign wealth funds, they could enjoy incomes 
unmoored from hydrocarbons and avoid the harder bargain of taxing citizens.

But the machine that generated wealth, influence and stability is now starting 
to falter. For nearly three months, the Iran war has largely shut the Strait of 
Hormuz. ThatтАЩs curbed energy flows from the region to the world, and income 
flows from the world back to the Gulf.

The six Gulf states are also spending more. Defense, economic support and 
logistics outlays are rising. Saudi ArabiaтАЩs first-quarter budget shows the 
hard numbers: one month of fighting cost about $17 billion, or1.5% of GDP <>.

And the costs will probably endure even after the war ends. There will be 
continued defense spending, both to replenish depleted systems and to reflect 
the new reality of the region being amore dangerous place 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/iran-war-trauma-of-middle-east-conflict-will-reshape-gulf-arab-states?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
.

There also will be a repair bill for damaged infrastructure. Qatar says one 
gas facility could takefive years to restore 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/iran-strike-to-cost-qatarenergy-20-billion-a-year-in-lost-sales?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
. New pipelines that bypass Hormuz, like the onethe UAE is constructing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/uae-to-complete-new-hormuz-bypass-oil-pipeline-by-2027?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
, will cost big bucks. On the revenue side, even after the conflict ends, 
export earnings may see lingering challenges. QatarтАЩs losses from its damaged 
gas facility alone could add up to $20 billion.

Combine lower revenues with higher spending, and the conclusion is 
unavoidable: the pool of Gulf capital available for investment abroad is 
shrinking.

One consequence, as this newsletter highlighted in March 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-14/war-damages-gulf-nation-ambitions-and-trump-deals-new-economy?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
, will be even greater doubt about the regionтАЩs recent pledges to US President 
Donald Trump of trillions of dollars worth of investments and deals. There will 
be many others.

ItтАЩs important to highlight that the flow of petrodollars was already changing 
before the Iran war. The clearest example comes from the regionтАЩs biggest 
economy, Saudi Arabia. The kingdomтАЩs original ambition was to globalize its 
savings. In 2016, Riyadh wanted foreign assets toeventually approach half 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2016-04-21/the-2-trillion-project-to-get-saudi-arabia-s-economy-off-oil?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 of its sovereign wealth fund portfolio.

Then reality got in the way. By 2024, Saudi Arabia already had shifted from 
being a net lender to the world to anet borrower <>, and was one of the largest 
bond issuers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-01-06/saudi-arabia-plans-37-billion-borrowing-spree-to-pay-for-huge-projects?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 in emerging markets. Late that year, Riyadh overhauled its goals, with the 
Public Investment Fund cutting its targeted share of global investmentsto just 
18% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-10-29/saudi-pif-governor-talks-up-kingdom-s-domestic-focus-and-ai-push?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
.

The Iran war has accelerated this shift in mindset. LIV Golf is preparing for 
potential bankruptcy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-19/liv-golf-begins-potential-bankruptcy-plans-for-end-of-season?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 after the sovereign wealth fundpulled funding 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-20/liv-golf-saudi-bridge-to-trump-falls-victim-to-spending-shift?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
. Riyadh is abandoning not just the $5 billion it poured into the venture over 
four years, but also a bridge to deepen ties with Trump, a major golf investor.

The Saudis also backed away <> from a $200 million deal with New YorkтАЩs 
Metropolitan Opera, citing the Hormuz disruption. Instead of sending 
petrodollars abroad, Saudi Arabia is now courting capital at home. Its $35 
billionAramco privatization 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/aramco-cracks-open-its-empire-to-wall-street-in-35-billion-push?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 push shows the new direction of travel for capital flows.

But Gulf money has done more than fund tech, sports and entertainment. It has 
helped keep fragile states afloat. Those capital flows helpedrescue Egypt 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2024-03-16/oil-rich-abu-dhabi-s-35-billion-gamble-on-egypt?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
, eased PakistanтАЩs default scare andsupported JordanтАЩs <> currency peg. Such 
demands will only grow, with Gaza, Lebanon and Syria all in need of rebuilding. 
Diminished Gulf resources will make such challenges all the greater.

To be sure, the Hormuz disruption isnтАЩt hitting all Gulf economies equally <>. 
Some are breaking even, and Oman is likelyearning more 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/iran-in-talks-with-oman-over-permanent-toll-system-for-hormuz?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260523&utm_content=4721>
 than before the war. But others, including Kuwait and Qatar тАФ key exporters of 
capital тАФ have seen their exports almost grind to a halt. And even winners face 
the risk of sudden income cuts during conflict.

The Iran war isnтАЩt just trapping oil and gas tankers, itтАЩs also rerouting 
money. Gulf states will spend less on flashy deals and foreign rescues. More 
money will stay home, funding security, subsidies and pipelines. The longer the 
conflict lasts, the more the Gulf will turn inward.
