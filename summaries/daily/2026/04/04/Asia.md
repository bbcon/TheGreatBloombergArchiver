Australian authorities are grappling with a sharp rise in e-bike-related injuries and public safety incidents, reflecting broader tensions between green transport ambitions and the practical challenges of regulating a fast-growing category of micro-mobility devices. The issue gained particular visibility in February when approximately 40 teenage riders on e-bikes disrupted traffic on the Sydney Harbour Bridge, an incident that went viral and intensified political pressure on state and federal governments. Serious injuries involving e-bikes have risen materially over the past two years, with trauma physicians pointing to a "perfect storm" of looser import standards, higher power limits, and surging adoption among younger riders.

The regulatory backdrop is instructive. Australia's federal government relaxed import safety standards in 2021, allowing higher-powered models into the market that industry participants describe as closer to motorbikes than bicycles. New South Wales compounded the issue in 2023 by raising the legal power ceiling to 500 watts, putting the state out of alignment with national norms and, critics argue, incentivising further tampering. Both decisions are now being unwound: New South Wales last month restored the 250-watt European standard, and the federal government reintroduced European import standards late in 2025. Minimum rider age requirements, likely in the 12–16 range, are under active consideration.

The episode illustrates a regulatory pattern evident across multiple jurisdictions — initial enthusiasm for low-carbon transport alternatives outpacing the governance frameworks needed to manage them safely. Whether Australia's course correction proves sufficient remains to be seen, as enforcement of power limits and age restrictions has historically been difficult in practice.
