**Monetary Policy: ECB and Bank of England**

Both major European central banks held policy rates unchanged during the week, though the context differed meaningfully. The BOE's Wednesday decision was a 5-4 vote in favor of holding—a more divided committee than the pre-meeting consensus framing implied—and Governor Bailey's subsequent endorsement of a roughly 50% probability of a March cut shifted the market interpretation toward a resumption of easing. The MPC's growing confidence that UK inflation has been contained has shifted the policy risk function toward the labour market, where softening signals have accumulated; sterling and short sterling rates will remain sensitive to incoming wage and employment data. The ECB's hold reflected continued patience as policymakers assessed whether ongoing disinflation is durable. Euro-area HICP sank further below the 2% target through the period, sharpening the policy debate, while ECB Governing Council member Stournaras downplayed concerns about recent euro appreciation. The asymmetric question of how the Council's reaction function handles target undershoots—versus the overshoots of 2022–23—remains an open and meaningful one for markets.

**UK Political Risk**

Prime Minister Starmer's position weakened materially through the week. Labour MPs were described as being in a "febrile, mutinous mood" over his ties to Peter Mandelson, whose appointment as US ambassador carries complications given Mandelson's longstanding association with Jeffrey Epstein. Parliament voted to compel disclosure of related documents, a significant development in its own right. Sterling came under pressure and gilt yields reflected a renewed political risk premium, though rivals have so far refrained from a formal leadership challenge. How long Starmer can maintain his position absent a credible challenger—or a political recovery—remains genuinely uncertain.

**Macro and Corporate**

German factory orders posting their fastest two-year rise offered a constructive datapoint for an economy that closed 2025 with stronger-than-expected growth. Chancellor Merz separately pursued Middle Eastern energy suppliers to reduce German reliance on US LNG, a notable strategic pivot given transatlantic frictions. On the corporate side, the week was eventful. Novo Nordisk's Monday plunge—among the largest single-day declines in its history—carried index-level implications for Denmark and reset sell-side assumptions around GLP-1 competitive dynamics. Shell reported adjusted net income of $3.26 billion in Q4, down 11% year-on-year, on softer crude, weak oil trading, and chemicals losses; it maintained its $3.5 billion quarterly buyback. UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat, underscoring capital return confidence post-Credit Suisse integration. HSBC moved toward a more Wall Street-style compensation model, preparing minimal or zero bonuses for underperformers. The proposed Rio Tinto–Glencore tie-up collapsed within 24 hours over price disagreements on Thursday; Glencore shares fell roughly 11%, leaving the company's strategic direction uncertain with Ivan Glasenberg remaining a key shareholder. Equinor cut its 2026 buyback to as much as $1.5 billion from $5 billion in 2025 after Q4 profit missed on lower commodity prices.

**Trade and Auto Sector**

French customs data released during the week provided concrete evidence of US tariff pass-through: Q4 2025 spirits exports to the US fell 47%, wine 39%, and perfumes and cosmetics 25%—meaningful declines for export-sensitive French consumer and luxury categories. Stellantis's €22 billion in EV-related charges on Thursday—exceeding analyst expectations and covering model cancellations and supplier compensation—extended the auto sector's retreat from electrification timelines that has now touched Ford, GM, Porsche, and Stellantis. The scale of the writedown carries implications for European auto supply chains and raises further questions about the continent's path toward 2035 combustion-engine phase-out targets.
