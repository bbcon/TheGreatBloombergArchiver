Kevin Can’t Wait (to Shake Up the Fed)

There’s a new sheriff at the Fed, and he’s a taciturn fellow. Kevin Warsh, who 
would make an excellent news editor, cut the Federal Open Markets Committee’s 
statement from 341 to 130 words, andrefused even to leave his own dot 
<https://bloom.bg/4fN6JuN> on the Fed’s plot of members’ projections for 
interest rates in the future.

Warsh’s affection for concision makes it much easier to report the Fed’s views 
in full. Here is what the FOMC said:

The Federal Open Market Committee approved the following statement for release 
by a 12–0 vote: The Committee decided to maintain the target range for the 
federal funds rate at 3-1/2 to 3-3/4 percent, in support of the Federal 
Reserve’s dual mandate. The Committee reaffirmed its policy of maintaining 
ample reserves in the banking system. Economic activity is expanding at a solid 
pace despite elevated uncertainty that owes, in part, to the conflict in the 
Middle East. Productivity growth and capital investment are strong. Job gains 
have kept pace with the workforce, and the unemployment rate has changed 
little. Inflation remains elevated relative to the Committee’s 2 percent goal, 
in part reflecting supply shocks that have driven price increases in certain 
sectors, including energy. The Committee will deliver price stability.

In this case, it might be more important to analyze form than to assess 
substance. The stripped-down message makes plain that Warsh isn’t going to keep 
up the policy of forward guidance. Its very brevity comes across as a 
surprisingly strong statement of hawkish intent, and there’s nothing in there, 
that I can see, to open up any excuse to cut rates.

That’s certainly how it was interpreted in the markets. This is how fed funds 
futures’ estimate of the number of 25 basis-point hikes by the end of this year 
moved during the day:

It also drove a very sharp tightening in financial conditions. Most 
meaningfully, this affected real yields. By provoking a rise in the Treasury 
Inflation Protected Securities (TIPS) 10-year yield to its highest since last 
May, Warsh appears to have succeeded in demonstrating that he isn’t there just 
to do the president’s bidding and cut rates. He’s also sprung a major surprise:

The FOMC also helped the dollar to its strongest rise of the year so far. 
Peace in Iran <https://bloom.bg/4xB22uo> was expected to reduce demand for the 
US currency as a haven and help it to decline — an explicit aim of the 
administration. This is an unwelcome surprise to much of the rest of the world:

For the future, Warsh also seems determined to wreak changes, albeit not quite 
yet, as he announced five task forces to cover: (i) communication, (ii) the 
balance sheet/operating system; (iii) alternative data sources; (iv)
productivity, jobs and artificial intelligence <https://bloom.bg/4fTma4M>; (v) 
the inflation framework. The task forces might not report until the end of the 
year, so this is more of a declaration of intent than anything else, and it 
preserves Warsh’s optionality. But it certainly suggests change is on the way.

One thing that’s not changing, however, is the Fed’s 2% inflation target. Many 
believe that it’s been tacitly abandoned, and a 3% target would have made it 
easier to cut rates, as President Donald Trump wants — but Warsh, it appears, 
is not going there.

However, even though Warsh himself refused to play the game of the dot plot, 
18 of his colleagues did, and he used the exercise to make clear that they 
thought they were likely to be hiking rates before they cut. They moved sharply 
toward hikes:

Overall, the average projected rate by the end of this year has risen from 
3.24% to 3.83%. So if Warsh set out his stall for a new communications strategy 
that eschews forward guidance, his new colleagues used the existing approach to 
make clear that they expect to move in a hawkish direction.

Dario Perkins of TS Lombard in London commented that scrapping the dot plot 
would be “easy – it was designed for an era of permazero rates – but it would 
be a much bigger challenge to shrink the Fed’s balance sheet, or move to 
completely new modelling and data sources.” In any case, those challenges 
couldn’t have been met this week — all lie ahead.

There was never any chance of a change of rates this week. The fall in oil 
prices had presented Warsh with a golden opportunity to duck the hard questions 
altogether and hint that he really was the dovish agent some had thought. He 
didn’t do that, which is to his credit. In the process he surprised the markets 
— and possibly his colleagues, and the president who appointed him. If he 
forges ahead with a regime of less forward guidance, we will all have to get 
used to more surprises like this.

Burnham All Down?

The UK is about to have its own Super Thursday. With the 10th anniversary of 
the Brexit vote coming next week, a divided Monetary Policy Committee of the 
Bank of England meets in the morning to decide whether to follow the European 
Central Bank in hiking rates, while about 200 miles to the north, the voters in 
the Makerfield <https://bloom.bg/4ewLIlV> byelection decide whether Greater 
Manchester’s mayor, Andy Burnham, should be given the parliamentary seat he 
needs if he is to challenge Keir Starmer for the prime ministership.

What once looked like a terrifying date with destiny now looks a little easier 
to navigate. The latest UK inflation numbers came in below expectations. The 
core consumer price index is now actually below its US equivalent, even though 
the UK is more dependent on energy imports:

At the last meeting of the MPC, the chief economist Huw Pill 
<https://bloom.bg/4uLIscu> voted to raise rates. Until a few days ago, the 
general expectation was that at least one more would join him. But the mild 
inflation added to the news from Strait of Hormuz to bring the projected Bank 
Rate crushing down. At the last MPC, the market was braced for three hikes this 
year. That’s now down to one:

That is in large part because UK rates are viewed as wholly dependent on the 
price of oil. This is an odd judgment given the widespread arguments that the 
BoE and other central banks shouldn’t be too hasty to react to a potentially 
transitory oil shock. But as Mizuho Group’s Jordan Rochester points out, the 
projected interest rate for the end of next year has been trading almost 
perfectly in line with fluctuations in oil:

If the MPC is unlikely now to produce a shock, the unlikely figure of Elon 
Musk is also making the Makerfield byelection more predictable. The sitting MP, 
Josh Simon, precipitated the contest by resigning last month, saying that he 
was doing so to allow Burnham a path to parliament and the prime ministership. 
As Nigel Farage’s populist-right Reform Party had just bested Labour in local 
elections in the constituency, all seemed set fair for an epic contest.

But Musk appears to have split <https://bloom.bg/4efsOku> the anti-Burnham 
vote by vocally supportingRestore Britain <https://bloom.bg/3SfUrRU>, a 
splinter group from Reform UK. Polls suggest that Burnham should win with some 
ease, while prediction markets now give him a 70% chance of swiftly winning the 
premiership:

How much damage would this do to UK assets? Starmer has said he will contest 
any challenge, meaning that the next few months could be taken up with a 
leadership contest, creating uncertainty. And Burnham at this point appears to 
beoutflanking Starmer from the left <https://bloom.bg/4uJNtlF>. An early 
campaign gaffe that the UK should not be “in hock to the bond markets,” along 
with his interest in nationalizing some utilities, pays directly into such a 
narrative.

But it’s questionable how much of an impact a Prime Minister Burnham would 
have. As prediction markets show, he is already thought likely to have the job 
by the end of this year, and it hasn’t provoked great alarm. Britain’s fiscal 
position would make it difficult to make significant changes, and the terrible 
disciplining example of formerPrime Minister Liz Truss, 
<https://bloom.bg/4xFQGWg> brought down by the gilts market within 49 days, 
will make any UK politician reluctant to tread too far. The bond market already 
prices in fiscal risks, as gilts trade at a higher yield than Treasuries, and 
has signaled elevated risk ever since Starmer’s victory two years ago — but 
it’s fair to assume that it has the most alarming political outcomes covered:

There are still plenty of variables. A Burnham loss, which now looks unlikely, 
would leave an extremely damaged Labour Party to limp on for three years, and 
force investors to confront the very different risks that would be posed by a 
Reform government. If Burnham ekes out only a narrow victory, it might dim his 
luster and give Starmer a better chance to hang on.

But the likelihood is that the UK limps on with much the same policies it 
currently has, whether under Starmer or under a slightly more talented 
politician, while the Bank of England keeps rates where they are. It’s not an 
inspiring prospect, but markets appear to be priced for it.
