Kevin Warsh's confirmation hearing before the Senate Banking Committee dominated domestic attention on Tuesday, though the political dynamics surrounding it may matter more than anything Warsh actually says. The nominee is expected to vow to protect the Fed's independence while simultaneously arguing that the central bank has overreached its statutory mandate in areas beyond monetary policy — a formulation carefully calibrated to reassure markets without alienating the administration that nominated him. The more consequential obstacle to confirmation remains Senator Thom Tillis's stance linking his support to the resolution of the case against incumbent Jerome Powell over Fed renovation expenditures. Warsh's opening remarks, leaked Monday, signal a preference for a narrower Fed role and a smaller balance sheet — positions that carry genuine market implications, though he appears to have avoided committing to specifics in his five-page statement. Any signal on quantitative tightening will be watched closely given the sensitivity of bond markets to balance sheet rhetoric.

US equity markets continued their April advance on low conviction. The S&P 500 is up approximately 9% this month, closing above 7,000 for the first time last week, prompting JPMorgan to raise its year-end target to 7,600. However, the rally's technical underpinnings remain fragile: fewer than half of S&P 500 members traded above their 50-day moving averages at the index's record, volume has run 11% below the six-month average, and investor sentiment indicators have actually declined during the advance. The Nasdaq 100 logged a 13-session winning streak — its longest since 2013 — before pausing Monday. On the corporate front, Apple confirmed that Tim Cook will transition to executive chairman in September, handing the CEO role to hardware chief John Ternus. Amazon gained roughly 3% in premarket trading after committing an additional $5 billion to Anthropic. Alaska Air suspended profit guidance and flagged a deeper-than-expected second-quarter loss, citing rising fuel costs attributable to the Strait of Hormuz disruption. Trump invoked the Defense Production Act to direct federal funds toward energy infrastructure, including coal, LNG, petroleum and grid projects, as his administration faces pressure over elevated fuel prices.
