Friday, July 03, 2026

US · Asia · Europe · LatAm · Global

US

  • June jobs data showed a sharp slowdown in hiring, coming in below consensus and materially undermining the case for near-term Fed rate increases; gold advanced 1.4% on the print.
  • Trump administration and outside allies are actively exploring additional pathways to remove Fed Board members, including Governor Lisa Cook and former Chair Jerome Powell, following the Supreme Court's narrow ruling this week that blocked Cook's immediate dismissal.
  • US equity cash markets are closed for Independence Day; S&P 500 futures rose 0.2% and Nasdaq 100 futures gained 0.8%, while the dollar headed for its worst weekly performance since April.

Labor Market and Monetary Policy

June nonfarm payrolls came in materially below consensus, delivering the most consequential US data point of the week. The weaker hiring print reduced market expectations for any near-term Fed rate increase, with gold extending gains and two-year Treasury yields declining in response. The data appears consistent with an economy that is decelerating gradually under the weight of elevated rates, though it remains to be seen whether one softer print constitutes a trend. Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh, notably, has signaled he views market prices as the primary guide for monetary policy, a framework that independent analysts at TS Lombard have criticized as insufficiently transparent regarding the Fed's reaction function.

Fed Independence Under Pressure

The Supreme Court's ruling this week reinforced the institutional independence of the Federal Reserve in principle, but the decision's narrowness — ruling only that Cook deserved a reasonable opportunity to defend herself, not that she is permanently shielded — has emboldened the administration. Trump allies are now treating the ruling as a procedural roadmap. The episode represents a sustained, deliberate effort to reshape the composition of the Fed's Board of Governors, and the outcome remains genuinely uncertain. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi surged 5.8%, the standout regional performer, recovering from two days of tech-led losses as AI sentiment stabilized.
  • The Japanese yen touched its weakest level against the dollar since 1986, prompting options traders to pay elevated premiums to hedge against intervention; the yen subsequently firmed as holiday intervention risk was priced in.
  • A fatal light-aircraft crash into Beijing's CITIC Tower triggered a selloff in China's low-altitude aviation sector, with regulatory uncertainty weighing on eVTOL-linked stocks including US-listed EHang.

AI Trade and the K-Shaped Asian Economy

The AI investment cycle is producing starkly divergent economic outcomes across Asia. Northeast Asia — Taiwan in semiconductors and advanced packaging, South Korea in memory, Japan in upstream materials and machinery — is benefiting directly from the data-center capex surge, which Bloomberg Intelligence estimates could push global data-center market size toward $1 trillion by 2027. Hong Kong has emerged as a critical re-export conduit, now accounting for more than half of China's $239 billion in chip imports in the first five months of 2026. South Korea's transition to 24-hour won trading beginning Monday, central to its bid for MSCI developed-market reclassification, arrives at a delicate moment: the won has weakened to levels last seen in 2009.

Japan-India Strategic Alignment

Japanese Prime Minister Takaichi's visit to New Delhi produced over 100 business agreements, covering semiconductors, energy supply chains, and critical minerals, alongside closer defense cooperation. The bilateral rapprochement, with China as the common strategic concern, reflects a concrete operationalization of the geopolitical framework Shinzo Abe outlined conceptually. China's simultaneous expansion of export controls on Japanese companies adds urgency to Tokyo's diversification push. ---

Europe

  • ECB President Christine Lagarde declined to rule out departing her role early to participate in French politics, injecting an element of institutional uncertainty into eurozone monetary policy.
  • Bank of England rate-setter Catherine Mann signaled openness to an "activist" rate hike if inflation expectations fail to improve, while Governor Bailey cautioned it is too early to contemplate cuts — the MPC thus remains on hold at 3.75% with a hawkish tilt.
  • Chancellor Merz stated that Germany's newly announced reform package could lift GDP growth above 1% in 2027, against a government forecast of just 0.5% for 2026.

ECB and Bank of England Policy

The ECB's policy stance is evolving against a backdrop of leadership uncertainty. Lagarde's comments introduce a non-trivial possibility of a transition at the top of the institution before her term concludes, which could affect the pace and sequencing of any further policy adjustments. Separately, ECB policymaker Emmanuel Moulin described the bank as in a "good position" following recent rate hikes. At the Bank of England, the internal debate between Mann's hawkish activism and Bailey's caution reflects genuine uncertainty about UK inflation's trajectory — UK companies, per Bloomberg Economics data, plan to raise prices by 4% over the coming year, double the 2% CPI target.

German Growth Outlook

The Stoxx 600 rose 0.2% to approach back-to-back record highs, with mining and utility stocks leading. Germany's reform package, encompassing tax relief and labor market measures, offers a meaningful upside scenario for an economy that had previously halved its own growth forecast for 2026. Whether the legislative and implementation timeline delivers on Merz's projections for 2027 remains to be seen, but the direction of fiscal policy in Europe's largest economy has shifted meaningfully toward stimulus. ---

LatAm

  • Senator Flávio Bolsonaro filed an 86-page submission urging the Trump administration to refrain from imposing additional duties on Brazilian exports ahead of October's election, highlighting the overlap between US trade policy and Brazilian electoral politics.
  • A new poll found a majority of Argentines disapprove of President Milei's handling of the corruption scandal involving former cabinet chief Manuel Adorni, who resigned days ago.
  • Venezuela's Acting President Delcy Rodríguez faces mounting political pressure, with nearly half of Venezuelans prioritizing new elections over post-earthquake reconstruction.

Brazil Trade Politics

The Bolsonaro family's lobbying of the Trump administration on trade tariffs has become a liability for Flávio Bolsonaro's electoral prospects, a dynamic that illustrates the increasingly direct linkage between US trade policy decisions and political outcomes in the region. The submission itself is an acknowledgment that prior engagement with Washington has generated domestic political costs, and the outcome of the tariff question ahead of October's vote will bear watching.

Argentina Political Risk

The Adorni corruption scandal has dealt a measurable blow to Milei's approval, arriving at a moment when the administration's reform credibility is central to investor confidence. Argentina's macro stabilization program remains ongoing, but political cohesion within the government appears to be fraying. The situation warrants monitoring, as a sustained deterioration in Milei's political capital could complicate his ability to sustain the fiscal adjustment that underpins the program. ---

Global

  • Brent crude fell approximately 30% in the second quarter, unwinding all war-related gains; Citigroup now forecasts Brent could reach $60/barrel by year-end as Hormuz flows normalize.
  • An estimated 8,000 seafarers from non-Gulf nations remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, with a US-Iran interim peace deal failing as yet to produce full clearance of the strait.
  • Leading European powers and some Gulf Arab officials are privately accepting that vessels transiting the Strait of Hormuz will need to pay fees to Iran and Oman, a significant normalization of what would constitute an effective toll on global energy trade.

Hormuz Normalization and Oil Outlook

The pace of crude market rebalancing following the US-Iran conflict is faster than many had anticipated. Citigroup's $60/barrel year-end forecast reflects a view that the fundamental supply-demand picture — Chinese buyers remaining absent, inventories drawing less than expected, and physical crude markets weakening — was always more bearish than wartime risk premia implied. Saudi Arabia's export volumes have reportedly recovered toward pre-war levels, and Qatar appears to have sent the first visible LNG tanker through the strait in approximately a week. However, the emergence of an effective transit fee regime, if it consolidates, would represent a structural change to the cost of energy shipment through the world's most critical chokepoint.

Monetary Policy Divergence

Global central bank policy is diverging in ways that are beginning to affect capital flows. The Fed's pause — reinforced by weak June payrolls — contrasts with the Bank of England's hawkish lean and the ECB's post-hike consolidation phase. Turkish inflation cooled for the first time since the Iran war's outbreak, aided by lower energy prices, a development that may provide some relief to an economy that has absorbed significant commodity-driven price pressures. The yen's weakness to multi-decade lows reflects Japan's continued monetary policy divergence from the rest of the developed world, and the risk of intervention remains live.
