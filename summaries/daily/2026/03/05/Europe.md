European markets are absorbing a renewed energy shock with uncomfortable echoes of 2022. European natural gas futures have risen approximately 60% since hostilities began, breaching €50 per megawatt-hour, while the euro has declined roughly 2% against the dollar since the US-Israeli campaign commenced. The currency move is consistent with a well-established dynamic: Europe's structural dependence on imported energy means commodity price surges translate directly into deteriorating terms of trade, lower purchasing power, and growth headwinds. It was precisely this mechanism that drove the euro below parity during the 2022 gas crisis, and the conditions for a repeat are present, though the severity will depend on conflict duration.

Politically, the energy price surge arrives at a precarious moment for European governments that have barely recovered from the 2022 shock and are already managing historically elevated debt levels. The European Central Bank's Bundesbank chief indicated the institution is "carefully monitoring" the impact of surging energy costs — language that falls short of signaling a policy response but acknowledges the dilemma between growth support and inflation containment that a sustained energy shock would impose.

In the UK, domestic politics are generating their own friction for Prime Minister Keir Starmer. Over 100 Labour MPs wrote privately to Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood urging a rethink of proposed immigration reforms that would tighten residency pathways for low earners. The scale of internal dissent — arriving before Mahmood's planned speech to persuade colleagues — suggests the government's parliamentary management is under strain on multiple fronts. Separately, the Bank of England is planning to war-game a full-blown AI economic shock, including potential incorporation into its banking stress tests, reflecting growing institutional concern that AI-driven job displacement could produce a wave of household and corporate loan defaults material enough to threaten financial stability.
