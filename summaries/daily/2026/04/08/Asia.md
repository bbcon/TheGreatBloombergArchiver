Asian markets registered broad gains following the ceasefire announcement, with the Topix recovering to its highest level since the war began on February 28 and the China CSI 300 Index advancing 3.5%. The relief rally reflected the unwinding of war-premium positioning rather than any fundamental improvement in regional economic conditions, which remain under strain from the energy shock.

China has been one of the most consequential actors in managing the supply disruption. Beijing granted independent teapot refiners additional crude import quotas to allow them to maintain fuel production at mandated levels amid the Persian Gulf disruption — a practical measure that reveals the extent to which China's energy security was stretched. On the LNG side, however, the picture appears more structurally challenging. Hopes for a strong rebound in Chinese LNG demand are fading even with the ceasefire in place; BloombergNEF projects imports will fall to 62.3 million tons in 2026, down from 68.4 million tons last year — a rare decline in nearly two decades of near-uninterrupted growth. Supply risks and elevated prices are expected to persist regardless of ceasefire terms.

The US-China relationship remained in focus as Trade Representative Jamieson Greer promoted the concept of a bilateral board of trade ahead of the anticipated Trump-Xi summit next month. Greer downplayed a parallel investment-focused group and indicated his pre-summit agenda would center on rare earths flows. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi is scheduled to visit North Korea this week for the first time in over six years, a visit that underscores Beijing's effort to consolidate relationships amid shifting geopolitical alignments.

The Reserve Bank of India held rates unchanged, with Governor Sanjay Malhotra acknowledging the ceasefire while cautioning that persistent supply chain disruptions continue to pose risks to growth and inflation. The rupee strengthened 0.5% following his remarks, partially reversing a roughly 7% decline over the past year. In Japan, real wages rose at the fastest pace since 2021, supporting the case for continued Bank of Japan normalization.
