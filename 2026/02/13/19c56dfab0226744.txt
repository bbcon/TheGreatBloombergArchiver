Return of a Dark Horse

When Donald Trump tapped Judy Shelton for a position on the Fed board in his 
first term, it ruffled a lot of feathers: An enthusiast for a monetaryrole for 
gold 
<https://www.centralbanking.com/central-banks/governance/7973352/judy-shelton-on-gold-tariffs-and-where-the-fed-went-wrong>
, she unsettled lawmakers in her own party so deeply that shefailed to win 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-12-03/waller-confirmed-for-fed-as-shelton-s-nomination-still-in-limbo?cmpid=BBD021326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 Senate confirmation in 2020.

But fast forward to today, and she’s making something of a comeback. 
Eagle-eyed viewers of Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent’s brace of congressional 
hearings last week would have noticed Shelton in attendance. More than that, 
she’s met privately with the Treasury chief to discuss central bank reform.

“I would be thrilled to rejoin” Trump’s economic team, Shelton said in a brief 
call Thursday, while adding her Bessent sit-down wasn’t an interview. The 
hourlong meeting spanned ideas forFed reform 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/warsh-call-for-fed-treasury-accord-stirs-debate-bond-market?cmpid=BBD021326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 and international monetary issues, she said. The Treasury didn’t respond to a 
request for comment.

Shelton, an Independent Institute senior fellow, shared her perspectives 
publicly <https://www.foxbusiness.com/video/6388684609112> on Fox Business last 
week, when she also took time to touch base with reporters at the Senate 
Banking hearing.

So now that she’s back in the conversation, what’s Shelton’s advocating? For 
starters, a wholesale rethink of how the Fed should model rates and the 
economy. Policymakers have been coming at the issue through the lens of demand, 
she says. If demand runs hot, raise rates to cool it and keep consumer prices 
contained — complying with the Fed’s statutory mandate.
Judy Shelton watches as US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent testifies during a 
Senate Banking, Housing and Urban Affairs Committee hearing in Washington on 
Feb. 5.Photographer: Tom Williams/CQ-Roll Call, Inc./Getty Images
Shelton, however, advocates coming at it from a supply perspective 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/fed-s-miran-says-there-s-still-a-variety-of-reasons-to-cut-rates?cmpid=BBD021326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.Lower rates and that, in combination with Trump’s other economic policies, 
will allow for companies to expand the nation’s productive potential. That will 
then head offinflationary pressures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/adam-posen-petersen-institute-president-thinks-inflation-will-hit-4-in-2026?cmpid=BBD021326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

She also views the $2.9 trillion that banks have parked at the Fed in the form 
of reserves as a resource that could be redeployed for lending to the “real 
economy.” The central bank currently pays 3.65% to keep the reserves locked in 
— part of its broader framework for controlling short-term rates.

Even if the Trump administration and Warsh were all on board with these ideas, 
implementing them would require much broader buy-in from Fed policymakers, and 
potentially Congress, where even some Republicans were wary about Shelton six 
years ago.

Back then, GOP Senator John Kennedy (who’s still on the Senate Banking panel) 
said, “Nobody wants anybody on the Federal Reserve that has a fatal attraction 
to nutty ideas.”

Nutty or not, Shelton’s ideas are now getting airtime.

Need-to-Know Research

Dissents on central bank policy-setting panels vary widely across developed 
nations, with theBank of England 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/boe-s-breeden-expects-rate-cut-within-next-two-months?cmpid=BBD021326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 seeing at least one minority vote at every meeting since 2022 but Sweden’s
Riksbank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/riksbank-names-watchdog-head-hjelm-to-board-as-rate-cut-debated?cmpid=BBD021326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 only registering them at fewer than 10% of its gatherings in that time, a 
Goldman Sachs analysis shows.

Dissents are less common when the unemployment rate is relatively low compared 
with its trend, and more common when it’s overshooting, as well as in the wake 
of changes in inflation, Goldman’s Sarah Dong wrote in a note Thursday. She 
also found that dissents tend to decline after leadership changes. (A finding 
that may be tested later this year at the Fed.)
Source: Goldman Sachs Global Investment Research
Dissenting votes also offer a signal: They can predict future policy moves, 
Dong’s analysis shows. “However, the signal decays when voters dissent at 
consecutive meetings,” she adds. Persistent dissenters can in those cases 
simply be registering their counter-consensus views. Other times, a dissenter 
can become theproverbial canary 
<https://notyourgrandfathersmining.ca/canary-in-a-coal-mine>, Dong says — 
warning of what’s to come.
