Nvidious Narratives

Markets are driven by narratives; George Soros <https://bloom.bg/3Rdcrfk>, 
Robert Shiller <https://bloom.bg/4filQMH> and others tell us that, and it’s 
true. We also love to attack hubris <https://bloom.bg/4v0cObs>, a sin ever 
since the Greek tragedians <https://bloom.bg/4eTW3tT> coined the word.  AOL’s 
merger with TimeWarner <https://bloom.bg/3Pzz64M> was the prideful peak of the 
dot-com bubble, Citi’s CEO disastrously called the top in 2007 by saying“as 
long as the music’s playing, you’ve got to get up and dance,” 
<https://bloom.bg/49YGk9q> and Glencore’s IPO <https://bloom.bg/4dyYS18> called 
the end to the bull market in metals in 2011.

What will hindsight make of Wednesday? Nvidia Corp. announced 85% sales growth 
and 62% margins, and its stock dropped because investors wanted more; then 
SpaceX <https://bloom.bg/4nFsIWv> announced an IPO in which  Elon Musk 
<https://bloom.bg/4dzWGXj> keeps 85%, with plans for data centers in space and 
asteroid-mining. This sounds like hubris <https://bloom.bg/4dDBFe8>, if not  
Ozymandias <https://bloom.bg/44KjfnH>. Or a  Bond <https://bloom.bg/4v715br> 
villain <https://bloom.bg/42MUA1i>.

SpaceX’s prospectus <https://bloom.bg/4nHf7xT> is literally jaw-dropping, and 
Points of Return will cover it in future. It rendered Nvidia’s incredibly 
strong earnings a non-event:

The focus has shifted to whether the chipmaker can keep its dominant share of 
the artificial intelligence “cake.”Nvidia’s revenue growth 
<https://bloom.bg/49ZLfH8> shows that the momentum of the AI data-center 
buildout is if anything accelerating:

The company rammed home its confidence with an additional $80 billion in share 
repurchases, in what ranks among the largest capital return commitments in 
corporate history. And profit margins continue to tower over industry 
benchmarks:

Wednesday’s earnings suggested Nvidia’s grip on pricing remains remarkably 
firm. Gross margins came broadly in line with analyst expectations. It still 
has pricing power, even if the latest sales forecast wasn’t as bullish as hoped:

So far, Nvidia’s pipeline of increasingly powerful chips is preserving its 
edge. Chief executive Jensen Huang says the Vera Rubin AI supercomputing 
platform, built around next-generation processors, remains on track to ship in 
the second half of the year. It will likely remain supply-constrained, which 
should be good news on pricing for investors, though less welcome for customers.

All of this contributes to a narrative that fits a market top (or 
alternatively, fits a market that shoots to the moon and thence to Mars).
Valuation <https://bloom.bg/4fyVpSP> can never help with timing, but does show 
we’re close to a peak.

But the catalysts are lacking. As Ian Harnett of Absolute Strategy Research 
shows, hikes in rates are a necessary condition for a major market top. US 
rates are falling:

Another necessary condition is an inverted yield curve, when short rates are 
higher than longer-term. That happened in 2022, when rate hikes provided a 
catalyst for the start of a bear market. But that inversion is over:

Circumstantial evidence proves nothing, but it looks like 2022 was the moment 
when the next secular bear market was fated to happen, and ChatGPT thwarted 
destiny. AI is of macroeconomic importance. While earnings keep growing on the 
scale of Nvidia, and optimism remains strong enough to persuade people to buy 
IPO shares in SpaceX, Anthropic or Open AI on what seem to be terrible terms, 
it’s hard for the market to go down.

Then we run into market logic’s final obstacle. Over history, whenever 
earnings have risen like this, they have always brought interest rates up with 
them.

It will be hard to avoid a bear market if the Fed hikes rates. And on that 
subject, the central bank is about to change hands…

—Richard Abbey and John Authers

Warsh and Peace

On Friday, with unusual pageantry, President Donald Trump will swear in Kevin 
Warsh to chair the Federal Reserve. It happens just as the Iran war seems to 
have swallowed the entire saga over choosing Warsh, along with the pitched 
debate over how independent the central bank should be. But it still matters.

When nominated in January, Warsh was a dove who intended to cut rates. A month 
later, war broke out in Iran, and with it changed the entire nature of his job. 
On Feb. 27, futures implied that the fed funds rate would be about 2.8% next 
summer; now, even with Warsh in place, it’s expected to top 4%:

The Federal Open Market Committee that Warsh will now chair appears from the 
minutes of Jerome Powell’s final meeting <https://bloom.bg/4uZpr6H> to be 
comfortable with these expectations, and more hawkish than previously thought. 
The Fed is coy about exact numbers of votes, but word choices paint a picture 
of a building consensus for tightening:

Almost all participants noted even after the conflict ended, the prices of oil 
and other commodities could remain elevated for longer than expected;

Almost all expected “continued upward pressure on inflation arising from 
supply chain disruptions, high energy prices, or the pass-through of higher 
input costs to other prices” if oil prices did stay high;

The vast majority noted an increased risk that inflation would take longer to 
return to 2% than they had previously expected;

A majority highlighted that some policy firming would likely become 
appropriate if inflation were to continue to run persistently above 2%;

Many would have preferred removing the language from the post-meeting 
statement that suggested an easing bias.

The sole change in the committee will see Warsh replace Stephen Miran, its 
most extreme dove. So his chances to corral a majority for cuts look 
negligible. He will have delivered for Trump if he can just dissuade his 
colleagues from hiking. Steve Englander of Standard Chartered Plc describes the 
Warsh FOMC as “capping rates rather than cutting,” while TS Lombard’s Steven 
Blitz suggests that for the new chairman, “no hike in June is a rate cut.”

He does, however, have more weapons at his disposal as chairman. He gets to 
assign his colleagues to committee, and appoint the Fed’s staff of economists. 
He also says he prefers trimmed meaninflation <https://bloom.bg/4uT9tLs> (in 
which outlying components are excluded and an average taken of the rest) to 
core measures, most closely watched at present, that exclude food and fuel.

There’s a good statistical case for this. It’s also very convenient for doves 
to make the change now, because core PCE is surging higher as the trimmed mean 
falls:

Englander suggested that Warsh could “delay hikes by pointing to low unit 
labor cost growth, trimmed-mean inflation and a couple of other indicators.” 
Still, this wouldn’t justify cuts. “There is no inflation crisis, but in our 
view one must be very selective to make a case for inflation is falling.”

Hiking rates would intensify the risk of stagflation; there are arguments to 
stay on hold. But many worry that Trump’s position could still have a malign 
impact. David Kotok of Cumberland Advisors warned: “Any Fed easing will only 
validate the inflation and make it worse while sending bond prices down and 
yields even higher than they would otherwise be.” Meanwhile, others have no 
choice but to hike…


Prabowo’s Elephant

In the last two years, Indonesia has lost its status as a darling among 
emerging markets. Ordinarily, Bank Indonesia’s decision to surprise markets 
Wednesday witha 50-basis-point hike <https://bloom.bg/4fxHlZF> would seem a 
proactive move to contain rising inflationary expectations from the energy 
shock. But that’s not the full story. The presidency of Prabowo Subianto has 
prompted investors to rethink the archipelago’sentire trajectory 
<https://bloom.bg/4v6FCzj>.

While economic nationalism <https://bloom.bg/42P9q7r> is not new in Indonesia, 
Prabowo is pushing the agenda too far for markets’ comfort. The Jakarta 
Composite Index has plunged by almost 10% this month, and as much as 3.5% on 
Tuesday. It’s down 26% for the year, making it the world’s worst equity 
performer:


The catalyst for the latest selloff is the former general’s reimposition of 
sweeping export controls <https://bloom.bg/3RDPZfn> to bring more resources 
under government sway, part of an effort to stabilize the rupiah asit trades 
near historic lows. <https://bloom.bg/4dwBr8E> The president said Wednesday 
that they’ll start with palm oil, thermal coal and nickel, which Indonesia 
dominates. Sales will eventually be handled by a government-appointed exporter 
controlled by Indonesia’s sovereign wealth fund. No wonder this unsettles 
investors. So far, the currency has dropped about 6% this year. This week’s 
selloff piles on the pressure:

Rupiah weakness persisted even after Bank Indonesia last week pledged “smart 
intervention” <https://bloom.bg/42IRAD4> to support it, including offshore and 
domestic non-deliverable forwards, as well as using monetary policy instruments 
to ease the pressure. Less than 48 hours later, BI hiked by a 
larger-than-expected 50 basis points. Even this jumbo move might prove 
insufficient. Indonesia is a net energy exporter, but the surge in oil and gas 
import costs has outpaced gains from coal exports. Jason Tuvey of Capital 
Economics suggests that concerns about policy better explain the rupiah’s 
struggles:

Today’s move is only likely to provide some brief respite for the currency – 
putting the rupiah on a more solid footing ultimately requires the authorities 
to shift away from the populist and interventionist policies adopted since 
President Prabowo came to power.

Before Bank Indonesia’s decision, it sold $3.45 billion of dollar- and 
euro-denominated bonds <https://bloom.bg/4uYuGDw> Tuesday. The government had 
earlier resorted tobuying back its bonds <https://bloom.bg/3PTSWrJ> to cool 
domestic yields and stem outflows. Higher US yields are driving up the dollar 
and fueling capital outflows across Asia, putting markets with idiosyncratic 
troubles like Indonesia under severe strain.

To quote HSBC’s chief Asia economist, Frederic Neumann, “Growth in much of the 
region is set to come under greater pressure, leaving central banks in a bind 
whether and how to respond.” He argues that “we are not out of the woods yet,” 
even after BI’s jumbo hike.

Currency stability is in BI’s mandate. It’s proactively addressing spillovers 
from rising prices, especially non-subsidized fuels. But Prabowo’s policies 
have left the bank to do the heavy lifting. The hike might help, but that will 
prove fleeting if Jakarta keeps defying investors’ logic.

— Richard Abbey
