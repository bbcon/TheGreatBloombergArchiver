The most consequential domestic development heading into this week is the resolution of the Fed leadership impasse. Senator Thom Tillis dropped his blockade of Kevin Warsh's nomination to chair the Federal Reserve after the Justice Department ended its criminal investigation of incumbent Chair Jerome Powell. The episode illustrates how institutional guardrails, however imperfect, retained enough force to produce an orderly outcome. Warsh, who carries both Wall Street and Fed credentials, is widely viewed as the most market-compatible nominee the administration could credibly advance.

The remaining uncertainty concerns Powell's own intentions. His term as chair expires next month, but his governor's seat runs two more years. Whether he elects to remain as a governor — a decision that now appears less certain given the collapse of the legal pressure campaign against him — is likely to be the most closely watched subtext at Wednesday's FOMC meeting. The meeting itself is broadly expected to produce an unchanged rate decision, though markets will parse the statement for signals that sustained energy cost pressures are shifting the committee's reaction function.

Equity markets enter the week at record highs, but the composition of the rally warrants scrutiny. The Philadelphia Semiconductor index has gained 47% over 18 consecutive positive sessions since markets perceived a thaw in the Iranian standoff — the longest winning streak in the index's history. At a trailing price-to-earnings multiple of roughly 60 times, valuations are elevated and appear to price in substantial margin expansion premised on chipmakers' pricing power over hyperscaler customers. That thesis faces a near-term test: Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft all report Wednesday, with Apple following Thursday. If any of these companies signal restraint in capital expenditure on chips, the feedback loop sustaining the semiconductor rally could weaken. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to 49.8 in April, its lowest reading since data collection began in 1978, suggesting the macro backdrop outside of equity markets remains more fragile than headline index levels imply.
