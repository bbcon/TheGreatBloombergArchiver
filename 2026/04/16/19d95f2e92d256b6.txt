Global Trade Alert <https://globaltradealert.org/> has tracked more than 250 
government policy responses to the Iran war since the bombings started on Feb. 
28.

Six weeks in, the situation is moving from short-term jolt to a long-term 
shock as the largest supply disruption in the history of the global oil market. 
Traffic through theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/iran-linked-ships-take-new-path-to-trickle-into-the-persian-gulf?cmpid=BBD041626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 is still largelychoked off 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/pakistan-boosts-mediation-efforts-as-us-iran-weigh-longer-truce?cmpid=BBD041626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 and the destruction of economic infrastructure will, in some cases, take years 
to bounce back from.

The numbers from GTA show a rush to boost supply via imports rather than 
domestic hoarding, mixed with efforts to curb consumption, especially in Asia.

Read More: China Moves to Ban Sulfuric Acid Exports as Iran War Hits Supply 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-10/china-moves-to-ban-sulfuric-acid-exports-as-iran-war-hits-supply?cmpid=BBD041626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>

“The policy response reflects its nature: broad-based, defensive and driven by 
supply security rather than strategic confrontation,” according the GTA’s 
latest analysis released Thursday. The reaction and measures used reveal “the 
kind of crisis this is,” it said.
Source: GTA’s Hormuz Crisis Policy Tracker
Here’s a breakdown from GTA:

 * The tools deployed are “are mostly economy-wide and consumer-oriented” 
rather than targeted at specific industries.
 * Almost a third are subsidies — either fuel tax cuts, excise reductions and 
VAT relief. They work in parallel with caps on retail energy prices, GTA said, 
and this is happening in a range of countries from Zambia, Germany and South 
Korea.
 * Almost 90% of import measures tracked by GTA are liberalizing, and energy 
isn’t the lone focus. Turkey, for instance, dropped import duties on 
fertilizers, and while India introduced temporary exemptions on ammonium 
nitrate, chemicals and plastics, according to GTA.
In the rush to boost supplies, there’s been coordinated action, too, by OPEC 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-05/opec-plans-symbolic-output-quota-hike-amid-war-delegates-say?cmpid=BBD041626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 and the International Energy Agency.

“But alongside this, at least 19 jurisdictions have imposed export 
restrictions on energy, fertilizer and agricultural products,” according to 
GTA, which compiles trade policy announcements as part of the Swiss-based St. 
Gallen Endowment for Prosperity Through Trade.

Read More: Indian Officials See Iran War Shock as Disruptive as Covid 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/indian-officials-see-iran-war-oil-shock-as-disruptive-as-covid?cmpid=BBD041626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>

On the demand side, nine Asian economies — including Bangladesh, Indonesia, 
Pakistan,the Philippines <bbg://news/stories/TCARUTT96OSG> and Vietnam — rolled 
out policies to reduce energy consumption. Only five non-Asia economies fall 
under this category of the GTA’s data, reflecting the region’s dependence on 
Mideast energy exports.

The data show how the responses to this crisis are different from disruptions 
like Covid.

Read More: World’s Anti-Recession Guardrails Are Weaker Than Ever 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-04-12/imf-world-bank-to-meet-in-dc-this-week-as-iran-shock-looms?cmpid=BBD041626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>

“Previous crises with an identifiable source and concentrated sectoral harm 
produced targeted, discriminatory trade measures,” GTA said in its report. 
“This crisis has multiple transmission channels and diffuse cost pass-through, 
making targeted trade measures an ineffective response.”

“The result is broad-based subsidies, price controls, import liberalization, 
and a scramble for supply that overrides even sanctions logic,” it concluded.

three quarters, according to a statement from the National Bureau of Statistics
