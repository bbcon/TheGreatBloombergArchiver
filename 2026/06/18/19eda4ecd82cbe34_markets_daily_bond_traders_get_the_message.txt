A hawkish Fed

Bond traders received Kevin Warsh’s message 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/warsh-rocks-bond-market-in-debut-sparks-surge-in-rate-hike-bets?cmpid=061826_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5630>
 loud and clear.

They dumped short-term Treasuries and piled into betting on interest rate 
hikes as soon as next month after Warsh used his debut press conference as Fed 
chairman to make clear the central bank won’t tolerate high inflation.

Two-year Treasury yields, which closely track monetary policy expectations, 
shot up 13 basis points, the most in more than a year, to 4.18%.Stocks slumped 
too <bbg://news/stories/TGSKGCKGCTK7>. Tumbling oil prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/two-saudi-oil-tankers-have-appeared-outside-strait-of-hormuz?cmpid=061826_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5630>
 should ease the selling pressure today.

The hawkish message was driven home by the projections of individual Fed 
members, half of whom expect to raise rates by the end of the year. Rob Kaplan, 
a Goldman Sachs vice chairman and former Dallas Fed president, said the central 
bank should moveas soon as September 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/goldman-s-kaplan-says-fed-may-hike-by-fall-sees-risk-of-more?cmpid=061826_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5630>
 if inflation doesn’t cool.

Warsh’s tone reassured investors that he won’t give in to Donald Trump, who 
elevated him to the central bank post after repeatedly lashing out at Jerome 
Powell for not slashing borrowing costs enough.

“If nothing else, the market has renewed confidence in the Fed’s inflation 
fighting ability and conviction,” said Ian Lyngen, head of US rates strategy at 
BMO Capital Markets. “It was a good day for central banking independence.” —

China’s bear market

Tech stocks around the world have powered higher this year in a rally fueled 
by the AI investment boom.So what’s wrong with the market in tech powerhouse 
China?

An index of Chinese stocks compiled by MSCI slumped 1.5% today and now is down 
almost 20% from its October peak 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/china-stock-gauge-heads-for-bear-market-as-tech-weakness-extends?cmpid=061826_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5630>
, on the cusp of a bear market. The index is dominated by internet and consumer 
companies listed in Hong Kong, but it lacks the chipmakers that are benefiting 
from AI spending and pushing markets higher in Taiwan and South Korea.

The internet companies that once drove enthusiasm for offshore Chinese 
equities are, in fact, spending big on AI, and they’re also plagued by fierce 
domestic competition andweak consumer sentiment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-s-first-consumer-spending-drop-since-covid-imperils-growth?cmpid=061826_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5630>
. Companies including Alibaba and Tencent reported disappointing revenue last 
quarter.

“The China tech stocks are victims of the success of their North Asian 
neighbors,” said Vey-Sern Ling at Union Bancaire Privee. “The opportunity cost 
of sitting on China tech stocks is very high when the other regions are 
outperforming.” —Charlie Zhu
