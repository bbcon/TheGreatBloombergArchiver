# Global Macro Briefing — February 12, 2026

**Oil and Iran diplomacy in uneasy equilibrium.** Crude edged higher as traders weighed President Trump's statement to Prime Minister Netanyahu that he will pursue talks with Iran and prefers a deal, even as Israel signaled discomfort with Washington's diplomatic opening. The market remains caught between a fundamental oversupply — which has kept a lid on prices and pressured upstream margins while delivering a windfall to US refiners — and tail risk around any disruption to Gulf flows. Traders also note that this year's price firmness has been aided by major refiners turning away barrels from sanctioned origins, a quieter but consequential structural shift. For now, the conflict premium remains contained; whether it stays that way depends on whether the diplomatic track holds.

**Monetary policy divergence widens.** A stronger-than-expected US payrolls print (+130,000) and a falling unemployment rate pushed the projected timing of the next Fed cut from June to July, lifting Treasury yields and tempering the two-cut consensus for 2026 — though Kevin Warsh's anticipated May arrival as Fed chair complicates the picture. The dollar nonetheless slipped, supporting a broad rotation: the MSCI Asia Pacific Index hit a record and is outpacing the S&P 500 by roughly 12 percentage points year-to-date, with Latin American equities up 21% and Korea's KOSPI up 30%. Non-resident EM portfolio inflows reached nearly $100 billion in January, the second-highest monthly print in two decades. The rally appears broad-based across debt, equity, China and ex-China, though absolute EM valuations are no longer cheap on price-to-book.

**European gas and carbon signals diverge.** Continental gas storage is heading into spring more depleted than a year ago, with little financial incentive to refill given summer prices trading above next winter's. Roughly 130 additional LNG cargoes may be needed to close the gap. Separately, EU carbon prices fell sharply after Chancellor Merz indicated openness to revising or delaying the bloc's emissions market — a notable softening of climate commitment from Europe's largest economy.

**China emissions inflect.** CO2 emissions fell 0.3% in 2025, the first non-pandemic annual decline, as renewables covered all incremental power demand. Whether this marks a durable peak remains to be seen; March's five-year plan will be the key tell.
