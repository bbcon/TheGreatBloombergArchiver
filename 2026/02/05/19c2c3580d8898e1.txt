Tech: It Turns Out It’s Complicated

Investors are assessing tech companies with more nuance than ever — and that’s 
creating a market reaction decidedly lacking in nuance. Last week’s divergent 
responses to Meta Platforms Inc. and Microsoft Corp.'s earnings showed that 
concern has moved beyond productivity to an attempt to identify whose profits 
will be hurt by artificial intelligence. Hence this week’s titanic market 
rotation.

Traders want to see strong results and are prepared to tolerate huge 
expenditures on AI if the bottom line is good. But if earnings disappoint, big 
capex is assumed to have been wasted. Alphabet Inc.’s fourth-quarter report 
followed a familiar script, with a slight twist. Revenue at Google’s parent 
beat expectations, but plans to raise capital spending sent the shares sliding 
in after-hours trading. They oscillated widely from there, but traders 
ultimately didn’t find the numbers reassuring:

Meanwhile, this is how the Nasdaq has performed in the past two months — there 
is a clear ongoing rotation away from technology into other parts of the market:

The downturn over the last few days for software stocks, however, is much more 
pronounced. The sector is down by more than 17% year-to-date. It turns out that 
AI can be bad even for software groups; the catalyst for Tuesday’s bloodbath 
was Anthropic’s latest AI tool. Thomson Reuters Corp., one of the casualties, 
plunged by as much as 20% — its biggest decline on record — on concern that the 
AI tool would deprive it of its large business in software for legal workflow. 
Any businesses at risk of being disrupted by AI can expect similar treatment. 
This is how the S&P1500 Software index has performed relative to the S&P 1500 
so far this decade:

How long this malaise continues largely depends on the factors driving it. AJ 
Bell’s Danni Hewson likens it to last year’s DeepSeek moment, and suggests the 
effects could be far-reaching:

Much emphasis has been placed on the frothy sums of money being circulated in 
the AI industry and the high valuations placed on companies within it. Perhaps 
less attention has been paid to which companies might find their business 
models under threat from AI, partly because it’s such a new and evolving 
technology with little visibility about the eventual winners and losers.

Even for perpetual tech bulls like Louis Navellier of Navellier & Associates, 
the rout signals something is amiss. The build-out demands of the massive data 
centers have powered a rally in memory-chip makers, but have brought a 
valuation hangover to earlier winners not doing so well in this boom. Advanced 
Micro Devices Inc. led the Wednesday selloff with a post-earnings slide of 17%.

If there is a danger of financial disruption, it comes from the effect on 
quant investors’ portfolios. Many use factors to drive their investments, 
particularly momentum — the tendency for winners to keep winning while losers 
keep losing — and value. Looking at the Bloomberg Factors To Watch function, 
value beat momentum by the biggest margin Wednesday sincethe day in November 
2020 <https://bloom.bg/49ZP2VE> when spectacularly successful early pilot 
results on the Covid vaccine convinced investors that the end of the pandemic 
was in sight. A move on this scale without such an obvious catalyst is very, 
very unusual, and some may have been caught out:

With unease around AI still unresolved, investors are looking elsewhere for 
value and finding it. Shares of companies with little direct tech exposure, 
from consumer staples to energy producers, are enjoying a rare moment in the 
sun. But it’s less a vote of no confidence in tech than a search for businesses 
that stand to benefit from a firmer economy. The Russell 1000 Value Index has 
advanced 8.6% since November, edging its growth counterpart by 14 percentage 
points:

Staples’ rebound, with Walmart Inc. topping $1 trillion for the first time 
this week, has been even more spectacular:

Confidence in the economy remains intact. Revisions to third-quarter GDP point 
to robust growth, driven by consumer spending and AI investment. Bank of 
America’s analysts, including Aditya Bhave, believe they will continue shaping 
the first half:

Consumers will soon benefit from One Big Beautiful Bill Act stimulus to the 
tune of $135-140 billion (0.6% of disposable income). Last year's shutdown 
should also boost first-quarter growth by 0.5-1 percentage point. All this sets 
the stage for front-loaded GDP growth in 2026.

But it appears that consensus forecasts are not accounting for these factors: 
They are almost flat for this year:

This is still very much a rotation, not a selloff. Roughly two-thirds of the 
S&P 500’s members gained for the day, and the gauge’s equal-weighted version 
hit an all-time high. But the money has been flowing into the stocks that have 
borne the brunt of the rout, so someone is going to take big losses. And even 
if the Magnificent Seven tech platforms are excluded, the US market is sharply 
underperforming the rest of the world:

Does tech’s sudden loss of momentum reflect more than a passing pause? As 
Navellier rightly points out, this is a “fast-moving situation” and could yet 
deliver meaningful gains in labor productivity and profit margins in the long 
run. But investors will need more convincing evidence, starting with Nvidia 
Corp.’s earnings later this month. The bar is being set high.

-- Richard Abbey

Land of the Rising Earnings

Japan has an election on Sunday <https://bloom.bg/4rwWNbq> and the market 
excitement is already intense. Attention naturally focuses on the charismatic 
new Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, who looks likely to win a fresh mandate, and 
whose words have sent the yen on a switchback journey over the last week. But 
for investors, something even more exciting is going on in the stock market, 
and it looks as though it can endure regardless of the result.

Years of corporate governance reforms to jolt the country’s old-line 
industries into life are suddenly having an effect. It’s even matching the 
intense hype around some of the central players in the US technology boom. That 
showed up on Tuesday ingood results

 from Palantir Technologies Inc., one of the most exciting and controversial 
players in Silicon Valley, and Sumitomo Electric Industries Ltd., a rather dull 
Japanese group that makes wires. Palantir gained 6.8%, but Sumitomo far 
outstripped it, up 12%. Amazingly, these two disparate companies’ share prices 
have both tripled since the US election:

(On Wednesday, Palantir was caught in the US tech bloodbath while Sumitomo 
gained some more.) There are many reasons for the sudden renaissance of a 
Japanese metal-basher, but the main one is that investors are finally prepared 
to trust the nation’s companies, and buy them because they’re cheap, rather 
than shun them as perpetual value traps. The Topix non-ferrous metals index, 
which includes Sumitomo, had been trading below book value for many years. 
Suddenly, it has hit its highest multiple in almost two decades:

Companies have aided this change of sentiment by at last heeding shareholders 
and paying out cash rather than hoarding it. Dividends-per-share have been 
rising sharply, as have stock buybacks. (If you are reading this on the 
terminal, open this chart in GP and view on a log scale):

Share prices remain vulnerable to moves in the yen. International investors 
have long priced Corporate Japan as if it’s one big exporter that needs a cheap 
currency. And indeed, a look at how this group’s revenues have grown over time, 
in yen and in dollars, shows how important currency depreciation has been to 
them:

The yen has plainly been a driver of the massive moves in the last few day.

The bond market will also drive her agenda. Yields on longer-dated Japanese 
Government Bonds have undergone a historic surge in recent weeks. As Japan has 
massive debt outstanding, it’s possible to read this as an incipient fiscal 
crisis, as Takaichi promises expansion. It can also be read as a belated 
normalization. David Roberts, fixed-income strategist at Nedgroup, said:

Much of what has been happening to Japanese bonds reflects catch-up to other 
G7 markets. Bank of Japan action curbed yield increases until a couple of years 
after other G7 Central Banks. Compared with say the US, most of the move could 
be seen as ‘mean reversion’ and has happened.

He added that the gap between Japanese 30-year and US 10-year bonds is “more 
or less the same” as it was five years ago. Takaichi has warned that the rise 
in yields won’t necessarily be capped, and the gap could narrow further, but an 
LDP victory is surely now priced in, and “you get paid an extra 3% to own 
30-year Japan when currency hedged into US.”

Meanwhile, the biggest takeaway from Japanese markets so far this year should 
be that the strength in stocks looks durable. This spasm of yen strength, like 
the last one around Liberation Day last April, sparked a brief fall in stocks. 
But the reaction in equities was far smaller this time and swiftly reversed:

Political excitement shouldn’t obscure the change that has been wrought in 
Corporate Japan — that will continue whatever the election result.



 Survival Tips 



My tip to any of you living in New York is to do an inverse Duke Ellington 
<https://bloom.bg/4agkHkA>: Don't take the A-train <https://bloom.bg/4thEMQ5>. 
Production of this newsletter was made harder by my decision to board an 
A-train (as I and most people in my neighborhood do every day) at just the 
wrong time. It came to a halt before it had fully left the platform, and stayed 
there for an hour. Then all the passengers (New Yorkers are a morepatient bunch 
than we're given credit for) <https://bloom.bg/4qhm1cE> had to troop to the 
back to get out of the one open door that hadn't left the platform. Our train 
was out of service. We were told to take the next one. The same thing happened. 
What rankled was having to sit in a motionless train with the doors closed for 
a total of close to two hours, without ever even leaving the station. Luckily, 
the book I was reading was good — you might try theMaisie Dobbs 
<https://bloom.bg/4ajU3Ht> novels by Jacqueline Winspear 
<https://bloom.bg/4ajjKb3>.
