Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — February 12, 2026

Asian equities extended a powerful run, with the MSCI Asia Pacific Index closing at a record and posting its strongest start to a year versus the S&P 500 this century — up roughly 13% year-to-date against 1.4% for US stocks. South Korea's KOSPI led the move, gaining another 3.1% to bring year-to-date gains near 30%, supported by AI hardware exposure, shareholder-return reforms, and relatively cheaper valuations. January portfolio inflows into emerging markets approached $100 billion, the second-strongest reading in two decades per IIF data, with allocations broad-based across China and the rest of EM. Whether this rotation reflects a durable re-rating or a tactical underweight unwind remains to be seen — EM equities are no longer convincingly cheap on price-to-book terms.

In China, regulators moved to end the prolonged auto price war, with the State Administration for Market Regulation banning below-cost sales and tightening oversight of rebate programs and digital platforms. The measures address Beijing's concerns over deflationary competition, though enforcement efficacy is uncertain given ongoing discounting. Separately, CREA analysis suggests China's CO2 emissions fell 0.3% in 2025 — the first non-Covid decline — as renewables absorbed all incremental power demand. Inbound tourism is on track to surpass 2019 levels, providing a modest offset to weak retail sales, which in December marked their softest reading since reopening.

Japanese assets rallied as markets digested Prime Minister Takaichi's decisive election win. The yen strengthened to ¥153 from near ¥160 in January, 30-year JGB yields fell roughly 40bp from January highs, and the Topix advanced. Washington and Tokyo are reportedly narrowing the first projects under the $550 billion investment vehicle tied to last year's trade deal, with candidates spanning SoftBank-led data centers, a Gulf of Mexico oil terminal, and synthetic diamonds for semiconductors. SoftBank separately posted a profit on OpenAI valuation gains.

In India, GQG is adding to a $24 billion position despite foreign outflows, while farmer protests against the US trade deal underscore domestic political constraints on Modi's tariff concessions. Singapore's Wong unveiled a budget framed around a "more dangerous" geopolitical backdrop.
