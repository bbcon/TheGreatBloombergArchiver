The dominant global theme remains the Iran war and its cascading economic consequences, now entering a fourth week with the trajectory of hostilities still deeply uncertain. Trump's weekend ultimatum — threatening to bomb Iranian power plants unless the Strait of Hormuz was reopened within 48 hours — was effectively rolled back Monday after markets sold off sharply, with the deadline extended by five days following what the administration described as diplomatic contact. The episode illustrates the feedback loop between market signals and presidential decision-making, though the underlying strategic impasse remains unresolved. The IEA estimates the supply disruption is comparable in magnitude to the combined effect of the 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis. Dallas Fed modeling suggests a Strait shutdown lasting through June would reduce global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points next quarter.

Gold posted its worst weekly decline since 1983 — down 10.5% — a counterintuitive move for a traditional safe haven that reflects a dash for liquidity, expectations of higher real rates, and dollar strength. Spot gold traded around $4,248 at session highs before the ultimatum extension provided partial relief. Brent crude held near $113 per barrel, with the Brent-WTI spread widening to levels exceeding those seen during the Ukraine invasion, reflecting the US's relative insulation as a major domestic producer.

Beyond the Strait itself, collateral damage is broadening. Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — the world's largest — has sustained damage that could take years to repair fully, affecting both near-term LNG supply and long-term investment calculus. Satellite data shows elevated flaring at impacted Gulf facilities, adding an underappreciated emissions dimension to the conflict's footprint. Fertilizer supply chains are under pressure, with both nitrogen and phosphate markets disrupted — a development with meaningful implications for global food production. The EU-Australia free trade agreement talks, being advanced by European Commission President von der Leyen in Sydney, reflect Europe's broader effort to diversify strategic and economic partnerships amid simultaneous pressure from US trade policy, the Ukraine conflict, Chinese export competition, and Middle East energy disruption.
