Back to Square One

The week started with bad news to digest from the Persian Gulf on the 
breakdown in ceasefire talks and the Strait of Hormuz blockade, with oil prices 
rising sharply once more. First-quarter earnings season kicked off with some 
unpleasant surprises fromGoldman Sachs <https://bloom.bg/4mspR2t> and the 
industrial bellwether Fastenal, both of which sold off. A flurry ofmore 
positive headlines <https://bloom.bg/4mpIKD0> about offers in the US-Iran 
negotiations didn’t come until after the close.

Given all this, the fact that the US stock market not only avoided falls but 
put together a good rally Monday is hugely significant. The S&P 500 rose by 
more than 1%. After a month in which stocks took their every lead from the oil 
price, it’s as though the tyranny has lifted. Crude prices have drifted upward 
since last Tuesday’s ceasefire announcement. Equities have carried on climbing 
with scarcely a rethink:

Perhaps most remarkably, the S&P has now recovered all its losses since war 
broke out <https://bloom.bg/4c9UMgA> on Feb. 28. With the enduring uncertainty, 
that is quite remarkable, although the US is certainly helped by its domestic 
oil production. Stock markets of net oil importers are still well down, 
particularly India and China, while big exporters likeBrazil 
<https://bloom.bg/3Ogqm34> are going gangbusters:

If we look at equity market volatility, the picture solidifies that the 
ceasefire announcement was treated as the moment that the whistle went to 
signal that it was safe to go back in the water. Both the VIX and Vstoxx 
indexes covering volatility in the US and European stock markets dropped 
sharply after that announcement, and barely budged after this weekend’s 
worrying developments:

This may be based partially in a reading of the geopolitical balance, but only 
in a limited way. Prediction markets offer us as good a quantification as we 
can get of conventional wisdom and the probabilities embedded in prices. 
Polymarket’s odds that the Strait would be operating as normal by the end of 
this month leapt on the ceasefire, but gave up all their gains after the 
weekend. A reopening this month is now only a 19% chance. It’s very hard to 
explain equities’ strong performance as expressing optimism about the situation 
in the Gulf:

So how do we explain it? Looking at the internals of global stock markets, 
momentum trades (in which winners keep winning and losers keep losing) had been 
doing well ahead of the outbreak of hostilities, particularly outside the US. 
They never took a serious reverse during the first month of the war, and then 
jumped with the ceasefire news:

That suggests strong pent-up appetite to continue with the bull market, driven 
by the factors we knew about six weeks ago; even if monetary easing is in 
jeopardy, fiscal largesse continues and corporate earnings momentum has been 
great. The MSCI All-World has made a return of 70% and delivered $35 trillion 
in gains in the last three years. To quote Andrew Lapthorne, chief quantitative 
strategist at Societe Generale SA: “Investors have been conditioned to buy the 
dip.”

The winners entering the war resumed winning, for all the geopolitical 
reversals of the last month. The strength of this relationship is remarkable, 
as illustrated in this chart from Lapthorne (which doesn’t include Monday’s 
performance):

With markets already primed for a bull run like this, it’s not surprising that 
stocks could brush off the weekend’s bad news. “This does not characterize a 
market pricing elevated tail risk,” Lapthorne said. “Rather, it suggests an 
investor base eager to resume the bull market and perhaps in turn complacent.’”

Alexander Altmann of Barclays offered an argument based on the latest 
developments. The key to the damage the conflict inflicts on the economy is its 
length. Until now, that has meant that every day without a resolution has been 
bad news. But with a ceasefire now in place, and the US launching a blockade, 
every day that passes without major Iranian attacks on Gulf oil infrastructure 
is positive. As he puts it:

The market’s perceived status quo for the conflict day-to-day is “the longer 
this goes on, the more oil prices go up, the worse it gets for risk.” However, 
today, we now have the opposite... The reverse UNO card played by POTUS means 
that the longer that Iran DO NOT retaliate, the more positive it is for risk... 
because it would imply that both parties are moving closer to a mutually 
acceptable resolution.

If traders wanted an excuse to sell, they had them. Peter Orszag, now CEO of 
Lazard, told a conference organized by Semafor in Washington that this might be 
a Wile E. Coyote moment in which healthy inventories beforeHormuz 
<https://bloom.bg/4vxZ49a> was blocked meant “the impact of what’s happening is 
not yet manifesting itself in most economies:

Supply shocks take a very long time to feed through into prices. That was true 
for Covid. It’s true with regard to tariffs. It will be true with regard to the 
Middle East.

This may yet prove to be true. But the news of attempts to restart 
negotiations (which drove a strong start toAsian trading 
<https://bloom.bg/4vto3KG>) and the power of the momentum that had been built 
up within the market is allowing stocks to stay levitated, Coyote-like.

To the Viktor the Spoils

Viktor Orban’s landslide defeat <https://bloom.bg/4mt2TIB> in Hungary has, all 
agree, importance far beyond the Central European nation of nine million. It 
shifts the dynamics of the European Union, eases at the margin some pressure on 
Ukraine, and is a symbolic rebuff for theculturally conservative and populist 
right-wing politics that has been on the advance.

Financial markets seem thoroughly OK with it. The Hungarian forint has rallied 
as polls made Orban’s defeat look ever more likely. Forex traders disliked a 
policy of the country cutting itself off from EU funding, and they have for the 
first time taken the forint above its level from the date of Orban’s last 
re-election in 2022 (a victory he said could be “seen from the moon”):

This wasn’t a judgment on Orban’s politics (which doubtless some traders like) 
but on his economics. Growth over the four years has been terrible, lagging all 
of Hungary’s most obvious peers. The neighboring former-communist states of 
Poland, Czechia and Slovakia have all enjoyed far stronger performance. Forex 
traders didn’t like this any more than voters did:

If there’s a political lesson, it might be that there is indeed a big 
constituency for Orban’scultural conservatism <https://bloom.bg/4cFqrqe> — but 
there’s a limit to how much people are prepared to pay for it in foregone 
economic growth. Others might take note.


The Terminator Returns

Forty-two years ago, Arnold Schwarzenegger’s classic The Terminator 
<https://bloom.bg/3Q4v1pm> imagined Skynet — an artificial superintelligence 
thatbecame self-aware <https://bloom.bg/4msuhpS> and turned on its creators 
when they tried to shut it down, ultimately launching a nuclear attack on 
humanity. What once read as dystopian fiction now feels less distant.

Today’s AI narrative is more grounded, and the potential to improve efficiency 
isn’t in doubt. What remains is whether corporations piling into the technology 
are able to turn their massive investment into profit, or are just “AI-washing.”

A more alarming second scenario is now cleaving into sight. Anthropic’s Claude 
Mythos model offers a glimpse into a more advanced iteration — one that 
highlights the plausible pathways through which AI could slip beyond human 
control and, in extreme cases, cause irreversible harm.

“Superagents,” as Points of Return detailed here <https://bloom.bg/41xAtn2>, 
can be helpful and complete specific routine administrative tasks, often with 
guardrails. But Mythos is capable of more than that. Initial assessments of the 
latest update — still not publicly released because of security concerns — 
suggest a system that can sidestep those constraints, and even adopt evasive 
behavior to mask its capabilities. Vikram Mansharamani notes onSubstack 
<https://bloom.bg/41x88NH> additional reasons why it has Wall Street executives’
hair standing on end <https://bloom.bg/4vpTdTa>:

In another case, after opting to edit files it didn’t have permission to edit, 
it erased its changes from the git history to hide its tracks. Across multiple 
instances, it hunted for credentials and escalated its own permissions — 
successfully accessing messaging services, source control, and even the 
Anthropic API itself. Again, evidence is mounting that we have created systems 
we may no longer be able to control.

For software companies, Mythos uncovered grave vulnerabilities that had never 
been previously thought of. If the technology stays in the right hands, that 
could be very helpful. But the market’s reaction suggests the latest iteration 
only accelerates the existential threat to the entire software industry’s 
business model from AI that has driven a historic re-rating of the sector. 
Amazingly, the S&P 1500 has now surrendered all of the sector’s gains relative 
to the market for this entire decade :

Investors’ apprehension stems from fears that the model could expose 
additional cybersecurity flaws across traditional software applications. 
Anthropic’s prudent decision to limit access to a select group of technology 
firms for early evaluation only reinforces those concerns.

Whatever the outcome of this assessment, it appears that Mythos marks an 
inflection point in AI models’ capabilities. Rather than despair, it makes 
sense that Wall Street is embracing the model as a double-edged sword to assess 
its cybersecurity vulnerabilities.Goldman Sachs is working with Anthropic 
<https://bloom.bg/4vIy7zI> to test the model internally.

While this pragmatism tends to confirm that the model or subsequent iterations 
could be used maliciously, the market’s latest reaction suggests a semblance of 
calm has returned. Software groups, particularly those specializing in 
cybersecurity, have pared some of the severe losses they suffered:

Did investors overreact? Possibly. The discovery of vulnerabilities should 
increase demand for cybersecurity rather than reduce it. Moreover, competitor 
OpenAI is also on track <https://bloom.bg/4cHIUCA> to release a model similar 
to Mythos. On that basis, Morningstar’s Malik Ahmed Khan argues that investors 
misinterpreted the significance of the model’s impact:

Cyber[security] has a clear adversarial dynamic, with new tools driving usage 
by both defenders and attackers. This dynamic means that new models and their 
advancements make cyber solutions even more necessary.

Khan views this as a buying opportunity for cybersecurity providers. Those 
best-positioned should be the larger vendors with the requisite scale, 
infrastructure, and sophistication to deploy these new AI modules.

Ultimately, as new iterations of large language models render earlier editions 
inferior, the trajectory is clear. These systems will only improve and, if 
skeptics are right, become more difficult to contain. Unlike theworld imagined 
<https://bloom.bg/4msZERr> in Terminator lore, there are no heroes waiting to 
step in, only guardrails, and even those are being tested as the response to 
Mythos’s initial assessment suggests.

—Richard Abbey
