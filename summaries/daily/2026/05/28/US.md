**Equity Markets**
US equities remained largely range-bound on Wednesday, with the S&P 500 gaining a marginal 0.2% despite a sharp intraday decline in crude prices — a decoupling that reflects the degree to which AI-driven earnings momentum has eclipsed energy as the dominant market narrative. Ed Yardeni's "FEMO" framework — fabulous earnings momentum rather than fear of missing out — appears to have genuine empirical support: the S&P 500's forward P/E of 20–22x looks defensible if the economy avoids recession, and Yardeni maintains the highest 2026 target on the Street at 8,250. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon and Bank of America's Brian Moynihan both projected strong second-quarter trading revenues at the Bernstein Strategic Decisions Conference, reinforcing near-term earnings optimism.

**Corporate Earnings and AI Spending**
Micron's sprint to $1 trillion is the standout corporate story of the week. Revenue nearly tripled in its most recent quarter — the fastest growth in the company's recorded history — yet the stock trades at only ~10x forward earnings, a valuation that reflects investor skepticism about the durability of the memory cycle. Snowflake's blowout guidance and its $6 billion Amazon infrastructure agreement underscore the continued build-out of enterprise AI infrastructure. Meta's launch of paid consumer subscriptions for its Meta AI chatbot represents a meaningful step toward monetizing its substantial AI capital expenditure.

**Monetary Policy and Macro Backdrop**
No Federal Reserve speakers or macro data releases materially moved markets Thursday. The VIX remains suppressed, and DataTrek's Nicholas Colas argues that robust labor market readings — initial claims and the unemployment rate both staying contained through last year's trade war and the current Iran conflict — have shifted the implicit "Trump Put" strike price well below current market levels. This macroeconomic resilience, paradoxically, may be reducing pressure on the administration to resolve the Hormuz standoff quickly.
