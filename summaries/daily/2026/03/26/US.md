US equity markets remained defensive, with S&P 500 futures falling roughly 0.7% as conflicting signals over US-Iran ceasefire prospects sapped risk appetite. The S&P 500 has now surrendered less than 5% since hostilities began nearly a month ago, a relative resilience that many on Wall Street attribute less to fundamental optimism than to the memory of April 9, 2025, when the index surged 9.5% on tariff relief — keeping would-be bears cautious about committing to the short side. The dominant posture across institutional desks is cash accumulation rather than directional conviction: Bank of America's latest fund manager survey recorded the largest jump in cash allocations since March 2020, with holdings at 4.3%, while Nomura's macro traders report having been instructed to stand down.

Goldman Sachs has raised its 12-month US recession probability to 30%, citing surging oil prices, and projects unemployment climbing to 4.6% by year-end from 4.4% in February. The OECD now sees US inflation reaching 4.2% in 2026, up from 2.6% last year and 1.2 percentage points above its December projection. Trump administration officials are separately stress-testing scenarios involving oil at $200 a barrel, an exercise framed as contingency planning rather than a forecast. The cost of US imports rose in February by the most in nearly four years, suggesting inflationary pressure was already building before the Middle East conflict began.

On the corporate side, a jury found Meta Platforms and Alphabet liable for damages in a social media addiction case — a ruling that may signal broader litigation risk — though both stocks edged higher, consistent with markets treating the decision as bounded rather than existential. Memory and storage stocks fell in premarket trading after Google researchers disclosed a compression technique that could reduce AI memory requirements, with Micron down 2.8% and Sandisk off 4.1%. Federal Reserve nominee Kevin Warsh's ambitions to shrink the Fed's $6.6 trillion balance sheet face a structural constraint: analysts note the task would require more than a single term to execute meaningfully.
