As governments around the world contemplate measures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-08/iran-war-nations-are-warned-against-worsening-fiscal-gaps-with-new-aid?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
 to shield their consumers from the latest blow to purchasing power — the 
energy-cost surge sparked by the Iran war — it may be unwise to expect anything 
notable from China in that regard.

Over the past couple of decades, policymakers in Beijing responded to external 
and internal shocks via demand management. In late 2008, the government of the 
day unleashed a massive credit salvo to stoke domestic spending in the face of 
the global financial crisis. Smaller but similar efforts came in the 2010s in 
response tointernal slowdowns 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-04-15/china-averts-a-hard-landing-with-a-credit-powered-trampoline?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
. Covid saw another credit injection. The various rounds of stimulus are easily 
visible in the chart below.

That template may not apply today, however, even though by some measures China 
seems to have room to maneuver. Inflation remains subdued and the central 
government’sdebt ratio 
<https://www.imf.org/external/datamapper/GG_DEBT_GDP@GDD/CHN/FRA/DEU/ITA/JPN/GBR/USA/FADGDWORLD>
 is notably smaller than other major economies, such as the US or Japan. For 
clues on why Beijing won’t likely avail itself of those measures, look no 
further than the new Five Year Plan, unveiled last month.

The underlying message of that plan is “shifting from the demand management to 
supply management,” says Dong Tao, who’s been analyzing China’s economy since 
the 1990s. “They pretty much already made the announcement that you cannot 
count on the fiscal policy and the monetary policy to boost economic growth.”

This amounts to what Tao regards as the fifth key pivot point in modern 
Chinese economic development.

“We are in the next pivot moment,” says Tao, the president of Springs Capital 
(Hong Kong), a Chinese asset manager. He’s previously held roles at Credit 
Suisse, including private banking vice chairman for greater China.

The arc of China’s post-Mao Zedong development famously begins with Deng 
Xiaoping’s 1978 reform and opening. It continues with his 1992 “southern tour,” 
during which he championed free-enterprise zones, and then the nation’s 2001 
entry into the World Trade Organization. The fourth “defining moment” was 
Beijing’s embrace oflarge-scale wage gains 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2011-02-22/china-wage-gains-undermine-global-bond-investors-as-inflation-accelerates?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
 nine years later. That, Tao says, sparked a consumption boom along with 
property and infrastructure investment.

“Now, China is moving from the cement economy towards the data economy,” he 
says. In other words, President Xi Jinping’s focus is on advancingnew 
productive forces 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-03-02/bloomberg-new-economy-xi-jinping-s-new-productive-forces-don-t-add-up?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
 in the tech sector rather than plowing more money into bridges and highways.

“Most analysts are telling you, ‘oh, they’re going to have X,Y, Z amount of 
fiscal spending, and the central bank’s going to cut the interest rate X 
times,’” Tao said in an interview this week. But that’s the wrong place to 
look, he says. “More importantly, they are handing the money to the supply 
side.”

State support is going toward technology firms 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-26/china-starts-state-backed-venture-funds-to-support-tech-startups?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
. “None of them are short on money,” Tao says.

By contrast, workers must make do with less. Whereas policymakers once focused 
on propelling wages (Tao estimated migrant laborers fueling China’s exports got 
a 40% raise in 2010), today they have to settle for stagnation.
Textile workers in Dongguan, China, in 2010. At the time, Beijing was 
championing “harmonious employment relations” by way of generous pay rises. But 
no more.Photographer: Nelson Ching/Bloomberg
Economist Alicia Garcia-Herrero at Natixis calculates average wage growth for 
urban private-sector workers in China fell to less than 1% by the end of 2025. 
By contrast, she notes it was “a healthy 8% before the pandemic.”

While China’s latest Five-Year Plan did discuss raising household consumption 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/top-china-official-vows-to-boost-consumption-to-overhaul-economy?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
 by increasing incomes, the government didn’t set specific numerical targets, 
such as for consumer spending gains. The probability that domestic consumer 
demand will remain subdued means China’srecord trade surplus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-14/china-ends-2025-with-1-2-trillion-trade-surplus-as-exports-soar?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
 likely “would only stay large — or grow even more,” Tao says.

That would be unwelcome news to US President Donald Trump, French President 
Emmanuel Macron and all the other foreign leaders who’ve been calling for China 
to rebalanceaway from its surpluses 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-07/macron-warns-eu-may-hit-china-with-tariffs-over-trade-surplus?cmpid=BBD041126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260411>
.

At this point, however, Beijing is signaling it just doesn’t care, argues 
Andrew Polk, partner and co-founder of Trivium China, who pointed to signals 
from last month’s annual China Development Forum. Polk noted that while Premier 
Li Qiang said China takes its trading partners’ concerns seriously, he also 
rejected criticism that surpluses were based on subsidies and protection.

“China’s plan is to double down on industrial innovation and upgrading, which 
almost by definition means continued reliance on export markets to absorb 
excess production,” Polk wrote in note April 5. “What’s striking is the pointed 
shift to a much more unapologetic attitude.”—Chris Anstey
