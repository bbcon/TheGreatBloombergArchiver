# Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — May 22, 2026

**China retains structural leverage as Xi-Trump dynamic stabilizes.** Following this week's Xi-Trump summit, both sides notably downplayed rare earths in their public remarks — a shift that itself signals where leverage now sits. Bloomberg Economics estimates roughly $1.2 trillion of US GDP touches rare-earth-dependent industries, and despite some $10 billion in global public capital flowing into alternative mines and refineries this year, even optimistic forecasts see Chinese dominance persisting through 2030. The next Xi-Trump meeting, likely at the White House in September, precedes the expiration of the current trade truce, which paused Beijing's threatened extraterritorial export controls. Separately, capital flows continue to favor China: domestic banks received $61.9 billion in net inflows on behalf of clients in April, the sixth month of inflows in seven, supporting both the yuan and equities. However, China's so-called "national team" is reportedly cutting ETF holdings by roughly 90% in H1, and exchanges are scrutinizing AI-fueled rallies — suggesting officials are managing froth rather than encouraging it.

**Japan faces a widening policy tension.** Core inflation slowed to a four-year low, ostensibly giving the BOJ room to pause, yet PM Takaichi reversed course to unveil a roughly $19 billion stimulus package including renewed energy subsidies — a response to Hormuz-driven fuel costs and yen weakness. The combination complicates the BOJ's hiking path while adding pressure to already restive JGB markets.

**India's rupee approaches record lows near 97/USD,** with the RBI reportedly weighing measures echoing its 2013 taper-tantrum playbook, including rate hikes, FX swaps and steps to attract offshore dollars. With global yields elevated and outflows persistent, the efficacy of such measures remains to be seen.

**Southeast Asia bears collateral damage from the Iran conflict.** Indonesia's plan to route coal, palm oil and nickel exports through a Danantara-controlled entity drew a Moody's warning over centralization risks. Thai and Philippine fishing fleets are idled by diesel costs, and DeepSeek is reportedly raising up to $10 billion at a $45 billion valuation, signaling continued investor appetite for Chinese AI despite commercialization risks the founder himself flagged.
