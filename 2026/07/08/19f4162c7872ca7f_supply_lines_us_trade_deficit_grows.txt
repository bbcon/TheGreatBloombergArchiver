US Trade Deficit Widens in May to Biggest in More Than a Year

The US trade deficit posted its widest gap since March 2025, just before the 
sweeping ‘Liberation Day’ tariffs, as companies ramped up imports ahead of 
another potential round of duties, and exports declined.

Imports rose 3.3% in May, the strongest increase in three months, reflecting 
increases in categories like consumer goods, semiconductors, computer 
accessories and autos, Commerce Department data showed Tuesday. Meanwhile, 
exports fell 3.2%, the first decline in five months, driven by a drop in the 
volatile nonmonetary gold category.

The May report generally reflects broader trends seen across 2026. Stronger 
crude oil exports following the conflict in Iran helped narrow the deficit 
through much of the first half of the year, as overall exports remain up nearly 
13% from a year ago. But AI investment has kept imports of capital goods 
elevated as the US continues to bring in products key to its buildout. (Click

It’s not clear whether that dynamic is durable, though. Importers have ramped 
up purchases of consumer goods ahead of changes to President Donald Trump’s 
tariff regime, including the July 24 expiration of a temporary 10% blanket 
tariff that has served as a stopgap since the Supreme Court ruled earlier 
duties unlawful.

The administration has promised that pending investigations into unfair trade 
practices by other countries will pave the way for higher tariff rates, while 
elevated fuel and transportation costs are adding to the incentive to stockpile 
goods.

Gold exports

Nonmonetary gold has been fluctuating wildly in recent months, after the US 
rushed to import gold last year amid concerns around tariffs on Switzerland — a 
large exporter of the metal. Since then, exports have both spiked and plunged, 
such as in May when nonmonetary gold fell about 52% from the prior month. 
Excluding gold, goods exports fell 2.4%, according to Bloomberg Economics.

The reversal likely reflects the unwinding of the stockpiling, according to 
Bloomberg Economics’ Troy Durie. “We brought all that gold in, now we’re 
sending it out,” he said, adding that the latest decline likely reflects a 
normalization of those earlier flows.

The widening gap suggests some “downside risk” to second quarter gross 
domestic product, Sal Guatieri, senior economist at BMO Capital Markets, said 
in a note.

Tariffs may be driving the month-to-month swings, but the longer-term backdrop 
is still one of “twin deficits,” according to Durie. A large fiscal deficit 
tends to produce a wider trade deficit, as stronger government spending lifts 
demand for imported goods.

—Maya Prakash in Washington
