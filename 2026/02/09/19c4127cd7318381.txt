Good morning. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer faces pressure as his chief of 
staff quits. Sanae Takaichi triumphs in Japan’s election. And activist investor 
Steven Wood faces a tougher fight at Swatch Group.Listen to the day’s top 
stories <https://bloom.bg/4nmEQLf>.

— Abhishek Shanker and Teo Chian Wei



UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s  leadership is under threat 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZXalRH> after Morgan McSweeney, his chief of staff, quit 
amid the controversy over former US envoy Peter Mandelson’s appointment 
<https://bloom.bg/4ceidWf>. A group of cabinet ministers may urge Starmer 
privately to step down, people familiar said. His allies expect him to survive 
the week. Hedge funds are betting against the pound <https://bloom.bg/3ZZ8KuA> 
versus the euro on fears of interest rate-cuts, political turmoil and heavy 
options demand. Meanwhile, UK intelligence chiefs warned political parties about
 foreign interference risks <https://bloom.bg/4cnAxw6>, including donations, 
coercion and honey traps.


Photographer: Toru Hanai/Bloomberg 
Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s snap election gamble paid off with a 
historic landslide, cementing her authority <https://bloom.bg/3ZrzmEt>. The 
ruling LDP registered its biggest postwar single-party victory and a two-thirds 
lower-house majority. Stocks rallied to fresh records <https://bloom.bg/3MxUk1v>
 and the yen edged away from the danger zone for intervention. Her main 
opposition now <https://bloom.bg/4kuQXFm> may not come from parliament, but 
from the markets.

In other election news: Socialist António José Seguro  won Portugal’s 
presidency <https://bloom.bg/4kluhHc> in the first runoff in four decades, 
defeating far-right rival André Ventura after a turbulent campaign. Spain’s 
Socialistssuffered another setback <https://bloom.bg/4cimisx> in a snap 
regional vote, highlighting waning support for Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez. In 
Thailand, early results from Sunday’s vote show theruling Bhumjaithai party 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZrzpjD> is on track to secure 191 seats in the 500-member 
House of Representatives — nearly triple the last election in 2023.

Asian stocks hit record highs as risk appetite returned 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZVNSnY>, with tech, crypto and gold rising on Wall Street 
momentum and Japan’s election boost. But Goldman Sachs expectsUS stocks to face 
renewed selling pressure <https://bloom.bg/3Mdl4V2> as trend-following funds 
unload equities regardless of direction. Meanwhile, Federal Reserve chair 
nominee Kevin Warsh hasfloated reviving a Fed-Treasury accord 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZrzqnH> to limit bond-market intervention.

Iran latest: President Masoud Pezeshkian described nuclear talks with the US 
as “a step forward <https://bloom.bg/4aeSXhv>,” while US President Donald Trump 
said that another meeting was set for this week. Israel's PM Benjamin Netanyahu 
isheading to Washington <https://bloom.bg/4aIF9Ld> to meet Trump on Wednesday 
to push him to impose limits on Tehran’s missiles and proxies.

 Deep Dive: Swatch Battle 


 <https://bloom.bg/3MxcPTN> Steven Wood Photographer: GILI BENITA 

American activist investor Steven Wood’s  one-man mission 
<https://bloom.bg/3MxcPTN> to overhaul the governance and culture of Swatch 
Group just became a lot harder.

 * The Swiss watchmaker and its plain-talking, cigar-smoking Chief Executive 
Officer Nick Hayek may have spent most of the past two years disappointing 
shareholders with tumbling sales and shrinking profits 
<https://bloom.bg/3MxcPTN>, but last month they surprised with an upbeat 
outlook and bullish predictions of a recovery this year. 
 * The market reaction was dramatic. The company’s shares — the most shorted 
in Europe — had their best day ever, and are up 15% this year. 
 * It was a timely riposte from Hayek, the son of Swatch’s founder, as Wood’s 
push to get on the board enters its second year. The CEO says he’s not troubled 
by the campaign, but recent statements suggest the company isn’t taking any 
chances.
