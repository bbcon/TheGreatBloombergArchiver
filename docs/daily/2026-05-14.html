Daily Brief

Thursday, May 14, 2026

US

The Senate confirmed Kevin Warsh as Federal Reserve chair by the narrowest margin on record, completing one of the most consequential and contentious leadership transitions at the central bank in decades. The confirmation sets up an immediate test of institutional independence, with markets already attentive to how Warsh may approach monetary policy at a moment of acute inflationary pressure. That pressure was on full display in Wednesday's producer price data. The PPI rose 6% year-on-year in April — its highest reading since 2022, excluding the post-pandemic surge — driven by war-related energy costs and compounded by freight transportation. More striking still, electronics components inflated 27% year-on-year as AI-driven memory chip shortages ended decades of persistent technology deflation. Oxford Economics now projects the PCE deflator reaching 3.8%, its hottest reading since May 2023, effectively foreclosing near-term rate cuts. Against that macro backdrop, equity markets continued to push higher. The Nasdaq 100 closed at yet another all-time record, with Cisco adding 19% after results signaled its AI pivot is gaining traction. Cerebras Systems raised $5.55 billion in the year's largest IPO, valuing the AI chipmaker at roughly $40 billion. Ford surged 13% after Morgan Stanley argued its CATL battery partnership positions its energy storage business as a potential hyperscaler supplier worth $10 billion. The simultaneous rise in the VIX alongside stock prices — a historically rare configuration — may signal that some investors are hedging aggressively into the rally rather than expressing genuine conviction. The concentration debate sharpened further. The top ten S&P 500 constituents — almost exclusively technology — now account for approximately 40% of index market capitalization, a level that invites comparisons to the Nifty Fifty era of the early 1970s. Unlike then, today's leaders post genuine earnings, but critics including Jeremy Grantham and Seth Cogswell argue that hyperscale AI infrastructure spending economics remain opaque and self-referential, and that competition among AI platforms will ultimately compress margins.

Asia

The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing dominated regional attention, delivering a day of carefully choreographed pageantry alongside sharper-edged substance. Xi greeted Trump with a full state welcome at the Great Hall of the People, and both leaders struck cooperative tones in public remarks. Trump called Xi "a great leader" and invited him to the White House in September. Xi declared that common interests "outweigh differences" and pledged China's door would "open wider," delivering the message directly to a delegation of US executives representing roughly $20 trillion in market capitalization, including Elon Musk, Tim Cook, Jensen Huang, and Kelly Ortberg. Beneath the cordiality, Xi delivered a pointed warning that mishandling Taiwan could push the bilateral relationship into a "highly dangerous situation," with Chinese state media publishing the remarks while the two leaders were still in the room. The Taiwan message appeared calibrated to establish a firm boundary without derailing trade discussions. On the economic side, China renewed import licenses for hundreds of US beef plants as an early gesture, while Treasury Secretary Bessent outlined discussions on a potential board of investment to allow Chinese capital into non-sensitive US industries, and suggested possible tariff relief on roughly $30 billion of non-critical trade. The question of whether the summit produces durable progress on semiconductors, rare earths, and agricultural goods will crystallize on day two. Elsewhere across the region, Asian central banks are facing rising strain. The Philippines, India, and Indonesia are burning foreign-exchange reserves to defend weakening currencies as oil import costs mount, with shrinking import cover pushing policymakers toward rate hikes. A Bank of Japan board member called for tightening "as early as possible," citing inflation risks from the Iran war, sending the yen higher and lifting June hike expectations. South Korea's equity market continued to exhibit speculative characteristics, with retail borrowing at record levels and a YouTuber with 1.3 million subscribers describing the mood as "nearly maniacal" — even as underlying chip earnings at Samsung and SK Hynix remain robust.

Europe

The UK economy delivered its strongest quarterly growth in a year, with GDP rising 0.6% in Q1 2026. The IT sector contributed meaningfully, and activity held modestly positive even in the early weeks of the Iran conflict. However, virtually all forecasters expect the pace to prove unsustainable. Bloomberg Economics projects near-stagnant growth over the remainder of the year as higher energy costs, tighter borrowing conditions, and political uncertainty compound one another. The political situation deteriorated further. Health Secretary Wes Streeting is expected to resign from Cabinet to mount a leadership challenge against Prime Minister Keir Starmer, while former deputy prime minister Angela Rayner signaled she is preparing her own bid following clearance from a tax investigation. Markets have long discounted a degree of Starmer instability — Polymarket has placed the odds of his departure before year-end above 50% for months — which limits the marginal selloff from fresh headlines. Nevertheless, UK 30-year gilt yields rose to levels last seen in 1998, and the political noise is beginning to concern overseas bond buyers. The spread between gilts and Treasuries has tracked the oil price since the outbreak of hostilities, suggesting the Iran war may be a more proximate driver of funding costs than domestic politics alone. The ECB's policy path also became less certain. Governing Council member Martins Kazaks warned that the data are pointing toward stagflation risks, and stated that a rate hike would be warranted if oil price increases feed through to inflation expectations. Two weeks after ECB President Lagarde signalled a tightening bias, a June move is now described as "less obvious" rather than imminent — reflecting the tension between above-target inflation and softening growth, a tension that Oxford Economics estimates could add 0.6 to 0.7 percentage points of additional inflation pass-through given elevated public inflation awareness.

Global

The Strait of Hormuz remained the most consequential single geographic flashpoint in global markets. US efforts to resolve the Iran war were set back after a commercial vessel was apparently seized by unauthorized personnel near the UAE, injecting fresh uncertainty over who effectively controls the waterway. A Japanese supertanker emerged in the Gulf of Oman after what appears to have been a covert transit, while two India-bound LNG cargoes also appeared to cross the strait — highlighting that flows are occurring, but under conditions of elevated risk rather than normalized passage. The energy disruption is rippling into global price data with increasing clarity. US PPI accelerated to 6% year-on-year, led by freight costs and energy, while US jet fuel exports surged to a record 455,000 barrels per day as airlines in Europe and Asia scrambled for supply. OPEC+ members are targeting a completion of quota restoration by end-September, though whether that alleviates tightness depends substantially on Hormuz conditions. WTI futures held near $101 per barrel. The summit's energy subtext was notable. Xi reportedly opposes any toll regime for Hormuz transit and expressed interest in purchasing more US oil to reduce Gulf dependency — an alignment of interests that could, at the margins, create an opening for US LNG as China's Qatari supply has been disrupted. The UN climate chief, speaking at Tsinghua University during Trump's visit, praised China's clean energy leadership explicitly as a counter-model to US withdrawal from Paris Agreement commitments. Big Tech's global debt issuance added a separate cross-regional theme: Alphabet has raised nearly $60 billion over four months in dollars, euros, pounds, Swiss francs, Canadian dollars, and yen, with banks warning the scale of AI-driven corporate borrowing risks crowding out local issuers in foreign-currency bond markets. Investors snagged 5% yields on 30-year Treasuries for the first time in nearly two decades, marking a notable level for long-duration pricing globally.
