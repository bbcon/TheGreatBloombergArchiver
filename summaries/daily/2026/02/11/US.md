**US Daily Briefing — February 11, 2026**

**Macro and the Fed.** The delayed January employment report, postponed by the recent brief shutdown, arrives this morning against a backdrop of softening data. Consensus looks for roughly 65,000 jobs added, with the unemployment rate holding at 4.4%. December retail sales unexpectedly stalled, the Q4 employment cost index posted its smallest gain since 2021, and the NFIB survey showed the share of small firms reporting hard-to-fill openings at a post-pandemic low. Markets have re-priced accordingly: traders now lean toward two Fed cuts this year under incoming Chair Kevin Warsh, partially reversing the hawkish drift that followed his January 30 nomination. The 10-year Treasury yield fell six basis points to back below its 200-day moving average, and the MOVE index continues its steady decline from last April's Liberation Day peak. Powell's framing of a possible "zero job growth, stable unemployment" equilibrium — a function of restricted immigration flows — complicates how a soft headline print should be read. A weak number that depresses consumption is a different signal than one driven by labor-supply contraction.

**Equities and the AI rotation.** With roughly 75% of S&P 500 names reported, Q4 blended earnings growth has reached a four-year high of about 14.5%, with eight of eleven sectors positive. The Russell 1000 Value has opened a meaningful lead over Growth since mid-December, consistent with a broadening that the extreme concentration of recent years — the top 10 names now 39% of the S&P 500 — had set up. At the same time, a more uncomfortable subplot is developing: investors are pre-emptively selling firms perceived as vulnerable to AI displacement. An Altruist tax-strategy tool drove Schwab, Raymond James and LPL down 7%+ Tuesday, the sharpest declines since April. Software, insurance brokers, and legal/financial information providers have all been hit in recent sessions. JPMorgan strategists view the software reaction as overblown; the dispersion bears watching.

**Corporate.** Ford guided to higher 2026 profit despite a $900 million Q4 tariff charge. Mattel (-32% premarket) and Lyft (-17%) disappointed on guidance; Cloudflare rose 14% on a strong outlook.
