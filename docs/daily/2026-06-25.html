Daily Brief

Thursday, June 25, 2026

US

  • Q1 GDP revision due Thursday; consensus expects a modest improvement but print remains below historical trend, with economy-wide corporate profit margins at post-WWII highs embedded in the release.
  • Micron Technology's Q3 earnings of $25.11/share (vs. $1.91 a year ago) and Q4 revenue guidance of ~$50 billion crushed the $43.2 billion consensus estimate, triggering a 15–17% after-hours rally.
  • Fed stress tests cleared all major banks — JPMorgan, Goldman Sachs, Citi, Wells Fargo, Morgan Stanley — prompting dividend increases across the sector.
  • Bitcoin fell to $59,023, its lowest since October 2024, as the debasement trade continues to unwind under Fed Chair Kevin Warsh's hawkish posture.

Corporate Earnings

Micron Technology delivered what Wall Street needed. Revenue guidance for the fiscal fourth quarter came in at approximately $50 billion, roughly 16% above the $43.2 billion analyst consensus, while EPS guidance of ~$31 per share exceeded projections of $25.31. Twelve-month trailing earnings have quadrupled in two quarters, and the company disclosed 16 multi-year strategic customer agreements averaging three years in length — a structural development that may reduce the cyclicality historically associated with memory-chip demand. Qualcomm also guided to more than $15 billion in AI data-center component sales by 2029, lifting semiconductor names broadly in premarket trading.

Monetary Policy and Markets

The unwinding of the debasement trade accelerated. Gold broke below $4,000 per ounce — down approximately 29% from its January peak — while the DXY dollar index closed at a 14-month high. Fed Chair Warsh's explicit prioritization of price stability at his maiden press conference has anchored rate expectations, with market-implied projections shifting toward an extended hold. Inflation analysts caution that price pressures now appear to extend meaningfully beyond energy, with Vincent Deluard of StoneX estimating a 3.5–4% CPI plateau even as oil retreats. Apollo's Torsten Slok raises a counterintuitive scenario: lower energy prices acting as a demand stimulus in an already-tight labor market, potentially forcing rate increases rather than cuts. ---

Semiconductors and AI

The Micron results reverberated most forcefully in Asia. Korea's Kospi staged a sharp recovery after two sessions of volatility driven by SK Hynix's pivot toward lower-margin DRAM chips. The $29 billion SK Hynix US equity issuance — the scale of which would ordinarily pressure shares — was absorbed positively in the context of Micron's demand signal. Japan's Kioxia separately announced plans for US depositary receipts in spring 2027 alongside a stock split, seeking access to deeper capital markets. Kioxia shares have risen approximately 900% this year, making it Japan's most valuable listed company.

China and Geopolitics

Anthropic's accusation that Alibaba deployed thousands of fraudulent accounts to access its Claude AI model illicitly represents, according to the company, the most extensive attempt by a Chinese firm to circumvent US AI export controls. Baidu and Xiaomi fell more than 3% in sympathy, reflecting broader investor concern about Chinese firms' competitive position in frontier AI. Separately, the US declined to send senior officials to the APEC meeting in Macau, citing what it described as China's discriminatory visa restrictions on American diplomats — a signal of continued friction in bilateral relations. ---

  • Average German and UK electricity prices are trending toward their highest levels since the 2022 energy crisis; French monthly average power costs are on track for their highest since 2023.

Energy and Grid Stress

Europe's power markets are under acute strain. The combination of record heat driving air-conditioning demand, low wind output from the same high-pressure system causing the heat dome, and thermally constrained nuclear generation along hot French and Swiss rivers has created a textbook supply-demand squeeze. French gas-fired generation rose to nearly 5 gigawatts on Wednesday — the highest since April — as system operators drew on dispatchable fossil capacity to balance the grid. The UK grid operator was briefly concerned about insufficient margin. The episode illustrates the structural vulnerability of the energy transition: renewable build-out has not yet been matched by the storage and interconnection needed to manage low-wind, high-demand extremes.

His foreign policy positions remain largely undefined, though allies have signaled flexibility on fiscal rules. Venezuela

The earthquake damage inflicted on Venezuela's airport infrastructure and urban areas arrives at a moment of profound economic fragility. The country is experiencing one of the world's fastest inflation rates alongside chronic power shortages. The destruction of key transport infrastructure will further impair the economy's ability to move goods and people, with reconstruction capacity constrained by fiscal exhaustion. No significant external financial support mechanism is currently in place.

Brazil and Regional Politics

The resignation of Brazil's Senate leader over the Banco Master scandal represents a meaningful political setback for the Lula administration ahead of the presidential cycle. It narrows the legislative coalition's credibility on financial oversight at a sensitive moment. In Chile, the narrow senate approval of Kast's omnibus economic legislation introduces modest reform momentum, though the thin margin suggests implementation risks remain elevated. Mexico's central bank decision, expected Thursday, is widely anticipated to hold rates steady against a backdrop of continued peso pressure and slowing growth. ---

Global

  • Brent crude fell below $72.48 per barrel — erasing all wartime gains — as tanker flows through the Strait of Hormuz recovered, with Goldman Sachs estimating Gulf exports running at nearly two-thirds of normal levels.
  • Gold dropped below $4,000 per ounce (down ~29% from January peak); Bitcoin fell to $59,023, its lowest since October 2024; the DXY dollar index closed at a 14-month high.
  • Trump stated that Iranian tolls on Strait of Hormuz shipping would constitute a red line, introducing a new potential friction point even as the broader peace process advances.

Oil and the Strait of Hormuz

The speed of oil's decline has surprised observers. Brent has now retraced its entire wartime premium in four sessions, driven by a rapid normalization of tanker routing and an inundation of Middle Eastern and African supply offers reaching buyers simultaneously. Fertilizer exports through the Strait have also recovered materially. The macroeconomic implications are complex: the energy-price relief is disinflationary at the margin, but Apollo's Torsten Slok raises the possibility that a rapid drop in oil prices functions as an effective tax cut, adding demand impulse to an economy that may already be running above potential — potentially net-inflationary.

Debasement Trade and Monetary Policy

The structural unwinding of the debasement trade represents the most consequential cross-asset shift of the period. Fed Chair Warsh's hawkish positioning has anchored the dollar and compressed gold and crypto simultaneously. Standard Chartered's Steven Englander attributes the dollar's strength primarily to real and nominal rate differentials, and expects the Fed on hold while the ECB cuts once in H1 2027 — a divergence that sustains dollar strength. Whether inflation proves transitory post-Hormuz or proves entrenched in non-energy categories will determine the durability of this repricing. That question remains genuinely open.
