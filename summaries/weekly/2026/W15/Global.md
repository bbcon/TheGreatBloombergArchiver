**The Ceasefire and Its Fragility**

The dominant event of the week was the announcement on Wednesday, April 8, of a two-week US-Iran ceasefire brokered with Pakistan serving as the key intermediary. Under the terms as understood, the American-Israeli military campaign was to halt in exchange for Iran reopening the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping. Oil markets responded immediately: Brent fell as much as 16% to approximately $91.70 a barrel Wednesday, and WTI tumbled nearly 19% — among the largest single-day crude declines on record, comparable only to the Covid demand collapse in 2020 and the allied entry into Kuwait in January 1991. By Thursday, however, WTI had partially recovered toward $98–99 a barrel as Tehran alleged ceasefire violations and Israeli strikes in Lebanon intensified. Only three vessels transited the Strait on Wednesday following the announcement, against a pre-war norm of roughly 135 per day, and physical crude markets remained acutely stressed — Dated Brent had briefly surged above $140 per barrel on Tuesday before falling toward $120. Vice President Vance was scheduled to lead US-Iran talks in Islamabad on Saturday, with Trump having pledged to maintain US forces in the Persian Gulf pending a final agreement. The ceasefire framework appears fragile and should be characterized as a starting point rather than a resolution.

**Energy Markets: Structural Consequences**

The EIA estimates the conflict has removed approximately 314 million barrels from global supply to date — roughly 80% of the IEA's record emergency reserve release. Goldman Sachs flagged Brent averaging above $100 through 2026 if the Strait remains closed for another month. Saudi Arabia disclosed that Iranian attacks have impaired its own production capacity, providing a partial price floor even on ceasefire days. OPEC+ warned of prolonged output impacts extending well beyond any eventual ceasefire. The NY Fed's Global Supply Chain Pressure Index rose in March to its highest since January 2023. The conflict is simultaneously accelerating a coal renaissance across Asia — where multiple countries have reported fuel shortages and some have implemented four-day working weeks — and generating measurable uplift in EV demand, with UK EV registrations hitting a record 86,120 units in March and similar trends visible in Thailand and the US used-car market.

**Geopolitical Realignment**

The broader geopolitical fallout appears significant. Not a single NATO member or Gulf ally directly joined the US-Israeli campaign, and French President Macron publicly called on medium powers to assert greater independence from Washington. The US formally requested that European NATO allies submit concrete plans within days to help secure the Strait of Hormuz — a shift from rhetorical commitment to operational demand. Pakistan's emergence as the key mediating channel, leveraging ties with Saudi Arabia, Iran, the US, and China simultaneously, reflects a meaningful realignment of diplomatic influence in the region. China's role in persuading Iran to accept a ceasefire — credited by Iranian officials to a last-minute Beijing push — illustrates the growing influence Beijing derives from its $270 billion financial footprint across the Gulf. That footprint constrains how openly China can back Iran, creating a nuanced position: quietly facilitating de-escalation while avoiding overt alignment with either side.

**Monetary Policy Divergence and Capital Flows**

Bloomberg Economics' aggregate measure of advanced-economy interest rates now sits approximately 35 basis points higher at end-2026 compared with projections from three months ago, reflecting widespread recognition that damage to Middle East energy infrastructure may prove durable. Foreign central bank holdings at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York fell approximately $82 billion since late February to $2.7 trillion — the lowest since 2012 — as Gulf producers liquidated Treasuries to defend currencies and fund wartime expenditures, a structural erosion of the petrodollar recycling mechanism. Emerging market equity markets suffered approximately $60 billion in outflows during the crisis, differentiated primarily by energy import dependence; Wednesday's ceasefire rally partially reversed those flows, though durability remains contingent on whether the Strait reopens in practice. The dollar rose roughly 2.2% since hostilities began, partially reversing an 8%-plus decline in 2025, though analysts including Robin Brooks of Brookings characterize the move as reflecting safe-haven flows rather than a structural shift, projecting a further 10% trade-weighted decline driven by political pressure on the Fed to cut rates despite elevated inflation. The IMF is poised to cut global growth forecasts as a result of the conflict, while the World Bank urged governments against worsening fiscal positions through poorly targeted stimulus.
