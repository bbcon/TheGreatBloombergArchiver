Bailing on the winners

The global AI trade has turned South Korea and Taiwan into Asia’s top two 
stock markets this year. Yet for foreign investors,the region’s most compelling 
destination lies elsewhere 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/global-funds-buy-japan-as-they-flee-asia-s-hottest-stock-markets?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
: Japan.

Overseas investors have sold almost $70 billion of Korean shares this year, 
while flows into Taiwanese equities have also flipped negative, data compiled 
by Bloomberg shows. By contrast, Japan drew $73.6 billion of inflows as of May 
22.

There are still plenty of Taiwan and Korea bulls out there. Goldman Sachs 
strategists justlifted their targets for both equity markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/goldman-lifts-kospi-target-to-12-000-upgrades-taiwan-to-buy?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
. Taiwan’s benchmark index would gain another 10% in the coming year under 
Goldman’s prediction, while the firm sees the Kospi in Korea going up another 
36%.

(The AI boom is creating problems for Taiwan’s central bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/taiwan-s-big-boss-central-bank-is-stamping-out-currency-swings?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
, which is trying to stabilize the currency since a surge in the Taiwan dollar 
erupted almost from nowhere last year, sparking the most violent swings since 
the 1980s.)

But investors’ retreat from Korea and Taiwan raises fresh questions about the 
durability of a blistering AI rally concentrated on a few chipmakers — Samsung, 
SK Hynix and Taiwan Semi — and accelerated by a rapid buildup of financial 
leverage. The contrast shows global investors’ growing preference for the 
Japanese market’s structural advantages, including its depth and diversity, as 
well as the country’s corporate governance reforms.

“Japan is the most investable Asia market after all, as it’s much bigger and 
broader,” said Chauwei Yak at hedge fund GAO Capital in Singapore. “Even though 
the Korea market has done better, it’s really like two stocks there.” —Winnie 
Hsu 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AV9ybw29-9g/winnie-hsu?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>

Bitcoin’s bear case

The latest Bitcoin selloff 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/bitcoin-s-break-with-tech-widens-as-strategy-s-sale-feeds-rout?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
 is being explained by an intuitive list of culprits: ETF outflows, 
geopolitical tensions andStrategy’s surprise sale 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-01/saylor-s-strategy-follows-through-on-pledge-to-sell-some-bitcoin?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
. Those are all valid. They may also be secondary to a broader shift:Bitcoin is 
increasingly losing a competition it did not have to fight during previous 
cycles.

For years, the crypto bull case benefited from a shortage of compelling 
alternatives. Interest rates were near zero, inflation fears were mounting and 
AI was still largely conceptual.Bitcoin thrived because it wasn’t competing 
against current cash flows. It was competing against dissatisfaction.

Today it faces a different market. Investors worried about inflation are 
buying gold, energy companies and commodity producers rather than Bitcoin. 
Investors seeking growth can buy AI beneficiaries generating real revenues and 
real earnings. Even investors who want exposure to the crypto economy have 
alternatives. They can buy exchanges, stablecoin businesses and 
tokenized-finance companies.

That helps explain why inflation is no longer providing a boost 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/bitcoin-s-inflation-hedging-promise-in-tatters-after-36-plunge?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
. Cleveland Fed President Beth Hammack this week warned that inflation risks 
may be becoming more persistent. A few years ago, that might have been 
interpreted as bullish for Bitcoin. Today, investors appear more interested in 
assets with direct exposure to energy, commodities and pricing power.

The bear case for Bitcoin is no longer that it is a fraud, a bubble or a 
failed technology. It is that scarcity is no longer enough. The world has 
become more interesting. —Sid Verma 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AS5FQL9RmV8/sid-verma?cmpid=060326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260603&utm_content=5128>
