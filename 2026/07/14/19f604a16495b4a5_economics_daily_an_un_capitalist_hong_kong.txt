Capitalist Roader No More?

After the Heritage Foundation kicked off its so-called Economic Freedom index 
<https://www.heritage.org/china/commentary/hong-kong-no-longer-what-it-was> 
more than three decades ago, one location dominated the top of the conservative 
US think tank’s rankings: Hong Kong.

Known for its light regulatory regime, low taxes, free flow of cross-border 
money and overall laissez-faire approach to business, the soon-to-be-former 
British colony wasjudged <> to have the world’s most open economy. That was 
decades after Hong Kong served as an escape for businesspeople fleeing Shanghai 
and other mainland locations upon the Communist Party’s takeover of most of 
China in 1949.

Even after Hong Kong returned to Beijing’s rule in 1997, the city retained its 
top position in the economic-freedom gauge — still viewed as an ideal gateway 
to China, with an independent judiciary, free press and deep financial markets. 
But things changed in 2020, when itlost its crown 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-03-17/singapore-overtakes-hong-kong-as-world-s-freest-economy?cmpid=BBD071426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6511>
 to Singapore.

While a top Hong Kong official predicted the city would regain its top rank, 
in 2021 the Heritage Foundationabandoned 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-03-04/hong-kong-dumped-from-economic-freedom-index-it-used-to-dominate?cmpid=BBD071426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6511>
 a separate grade for Hong Kong altogether — judging it have become “almost 
indistinguishable in many respects from other major Chinese commercial centers 
like Shanghai and Beijing.”

Now adding to that argument: Hong Kong is adopting its first “five-year plan,” 
the basic foundation of Communist economic planning dating back to Joseph 
Stalin’s Soviet Union.
The western artificial island, top, the eastern artificial island, middle, and 
the Hong Kong Link Road section of the Hong Kong-Zhuhai-Macau Bridge in Hong 
Kong, China, on Friday, June 24, 2022Photographer: Billy H.C. Kwok/Bloomberg
“Hong Kong is moving from a market-driven model to a government-guided 
investment model,” Alicia Garcia Herrero, chief Asia Pacific and Middle East 
economist at Natixis, wrote in a note last week. A bigger government role will 
come “with active usage of public balance sheets to reshape the economy, which 
is a big change from the past policymaking,” she said.

To be fair, industrial policy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-08-28/us-sees-a-new-kind-of-industrial-policy-emerge-under-trump?cmpid=BBD071426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6511>
 and a stronger role for government planning is a trend of the current era, 
with policymakers in the US and other free-market economies focused on 
reshaping supply chains and prioritizing economic security. Hong Kong’s plan 
will come with increased integration with the “Greater Bay Area” in coastal 
southeast China.

But joining Chinese provinces in adopting five-year plans also reflects a 
wider shift toward government controls in myriad aspects of Hong Kong society 
in recent years as Beijing sought toremove opposition 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-03-04/china-moves-to-curb-hong-kong-opposition-s-role-in-elections?cmpid=BBD071426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260714&utm_content=6511>
.

“The first Hong Kong five-year plan for 2026–2030 is symbolic,” Garcia Herrero 
wrote. “It does not mean abandoning the free market, but it does show the 
government wants to set clearer industrial priorities and alignment with 
Beijing.”

Need-to-Know Research

The European Union risks missing its goal of becoming more self-sufficient in 
critical raw materials, according to Germany’s Ifo institute. The bloc accounts 
for more than 5% of global mining output for only four of 27 minerals examined. 
For another nine vital inputs, its share is zero.

“Europe should have already established stable partnerships with resource-rich 
countries and systematically developed its own reserves, but so far there is 
little more than declarations of intent,” said Isabella Gourevich, one author 
of the report. “The EU should now back up its partnerships with resource-rich 
countries through actual projects and funding commitments.”
Soure: Ifo Institute
Together with Jakob Simmerding and Lisandra Flach, she argues that the EU 
could also develop domestic processing capacity within the next few years. Over 
the medium term, it could extract some critical raw materials itself too.

But while Europe has considerable geological potential, it’s poorly mapped. In 
Germany, for example, detailed data on deposits and mining sites are published 
only for Baden-Württemberg.
