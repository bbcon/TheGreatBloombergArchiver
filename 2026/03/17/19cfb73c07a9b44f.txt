On the eve of D-Day, Dwight D. Eisenhower invoked “our brave Allies and 
brothers-in-arms” in his rallying cry for the battle ahead.

President Donald Trump threatened NATO 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-demands-help-from-other-countries-to-secure-hormuz?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 with a “very bad future” unless fellow Alliance members aid the US in its war 
against Iran.

The response of US allies and rivals alike to Trump’s loaded request for help 
to secure the Strait of Hormuzhas been blunt 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-gets-chilly-response-from-allies-over-plea-for-hormuz-help?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
.

Berlin was uncharacteristically scathing. The UK, habitually first in line to 
join past US military operations from Iraq to Afghanistan, made clear that it’s
sitting this one out 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/japan-stays-guarded-on-trump-s-ship-demands-ahead-of-summit?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
. Japan — the closest US ally in Asia — said it had no plans to dispatch ships 
to escort tankers in the Gulf.
Ships leave Hormuz near Iran’s coastline.
That’s despite Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth and Secretary of State Marco 
Rubio hitting the phones to Japan and South Korea respectively. They agreed to 
keep talking.

In China, state-run media couldn’t contain their scorn 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/trump-floats-delaying-xi-summit-if-no-help-for-hormuz-ft-says?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 for Trump’s request that Beijing help.

Such a clear rejection hasn’t been witnessed since Europe refused to bow to 
Trump’sdemands for control of Greenland 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-19/why-does-trump-want-greenland-could-he-really-take-over-the-danish-territory?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
. He duly backed off.

Backing off isn’t so straightforward now, when the US is fighting a war 
jointly with Israel in which massive military superiority is running into 
Iran’s ability to control a naval choke point and upend the world economy.

Trump is learning that friends are scarce 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-appeals-for-help-with-hormuz-threatens-more-kharg-strikes?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 when he fails to consult or even notify them of his war plans, aftermocking 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-23/starmer-condemns-appalling-trump-remarks-on-afghanistan-troops?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 their past military contributions.

Standing up to Trump can pay electoral dividends, too.

A vocal critic of the war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/spain-s-sanchez-says-trump-s-war-on-iran-undermines-global-order?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
, Spain’s Pedro Sánchez saw his Socialistspost unexpected gains 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/sanchez-socialists-claim-turnaround-as-spain-s-far-right-falters?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 in a regional ballot on Sunday.

It remains unclear whether the resistance will translate to other areas, like 
trade or efforts to regulate US Big Tech.

But on Iran, Trump looks increasingly on his own. — Alan Crawford 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AOTqYzZDKuY/alan-crawford>

Trump requested a postponement for about a month of his summit with Chinese 
leader Xi Jinping that was due to begin March 31, sayingit was important 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-says-he-asked-china-to-delay-xi-summit-due-to-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 for him to remain in Washington to oversee the Iran war. The delay — thelatest 
example 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-s-china-delay-echoes-pattern-of-world-stage-leverage-plays?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 in a pattern of postponing or calling off high-profile meetings — gives 
officials in Beijingan opportunity to regroup 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-s-delay-of-xi-summit-buys-china-time-to-game-out-iran-war?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 and assess the full scale of the economic and diplomatic fallout from the 
conflict.

Pakistan launched airstrikes on Afghanistan’s capital late yesterday, 
triggering afresh exchange 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/afghanistan-says-400-killed-in-pakistan-airstrike-on-kabul-hospital?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 of accusations. Afghanistan’s Taliban government said the attacks killed at 
least 400 people at a drug-treatment hospital while Islamabad said it had 
targeted military infrastructure. Pakistan declared an “open war” with its 
neighbor last month and has since struck military and other facilities deeper 
inside the country, including in the capital, Kabul.

Irish Premier Micheál Martin will gift Trump the traditional bowl of shamrock 
when he meets the US president for St. Patrick’s Day, part of a charm offensive 
tosafeguard an economic model 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/ireland-woos-trump-in-st-patrick-s-day-charm-offensive?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 built on US investment. The meeting at the White House has historically been a 
soft-power opportunity for Ireland on its national holiday, but is now fraught 
with danger. Trump has accused European countries of taking advantage of the 
US, and Dublin has at times been singled out.

Newly inaugurated Chilean President José Antonio Kast traveled to the border 
with Peru and Bolivia yesterday to oversee the start of construction on 
barriers tokeep out migrants 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/chile-s-kast-races-to-harden-border-cut-taxes-slash-red-tape?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
, while his administration is promoting laws to cut taxes and slash red tape. 
Trump ally Kast announced a so-called Border Shield Plan during his campaign, 
saying Chile faces an “unprecedented migration crisis” and that the porous 
frontier has become a lawless corridor for illegal migration, crime, drug 
trafficking and human smuggling.

The jolt from Russia’s invasion of Ukraine has triggered Germany’s biggest 
military transformation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/merz-s-600-billion-defense-push-is-rippling-across-germany?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 since the Cold War. Chancellor Friedrich Merz earmarked an initial €600 
billion ($690 billion) in spending as Trump voiced frustration with 
guaranteeing Europe’s security. What began as geopolitical necessity, however, 
is becoming meaningful economic stimulus as streamlined procurement helps 
producers secure record orders for everything from artillery shells to attack 
drones.

And Finally

Trump promised Venezuelans economic prosperity after removing Nicolás Maduro 
from power, yet life hasonly gotten harder 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/venezuela-s-600-inflation-undercuts-trump-s-boasts-of-revival?cmpid=BBD031726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 in the two months since. Oil output fell more than 20% in January and exports 
plunged, limiting the flow of much-needed dollars that many Venezuelans 
typically use instead of the depreciated local currency. That’s spurred 
inflation to around 600%, causing more grief for Venezuelans earning paltry 
stagnant wages.
