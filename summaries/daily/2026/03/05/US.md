US equity markets are showing tentative signs of stabilization following sharp early-week declines, with S&P 500 futures trading near 6,871 — down marginally on the session. The recovery in US stocks relative to the rest of the world reflects a familiar safe-haven dynamic: the dollar is on track for its largest weekly gain in over a year, Treasury yields have partially reversed their sharp pre-war decline (the 10-year trading around 4.12%), and US money-market funds have swelled to a record $8.27 trillion as investors seek refuge. Assets in money-market funds at record levels suggest the flight-to-safety impulse remains intact even as equities stabilize.

The conflict's inflationary implications are now complicating the monetary policy outlook in a meaningful way. Gasoline prices have risen 25 cents to $3.25 per gallon in a single week, diesel has surged nearly 30% since hostilities began, and market-implied estimates for the fed funds rate at year-end have risen to their highest level in nine months. That trajectory makes it substantially harder for an incoming Fed chair — presumably Trump's reported nominee Kevin Warsh — to pursue the rate cuts that markets had been pricing earlier this year. The tension between geopolitical inflation risk and any easing ambitions remains unresolved.

In corporate news, Morgan Stanley announced it is cutting approximately 3% of its global workforce, or roughly 2,500 employees across all business lines, citing individual performance. Private credit stress continues to intensify: semi-liquid funds including Blackstone have raised their quarterly redemption caps to 7% from 5%, Fitch reported a trailing 12-month default rate of 5.8% in January — the highest since tracking began in 2024 — and short interest against Blue Owl Capital has reached an all-time high. Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon acknowledged watching the sector for "frothiness" while characterizing broad portfolio performance as "reasonably well." The private credit complex's heavy exposure to software, a sector now down roughly 30% against the broader market over six months amid AI disruption fears, remains the central vulnerability. Systemic risk appears contained for now given relatively modest bank leverage, but the asset class's role as the de facto arbiter of corporate lending standards will face a meaningful test this cycle.
