VOOmmmmmm! 

The world has its first-ever trillion-dollar exchange-traded fund: Vanguard’s 
S&P 500 fund, ticker symbol VOO <https://bloom.bg/43iM1eO>. Trends in fund 
management had pointed to this. Vanguard pioneered and now dominates passive 
investing, the preferred vehicle is the ETF, the economics of such funds 
improve with scale ascosts can be defrayed <https://bloom.bg/4vNzFHZ>, and the 
base of any sensible asset allocation should be a broad exposure to the US. No 
wonder VOO got to the landmark first.

Except this comes as quite a surprise. The late Jack Bogle, Vanguard’s 
legendary founder,disliked the ETF format <https://bloom.bg/4vnxhHu>, arguing 
that the ability to trade during the day should be irrelevant for the 
buy-and-hold investors the company existed to serve. Vanguard resisted offering 
ETFs until well after they had become established.

If there was a most likely candidate to break the trillion-dollar landmark 
first, it wasState Street’s ETF tracking the S&P 500, known as SPY 
<https://bloom.bg/43e9Lkm>, which evangelized the entire sector. It was 
launched in 1993, and had already topped $100 billion in assets before VOO was 
even launched. The Vanguard fund only overhauled SPY’s assets for the first 
time early last year. But brand and distribution power won out:

What of the future? The tale of the race to become the first $100 billion fund 
is instructive. It pitted Fidelity’s Magellan, an active mutual fund managed 
for many years bythe great Peter Lynch <https://bloom.bg/43dCEx6>, against 
Vanguard’s S&P 500 offering, which was basically SPY in mutual fund form. In 
the 1990s, it was regarded as the proof of concept for the whole discipline of 
passive investing.

Unlike index trackers, an active fund like Magellan has diseconomies of scale. 
Lynch’s stunningly successful career attracted inflowsen masse that grew ever 
harder to deploy. Buy a small cap that doubles in a fund that size, and it 
barely moves the needle. Magellan ran into difficulties after Lynch retired; it 
reached the $100 billion mark before the Vanguard fund could catch up, but 
found it impossible to continue outperforming the index year in and year out:

After that, logic caught up. It couldn’t make sense to run an active long-only 
fund of such a scale. Its investment performance remained creditable, but 
investors pulled their money. This is what has happened to Magellan’s assets 
since 1980:

Fidelity adapted its business model and remains one of the most powerful 
companies in investment, while Magellan has made a decent return for those who 
stayed invested. It’s highly unlikely that VOO will suffer a fate so brutal. 
But anxiety about whetherpassive funds warp indexes <https://bloom.bg/4dXkHaU> 
and can be gamed are increasing. The IPOs coming up later this year have shone 
light on the contradictions of passive funds, which will have to buySpaceX 
<https://bloom.bg/4fZurnA>, OpenAI, Anthropic and the others, whatever the 
price.

Magellan was named after a navigator whose ship circumnavigated the globe, but 
he didn’t survive <https://bloom.bg/4vk7UWU> to see out the journey. VOO’s 
voyage could be similarly exciting.

Statutory Reminder About Iran and Prices

The US president appears to have lost interest in the Iran war. Donald Trump 
doesn’t care if the “very boring” <https://bloom.bg/4fkLNuV> peace negotiations 
succeed or not, and markets appear to agree with him. The S&P 500’s streak of 
gains stopped at nine after a fall on Wednesday, but stocks’ performance 
relative to bonds shows that for markets, the war might as well have ended in 
March:

There are valid reasons for bullishness, but equally valid bearish points 
stemming from the war are being ignored. The latest ISM surveys suggest that 
both manufacturers and — more significantly — services companies are paying 
higher prices:

The ISM services prices index was a good warning signal of the coming 
inflation spike in 2021; it shouldn’t be ignored. Meanwhile, oil inventories 
have helped withstand the supply shock to date. They exist for occasions just 
such as this. But the US can’t draw down much more:

Gasoline prices at the pump have dipped recently, providing some reassurance. 
But futures for December have turned upward, and it’s unwise to expect this 
relief to last:

As for crude, the first-month Brent future remains below $100, but rising. 
Prices for the end of this year and next summer have consolidated at a level 
only just below their highs. They’re not signaling hyperinflationary disaster, 
but very much portend rising pressure that will require a monetary response:

The case for central banks to tighten is strengthened by anecdotal evidence 
that the US economy has grown a little faster in the last three months. In 
terms of growth (if not inflation), the shock from capital expenditures to fuel 
AI has more than counteracted any shock from Iran. Thelatest edition 
<https://bloom.bg/3Q0oNa8> of the Federal Reserve’s regular Beige Book 
<https://bloom.bg/3PDJbhq>, a collection of subjective reports from its 
branches, suggests that activity remains strong. Oxford Economics compiles the 
following index using word counts:
Source: Oxford Economics, Federal Reserve 
Inflation itself isn’t yet a concern. Tariffs are slipping from the agenda. 
But oil and gas seem to preoccupy bankers and businesses, even if they bore the 
president:
Source: Oxford Economics, Federal Reserve 
The Fed’s summary on prices is that they increased “at a moderate to strong 
pace overall,” with most reporting that inflation had increased:

Energy-related costs tied to the conflict in the Middle East were the primary 
driver of inflationary pressures, with spillovers into shipping, packaging, 
groceries, and fertilizer. Non-labor input costs continued to rise faster than 
selling prices, contributing to broader concerns about margin compression. The 
ability to pass on higher costs remained mixed across sectors, particularly 
among consumer-facing firms. Consumer uncertainty and concerns about fuel 
prices impacting households were noted by several Districts.

The AI excitement rose to a new height in time to thwart a major stock market 
selloff. But evidence that there will be a price to pay in terms of higher 
inflation and rates to combat it is accumulating.


¥160 and Counting

Japan’s Ministry of Finance has threatened intervention 
<https://bloom.bg/3Q6nMgK> to strengthen the yen, and the Bank of Japan has 
strongly hinted that it will raise rates this month <https://bloom.bg/4ucGqBO>. 
Both should strengthen the yen — and so it dropped back through the line in the 
sandof 160 to the dollar that prompted an intervention in April.

Japanese bonds have undergone two years of dramatic normalization as their 
rates have converged with the rest of the world, which should support the 
currency — but still the yen keeps being pushed back to 160:

This also makes the yen’s weakness compared to the Chinese yuan ever more 
pronounced, exacerbating tensions as China’s tourists and businesses can buy up 
Japanese assets. The BOJ now seems very likely to hike this month:

What is going wrong? The Brookings Institution’s Robin Brooks 
<https://bloom.bg/3Qh4Ko6> argues that Japan’s problem is its huge debt 
overhang, and it needs to show it means business by selling government assets 
to deal with it. That would have much more impact than another intervention.Bob 
Elliott in the Nonconsensus <https://bloom.bg/43aqFAj>newsletter argues that 
economic growth remains too weak, and inflation too low, for rates to rise much 
more — and that means a weaker currency must remain the natural escape valve.

Exciting changes are afoot in Japan. Persistent yen weakness is a key sign 
that markets aren’t yet convinced.


Tariffs 3.0

Washington’s latest tariff offensive lacks the shock and awe of Liberation Day 
<https://bloom.bg/43ivRSH>. The proposed levies effectively replace the 10% 
flat duty – introduced after the Supreme Courtrebuffed tariffs 
<https://bloom.bg/4e3MWVg> imposed under presidential emergency powers – that 
will expire in late July. The administration presents the new proposal as a 
curb againstimports produced with forced labor <https://bloom.bg/4e87dJD>. It 
raises the effective US tariff rate by only around 0.5 percentage points from 
its current level of 10.7%.

But for the 60 economies caught in the crosshairs, the measures threaten to 
strain a global trading system already grappling with supply-chain disruptions 
from the US-Iran conflict. Mexico and Canada are included, but the new tariff 
won’t apply to goods that comply with theUS-Mexico-Canada trade agreement 
<https://bloom.bg/4e7vUWp>, according to the US Trade Representative, mirroring 
an earlier exemption to the Liberation Day tariffs:

Taken together, the economies — which also include China, the European Union, 
India, and the members of the Association of Southeast Asian Nations — 
accounted for 99% of US imports in 2024, according to Bloomberg Intelligence. 
It’s unclear whether other countries will be roped in.

The proposal also had an impact on foreign exchange markets, coinciding with a 
rally taking the dollar index to its highest level in nearly a month, pushing 
it further above its 50-day moving average:

Marc Chandler of Bannockburn Global argues that the tariff developments 
partially explain the dollar’s surge, alongside the Iran impasse. As the US is 
increasingly the source of both trade and geopolitical shocks, why does capital 
continue to gravitate toward the dollar? Chandler argues:

I don’t think there is a choice. To me, the fact that the US has a large 
current account deficit means that foreign investors must accumulate US assets; 
the only question is which assets and what prices.

Even if this is only a modest adjustment to levies, it could still be very 
useful for the administration. Bloomberg Economics’ Maeva Cousin argues that it 
would restore some flexibility to Trump’s ability to adjust tariff rates. That 
could threaten the countries, like China, who benefited from the Supreme Court 
decision.

—Richard Abbey
