Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Senior Reporter Jack Farchy writes about how commodity traders were perfectly 
positioned to make the most of the Middle East disruption.

Commodity traders are having a good war.

It’s not just the lucrative trading opportunities created by the surge in 
price volatility. In this crisis, the industry is also better prepared than 
ever.

You didn’t need to listen to the panels at the FT Commodities Global Summit 
this week to understand that traders aremaking big money 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/commodity-traders-reap-billions-in-profit-as-war-upends-markets?cmpid=BBBXT042226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
. Just walking the corridors of the Beau Rivage hotel in Lausanne, it was 
visible in the body language of executives.

Many were making comparisons with 2022, when Russia’s full-scale invasion of 
Ukraine triggered an energy crisis that was a bonanza for the traders.

Preliminary numbers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-19/vitol-made-about-2-billion-in-first-quarter-despite-war-losses?cmpid=BBBXT042226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 for the first quarter point to a boon of similar magnitude. However, it’s too 
early to say whether this year’s haul will be comparable to the combined profit 
of more than $30 billion made by the five largest oil traders in 2022.

The process of earning that prize brought considerable financial stress.

As oil and gas prices soared in 2021 and 2022, commodity traders had to put up 
enormous amounts of extra capital, known asmargin calls 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-10-08/top-traders-boost-loans-cut-positions-on-gas-spike-margin-calls?cmpid=BBBXT042226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
, to cover potential losses on futures positions. This prompted panicked calls 
to banks and brought some commodity houses to the brink of failure.
An oil tanker off Singapore.Photographer: Ore Huiying/Bloomberg
This time, the industry and its banks hit the ground running.

Several of the biggest traders were talking to banks about back-up liquidity 
as soon as the war began. Between them, Vitol Group, Trafigura Group and Gunvor 
Group have raised $7.5 billion in such facilities. So far they have remained 
largely untouched, according to several executives and financiers.

And traders have adjusted their books to be less at risk of a liquidity 
squeeze — buying options to protect against a price spike; moving hedges from 
exchange-traded futures, which require daily margin payments, to off-exchange 
swaps with banks that have bespoke margin arrangements.

The largely European banks that dominate commodity-trade finance have also 
spent the past few years educating their senior management — and regulators — 
so are more relaxed about the industry’s need for more cash when prices rise.

To be sure, there are still stresses, particularly from cargoes and crews 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/mercuria-was-able-to-get-ships-out-through-hormuz-ceo-says?cmpid=BBBXT042226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 stuck in the Middle East. Vitol Chief Executive Officer Russell Hardy 
described a period of “triage” early in the war as the firm raced to reorganize 
its giant network of oil flows.

But when the dust settles, they are likely to be some of the biggest winners 
from the crisis.

—Jack Farchy, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

The European Union’s oil refiners cut carbon emissions 1.3% last year but 
still had to pay 12% more for allowances under the Emissions Trading System, 
according to BloombergNEF. The industry paid €2.3 billion ($2.6 billion), or 56 
cents for every barrel it refined, eroding margins when operating costs were 
already higher than global peers.
