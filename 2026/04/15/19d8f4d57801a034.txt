War Is Over (Say the Markets)

It’s safe to go back in the water (unless, perhaps, we’re talking about the 
waters of the Strait of Hormuz). The main US indexes arenow higher 
<https://bloom.bg/3QaRWPR> than they were when war broke out in Iran, and 
almost back to the all-time highs they set in the months before. Most have now 
rallied more than 10% since last week’s ceasefire.

There’s room to argue about peace prospects <https://bloom.bg/4mFvPNx>. A US 
blockade of the Strait is in many ways an escalation, and brings American 
troops into harm’s way. Signum Global Advisors’ Andrew Bishop points out that 
Pakistan and Iran have an incentive to play up good news, while for the US, 
“military troops and assetsen route to the Middle East have yet to arrive, and 
it might as well therefore keep markets calm.” He cautions that President 
Donald Trump’s incentives to “ride the wave” of good news make it hard to read 
much into it.

But the blockade is holding, and the market likes it as a strategy for reasons 
explained by Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution:

A blockade may signal a shift in US strategy away from warfare to economic 
countermeasures. If that’s true — and I think it is — this is the clearest sign 
yet that we’ve entered a negotiating phase and that the war is winding down. 
This’ll pull down futures prices for oil still further.

They’ve come down a long way, but remain far above February levels, while the 
price predicted for the end of this year remains close to $80 per barrel for 
Brent. Longer-term futures have always implicitly predicted that the supply 
disruption wouldn’t last long and have moved less:

But the decline of the last week is enough to spark renewed buying of stocks, 
because investors were braced for worse. The latest Bank of America global 
survey of fund managers, which took place over the week straddling the 
ceasefire announcement, found that they were braced for Brent at $84 per barrel 
by the end of this year:

In another sign that risk managers are relaxing, the dollar has given up most 
of its gains during the war when people were using it as a shelter. The DXY 
dollar index, comparing to developed market currencies, is back below its 
200-day moving average:

Kit Juckes of Societe Generale SA argued that last year the euro rallied “in 
response to the belief that if Trump wanted a weaker dollar, he could probably 
succeed in getting one”:

Now however, EUR/USD is lagging the rates market. That makes sense — the US 
economy is expected to grow a good bit faster than the euro zone this year, is 
less vulnerable to the oil price shock and still has some safe haven status.

How long can this continue? Deutsche Bank AG’s George Saravelos 
<https://bloom.bg/48mqTYb> proclaimed that it’s “time to sell the US dollar 
again.” Speculation about a dovish Federal Reserve under new management has 
eroded the extra yield US bonds can pay, while the oil shock may have made a 
bigger hit on consumers in the US than elsewhere. He said:

For all the talk of demand destruction from higher energy prices, prices at 
the pump for the most exposed region havebarely moved <https://bloom.bg/4sBQaVp>
 thanks to the instigation of powerful price caps. Remarkably, the US consumer 
is facing a much bigger income hit than all major Asian economies.

American Exceptionalism, Again

One reason US stocks are rallying: The BofA survey finds fund managers  
<https://bloom.bg/3QaRX6n>no longer believe they’re overvalued 
<https://bloom.bg/3QaRX6n>. Bank of America has been asking about this in its 
monthly survey of global fund managers since 2001. This month’s survey shows 
the smallest proportion thinking US equities are overvalued in more than seven 
years:

Are they right? The most popular measure of long-term valuation that fund 
managers tend to use is the cyclically adjusted price/earnings (CAPE) multiple, 
as popularized by Yale University’sRobert Shiller <https://bloom.bg/4dPlCf5>, 
which compares share prices to real average earnings over the prior 10 years. 
On that basis, Shiller’s calculations suggest that the S&P 500 did get cheaper 
in the last few months as war prompted people toward the exits. But it 
certainly doesn’t look remotely cheap by historical standards:

There’s a huge and intense academic debate over whether the CAPE can 
legitimately be expected to rise over time. Waiting for it to get as low as it 
did in the great buying opportunities in the early 1980s and 1930s would have 
meant missing out on the remarkable reversal after the Global Financial Crisis. 
But it remains very hard to say that US stocks aren’t expensive.

Using CAPE to compare to other markets, the US tops the world. These figures 
were compiled by Citi Global Intelligence:

If we assume that current bullish forecasts for US earnings growth in the next 
year are correct, and compare US stocks to the rest of the world on the basis 
of forward earnings premium, we’re again seeing the US becoming sharply cheaper 
after the attack on Iran. But the premium never got back down to its 
pre-pandemic levels and rebounded very sharply once Trump was perceived, in the 
market argot, to chicken out (just as with last year’s Liberation Day tariffs):

If investors really do believe that US assets are cheap again, then that could 
attract funds back into the dollar. But it’s perhaps best to chalk that 
particular BofA finding up to the strange conditions created by the war. With 
risk appetite back, US stocks are again undeniably expensive, and that will 
continue to give investors a reason to exit the dollar.


No Easy Way Out

American consumers were under immense stress even before the shock from the 
war.Sentiment <https://bloom.bg/4mARmak> has fallen to a record low due to the 
conflict, andrecord-high credit delinquencies <https://bloom.bg/4sFbXvt> have 
coincided with a surge in popularity of “Buy Now, Pay Later” consumer loans, 
which typically allow users to pay for purchases in four equal installments.

The appeal of these terms, and the initial lack of reporting to credit 
bureaus, fueled BNPL’s rapid adoption, but their relative opacity has begun to 
fade. Over the last year, regulators have pushed to bring these loans onto 
credit profiles. It’s not yet clear whether this has changed individuals’ 
behavior, but Bloomberg Intelligence’s Diksha Gera believes this should 
ultimately benefit consumers and the industry as a whole. BNPL assets’ shorter 
duration and contextual underwriting provide more tools to manage credit 
quality than longer-duration alternatives such as credit cards:

Smaller average order value, more advanced contextual underwriting models, and 
a faster turnaround mean that BNPL companies could see a spike in credit events 
ahead of other consumer-lending segments, but that should also allow for a 
faster adjustment within underwriting standards.

For now, fragile consumers might continue to prop up BNPL companies. But the 
share price performance of Klarna, the sector’s flagship, since it went public 
in September suggests all is not well:

Its share price has plunged over 60%, wiping out more than $11 billion in 
market value. At its 2021 peak, the Swedish company was worth more than $45 
billion. Almostseven months after its IPO <https://bloom.bg/41zZPRd>, that 
valuation has collapsed to about $5 billion. That’s been driven by two 
consecutive quarters of losses since going public, with a profit outlook that’s 
no clearer.

Other idiosyncratic factors might explain this dramatic fall, but it’s hard to 
extricate the fortunes of BNPL companies from the broader downbeat in consumer 
sentiment. A recentstudy by LendingTree found that nearly half of BNPL 
<https://bloom.bg/4cIDZBo> users say they’ve paid late on at least one 
installment in the past year, a major jump from recent years. Obviously, this 
is a cause for concern. To quoteLendingTree <https://bloom.bg/4cmF3JJ>’s Matt 
Schulz, “This is just further proof of how many Americans are struggling with 
high prices today and looking for ways to make ends meet.”

“They’re increasingly paying late, taking out multiple loans at a time, and 
using these BNPL loans for essentials like groceries. None of that is a sign of 
a confident, thriving consumer,” he adds. Still, some 54% of respondents said 
they couldn’t make ends meet without the loans; the service obviously appeals 
to a need.

Beyond the US, usage rose by more than 30% last month, while Klarna’s app 
download climbed by more than 48% year-on-year. But the challenge is to turn 
this popularity into profit without being dragged down by increasingly fragile 
consumers. Businesses are slowly tweaking their business models to stay 
competitive, as Jason Mikula points out in thisSubstack 
<https://bloom.bg/4mFvRoD>. Perhaps they’re coming to realize that their 
services may fulfill a need for people, but there may simply not be enough 
margin to go around.

—Richard Abbey


Coming Soon: US Midterms

One of the many reasons that Trump has seemed so totally in control of the US 
since returning to power was the status quo in the Senate. The Republicans have 
a majority, and most of the third of seats coming up for election later this 
year are held by Democrats. The slate of Republican-held seats offers few 
obvious opportunities for a reversal. Thus, his chances of avoiding a big 
midterm defeat that cuts off his political capital — a fate that befalls many 
second-term presidents — have looked very good. Until now.

The House has always looked likely to fall to Democrats. But investors are 
increasingly waking up to the notion thatthe Senate <https://bloom.bg/4vunobR>, 
and with it the ability to confirm judges and cabinet appointees, might also 
fall. The BofA survey shows that a Democratic House and Republican Senate are 
still seen as more likely, but a month of war in Iran has moved their estimate 
of the chance of a clean sweep up to 36%:

Prediction markets have gone further, with betters on Polymarket now putting 
the odds of a blue sweep above 50%:

If the problem of the Trump presidency to date has been a lack of guardrails, 
a Democratic Congress should certainly remedy it. Gridlock has its virtues. And 
on the issues that theoretically matter most to markets, this isn’t something 
to fear — Democrats would not rein in fiscal spending, one of the key points of 
optimism at present. The big, market-friendly Trump tax cut has already 
happened and wouldn’t be rolled back.

But the transition from an almost unchecked president to an environment where 
executive and legislature are in unusually intense conflict wouldn’t be easy. 
It’s a risk that could drive markets ever more over the months ahead.
