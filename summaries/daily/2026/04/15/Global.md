The dominant cross-cutting theme remains the US-Iran conflict and its Hormuz consequences, even as markets price in a negotiated resolution. President Trump told multiple US outlets he sees the war as "very close to over" and hinted at significant progress within days. A second round of talks is being arranged, and a ceasefire entered its second week. Markets have responded as though the conflict is effectively concluded: oil futures have fallen sharply, the Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index has retraced most of its war-driven gains, and equity markets globally have recovered their losses. However, the gap between market pricing and geopolitical reality remains meaningful.

Operationally, the Strait of Hormuz remains near-closed. An Iraq-bound supertanker successfully transited the strait on its second attempt, making it the first crude carrier to do so since the US blockade on Iranian ports came into force — a data point that illustrates how thin actual traffic remains. WTI crude was trading near $91-92 per barrel, and Brent year-end futures are still priced around $80, implying that the market does not expect a full and immediate supply normalization. The Port of Los Angeles chief estimates that even after the strait reopens, full shipping normalization will take approximately four and a half months.

The IMF's Spring Meetings provided a sobering macro backdrop. The Fund cut its global growth forecast and outlined a scenario in which prolonged conflict brings the world close to recession. Global trade volume growth is now projected at 2.8% for 2026, below global GDP growth of 3.1%. Brazil's new finance minister warned that a sustained conflict could force central banks worldwide to tighten to contain energy-driven inflation — a dynamic that would compound pressure on emerging market sovereigns already strained by high energy import costs.

Russia's oil export position is itself under pressure: Ukrainian drone strikes on the Novorossiysk port have disrupted Black Sea flows just as Baltic exports recover, bringing Russia's four-week average shipments to their lowest since August at 3.22 million barrels per day. That constrains Russia's ability to fully capitalize on elevated prices and limits the degree to which it can substitute for lost Gulf supply. Meanwhile, the broader energy security debate is accelerating the case for geothermal power in Europe, where more than 60 companies and investors have written to EU leaders urging stronger support for a technology that is domestic, dispatchable, and — as one advocate noted — significantly harder for adversaries to attack than surface infrastructure.
