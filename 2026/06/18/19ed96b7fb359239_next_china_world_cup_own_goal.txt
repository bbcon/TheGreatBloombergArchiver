Hi, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai, where I’ve been staying up far too late 
watching World Cup matches 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/latest/world-cup?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
.

And exactly none of them involve China’s team, even though the nation has the 
world’s second-biggest economy and wins its fair share of Olympic medals in 
both the summer and winter.

China is out of the marquee sporting event even after FIFA expanded the field 
of qualifiers to 48 nations. About the closest things people in China have to 
cheer forthis World Cup 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-14/fifa-world-cup-2026-has-smooth-start-despite-transportation-and-ticket-prices?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 are a native-born referee and Labubu, which made an appearance during the 
opening ceremony.

It’s a far cry — emphasis on cry — from President Xi Jinping vowing about a 
decade ago to transform China into a soccer superpower. Billions have been 
spent to build stadiums. Property moguls once spared no expense buying European 
soccer clubs and the contracts of top athletes for teams back home.

Fast forward to 2026 and China’s global soccer ranking has actually regressed, 
falling all the way to 91st globally from 82nd in 2016, when the government 
unveiled its blueprint for becoming a powerhouse in the sport. The Chinese 
women’s team acquits itself much better, ranking No. 16.

What’s gone wrong? It’s a question that I asked Eric Zhu, an economist at 
Bloomberg Economics and football fan, who recently researched the topic. Below 
is a conversation I had with him that’s been lightly edited for clarity.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-14/world-cup-s-smooth-start-eases-concerns-for-host-nations-video?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>

Your research is really interesting because you use dot plots and other 
economic analysis to examine China’s long-standing failure to field a World Cup 
men’s team. Why did you do that and what were your conclusions?

Eric: This is the first time I used rigorous economic analysis to explain the 
reasons behind that. It’s not only about football, it’s also socio-economic. 
It’s a long-term puzzle as to why China can’t qualify for the World Cup.

Also, I am a football fan and my son also plays. It doesn’t seem like China is 
short of talent and everyone is talking about why it can’t have Messi or a 
Ronaldo. China has lots of talent but the key issue is those talents don’t end 
up playing football and instead go to Peking or Tsinghua universities and 
become engineers or bankers.

To some extent, China’s economic success has prevented parents from investing 
too much in football because there are opportunity costs. If you let your kids 
devote a lot of time to football, you have to sacrifice time studying. The 
return on education in China is very high. As long as you study well, you can 
get into a top university.

The opportunity cost of playing football is too high. You have to give up the 
opportunity to go to college. That’s why they end up on the education track 
instead of the football track. That’s my key conclusion. The wage inequality is 
also bigger in China. If you get a better education, you also get a much higher 
wage premium than uneducated people receive. You also have to consider that 
Chinalacks a strong social safety net 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-06/will-ai-disruption-force-xi-jinping-to-turn-china-into-a-welfare-state?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
. That adds to parents’ anxiety. If my child can’t become a footballer, there 
is no social protection for him if he doesn’t have a good education.

You imply that China has been a victim of its own economic success. Can you 
explain how that has impacted the development of soccer and also does that mean 
that with the economic slowdown, we are going to see the opposite happening?

I don’t want to say it’s a victim because I think China’s economic success is 
still a very big achievement. It has benefited the whole country and pulled 
lots of people out of poverty. I think as the Chinese economy slows down it may 
be a good thing. It can be a turning point for football because parents may 
find out that with AI,the world is changing,  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/china-plans-mechanism-to-evaluate-ai-impacts-on-job-market?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
and maybe the value of education is decreasing. So there is less emphasis on a 
child’s education.

If that gives kids more freedom, I think that would be a good thing for 
football. And if China’s national team can really achieve success, this can be 
a plus for the economy. People may be more willing to spend more —a blessing 
for consumption 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/china-s-economy-recovery-threatened-by-300-billion-in-bad-consumer-debt?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
.

You say that a grassroots, bottom-up approach would be more effective than the 
government’s top-down approach. Can you explain that?

It depends on what sports you are talking about. If it’s individual sports 
like gymnastics, diving and weightlifting, we usually see China achieve great 
success in the Olympic Games. Those are the kinds of sports that work in the 
China model because the competitive environments of those sports are very 
stable, very predictable. You can be perfect by just practicing.

For team sports like football and basketball, you cannot do that because the 
environment is very unpredictable. It’s not going to help you improve in the 
long term. And also, I think private investment is not interested in this 
because you have to manage like 20 players for many years, plus coaching and 
other staff. It’s a very risky investment. In the end, for most countries that 
have great success in football, they have a very huge, nationwide grassroots 
system. That’s the only way you make team sports work, and China doesn’t have 
that right now.
Belgium and Egypt do battle in Seattle on Monday.Photographer: Alex 
Grimm/Getty Images via Getty Images North America
Given China’s demographics, for example fewer births but also high youth 
unemployment, will that impact the development of the sport in the future?

Over the next decade or two decades, I’m more optimistic on the football side 
but more pessimistic on the economy side. Thejob opportunities are becoming 
weaker 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-04/china-s-robotaxi-dilemma-how-to-lead-in-ai-without-fueling-unemployment?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 because of AI competition, which means the return from education is also 
decreasing. The population pool is shrinking, so you no longer face so much 
competition in the labor market. At the same time, lots of jobs face 
competition from AI, so the certainty and the return from a college degree is 
probably decreasing over the years.

That will give parents more incentive to encourage children to do sports. 
Parents should place less emphasis on education. That’s the only chance for 
kids to play more football.

As a New York Knicks fan, I waited 53 years for a championship. Do you think 
China will field a World Cup team in our lifetime?

I’m predicting that in the next decade or two, we should be seeing China 
regularly qualifying for the World Cup. And if FIFA is willing to expand the 
number of qualifying teams, that’ll help too.
If New York Knicks fans can finally celebrate a title, there’s hope for 
everyone, including the Chinese men’s soccer team.Photographer: David Dee 
Delgado/Getty Images via Getty Images North America

The Hangover

China is waking up to a consumer hangover that’s sending sales of everything 
from cars to furniture into a slump unseen since the pandemic lockdowns almost 
four years ago.

This week, data for retail sales, a key measure of consumer demand, came in 
well below already-bleak forecasts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-s-first-consumer-spending-drop-since-covid-imperils-growth?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 for May, with a decline of 0.6% from a year earlier. A look under the hood 
reveals the predicament is even more dire than that figure in some ways: 
purchases of home appliances and cars both plunged by double digits.

Even catering — which has generally held up better than goods purchases — just 
inched up at the slowest pace since the end of 2022.

To a large extent this situation is the result of earlier purchases spurred by 
government subsidies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-30/china-unveils-initial-9-billion-in-consumer-subsidies-for-2026?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 that prompted many people to buy durables like washing machines and autos. And 
it turns out there are only so many washing machines China can consume.

This year, the government has scaled back funding support for its flagship 
consumer goods trade-in program to 250 billion yuan ($37 billion), down from 
300 billion yuan in 2025. Considering this, it’s not surprising that car sales 
fell again. Theenergy shock stemming from the Iran war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-energy-transition-iran-war/?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 didn’t help, either.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-16/china-s-consumer-spending-drop-imperils-growth-video?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
WATCH 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-16/china-s-consumer-spending-drop-imperils-growth-video?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
: Consumer spending contracted last month for the first time since the pandemic.
Beijing seemed to acknowledge that the tapering of subsidies was a headwind. 
While government spokesman Fu Linghui linked the slump in retail sales and 
investment to factors including heavy rain, he also pointed to last year’s high 
level of spending driven by the subsidies and a shift to new growth drivers.

Another key factor limiting a consumption recovery is a lingering property 
crisis 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/latest/china-s-property-crisis?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
. There’s been speculation recently that the sector, a major repository for 
household wealth,may finally be turning a corner 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-23/light-at-end-of-the-tunnel-for-china-s-property-slump?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
.

But right now that seems mostly limited to big cities, where better earnings 
from the AI boom are enjoyed. In the rest of the country, things are more 
concerning. Overall, both new and second-hand home prices slid at a quicker 
pace compared with April.

It’s not all doom and gloom. The services production index rose last month, 
the latest indication that consumers are more willing to shell out when it 
comes to travel or entertainment.

Still, the bigger economic picture is one of disappointment, especially 
considering that China’s leaders made strengthening domestic demandtheir top 
economic priority 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-08/china-s-politburo-vows-to-keep-fiscal-monetary-policy-stances?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 for the year. With the conflict in the Middle Eastapparently winding down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/trump-blows-through-his-iran-red-lines-in-justifying-peace-deal?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
, it’s possible consumer sentiment will shift in the months ahead.

In the view of many analysts, though, a rebound would probably have to wait 
until people see better employment prospects and the housing crisis ends.

Wei He, China economist at Gavekal Dragonomics, said that for now, “there is 
no reason to expect a major turnaround.”

“Weak labor-market conditions will continue to weigh on household income 
growth, and the slow-burn property price decline will sap both the balance 
sheets and confidence of anxious consumers.”

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

Food-safety concerns returned to the fore in China this week because the 
government summoned Sam’s Club representatives over what it described as a 
recent wave of problems.

The government said it ordered Sam’s Club to strictly prevent problems across 
its supply chains. It did not detail the problems but state media reports 
pointed to customers who said they found a false tooth in a cake, a mouse in a 
box of mochi rice cakes and maggots in udon noodles.

Walmart China didn’t immediately respond to a request for comment about the 
summons.
A Sam’s Club store in Shenzhen. China’s middle class has embraced the chain.
Photographer: Gilles Sabrie/Bloomberg
News of the summons quickly became the top trending topic on Weibo after it 
was announced Monday, attracting more than 80 million views. Many users said 
they were disappointed by the brand, while some said they had noticed a decline 
in product quality in recent years.

The members-only US retailer has built a loyal following among the middle 
class in China, where food scandals liketainted cooking oil 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-07-10/tainted-cooking-oil-scandal-revives-china-food-security-concerns?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
 and contaminated milk powder haveleft deep scars on the public psyche 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2019-01-21/china-s-lethal-milk-scandal-reverberates-a-decade-later?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
.

Against that backdrop, Sam’s Club positioned itself as a trusted premium 
alternative for shoppers willing to pay for an annual membership in exchange 
for higher-quality products.

The chain has expanded rapidly in China, opening 10 new branches last year and 
bringing its total number of stores nationwide to 63. For many families, a 
weekend trip to Sam’s Club is more than a grocery run — it’s also a lifestyle 
and status marker.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed?cmpid=BBD061826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5615>
That could change if consumers begin to question whether the store still 
offers the reliability its members are paying for.

“People were happy to pay hundreds of yuan for an annual membership, simply 
looking for peace of mind, reliability and consistent quality,” a Weibo user 
who said they are a longtime Sam’s Club customer posted. “But now, as they open 
more and more locations, their fame grows and their profits soar. Ironically, 
they have gradually lost sight of their founding mission.”

Still, some people defended the chain, saying that despite the problems, Sam’s 
Club was more reliable than many domestic options.

“They should summon more Chinese supermarkets,” one Weibo commentator wrote.
