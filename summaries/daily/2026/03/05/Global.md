Six days into the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran, the conflict has acquired genuinely global characteristics. The geographic footprint has expanded markedly: NATO air defenses downed a ballistic missile over Turkey, Arab Gulf states intercepted Iranian drones and missiles overnight, Israel is conducting waves of airstrikes on Tehran, and a US submarine conducted the first American submarine attack on a surface warship since the Second World War — sinking the IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka in Indian Ocean waters. Iran has vowed escalating retaliation, and US Defense Secretary Pete Hegseth estimated operations could last three to eight weeks. The endgame remains opaque.

Energy markets are the clearest transmission channel to the global economy. Brent crude has gained approximately 12% in the first three days of the week and continued higher Thursday. European natural gas is up roughly 60% on the week. Persian Gulf airspace and airport closures have disrupted roughly 13% of global air freight capacity — the three Gulf hub carriers alone — with Kuehne + Nagel estimating an 18% loss in global air cargo capacity. The Strait of Hormuz, handling 2-3% of global container volumes, has approximately 100 vessels stranded in the Gulf range. Container shipping surcharges are being applied, and supply chain stress is spreading from Southeast Asia toward Chinese and European ports. Importantly, Iran's military commander stated Tehran "does not believe in closing the Strait of Hormuz at all" — a signal worth monitoring as it would be the most severe escalation scenario.

The macro divergence story that defined early 2026 — the "Sell America, Buy Asia" trade — has sharply reversed. The dollar's safe-haven demand, the US's relative energy self-sufficiency, and a pre-existing positioning setup in which non-US assets had been outperforming all contributed to a rapid reversion. Emerging market currencies and equities have given back recent gains at speed. Russia, notably, stands to benefit economically from elevated energy prices the longer the conflict continues. The IMF's Kristalina Georgieva warned that the war will test global resilience and that shocks "will keep coming" — an assessment consistent with the OECD's concurrent warning that sovereign debt markets, with their shortened duration profiles and reduced central bank ownership, are structurally more vulnerable to sudden shocks than surface stability implies.
