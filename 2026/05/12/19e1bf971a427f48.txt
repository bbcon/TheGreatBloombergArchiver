Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Alex Longley looks at the factors helping to shield the oil market 
from the effects of the Iran war and the risks ahead.

Ever since the war in Iran began, the oil market has been pulling every lever 
it can to make sure the world has enough supply.

In the past few weeks, two measures in particular have come to the fore. 
Traders are now questioning how much longer they can be sustained.

The first has been an unprecedented surge in US exports. 

Toward the end of last month, the country was exporting more than 14 million 
barrels 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/us-crude-exports-surge-to-record-above-6-million-barrels-a-day?cmpid=BBBXT051226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4383>
 of crude and refined products a day, an all-time high.

The increase has been propelled in part by efforts to tap emergency stockpiles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/us-unleashes-another-wave-of-emergency-oil-as-gas-prices-bite?cmpid=BBBXT051226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4383>
, with the latestweekly drawdown <https://www.spr.doe.gov/dir/dir.html> 
totaling a record 1.22 million barrels a day.

Those releases have more room to run. But they canтАЩt continue indefinitely, 
and pressure on inventories is likely to intensify as the summer driving season 
approaches.

The other key factor keeping a lid on prices has been less obvious, but 
arguably more impactful тАФ asudden collapse 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-10/the-physical-oil-squeeze-eases-for-now-as-buyers-back-away?cmpid=BBBXT051226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4383>
 in Chinese crude purchases.

Some of the nationтАЩs large oil companies have been reselling cargoes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-22/china-s-oil-majors-sell-barrels-as-run-rates-drop-to-2022-low?cmpid=BBBXT051226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4383>
 from West Africa in recent weeks. The move came shortly after state refiners 
were given permission to draw from commercial storage, helping to ease supply 
pressures.

Crude futures are up almost 50% since the start of the war, with the effective 
closure of the crucial Strait of Hormuz keeping vast quantities locked up in 
the Persian Gulf. But prices havenтАЩt breached levels seen in 2022, when 
RussiaтАЩs full-scale invasion of Ukraine drove benchmark Brent toward $130 a

The boss of trading house Mercuria Energy Group Ltd. said late last month that 
Chinese selling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/china-aggressively-sold-oil-in-recent-weeks-mercuria-ceo-says?cmpid=BBBXT051226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4383>
 had тАЬtaken out a lot of demand from various countries,тАЭ but that the move 
could only continue for another three weeks or so.

Morgan Stanley also said that ChinaтАЩs slowing imports тАФ and AmericaтАЩs robust 
exports тАФ canтАЩtmaintain their current pace 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/oil-market-in-race-against-time-on-hormuz-morgan-stanley-says?cmpid=BBBXT051226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4383>
 forever.

тАЬNeither seem sustainable in the long run,тАЭ analysts led by Martijn Rats wrote 
in a note this week. If Hormuz stays blocked until late June or even July, they
