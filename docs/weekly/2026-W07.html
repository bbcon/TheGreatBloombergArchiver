Weekly Brief

Feb 09 – Feb 15, 2026

US

  • January payrolls rose 130,000 — the strongest print in over a year — pushing the expected first Fed cut from June to July and complicating the two-cut consensus for 2026.
  • House Republicans voted to terminate Canadian tariffs in the most significant legislative pushback to Trump's trade agenda to date; the bill faces a near-certain presidential veto.
  • AI disruption fears broadened into a cross-sector repricing, dragging software, wealth managers, logistics, and commercial real estate names sharply lower across the week.
  • The EPA rescinded the 2009 endangerment finding underpinning federal greenhouse-gas regulation, setting up litigation likely to reach the Supreme Court.
  • Power-cost inflation — projected near 6% annually through 2027 as data centers absorb 40% of incremental US electricity demand — is emerging as a potential midterm liability for the GOP.

Equity Markets and the AI Rotation

The week's defining market theme was not the headline indices but the turbulence beneath them. The S&P 500 ended the period up roughly 1.4% year-to-date, ranking 69th among 92 global benchmarks tracked by Bloomberg and trailing world ex-US equities by approximately 7.7 percentage points — an underperformance that reflects a sustained rotation rather than a transient blip. Within US markets, the Russell 1000 Value extended a meaningful lead over Growth, the Dow Jones Transportation Average outpaced the S&P 500 by 13 percentage points over six weeks, and equal-weighted and small-cap indices hit all-time highs even as the Magnificent Seven remained below recent peaks. The catalyst appears to have been a sequence of AI capability releases — from Anthropic and Google, among others — that investors interpreted not as demand drivers but as displacement threats to incumbent software, insurance-broker, logistics, and financial-data business models. The episode that crystallized this mood arrived Thursday, when Algorhythm Holdings, a micro-cap formerly focused on karaoke equipment, promoted an AI freight platform, sending C.H. Robinson down 15% and Landstar 16% — a reaction that, while extreme, illustrated how quickly AI narratives are being mapped onto equity valuations. Goldman's trading desk flagged additional selling pressure from CTA unwinds; Friday's bounces ran on thin volume and short-covering rather than fresh conviction. Q4 blended earnings growth reached roughly 14.5%, a four-year high, with eight of eleven sectors positive — providing a fundamental floor that may limit the downside but has not prevented the rotation.

Monetary Policy and Fed Succession

The delayed January payrolls report — postponed by a brief government shutdown — arrived Wednesday with a 130,000 print that temporarily anchored the hawkish case. Treasury yields rose on the data, the 10-year having briefly fallen back below its 200-day moving average earlier in the week on soft December retail sales and a benign Q4 employment cost index. The January CPI release, expected to show core inflation easing toward 2.5%, will be the next critical input. Fed succession dynamics added an unusual overlay: President Trump claimed nominee Kevin Warsh could deliver 15% GDP growth — a figure detached from any plausible potential-output estimate — while separately, Judy Shelton resurfaced in policy discussions with Treasury Secretary Bessent, including conversations about redeploying the $2.9 trillion in bank reserves parked at the Fed. Treasury Secretary Bessent also urged the Senate to accelerate Warsh confirmation hearings, despite a Republican senator's pledge to block nominations pending resolution of a criminal investigation involving Chair Powell. The MOVE index continued its steady decline from April's Liberation Day peak, suggesting bond markets are treating the institutional uncertainty as manageable for now — though that complacency may face tests once Warsh arrives in May.

Trade, Fiscal, and Corporate Developments

The Republican House vote to terminate Canadian tariffs — passed with six GOP crossovers — exposed meaningful fissures within the party heading into midterm primaries, with swing-district members increasingly sensitive to affordability pressures. Trump's economic approval ratings have softened as Democrats outperformed in recent special elections. Washington finalized a trade pact with Taiwan featuring $44 billion in US LNG and crude purchases and $15 billion in civil aircraft orders — a deal with both commercial and strategic dimensions given Taiwan's semiconductor centrality. The EPA's rescission of the 2009 endangerment finding sets up prolonged litigation and potentially constrains future climate rulemaking regardless of political control, a development with long-run implications for utility capex planning and corporate emissions strategy. In corporate news, Ford guided to higher 2026 profits despite a $900 million Q4 tariff charge; Cisco fell roughly 8% on soft guidance tied to rising memory costs; Cloudflare rose 14% on a strong outlook; and Goldman Sachs general counsel Kathy Ruemmler announced her summer departure following renewed scrutiny of Epstein ties.

Power Infrastructure as a Structural Constraint

The Goldman Sachs analysis projecting data centers will absorb 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation near 6% through 2027, arrived alongside the IEA's annual outlook framing the coming decade as the "Age of Electricity." The policy response — pressing hyperscalers to internalize generation capex — addresses the optics but leaves open the critical question of cost allocation between commercial and residential ratepayers. If non-AI consumers absorb a disproportionate share, headline electricity inflation could run higher still, complicating the Fed's disinflation narrative and sharpening the political stakes around November's midterms. The used EV market offered a parallel bifurcation: used sales rose 13.5% year-over-year in Q4 to nearly 89,000 units, while new EV sales fell 36% following the expiration of federal purchase incentives — a divergence with implications for OEM residual values and dealer economics. ---

Asia

  • Prime Minister Takaichi's LDP secured a two-thirds lower-house supermajority — the party's largest postwar single-party mandate — sending the Nikkei 225 up nearly 4% on Monday to a record high and reshaping Japan's fiscal-monetary outlook.

  • China-Japan consumer tensions mounted after Takaichi's Taiwan comments, with bookings to Sapporo projected to fall 62% in January-February as domestic Chinese resort alternatives surged.

Japan: Takaichi's Mandate and Its Tensions

The week's most consequential single event in Asia was Prime Minister Takaichi's snap-election landslide, which delivered a result prediction markets had assigned roughly even odds just a fortnight prior. The Nikkei 225 surged nearly 4% to a record on Monday and added 2.8% on Tuesday, while JGB yields — which had spiked sharply in January — edged lower and the yen steadied. Finance Minister Katayama's assurance that a proposed two-year food consumption tax cut would not require additional bond issuance provided an important initial anchor, helping to decouple the equity rally from bond-market alarm. By mid-week, the yen had strengthened to ¥153 from near ¥160 in January, and 30-year JGB yields had fallen roughly 40 basis points from January highs — a combination of equity gains and bond stability that the new prime minister needs to sustain. The structural tension, however, remains unresolved. Planned fiscal expansion sits against a debt-to-GDP ratio of approximately 230%, and Takaichi's well-documented skepticism toward BOJ rate hikes creates an awkward configuration at a time when domestic inflation has not fully receded. Evercore ISI frames the downside as a "fat right tail" — a Japanese analogue to the UK's 2022 Truss episode — should political authority erode fiscal restraint. Washington and Tokyo are meanwhile reported to be narrowing the first projects under the $550 billion investment vehicle tied to last year's trade deal, spanning SoftBank-led data centers, a Gulf of Mexico oil terminal, and synthetic diamonds for semiconductors. The China relationship adds a further complication: Takaichi's comments on a potential Taiwan military response have cooled Beijing's posture, with measurable economic consequences in tourism flows, and Xi faces a genuine strategic dilemma over whether to engage Japan's most popular postwar leader or maintain studied distance given Tokyo's alignment with Washington.

China: Sentiment Inflection, Uneven Fundamentals

Foreign positioning toward Chinese equities continued to improve, with Goldman Sachs and BNP Paribas surveys pointing to accelerating allocations, Qube's China long-only fund growing more than tenfold year-on-year to $2 billion, and Eight Roads reversing plans to sell approximately $400 million in Chinese tech stakes — a reversal reflecting improved valuations and a lower temperature in US-China relations. January portfolio inflows into EM broadly approached $100 billion, with allocations broad-based across China and ex-China. Against this, the fundamental picture remains mixed. January factory-gate deflation moderated more than expected, though Morgan Stanley's Robin Xing cautioned there is "no decisive reflation yet," and the improvement leaned heavily on the global metals rally. Consumption fragility was underscored by the abrupt closure of all Shanghai outlets of the premium restaurant chain Xiaonanguo days before Lunar New Year. SMIC warned that breakneck AI chip spending may be pulling forward years of demand, raising the prospect of underutilized data center capacity — a notable internal caution from within the supply chain. Regulators moved to end the auto-sector price war by banning below-cost sales and tightening oversight of rebate programs, addressing Beijing's deflationary concerns, though enforcement efficacy against deeply entrenched discounting practices remains uncertain. The PBOC injected $86.4 billion via 14-day repos ahead of the Lunar New Year holiday, with Industrial Securities forecasting over $500 billion in total liquidity provision.

Regional Developments: India, Korea, Indonesia, and Beyond

India's week was complicated by shifting US trade-deal language: Washington revised its public fact sheet, removing references to pulses and softening language on Indian commitments to American goods, compounding confusion already flagged by farmer groups and raising questions about the deal's domestic political durability for Prime Minister Modi. GQG Partners nonetheless continued adding to a $24 billion India position, and Goldman Sachs vaulted to fourth in Indian equity underwriting in 2025, overtaking Morgan Stanley in stock sales for the first time in a decade. South Korea's KOSPI gained approximately 30% year-to-date, supported by AI hardware exposure, shareholder-return reforms, and relatively attractive valuations, though the market continues to trade at a governance discount to regional peers. Indonesia ordered Weda Bay Nickel — the world's largest nickel mine — to cut permitted output to 12 million tons from 42 million, an aggressive supply-side intervention aimed at supporting global prices with immediate implications for stainless steel and battery material markets. Singapore's Prime Minister Wong unveiled a budget centered on AI adaptation and trade fragmentation, while the city-state upgraded its 2026 growth outlook on AI-infrastructure demand. Taiwan received a formal trade pact with the US featuring $44 billion in LNG and crude purchases and $15 billion in civil aircraft orders, reinforcing the island's strategic and economic centrality. The RBA's Deputy Governor Hauser reiterated that Australian inflation remains "too high," maintaining the hawkish tilt that distinguishes the RBA from most developed-market peers. ---

UK: Political Crisis and Market Containment

The week's most consequential European development was the intensification of Prime Minister Keir Starmer's political difficulties, rooted in the fallout from the 2024 appointment of Peter Mandelson as ambassador to Washington. The disclosure that Mandelson had shared market-sensitive policy decisions with Jeffrey Epstein while serving as deputy prime minister compounded an already damaging narrative, prompting the resignation of Starmer's chief of staff and communications director within 48 hours. Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar's public call for Starmer to stand down on Monday represented the most significant internal rupture to date; prediction markets by mid-week priced Starmer's departure by end-June. Gilt yields rose following Sarwar's intervention, and hedge funds extended short positions in sterling against the euro, citing the combination of political turmoil, Bank of England rate-cut expectations, and elevated options demand. Starmer secured a temporary reprieve by winning public backing from his full cabinet — including Wes Streeting and Ed Miliband — though allies and analysts alike characterized the relief as tactical rather than strategic, with would-be challengers understood to be biding their time. The succession question may prove the more durable concern for fixed income: the most plausible candidates sit to Starmer's left and are perceived as more fiscally expansive, adding a medium-term risk premium that the 2022 Truss episode has made gilt traders especially sensitive to. Fourth-quarter GDP barely expanded, with services stagnating, amplifying the political pressure on an already weakened government.

Corporate: BP's Inflection and Broader Earnings

BP's week-long deterioration — shares fell 5% on Tuesday and extended to a decline of as much as 7.7% on Wednesday after executives offered no timeline for restoring buybacks — crystallized a strategic divergence that incoming CEO Meg O'Neill inherits rather than created. With roughly $60 billion in total debt and financial obligations, the suspension of shareholder returns is arithmetically defensible; the problem is competitive isolation. TotalEnergies trimmed but maintained its buyback; Chevron, Exxon, and Shell held theirs steady. The contrast will weigh on BP's relative valuation until a credible deleveraging and returns pathway is communicated. Elsewhere on the corporate tape, Nuveen's $13.5 billion acquisition of Schroders at a 29% premium ended more than two centuries of independence for the UK's largest standalone asset manager, preserving the brand and London headquarters while raising the question of what the transaction implies for mid-tier European asset managers facing similar structural pressures. Kering rose 10% in Paris on a smaller-than-expected Gucci sales decline and a special dividend — a welcome exception to the difficult luxury narrative reinforced by L'Oréal's disappointing sales on Thursday. Adyen sank 20% in Amsterdam and Dassault Systèmes fell around 20% in Paris, extending the AI-displacement selloff that has now reached European software and fintech. Heineken rose 3.6% after announcing a roughly 7% global workforce reduction in response to softening beer demand. Elliott Investment Management disclosed a stake in London Stock Exchange Group, a development that may catalyze strategic pressure on management as the exchange contends with AI-related disruption and a sustained decline in listings.

Macro and Policy: Norway, France, and EU Competitiveness

Norway's January CPI surprise was the week's sharpest country-specific macro signal, driving 10-year yields to levels not seen since early 2009 and shifting OIS pricing toward a March Norges Bank hike — a notable divergence from the broad DM easing trajectory. Eurozone yields fell on the same session, suggesting markets treated the Norwegian print as idiosyncratic given the energy-heavy economy's historically erratic inflation dynamics, rather than a regional signal. In France, Bank of France Governor François Villeroy de Galhau's unexpected announcement of a June departure — roughly 16 months ahead of schedule — handed President Macron the opportunity to make a six-year appointment ahead of presidential elections in which Le Pen or Bardella are polling competitively. Given Bardella's stated preference for ECB quantitative easing to address French debt, the political stakes around the choice are material for European monetary architecture. At the EU level, the competitiveness debate sharpened: Macron pressed his "Made in Europe" agenda ahead of a key summit, Chancellor Merz pushed back against protectionism while signaling openness to reworking the EU Emissions Trading System if compliance costs weigh materially on industry — the steepest drop in EU carbon prices since 2022 followed — and ECB Executive Board member Schnabel called for accelerated structural reform, underscoring official concern that monetary policy alone cannot close the growth gap with the US. ---

Oil Markets and Iran Diplomacy

The first round of US-Iran nuclear negotiations in Oman concluded on a constructive note last week, with a second round scheduled for this week, but oil markets proved reluctant to fully discount the geopolitical risk premium. Brent remained up more than 10% year-to-date even as it tracked toward back-to-back weekly declines, with SEB's Bjarne Schieldrop capturing the market's caution: "the Iranian risk premium cannot be fully defused as long as US warships are located where they are." The fundamental tension is structural — meaningful divergence persists between Iran's insistence that talks remain confined to its nuclear program and Secretary of State Rubio's demand for a broader framework covering ballistic missiles and proxy financing. Trump indicated he could accept a narrower deal, offering a politically expedient win ahead of midterms, but Netanyahu traveled to Washington Wednesday explicitly to press for tighter constraints, and prior negotiations with Tehran culminated in US participation in Israel's 12-day strike campaign — a reminder that the path is not linear. At Thursday's London oil trading summit, sentiment was conflicted: bears cited ample supply and current price levels as evidence the market is pricing fundamentals correctly, while bulls pointed to sanctioned Russian and Iranian crude accumulating on tankers and argued unsanctioned barrels show little sign of the much-discussed surplus. Pimco's Greg Sharenow summarized the prevailing mood: "The uncertainty band is wider than we would have thought six or 10 weeks ago."

Monetary Policy Divergence

The week produced some of the sharpest cross-country monetary policy divergence observed this cycle. Norway's January CPI surprise drove 10-year sovereign yields to levels not seen since early 2009, shifting OIS pricing toward a March hike. A senior BOJ hawk signaled that conditions for Japan's next rate hike could align by spring, even as Prime Minister Takaichi's fiscal expansion and implicit skepticism toward BOJ tightening complicate the institutional backdrop. Russia's central bank delivered an unexpected rate cut; South Africa's Reserve Bank is now expected to cut three further times; Peru held steady. In the US, the January payrolls beat pushed the expected first Fed cut from June to July, though the two-cut consensus for the year remained intact. The MOVE index continued its steady decline from last April's Liberation Day peak, suggesting bond markets are treating the political noise around Fed succession — including Trump's claim that Warsh could deliver 15% GDP growth — as manageable for now. Whether that equanimity persists once Warsh formally arrives in May and faces his first inflation test remains to be seen.

Electricity, AI, and the Energy Transition

The IEA's annual power report, framing the coming half-decade as the "Age of Electricity," provided the week's most consequential thematic input. The projection that global electricity consumption will grow roughly 50% faster than in the prior decade — and outpace GDP growth — marks a structural break with historical relationships that carries implications across capex cycles, utility regulation, and inflation dynamics. Goldman Sachs economists Abecasis and Wei project data centers will account for 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation holding near 6% through 2027 — a figure that would complicate the Fed's disinflation narrative and the cost-of-living politics shaping November's midterms. China's ability to add generating capacity at a pace that materially exceeds the US build-out positions it on a more favorable power-cost curve for AI infrastructure, even as US export controls constrain its position in advanced chips. Europe's lag in the AI buildout has incidentally reduced its grid strain, though the competitive liability likely outweighs that modest offset. Against this, China's CO2 emissions fell 0.3% in 2025 — the first non-pandemic annual decline, as renewables absorbed all incremental power demand — while the US moved in the opposite direction by rescinding the 2009 endangerment finding. The policy divergence between the two largest emitters appears increasingly structural and will shape investment flows, trade relations, and geopolitical competition through the decade.

The dollar's softening provided the mechanical tailwind, but the rotation also reflects a deliberate reappraisal of US asset concentration risk among a broad range of institutional investors.
