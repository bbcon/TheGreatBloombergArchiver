**Energy and the Strait of Hormuz**
The US sanctions waiver for Iran represents a meaningful de-escalation signal, but the downstream effects on global inflation remain unresolved. Brent's modest 7% gain since the conflict began reflects markets' prior assumption of a contained, temporary disruption — meaning the upside from a resolution was always limited. More consequential may be the lagged pass-through still working through the system: natural gas prices typically take three to four months to reach household bills, jet fuel surcharges have yet to be fully passed on to airfares, and fertilizer costs — elevated by both Strait disruption and higher gas-based production costs — have yet to fully feed through to food prices. A fifth of global ammonia-based fertilizer exports originate in the Gulf. Emerging market central banks, already operating in an environment where the FOMC has tilted hawkish, cannot yet declare the inflation episode over.

**Monetary Policy Divergence**
The Fed's hawkish shift is reverberating globally. EM central banks face a familiar dilemma: domestic inflation elevated by the energy shock, a stronger dollar environment if the Fed hikes, and limited room to ease. Japan's finance minister held talks with Treasury Secretary Bessent, providing only a brief boost to the yen, which remains near four-decade lows — raising the risk of intervention. The ECB's Lagarde called for any G7 currency discussions to include China, underscoring the degree to which currency misalignment has become a systemic concern alongside the energy shock.
