**Monetary Policy**
Kevin Warsh's inaugural FOMC meeting produced the expected hold, but the surrounding communication represented a meaningful hawkish shift. The committee statement was cut from 341 to 130 words, stripped of forward guidance and all reference to the maximum employment mandate; the sole concluding line read "The Committee will deliver price stability." Warsh declined to submit his own dot plot, signaling intent to de-emphasize that tool, though his colleagues' projections moved sharply: the average end-2026 rate rose to 3.83%, up from 3.24%. Goldman's Rob Kaplan suggested the Fed may need to act as soon as September if inflation remains elevated. Warsh also announced five task forces covering communications, the balance sheet, alternative data, AI and productivity, and the inflation framework — signaling structural regime change whose outcomes are unlikely to materialise until year-end. The 10-year TIPS yield rose to its highest since May 2025, and the dollar recorded its strongest two-day rally in three months, complicating the administration's stated preference for currency weakness.

**Industrial Production and Data**
May factory output was little changed following April's upwardly revised 0.7% gain, with total industrial production rising only 0.1% — the first meaningful deceleration after four consecutive months of gains. The Federal Reserve's own data suggests supply-chain disruptions linked to the Hormuz closure and elevated tariff-driven input costs are beginning to restrain activity. May PPI of 6.5% YoY confirms those cost pressures remain acute, leaving the Fed with limited room to look through inflation even as growth softens.

**Equity and Corporate Markets**
Risk assets oscillated across the week. S&P 500 futures gained 1.2–1.3% Monday on Hormuz relief, paused Tuesday as deal-durability concerns emerged, slumped on the hawkish Fed Wednesday, then partially recovered Thursday as Iran interim deal optimism and lower oil prices provided an offset. The Nasdaq outperformed throughout, supported by SpaceX momentum — the stock rallied approximately 49% over four sessions to a $2.75 trillion market cap, overtaking Amazon to become the fifth-largest stock globally, and is preparing a bond offering of at least $20 billion. Travel stocks led Monday's gains, with United Airlines up 4.3% and Delta up 3.9%, while Exxon and Chevron each fell roughly 2.5%. Nvidia received $85 billion in orders for a $25 billion investment-grade bond sale. The DOJ cleared Paramount-Skydance's $110 billion acquisition of Warner Bros. Discovery. A record $119.2 billion flowed into US equity funds in the week through Wednesday per BofA/EPFR data, though BofA warned a Republican Senate loss represents the clearest near-term downside scenario.
