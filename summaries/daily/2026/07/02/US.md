• June nonfarm payrolls report due today — Bloomberg Economics forecasts a beat above May's already-strong print, boosted by World Cup-related leisure and hospitality hiring.
• Fed Chair Warsh at ECB Sintra forum reiterated abandonment of forward guidance; yield curve bear-flattening as two-year yields rise faster than tens, signaling "higher for longer" expectations.
• JOLTS job openings for May printed above pre-pandemic 2019 highs, offering no case for near-term rate relief.
• Washington confirmed it will not renew the USMCA, opting instead for annual reviews — materially raising trade uncertainty across $1.6 trillion in North American commerce.

**Monetary Policy**
Fed Chair Kevin Warsh used his global-stage debut at the ECB's Sintra forum to reinforce his inflation-hawk credentials, stating that inflation expectations have declined over the first four weeks of his chairmanship and that "inflation risks have come down." He doubled down on abandoning forward guidance, a stance the market is absorbing through a bear-flattening yield curve — two-year Treasury yields rising sharply relative to ten-year yields — which analysts at Global Liquidity Indexes interpret as consistent with tighter financial conditions rather than benign disinflation. Bloomberg Economics expects rates to remain on hold through 2026, with cuts deferred to 2027.

**Labor Market and Trade Policy**
JOLTS openings for May moved back above pre-pandemic highs, reinforcing the picture of a resilient labor market that gives the Fed scant justification to ease. The June payrolls print, due today, is expected to confirm continued momentum. Separately, the administration's decision to forgo USMCA renewal in favor of annual reviews introduces a structural layer of uncertainty for manufacturers operating North American supply chains, with Mexican and Canadian officials now forced to anticipate opaque yearly renegotiation terms.
