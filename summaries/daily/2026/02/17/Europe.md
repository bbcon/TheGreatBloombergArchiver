Eurozone macro signals were mixed today. German investor optimism, as measured by the ZEW survey, unexpectedly declined, tempering hopes that Europe's largest economy is on the cusp of the strong recovery markets had begun to price in. Set against that, eurozone finance chiefs used the day to push for a larger global role for the euro — a theme gaining traction as US policy unpredictability weighs on dollar credibility, though concrete steps remain limited. Bloomberg Economics flagged a broader concern relevant to ECB watchers: Australia's surprise rate hike, premised on downgraded productivity and a higher neutral rate, may foreshadow a similar reassessment in Europe, where weak productivity has so far been acknowledged but not yet operationalized into policy. For now, the ECB's framing remains anchored on demand and disinflation.

In the UK, the macro picture tilted decisively dovish. Unemployment rose to its highest level since the pandemic and wage growth eased further, prompting traders to fully price half a point of Bank of England rate cuts by year-end. BBVA's Roberto Cobo Garcia suggested a March cut now appears increasingly likely and that two cuts may prove insufficient given the deteriorating labour market. Politically, Prime Minister Keir Starmer reversed plans to cancel some May local elections following backlash, the latest U-turn under his premiership, and sought to steady his government after the Mandelson-Epstein scandal that briefly threatened his position.

On the corporate side, Germany's Hapag-Lloyd agreed to acquire ZIM Integrated Shipping at a valuation of roughly $4.2 billion, sending ZIM shares up 35%. Qiagen fell nearly 5% in Frankfurt after reports that Danaher is nearing a roughly $10 billion deal for Masimo, reducing the probability of a Qiagen takeout. Novo Nordisk confirmed expanded investment at its Athlone, Ireland facility to produce Wegovy in oral form for non-US markets, reinforcing Ireland's pharmaceutical export base even as Trump continues to single out the country's $112 billion goods surplus with the US. In Denmark, Prime Minister Mette Frederiksen is weighing a snap election to capitalise on her recovered standing following the Greenland dispute with Washington. The Stoxx Europe 600 closed roughly flat.
