Housing Woes

Donald Trump’s administration, just like Joe Biden’s, has argued that American 
households are increasingly better off because their incomes are growing faster 
than consumer prices. But voters keep on rejecting that argument.

The data suggest officials aren’t wrong. The latest average hourly earnings 
figure, for December, showed a 3.8% increase from 12 months before — at least a 
full percentage point higher than any of the main gauges of US inflation, 
whether CPI or PCE or headline or core.

Calculations from Motio Research, a startup that produces household income 
<https://motioresearch.com/household-income-series/> insights using Census 
Bureau data, also show that the median household saw an increase of more than 
1% over the 12 months to December.

And yet last week’s consumer confidence report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-27/us-consumer-confidence-plummets-to-lowest-level-since-2014?cmpid=BBD020426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260204>
 showed the worst sentiment in more than a decade. Much of that deterioration 
stems from concerns about the job market. But when it comes to costs, housing 
is key, as a Monday note from Goldman Sachs economist Elsie Peng, detailed.

“Real disposable income, both in aggregate and for each income quintile, 
returned a while ago to roughly its pre-pandemic trend,” Peng wrote. “But 
housing, particularly owner-occupied housing, continues to present a major 
affordability challenge, especially for low-income families.”

She lists a number of figures that illustrate the challenge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-31/trump-s-housing-affordability-plans-are-stalling?cmpid=BBD020426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260204>
, for both would-be homeowners and for renters:

 * The bottom fifth of American earners shells out some 55% of income for 
rent, with the next quintile at 40%. Both measures are up roughly 5 percentage 
points from 2000.
 * “For young married couples considering buying their first home, the average 
down payment is now 70% of their annual household income.” That’s up from 58% 
in 2019 and 45% in 2000.
 * “For households living in large metropolitan areas or in the bottom income 
quintile, the combined cost of the down payment and first-year mortgage has 
risen from 90-120% of income in 2000 to 160-200% today.” 
Housing is more important than other costs for many reasons, of course, but 
Peng highlights two. Especially for lower-income households, it’s a key 
mechanism for building wealth. It can also be vital to obtain quality schooling 
for children, along with other public amenities.

High housing costs mean “higher barriers to building wealth and more limited 
access to education services, job opportunities in large and growing cities, 
and long-term social mobility,” Peng wrote. So no wonder it’s a politically 
charged issue.

Bessent said last year the administration was going to announce an 
affordability czar <https://x.com/theallinpod/status/1902223565203665084?t=1965>
. If and when that happens, whoever gets the job may want to prioritize housing.

Need-to-Know Research

The tariff shocks of the past eight years have undone more than two decades of 
trade integration between the world’s two largest economies, with China’s share 
of US imports falling to 9% as of the middle of last year, back to where it was 
a quarter century ago when China became a member of the World Trade 
Organization.

But the fast-moving “Great Reallocation” that started during the first Trump 
administration and has accelerated since his return to the White House has so 
far amounted to “selective decoupling” with China, not an all-out retreat into 
deglobalization, Laura Alfaro at Harvard University and Davin Chor at Dartmouth 
College wrote in a working paper.
An Anatomy of the Great Reallocation in US Supply Chain Trade (NBER Working 
Paper 34490)
The authors, who looked at more than 5,300 product categories from US Census 
Bureau data over the 2017-2025 period, found that the reduced dependence on 
China has so far benefited countries that were already among the US’s largest 
trade partners, especially Vietnam, Mexico and Taiwan.

“This diversification has been largely limited to a reshuffling of import 
shares among the top-20 import trade partners of the US,” they said in the 
paper, published by the National Bureau of Economic Research.
