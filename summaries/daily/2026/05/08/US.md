The dominant financial news of the day centers on growing anxiety over US sovereign debt sustainability. DoubleLine Capital's Jeffrey Gundlach disclosed he is repositioning portfolios for an extreme scenario in which Washington restructures its obligations — potentially cutting coupons to as low as 1% — though he places the probability below 30%. The disclosure is notable less for its immediacy than for its direction: Gundlach joins Apollo's Marc Rowan and Bridgewater's Ray Dalio in issuing unusually direct warnings about the trajectory of US fiscal and financial conditions. US public debt held by the public has now surpassed $31 trillion, exceeding annual GDP, though five-year CDS-implied default probabilities remain below 1%, underscoring the gap between market pricing and investor rhetoric.

On trade and fiscal policy, a US federal trade court declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful in a fresh legal setback, following the Supreme Court's earlier vacating of prior levies. The administration has given the EU until July 4 to ratify a trade deal or face higher duties, signaling continued assertiveness despite the judicial headwinds. The April non-farm payrolls report, expected to show a solid 65,000 increase, will be closely watched for signs of labor market resilience.

In equity markets, S&P 500 futures edged higher on a technology-led rally, with Nasdaq 100 futures up 0.7%. Cloudflare fell as much as 17% in premarket trading after announcing job cuts alongside a disappointing revenue forecast, while Trade Desk sank 15% on a guidance miss. Block advanced 7.3% after AI-driven cost restructuring improved its profit outlook. Consumer stress remains a subtext: Kraft Heinz's CEO warned that lower-income shoppers are "literally running out of money at the end of the month," with elevated gasoline prices — a direct consequence of the Iran conflict — compounding pressure on household budgets.

New York Governor Kathy Hochul moved to soften the state's landmark climate law, pushing the emissions-enforcement deadline to 2028 from an immediate court-ordered timeline, citing energy affordability concerns. The decision reflects a broader pattern across US states where Iran war-induced fuel price increases are weakening political support for near-term climate mandates.
