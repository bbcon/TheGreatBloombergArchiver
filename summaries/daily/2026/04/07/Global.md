The defining macro variable of April 2026 remains the Strait of Hormuz, effectively closed since the US-Israeli offensive against Iran began on February 27. The waterway normally channels approximately one-fifth of global oil and LNG supply; its sustained blockage has driven Brent crude up roughly 50% since the conflict began, with WTI prompt spreads trading near record backwardation levels—close to $15.50 per barrel at one point Monday—signaling acute near-term supply anxiety. Trump's 8 p.m. Tuesday deadline for Iran to agree to a deal including Hormuz reopening represents the latest in a series of ultimatums that have each produced market volatility without resolution; Iran continued missile and drone attacks across the Gulf in the hours before the deadline, clouding the outlook.

The secondary effects are now widespread. The Fed Reserve Bank of New York's Global Supply Chain Pressure Index rose in March to its highest since January 2023. Asia is bearing the greatest burden: multiple countries have reported fuel shortages, and some have implemented four-day working weeks. The conflict is accelerating a coal renaissance in Asia as LNG alternatives become unavailable, while simultaneously generating a meaningful uplift in EV demand—UK registrations hit a record 86,120 units in March, and similar trends are visible in Thailand and the US used-car market. The IEA has described the supply disruption as the largest in market history, and OPEC+ has warned of prolonged output impacts even after hostilities end.

The dollar has risen roughly 2.2% since hostilities began, partially reversing an 8%-plus decline in 2025, though analysts including Robin Brooks of Brookings argue the move reflects safe-haven flows rather than a structural shift and project a further 10% trade-weighted decline this year driven by political pressure on the Fed to cut rates despite elevated inflation. Foreign central bank holdings at the Federal Reserve Bank of New York have fallen approximately $82 billion since late February to $2.7 trillion—the lowest since 2012—as Gulf producers liquidate Treasuries to defend their currencies and fund wartime expenditures, a structural erosion of the petrodollar recycling mechanism. The UN Security Council was due to vote on a softened Hormuz resolution hours before Trump's deadline, with Russian and Chinese vetoes remaining possible.
