The defining market dynamic of March 3 was the collision between a genuine geopolitical shock and an already-complicated global inflation and monetary policy outlook. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively halted for tanker traffic, Brent crude rose nearly 5% on Tuesday to top $80 a barrel, and has now reached levels last seen in July 2024. European natural gas prices surged more than 20% for a second consecutive session following Iranian drone attacks on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility — the world's largest, accounting for approximately one-fifth of global LNG supply. QatarEnergy has provided no timeline for restart, and the uncertainty is acute: if the outage extends for weeks, Europe and Asia will compete for a finite pool of alternative supply, pushing prices materially higher. The 2022 energy crisis offers a reference point, though current price levels remain far below that episode's peak.

The monetary policy implications are increasingly difficult for central banks to navigate. A sustained $20-per-barrel oil shock could add up to one percentage point to global inflation, according to Société Générale, at a moment when most major economies have not yet returned inflation to target. Traders are unwinding rate-cut expectations across the UK, Turkey, Hungary, and potentially the US, producing the counterintuitive result that sovereign bonds — normally a haven — are selling off alongside equities. Gold wavered after earlier gains, while the US dollar extended its advance, with the DXY index rising almost 1% on Monday and extending gains Tuesday, breaking above its 200-day moving average.

Emerging markets face a differentiated but broadly negative picture. The FTSE EM index fell 2% Monday, its worst session in eleven months, with EM currencies under pressure as the dollar strengthened. Oil-exporting EMs such as Brazil and Colombia stand to benefit from elevated prices, while net importers including Mexico, Chile, and much of Asia face headwinds. The container shipping industry, gathering for its major annual conference in Long Beach, California, effectively abandoned its pre-conflict agenda of managing overcapacity, with leading voices warning that the logistics disruption could define 2026 as completely as prior crises shaped recent years. Global supply chains, only recently recovering from successive shocks, appear to be entering another period of elevated uncertainty.
