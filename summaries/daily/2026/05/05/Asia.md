Asian equity markets pulled back modestly from recent highs in thinned holiday trading, with the Hang Seng declining 0.8%, as the renewed Iran-US exchange of fire rekindled inflation concerns and prompted risk reduction across the region. The most immediate transmission mechanism is through oil-dependent currencies: the Indonesian rupiah set a new record low, the Indian rupee also touched historic lows, and the Philippine peso approached all-time weak levels as investors shed exposure to economies most vulnerable to the energy shock.

Despite the macro headwinds, Indonesia delivered a notably strong first-quarter GDP print of 5.61% year-on-year — a three-year high — supported by record government spending. That pace ranks Indonesia among Asia's fastest-growing large economies, though it trails Taiwan's AI-fueled 13.7% expansion. Hong Kong's economy also expanded at its fastest pace in nearly five years in Q1. The bifurcation between AI-leveraged and oil-exposed economies is becoming a defining feature of the regional growth landscape.

Japan's diplomatic posture during the holiday week merits attention. Prime Minister Takaichi conducted overseas visits to Vietnam and Australia — deepening what both sides characterize as a quasi-alliance — while Defense Minister Koizumi deployed to the Philippines to observe Japanese troops exercising there for the first time since World War II. The conspicuous absence of any substantive China engagement, set against the backdrop of a ruling-party delegation visiting Taiwan and prior statements on potential Japanese military involvement in a Taiwan Strait scenario, suggests the bilateral relationship remains under meaningful strain. A senior LDP lawmaker visited China briefly but met no officials.

The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a third consecutive rate hike in an 8-1 vote, unwinding all of last year's easing. Governor Bullock acknowledged the hardship the cycle is imposing on households but signaled the board has little alternative given pre-existing inflation pressures, a loose fiscal stance, and weak productivity. The RBA now leads developed-market peers in the hawkish response to the energy shock.
