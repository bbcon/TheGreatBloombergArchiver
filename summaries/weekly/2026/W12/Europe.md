**Central Banks Pivot Hawkish**

The week's central bank decisions across Europe represented the most significant monetary policy repricing in the region since the 2022 energy shock. The ECB held its key rate at 2.0% on Thursday, in line with consensus, but dropped language describing policy as being in a "good place" and shifted its forward guidance materially. Traders are now fully pricing two ECB hikes this year — a dramatic reversal from the easing trajectory that prevailed before the conflict began. Governing Council member Joachim Nagel stated Friday that a hike as early as April is conceivable if energy-driven inflation risks materialize, explicitly referencing the post-Ukraine 2022 episode. The Bank of England similarly held rates, but Governor Andrew Bailey's explicit statement that "the prior expectation of scope to cut has changed" drove a sharp repricing in gilts. UK 10-year yields rose to their highest level since 2008, with the move amplified by February borrowing data that came in above forecast due to a surge in debt-interest costs. Sweden's Riksbank also held. The direction of travel across European central banks is now unmistakably toward tighter policy.

**Energy Shock and Market Divergence**

European natural gas futures jumped as much as 35% on Thursday following Iranian missile strikes on Qatar's Ras Laffan complex, pushing benchmark prices to more than double their pre-war levels. Dubai crude contracts for Gulf-loaded oil are up over 80% this month. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 2.0% on Thursday, though it had managed modest gains of 0.3% and 0.6% earlier in the week. France's CAC is down 7.6% in March and Switzerland's SMI off 8.1%. One partial cushion is Europe's expanded renewable fleet — Rabobank estimates electricity prices would be approximately one-third higher absent post-2022 growth in solar and wind capacity, with France's nuclear fleet also operating more reliably than during the prior energy crisis. Evening price spikes nonetheless point to ongoing vulnerability. Germany restricted petrol stations to one price hike per day and tightened antitrust oversight, a measure reflecting political sensitivity rather than substantive market intervention. At the EU Brussels summit, leaders called for a moratorium on strikes against energy infrastructure, with Italian Prime Minister Meloni described as particularly animated.

**Politics and Geopolitics**

Chancellor Friedrich Merz's political standing remains precarious, with recent polling showing fewer than a quarter of Germans hold a positive view of him. Germany's €600 billion defense spending program is generating tangible economic stimulus, with defense suppliers reporting record order books. Trump explicitly warned that the US "will remember" whether NATO allies support efforts to reopen the Strait of Hormuz, adding that insufficient response would be "very bad for the future of NATO" — a pressure that European governments are weighing against years of accumulated skepticism about Washington's reliability. Irish Taoiseach Micheál Martin defended UK Prime Minister Starmer after Trump criticized Britain for declining to join the war effort. Starmer faces mounting pressure from Labour's left flank ahead of anticipated losses in May's local elections, while Nigel Farage's Reform UK continues to lead in national polls but faces fragmentation from newer far-right parties including Restore UK and Advance UK. The European Commission is reported to be in the final stages of concluding a long-delayed trade agreement with Australia.

**Corporate News**

UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank that would allow the Italian lender to increase its shareholding beyond the 30% threshold, easing the path toward a potential full acquisition. Unilever confirmed it is evaluating a separation of its food assets and has received an offer from McCormick for that division, valued by Barclays at approximately €28–31 billion — potentially the largest restructuring in the company's near-century history. HSBC announced it is weighing cuts to approximately 20,000 roles as part of an AI-driven restructuring of its middle and back offices. Logitech fell 5.7% in Zurich after a UBS downgrade on weaker gaming market signals, while HelloFresh dropped 9.6% in Frankfurt after issuing guidance materially below analyst expectations. Prudential Plc reported an increase in new business profit and announced an additional $1.2 billion share buyback. Italy's Giorgia Meloni government is consulting on restarting nuclear energy production as a long-term response to the country's elevated energy prices.
