A crucial summit

Donald Trump is headed to China, where he’ll meet an emboldened Xi Jinping 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/trump-faces-emboldened-xi-in-china-as-iran-war-clips-us-leverage?cmpid=051326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4429>
 for a summit that could have lasting ramifications for markets and the world’s 
two biggest economies.

Trump touches down in Beijing on Wednesday evening. On the agenda are trade, 
tariffs, critical minerals and energy, with the US president looking to secure 
headline-grabbing deals on exports like soybeans and Boeing planes.

One potential outcome that’s getting less attention? According to TS Lombard’s 
Rory Green, a “Beijing Accord” could emerge that includes an implicit agreement 
for China’s currency, the yuan, to appreciate.

This could be something of a win-win. For China, a stronger yuan boosts 
domestic demand, lowers import costs, and supports its long-term goal of 
currency internationalization. For Trump, a relatively weaker dollar is vital 
for his push to revive US manufacturing competitiveness.

Wall Street is split over the yuan’s path 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/morgan-stanley-sees-yuan-rally-slowing-diverging-from-bulls?cmpid=051326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4429>
. Morgan Stanley sees some room for appreciation, but does not view the yuan as 
significantly undervalued. The more bullish camp includes Goldman Sachs, which 
estimates the yuan is more than 20% undervalued.

Memory crunch winners (and losers)

The AI boom is driving huge demand for memory chips 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/deepening-memory-crunch-widens-gap-between-stock-winners-losers?cmpid=051326_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4429>
. That’s great news for companies that make them, and bad news for buyers.

Memory makers like Micron and Samsung have surged to record highs on 
blockbuster results driven by buoyant prices. At the same time, consumer 
product firms from HP to Nintendo have been weighed down by profit pressures 
stemming from higher chip costs.

The latest evidence of the crunch: Memory pricing was mentioned more than 550 
times in company earnings calls and quarterly reports so far this year — 
already more than any full year in data going back to 1999.

Manufacturers are resorting to price increases to cushion margins, even at the 
risk of hurting demand for their products. There’s no relief in sight.

“What we now hear from those close to the issue is that we might see the 
crunch continuing in some manner potentially as far as 2030,” said Michael 
Brown of Pepperstone. —Winnie Hsu 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AV9ybw29-9g/winnie-hsu>
