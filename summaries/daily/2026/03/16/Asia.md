China's January-February economic data released Monday surprised to the upside, offering an encouraging snapshot before the Iran conflict's full weight can register. Industrial production rose 6.3% year-on-year — the fastest pace since September — while retail sales and fixed-asset investment both exceeded forecasts, the latter expanding unexpectedly. The rebound suggests domestic momentum was building heading into the crisis, though analysts cautioned that if the Strait of Hormuz closure persists and stalls export activity, sustaining this trajectory will be difficult.

The geopolitical pressure on Asia is intensifying. China, India, Japan, and South Korea collectively account for roughly 70% of crude shipments transiting Hormuz under normal conditions, making the region the most acutely exposed to the supply disruption. Standard Chartered's Eric Robertsen identified stagflation — not inflation alone — as the primary risk. Asian currencies are under significant pressure, with Indonesia, India, the Philippines, and Taiwan all intervening in foreign-exchange markets over the past two weeks. The yen has approached its 2024 all-time low despite meaningfully higher interest rates, and Japan's finance minister signaled readiness to respond. Japan also began releasing oil reserves.

India finds itself in a particularly difficult position. Prime Minister Modi secured safe passage for two LPG tankers through Hormuz — placing India among a small group of nations, alongside China and Russia, with ships transiting the strait — while simultaneously managing US relations. India takes 90% of its LPG imports from the Middle East, and alternatives from the US would take at least 40 days to arrive at higher cost. State elections in April will test Modi's handling of the energy crisis.
