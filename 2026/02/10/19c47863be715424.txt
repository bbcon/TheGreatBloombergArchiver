Qatar’s LNG Growth Puts Long-Term Sales Plan to the Test
The Gulf nation needs more supply deals to match its plans for capacity growth.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Energy Team Leader Stephen Stapczynski explores why Qatar’s multiple wins 
in securing long-term customers for its LNG may still not be enough to absorb a 
planned surge in supply.

Tiny Gulf state Qatar is a giant in liquefied natural gas 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-03-31/qatar-signs-deals-for-more-ships-ahead-of-massive-lng-expansion?cmpid=BBBXT021026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 and has enjoyed remarkable successlocking in 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-08-26/qatar-set-to-sign-new-15-year-lng-supply-deal-with-kuwait?cmpid=BBBXT021026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 sales of the fuel decades into the future. But to match its ambitious 
expansion plans, it must sell even more.

The nation’s latest wins were on display at last week’s LNG2026 
<https://lng2026.com/> conference in Doha, where state-run QatarEnergy revived 
languishing trade with Japan through a27-year supply deal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-03/qatar-set-to-sign-new-lng-sales-deal-with-japan-utility-jera?cmpid=BBBXT021026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 and even secured Malaysia — a traditional exporter — as a customer.

With these deals added, Qatar has agreed to sell about 39 million tons under 
long-term contracts since 2022, according to BloombergNEF — all to be fed by 
new facilities coming online from this year. That’s more LNG than India, the 
world’s fourth-largest buyer, consumes in a year.

This is a huge achievement. But these deals will absorb only about 60% of 
Qatar’s planned expansion in production — and that’s without including volumes 
from the country’s LNG export project in the US, which is set to start soon 
without long-term buyers nailed down.

Nor does it cover existing contracts, which will expire over the next few 
years with no certainty of renewal.

The most obvious customers have already signed up, leaving fewer new markets 
to crack.Talks with India 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-26/india-s-lng-buyers-stall-deals-as-they-await-record-supply-wave?cmpid=BBBXT021026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
, for example, have stalled as Qatar has resisted cutting prices.

This is forcing a rethink. For decades, Qatar has sold almost all its LNG 
under long-term contracts — an approach that has insulated the country from the 
volatility of the spot market.

That model, however, will no longer be sufficient as the volumes committed lag 
behind the pace of capacity growth. Qatar knows this, and has been building its 
expertise in spot sales.

With less urgency to push out volumes to meet contractual commitments, the 
country may even slow the ramp-up of new supply. Qatar was already forced to
push back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/qatar-pushes-start-of-its-massive-lng-expansion-to-end-2026?cmpid=BBBXT021026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260210>
 the start of its multibillion-dollar expansion amid supply-chain bottlenecks.

In a worst-case scenario, its growth ambitions could be trimmed. And given 
Qatar’s outsized role in LNG, any shift in strategy would have repercussions 
throughout the global market.

—Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Renewable jet fuel supply surged 157% last year as other key fuels saw sharp 
declines, according toBloombergNEF <>. Overall US biofuel supply contracted, 
led by biodiesel and renewable diesel, following changes to tax credits. While 
renewable jet fuel imports were more resilient, the strong growth was primarily 
driven by higher domestic production, which made up 84% of supply.
