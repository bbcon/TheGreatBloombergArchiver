You can bet on it

By Eric Roston <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQdgGkZhizQ/eric-roston>

Everybody talks about the weather, but nobody puts their money where their 
mouth is. At least, until recently.

The rapid rise in popularity of prediction markets, such as Kalshi and 
Polymarket, has made it so you can wager on just about anything online, at any 
time.

Bets on how hot Denver will be tomorrow or if 2026 will be the hottest-ever 
year make up a small percentage of overall prediction market volume. TheyтАЩve 
grown in attention proportional to the platforms themselves, particularly for 
short-term weather. Trading volume over a late January snowstorm in New York 
surpassed $6 million. The weather markets are so active that at least one 
startup is testing its commercial forecasting software on the markets.
Photo Illustration by Ezekiel Aquino for Bloomberg; Video Source: Getty Images 
(2)
Last month, Patrick Brown, head of climate analytics at Interactive Brokers, 
looked at how traditional weather forecasts stack up against prediction markets 
and found the markets outperformed the US National Weather Service.

The ability of markets to discover useful information is the motivation for 
lower-profile, bespoke prediction markets more in line with the vision that 
famous economists have long wondered about.

Mark Roulston, a PhD planetary scientist, years ago developed a passion for 
prediction markets as a way to help institutions extract hidden information 
from diverse groups of researchers, who are invited to bet on their expertise 
using other peopleтАЩs money. HeтАЩs the operating director of a Lancaster 
University platform that asks experts to compete in forecasting medium-term 
weather and climate events, like cyclones or El Ni├▒o phases of the Pacific 
Ocean.

тАЬWe create markets not because we think, тАШOh, this is something lots of people 
will want to bet on,тАШтАЭ Roulston said. тАЬWe create a market because thereтАЩs an 
end user тАФ like a reinsurance company тАФ that says тАШWe would like better 
predictions of this.тАЩтАЭ

While public weather prediction markets have shown some degree of value, the 
same is less likely for climate change. The complexity of how rising carbon 
dioxide is impacting the whole planet тАФ let alone massive natural climate 
patterns like El Ni├▒o тАФ may make the topic unsuited for popular online 
prediction markets, said Madison Condon, an associate professor of law at 
Boston University, who writes about climate models and projections.

ThereтАЩs definitely space for scientists to pool knowledge outside of complex 
Earth system models, she said, but markets may not be тАЬthe best way for 
synthesizing that domain knowledge or expert judgment.тАЭ

тАЬItтАЩs not like a basketball game,тАЭ she said. Projecting what climate change 
may do to hurricanes, or if the next El Ni├▒o is on its way, тАЬis just a
