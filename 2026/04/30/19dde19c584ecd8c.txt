Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Energy Team Leader Stephen Stapczynski looks at China’s role in keeping a 
lid on natural gas prices during the Iran war.

Hopes for a quick reopening of the Strait of Hormuz are fading fast <>, 
sending oil towartime highs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-30?cmpid=BBBXT043026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=4000>
. Natural gas prices, however, remain relatively calm.

While Brent crude has jumped by about a third since the beginning of last 
week, European gas is still about 20% below the level reached in the early days 
of the conflict.

It isn’t that gas is suffering less of a disruption. The Iran conflict has 
removed about 7 million tons a month of the liquefied form of the fuel from 
global markets, roughly a fifth of last year’s supply and more than the 
comparable proportion for oil.

The big difference is that demand destruction 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/iran-war-pushes-asia-to-think-twice-before-doubling-down-on-lng?cmpid=BBBXT043026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=4000>
 has happened a lot more in natural gasthan for oil 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-25/the-hormuz-billion-barrel-oil-shock-is-about-to-crash-demand?cmpid=BBBXT043026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=4000>
. That’s particularly true in Asia, where buyers are more dependent on the 
Middle Eastern LNG.

Their restraint is a gift to the wider market, freeing up more shipments for 
rival buyers in Europe and helping to keep a lid on prices.
A storage tank at a Sinopec LNG plant in Chongqing, China.Photographer: Na 
Bian/Bloomberg
China is the biggest exemplar 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/china-s-april-lng-imports-set-to-hit-eight-year-low-kpler-says?cmpid=BBBXT043026_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=4000>
 of this trend. The world’s top LNG importer is on track to cut its April 
purchases by nearly 1.3 million tons from a year earlier, following a roughly 1 
million-ton reduction in March, according to data from Kpler. No other country 
comes close to matching that decline.

The pullback is possible thanks to years of planning by Beijing. China has 
steadily increased pipeline imports and domestic gas production, giving it 
greater flexibility to curb LNG buying on the spot market when prices go too 
high. Chinese companies also resell contracted cargoes to overseas buyers 
willing to pay more.

However, China’s retreat may be nearing its limits. The cost of domestic 
trucked LNG — a key benchmark — has climbed in Guangdong, an industrial hub 
heavily reliant on gas. Higher local prices could incentivize China to import 
more.

Flows are already edging up. The 30-day moving average of China’s LNG imports, 
which has hovered near a five-year seasonal low, is beginning to rise.

It that’s sustained, the global fight for gas supplies could be back on.

—Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News
