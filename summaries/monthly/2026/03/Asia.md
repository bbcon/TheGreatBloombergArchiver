**Regional Tariff Arithmetic and Early Trade Disruption**

Asia's month opened with significant reconfiguration of its US trade exposure. The Supreme Court's early-month invalidation of IEEPA tariffs reduced average US levies on Chinese goods from 32% to approximately 24%, with the Asia-wide weighted average falling from roughly 20% to 17%, per Morgan Stanley estimates. Hong Kong's Hang Seng China Enterprises index rallied 2.65% on the first Monday of the month. The subsequent 10% global Section 122 tariff created a more complex winners-and-losers map, with India — which had been advancing toward an interim deal — postponing its trade mission citing uncertainty over the new framework's legal durability. By month-end, a pending $14 billion US arms package for Taiwan had emerged as a significant latent irritant, and a fresh Section 301 investigation targeting South Korea, India, Taiwan, and others added further trade complexity. Preparatory talks in Paris between Bessent, USTR Greer, and Vice Premier He Lifeng laid the ground for a Trump-Xi summit, originally planned for late March, then postponed to approximately May 14–15.

**China: Resilience With Structural Caveats**

China's position across March was one of relative but qualified insulation from the Iran shock. January–February industrial production rose 6.3% year-on-year — the fastest pace since September — and retail sales and fixed-asset investment both exceeded forecasts. February trade data were striking: exports surged 21.9% year-on-year in the first two months, generating a record $214 billion surplus, though exports to the US fell 11% on tariff effects. Commercial oil inventories reached a record 851 million barrels, and Beijing instructed its largest oil refiners to suspend diesel and gasoline exports as a precautionary measure mid-month. The National People's Congress set the GDP growth target at 4.5%–5% — the lowest since 1991 — with Premier Li characterizing the transition as "formidable." The 15th Five-Year Plan prioritized AI, semiconductors, and advanced manufacturing, with defense spending rising 7%, the slowest since 2022. Officials made their strongest commitment yet to ending deflation in 2026, and Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, and Macquarie projected producer prices could turn positive as early as March — though the key uncertainty is whether this reflects genuine demand recovery or cost-push dynamics. The absence of meaningful household consumption stimulus disappointed investors. FGE NexantECA indicated Beijing is close to drawing on its estimated 1.4 billion barrels of commercial reserves as Hormuz disruption persists.

**China: Markets, Technology, and Geopolitics**

Chinese equity markets were volatile across the month. Mainland investors sold a record HK$27.7 billion of Hong Kong shares in a single session in the second week. In the final week, Alibaba and Tencent combined lost approximately $66 billion in market capitalization over roughly 24 hours after results from both companies disappointed on AI monetization; Alibaba's quarterly net income fell 67%. Simultaneously, LLM providers MiniMax and Zhipu gained approximately 20% and 19% respectively following Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang's endorsement of the OpenClaw agentic AI platform — Nvidia confirmed it is ramping H200 production for Chinese customers following the administration's reversal on export controls. Chinese authorities restricted state enterprises from deploying OpenClaw on office computers citing cybersecurity concerns. A record $112 billion discrepancy between Chinese export figures and US Customs arrival data for 2025 suggests up to a quarter of China-origin shipments may have bypassed tariff controls. ChangXin Memory Technologies reportedly more than doubled revenue to approximately $8 billion in 2025, reflecting continued momentum in China's domestic semiconductor buildout.

**South Korea: Record Rally, Record Volatility**

South Korea provided the month's most dramatic individual market narrative. The Kospi had risen nearly 50% year-to-date entering March, driven by Samsung Electronics up 70% and SK Hynix up 57% on AI infrastructure demand — the total equity market capitalization reached $3.76 trillion, surpassing France to rank ninth globally. That rally reversed violently in the second week: the Kospi fell 7.2% on Tuesday following a long weekend as catch-up selling accelerated, then collapsed a further 12% Wednesday in the index's largest single-day decline in its recorded history. President Lee Jae Myung, abroad in the Philippines, convened an emergency cabinet session and invoked the 100 trillion won market stabilization program; the Kospi rebounded 10% Thursday. Global funds sold a net $11 billion of emerging Asian equities excluding China in that week alone — the largest outflow since March 2022 — with South Korea absorbing $1.6 billion. South Korea's won traded near its lowest since 2009 by month-end, and parliament approved a $350 billion US investment pledge even as Washington simultaneously launched a trade investigation targeting Seoul. Export data for the first 20 days of March showed semiconductor shipments to China up 164%, illustrating the depth of supply-chain integration.

**Japan: Policy Constraints and Import Pressure**

Japan's month was shaped by the intersection of political resistance to BOJ normalization and accelerating import costs. Early in the month, the yen fell approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar after Prime Minister Takaichi expressed apprehension over further rate increases at a meeting with Governor Ueda. Takaichi nominated two reflationist academics to forthcoming BOJ board openings, reinforcing dovish concerns — former Governor Kuroda publicly called for hikes and warned the yen may be "somewhat too weak," a striking inversion of roles. The yen weakened to approximately 159.5 per dollar by the final week, amplifying import cost burdens. Governor Ueda cited Middle East uncertainty as materially clouding the timing of any further adjustment. Japan launched its largest emergency oil stockpile release on record and the Finance Ministry made inquiries about potential intervention in crude oil futures markets. February export data showed shipments to the US falling 8% year-on-year and car exports by value down 14.8%. Toyota reached a landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over Toyota Industries at ¥20,600 per share — a 9.6% premium on a deal valued at approximately ¥6.7 trillion — a meaningful step in Japan's capital efficiency reform agenda.

**India and Southeast Asia**

India's position grew increasingly complex. New Delhi had been reducing Russian oil purchases under US pressure, pivoting toward Gulf supply — precisely the channels most disrupted by the Hormuz closure. Washington's one-month sanctions waiver allowing Indian refiners to resume Russian crude purchases provided material relief, though it simultaneously illustrated India's acute energy exposure. The rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to a record low, with Nomura projecting 96 by end-June; over 330 million households relying on LPG saw delivery times stretch from five to 25 days in some regions. A US submarine attack on the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka mid-month — following the ship's participation in an Indian Navy-hosted fleet review — created a diplomatically fraught situation, forcing Prime Minister Modi to call for "dialog and diplomacy" while facing pointed questions about sovereignty in India's own maritime neighborhood. The Philippines declared a national energy emergency, warned of potential aviation grounding due to jet fuel shortages, and is reportedly considering setting aside South China Sea territorial disputes to explore cooperative oil exploration with Beijing — a geopolitically significant pivot. Southeast Asian governments imposed emergency conservation measures: Thailand ordered civil servants to work from home, the Philippines shifted to a four-day government work week, and Vietnam faces potential airfare increases of up to 70%. What remains unresolved across the region at month-end is whether the Hormuz disruption proves temporary — enabling a return to prior growth trajectories — or persistent enough to accelerate structural energy transitions and force durable geopolitical realignments.
