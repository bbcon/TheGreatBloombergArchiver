Asia

Australian authorities are grappling with a sharp rise in e-bike-related injuries and public safety incidents, reflecting broader tensions between green transport ambitions and the practical challenges of regulating a fast-growing category of micro-mobility devices. The issue gained particular visibility in February when approximately 40 teenage riders on e-bikes disrupted traffic on the Sydney Harbour Bridge, an incident that went viral and intensified political pressure on state and federal governments. Serious injuries involving e-bikes have risen materially over the past two years, with trauma physicians pointing to a "perfect storm" of looser import standards, higher power limits, and surging adoption among younger riders. The regulatory backdrop is instructive. Australia's federal government relaxed import safety standards in 2021, allowing higher-powered models into the market that industry participants describe as closer to motorbikes than bicycles. New South Wales compounded the issue in 2023 by raising the legal power ceiling to 500 watts, putting the state out of alignment with national norms and, critics argue, incentivising further tampering. Both decisions are now being unwound: New South Wales last month restored the 250-watt European standard, and the federal government reintroduced European import standards late in 2025. Minimum rider age requirements, likely in the 12–16 range, are under active consideration. The episode illustrates a regulatory pattern evident across multiple jurisdictions — initial enthusiasm for low-carbon transport alternatives outpacing the governance frameworks needed to manage them safely. Whether Australia's course correction proves sufficient remains to be seen, as enforcement of power limits and age restrictions has historically been difficult in practice.

Global

The dominant cross-cutting theme in this period's coverage is the intersection of the ongoing US-Iran conflict with energy markets and the accelerating, if uneven, transition away from fossil fuels. Oil prices are described as continuing their "wild ride," with supply shock concerns persisting amid the Middle East conflict. The key strategic question being debated among policymakers and investors is whether an extended period of elevated oil prices will catalyse a durable shift toward renewables and alternative transport, or whether governments will default to demand-side subsidies — fuel tax cuts — that relieve near-term cost-of-living pressure while reinforcing hydrocarbon dependency. Spain, South Africa, and Poland are among countries that have implemented or are considering significant fuel tax reductions, a response that advocates warn could strain both road maintenance revenues and long-term decarbonisation trajectories. Climate and energy transition investors appear to be watching the conflict closely for its second-order effects. Commentary from clean energy nonprofit RMI suggests that sustained high oil prices create a "behavioral opening" for switching, and that city-level infrastructure interventions — bus lanes, cycling infrastructure — can be deployed cheaply and quickly enough to demonstrate results before public support erodes. The historical precedent most frequently cited is the 1973 oil crisis, which redirected Dutch and Danish transport planning away from car-centric expansion and toward cycling infrastructure, a shift now widely credited with shaping those cities' modal mix for decades. On the energy technology side, the US AI infrastructure buildout's dependence on Chinese electrical equipment was flagged as a structural vulnerability, adding a further dimension to the broader US-China technology decoupling narrative. Global air quality worsened in 2025, according to separate reporting, a data point that sits in tension with the volume of clean energy investment flowing into the sector and suggests that emissions reductions have yet to translate into measurable atmospheric improvement at scale.
