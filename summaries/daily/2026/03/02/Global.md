The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike marks the most consequential geopolitical rupture since Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and arguably the most direct US targeting of a foreign head of state in the modern era. Oil markets reacted with Brent crude surging approximately 12.5% before partially retracing, trading above $80 per barrel. The Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil exports transit — has effectively closed, with tanker traffic all but halted and more than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs announcing withdrawal of war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels starting Thursday. Saudi Arabia suspended operations at its 550,000 barrel-per-day Ras Tanura refinery following a drone strike, and QatarEnergy shut LNG production at the world's largest export plant. ICE gasoil futures surged more than 20% intraday, the largest gain since March 2022.

The range of oil price scenarios remains wide. A brief conflict and rapid regime transition might return Brent toward $60 per barrel; a full Hormuz disruption could push prices to $125 or beyond; a middle scenario of constrained but continuing flows might see an $80 ceiling. The binding near-term constraint appears to be the insurance market rather than an explicit military blockade, as commercial shipping has self-suspended pending war-risk coverage restoration. OPEC+ announced a modest April production increase on Sunday, but additional volumes are of limited utility if tankers cannot safely transit the strait.

Trump's objectives have remained notably fluid — variously described as taking four to five weeks or ending within days, seeking regime change on a "Venezuela model" or opening negotiations with new Iranian leadership. Iran's own signals are contradictory: President Pezeshkian vowed retaliation while security chief Larijani publicly rejected US talks even as the Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries. The Assembly of Experts must now choose Khamenei's successor, with the choice between a continuity hardliner and a more pragmatic figure carrying profound implications for the conflict's duration and the region's stability. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, whose influence expanded substantially under Khamenei, represents a significant institutional actor unlikely to relinquish leverage without resistance. Markets, for their part, are trading probabilities rather than resolved outcomes — a posture that may persist for some time.
