**ECB Financial Stability Warning**

The ECB's Financial Stability Review struck a cautious tone, noting that despite some recent price adjustments, asset valuations remain elevated relative to historical norms. The central bank identified geopolitical developments — explicitly including the Iran conflict — fiscal trajectories, and macro-financial imbalances as risks that markets appear to be discounting. The Bank of England's Deputy Governor Sarah Breeden offered a similar assessment last month, observing the tension between high asset prices and the breadth of underlying risks. These warnings do not imply imminent policy action, but they signal that European regulators are increasingly attentive to the possibility of a disorderly market correction.

**BP Leadership Turmoil**

The dispute between BP and its ousted chairman Albert Manifold threatens to become a protracted public controversy. Manifold has stated he was dismissed without warning or explanation and intends to challenge the company's version of events, which cited concerns over "governance standards, oversight and conduct." People close to the company have pointed to allegations of aggressive behavior toward employees and mishandling of sensitive information. The episode adds to a period of significant strategic and leadership instability at BP, and the public nature of the disagreement complicates the board's position.

**Swiss Immigration Vote**

The June 14 referendum on capping Switzerland's population at 10 million — requiring a sharp reduction in immigration — has become a source of material concern for businesses operating in the country. Companies including Roche, Google, and Logitech have drawn attention to their dependence on international talent, and Roche's chairman has characterized the initiative as dangerous for both society and the economy. With polls showing the electorate closely divided, the outcome carries genuine uncertainty for Switzerland's position as a hub for global business.
