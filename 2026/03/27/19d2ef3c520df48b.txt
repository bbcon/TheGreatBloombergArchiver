Ruined Spring?

Just weeks ago, this year’s key spring selling season for the US housing 
market looked to be something of an improvement on last year’s, which many 
industry experts had described as “frozen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-06-26/us-housing-market-spring-is-a-bust-with-double-whammy-hit?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
.”

By the last week of February, average 30-year fixed mortgage rates were at 
last back downbelow 6% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/sub-6-mortgage-rates-vanish-as-iran-war-sparks-inflation-fears?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 for the first time since 2022, back when post-Covid inflation was raging. 
Relatedly, an index of affordability from the National Association of Realtors 
capped an eighth straight monthly advance — taking it to the highest level 
since, again, 2022.

But now, the outlook has been upended by the US-Israeli war on Iran, just as 
the spring season kicks off.

Mortgage rates are back on the climb 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/mortgage-rates-jump-to-6-38-as-war-rattles-housing-market?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
, with 30-year rates now at a six-month high of 6.4%. That’s a reflection of 
the selloff in US Treasuries that serve as a benchmark for long-term borrowing 
costs, due in turn to investors ratcheting up their expectations for inflation 
thanks to skyrocketing energy prices.

At a JPMorgan Chase conference last week, homebuilders warned that conditions 
were “somewhat inconsistent and fragile.”

Indeed, while mortgage rates were falling before the Iran conflict, some of 
the housing indicators were far from healthy. Sales ofnew homes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/us-new-home-sales-fell-to-the-lowest-since-2022-in-january?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 in January hit the lowest level since 2022, though poor weather played some 
role.

Going forward, things may evolve quickly. One top builder, KB Home, earlier 
this week lowered its full-year guidance, citing the war as a contributing 
factor. “We saw pretty good sales results the first week of March,” CEO Robert 
McGibney said. “But the last couple of weeks have been a little softer than we 
would like to see or what we normally get this time of year.”

Besides rising mortgage rates, housing experts are also eyeing the job market, 
which is critical for underlying demand. The92,000 plunge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/us-unexpectedly-sheds-92-000-jobs-unemployment-rate-rises?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 in February payrolls wasn’t encouraging on that score. A retreat in equities, 
which have dropped on growth concerns, is another source of angst.

“The conflict poses downside risks to the labor market and the broader economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-27/why-today-s-ai-boom-won-t-repeat-the-1990s-economy?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 through weaker demand stemming from inflation and erosion of household 
wealth,” UBS analysts Tainan Costa and Ana Julia Zerkowski wrote in a note 
Wednesday.

Need-to-Know Research

Most of the discussion in financial markets about the impact of the Iran war 
has focused on the global effects of the plunge in energy supply — but what 
about the implications for theGulf nations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/iran-war-trauma-of-middle-east-conflict-will-reshape-gulf-arab-states?cmpid=BBD032726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260327>
 themselves? Standard Chartered economists indicated Thursday they may prove 
resilient.

“We expect the economic impact to remain contained overall,” Bader Al Sarraf 
and Razia Khan wrote in a note. Gulf Cooperation Council nations’ massive 
currency reserves and sovereign wealth assets offer “a substantial buffer 
against domestic and external shocks, helping to underpin longer-term 
macroeconomic stability.” There will be divergences, they noted.

In general, the longer the disruption goes, the price effects on energy 
exports will increasingly offset volume losses when it comes to budgets, the 
duo said — “particularly for crude exporters with sufficient export 
flexibility,” such as Saudi Arabia. While prolonged turmoil “would have a 
material impact,” recovery would still be “in sight,” thanks in part to the 
region having diversified beyond oil over time. That’s helped created “stronger 
domestic demand dynamics,” they said.
