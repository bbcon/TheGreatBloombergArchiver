**Strait of Hormuz: Deal or Delay?**
The sequence of events over the past 48 hours illustrates the gap between headline optimism and operational reality in the Iran negotiations. Trump's assertion that talks are "proceeding nicely" was followed within hours by US Central Command strikes on Iranian missile launch sites and vessels attempting to lay mines — characterized by US authorities as defensive. Brent, which fell sharply Monday on thin holiday volumes, recovered to approach $99.50 a barrel. Prediction market odds of a swift return to normal in Hormuz declined on the strike reports, though financial markets appeared to treat the incidents as tactical rather than strategic, with equity futures and bond markets broadly holding Monday's moves. Republican hawks, including Senator Lindsey Graham, have argued the emerging terms concede too much to Tehran. Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei, meanwhile, declared that US military bases in the region will no longer be safe — language that complicates any clean resolution. Rubio acknowledged talks could take several more days.

**China's Mediation Role and Geopolitical Architecture**
Beijing's positioning in the Iran talks merits attention beyond the immediate deal mechanics. President Xi met Pakistani Army Chief Munir in Beijing, thanking him for his mediation efforts, while an Iranian delegation traveled to Doha for consultations that included discussion of frozen Iranian funds. The reported possibility of Iran's highly enriched uranium being transferred to China — however uncertain — would represent an unusual expansion of Beijing's direct involvement in Middle Eastern security arrangements. China has strong economic incentives: its export-dependent economy is sensitive to both demand destruction abroad and elevated input costs from high oil prices. The episode is consistent with a broader pattern of Beijing seeking to shape outcomes in regional conflicts where its economic interests are materially affected, without assuming visible diplomatic leadership.
