Warsh at Sintra

A fortnight after his first appearance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-18/warsh-establishes-inflation-credibility-now-sets-up-fed-regime-change-later?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 before the press as Federal Reserve chairman, Kevin Warsh on Wednesday marked 
his debut in the role on the global stage, at the European Central Bank’s 
version of the Fed’s annual Jackson Hole event — the Forum on Central Banking.

Economists, investors and reporters were likely fully aligned in expecting 
Warsh to repeat that he wasn’t going to provide “forward guidance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/warsh-says-fed-charting-new-course-repeats-no-forward-guidance?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
” on monetary policy.

What they perhaps weren’t anticipating was that the first mention of the term 
in the main panel discussion in Sintra, Portugal, was from ECB President
Christine Lagarde 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/lagarde-says-inflation-growth-risks-more-broadly-balanced-now?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
. “If I have one regret, it’s to have felt bound and compelled by forward 
guidance,” she said.

It sounded like something of an overture from, effectively, the dean among 
heads of the world’s main central banks. Last year, Lagarde heaped praise on 
Warsh’s predecessor, Jerome Powell, who received astanding ovation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-07-02/fed-s-powell-gets-love-bombed-in-europe-after-trump-s-taunts?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 from participants on the opening evening.

Warsh quickly reciprocated the positive vibe. “I liked her when we met 20 
years ago when she was finance minister” of France, he said. “After that 
answer, I love her.” He went on to claim that many attendees at the confab were 
“keen to think anew about the conduct of monetary policy.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-07-01/fed-chairman-warsh-says-inflation-risks-have-come-down-video?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
Whether true or not, both Warsh and his counterparts have incentives to appear 
to be on the same page.

On the new Fed chief’s part, making the argument that other central banks are 
doing some fresh thinking would add to the appearance of legitimacy of his own
re-examination 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-23/warsh-s-fed-regime-change-may-require-patience-consensus?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 of Fed policymaking. From the ECB’s perspective, an offering of overseas 
support for Donald Trump’s Fed pick could at the margin provide some 
encouragement to resist any presidential pressure to slash rates.

Regardless of the intention, the bonhomie was clear. When the moderator asked 
Warsh about where he wanted to take the Fed’sbalance sheet 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/warsh-signals-long-road-ahead-for-a-smaller-fed-balance-sheet?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
, Lagarde jumped in for him to say, “No forward guidance!” — which Warsh then 
echoed.

Turning to the five task forces he unveiled last month, Warsh advised 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/fed-s-warsh-picks-senior-bessent-staffer-to-serve-as-adviser?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 that news is likely coming next week on membership of those panels, which will 
include non-Americans. “Sometimes we need a foreigner to sort of see things 
clearly,” he said. Bloombergreported 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/warsh-taps-ex-boe-chief-king-for-new-communications-task-force?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 Warsh has tapped former Bank of England Governor Mervyn King to co-lead the 
communications panel — adding to the “nothing outrageous going on here” aura.
Mervyn King, former governor of the Bank of England, speaks during a Bloomberg 
Television interview in New York in 2017.Photographer: Christopher 
Goodney/Bloomberg
Warsh then offered that the task forces, which also cover data, jobs and 
productivity, the balance sheet and frameworks for inflation, could provide 
lessons for other central banks, too. But Lagarde jumped in to say “at the ECB, 
we went through the same process” already — perhaps to keep the rookie from 
getting ahead of himself.

Where might the new Fed chief want to take things 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/newsletters/2026-07-02/warsh-must-beware-of-yield-curves-flattening-to-deceive?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
? Andrew Sacher, who worked with him at the White House National Economic 
Council in the early 2000s, highlights that Warsh “clearly is more optimistic 
than the other central bankers about AI’s productivity-enhancing effects.”

Warsh said he was confident that, while the AI investment boom is spurring 
demand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-11/us-inflation-driven-partly-by-ai-boom-fueling-economy-stocks?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 now, “I’m confident we’re going to see the supply at some point.” He also 
speculated that in six months’ time, business surveys will be showcasing the 
importance of AI-driven productivity.

Sacher, now senior US economist at Bloomberg Economics, says “it was the first 
time Warsh was so forthright on AI’s positive productivity impact since he 
became chairman.” The Fed chief also tempered his message on inflation from 
last month, this time saying thatprice risks had retreated 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-01/warsh-says-fed-charting-new-course-repeats-no-forward-guidance?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 in recent weeks.

The bottom line for Bloomberg Economics: Warsh’s remarks underline their 
expectation for the Fed to keep interest rates steady in 2026 and cut in 2027.

Need-to-Know Research

Politicians on both sides of the Atlantic are revisiting the banking 
regulations put in place since the 2008 financial crisis, arguing a cleanup is 
required to make the industry more competitive. While the USloosens rules 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/us-twist-on-new-bank-trading-rules-fuels-parity-demands-in-eu?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 for its biggest lenders, themantra in Europe 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-04/eu-delays-bank-trading-book-rules-to-blunt-wall-street-advantage?cmpid=BBD070226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260702&utm_content=5954>
 has been “simplification without deregulation.”

But is it that simple? One of the papers 
<https://www.ecb.europa.eu/pub/pdf/sintra/ecb.forumcentbankpub2026_Giannetti_paper.en.pdf>
 at Sintra argued officials should think twice before sweeping rules aside. 
“Stringency” and “complexity” have proved the most effective attributes in 
lowering financial system risks since 1990, Cameron Ellis, Mariassunta 
Giannetti, and Chotibhak Jotikasthira wrote. Regulatory tightening without 
added complexity led to smaller and more transient reductions of risks, they 
said.
The National Palace of Sintra, Portugal.Photographer: Jose Sarmento 
Matos/Bloomberg
“Complexity is, on this evidence, not unnecessary regulatory baggage,” the 
paper argued. That’s because it seems to make stringent regulation harder to 
circumvent. Europe’s push to simplify its laws may still be “defensible,” but 
only if policymakers manage to target the right kind of regulatory excess. 
There’s a risk their effort does “not distinguish complexity that supports 
binding constraints from complexity that is mere residue,” the authors wrote.
