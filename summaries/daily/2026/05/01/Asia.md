Japan intervened in foreign-exchange markets for the first time since 2024, purchasing yen and selling dollars after issuing a "final" warning to investors. The move triggered an intraday strengthening of as much as 0.7%, with dollar-yen reaching 155.5 before partially retracing to around 156.5. Analysts at Rabobank cautioned that intervention can stabilize markets only briefly, noting that Japan's underlying structural dependence on imported energy — now made substantially more expensive by the Strait of Hormuz disruption — continues to exert downward pressure on the currency. The Bank of Japan's recent policy meeting produced a split vote, with dissenting members favoring an outright rate hike, raising the probability of a June move. Japan's top currency official also indicated readiness to intervene in crude oil futures markets, where speculative positioning has been amplifying yen weakness, an unusual extension of currency-management posture into commodity markets.

South Korea's export data for April showed continued strength, with semiconductor demand anchoring growth despite the mounting cost pressures associated with elevated energy prices. The resilience of Korean tech exports provides some offset to the broader regional drag from the Iran conflict.

In China, an IMF official noted nascent signs of inflation returning as energy costs rise, though the official cautioned that more durable price gains are needed to fully reverse entrenched deflationary pressures. China's upscale automotive brands, including BYD's luxury segment, face pointed criticism for pricing strategies in European markets — charging multiples of domestic prices — that appear poorly calibrated for competitive penetration. Taiwan's main opposition leader, Kuomintang chair Cheng Li-wun, sought a meeting with President Trump during a planned US visit, positioning herself as a potential channel for dialogue with Beijing — a rare move that risks complicating cross-strait dynamics.

India's crisis deserves separate attention: every one of the world's 50 hottest cities this week was located in India, with temperatures exceeding 40°C across densely populated states. The combined pressure of an unusually severe pre-monsoon heatwave and energy supply disruptions linked to the Iran war is straining power grids, with the McKinsey Global Institute estimating heat-related productivity losses could reach $250 billion. Monsoon relief is not expected until June.
