Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
power reporter Naureen Malik reports from CERAWeek on the impact of the Middle 
East war on the data-center industry and its plans for expansion.

US tech giants have flocked to the Persian Gulf in recent years to take 
advantage of the region’s ample cheap energy and real estate.

But drone strikes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/how-amazon-data-centers-became-a-casualty-of-iran-war?cmpid=BBBXT032626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
 on data centers in countries including Iran, the United Arab Emirates and 
Bahrain are starting to force a rethink on where they locate their critical 
infrastructure.

Executives at the CERAWeek by S&P Global <https://www.ceraweek.com/en> 
conference in Houston broached the issue this week, and suggested that 
persistent volatility in the Middle East may drive new data-hub projects 
elsewhere, including to the US.

“If your plan was to put all your data centers in the Gulf states, recent 
events might suggest that that’s not a great long-term secure strategy,” said
Larry Coben <https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/3897961>, the outgoing 
boss of Texas-based power producerNRG Energy Inc 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/0812320D:US?cmpid=BBBXT032626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
.
A model of the largest data center in the UAE under construction in Abu Dhabi.
Photographer: GIUSEPPE CACACE/AFP via Getty Images
On the face of it, the prospective shift sounds like good news for power 
companies in America. In sharp contrast to much of the world, US natural gas 
prices remain stable, potentially aiding efforts to attract new energy-hungry 
data hubs.

Indeed, more gas-fired generation is already under construction to meet 
surging demand.

But while data centers offer the biggest opportunity for the US power industry 
in decades, they also pose the biggest threat.

The country’s grids are already under severe stress from age and extreme 
weather. Now, network operators are scrambling to line up extra capacity to 
meet soaring consumption from tech hubs, all while facing government demands to
keep energy affordable 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/soaring-power-prices-have-us-politicians-searching-for-solutions?cmpid=BBBXT032626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
.

As far as the Gulf investment case is concerned, much may depend on the 
duration of the current war, its outcomes and the continued efforts of 
governments there to lure big projects.

The industry there may still flourish, but computing capacity has clearly 
become a legitimate wartime target. Server farms are viewed as potential nodes 
of state power, with defense technology increasingly relying on AI for 
surveillance and drone navigation.

Risk assessments will likely spur tech companies to beef up security and 
resilience measures, and spread their investments across multiple 
jurisdictions. That may see the US, which already has the highest number of 
data centers in the world, become a destination for further growth.

—Naureen Malik, Bloomberg News
