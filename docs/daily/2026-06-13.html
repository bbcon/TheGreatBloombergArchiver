AI Investment Cycle

The scale of US hyperscaler capital expenditure has become a defining macro variable of 2026. Morgan Stanley's estimate of $805 billion for the year — rising to $1.12 trillion in 2027 — represents a structural demand shock that is reverberating through global supply chains, most acutely in advanced semiconductor and hardware manufacturing concentrated in East Asia. Whether this investment ultimately delivers the productivity acceleration some economists anticipate remains uncertain, but its near-term effects on trade flows and corporate earnings are already measurable. Notably, demand appears to be outpacing even these elevated supply estimates, prompting the Bank of England Governor to raise the prospect of AI capacity rationing.

Capital Flows

A consequential second-order effect is emerging in cross-border capital flows. Asian current-account surpluses of historic magnitude — driven by AI-related export revenues — are being recycled back into the US, increasingly as direct investment in manufacturing rather than Treasury purchases. Japan and South Korea have pledged a combined $900 billion in US investment commitments, partly in response to tariff pressure from the Trump administration. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's AI-related exports are on track to reach 30% of GDP in 2026, with Goldman Sachs forecasting a current-account surplus exceeding 10% of GDP — a record — and Bank of America raising its surplus estimate to 15% of GDP ($289 billion).
  • Taiwan raised its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 9.6%, supported by the largest export surge in 50 years, driven by TSMC's advanced semiconductor output.
  • Indonesia rate hike expectations are mounting amid market turmoil, adding a divergent policy signal within the region.

AI Super Surplus

Goldman Sachs economists have coined the term "AI super surplus" to describe the outsized current-account positions accumulating in South Korea and Taiwan as a direct result of US hyperscaler demand. South Korea recently recorded its largest export increase since the 1980s, led by chips and IT products; the Bank of Korea responded by raising its 2026 growth forecast to 2.6% from 2.0%. Taiwan's position is more acute: GDP growth of 9.6% would represent an extraordinary acceleration, underpinned by TSMC's dominance in advanced packaging — the majority of which is reportedly reserved for Nvidia. SK Hynix has sold out its entire 2026 high-bandwidth memory production, in large part due to Nvidia procurement.

China Constraints

China presents a notable contrast. US export controls on high-end semiconductors are, according to Gavekal analyst Tilly Zhang, increasingly binding as AI investment scales exponentially. American companies are effectively locking up advanced capacity — TSMC packaging, HBM output — leaving Chinese firms further down waiting lists. The result is that China's own AI ambitions, a stated national priority under President Xi, are generating meaningfully less stimulus for the broader regional and global economy than the US-driven cycle. ---

Global

  • Morgan Stanley estimates global AI capex at $805 billion in 2026, rising to $1.12 trillion in 2027 — demand that appears likely to exceed available supply capacity.

Energy and Geopolitics

The US-Israel conflict with Iran remains the primary geopolitical risk variable shaping global macro conditions, with energy price implications particularly acute for oil-importing Asian economies. The World Bank's assessment — that two-thirds of global economies face economic damage — reflects the breadth of the shock's transmission through energy costs, trade finance, and confidence channels. The full economic magnitude remains uncertain and will depend on conflict duration and Strait of Hormuz disruption severity.

AI as a Global Macro Variable

The AI investment cycle has graduated from a sector-level theme to a macroeconomic force. Its effects are now visible in national GDP forecasts, current-account balances, capital flow patterns, and central bank communications across multiple regions simultaneously. The concentration of supply capacity in Taiwan and South Korea, combined with US export controls limiting Chinese access, is producing a lopsided geographic distribution of economic benefit — one that raises policy questions around surplus recycling, domestic investment absorption, and the potential hollowing out of manufacturing in capital-exporting Asian economies.
