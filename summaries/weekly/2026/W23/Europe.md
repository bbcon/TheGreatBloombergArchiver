• Eurozone CPI rose above 3% YoY on Tuesday for the first time in more than two and a half years, beating prior readings and cementing expectations for an ECB rate hike at the June meeting.
• Eurozone Q1 GDP was revised on Friday to show a contraction — missing prior estimates of flat growth — principally due to a sharp decline in Irish national accounts, though the result did not appear to alter the ECB's tightening trajectory.
• UniCredit secured commitments taking its Commerzbank stake above 30% during the week, adding significant momentum to its €38.6 billion acquisition bid.
• Andy Burnham formally confirmed he will challenge Keir Starmer for the Labour leadership, introducing a degree of governing uncertainty into UK politics.

**ECB Policy and Inflation**
The breach of 3% eurozone CPI on Tuesday is the most consequential European data point of the week. The acceleration — driven in meaningful part by energy costs linked to the Strait of Hormuz disruption — moves the ECB into territory where further accommodation is difficult to justify. Executive Board member Isabel Schnabel's earlier statement that the bank can no longer "look through" war-driven inflationary pressures had signaled the rhetorical shift; Tuesday's print confirmed the data are moving in the same direction. Market pricing shifted decisively toward a rate hike at next week's meeting, with two additional increases priced for 2026. The growth revision announced Friday — eurozone Q1 GDP now showing a contraction rather than stagnation — is principally attributable to Irish national accounts distorted by multinational profit-shifting, a known measurement issue, and analysts do not believe it reflects genuine underlying weakness at the regional level. The tension between a contracting growth print and a tightening bias nonetheless warrants monitoring.

**Banking and Corporate News**
UniCredit crossing the 30% ownership threshold in Commerzbank during the week effectively triggers a formal takeover offer process under German law and increases pressure on Commerzbank's board and regulators to engage substantively with the €38.6 billion bid. Cross-border European bank mergers have historically encountered political resistance in Germany, and the path to completion remains genuinely uncertain, but the momentum shift is real. Separately, Akzo Nobel fell 19% in Amsterdam after Nippon Paint and Sherwin-Williams abandoned acquisition efforts simultaneously — among the sharpest single-session moves in European equities this week. Inditex gained 5.7% in Madrid on stronger second-quarter sales momentum, offering evidence that consumer spending in key European markets retains some resilience despite the inflationary backdrop. UBS raised $1.5 billion in Additional Tier 1 capital — its first such issuance since Switzerland paused plans to tighten AT1 rules — signaling a selective reopening of bank capital markets.

**UK Political Economy**
UK political risk accumulated through the week. Andy Burnham's formal entry into the Labour leadership contest introduces governing uncertainty, though markets have not yet priced a meaningful risk premium into sterling or gilts. The gilts market is on track for its third annual loss in five years; financial institutions are advocating for a retail distribution push, citing the gap between the 4%–5.5% yield on government bonds and the 1.7% average savings rate, with pension funds reducing gilt exposure and increasing reliance on overseas buyers and hedge funds. Survey evidence points to UK firms planning 4% price increases over the next year, and large retailers have shed nearly 18,000 jobs over the past 12 months — consistent with the impact of higher employer National Insurance contributions. UK house prices fell at their fastest pace in nearly a year, compounding the political headwinds facing the Starmer government alongside leaked diplomatic exchanges involving former US ambassador Peter Mandelson.
