China Shakes the World

“Let China sleep <https://bloom.bg/4fNfch0>, for when she wakes, she will 
shake the world.” So, allegedly, said Napoleon — although it looks likeone of 
those quotes never actually uttered <https://bloom.bg/3TaDhWp> by the famous 
person to whom it was attributed. It doesn’t much matter, as whoever said it 
was right. China Shakes the World <https://bloom.bg/4hr2Ufp> became the title 
of a book by James Kynge explaining how the country pulled off its first great 
economic shock, as millions of rural people poured into the cities to work in 
the huge new manufacturing industries.

That shock meant lower prices for everyone else, but also contributed to the 
ills of inequality and an increasingly K-shaped economy that have dogged the 
West for a generation. China no longer dominates attention in the same way, but 
it may now be shaking the world again, this time from a profoundly different 
position.

One impact came this week when the so-called “national team” 
<https://bloom.bg/4ftMG2u> — a group of investment organizations beholden to 
the government — started buying shares in bulk, particularly in tech.

Like chipmakers in South Korea and Taiwan, Chinese tech groups had been on a 
volatile ride culminating in a 30% slide in July. Once the national team 
started buying, the rally was instant. The CSI-300 tech sector soared 9% on 
Tuesday for its best day since Sept. 27, 2024, when the leadership made a much 
ballyhooed“whatever-it-takes” <https://bloom.bg/4pwJvvG> statement of intent to 
stimulate the economy.

This latest action fits into a new and slightly subtler plan for the state to 
intervene selectively in sectors that can boost China’s competitiveness. It’s a 
significant change from the huge investments in infrastructure that drove the 
first shock, and in line with a marked shift of emphasis in thelatest Five-Year 
Plan <https://bloom.bg/3Tf2lvm>. Led in previous iterations by attempts to 
build demand, this analysis from Alpine Macro and Oxford Economics shows that 
China intends to concentrate on turning itself into a technological powerhouse 
by 2030:

Anemic Economy and Stock Market

The new approach might be needed to rescue a stock market that’s been in 
serious decline. Chinese stocks rallied sharply compared to the rest of the 
emerging world in 2020 as it was able to reopen swiftly after the pandemic 
broke out in Wuhan. But after that came Xi Jinping’s crackdown on the private 
sector, most famously intervening to stop the IPO of Ant Group, prompting fund 
managers to label the country “uninvestable.” Thedraconian 
<https://bloom.bg/4hr2Qwb>  Zero-Covid <https://bloom.bg/3Rx0NML> lockdown 
policy to check the omicron variant did further damage.

Stocks rallied after the leadership unveiled stimulus measures nearly two 
years ago, and gained again after the startling DeepSeek large language model 
prompted a rethink of whether the US was really dominant in artificial 
intelligence. But since then, underperformance has continued:

This is partly because of how well the big chipmakers in Taiwan and South 
Korea have done, but it can’t be attributed to valuations. The country is now 
trading at a slight premium to the rest of EM:

Rather, the problem is that the domestic economy is anemic and over-reliant on 
AI to drive specific exports. As Larry Hu of Macquarie points out, China’s 
shipments of products needed for AI infrastructure have surged:

Once again, western demand for China’s manufactures remains strong. Hu argues 
that this is exacerbating inequality:

Strong exports have made China’s two-speed economy even more pronounced. It's 
because Beijing follows the “Just Enough” rule: doing just enough to meet the 
growth target. As the strong track (exports) gets stronger, the weak track 
(consumption) has to get weaker.

Yan Wang of Alpine Macro agrees, saying that the two-speed economy is a 
“deliberate policy choice,” as Beijing now views technology as the key to 
lifting productivity and improving living standards.

This year, growth has been further hampered by the war in Iran. China has 
reduced oil imports <https://bloom.bg/4pCdVNa> massively to weather the 
situation, making itself what Hu calls the “swing factor” in global oil prices. 
Imports are now back to where they were in 2016:

Some of this has come from tapping inventories, while reduced demand for 
travel has brought down retail fuel sales by 10% by volume this year. But Hu 
points out that 60% of the second-quarter slowdown in industrial production was 
concentrated in the oil supply chain. China has an interest in bringing the 
conflict to an end.

The Innovation Shock

Against this difficult backdrop, the evidence grows stronger that China is 
creating what Wang calls a “parallel tech universe” that can compete with the 
US on AI and other advanced technologies. He points out that China launched an 
orbital booster recovery rocket earlier this month, only the second country to 
achieve this.ChangXin Memory Technologies <https://bloom.bg/3Rng2I7> last week 
raised almost $10 billion in a Shanghai IPO, the biggest mainland floatation 
since 2010. And crucially, the startup Moonshot AI has launched theKimi K3 model
 <https://bloom.bg/4wiim26>, which shocked established US competitors with its 
quality.

This confirms a trend. The amount of US traffic directed to Chinese models has 
now almost caught their American rivals. The turnaround has happened inside 18 
months, as this Alpine Macro chart shows. At the start of 2025, AI was almost 
synonymous with OpenAI and ChatGPT in the way that Google stands for internet 
search. No longer:

A big reason, naturally, is price. Chinese models are far cheaper than the 
offerings from US champion Anthropic PBC:

But they needed to reach a certain level of quality before their low prices 
could help them. As this Stanford survey suggests, US models are still slightly 
superior, but the gap has almost been closed:

This shakes the notion of US superiority, but doesn’t necessarily inhibit the 
waves of money flowing into building out American data centers. Bank of 
America’s equity researchers suggest that cheap prices are more about a choice 
of business model for the Chinese. They also expand the demand for memory chips 
and computing capacity.

A rival ecosystem is developing that has generally been achieved with far less 
intense use of capital. The counterpoint is stark for the US, where Google 
owner Alphabet Inc. beat second-quarter expectations but then saw its shares 
sell off sharply once it announced that it intended toincrease capital 
expenditures <https://bloom.bg/4pyQSTx> still further:

The irony is that China and the US appear to be changing places 
<https://bloom.bg/4wB8VLD>. Louis-Vincent Gave of Gavekal says:

When it comes to AI capital spending, China has turned out to be far less 
Chinese than one might have anticipated. For an obvious reason: Because of US 
sanctions on high-end semiconductors, Chinese companies did not have the option 
to throw vast amounts of money in a bid to become the biggest AI provider 
(otherwise they most likely would have!). Instead, Chinese companies had to 
find workarounds, whether through open-sourcing software, stacking less 
powerful and cheaper local chips, investing more in China’s electricity grid…

US corporates are throwing capital around Chinese-style. DeepSeek and now 
Moonshot suggest that the new, capital-disciplined model might be working 
pretty well.

One Chokepoint to Another

If you need a primer on the Middle East, we can provide a two-minute video 
<https://bloom.bg/3TbKEwM> explaining the significance of Iran’s attempt to add 
a threat to blockade the Bab el-Mandeb passage to the Red Sea to its ongoing 
campaign to halt traffic in the Strait of Hormuz:
 <https://bloom.bg/3TbKEwM> 
Mandeb is less important for oil, but instead threatens to hit containerized 
shipping hard because it controls access to Suez. If Iran’s Houthi allies in 
Yemen can close it, then shipping between Europe and Asia will have to go 
around the entire African continent, and globalization takes another serious 
knock.

How serious is such a risk? The two chokepoints are about the same size, and 
recent history shows that the Houthis can exert quite an effect. In the early 
days of the war in Gaza, in late 2023, their attacks on shipping drove a fall 
of more than 50% in the container vessels crossing the waterway. The plunge was 
comparable to Iran’s campaign against tankers through the Persian Gulf 
<https://bloom.bg/4fgkMIH>:

Polymarket bettors now put the odds that Mandeb is effectively closed by the 
end of this year at 33%, so the Houthi scenario is still far from a base case. 
But it’s a growing risk that has put upward pressure on oil prices. The average 
price of a gallon at the pump in the US is now back up to $4.06, having 
bottomed at $3.78 two weeks ago. Gasoline futures have rebounded 19%. Best to 
pay attention.



 Survival Tips 



In baseball, as in markets, I refuse to be fooled by randomness 
<https://bloom.bg/4vIM6UI>. The Red Sox have just matched their franchise 
record (set in 1946 by a team that included Ted Williams) by winning15 games in 
a row <https://bloom.bg/4vP0jjh>. The Orioles have now ended the streak, so I 
get to offer up songs byTaylor Swift <https://bloom.bg/4ftvjPn> rather than 
Ringo Starr <https://bloom.bg/4wimmzN>. So many consecutive wins is extremely 
implausible; it’s only been done 23 times in the history of major league 
baseball. This one has rested on all kinds ofentertaining flukes and comebacks 
<https://bloom.bg/4pzhJ1D>. But I’m not sure I’m that excited. The Red Sox 
don’t have anyone half as good asWilliams <https://bloom.bg/4pxImE6>, and they 
are at present only three games above .500. It’s been fun, but some consistency 
and excellence of the kind that leads to a championship would be a better. 
Streaks are a distraction. Sorry if I’m a curmudgeon; does anyone have any 
great and meaningful streaks to offer in reply?
