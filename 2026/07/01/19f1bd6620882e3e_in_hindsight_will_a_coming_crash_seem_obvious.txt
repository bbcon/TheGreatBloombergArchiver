Premature Hindsight

The first half of 2026 is in the books and it’s been glorious. So much so, 
indeed, that we need an early look at the trades that have made money for
Hindsight Capital LLC <https://bloom.bg/4gh2O9N>, my imaginary hedge fund which 
invests with the benefit of perfect knowledge of how prices are going to move 
in advance. Normally, I only go through this exercise at the end of the year, 
but for a couple of reasons I think it’s worth taking a snapshot now.

First, the cross-currents from artificial intelligence investment 
<https://bloom.bg/447fwR9> and the energy shock <https://bloom.bg/4vJknEq> have 
delivered spectacular profits for those who were in the right place. A lot of 
very straightforward macro trades have more than doubled already. Second, 
there’s an active debate about whether there are bubbles ready to burst. It’s 
possible that with the benefit of another six months’ hindsight, we’ll think it 
was always obvious that a crash was coming, because these trades were always 
unsustainable. Is that true?

The 2008 Precedent

At the outset, it’s worth setting out the classic example of an unsustainable 
trade, fromthe crisis year of 2008 <https://bloom.bg/4axLqdb>. In the first 
half of that year, as credit markets steadily collapsed, the way to profit was 
to pile into real assets,particularly oil <https://bloom.bg/4gg98hL>. A trade 
of shorting US banks and putting the proceeds into oil would have more than 
doubled your money.

But in early July, the trade collapsed under its own weight. If you were 
betting on the banking system to implode, people began to grasp, then there was 
no way there could be enough demand for oil to support record crude prices. The 
result was a spectacular reversal as oil crashed. By the end of 2008, the main 
US bank index had actually outperformed oil for the year:

That is the template. Will hindsight group what is happening now with the 
extraordinary events from January to June 2008?

Hindsight: The AI Trades

The biggest gains have gone to those who poured into chip stocks while 
shorting software. This was on the basis that AI would create ever greater 
demand for semiconductors, while destroying the business model for software 
groups. All of these trades were perfectly visible six months ago, but it 
turned out that there was much more life in them. Over the last six months, a 
trade of shorting the S&P 1500 software index and buying the SOX semiconductors 
index would have returned 150%:

That is just in the US stocks. South Korea, dominated by the huge chipmakers 
Samsung Electronics Co. andSK Hynix Inc. <https://bloom.bg/4gg709U>, is an even 
more extreme example. The Kospi index has almost doubled for the year. A 
Hindsight Capital trade coupling the Kospi with selling short the Jakarta 
Composite index, to take advantage of investors’ collapse of confidence in the
administration of President Prabowo Subianto <https://bloom.bg/4vFykmL>, would 
have trebled your money in six months:

The AI trade has also driven a counterintuitive eclipse for the Chinese stock 
market. China is engaged in a determined effort to compete with the US in 
producing AI models. That could yet have major strategic and geopolitical 
effects, but for now it means that Chinese companies are spending a lot of 
money, generally outside the country (particularly with the big chipmakers 
elsewhere in the emerging world), while any positive effects are yet to feed 
through to boosting domestic demand.

MSCI’s China index has had a very difficult six months, and a trade of 
shorting it and putting money in the rest of EM would have made a profit of 
more than 60%:

Hindsight: The Hard Money Trades

Beyond the Hindsight trades most directly tied to AI, there was also money to 
be made out of a huge swing in sentiment around hard assets. The year started 
with precious metals hitting records. Bitcoin — which we now know helped 
President Donald Trump and his family make some $1.2 billion from crypto 
businesses during 2025 — had hit its own all-time high in October. Both were 
ready to turn.

The war in Iran convinced investors that oil was a more reliable hard asset. 
It also revived concerns about inflation, and dampened fears that central banks 
would respond with excessive dovishness. ThenKevin Warsh arrived at the Federal 
Reserve <https://bloom.bg/4ePs2d0> and announced himself as quite a hawk — 
certainly very different from the obedient doves who had been in the frame for 
the chairmanship last year. Put this all together, and the“debasement trade” 
<https://bloom.bg/4atoko2> of buying assets that would benefit from inflation 
and central bank fecklessnes was tanked.

A trade of buying Brent crude (up 19.8% for the year even as peace talks 
continued) and shorting Bitcoin (down 33.3%) would have made Hindsight Capital 
79.4%:

More aggressively, a trade of buying US gasoline futures — whose price has 
stayed far more buoyant than crude oil and gained 76.7% during the half — while 
shorting silver (down 18.3%) has been quite spectacular, making 116%:

The surface similarities of all of these trades to the long oil/short banks 
gambit in 2008 are evident. None of them can continue indefinitely without 
collapsing under their own economic logic, and as in 2008, there are plenty of 
people sitting on paper profits who will be anxious to bank them. But that 
doesn’t prove that the trades will go the same way, and it certainly doesn’t 
help with timing.

Economic Logic

Investment bubbles sow the seeds of their own destruction if they attract more 
capital than they can productively use, and starve other areas that need it. 
That point might be approaching. Arthur Budaghyan of BCA Research argues that 
capital expenditures are overshooting. In the US, he points out, capex on 
everything other than tech is actually falling:

That might suggest that the AI boom is leading to malinvestment. Budaghyan 
compares tech capex’s share of overall gross domestic product with previous 
extreme doses of speculation. Famous housing bubbles burst when residential 
investment reached 7% of GDP in the US, 9% in Japan, and 8% in Spain. In all 
cases, these numbers halved swiftly once the bubble burst. US tech-related 
capital expenditures peaked at 5% of GDP in nominal terms in 2000 and are now 
slightly higher. In real terms, this number is now above 7%:

A similar exercise looking at US imports similarly finds that while tech 
products are still increasing (helping to explain why Korean stocks are 
performing so well), all other imports are in significant decline. The advent 
of tariffs and the attempt to retreat from globalization evidently have 
something to do with this, and the numbers are skewed by importers’ attempt to 
front-run the new levies in the first half of last year. But it still looks as 
though the money going into data centers is increasingly coming at the expense 
of everything else:

None of this proves that the market will sell off forthwith. But it does look 
as though AI is beginning to suck in capital that others cannot spare. 
Budaghyan’s argument that the current trends only have a matter of months to go 
looks reasonable.

Speculative Excesses

Market technicals offer the other way to gauge whether a bubble is ready to 
burst. If there are signs of extreme speculation, it’s time to consider taking 
evasive action even if the earnings undergirding it are phenomenal (which they 
are). As Points of Return has covered, big IPOs have a strong historical 
tendency to come at a major market top for the sectors involved.

As a general rule of thumb, the more a market is reliant on retail investors 
to keep going, the greater the risk that a bubble will burst. And Budaghyan 
points out that the amazing advance in Korea has happened despite a veritable 
exodus of foreign capital. Yes, SK Hynix and Samsung have made great profits, 
but the Kospi still looks alarmingly reliant on the exuberance of Korean retail 
investors:

Is the Korean stock market really going to feature in Hindsight Capital’s 
trades for the full year? It’s hard to imagine that its extreme rally can last 
another six months without reversing, but we don’t yet have the benefit of 
hindsight.

Skewed Perceptions

One other result of the AI boom has been to skew perceptions and force a 
rethink of the way we approach diversification. As Pictet illustrates in this 
chart, the MSCI emerging markets index has suddenly become a play on 
information technology:

The extraordinary variations between different Asian markets that were all 
vulnerable to the oil shock were almost entirely attributable to their exposure 
to AI.

In these circumstances, geography doesn’t particularly help diversify, and 
there is little to be gained by holding more countries. The critical point is 
to manage the risks and opportunities posed by oil, and the AI boom. To quote 
the team at Pictet:

Diversification is not defined by how many countries or stocks you hold, but 
by the independence of return drivers. Today, as those drivers become more 
aligned than many investors realize, the label on the tin no longer matches the 
contents.

Budaghyan also notes that even the apparent overall resurgence of EM is 
entirely attributable to the tech hardware companies of Taiwan and South Korea. 
Include them, and EM is outperforming. Exclude them, and it’s lagging:

This isn’t just in relative terms. Exclude hardware, and the EM boom has 
fizzled in recent weeks, even as the overall index has surged:

Time will tell whether such a dramatic skew toward one sector can possibly be 
sustained.
