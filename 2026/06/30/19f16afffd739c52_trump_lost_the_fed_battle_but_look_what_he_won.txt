The Federal Reserve Is Special

Channeling Chrissie Hynde <https://bloom.bg/44Bt8UT>, the Fed is special. 
That’s what the US Supreme Court has decided in a dramaticpair 
<https://bloom.bg/4waDTt8> of rulings <https://bloom.bg/4eCh2ks> that the 
president cannot fire Lisa Cook as a Federal Reserve governor without allowing 
her due process overallegations of mortgage fraud <https://bloom.bg/4456WSX>, 
but that he can fire officials at other independent federal agencies more or 
less at will.

These issues are self-evidently profound. Presidential attacks on the Fed’s 
independence <https://bloom.bg/4v8vk14> have roiled markets over the last year. 
Yet this ruling caused barely a market ripple. In the short term,the 
president’s new-found ability to fire members <https://bloom.bg/4oShn5M> of the 
Commodities Futures Trading Commission and the Securities and Exchange 
Commission could be a bigger deal.

Investors hated the idea of a politically-controlled central bank, but Cook’s 
victory had little impact for several reasons:

 * It was largely expected after justices revealed some of their thinking in 
oral questions earlier this year. The surprise was that the vote was as narrow 
as 5-4. 
 * The outlook for monetary policy has shifted since Trump tried to fire her 
last August, asrising inflation and the fallout from the Iran war 
<https://bloom.bg/4eLbmVr> rule out rate cuts for now. 
 *  Kevin Warsh <https://bloom.bg/4oW6UpZ> has now replaced Jerome Powell as 
chairman, and set out a more hawkish agenda than the president was urging last 
summer. 
 * Most importantly, the chance to install a pliant pro-Trump majority 
<https://bloom.bg/4gKHqKb> on the rate-setting Federal Open Market Committee 
has gone, if it ever existed.
The FOMC is divided into seven governors (including Cook), and the heads of 
the 12 regional Federal Reserve banks, who take it in turns to vote on rates. 
Enforcing the president’s favored ultra-dovish policy required installing a 
majority.

The seven governors do have the option to veto the appointments of new 
regional bank heads, who started new five-year terms earlier this year. The 
urgency to replace Cook came from the desire to have the necessary four 
governors in place in time to use their vetos. It was always questionable 
whether other Republican appointees were prepared to go along with this, and 
the moment has now passed.
It’s already too late to rig rate decisions rates by firing Lisa Cook. 
Photographer: Kevin Dietsch/Getty 
Firing Cook at this point would not shift the majority on the FOMC. 
Ironically, she is probably more inclined toward dovishness than most of her 
colleagues in any case. Had she resigned under pressure last summer, an 
audacious attempt to take political control of the Fed would have begun to look 
very possible. Now, if she were forced to resign after being found guilty of 
mortgage fraud (still a possibility, although her legal team suggest they have 
plentiful grounds for defense), it would be close to a non-issue. Fed 
independence is as important as ever; Cook is no longer at the center of it.

For now, direct presidential control over the SEC and CFTC could matter far 
more. Crypto could be directly affected. The SEC, by allowing Bitcoin 
exchange-traded funds, and the CFTC with its clearance of derivatives linked to 
perpetual crypto futures, have both helped pump money toward the industry. The 
CFTC has also fueledthe boom in prediction markets <https://bloom.bg/44BmG06>, 
which offer an end run around many existing gambling and investment 
regulations. The administration has known close links to the crypto industry, 
and the unimpeded ability to fire its regulators is a big deal.

For the future, politics is highly polarized. A change of party in the White 
House could now mean a very swift and drastic shift to regulations. That won’t 
help confidence in the US as a jurisdiction, and should stoke up plenty of 
volatility before elections. But at least, by a 5-4 majority, we can be 
reassured that the Fed is special.

A Yen for Speculation

Japanese people have far  more painful and important things to contemplate 
<https://bloom.bg/4eOsk3X> than their currency at present. The yen has just 
dropped below 161.90 per dollar <https://bloom.bg/4g9MkQR> for the first time 
sinceChristmas Eve, 1986 <https://bloom.bg/3QRCs3L>. If any more confirmation 
were needed that profound changes are afoot in the country, this would be it:

It’s not just about the dollar’s strength. Indeed, the dollar exchange rate 
understates the yen’s weakness. Japan has had far less inflation than anyone 
else for most of the last 40 years. That should mean the yen strengthens, to 
hold itsbuying power <https://bloom.bg/4eDH9Yi> equal to other currencies. 
Taking this into account, Japan’s real effective exchange rate against a range 
of currencies has weakened spectacularly. Small wonder tourists can’t believe 
how cheap everything is in Tokyo:

This selloff looks odd because yields on Japanese government bonds have been 
rising compared to Treasuries. That should strengthen the yen as it becomes a 
more attractive currency to park funds, but it hasn’t worked:

A combination of rising bond yields and a falling currency, often seen in 
emerging markets crises, implies loss of confidence in Japan’s ability to pay 
its debts.

How long can this go on? Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi 
<https://bloom.bg/4gNtZsU> has made clear she’d like a weak yen to help 
exporters, but there are limits. It grows ever easier for foreigners to buy 
Japanese assets — a problem for a nationalistically minded government like hers.

For Kit Juckes of Societe Generale SA, the yen is “untradeable,” while Japan’s 
need for stability means that “the unknown is at what level, not if, we will 
see intervention.”

Jordan Rochester of Mizuho concurs that the “path of least resistance” is 
still to buy the yen. He also points out that speculative positioning has grown 
very negative against the currency, which could provide theMinistry of Finance 
with an excuse to intervene <https://bloom.bg/4oTfAO9> if it wants one:

Others take a different position. Citi’s macro strategy team started a short 
on dollar/yen — betting on the yen to strengthen — on May 21 when the exchange 
rate stood at 159.10. The bank’s strategists had expected direct intervention 
to follow the Bank of Japan’s decision to raise rates two weeks ago, but that 
hasn’t happened — possibly because the latest leg of yen weakness may have more 
to do with dollar strength than any idiosyncratic problems for Japan.

It gave up and closed the short (meaning it took a loss) on Monday. Giammarco 
Miani of Citi ruefully explained: “The MoF can stay patient longer than we can 
remain short the dollar against the yen, hence we close our trade.”


Deals Recovery

The deals scorecard for the first half of 2026 begins to look a lot like what 
many expected when Donald Trump returned to the White House last year. A few 
mega-deals have skewed the picture, most notably theSpaceX 
<https://bloom.bg/4p5nK5Z> IPO, NextEra’s acquisition of Dominion Energy 
<https://bloom.bg/4oVG01r>, and a host of AI-related transactions. But the 
broad resurgence largely defied the Iran conflict, which impacted dealmakers’ 
confidence and led many to pause or delay. KPMG’s Dean Bell notes 
<https://bloom.bg/4ge6o4A> that the conflict also spurred strategic dealmaking 
in sectors like energy, as industries critical to meeting rising energy demand 
and supporting the expansion of AI looked to build scale:

Globally, PwC estimates that deal value will exceed $4 trillion this year, the 
most since 2021. So far, of the more than $2.6 trillion in announced deals, 
more than half has come from transactions of $5 billion or more. The test that 
suggests this recovery is truly broad-based is venture capital’s performance. 
PitchBook’s top-line figures show record-breaking VC activity in the first half 
of 2026, with the first quarter seeing the biggest volume of exits ever. If its 
deal value were a full-year total, it would have been the third largest:

AI continues to dominate the VC landscape. OpenAI’s $122 billion 
<https://bloom.bg/4eA9gHM> funding round alone exceeded the combined value of 
every quarterly VC deal previously recorded. PitchBook’s Kyle Stanford notes 
that the transaction was more than four times bigger than the quarter’s 
second-largest deal, Anthropic’s $30 billion raise. Both companies havefiled 
for IPOs <https://bloom.bg/3TasXgP> that will likely determine whether lofty AI 
valuations can continue. As AI accounts for about 30% of VC-backed unicorns at 
present, receptions for the mega-IPOs could have a profound impact on the 
sector.

Recent IPOs’ share price performance has rebounded recently, with the IPOX-100 
index outstripping the S&P 500 after several years in the doldrums. But 
Stanford warns that weak performance — as often seen from mega-IPOs in their 
first year of trading — could shut the pipeline. That lack of an exit strategy 
would then dampen demand for secondary issuance. 

IPOs also impact the public markets. Goldman Sachs’ Ben Snider points out that 
they are a clear indication of positive sentiment, both among corporates and 
investors, but also risk raising concerns about the kind of euphoria that 
precedes the bursting of a bubble. IPOs accelerated ahead of the dot-com 
implosion in 2000.

Valuations aren’t as high as they were 26 years ago, but they’re high by 
historical standards. And of course there is rapid technological change. That 
is where the similarities end, Snider argues:

If you look at the 25-year average, there’s about 100 deals per year. We’re 
tracking close to that average. In 2021, there were over 250 IPOs, in 1999 
almost 400. So, although the dollar volume is quite elevated and we’re seeing 
an acceleration in activity, we’re a far cry from the level of euphoric 
sentiment we saw in those episodes.

Deal activity could easily accelerate if the uneasy peace with Iran holds. 
Bell argues that eliminating that major geopolitical uncertainty would prompt a 
big bounce.

—Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



Watch the World Cup. You never know when it will serve up a classic like 
Brazil 2, Japan 1. But it’s painful when great players miss out through injury. 
Japan took the lead, but were eventually overhauled without their starKaoru 
Mitoma, <https://bloom.bg/3Ra3tQ8> who hurt his hamstring playing for Brighton 
at the end of the regular season. So cruel. To see what the world missed, here 
are hisgoal of the season against Chelsea in 2025 <https://bloom.bg/4ge1XXw>; 
thegoal that knocked Liverpool out of the FA Cup <https://bloom.bg/3QTRMNg> in 
2023; hisextraordinary run to score against Wolves <https://bloom.bg/4eALz21> 
later in 2023; andthis year’s goal of the season contender 
<https://bloom.bg/3QOTaRh> against Spurs. It’s a crying shame that the world 
didn’t get to see him in North America. And congratulations to Brazil on a 
great victory.
