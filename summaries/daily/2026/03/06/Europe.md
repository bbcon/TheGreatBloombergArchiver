European markets remained under mild pressure, with the Stoxx Europe 600 declining 0.28% amid the broader risk-off tone generated by the continuing Iran conflict. The most consequential regional development lies not in equities but in monetary policy signaling: the European Central Bank's policy trajectory appears to be shifting, with traders now fully pricing in a 2026 rate hike as the oil price surge fans inflation concerns. Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel was unambiguous on Thursday, stating that the inflation impact of the Iran war currently outweighs the growth drag — a view that, if shared by the broader Governing Council, would represent a meaningful pivot from the easing posture of recent quarters. Euro-area fourth-quarter GDP was simultaneously revised lower than initially reported, with trade weighing on output, creating a stagflationary tension that policymakers will need to navigate carefully.

Germany provided a rare constructive energy datapoint: unusually strong solar generation in March, with installations feeding over 40 gigawatts into the grid for five consecutive days, has helped cap domestic electricity prices even as global energy markets surge. The episode illustrates the buffering capacity of domestic renewable generation during external supply shocks — a dynamic likely to reinforce policy arguments for accelerated energy transition investment across the continent. Bloomberg Opinion's Marcus Ashworth separately argued this environment represents an opportune moment for the EU to pursue joint bond issuance.

The UK confronted a longer-term structural concern: London's child population is declining at the fastest pace in Britain, with the under-10 cohort falling by 99,100 between 2013 and 2023 even as total population rose by 506,000. The capital's fertility rate has dropped below 1.2 births per woman, well below the England and Wales average of 1.41. The demographic implications for school infrastructure, public services, and long-run fiscal capacity are material. Spain also emerged as a flashpoint in the Iran war's diplomatic fallout, with the Trump administration threatening to cut off LNG exports to Madrid after Spain denied access to military bases — a threat that, if executed, would expose Spain to higher marginal gas procurement costs.
