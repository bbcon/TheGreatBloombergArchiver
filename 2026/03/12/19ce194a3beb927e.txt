 War and deficits 



Investors are growing uneasy <https://bloom.bg/4uuhXcA> about the financial 
cost of the Iran war, sending long-term government bonds lower amid concerns 
the conflict could swell budget deficits.

The selloff has pushed the 30-year Treasury yield to close to 4.90%, the 
highest in a month, as investors factor in the cost of military operations.

The conflict’s fiscal consequences are reverberating across bond markets 
worldwide.Yields have spiked from the UK to Germany to Australia to Japan amid 
the prospect of more borrowing to pay for defense and to shield households from 
higher energy costs. Globally, bonds todaysurrendered their year-to-date gains 
<https://bloom.bg/4bknE3M>. 

For investors, the cost of the war is adding to concerns over already large 
deficits, prompting them to demand higher compensation for longer-dated bonds. 
Donald Trump yesterday repeated his suggestion that attacks may end soon, but 
offered no specific timeline.

“Long-end rates is a fiscal story and a government credibility story,” said 
Gang Hu at Winshore Capital Partners. “It reflects expectations that Trump 
needs to spend money to fund the war and subsidize consumers for higher oil 
prices.” —Greg Ritchie <https://bloom.bg/4jcnAFR>, Ruth Carson 
<https://bloom.bg/42kcWY2>, and Ye Xie <https://bloom.bg/3PSSLJP>

 Software disconnect 



Software stocks, sinking for months now on fears of AI disruption, appear to 
have found a bottom <https://bloom.bg/4bmjHM2> — at least for now.

A widely followed iShares ETF that tracks the sector is up 11% since the close 
on Feb. 23, when Citrini Research rattled the market with its dystopian vision 
of an AI future.

Even with those gains, which have been tempered by a dip this week, the stocks 
look cheap as a result of the selloff that began in the second half of last 
year. A Goldman Sachs basket of software stocks trades for less than 22 times 
estimated earnings, well below its average multiple from the past decade of 52.

Earnings are a big reason for the rebound: Even as the stocks sold off, profit 
estimates for 2026 were rising. Analysts project that software and services 
companies in the S&P 500 will deliver 21% earnings growth this year, up from a 
forecast of 17% at the end of 2025.

“We see a big disconnect between valuations and high-quality fundamentals, 
where the risks seem exaggerated,” said Hua Cheng at fund manager Mirova. —
Jeran Wittenstein <https://bloom.bg/4ai60gd> and Ryan Vlastelica 
<https://bloom.bg/3Xk5l8K>
