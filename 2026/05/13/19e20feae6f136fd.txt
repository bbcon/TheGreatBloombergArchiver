Surprisingly healthy demand for container shipping will help underpin ocean 
freight rates into the second half of 2026 as carriers try to adapt to the 
10-week closure of theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/trump-repeats-war-threats-against-iran-ahead-of-meeting-with-xi?cmpid=BBD051326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4353>
 and soaringenergy costs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-may-13?cmpid=BBD051326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4353>
.

Those were among the takeaways from a Bloomberg TV interview Wednesday with 
Hapag-Lloyd CEO Rolf Habben Jansen. He said the world’s fifth-biggest carrier 
is facing about $250 million in additional monthly costs since the Iran war 
started on Feb. 28 — about half the level No. 2 Maerskcited last week 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/maersk-says-mideast-war-had-limited-impact-in-first-quarter?cmpid=BBD051326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4353>
.

Habben Jansen said demand has been a little surprising on the upside 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-09/chinese-export-growth-rebounds-as-war-fails-to-curb-trade?cmpid=BBD051326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4353>
.

“When you look at the market today, then demand is actually quite strong,” he 
said. “We see volumes picking up, particularly out of Asia and especially the 
month of April has been fairly good, and also if we look ahead into May and 
June, then we see that volumes are very decent.”

Related: LA Port’s April Imports Top Last Year’s Front-Loading Rush 
<bbg://news/stories/TEW1JHT96OSG>

Outside the disrupted Persian Gulf, “all the other markets are actually pretty 
strong,” Habben Jansen said. “When you talk to customers, I would not be 
surprised if we see strong volumes until the end of this quarter, and probably 
also into the third quarter.”

Based on futures trading and the usual seasonal pickup in demand starting in 
late June, he said “you see that short-term rates are going up, and today, 
there are no signs that that will be any different in 2026.”

The Hamburg-based company earlier today reported 
<https://www.hapag-lloyd.com/en/company/ir.html> first-quarter group profit 
that was significantly below the same period in 2025, while keeping its outlook 
unchanged given theuncertainty 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/economic-crosscurrents-go-global-as-energy-shock-meets-ai-wave?cmpid=BBD051326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260513&utm_content=4353>
 in the Gulf.

“It’s just very tough to predict what’s going to happen,” Habben Jansen said. 
“Right now, we are still planning or expecting to see some kind of return to a 
normality in the upcoming couple of months, but in the end, nobody really 
knows.”
