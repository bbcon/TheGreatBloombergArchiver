**China: Resilience With Caveats**
China's position across the week was one of relative but qualified insulation. Equities declined less than global peers, the yuan held broadly steady, and government bond yields barely moved. Beijing's years of investment in renewables, electric vehicles, and strategic oil stockpiling — with onshore commercial inventories reaching a record 851 million barrels — provided a tangible buffer against the Hormuz disruption. February trade data released Tuesday were striking: exports surged 21.9% year-on-year in the first two months of 2026, well above the 7.2% median estimate, generating a record $214 billion trade surplus for the period. The strength was geographically broad, though exports to the US fell 11% reflecting tariff effects. Bloomberg Economics cautions that China's primary vulnerability is an export demand shock rather than domestic inflation: a sustained oil price above approximately $108 per barrel is estimated to begin meaningfully pressuring external demand in ways that would subtract measurably from GDP growth.

**China: Policy and Geopolitics**
The 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030), finalized through the National People's Congress, prioritized advanced manufacturing, semiconductors, and AI while maintaining the fiscal deficit at 4% of GDP. Analysts at Oxford Economics and Gavekal noted that the plan's twenty main indicators are largely unchanged from the prior cycle, and the absence of meaningful household consumption stimulus disappointed investors hoping for demand-side rebalancing. Separately, Beijing enacted legislation cementing Xi Jinping's assimilation policy for ethnic minorities, the PLA resumed aircraft incursions near Taiwan's sensitive airspace, and Chinese authorities moved to restrict state enterprises and government agencies from deploying OpenClaw AI on office computers, citing cybersecurity concerns — a decision that sent Minimax down 6.5% and Zhipu down 6.1% in Hong Kong on Thursday. Foreign Minister Wang Yi described 2026 as a potentially defining year for US-China relations, with the Trump-Xi summit framework for March 31–April 2 in Beijing taking shape through preparatory talks in Paris.

**Japan, South Korea, and Southeast Asia**
Japan's Nikkei 225 fell 5.2% on Monday before rebounding 2.88% on Tuesday, but declined again 1.04% on Thursday as global risk appetite softened. The yen weakened to approximately 159.5 against the dollar by Friday, its softest level since 2024, amplifying Japan's import cost burden and raising stagflation concerns in an economy where activity was already sluggish. More than a third of surveyed economists now expect the Bank of Japan to raise rates in April. South Korea's won traded near its lowest level since 2009; President Lee Jae Myung called for fuel price caps, and parliament approved the $350 billion US investment pledge even as Washington simultaneously launched a trade investigation targeting Seoul. Southeast Asian governments imposed emergency conservation measures across the week: Thailand ordered civil servants to work from home full-time, the Philippines shifted to a four-day government work week, and Vietnam faces potential airfare increases of up to 70%.

**India**
India's exposure to the crisis intensified across the week. New Delhi had been reducing Russian oil purchases under US pressure since December, pivoting toward Saudi and Iraqi supply — precisely the channels most disrupted by the Hormuz closure. Washington's announcement of a one-month sanctions waiver allowing India to resume Russian crude purchases provided material relief; Indian refiners immediately absorbed available Urals spot cargoes, with tankers carrying over 20 million barrels already within a week's sailing time of Indian ports. Nonetheless, the rupee came under sustained pressure, capital outflows accelerated, the Reserve Bank of India intervened in currency markets, and Indian carriers hiked long-haul ticket prices 15%. India is simultaneously in direct negotiations with Iran to secure safe passage for more than twenty tankers through the Strait of Hormuz — a transactional engagement with Tehran that reflects New Delhi's pragmatic approach to energy security.
