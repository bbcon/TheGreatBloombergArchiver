Weekly Brief

Feb 23 – Mar 01, 2026

US

Tariff Regime in Transition

The dominant US macro development of the week was the Supreme Court's invalidation of President Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs and the administration's rapid pivot to a replacement framework under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. Trump signed an executive proclamation imposing a 10% global tariff effective Tuesday at 12:01 a.m., subsequently signaling an intention to raise the rate to 15% — though the timeline remained unfinalized through the week. The legal foundation appears fragile: Section 122 requires evidence of "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold most economists, including former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath, argued the US does not meet. More than 1,500 refund lawsuits had already been filed on approximately $170 billion in contested duties by mid-week, and Treasury Secretary Bessent framed tariff repayments as "corporate welfare." Courts may move too slowly to constrain duties that expire in 150 days, preserving policy uncertainty in the interim.

Equity Markets and AI Disruption

US equity markets ended February under meaningful pressure, with the S&P 500 on course for its largest monthly decline since April, down roughly 0.4% — a striking underperformance relative to European and emerging-market benchmarks. AI disruption anxiety remained the dominant sector narrative. IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years on Monday following Anthropic's announcement that its Claude Code tool can modernize COBOL, the language central to IBM's mainframe business. Software names broadly repriced: Salesforce fell roughly 4% on Wednesday on an underwhelming outlook, C3.ai tumbled 24% on a revenue guidance cut, and Trade Desk dropped 17% on a weak forecast. Nvidia's Thursday earnings — a revenue forecast of approximately $78 billion that beat consensus — produced a muted after-hours response, reflecting an increasingly demanding bar. Partially offsetting, Dell gained 9.8% on robust AI server demand, and Block surged as much as 27% in extended trading Friday after announcing the elimination of approximately 4,000 positions framed as an AI-driven productivity bet.

Treasury Market and Federal Reserve

US Treasuries delivered their best monthly performance in roughly a year, with long-dated securities gaining approximately 4% in February, as a flight-to-quality bid absorbed equity and geopolitical volatility. The performance underscores the market's continued treatment of US government bonds as the primary haven asset. Fed Governor Lisa Cook and Richmond Fed President Tom Barkin both spoke at the National Association for Business Economics conference, each cautioning that AI-driven labor market reorganization may be the most significant structural shift in generations — and one that conventional rate policy cannot adequately offset. Kevin Warsh, Trump's expected Fed chair nominee, continued to attract analytical attention, with Deutsche Bank sketching potential reforms to the dot-plot framework and Bloomberg Economics confirming a marked hawkish-to-dovish shift in his public statements since 2025.

Macro Backdrop and Storm Disruption

The macro context entering the week was notably mixed: 2025 GDP growth was confirmed at 2.2% — the slowest since the post-COVID expansion — with core inflation ending the year at 3%, a full percentage point above target. January payrolls of 130,000 represented a meaningful rebound from the approximately 15,000 monthly average through 2025, and unemployment edged to 4.3%. A major winter storm named Hernando paralyzed the Northeast through Tuesday, canceling more than 10,000 flights, suspending Delta operations at LaGuardia, JFK, and Boston Logan, and sending US natural gas futures up nearly 7% as heating demand surged.

Geopolitical Risk and Fiscal Exposure

Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iran dominated the weekend close, introducing a new political risk dimension for President Trump ahead of November midterm elections. Having campaigned on anti-interventionism, Trump has now presided over actions against Venezuela, Nigeria, Iran, and now a direct assault on Iranian nuclear facilities — a cumulative departure from MAGA orthodoxy that polling suggests may complicate Republicans' path to retaining Congress. The energy price channel is the most immediate domestic economic concern: oil already up nearly 20% year-to-date means further upside will translate into gasoline price pressure on households already feeling financially strained. On the corporate side, Berkshire Hathaway's PacifiCorp faces approximately $55 billion in wildfire-related claims, with its credit rating sitting one notch above junk at S&P — a complex inheritance for Greg Abel as he penned his first annual letter as Warren Buffett's successor. ---

Asia

Regional Tariff Arithmetic and the Shifting Trade Calculus

The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariffs materially reconfigured Asia's trade exposure early in the week. Morgan Stanley estimated average US levies on Chinese goods fell to 24% from 32%, with the Asia-wide weighted average dropping to approximately 17% from 20%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng China Enterprises index rallied 2.65% on Monday, though mainland China and Japan markets were closed for holidays, limiting broader price discovery. The subsequent activation of a 10% global Section 122 tariff on Tuesday created a more complex winners-and-losers map: some adversarial nations fared relatively better while prior beneficiaries lost preferential arrangements. India, which had been advancing toward an interim deal with Washington, postponed its trade mission following the ruling, citing uncertainty over the new framework's legal durability.

China: Currency, Consumption, and Diplomatic Positioning

China's yuan ended its longest winning streak since 2010 on Friday after the People's Bank of China moved to temper appreciation by removing the 20% reserve requirement on foreign-currency forward contracts, effective March 2. The adjustment signals that authorities regard excessive yuan strength as a risk to exporters at a time of entrenched deflationary pressure — China's record $1.2 trillion trade surplus continues to generate currency inflows that complicate domestic policy. Lunar New Year tourism data offered a mixed read: 596 million trips were recorded over the nine-day break, 95 million more than a year earlier, but per-trip spending of 1,348 yuan fell marginally short of the prior year, consistent with a consumer that is mobile but cautious. Diplomatically, Beijing warned against further tariff increases ahead of the planned Trump-Xi summit in Beijing on March 31, while a record $112 billion discrepancy between Chinese export figures and US Customs arrival data for 2025 suggested that up to a quarter of China-origin shipments may have bypassed tariff controls, complicating both revenue collection and bilateral trade assessment.

Japan: BOJ Constraints and Cross-Shareholding Reform

Japan's week was shaped by two political economy developments with opposing implications. The yen fell approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar on Tuesday following a Mainichi newspaper report that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi expressed apprehension over further Bank of Japan rate increases at a recent meeting with Governor Ueda — a signal that political resistance to normalization may be more entrenched than markets had assumed following her election victory. Later in the week, Takaichi nominated two reflationist academics to forthcoming BOJ board openings, reinforcing those concerns and pressuring both the yen and JGBs. Notably, former BOJ Governor Haruhiko Kuroda publicly called for rate hikes and warned the yen may be "somewhat too weak" — a striking inversion of roles. On the structural reform side, Reuters reported Toyota is preparing to unwind approximately ¥3 trillion ($19 billion) in cross-shareholdings with banks and financial institutions, a meaningful step in Japan's multi-year effort to improve capital efficiency.

South Korea and the AI Hardware Rotation

South Korea emerged as one of the week's clearest beneficiaries of the AI infrastructure rotation. The Kospi advanced 1.91% on Wednesday to 6,084, with Samsung Electronics up 70% year-to-date and SK Hynix gaining 57%. The Bank of Korea held its benchmark rate at 2.5% while upgrading both growth and inflation forecasts, having previously told parliament that the semiconductor cycle is exhibiting unusual strength and exports jumped 47% in the first 20 days of February on a working-day-adjusted basis. South Korea's total equity market capitalization reached $3.76 trillion, surpassing France to rank ninth globally. Foreign funds nonetheless sold a record net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks on Friday after the benchmark's near-50% year-to-date run, a sign that profit-taking pressure is building at elevated levels. The National Pension Service posted an 18.82% return for 2025, its strongest on record.

Iran Shock: Asian Energy Exposure

Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iran introduced the week's most consequential late development for Asian economies. China, Japan, South Korea, and India are all major importers of Middle Eastern crude, much of which transits the Strait of Hormuz. Bloomberg Economics estimated a full Hormuz closure could push oil to $108 per barrel — representing a significant terms-of-trade deterioration for energy-import-dependent economies. China's position is particularly complex: Beijing is Iran's largest oil trading partner, and the strikes place it in a diplomatically awkward position vis-à-vis Washington, with whom trade relations are already strained ahead of the March 31 summit. ---

Europe

Equity Outperformance and the AI Rotation

European equities extended what has become the defining cross-regional market theme of early 2026. The Stoxx Europe 600 is set to close February with an eighth consecutive monthly gain — its longest winning streak since 2013 — ending the month up 3.7%. The region's relative insulation from AI disruption risk and an underappreciated cyclical recovery appear to be drawing capital from US equity allocations. European companies in the Stoxx 600 announced €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date through February, the highest ever for the January-February period according to Barclays. US borrowers simultaneously issued record volumes of euro-denominated "reverse Yankee" bonds, attracted by yields approximately 170 basis points below comparable US-denominated issuance — a tangible capital flow signal toward European debt markets.

HSBC and Corporate Earnings

HSBC reported full-year pretax profit of $29.9 billion on Wednesday, surpassing analyst expectations and sending shares up 5.4% in London trading. The result was underpinned by wealth management strength consistent with CEO Georges Elhedery's strategic pivot, and the bank's bonus pool rose 10% to $3.93 billion, the highest in at least a decade. Diageo fell 5.8% on Wednesday after cutting full-year organic net sales guidance for the second time this fiscal year, now projecting a decline of 2–3%, illustrating the compounding risk of simultaneous demand weakness in the US and China. Aston Martin announced plans to reduce its workforce by approximately 20%, citing Trump's tariffs. Johnson Matthey shares fell 17% on Monday after Honeywell reduced its offer price for the Catalyst Technologies business, and JD Sports rose 4.4% on a £200 million buyback announcement.

Trade Policy and EU Industrial Strategy

The European Parliament's trade chief Bernd Lange moved to freeze ratification of the EU-US trade agreement pending clarification of Washington's revised policy framework — a signal of the fragility of arrangements negotiated under the now-invalidated IEEPA tariffs. An EU assessment confirmed that Trump's new 10% global tariff, layered on existing levies, will push cumulative rates on certain EU exports above the 15% ceiling permitted under the EU-US agreement. Separately, the US warned the EU against advancing "Made in Europe" local-content rules, while Brussels finalized a draft industrial act with strict criteria favoring European firms in public procurement and requiring some foreign competitors to share technology and hire locally — a meaningful departure from the free-trade framework governing EU industrial policy for decades. Italy's industry minister escalated pressure on the EU's Emissions Trading System, calling it "ineffective and harmful," following an 8% carbon price decline after Chancellor Merz indicated openness to revisions.

UK Politics and Fiscal Outlook

Prime Minister Keir Starmer's Labour government faced a significant political setback when the Green Party won the Gorton and Denton by-election on Thursday with 40.7% of the vote, with Reform UK second at 28.7% and Labour trailing third at 25.4% — illustrating two-front pressure from both the progressive left and the nationalist right. Starmer is already registering as the most unpopular UK prime minister in modern times by some polling measures. On the fiscal side, Chancellor Rachel Reeves is expected to deliver a deliberately low-key economic update next week, with gilt issuance projected to be reduced — an outcome markets are likely to receive constructively. Separately, the collapse of London-based Market Financial Solutions created acute exposures for several Wall Street institutions, with Barclays carrying approximately £600 million, Atlas roughly £400 million, and Jefferies around £100 million.

Germany and Iran Shock Transmission

Germany's Ifo business confidence rose more than anticipated this week, and fourth-quarter GDP data confirmed a nascent recovery driven by domestic spending, with factory orders having jumped in December by the most in two years. Chancellor Merz's visit to Beijing midweek reinforced his administration's more balanced but watchful posture toward China, warning German business against deepening investments without government backstop. Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iran introduce a new complication: the eurozone is a large net energy importer, and a sustained move toward $108 per barrel — Bloomberg Economics' Hormuz closure estimate — would exert renewed inflationary pressure on an economy the ECB has only recently guided toward its 2% target, complicating the easing path at a particularly delicate juncture. ---

Global

US-Iran Strikes: The Week's Defining Event

Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities represent the most consequential geopolitical development for global energy markets in several years, arriving after a week in which diplomatic and military signals were simultaneously escalating. US-Iran nuclear talks resumed in Geneva on Thursday, with special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner meeting Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi, and both sides agreed to reconvene as soon as next week. Satellite imagery released during the week showed Iranian engineers actively reinforcing and concealing nuclear sites from last June's strikes, hardening tunnels and burying facilities — a reminder that military action can destroy physical infrastructure but cannot eliminate scientific knowledge, stockpiled material, or political will. A NATO AWACS aircraft in Turkey reportedly shifted its operational focus from Russia to Iran. Trump's overnight video address acknowledged the possibility of US casualties — a significant admission given the open-ended nature of the commitment.

Oil Markets and Strait of Hormuz Risk

Brent crude entered the week already up approximately 18–19% year-to-date, trading above $70 per barrel, reflecting a geopolitical risk premium that Saturday's events are likely to extend materially. Bloomberg Economics estimates a full Strait of Hormuz closure — through which approximately 20% of global oil flows — could push oil to $108 per barrel. Supertanker earnings had already surged to levels not seen since early 2020 during the week, with VLCC day-rates on the Middle East-to-China route exceeding $200,000, a rise of more than 600% year-to-date, driven by elevated volumes at sea and a concentrated market position being assembled by South Korean shipowner Sinokor Group. OPEC+ was set to meet Sunday with delegates signaling readiness to resume previously suspended output increases — a partial offset, though the group's ability to compensate for Hormuz disruption is constrained by logistics rather than production capacity.

Global Monetary Policy: Inflation Risk Reintroduced

The energy price shock materially complicates the easing cycles underway or anticipated across multiple central banks. The ECB, which had guided the eurozone toward its 2% inflation target, now faces the possibility of another energy-driven cost-push episode. French and Spanish inflation both surprised to the upside in February data released Friday, reinforcing caution ahead of forthcoming ECB meetings. In Asia, Japan, South Korea, and India — all major energy importers — face terms-of-trade deterioration if oil sustains elevated levels. The Federal Reserve's path is similarly complicated: core inflation already sits at 3%, a full percentage point above target, and sustained energy price elevation provides little room to accelerate easing even as AI-driven labor market disruption raises novel questions about the transmission of conventional monetary tools.

Global Debt and Private Credit Stress

Global debt reached $348 trillion at end-2025 per the Institute of International Finance, a fresh record driven by a $29 trillion annual increase — the fastest accumulation since the pandemic — underscoring fiscal vulnerability in a persistently elevated rate environment. Blue Owl Capital's decision to gate one of its private credit funds deepened scrutiny of the $1.8 trillion sector, with UBS raising its worst-case private credit default scenario to 15% on Friday, up from 13% less than a month earlier, citing rapid AI disruption as a clearer catalyst than previously modeled. Private equity returned distributions equivalent to just 14% of net asset value in 2025 — the second-lowest since 2008 — with $3.8 trillion in unsold assets weighing on the industry. The collapse of London-based Market Financial Solutions added a further data point to the stress narrative in non-bank lending.

Reserve Currency Dynamics and Dollar Entrenchment

Despite sustained "sell America" rhetoric, the dollar's reserve currency status appears further entrenched by the Iran shock, at least in the near term. Global investors continue to accumulate dollar-denominated assets, and the structural barriers facing alternatives remain unresolved. The ECB unveiled a repo facility extending euro liquidity to most foreign central banks — its most concrete step yet toward promoting the euro's international role — but German Bund annual trading volumes of roughly €6.74 trillion amount to less than two weeks of US Treasury turnover, illustrating the fragmentation problem. China's yuan ambitions face analogous constraints: foreign ownership of Chinese government bonds has declined to just under $400 billion, compared with $9.3 trillion of foreign-held Treasuries, reflecting persistent impediments from capital controls, limited market liquidity, and a banking sector that holds rather than trades fixed income. Reserve currency transitions, history suggests, occur over decades rather than geopolitical episodes.
