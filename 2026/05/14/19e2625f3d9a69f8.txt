An Early Peak

The UK economy’s roaring start of the year already looks like its peak as war 
in the Middle East and political drama in Westminster are likely to force a 
downshifting in growth.

Britain’s gross domestic product rose 0.6% in the first quarter of 2026 — its 
best performance in a year, the Office for National Statistics said Thursday. 
There was growth across the board and the economy even managed modest expansion 
in the initial weeks of the Iran conflict. The IT sector played a particularly 
large role in March’s growth, which some say could signal tailwinds from AI.

To be sure, these figures will raise plenty of eyebrows in the City of London.

 of the UK’s peculiar habit of growing much faster in the first three months of 
the year than the rest. And virtually everyone expects the economy to stagnate 
or even contract over the rest of the year as higherenergy prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/us-efforts-to-end-iran-war-stumble-as-ship-seized-near-uae?cmpid=BBD051426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4442>
 and tighter borrowing conditions start biting.

Looming over all of this is Prime Minister Keir Starmer’s fight for survival 
after his party suffered disastrous losses in local elections. Some of his 
fiercest Labour Party rivals are gearing up to challenge him soon. Regardless 
of whether they succeed or not, a leadership contest is set to push up 
borrowing costs and act as a drag on the economy.

“The UK economy had a strong start to 2026, mimicking the pattern seen in 
recent years. We don’t expect the pace to last, as higher energy costs and 
tighter funding conditions are likely to bring growth to a near halt in the 
coming quarters,” Ana Andrade and Dan Hanson of Bloomberg Economics wrote in an
analysis <>. “The recent political turmoil, which will also drag on activity, 
only reinforces that view.”

Need-to-Know Research

Outgoing Fed Chair Jerome Powell has been among those arguing that the recent 
years of high inflation mean central banks need to be all the more on guard for 
temporary shocks feeding through to broader, sustained price increases. Oxford 
Economics now quantifies that extra feed-through risk.

“Our analysis shows that in a period of heightened awareness, inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/us-producer-prices-rise-most-since-2022-on-higher-energy-costs?cmpid=BBD051426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260514&utm_content=4442>
 rises significantly more than usual in all major economies,” Daniel Harenberg, 
lead economist at the research group, wrote in a note Wednesday. The cost of 
being in a “high inflation attention” state is about 0.6 to 0.7 percentage 
point extra inflation from the oil-supply shock for the main economies, he 
wrote.

“This means that to keep inflation expectations under control, central banks 
may need to pivot towards tighter monetary policies than markets currently 
anticipate — especially if upside risks materialize,” Harenberg wrote.
