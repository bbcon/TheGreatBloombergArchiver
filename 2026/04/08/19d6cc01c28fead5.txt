To understand why President Donald Trump’s year-old trade war hasn’t caused 
inflation to spike higher, consider an ordinary bottle of European wine.

The US slapped a 10% tariff on wines from the European Union in April last 
year before hiking it to 15% in August. Since then, prices have risen 
gradually, at different speeds for different origins, grapes and labels.

Vintners, importers, shop owners and restaurateurs spent the past year trying 
to outmaneuver Trump’s tariff threats and have done what they can to avoid 
passing the full cost on to consumers.

They stockpiled bottles before the levies took effect and struck agreements to 
split tariff costs, anxious to avoid hurting demand at a time when younger 
Americans are imbibing less. They moved imported bottles straight from the dock 
into bonded warehouses, to delay paying the tariffs, and dropped lesser-known 
labels.

Other industries reeling from Trump’s tariff announcements similarly scrambled 
to adapt. But with wine, history shows tariffs do hit consumers — it just takes 
a while to reach them. (Read this Big Take 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-04-02/trump-s-tariffs-on-eu-wine-raise-prices-despite-industry-efforts?cmpid=BBD040826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260408>
 for more on the winemakers, importers, distributors and retailers managing the 
fallout from the trade war.)

In his first term, Trump imposed a 25% tariff on most wines from France, 
Germany and Spain, levies that were suspended two years later by President Joe 
Biden. Astudy last year 
<https://econ.duke.edu/news/study-reveals-us-consumers-paid-more-tariff-cost-european-wines>
 found that US retail prices for those imports eventually rose 6.9%, as each 
link of the supply chain adjusted its own prices to compensate. The effects 
took a year to arrive.

“Most of the price changes are flat for 12 months in retail after the tariff,” 
said Felix Tintelnot, an associate economics professor at Duke University and 
one of the study’s authors. “It really does take some time.”

Back then, foreign producers reduced their wholesale price by about 5% in 
order to maintain sales. Importers absorbed the biggest share of the tariff in 
the form of reduced profits, and by the time the duties reached the consumer, 
prices had climbed about 7%, according to the paper.

And while prices didn’t climb the full 25%, the economists found that 
consumers still paid more than the government collected after each tier of the 
supply chain made adjustments to their markups.

“The interesting twist is that despite all of that, the consumer was still 
paying for all this tariff revenue, right?” Tintelnot said.
