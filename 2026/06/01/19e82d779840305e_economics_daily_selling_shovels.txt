Picks and Shovels

“When everybody is digging for gold, it’s good to be in the pick and shovel 
business.”

The old adage for gold bonanzas is proving pretty accurate when it comes to 
artificial intelligence right now. The revolutionary technology’s promise of a 
once-in-a-generation productivity leap from the likes ofAnthropic’s 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/anthropic-raises-at-965-billion-valuation-eclipsing-openai?cmpid=BBD060126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5017>
 Claude and OpenAI’s ChatGPT is the precious metal in this analogy, making the 
chips that process all that information the picks and shovels of the AI boom.

That puts South Korea and Taiwan in a very good place. In the latest run of 
red-hot data, purchasing managers reports for both economies powered ahead in 
May whileKorean exports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-01/s-korea-s-export-momentum-continues-backing-bok-s-hawkish-case?cmpid=BBD060126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5017>
 jumped the most since 1984 on an adjusted basis, data Monday showed. Chip 
exports jumped a record 169.4%.

Riding the AI wave, stock markets in South Korea and Taiwan and Japan all hit 
records Monday.

To economists at Nomura, the AI revolution is among eleven structural shifts 
that’s triggering a rewiring of Asia’s economies.

“For three decades, Asia’s growth ran on cheap labor moving into factories, 
deep integration into global value chains, heavy capital spending and the 
gravitational pull of China’s industrialization,” the economists wrote in a May 
29 report. “Those forces remain relevant, but positioning in the AI stack has 
become a fourth variable that in some cases dominates the other three.”

They developed a matrix that assesses AI-hardware production and AI adoption 
to help determine how much of the capex cycle converts into GDP growth, export 
revenue, corporate earnings and employment, and how quickly.

It shows high-production economies (China, Korea, and Malaysia) benefit first 
and most visibly through exports, capex and corporate earnings. Meantime, high 
adoption-led economies (Singapore and Japan) benefit later through firm-level 
productivity, services competitiveness and higher-value regional functions. 
Dual winners (China and Korea) stand to benefit from both channels.

Already this year, the chip boom has helped north Asia’s tech-heavy economies 
shake off much of the drag from higher oil prices stemming from the war in 
Iran. But beneath the surface, some cracks are beginning to show.

“Look closely, and things are a little more brittle than they first appear,” 
warns Frederic Neumann, chief Asia economist at HSBC in Hong Kong. “Any 
pull-back in AI hardware demand would quickly expose the narrowness of the 
current export boom. True, there is little sign of this occurring any time 
soon…but with numbers like these, a reversal must come eventually.”

Need-to-Know Research

Much of the economic analysis on the Trump administration’s immigration 
crackdown has focused on its impact in slowing the pace of growth in US labor 
supply — generating the unusual condition of both a sharp drop-off in payroll 
increases and a relatively stable unemployment rate since early 2025.

Now, the Budget Lab warns 
<https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/lower-immigration-means-lower-productivity-growth>
 of long-term consequences for productivity. The Yale University research 
center says the issue is the hit to the formation of new businesses, which 
“account for a disproportionate share” of productivity gains. That’s because 
young firms tend to implement new ideas more frequently than old ones and draw 
workers and capital away from less productive companies.
Note: EVW ‘high’ and ‘low’ are scenarios for higher and lower net immigration
“The falling number of working-age immigrants and, eventually, their would-be 
native-born descendants reduces new-business formation,” the Budget Lab’s Abhi 
Gupta wrote in a note last week. Even if immigration returns to a pre-crackdown 
baseline by 2029, there are likely to be long-run effects for business 
formation and productivity, he wrote. The number of missing new firms peaks in 
the early 2030s at between 9,000 and 16,000, the analysis showed.
