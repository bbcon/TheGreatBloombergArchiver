Musk’s entangled empire

One way to think about SpaceX is that Elon Musk is building a multiplanetary, 
AI-infused version of General Electric.

Once regarded as the embodiment of American industrial ingenuity, GE amassed a 
mammoth collection of loosely related businesses, from light bulbs and jet 
engines to the TV network NBC, and ran a vast finance operation.

For its part, SpaceX makes reusable rockets, provides satellite internet, 
operates a social media platform and houses a fast-growing AI company. Musk 
wants to add space tourism and moon hotels in the future.

The model worked for GE, until it didn’t. In recent decades, opaque deals 
between businesses and untransparent balance sheets turned investors against 
the conglomerate model, and GE’s empire has broken off into pieces.

Today, Bloomberg’s Big Take 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-06-08/spacex-ipo-puts-elon-musk-s-intertwined-business-empire-under-scrutiny?cmpid=060926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5313>
 is about the links between Musk’s hardware, software and AI businesses, and 
the reasons why some investors are turning away from what promises to be the 
biggest IPO in history.

 * A Danish pension fund has said it won’t invest in SpaceX because it is 
“grossly overvalued” and has a “catastrophic governance structure.”
 * Morningstar has valued SpaceX at $780 billion after evaluating its finances 
— less than half of what Musk thinks the company is worth.
On the other hand: The IPO is well oversubscribed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-08/spacex-ipo-is-said-to-be-well-oversubscribed-orders-close-wed?cmpid=060926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5313>
 with multiple institutional investors placing orders for about $10 billion or 
more of shares, according to people familiar with the matter. Trading is 
expected to start on Friday.

Investors will want to think about timing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/spacex-anthropic-openai-can-rewrite-history-for-megacap-ipos?cmpid=060926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5313>

The market’s message to Kevin Warsh

Starting a new job can be tough. That’s true even for Federal Reserve chairmen.

Kevin Warsh will helm his first central bank meeting next week against a 
charged backdrop. President Trump has demanded lower borrowing costs, but the 
market issending a different message 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/treasury-market-is-telling-kevin-warsh-rates-need-to-be-higher?cmpid=060926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5313>
: Interest rates aren’t high enough.

Yields on policy-sensitive US two-year notes surged to the highest level in 
over a year after economic data led traders to price in at least one 
quarter-point rate hike as soon as October. They’re now well above the Fed’s 
current policy band.

The reset upwards intensified last week after a strong jobs report, 
reinforcing a growing conviction that rates need to rise in order to rein in 
inflation pressures and temper the risk of an AI-induced boom overheating the 
economy.

“For the first time in a while, we are considering a scenario where the US 
economy actually starts overheating,” said Andrzej Skiba of RBC Global Asset 
Management. —Michael MacKenzie 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVgVy7_JF34/michael-mackenzie?cmpid=060926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260609&utm_content=5313>
