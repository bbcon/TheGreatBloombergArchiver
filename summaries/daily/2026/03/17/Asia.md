China remains the central geopolitical pivot of the Iran conflict, facing competing pressures that make a clear policy response difficult. Trump's request to delay his planned March 31 summit with President Xi Jinping — ostensibly to remain in Washington to oversee the war — is widely interpreted in Beijing as a tactical maneuver. Chinese state media have responded with undisguised scorn to US appeals for help reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and the structural leverage Washington once wielded through tariff threats has diminished, partly due to US Supreme Court rulings and partly due to China's commanding position in rare earth mineral supply chains. Beijing appears likely to engage on its own terms and timetable, if at all.

Chinese economic data released since the National People's Congress offers modest reassurance. Domestic demand rebounded in January and February, and fixed investment — which had been contracting — turned positive. Chinese equities have regained some footing, though they remain below their pre-2021 highs. The risk, as Gavekal Research notes, is that rising commodity prices compress margins for manufacturers unable to pass costs through to consumers, potentially triggering further investment pullback and forcing additional policy stimulus. China's 2026 growth target of approximately 5% relies more heavily on exports to non-US markets than on domestic consumption, making an oil-driven global slowdown a material threat.

Elsewhere in the region, Australia's central bank raised its benchmark rate for a second consecutive meeting, standing out as one of the few economies tightening policy amid the global uncertainty. Indonesia's central bank delivered a hawkish hold while simultaneously tightening foreign-exchange regulations to defend the rupiah — the benchmark index is down approximately 14% in March. South Korea has followed China and Thailand in capping refined fuel exports to protect domestic markets. Japan faces a specific industrial supply chain vulnerability from tightening naphtha supplies, with multiple petrochemical firms announcing production cuts, and the Bank of Japan may consider lifting borrowing costs as soon as April. Pakistan's airstrikes on Kabul, which the Taliban government says killed at least 400 people at a drug treatment hospital, represent a separate and serious escalation risk in the region.
