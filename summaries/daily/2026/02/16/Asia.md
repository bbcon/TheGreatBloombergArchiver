# Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — February 16, 2026

Coverage across the region was constrained by Lunar New Year holidays, with mainland China, Hong Kong, Taiwan, South Korea and several Southeast Asian markets either closed or operating on shortened sessions. Activity nonetheless centered on a handful of consequential developments.

**China policy and US tech tensions.** Xi Jinping called for anchoring China's economic growth around domestic demand as the "main driver" — a notable rhetorical pivot that reinforces Beijing's pivot toward consumption-led rebalancing, though concrete fiscal follow-through remains the test. Separately, the Pentagon briefly added Alibaba, BYD and Baidu to its list of companies deemed to be aiding the Chinese military, only to withdraw the designation minutes later without explanation. The episode triggered modest selling — Alibaba slid more than 3% in Hong Kong, BYD and Baidu fell roughly 1% — and underscores the unpredictability of US-China tech friction even absent formal policy shifts. Alibaba simultaneously unveiled a major upgrade to its flagship AI model, positioning ahead of DeepSeek's anticipated next release. Chinese equities' bull run has, however, begun to falter as the earnings season approaches with subdued expectations.

**Japan: weak growth complicates BOJ path.** Fourth-quarter real GDP expanded just 0.2% annualized, far below the 1.6% consensus, with consumer spending up only 0.1%. The print strengthens the case for Prime Minister Takaichi's expansionary fiscal stance, and a former currency chief argued the BOJ is "behind the curve." Separately, Mizuho Financial shares fell about 5% after reports that employees are under investigation for suspected insider trading.

**Southeast Asia and Australia.** Thailand's Q4 GDP rose 2.5% year-on-year, exceeding all forecasts and prompting an upward revision to 2026 growth projections on stronger exports and tourism. Malaysia's king publicly placed the MACC anti-graft agency on his "radar" following allegations against its leadership, with street protests in Kuala Lumpur. In Australia, a Macquarie-led consortium's A$11.7 billion bid for Qube revived dealmaking momentum after a thin 2025. India tightened equity trading curbs amid ongoing efforts to cool speculative activity.

**Memory chips.** DRAM prices rose 75% from December to January, with Asian suppliers Samsung and SK Hynix among the principal beneficiaries.
