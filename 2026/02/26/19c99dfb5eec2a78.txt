Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Alex Longley looks at the ramifications of a historic surge in the 
cost of hiring an oil supertanker.

Oil tanker earnings are soaring to levels not seen since the start of the 
decade, sparking jitters for refiners and affecting trade flows.

The daily rate to hire very large crude carriers on their benchmark route from 
the Middle East to China has topped $200,000 a day, an increase of more than 
600% this year to the highest since early 2020.

The surge is already affecting oil prices. Crude from regions that typically 
face a long-haul to market, notably West Africa, is being offered at deep 
discounts as high shipping costs make it less attractive to buyers in Asia.

There are two big drivers of the shift — one short-term, the other potentially 
far-reaching.

Since the end of the Northern Hemisphere’s summer last year, the amount of oil 
transiting the world’s oceans on any given day has soared. Right now it’s about 
the same level seen in the opening months of the Covid-19 pandemic, when an 
OPEC+ price war and collapse in fuel demand caused a huge buildup of crude at 
sea.

Some of the recent increase has been in cargoes from sanctioned producers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/russia-and-iran-slashing-prices-to-china-as-oil-piles-up-at-sea?cmpid=BBBXT022626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260226>
 such as Russia that are struggling to find buyers. There’s also been a jump in 
volumes from the US.

The Saudis appear to have significantly lifted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/saudi-arabia-iran-drive-up-oil-exports-as-mideast-tensions-rise?cmpid=BBBXT022626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260226>
 shipments overseas, with exports up about 400,000 barrels a day so far this 
month. The kingdom’s biggest tanker owner has been booking other vessels to 
move this crude.

This comes at a time when suppliers are bracing for a conflict between Iran 
and the US that could potentially disrupt oil flows from the Persian Gulf.

The second factor helping to push rates sky-high is a bold bet 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-16/niche-corner-of-the-oil-market-is-rocked-by-huge-bet-on-tankers?cmpid=BBBXT022626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260226>
 by a South Korean shipowner backed by one of the industry’s richest men, 
Italian billionaire Gianluigi Aponte.

Sinokor Group has moved to buy and hire dozens of ships, quickly making it the 
biggest operator of very large crude carriers. A rival owner estimates the 
company could eventually have 150 ships of this type, about 40% of the total 
fleet.

Such concentration of ownership is a material shift in the long-term 
competitive structure of the tanker market.

“If not for Sinokor” tanker rates could never reach these levels, said John 
Kartsonas, founding and managing partner at commodity trading firm Breakwave 
Advisors LLC.

—Alex Longley, Bloomberg News
