The US-Iran conflict, now one month old, is functioning as a stress test for the post-Cold War architecture of US-led alliances, global energy markets, and the political economy of the energy transition simultaneously. Oil prices have surged globally — the commodity's fungibility means no economy is insulated — but the distribution of harm is highly unequal, shaped by import dependence, fiscal capacity, and currency strength. The gap between a US airline CEO warning of 20% fare increases and a Philippine president warning of grounded aircraft captures that distribution vividly.

In energy markets, the conflict is reinforcing a structural argument that had been gaining traction before the war: that dependence on fossil fuel imports from politically volatile regions carries security costs that renewables and electrification can partly mitigate. European Commission Executive Vice-President Teresa Ribera made the point explicitly this week, arguing that the disruption of global fossil fuel markets would prove "a very relevant driver of the green economy." Early data suggests the conflict is accelerating consumer uptake of electric vehicles, solar panels, and other low-carbon technologies in several markets — a feedback dynamic that, if sustained, could compress the long-run demand outlook for Gulf producers even as near-term prices remain elevated.

Geopolitically, the war is reshaping alliance calculus in ways that may prove durable. Nations that had pivoted away from China and Russia toward deeper US alignment are now reassessing that strategy under the combined pressures of trade tariffs, energy disruption, and reduced security commitments. Turkey's $8 billion drawdown of gold reserves has added a further wrinkle to commodity markets. Forecasters acknowledge significant uncertainty: the conflict's trajectory remains genuinely unclear, Washington continues to send mixed signals about scope and duration, and the nonlinear risks — to energy infrastructure, shipping chokepoints, and confidence — are by definition difficult to model. The honest assessment is that the range of outcomes remains wide, and the current market pricing may not fully reflect the left tail.
