**European Equity Outperformance**
The defining market theme of the week was European equities' relative strength against a US backdrop weighed by AI disruption fears. The Stoxx Europe 600 closed February up 3.7%, extending an eight-month winning streak not seen since 2013, with European companies announcing €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date — the highest ever for a January-February period, according to Barclays data. Goldman Sachs promoted what it labeled a HALO basket — Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence, spanning utilities, miners, industrials, and luxury goods — arguing these capital-intensive sectors are structurally insulated from AI displacement. The basket has outperformed capital-light peers by 35% since early 2025. This rotation reflects both the relative absence of high-multiple software names in European indices and a cyclical recovery that may be underappreciated by consensus. Germany provided some of the firmest datapoints: fourth-quarter GDP showed recovery taking hold, factory orders jumped in December by the most in two years, and the Ifo business confidence survey rose more than expected, suggesting early benefits from Merz's defense and infrastructure investment program are beginning to materialize, though the trajectory remains uncertain.

**Corporate Earnings and Stress**
HSBC's full-year results were the standout European corporate event, with pretax profit of $29.9 billion exceeding analyst expectations and the bonus pool rising 10% to $3.93 billion — the highest in at least a decade — as CEO Elhedery's pivot toward a more performance-differentiated compensation structure takes shape. The wealth division was the principal earnings driver, consistent with broader private banking flows into Asia. Diageo's second guidance cut of the fiscal year — projecting a 2–3% organic net sales decline — underscored how simultaneous consumer demand weakness in the US and China creates compounding risk for globally diversified consumer staples companies. Aston Martin announced plans to reduce its workforce by approximately 20%, citing Trump's tariffs among the complicating factors. The London Market Financial Solutions collapse, exposing Barclays, Atlas, and Jefferies, added to a broader narrative of stress in private credit and non-bank lending that spans both sides of the Atlantic this week.

**Politics and Trade Policy**
Labour's by-election loss in Gorton and Denton to the Green Party, with Reform UK second, illustrates the breadth of dissatisfaction with the Starmer government from both flanks. With local elections approaching and Starmer registering as the most unpopular UK prime minister by some modern polling measures, the political backdrop has become materially uncomfortable. Chancellor Reeves is expected to deliver a low-key economic update, with gilt issuance projected to be reduced — likely a constructive signal for markets given recent UK fiscal sustainability concerns. At the EU level, the European Parliament's trade chief moved to freeze ratification of the EU-US trade agreement pending clarification on Washington's revised framework, while an EU assessment confirmed Trump's 15% global tariff would push cumulative levies on certain EU exports above the EU-US agreement ceiling. Italy's industry minister escalated pressure on the Emissions Trading System, calling it "ineffective and harmful," following an 8% decline in European carbon prices after Merz indicated openness to revising or delaying the program — a signal with meaningful implications for climate policy credibility across the bloc.
