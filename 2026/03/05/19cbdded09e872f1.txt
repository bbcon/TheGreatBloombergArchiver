The Cost of Air Cargo Is Rising Amid Mideast War Disruptions
Air cargo rates have risen over the past week as carrier services in the 
Mideast remain limited

<https://sli.bloomberg.com/click?s=1149074&li=17153185&m=11b724c487f61753eb1a7a4a5f3ef261&p=03052026&lctg=6689f0ec-2ca1-4687-9972-bdcb0c2dfbdf&stpe=default>
Air cargo rates are climbing and ocean container networks are showing signs of 
stress amid mounting capacity constraints during the first week of the US and 
Israel’s war with Iran.

According to the Freightos Air Index 
<https://www.freightos.com/enterprise/terminal/fax-global-freightos-air-index/>
, rates to Europe from Southeast Asia have risen more than 6% to $3.82 per 
kilogram since last Friday — the day before the bombing started. 
Mideast-to-Europe rates increased 8% to $1.62 a kilo, and China-to-US prices 
jumped 15% to $6.90.

The price pressure stems from airspace that remains closed around the Persian 
Gulf region, leavingQatar Airways 
<https://www.qrcargo.com/s/about-us/press-releases/a0zP100000JdGrZIAV/temporary-suspension-of-qatar-airways-flights-due-to-qatari-airspace-closure>
,Emirates <https://www.skycargo.com/my-shipments/service-updates-alerts/> and 
Etihad <https://www.etihad.com/en/manage/flight-status#travelupdates> providing 
limited freight service. Combined, those three make up about 13% of global 
capacity. Their hubs in airports like Dubai’s account for about a quarter of 
all China-to-Europe capacity, according to Aevean data cited by Freightos.

Stefan Paul, the CEO of Kuehne + Nagel, estimated on a conference call Tuesday 
that the capacity loss in air freight globally is about 18% — a gap the Swiss 
freight forwarder is trying to cover with charters.

Covid Comparison

By late this week or early next, “we will see, most probably certain backlogs 
arising in Southeast Asia and in China for the European and the US 
marketplace,” Paul said. “Then the question is: What is happening on the demand 
side on the customer side, because there is a mismatch most probably then 
coming similar to the Covid times on supply and demand.”

Judah Levine, director of research with Freightos, said the war’s impact on 
the container market are mostly localized so far. But that could change the 
longer it drags on, and there are already signs of pileups outside the Gulf 
area.

Levine said in a note Wednesday that the Strait of Hormuz handles about 2% or 
3% of global container volumes, and that currently there are about 100 of these 
vessels now “stranded in the Persian Gulf range.”

MSC, the world’s largest container line, advised customers 
<https://www.msc.com/en/newsroom/customer-advisories/2026/march/war-risk-surcharge-trade-from-middle-east-to-subsaharan-africa-and-indian-ocean?_gl=1*16s76hu*_up*MQ..*_ga*OTM3ODAwNTIzLjE3NzI3MDU5MzU.*_ga_9HMJRMP77C*czE3NzI3MDU5MzQkbzEkZzEkdDE3NzI3MDYwNDYkajYwJGwwJGgwJGRES25uYm5nYVNVRjBIUkNha1EyZl9fRU9MSkd3MjJyZWdR>
 of an immediate “war risk surcharge” of $3,000 per 40-foot container for all 
cargo moving from the Arabian Peninsula to markets in sub-Saharan Africa and
another fee 
<https://www.msc.com/en/newsroom/customer-advisories/2026/march/war-trade-from-ipak-sri-lanka-bangladesh-to-africa-and-indian-ocean?_gl=1*1qdf8fo*_up*MQ..*_ga*OTM3ODAwNTIzLjE3NzI3MDU5MzU.*_ga_9HMJRMP77C*czE3NzI3MDU5MzQkbzEkZzEkdDE3NzI3MDU5NDMkajUxJGwwJGgwJGRES25uYm5nYVNVRjBIUkNha1EyZl9fRU9MSkd3MjJyZWdR>
 for goods moving from the Indian subcontinent. The regional turmoil in 
maritime traffic is “causing disruption throughout our network,” it said.

Port Disruptions

Shipping continues to be a target. Operations at Oman’s Duqm port were 
suspended Tuesday and resumed Wednesday following drone attacks, a spokesperson 
said. The Port of Antwerp-Bruges in Belgium is a shareholder in Port of Duqm.

Read More: India Automakers Delay Mideast Shipments as War Snarls Exports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/india-automakers-delay-mideast-shipments-as-iran-war-snarls-exports?cmpid=BBD030526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>

Barring an unexpected end to the conflict in the near term, the shipping 
snarls could widen.

Saudi Ports Authority officials have discussed ways to improve supply chains 
stability with Cosco and Maersk in recent days, according toposts on X 
<https://x.com/MawaniKSA/status/2029501562402287701>.

Earlier this week, Sri Lanka’s President Anura Kumara Dissanayake said the 
country received requests from shipping lines to store containers en route to 
ports affected by the Middle East conflict.

“The bottlenecks are going to be widespread all over Asia,” said Lars Jensen, 
the CEO of Vespucci Maritime. “The ships that are now waiting to get into the 
Gulf obviously can’t, and they’re going to ditch their cargo in the ports close 
to there. It’ll be Khor Fakkan, Sohar, Salalah, and then it’s going to snowball 
from there. it’s going to go over to Colombo, then it’s going to go all the way 
over to Asia.”

Meanwhile, a bulk carrier signaled it was Chinese-owned as it transited the 
Strait of Hormuz this week, highlighting how vessels are trying toensure safe 
passage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/bulk-carrier-claiming-to-be-chinese-passes-through-hormuz-strait?cmpid=BBD030526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 through the waterway during the war.

Speaking to Iranian state TV, military commander Amir Heydari said “we do not 
believe in closing the Strait of Hormuz at all.”
