**China: Trade, Currency, and Geopolitical Complexity**
China emerged from the Supreme Court's IEEPA ruling as one of the clearer near-term beneficiaries, with Morgan Stanley estimating average US levies on Chinese goods fell to 24% from 32%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng China Enterprises index rallied 2.65% on Monday. The improved tariff arithmetic arrives ahead of President Xi Jinping's scheduled hosting of Trump in Beijing on March 31, materially altering the negotiating dynamic: Xi enters the summit with export volumes at full capacity, reduced average levies, and a US counterpart operating under legally constrained trade authority. Whether Beijing capitalizes on this position or maintains a measured posture to avoid fresh friction remains to be seen. China simultaneously warned against further tariff increases and issued new blacklistings of 20 Japanese entities including Mitsubishi Heavy affiliates — the first addition of Japanese firms to the list since its inception. A record $112 billion discrepancy between Chinese reported export data and US Customs arrivals for 2025 suggests that as much as a quarter of China-origin shipments may have bypassed US tariff controls, complicating revenue collection and any analytical assessment of the bilateral trade relationship. The PBoC's move to temper yuan appreciation by easing forward contract reserve requirements reflected concerns that China's record $1.2 trillion trade surplus continues to generate currency appreciation pressure at odds with an economy still grappling with deflationary dynamics. Lunar New Year data offered a mixed signal: 596 million tourist trips — 95 million more than a year prior — and record total tourism spending, but per-trip expenditure of 1,348 yuan fell marginally below the prior year, consistent with a consumer that is mobile but cautious.

**Japan: Policy Tensions Mount**
Japan's monetary policy outlook became more contested across the week. Reports that Prime Minister Takaichi had expressed apprehension over further BOJ rate increases in a meeting with Governor Ueda — taking a tougher stance than at their November meeting — sent the yen down approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar on Tuesday, reviving concerns that political resistance could constrain normalization. Later in the week, Takaichi nominated two reflationist academics to forthcoming BOJ board vacancies, reinforcing those concerns and sending the yen and JGBs lower. Notably, former BOJ Governor Haruhiko Kuroda — himself the architect of Japan's long-running easing experiment — called publicly for rate hikes and warned the yen may be "somewhat too weak," a striking inversion of his own legacy. On corporate governance, Toyota is preparing to unwind approximately ¥3 trillion ($19 billion) in cross-shareholdings with banks and financial institutions, a meaningful step in Japan's multi-year capital efficiency effort.

**South Korea and the Broader Regional Rotation**
South Korea's equity market continued to attract attention as a structural beneficiary of corporate governance reform and semiconductor cycle strength. The Kospi advanced 1.91% to 6,084 on Wednesday, with total market capitalization rising to $3.76 trillion, surpassing France to rank ninth globally. Overseas investors purchased a net $2.77 billion of Taiwan equities on Tuesday — the largest single-day foreign inflow since December 2005. The Bank of Korea held its benchmark rate at 2.5%, upgrading both growth and inflation forecasts and publishing new dot-plot projections. South Korea's fertility rate rose for a second consecutive year — a rare positive demographic signal — though foreign funds sold a record net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks on Friday after the benchmark's near-50% year-to-date advance, suggesting some profit-taking in one of the world's best-performing markets. Saturday's Iran strikes introduced a new downside risk variable: as a major crude importer with limited domestic energy production, South Korea is acutely exposed to any sustained disruption in Strait of Hormuz flows.
