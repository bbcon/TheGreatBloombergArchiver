China set its official GDP growth target at approximately 5% for 2026 while deliberately avoiding a large-scale fiscal stimulus package, signaling that policymakers remain cautious about the economy's structural trajectory. The decision reflects a familiar tension: acknowledging near-term headwinds — including export exposure to US tariffs and subdued domestic consumption — while resisting the balance-sheet expansion that larger stimulus would entail. Markets had watched the target-setting closely for signs of a more aggressive demand-side response; the absence of one may temper near-term risk appetite for China-exposed assets.

India moved to insulate its economy from the spillover effects of the US-Iran conflict, with policymakers focused on the dual exposure of elevated oil import costs and potential disruption to trade routes through the Persian Gulf. India's dependence on Gulf energy supplies makes it structurally vulnerable to any sustained increase in crude prices or Strait of Hormuz disruption, and the government's defensive posture reflects that sensitivity. The precise policy measures remain to be detailed, but the signal of active contingency planning is itself notable.

South Korea's ongoing equity market reform initiative — the so-called "Korea Discount" program aimed at improving corporate governance and shareholder returns — faces a significant test as global risk sentiment becomes more complex. The program has attracted investor attention since its inception, but execution risk remains elevated given the country's chaebol-dominated corporate structure and the tendency for reform momentum to stall. Separately, geopolitical currents, including the minerals competition in Africa and the Iran conflict's effect on energy prices, create second-order headwinds for Asia's manufacturing exporters broadly, as input costs rise and trade route uncertainty increases.
