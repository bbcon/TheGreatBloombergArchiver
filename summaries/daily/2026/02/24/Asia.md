Two developments dominated Asian market attention Tuesday: renewed AI-driven risk aversion and a sharp move in the Japanese yen. The yen fell approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar following a Mainichi newspaper report that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi expressed apprehension over further Bank of Japan rate increases during a recent meeting with Governor Kazuo Ueda — taking what the newspaper described as a "tougher stance" than at their November meeting. The report appeared to revive concerns that political resistance could constrain the BOJ's policy normalization path. Markets had broadly anticipated that Takaichi, whose election victory earlier this month had been welcomed by investors, would adopt a more accommodative posture toward monetary tightening. The Mainichi report, if accurate, suggests that expectation may have been premature.

Japan also raised concerns directly with Washington, with Trade Minister Ryosei Akazawa calling Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick to request that the new 10% global tariff not leave Tokyo in a worse position than the terms agreed under last year's bilateral trade deal.

China's escalating pressure on Japan deepened further as Beijing blacklisted 20 Japanese entities — including affiliates of Mitsubishi Heavy Industries — on its export control list, effective immediately. This marks the first addition of Japanese firms to the list since its inception in January 2025. An additional 20 entities, including Subaru and Mitsubishi Materials, were placed on a monitoring list subjecting them to heightened scrutiny on dual-use export applications. The moves appear calibrated to signal continued displeasure following Takaichi's landslide election victory.

Separately, Shein announced plans to invest over 10 billion yuan ($1.45 billion) in intelligent supply chain infrastructure in Guangdong province — a notable strategic pivot for a company that had previously sought to distance itself from its Chinese origins, relocating its headquarters to Singapore and initially pursuing a New York listing. The announcement, made by founder Xu Yangtian at a Guangzhou conference, signals a tactical reorientation toward Beijing as Shein's Hong Kong IPO remains stalled and external pressures mount. In North Korea, Kim Jong Un's sister Kim Yo Jong was elevated to department director within the Workers' Party Central Committee as the party's first congress in five years convenes in Pyongyang.
