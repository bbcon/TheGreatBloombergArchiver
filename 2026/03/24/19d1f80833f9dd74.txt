The aura of invincibility has come off Giorgia Meloni 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/meloni-hits-an-unexpected-hurdle-on-way-to-re-election-campaign?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
.

Italy’s prime minister is something of a model for far-right contenders in 
Europe, where she serves as an example of how a rabble-rousing provocateur can 
successfully transition to the mainstream.

In Brussels, she earned a prominent seat at the table in a European Union 
hierarchy suspicious of an anti-establishment populist tide. She managed to 
stay out of Donald Trump’s sights when he took jabs at traditional US allies of 
all stripes.

Now she’s suffered defeat 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/italian-exit-polls-show-meloni-facing-narrow-referendum-defeat?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 — in a referendum of her own making on a judicial overhaul few Italians 
understood. It became a plebiscite on her, exposing her over-confidence a year 
ahead of a general election she was looking to win to lay down her legacy.
Meloni in Brussels on March 19.Photographer: Nicolas Tucat/AFP/Getty Images
Instead, she must correct course just as she becomes a veteran among Group of 
Seven leaders, with her political frenemy French President Emmanuel Macron 
exiting the scene.

Still, Meloni is not alone in experiencing a setback.

In France, the far right failed to take key cities 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-22/france-s-far-right-misses-key-targets-in-municipal-elections?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 in municipal elections,blunting the sense of inevitability 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/french-elections-show-far-right-still-has-work-to-do-in-runoffs?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 that Marine Le Pen — or her dauphin, Jordan Bardella, if she’s barred from 
running — would finally claim the presidency in 2027.

In Slovenia, a far-right insurgency was shut out of power 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-22/slovene-premier-on-path-to-beat-nationalist-jansa-in-close-vote?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 by a whisker, while in Spain, Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez’s Socialists 
scuppered the far-right’s rise with asurprise comeback 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/sanchez-socialists-claim-turnaround-as-spain-s-far-right-falters?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
.

Nigel Farage is meanwhile losing his polling gloss 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-14/nigel-farage-s-reform-is-challenged-by-rupert-lowe-s-restore?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 in the UK. He was an architect of the Brexit movement that won its referendum 
— a tool traditionally favored by populists, until they go awry.

Next up is Viktor Orban, the conservative nationalist who’s turned Hungary 
into an illiberal state. Despite his iron grip on power, surveys suggesthe 
faces an uphill struggle 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/magyar-vows-to-oust-orban-s-allies-if-he-wins-hungary-s-election?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
 for re-election in April.

For Orban, it’s all looking much too close for comfort. — Flavia Krause-Jackson

Chart of the Day

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent regularly says his motivation to get “out 
from behind my desk 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/bessent-s-longshot-deficit-goal-undercut-by-war-and-tariff-news?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
” and enter public service was concern about growing US debt. Developments over 
the past several weeks — from the Supreme Court ruling on tariffs to the war on 
Iran and its effect on inflation and interest rates — have left those alarms 
ringing even louder. It all means Bessent’s objective of getting the fiscal 
deficit-to-GDP ratio below 4% by the time Trump leaves office in January 2029 
looks even tougher.

And Finally

It’s been half a century since the fall of Saigon ended the Vietnam War, the 
first conflict of the television age, when journalists had almost unrestricted 
access to the battlefield. In today’s Iran conflict, the first military 
engagement involving America of the social-media era, the world is facing an
information black box 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/iran-war-marks-new-era-of-media-crackdowns-ai-generated-images?cmpid=BBD032426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260324>
. Internet blackouts in the Islamic Republic, widespread arrests of anyone who 
films strikes in Gulf countries, Israeli reporting restrictions and a US 
administration hostile to mainstream media — the result is extreme difficulty 
in conveying what is happening in the conflict zones, and a public left trying 
to make sense of it all.
