US equity markets ended Monday modestly higher but well below their intraday peaks, as investors quickly tempered their initial enthusiasm following President Trump's Truth Social post claiming "productive conversations" with Iran had produced progress toward a resolution. The Nasdaq 100 closed roughly flat relative to where it stood Friday morning, an outcome that itself signals something important: the market's ability to respond to presidential jawboning on geopolitical risk appears to be diminishing. As Brad Conger of Hirtle Callaghan observed, investor faith in Trump's capacity to end the conflict the way he might reverse a tariff is likely misplaced — the war involves a motivated adversary with independent agency.

The fiscal outlook continued to deteriorate in the background. The Pentagon has requested $200 billion in supplemental funding for the Iran conflict, which economists warn could push the 2026 federal deficit toward $2 trillion, roughly double Treasury Secretary Bessent's original 3%-of-GDP target. The Supreme Court's earlier invalidation of most of Trump's tariff regime has further reduced anticipated revenue, while the oil-driven inflation shock is suppressing the probability of Federal Reserve rate cuts that might otherwise have relieved interest costs. The Treasury paid approximately $970 billion in net interest on federal debt last year; Goldman Sachs economists project the deficit will exceed 6% of GDP through 2029.

On the corporate side, Apollo Global Management moved to cap redemptions from its $25 billion Apollo Debt Solutions vehicle at 5% of outstanding shares after clients sought to withdraw 11.2%, highlighting mounting stress in private credit markets as the conflict and rate expectations tighten. Meanwhile, unusual trading patterns prior to Trump's Monday post — with roughly 6 million barrels of oil futures changing hands in the two minutes before publication, against a five-day average of 700,000 — drew regulatory scrutiny and commentary from market participants concerned about information leakage around presidential communications.

Airline sector stress is also crystallizing domestically. Bloomberg Intelligence estimates sustained elevated jet fuel prices would raise US carriers' costs by 15–23%, with budget operators most exposed. Delta and Southwest are relatively better positioned given stronger balance sheets, while Frontier and JetBlue face greater vulnerability.
