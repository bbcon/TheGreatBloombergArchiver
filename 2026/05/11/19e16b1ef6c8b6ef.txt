Expectations are low for this week’s Beijing summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/xi-s-has-1-2-trillion-rare-earths-leverage-ahead-of-trump-visit?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 between the leaders of the world’s two largest economies. But let’s imagine 
for a moment US President Donald Trump and China’s Xi Jinping sitting down and 
delivering the best outcome imaginable for the global economy. What would that 
look like?

Before we go any further there’s an enormous caveat. The two men involved, 
embedded suspicions, dueling ideologies and national rivalries that mean any 
economic links are viewed as potential security risks all combine to make this 
an intentionally naive exercise. But Monday mornings in Spring should be about 
innocence and hope. So here goes.

Preview: Trump Aims to Press Xi Over China’s Approach to War in Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-10/trump-aims-to-press-xi-over-china-s-approach-to-conflict-in-iran?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>

There is no issue bigger in the world today than managing the breakneck rise 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/goldman-sees-rates-pressure-as-ai-fuels-k-shaped-korea-taiwan?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 of artificial intelligence. So a productive summit would surely involve the 
two leaders agreeing on how to do so. Even only a verbal commitment not to use 
AI in war or to attack each other’s economies would be a good start.

Chokepoints

If there is one thing rivaling AI as a force in today’s global economy it is 
the growingenergy crisis 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/us-iran-wrangle-over-terms-of-deal-to-end-war-and-reopen-hormuz?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 tied to the Iran war and closure of the Strait of Hormuz. It is linked to a 
bigger issue: The world’s chokepoints. What if the two leaders committed to a 
belief in free navigation of any of those — whether they be geographic, digital 
or financial — and pledged not to weaponize them?

The International Monetary Fund and others have been warning 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/iran-war-debt-overhang-add-to-concerns-at-imf-world-bank-meetings?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 of rising imbalances in the global economy, with the US and China the two main 
contributors to that. Imagine if the two leaders pledged to actually address 
that? The main cause of the imbalances is Chinese consumers spending too little 
and Americans writ large spending too much. A pact calling for fiscal restraint 
in the US and more largesse from Beijing might be a start.

Read More: Trump Risks Confidence in US Role as Guardian of Global Shipping 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-05/trump-s-hormuz-threats-risks-faith-in-us-role-as-guardian-of-global-shipping?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>

Industrial subsidies from cheap electricity to subsidized loans are behind 
much of the manufacturing overcapacity leading to asurge in Chinese exports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-09/chinese-export-growth-rebounds-as-war-fails-to-curb-trade?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
. They were part of trade discussions during Trump’s first term. They also were 
embraced as an economic tool during the Biden administration. So why not reach 
an agreement to negotiate new rules, restraint and greater transparency?

They are Trump’s favorite 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/trump-s-treasured-negotiating-edge-dulled-by-tariff-defeat?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 economic instrument. But tariffs have been largely ineffective over the past 
eight years in resolving global imbalances or even causing the decoupling 
proponents want. The uncertainty around their implementation have also acted as 
a brake on investment on both sides of the Pacific. Declaring a moratorium on 
import taxes seems unlikely. But how about creating some stability by agreeing 
not to raise them any further for the next year as part of an extended 
ceasefire?

As part of that discussion, both sides should agree on and publish lists of 
strategic sectors they want to protect as well as a strict definition of what 
qualifies as national security. Surely both sides can agree that anything 
related to quantum computing or AI fits the bill while kitchen cabinets don’t?

Car Collaboration

Much of China’s economic rise was built on joint ventures and other 
relationships with western companies from Apple to Volkswagen. But in sectors 
likeelectric vehicles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-08/stellantis-to-deepen-china-pact-with-joint-ev-spain-plant-shift?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 Chinese companies now lead the world. So it’s time for knowledge to flow the 
other way. How about a BYD joint venture with Ford or GM to build EVs in 
Michigan and, to take the China model a step further, a research center to boot?

Read More: Anti-Recession Guardrails Are Weaker Than Ever Across the Globe 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-04-12/imf-world-bank-to-meet-in-dc-this-week-as-iran-shock-looms?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>

There are other meaningful things the two leaders could agree to, of course. 
Debt relief for any poor country that spends more servicing its debt than on 
education and health care would be a start. So too would be a commitment to 
create jobs in those countries where high birth rates have created a vast pool 
of underused human capital. Or one not to start economically disruptive wars 
whether in Iran or Taiwan.

Few if any of those things seem likely to happen, of course. But every so 
often it’s worth laying out hopes rather than realities. Especially in Spring.

Charted Territory

Rail alternatives 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-08/iran-turns-to-china-rail-link-to-try-to-bypass-us-blockade?cmpid=BBD051126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260511&utm_content=4282>
 | Iran is boosting trade with China via rail to counter a US naval blockade, 
with freight trains from Xi’an to Tehran rising from about one a week to one 
every three or four days. Cargo rates have surged, with standard 40-foot 
container along the route fetching as much as as $7,000, roughly 40% more than 
typical levels, Bloomberg reports. The rail route goes through Kazakhstan and 
Turkmenistan.
