• Eurozone CPI rose above 3% YoY in W23 — the first breach of that level in more than two and a half years, beating prior readings — prompting the ECB to deliver a 25 basis point rate hike to 2.25% in W24, its first since 2023.
• UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer resigned in W26; Andy Burnham, who had won the Makerfield by-election Thursday with 24,927 votes versus Reform UK's 16,000, emerged as near-certain successor with no acute stress in gilts or sterling.
• BMW guided full-year profit margins as low as 1% in W25, citing weak Chinese demand and Middle East disruption; shares fell 6.5% Wednesday, crystallizing a sector-wide vulnerability for European automakers.
• Eurozone Q1 GDP was revised to show a contraction in W23, principally attributable to Irish national accounts distortions; the June composite PMI recovered to 49.5 in W26, above the 48.5 consensus, though remaining below expansion for a third consecutive month.

**ECB: Inflation vs. Growth**
The ECB's June hike stands as the most significant developed-market monetary policy action of the period. Policymakers concluded that oil-driven inflation — running well above target for more than three months — combined with broadening price pressures made further inaction untenable. President Lagarde cited sufficient economic resilience to absorb tighter conditions; critics including Berenberg's Holger Schmieding explicitly invoked the ECB's 2011 error of hiking into a deteriorating growth environment. Despite falling oil prices following the Hormuz ceasefire, Chief Economist Philip Lane pushed back against assumptions that energy relief removes the case for further tightening, arguing the conflict has inflicted durable economic damage. Markets are pricing approximately 75 basis points of total 2026 tightening, a path the consensus views as potentially too aggressive given current growth dynamics. Updated ECB projections show both higher inflation and weaker growth — a stagflationary bind that the hike decision itself does not resolve.

**UK: Political Transition and Economic Constraints**
Starmer's departure — on the tenth anniversary of the Brexit referendum, marking the sixth prime ministerial change since 2016 — arrived alongside already-strained fiscal dynamics. Burnham inherits a gilt market conditioned by the 2022 Truss episode: Mizuho's Jordan Rochester noted that any "whiff of fiscal laxity gets punished by the gilt market," a constraint that likely disciplines pace rather than preventing change. UK core CPI printed below expectations in W25, and the MPC voted to hold Bank Rate with Chief Economist Huw Pill the sole dissenter; rate-cut expectations have firmed as falling oil prices reduce tightening pressure, though the heatwave-driven energy price surge — electricity prices reaching their highest since the 2022 crisis — introduces a near-term complication. Bloomberg Economics' ten-year Brexit assessment estimated the departure has cost between 2% and 4% of GDP, with a central estimate of 2.5% equating to roughly £30 billion in forgone annual tax revenue.

**Corporate and Structural Developments**
UniCredit crossing the 30% ownership threshold in Commerzbank during W23 effectively triggered a formal takeover process under German law; the path to completion of the €38.6 billion bid remains genuinely uncertain given historical German political resistance, but momentum is real. BMW's profit warning crystallizes the auto sector's simultaneous exposure to softening Chinese demand, Middle East supply-chain disruption costs, and an accelerating structural shift to EVs where Chinese manufacturers hold a cost advantage — a triple headwind with no near-term resolution. Europe recorded its worst heatwave on record in W26, with temperatures 5°C–12°C above seasonal averages, forcing French nuclear curtailments and disrupting Rhine barge traffic below half capacity — supply-side risks that may weigh on July activity data.
