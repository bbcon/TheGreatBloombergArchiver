A Civilization Survives

These have been 24 eventful hours in the annals of war and peace. They dawned 
in the US with the leader of the free world threatening to commit genocide. 
This is what President Donald Trump saidon Truth Social 
<https://bloom.bg/4e9P2oh> early Tuesday:

Words fail. However, a little more than an hour before his deadline came a 
flurry of activity. Mediator Pakistan asked for a cessation, and then Trump 
<https://bloom.bg/41l0l5v> called off the scheduled bombing and announced a 
two-week ceasefire to start once Iran reopened the Strait of Hormuz. Markets 
responded to the hard fact that civilization would not be killed off and oil 
prices tumbled. Then came critical confirmation from Iran’s leadership.They 
really have agreed <https://bloom.bg/3Qr52Z9> to the ceasefire. Talks under 
Pakistan’s aegis are slated for Friday. A little later, it filtered out that 
Iran still intended to charge for passage through the international waterway.

Compared to what had seemed possible when the American day started, this was 
great news and the oil market responded accordingly:

Putting the events in recent context, this was West Texas Intermediate’s 
biggest daily fall since the pandemic:

Going further back, the market is pricing this as a truly historic 
development. These are the 10 biggest daily declines in the Brent crude 
contract since 1990. Only two selloffs, during the Covid-19 chaos in 2020 and 
in January 1991 when allied forces entered Kuwait in Operation Desert Storm 
<https://bloom.bg/3OryHRq>, have been greater:

It’s encouraging that the oil shock resolved most satisfactorily for the 
capitalist world, rather than like theYom Kippur War <https://bloom.bg/41lrdSU> 
of 1973 and the disastrous embargo that followed.

When Iraq’s Saddam Hussein invaded and was later expelled from Kuwait by 
US-led forces in 1990-91, oil more than doubled and then returned to where it 
started in less than 12 months. So far, this shock has followed much the same 
contours:

The initial  market reaction <https://bloom.bg/4vi75im> is treating the 
ceasefire as a moment like the start of Desert Storm, when the resolution 
suddenly became clear. If that comes to pass, it will have arrived quicker this 
time, without putting the world through the extremes of the Kuwait crisis.

But there is reason for caution. Oct. 22, 1990 saw what at the time was the 
biggest drop in the oil price in history, as traders grew in confidence — 
driven in part byreports of a dream that Saddam had had 
<https://bloom.bg/4mjJdH9> — that war could be avoided. They proved wrong, and 
the price spike had further to go.

This selloff seems better founded. The key point is that Iran was thought 
highly unlikely to agree to any ceasefire. Letting ships through the Strait 
again surrenders their greatest leverage, and gives the rest of the world the 
chance to restock and prepare for a potentially more drawn-out conflict ahead. 
It also gives US military forces, not all of which are yet in position, the 
time to reach the Gulf.

Given the tolerance for pain the regime had already demonstrated, along with 
willingness to use lethal force against its own citizens, the consensus was 
that the leadership would not allow Trump to claim any victory without putting 
US lives at risk in a ground operation. Any suggestion that this is wrong, and 
that the Iranians may be more ready to sue for peace than thought, is directly 
positive for risk assets.

That has already shown in surging US equity futures. In early Tokyo trading, 
the Topix index is back to its highest point since war broke out on Feb. 28:

It also looks as though it’s been enough to break the upswing in the dollar as 
people had looked for a haven:

These are all reasonable snap judgments. Now the questions move on. The Strait 
remains the most important: Will Iran really allow ships to pass 
<https://bloom.bg/4cumbd8>, and if it succeeds in levying fees, could this 
legitimize de facto nationalization of the waterway? If so, it might be a 
face-saving “win” for the US, but would hasten the retreat from globalization 
into spheres of influence.

And are there terms to which both sides can agree? Iran doesn’t want this to 
happen again, and will want guarantees against future attacks from the US or 
Israel. That will be difficult. Does it get to re-enter the global economy with 
sanctions lifted? Who pays for the damage? Will the regime give up its nuclear 
program, and how can this be policed?

Last month, Points of Return warned that the question was no longer TACO 
(Trump Always Chickens Out) but WACO (Will the Ayatollahs Chicken Out?) 
<https://bloom.bg/4e81S6n>. That remains the case. The theocracy is more 
willing to compromise than thought, which is a big deal. But the rest of the 
world, and a protracted recovery for risk assets, is still in the uncomfortable 
position of relying on the leaders in Tehran.

For the next 24 hours as markets get the chance to react to this news, let the 
good times roll. No civilizations have ended tonight.

Inflation

It’s not long since inflation was the political issue that decided the last 
presidential election, and we’re about to learn a lot about where it is now 
<https://bloom.bg/4mgbLkB>. US personal consumption price expenditure data for 
February (a month in arrears, but the number the Federal Reserve targets) is 
due Thursday, followed by the consumer price index for March on Friday. It’s a 
given that last month’s leap in gas prices will have pushed up inflation, and 
much still depends on how long supply disruptions persist — but this will have 
great significance as the first chance to assess the war’s initial damage to 
prices.

What is beyond doubt is that the headline number will rise. That’s what 
happens when the year-on-year rise in a major index component like gasoline 
<https://bloom.bg/41S0YUf> goes from zero to more than 25% in barely a month:

Headline inflation (including fuel) will therefore almost certainly return 
above the 3% upper range of the Fed’s target. It has never fallen back to the 
desired 2% after the post-Covid spike. If economists polled by Bloomberg are 
right, this will be the highest print in two years — but still nothing serious 
compared to 2022:

The question for central bankers is whether the jump in gas prices will 
contaminate consumers’ expectations for inflation further into the future. So 
far, they will find the evidence encouraging. The latest New York Fed 
<https://bloom.bg/4bXVptr> survey shows consumer expectations for inflation 
three years hence barely budging over the last month (while forecasts for the 
next year naturally rose a bit):

Markets seem intent on rerunning the script from last year’s Trump-driven 
inflationary shock. The response to the tariffs was to raise one-year inflation 
swaps far more than two-year swaps, reflecting a belief that the impact would 
be purely transitory <https://bloom.bg/4sqoR0f> and pass out of the numbers 
after a year. By the end of 2025, the one- and two-year swaps were identical 
again. This time, once more, the market is positioned for a rise in inflation 
that dwindles after a year:

Longer term expectations, which should be more important to central bankers, 
suggest almost total calm. The five-year/five-year breakeven rate, giving the 
bond market’s prediction of the average inflation over the five-year period 
that starts five years hence, has dropped a bit in the US and remains below 
2.2%. It’s not true of the euro zone, where breakevens have risen but are at 
this point below 2%. This is markedly different from the period around the 
Ukraine invasion four years ago, which prompted a swift rise in long-term 
expectations:

The Ukraine invasion came when the inflation shock was taking shape, and 
there’s far less stimulus in the pipeline now than at the tail-end of the 
pandemic. But it’s still surprising to see this supply disruption taken so 
calmly, as it’s widely accepted to be far greater than in 2022.

While expectations remain calm, central bankers can also relax. At some point 
in the next few weeks, the Senate will decide whether to ratify the nominee 
Kevin Warsh <https://bloom.bg/4slgVxi> as the next chairman of the Fed. He’s 
due to start next month under the presumption that he will err on the side of 
cutting rates if he possibly can — despite past history as a hawk. Europe, 
which is more directly exposed to the conflict, appears less well-anchored and 
the gap between expected policy rates by the end of this year has narrowed 
significantly. With inflation more serious in the UK, the fed funds rate is now 
expected to be well below the Bank of England’s Bank Rate by year-end:

All else equal, this should weaken the dollar, which has strengthened during 
the war. But these assumptions will come in for their first major test at the 
end of this week with an initial look at March data. Meanwhile, the Institute 
of Supply Managers’ survey of non-manufacturing companies, responsible for the 
bulk of the US economy, offered what could be an alarm signal. More than 70% of 
purchasing managers complained that the input prices they were paying were 
rising. Outside of the 2021-22 spike, that hadn’t happened since the immediate 
aftermath of the Global Financial Crisis; it was the biggest month-on-month 
rise for this measure in 13 years. Over time, this measure has a strong 
tendency to rise and fall with inflation excluding shelter, so it could bode 
ill for hopes that inflation stays contained:

This looks alarming. Florian Ielpo of Lombard Odier Investment Management 
suggests that the survey’s combination of weakening employment and rising 
inflation “increasingly resembles stagflationary conditions” — although this 
should be counterbalanced by strong new orders.

Even with a ceasefire, it will take a while for the fog of war around markets 
to dissipate. The progress of inflation, and the new Fed’s response to it, will 
be vital in determining markets’ progress in what we can all hope will be a 
postwar environment.
