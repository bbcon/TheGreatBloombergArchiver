Struggling to stay cool

By Laura Millan 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASzdyi2b6bE/laura-millan?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>

The streets of Madrid’s Puente de Vallecas district had emptied out by 4 p.m. 
on a recent June afternoon. It was siesta time, when shops close and people 
retreat home to avoid temperatures approaching 40C (104F). But Meqrane Sahraui 
remained outside, huddled in the shade by a playground with two of his four 
children. Without a permanent home, they’d been sleeping on the streets of the 
Spanish capital for over two weeks.

The family quietly drank cool water, waiting for the shade to reach the swings 
and slides. They were shaken from the night before, when their 14-year-old son 
had been hospitalized with a fever. Sahraui, an unemployed construction worker 
of Algerian origin, found a friend who could take the boy in to recover and 
cool down. Sahraui blamed days of exposure to a brutal heat wave.

“His head started aching all the way to the eye because of the heat, because 
we’re directly exposed. We’re outside all the time,” Sahraui, 43, said. “We’re 
buying 10 bags of ice a day; there’s nothing else we can do.”

Central and Western Europe experienced their worst heat wave on record in late 
June,testing the resilience 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/europe-faces-weeks-of-extreme-heat-as-fire-and-power-risks-build?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
 of homes, schools, infrastructure and healthcare systems. More than 7,000 
people died from heat exposure last month inGermany 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/germany-recorded-5-000-excess-deaths-in-late-june-heat-wave?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
, Spain and France, according to preliminary estimates. This week, a second 
heat wave again pushed temperatures above 40C in parts of Spain.
Jonatan Borrero, 40, and his son wait for neighbors in Nueva Numancia to come 
down and play water games at the Cerro del Tío Pío park.Photographer: Scarlett 
Casciello for Bloomberg
Children are particularly vulnerable: Along with the very old, they face the 
highest risk of heat-related hospitalization, according to a 2024study 
<https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC11110655/> of admissions in Spain. 
Kids are less able to regulate their body temperature by sweating, less likely 
to recognize signs of dehydration and more likely to spend time outdoors, 
leaving them prone to heatstroke. Symptoms can include dizziness, nausea and 
loss of consciousness, and severe cases can cause neurological damage or death, 
especially among newborns.

“We see everything from first-degree burns on children’s skin from solar 
exposure to patients with chronic illness, like epilepsy, that have more 
frequent attacks,” says Ester Armela, an emergency room doctor and the head of 
the Madrid region’s emergency services system SUMMA 112.

There are other effects as well, says Isabel Martínez, an associate doctor at 
La Paz Hospital in Madrid and a member of the environmental health committee at 
the Spanish Pediatrics Association. “We know temperatures above 26C or 27C can 
negatively impact children’s ability to concentrate, memorize and think,” she 
says.

Madrid’s residents are used to temperatures considered high by European 
standards, and about 65% of homes are equipped with air conditioning,according 
to housing marketplace Idealista 
<https://www.idealista.com/news/inmobiliario/vivienda/2024/07/04/818081-el-41-del-parque-de-viviendas-en-espana-tiene-aire-acondicionado>
, compared with just7% of UK homes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/london-s-expats-struggle-to-adapt-to-extreme-heat-without-ac?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
. But the recent heat waves have taken a toll on the city, especially on 
parents of young children, making Madrid a preview of the challenges ahead for 
other cities on the world’s fastest-warming continent. As a mother of two young 
children in Madrid, I’ve already learned to factor the heat into every aspect 
of my life.

“Spain is adapted to hotter conditions, but things are only getting worse,” 
says Blanca Paniello-Castillo, a researcher at the Barcelona Institute for 
Global Health.
Slides, swings and other playground features in Madrid’s Cerro del Tío Pío 
park heat up under the sun on a late-June afternoon.Photographer: Scarlett 
Casciello for Bloomberg
In Madrid the oppressiveness of summer is not declared by forecasters but by 
the plastic water sprayers that mothers and children carry everywhere as soon 
as temperatures begin to rise. During heat waves, pediatricians and 
public-health officials recommend that children stay hydrated and avoid playing 
outdoors. But as the hot season starts earlier and lasts longer, even going to 
school, which ends in the third week of June and resumes in the second week of 
September, can become a hazard. In 2017, 47 children suffered heat strokes in a 
school in Madrid. Since then, parents have periodically staged demonstrations 
across Spain calling for better insulation and air conditioning in schools.

In June, Greenpeace released research showing some classroom desks in Madrid 
reached 35C and playground swings 38C. The measurements were taken on days 
without official heat alerts, suggesting that even routine summer temperatures 
can pose risks to students, says Elvira Jiménez, head of climate adaptation at 
Greenpeace in Spain.

Part of the problem is that few schools have air conditioning and school 
playgrounds are often patches of cement with very little shade. “Every other 
government-owned building in the country, from healthcare centers to offices, 
has air conditioning — but not schools,” Jiménez says. “There’s an 
incomprehensible double standard with children.”

Wealthier Madrileños who can work remotely and own a second home somewhere 
cooler increasingly leave the city for the entire summer. But for most working 
parents, the only option is to send their children to urban summer camps, often 
in the same schools they attend during the year. That means spending up to 
eight hours a day in buildings that were already too hot in June and September.

Better models

By Akshat Rathi 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUdOobfNzkQ/akshat-rathi?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
 andSummer Maxwell 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AYSnifPVQIs/summer-maxwell?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>

As back-to-back heat waves batter the US 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-03/sweltering-heat-dome-threatens-us-holiday-events-and-power-grids?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
 andEurope 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/europe-faces-weeks-of-extreme-heat-as-fire-and-power-risks-build?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
, scientific models linking climate change to extreme weather are increasingly 
important for policymakers and commercial enterprises like insurers. But a 
growing political backlash aims to undermine their credibility.

Climate scientists can now quickly assess how much worse a given 
extreme-weather event has been made by human-caused climate change. Attribution 
science, as it’s known, has existedsince the early 2000s 
<https://www.carbonbrief.org/qa-the-evolving-science-of-extreme-weather-attribution/>
, but improved computational resources and scientific understanding in recent 
years have made the findings more useful to policymakers.
Pedestrians during a heatwave in the French Riviera city of Nice on July 6.
Photographer:Valery Hache/AFP/Getty Images
Some studies can now be completed even as a weather event is ongoing. During 
the heat wave that hit the eastern US earlier this month, researchers with 
World Weather Attribution found the extreme combination of high temperatures 
and high humidity would have been “virtually impossible 
<https://www.worldweatherattribution.org/fossil-fuels-are-heating-americas-250th-birthday/>
” without the greenhouse gases released by burning fossil fuels.

“This is a very mature science now,” said Mingfang Ting, professor of climate 
science at Columbia University, speaking on Bloomberg’sZero podcast 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/super-el-nino-will-give-us-a-glimpse-of-life-in-2035?cmpid=BBD071026_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6410>
. “How likely an event would be to happen in a world without fossil fuels, 
versus with fossil fuels? We run models with those two conditions and then get 
a probability of the event happening,” she said.

As attribution science becomes more precise, some academic experts 
<https://voelkerrechtsblog.org/on-the-science-coloured-glasses-of-the-icj/> 
believe the research will be increasinglyused in court 
<https://www.lse.ac.uk/granthaminstitute/wp-content/uploads/2026/06/Constructing-the-causal-chain_final.pdf>
 to hold companies and governments financially liable for the damage caused by 
climate change.
