Multiple waves of supply chain disruptions over the past decade have resulted 
in a couple main lessons — among them, the need for resilience against shocks 
and multiple options in case one source proves unreliable.

Both are being taught again in the Persian Gulf region, where the closure of 
the Strait of Hormuz over nearly four months is constricting not just energy 
but consumer and industrial goods that flow through the region’s ports and 
free-trade zones, like those operated byDP World 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-07/dp-world-and-war-risk-insurance-for-cargo?cmpid=BBD061526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5490>
 in the United Arab Emirates.

World

 ending hostilities and set the stage for months of tough negotiations on the 
fine print.

Oil markets reacted with enthusiasm 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/hormuz-trade-will-take-months-to-return-to-normal-analysts-say?cmpid=BBD061526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5490>
 because the pactshould reopen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/us-and-iran-say-they-ve-agreed-deal-to-reopen-hormuz-this-week?cmpid=BBD061526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5490>
 Hormuz and restore trade flows. But that return to normal couldtake months 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/hormuz-trade-will-take-months-to-return-to-normal-analysts-say?cmpid=BBD061526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5490>
 given the number of vessels involved the the lack of clarity about a permanent 
solution, analysts warn.

Yet as Bloomberg reports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/iran-s-strait-of-hormuz-closure-turns-desert-roads-into-vital-trade-routes?cmpid=BBD061526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5490>
 on Monday, alternatives to maritime shipping through Saudi Arabia, Oman and 
the UAE have been quickly established first to ensure critical items like food 
and medicine reach their destination.

These trucking, rail and smaller port operators that’ve been busier lately 
can’t match all the capacity that’s been hit by the shipping blockages, but at 
least some may wind up being permanent ways to diversify supply chains and make 
them less susceptible to flareups of unrest.

“Disruption made us move faster,” said Farid Belbouab, CEO of Gulftainer, 
which operates the Khor Fakkan port in eastern UAE, just outside the Persian 
Gulf and the Hormuz strait. “Long term, the east coast isn’t a backup — it’s 
core to the UAE’s trade architecture.”
