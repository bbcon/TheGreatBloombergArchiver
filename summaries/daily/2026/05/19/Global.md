# Global Daily Briefing — May 19, 2026

**Iran Standoff and Energy Markets.** President Trump said he called off a planned strike on Iran after Saudi Arabia and other Gulf allies asked for more time to pursue diplomacy, though he offered no detail on targets or terms beyond a demand for "no nuclear weapons." Brent slipped 1.8% to around $110/barrel and WTI eased to $108 on the news, but the relief was modest given that the Strait of Hormuz remains restricted. Washington issued a fresh monthlong waiver permitting sales of Russian crude already loaded on tankers, an unusual concession aimed at containing fuel costs. Knock-on effects are widening: Singapore's state LNG buyer has secured non-Middle East cargoes through year-end, Indian carriers are lobbying refiners to delay jet fuel hikes, and Ryanair's CEO warned of corporate casualties among European airlines if the chokepoint persists.

**Bond Markets Buckle Globally.** The energy shock is compounding a synchronized sovereign bond selloff. US 10-year yields touched 4.63%, the highest since February, with 30-year yields approaching post-2007 peaks; Citi flagged 5.5% as the next technical level. Japanese super-long yields hit fresh records following PM Takaichi's supplementary fiscal package, and UK gilts are pricing political risk around a potential Burnham premiership. The drivers extend beyond oil — sticky inflation, AI-related capex, deteriorating fiscal trajectories and aging demographics are reinforcing one another. Kevin Warsh's Fed swearing-in Friday adds another variable: any dovish tilt risks accelerating the selloff rather than easing financial conditions.

**Putin-Xi in Beijing.** Putin arrived in Beijing seeking to leverage Middle East energy disruption into better terms on the Power of Siberia 2 pipeline. The asymmetry is pronounced — Sunday's record Ukrainian drone strike on Moscow underscored Russia's vulnerabilities, while Xi, fresh from his summit with Trump, can afford to negotiate firmly. Progress on gas pricing remains unconfirmed.

**Climate.** Scientists have formally retired the RCP8.5 worst-case warming scenario as implausible given policy progress and clean-tech maturation, a technical revision Trump characterized as vindication. BloombergNEF's New Energy Outlook now projects 2.4C warming by 2050 under current trajectories, with solar set to become the largest single power source by 2032.
