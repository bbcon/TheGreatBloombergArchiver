The Strait of Hormuz remains the central fault line in global markets. Three weeks into the double-blockade, President Trump announced "Project Freedom," a plan to guide neutral vessels through the waterway — but the initiative has left shipping executives confused given a lack of operational detail and the US Navy's stated non-involvement. The head of Iran's parliamentary National Security Commission characterized any US interference as a violation of the existing ceasefire. Oil prices fluctuated sharply on the mixed signals, with WTI touching $106 per barrel in some trading windows. Brent futures for end-2026 reached new highs, and 2027 contracts remain elevated, suggesting markets are not pricing a near-term resolution. Global LNG exports fell to their lowest level in nearly two years in April, while Dubai International Airport reported a 66% year-on-year drop in March passenger traffic.

The US has emerged as the world's largest crude exporter once again, shipping over 250 million barrels in the past nine weeks to compensate for constrained Middle Eastern supply. Analysts are beginning to flag limits to this cushion. OPEC+ major producers agreed on a modest symbolic increase of 188,000 barrels per day for June, largely a signaling exercise following the UAE's surprise exit from the bloc. Abu Dhabi's ADNOC simultaneously announced $55 billion in accelerated project awards.

The geopolitical contest for influence in the Caucasus is intensifying, with the US signing a 99-year agreement with Armenia for management of a transit corridor linking Azerbaijan to Nakhchivan — a move that directly challenges Russian influence in a region Moscow has historically dominated. Turkey, Iran, and the EU are also asserting competing interests. For middle powers globally, the dilemma is acute: the Hormuz shock is delivering an immediate, visible economic hit through energy prices, inflation, and disrupted supply chains, while the longer-run challenge of Chinese industrial overcapacity feels slower and more diffuse — even as Beijing's sanctions defiance raises the stakes of the structural US-China contest ahead of their anticipated summit.
