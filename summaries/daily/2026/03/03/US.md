US financial markets entered a period of significant stress on Tuesday as the fourth day of the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran concentrated investor attention on the intersection of geopolitical risk and domestic inflation dynamics. S&P 500 futures fell 1.88%, extending Monday's session in which the main indexes had held roughly flat — a relative calm now appearing fragile. The more durable market signal came from the bond market, where the 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.104%, climbing six basis points on the session, and briefly recording its largest single-day decline in price since the tariff-driven turmoil of last April.

The catalyst for renewed rate anxiety predates the conflict but is now amplified by it. The ISM manufacturing survey for February showed the prices-paid component surging to its highest level since 2022, a reading that surprised to the upside by the widest margin on record. Respondents were pointed in their frustration, with one manufacturer noting that domestic steel and aluminum tariffs were simultaneously raising input costs and compressing demand — precisely the opposite of their intended effect. Former Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen, speaking at an industry conference, said the Iran conflict leaves the Federal Reserve "even more on hold," a view that aligns with market pricing, which has continued to pare rate-cut expectations.

JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon captured the prevailing mood among institutional leaders, cautioning that market exuberance does not match what he described as an economy that is merely "fine," and flagging higher inflation as a potential spoiler. The Trump administration indicated it has no immediate plans to tap the Strategic Petroleum Reserve in response to surging crude prices, despite WTI rising more than 8% to nearly $77 a barrel. On the corporate side, MongoDB shares sank 27% following a weaker-than-expected revenue forecast, dragging down enterprise software names broadly. Blackstone's flagship private credit fund faced a record redemption request equivalent to 7.9% of shares, approximately $3.8 billion, the latest indicator of mounting stress in that asset class.
