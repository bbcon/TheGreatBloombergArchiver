Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Alex Longley discovers that oil traders’ love of volatility has its 
limits.

At the oil industry’s annual gathering in London this week, the traders and 
executives who descended on the bars and restaurants of the Mayfair district 
were in an unusual mood.

People who buy and sell oil for a living typically say that volatility is good 
— large price swings are opportunities to make money. But 2026 has had such a 
wild start, with an extraordinary number of factors moving prices from one day 
to the next.

US military activity near Venezuela and Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/trump-says-he-sees-iran-talks-resolving-over-the-next-month?cmpid=BBBXT021326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
; disruption to supplies fromKazakhstan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-31/kazakhstan-starts-gradual-restoration-of-output-at-tengiz-field?cmpid=BBBXT021326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
; global financial flows betting on debasement of the dollar; atrade deal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-06/trump-removes-india-s-25-russia-related-tariff-after-deal?cmpid=BBBXT021326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 between the US and India that appears to be stranding Russian crude at sea.

There’s been a new driver of prices almost every day. 

As one veteran analyst said: January 2026 may end up being the perfect case 
study of all the various factors that can possibly move the price of crude.

Analyzing global oil markets has rarely been harder. You can add up all the 
supply and demand in the world, but in such a volatile environment you might 
not see the impact on prices you’d expect.

Many traders are still bearish. With all the geopolitical tensions, they argue 
that the current Brent crude price of $70 a barrel is a sign of how much supply 
is out there. But they also see big risks.

“If you don’t go through this period where you have something new happening 
all the time, I think oil could potentially weaken,” Greg Sharenow, who helps 
manage nearly $20 billion as head of Pimco’s commodity portfolio investment 
team, said this week.

Those who are more bullish point to the build-up 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/top-oil-traders-say-sanctions-are-starting-to-lift-crude-prices?cmpid=BBBXT021326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 of sanctioned Russian and Iranian crude on tankers. If you look only at 
unsanctioned barrels, these traders say there’s little sign of the 
much-discussedglobal surplus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/oil-stockpiles-swelled-last-year-by-most-since-2020-iea-says?cmpid=BBBXT021326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

With all this oil held at sea and the daily rate to hire a tanker already 
sky-high, supply chains may be stretched to breaking point, forcing sanctioned 
producers to shut down some output.

Nobody is particularly confident about what will happen next.

“The uncertainty band is wider than we would have thought six or 10 weeks 
ago,” said Sharenow.

—Alex Longley, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

first time in 2025, according to BloombergNEF. A policy‑led rush in the first
