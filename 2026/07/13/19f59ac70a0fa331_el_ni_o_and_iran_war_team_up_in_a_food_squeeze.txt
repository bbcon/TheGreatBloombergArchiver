Pricey Combo

Investors wanted June’s ceasefire in the Gulf to hold. Iran’s latest closure 
of the Strait of Hormuz shows how fragile the truce always was, and prompted a 
5% rally in Brent crudeat Monday’s opening <https://bloom.bg/3RdGGCZ>. Reaching 
$79 per barrel, it was 12.7% from its low set earlier this month. Butfood 
shocks can be even more damaging <https://bloom.bg/4gAZZR5>, and fertilizer is 
possibly the greatest casualty of this war. There could be far-reaching 
repercussions for global food security. The Strait is a vital conduit for urea, 
whose price this year has mimicked crude oil:

Before this weekend's announcement of the Strait’s full closure,  key 
fertilizer benchmarks <https://bloom.bg/4pjJdbu> had reacted relatively calmly 
compared with the sharp surge in March. That reinforced the view that investors 
either expected the latest flare-up to prove of short duration, or believed 
underlying demand remained constrained, particularly outside the peak 
fertilizer application season:

It grows ever harder to view this latest outburst as short-lived, which raises 
the question of why markets are remaining so calm. Marko Papic of BCA Research 
suggests that they no longer regard the Strait as a binary issue (either closed 
or open), which limits the reaction:

Our framework was the 1980s Tanker Wars, a period of four years during which 
warfare in the Persian Gulf was a continued reality, but oil got through. As 
such, geopolitical risk in the Strait of Hormuz is not binary – either 1 or a 0 
– but rather a continuum.

That leaves open the question of how much pain lies ahead for farmers. Could a 
seriously disrupted Strait drive prices back toward their levels after Russia 
invaded Ukraine in 2022?

Other factors are at play. Bloomberg Intelligence’s Daniel Cole points out 
that nitrogen prices are falling, fueled by slower demand in many markets and 
the launch of summer fill programs to ease pressure in the US. But Gulf 
disruption is a problem:

Prior to last week’s developments, we were generally seeing the global market 
adjust to the events in the Middle East, with prices falling from the April 
spike for most nitrogen products. NOLA [New Orleans] urea hit a high of $780 in 
mid-April. So even though it’s bounced a little this week, we’re nowhere near 
the high we saw this spring.

A return to 2022 highs appears unlikely, but the two disruptions share 
similarities: Major fertilizer regions were drawn into conflict at the same 
time of year, just ahead of a key application season. There’s one crucial 
difference — the 2022 surge was also driven by soaring input costs, 
particularly European natural-gas prices as measured by the TTF benchmark. 
There’s been no repetition:

This year’s more limited surge could still have a serious effect on food 
prices. Inflationary pressures are elevated enough that the Federal Open Market 
Committee contemplated a rate hike at its most recent meeting. Fertilizer 
prices rose more than 30% early in the conflict, just as farmers needed to buy, 
and some of those higher costs could eventually feed through to grocery bills.

So far, food prices have remained contained. The Bloomberg Agriculture 
Subindex, dominated by major fertilizer-consuming crops such as corn, wheat, 
soybeans, and rice, remains far below its 2022 peak, doing little to encourage 
stronger fertilizer demand. Mediocre crop prices leave growers unwilling to pay 
up for fertilizer, softening demand when supply is disrupted:

Extreme weather associated with El Niño <https://bloom.bg/4eXTmqT> poses a 
greater threat. Oxford Economics’ Lucila Bonilla notes that economies in 
Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa face the greatest risk due to their 
exposure to rice and maize yield losses, the large share of food in their 
consumer price indices, and the pass-through from global grain prices:

The main channel through which El Niño affects economies is domestic supply 
disruption rather than a global commodity price shock. Thin grain trade and 
regional rice markets mean the impact on inflation will depend on where crops 
fail, where imports come from, and whether governments absorb the shock through 
subsidies and price controls or let more of it pass through to consumer prices.

But if the war in Iran drags on and fertilizer prices remain elevated while El 
Niño weighs on crop yields, food inflation could prove stubborn. It’s now a 
political issue, even in the US, where consumers have noticed that prices never 
came down after their post-pandemic surge (on the terminal, open this chart in 
GP to view on a log scale):

There’s little or nothing central banks can do about this, but it would make 
it all the harder for them to ease monetary policy. It’s not just oil prices 
that have central bankers’ eyes turned anxiously toward the Gulf.

—Richard Abbey

Big in Japan

Japan wants its big money managers to invest more at home, and it’s going to 
take measures to make them do it <https://bloom.bg/4pnpF6g>. That was the very 
surprising announcement Friday from Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama. It 
directly included the nation’s enormous $1.6 trillion General Pension 
Investment Fund, and had an immediate impact on the bond market. Not since the 
shock of the Liberation Day tariffs last year has the 10-year Japanese 
government bond dropped so much:

It was time for something to reverse JGB yields, as they’d risen far ahead of 
the rest of the world in recent months. Thirty-year yields exceed even 
equivalent German bunds, even though the European Central Bank has raised rates 
far more aggressively in the years since the post-pandemic inflation surge:

Why have Japanese yields behaved like this? Fiscal policy is one explanation. 
Even with recent efforts at rearmament, Germany runs a tight ship. Japan has 
the highest debt load in proportion to its gross domestic product of any 
country in the world. But it can also be read as frustration with the Bank of 
Japan’s slow tightening. To quote Albert Edwards of Societe Generale SA:

Most market commentators believe that the BOJ is tightening too slowly and so 
the yield curve has steepened sharply – despite continued QE support. The 
financial markets are sending a message much like the crowd at a rowdy football 
match chanting in frustration at their own team’s manager: “You don’t know what 
you’re doing!”

And indeed, as Edward shows, the rise in long Japanese yields has been extreme:
Source: Societe Generale SA 
This has been driven in large part by big outflows from foreign investors, and 
has contributed to the record weakness of the yen. So it makes sense that the 
government is trying to prod domestic investors to bring money back:

The GPIF itself has contributed by moving its big Japanese bond holdings 
toward shorter maturities (and thereby pressing longer yields upward). Bank of 
America reports that the weighted average maturity of its top 15 holdings 
declined from 9.6 years in March 2024 to 8.5 years a year later, to 7.3 years 
this March, the end of the most recent fiscal year.

Could this move the yen? Deutsche Bank’s FX strategist George Saravelos argues 
that the main problem remains low short-term yields compared to everyone else, 
and that the yen can strengthen either via the BOJ hiking rates to 2% much more 
quickly than currently priced (overnight index swaps currently put rates at 
1.2% at year-end), or by “a more concerted effort to shift domestic investment 
back onshore.” It’s not surprising that they are resorting to the latter, and 
Saravelos warns that the sharp fall in the yen afterthe 2014 move by the GPIF 
to shift into stocks <https://bloom.bg/4h7H7t4> shows that a change in domestic 
flow expectations can generate big moves in the currency even when policy takes 
longer to implement.

Brent Donnelly of SpectraMarkets said:

You know who controls the Japanese pension funds? The Japanese government. 
This is probably a watershed moment for the yen, but time will tell.

He predicted it would be more of a “slow turning of the ship” to start. But he 
identified the key broader issue, which is the steady weaponization of 
investment flows as the world retreats from globalization. The UK is another 
example of a country trying to find ways to get its investors to bring money 
home; the fallout from the fracturing of NATO, via European rearmament, likely 
means fewer European funds will go to the US.

It’s not at all certain that this gambit will turn things around for Japan, 
whose economy is riven with as many contradictions as ever. But it does seem to 
add to the inevitability, as the US currently accounts for some two-thirds of 
global stocks by market capitalization, that the retreat from globalization 
will mean downward pressure on the dollar and US assets.



 Survival Tips 



Argentina and England meet in the World Cup semifinal on Wednesday. There’s 
history to this, and not just theFalklands (or Malvinas) war of 1982 
<https://bloom.bg/4w1bGpa> — which did at least bring down a particularly nasty 
military regime and usher in democracy for Argentina. It will pit Lionel Messi (
eight goals <https://bloom.bg/4h9OgZW> in the tournament) against Harry Kane 
and Jude Bellingham (six each). Either side could easily win — although the 
winner is unlikely to beat the victor of the other semifinal between France and 
Spain.
Confusion over Rattin’s sending-off led to the use of red and yellow cards. 
Photographer: Central Press/Hulton/Getty 
But it’s the pure sports rivalry that makes this a great occasion. This will 
be their sixth World Cup meeting, but the first since 2002. In 1962, Englandwon 
3-1 in Chile <https://bloom.bg/4gADeNi>, knocking Argentina out at the group 
stage in front of a crowd of 10,000. In 1966, the controversy started. They met 
in the quarterfinals, Argentina’s captainAntonio Rattin was sent off 
<https://bloom.bg/4vYy56e> at Wembley for no obvious reason, England won 1-0, 
and went on to take the Cup (with moredubious refereeing 
<https://bloom.bg/44rc97O> in the final <https://bloom.bg/4eYVBdy>). Rattin, 
who died this weekend at 89, had no idea why he was sent off, prompting the 
innovation of yellow and red cards in 1970. In 1986, they met in the 
quarterfinal andMaradona <https://bloom.bg/3SWNyoV> scored the “hand of God” 
<https://bloom.bg/4eYVCy8> (not easy to spot in real time), and then possibly 
the greatest goal ever <https://bloom.bg/4fbCbRA>on the way to winning the cup. 
Had his handball for the first goal been spotted,England will tell you 
<https://bloom.bg/4yk8UwF>, he would have been sent off and couldn’t have 
scored thesublime second <https://bloom.bg/4fazxLT>. 

Then came the 1998 round of 16, more great goals <https://bloom.bg/4feC5IS>, 
andDiego Simeone’s gamesmanship <https://bloom.bg/4pgLLqN> to goad a gauche 
young David Beckham toretaliate for a foul <https://bloom.bg/4heUDLs> and get 
himself sent off <https://bloom.bg/3Tvh8C2>. Argentina won on penalties and 
then lostone of the great quarterfinals <https://bloom.bg/4sDLoY9>. Simeone’s 
son is now in the Argentine team, while Beckham is ever-present in the 
commercial breaks. In 2002, England knocked Argentina out in the group stage 
with aredemptive Beckham penalty <https://bloom.bg/4vZHryV> in a dull game. 

This installment will be fun. Please can the better team win (even if it’s 
Argentina) without controversy. Have a great week everyone.
