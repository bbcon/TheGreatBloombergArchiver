Reversal Day

And breathe. Worries on several fronts have been suddenly assuaged. President 
Donald Trump isnot going to charge 20% <https://bloom.bg/4f29pE7> to take 
tankers through the Strait of Hormuz <https://bloom.bg/4pl0cdr>; he chickened 
out more quickly than usual and flipped his position within 24 hours. And not 
only is inflation falling, but headline prices are actually going down, and so 
are core prices. This means we can also reverse Monday’s warning from the 
Federal Reserve that it might raise rates later this month.

These events are related. It’s not often that the US consumer price index goes 
down from month to month, and it almost requires a big drop in the oil price:

But these were genuinely encouraging numbers. Decades can go by without 
month-on-month falls in the core index, excluding food and fuel, but it 
happened in June:

To show how broad-based the decline in price pressure was, there were falls in 
the year-on-year rate for all four main components of the index:

Tighter statistical analysis also confirmed this disinflation. The Fed’s 
“supercore” measure of services excluding housing fell, although it remains 
above 3%. The Atlanta Fed’s gauge of sticky price inflation, affecting goods 
and services whose prices are hard to move, dropped to its lowest since the 
pandemic; and the Cleveland Fed’s trimmed mean, excluding outliers, also dipped 
below 3%. After an alarming rise, the reflation trend cooled across the board:

Oxford Economics’ Bernard Yaros summarized the inflationary dogs that didn’t 
bark:

There are three inflationary forces that the Fed is on high alert for: 
tariffs, AI, and oil passthrough. Tariff effects were not discernible, while 
AI-related price pressures weren’t as evident as expected. There was some sign 
of oil passthrough to certain consumer goods, and this feedthrough process 
could take longer than expected, given recent events in the Middle East.

One problematic political issue — although politicians aren’t drawing 
attention to it — is immigration. Bill Adams, chief US economist at Fifth 
Third, argued:

Labor-intensive services provided by industries with large proportions of 
immigrant workers continued to see outsize price increases. Home healthcare 
services rose 10.7% on the year, nursing homes and adult day services 4.5%, and 
daycare and preschool 3.5%, all outpacing core CPI.

To capture the stresses in US society in microcosm, look at the contrast 
between fees for college tuition, still suffering disinflation as the middle 
class reacts to being priced out, and elderly care, growing far more expensive 
with less migrant help:

The market judgment on all of this <https://bloom.bg/455lTVu> continues to 
look overdone. One-year inflation swaps dropped below the Fed’s target of 2% 
for the first time since the 2024 election, suggesting that investors think 
Warsh is on course for a swift victory:

And there was also a sharp turn in expectations for a hike later this month, 
which surged Monday after Fed Governor Christopher Waller warned this was 
possible. Tuesday’s drop was, according to Deutsche Bank, the second-largest 
drop in pricing for the next Fed meeting after a CPI release since 2008. With 
Warsh determined not to pre-commit, expect fluctuations like this to become 
more common:

The consequent drop in yields spurred further recovery in stocks, although 
gains were somewhat muted, with the S&P 500 gaining only 0.38% for the day. 
June’s major fall for stocks relative to bonds, however, is over. Like the hits 
from the Liberation Day tariffs and Iran war, the market has recovered from its 
alarm over chipmakers:

There are a number of reasons why the market isn’t more excited. First of all, 
Trump’s reversal on a 20% toll still left the future of oil flows through the 
Strait of Hormuz in jeopardy. The oil price did dip, but only briefly:

With oil prices bouncing back, July isn’t going to be another month of 
negative inflation, and the most likely next move in the fed funds rate 
continues to be upward. However measured, inflation remains too high for the 
central bank to feel comfortable cutting. Kevin Warsh made that clear in 
testimony to Congress, when he pointedly declined an invitation to declare 
“mission accomplished.” Stephen Brown, US economist at Capital Economics, 
argued:

The AI investment boom, coupled with signs of rebounding consumer demand, will 
continue to keep core inflation above target. We still think it’s a matter of 
when, rather than if, the Fed will raise interest rates.

Others disagree, but one month’s undeniably encouraging inflation data still 
leaves prices rising far faster than the Fed’s target, amid geopolitical risk 
that is worsening again. This is unlikely to be the last reversal day of 2026.

Surprise Surprise <https://bloom.bg/4pFpm6Z> 

The second-quarter earnings season is off to a strong start. Wall Street’s 
biggest bankscomfortably exceeded optimistic expectations 
<https://bloom.bg/4vv8vVy>. JPMorgan Chase, Bank of America, Citigroup, Wells 
Fargo, and Goldman Sachs all rose to the occasion, revealing that they had 
feasted on trading profits in the quarter. Anxiety generated by artificial 
intelligence and the Iran war translated into volatility, which the bankers 
were able to turn into fantastic revenues from trading.

The biggest US bank, JPMorgan Chase, reported its highest quarterly profit on 
record, buoyed by a stellar trading-desk performance and the payoff from a 
long-held stake in Visa. That seemed excellent, as Jamie Dimon’s bank generated 
arecord $6 billion from equity trading <https://bloom.bg/4yorJyN> — but rival 
Goldman Sachs posted an even bigger $7.42 billion, the highest quarterly 
trading haul ever recorded by a Wall Street bank. To quote Dimon, “The markets 
are booming right now. It’s getting close to as good as it gets.” On that 
basis, the surge in the KBW Bank Index to record highs is understandable — but 
ifDimon is warning that this could be close to the peak 
<https://bloom.bg/4pnZHPN>, he should be taken seriously:

It’s not just about earnings. The recovery in valuations is also impressive, 
with the index’s price-to-book multiple, the standard way to value banks, at a 
post-Global Financial Crisis high:

Outperformance wasn’t uniform. Citigroup’s equities trading revenue jumped 
more than 45% to $2.3 billion, but investors weren’t satisfied.Jane Fraser’s 
bank fell <https://bloom.bg/4vPqbeV> as much as 5.9% as concerns grew over 
whetherCiti <https://bloom.bg/44woM1p> could close the gap in equity trading 
revenue with its larger rivals.

The selloff underscored the stakes for Citigroup, as four its five main 
divisions — banking, services, markets, and wealth — surpassed analysts’ 
estimates compiled by Bloomberg. Earnings per share exceeded all 20 estimates. 
But Citi needs to turn around equities trading to keep its shareholders happy. 
Chief Financial Officer Gonzalo Luchetti’s admission that “we have some work to 
do in scaling it. It will take a little bit of time to play out,” suggests the 
turnaround remains a work in progress.

For the broader sector, it makes sense to question how long this earnings 
momentum can last in a financial landscape rattled by the growing use of 
digital assets and fears of AI disruption. Bank of America’s Ebrahim Poonawala 
suggests that bank stocks have emerged as an island of stability, but the 
question is whether investors rotating out of chip stocks will use them as a 
vehicle to maintain exposure to the US economy.
Is Dimon suggesting a peak is near? Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg 
They will certainly find it hard to keep up their present performance without 
profiting from the technology-driven investment cycle. In the first quarter 
alone, demand for external capital surged as the race to build AI 
infrastructure pushed combined net equity and corporate bond issuance to a 
record annualized $3.24 trillion. This creates an opportunity to boost banking 
returns in the classic fashion, by lending more.

Gavekal Research’s Tan Kai Xian contends that US banks can lend directly to 
companies to finance their AI-related capex, earning interest income, while 
capital raises and IPOs can boost fee income:

Xian argues that yields can rise further without derailing capex, providing 
returns on invested capital remain above the cost of capital.June’s CPI surprise
 <https://bloom.bg/4f29uaT>, postponing any Fed rate hike, should ease pressure 
to raise deposit rates. As deposit growth this year is outpacing the expansion 
in commercial bank lending and securities holdings — illustrated in the Gavekal 
chart below — this looks like strong evidence that banks have capacity to 
extend more credit:

Ultimately, the banking industry is more resilient than many had expected. 
With a strong economy, classic opportunities to make money from trading, and 
steadier funding costs, the environment is the friendliest in years. That 
doesn’t guarantee another leg higher. While the US economy continues to grow, 
the burden of proof rests less with the banks than with investors still 
searching for reasons to doubt them.

—Richard Abbey


Big Blue Yonder

One other problem remains: Investors aren’t clear how to gauge the winners and 
losers from AI, and that is creating spectacular volatility in the stocks most 
affected.IBM <https://bloom.bg/4vrdo21>, which once held the title of biggest 
US company, released results that the market found disappointing, and was 
rewarded with the first daily fall of more than 25% in its long history:

Meanwhile, American Depositary Receipts in SK Hynix <https://bloom.bg/4yC7UUW>
, the Korean chipmaker now valued more highly than IBM ever was, went into 
overdrive. The ADRs, which launched last week, gained 27.2% on Tuesday, roughly 
double IBM’s highest-ever gain. A panoply of levered exchange-traded funds now 
allow peopleto trade this volatility <https://bloom.bg/3QY2umd>, although it’s 
not clear why they would want to.

SK Hynix’s capitalization in the Korean market fell by $504 billion in three 
weeks before regaining $56 billion on Tuesday. It remains $603 billion higher 
than on Jan. 1.

No, this can’t be healthy. Points of Return will come back to the issue. But 
it’s not surprising that banks are making money out of equity trading.
