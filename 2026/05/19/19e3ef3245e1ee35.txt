Deep Dive: Milan’s Big-City Commuters
The Porta Nuova train station in Turin, Italy.Photographer: Giuliano 
Berti/Bloomberg
Most weekday mornings, Giorgio Ferrero cycles through Turin’s quiet, arcaded 
streets to the city’s main train station. Within the hour he’s in Milan for his 
job as a lawyer at Prada Group.

 * The roughly 140-kilometer commute is a daily routine for a growing cohort 
of young executives who, like Ferrero, live in Turin while holding down jobs in 
Milan.
 * An unprecedented influx of wealth has pushed up rents and living costs in 
Milan. Parts of the city once seen as a second-tier financial center have 
become unaffordable, even to salaried professionals.
 * Turin’s lower housing prices, elegant architecture, green spaces and 
measured pace of life have meanwhile drawn residents in droves.
 * The cost-of-living gap has helped create a rare commute 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-05-19/milan-s-wealth-boom-sends-rents-and-living-costs-soaring?cmpid=051926_morningemea&utm_campaign=morningemea&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4623>
 from one big city to another, taking slightly less than an hour.
