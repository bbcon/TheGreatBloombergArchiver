European markets bore significant pressure on Thursday, with the Stoxx Europe 600 falling 2.0% as the combination of surging energy costs and a hawkish repricing of central bank expectations weighed on equities. European natural gas futures jumped as much as 35%, pushing benchmark prices to more than double their pre-war levels following Iranian missile strikes on Qatar's Ras Laffan complex. The energy shock is feeding directly into inflation expectations across the region.

The ECB held its key rate at 2.0% at its March meeting, in line with consensus expectations — the rate has been at that level since last June. The policy decision itself was largely mechanical given the circumstances, but the forward guidance has shifted materially. Traders are now fully pricing two ECB hikes this year, a dramatic reversal from the easing trajectory markets anticipated before the conflict began. The Bank of England also held rates, with markets having abandoned prior near-certain expectations of a cut and now pricing a hike as the most likely next move. Sweden's Riksbank similarly stood pat. The direction of travel across European central banks is now unmistakably toward tighter policy, a consequence of an energy shock whose inflationary pass-through the region has historically absorbed more acutely than the US.

In the UK, the labour market showed some resilience ahead of the BOE decision, with unemployment steady and employment modestly higher. The UK government separately announced higher tariffs on steel imports and reduced import quotas, framing the measure as support for the domestic industry amid global competition — a decision with some political resonance given the broader trade environment. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer faces mounting pressure from Labour's left flank ahead of anticipated losses in May's local elections, with Nigel Farage's Reform UK continuing to lead in national polls. HSBC's announcement that it is weighing cuts to approximately 20,000 roles as part of an AI-driven restructuring of its middle and back offices is among the first concrete signals of how the technology may reshape large financial institution workforces.
