India Is Unlikely to Break Its Russian Oil Connection No Matter What Trump 
Claims
India’s Russian oil imports are unlikely to go to zero.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, New 
Delhi-based oil reporter Rakesh Sharma asks what’s next for the Russia-to-India 
crude trade.

One of the big question marks hanging over the global oil market is the extent 
to which India will throttle down its buying of Russian crude.

The US President appeared to give an answer on Monday. In a post on Truth 
Social, Donald Trump said hewould cut tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-03/trump-s-surprise-trade-deal-with-india-resets-fractured-ties?cmpid=BBBXT020526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260205>
 on Indian goods after a deal with Prime Minister Narendra Modi that included a 
pledge to stop buying Russian oil.

But, as with a lot of Trump’s proclamations, reality was more complicated.

Modi soon confirmed there had been a trade deal, but he was silent about oil 
purchases. Indian refinerssought clarification 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-03/india-s-refiners-seek-clarity-on-russian-oil-after-trump-deal?cmpid=BBBXT020526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260205>
 from New Delhi on whether they must stop buying Russian crude, but it’s 
unclear if they got an answer.
A tanker carrying Venezuelan oil docked in Texas.Photographer: Mark 
Felix/Bloomberg
There are good reasons to believe Indian buyers won’t totally walk away from 
Russian oil any time soon. For a start, those barrels can get to the country 
faster than US and Venezuelan oil, with lower freight costs.

American crude — primarily of the light, sweet variety — is not the best fit 
for many Indian refineries that prefer heavy, sour grades. Venezuelan crude 
might be more suitable, but with production of just 800,000 barrels a day, 
there’s alack of supply 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/venezuela-oil-won-t-top-others-in-region-for-years-rystad-says?cmpid=BBBXT020526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260205>
.

Then there’s the fact that abandoning Russian purchases would hurt a 
long-standing relationship with Moscow, and India’s diplomatic balance.

Most critically, there’s price. Heavy markdowns are the thing that attracted 
India to Russian crude in the first place, and these discountsappeared to widen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/steeper-discounts-on-russian-oil-test-india-response-to-us-deal?cmpid=BBBXT020526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260205>
 this week following the US-India deal. That’s tempting for the country’s 
refiners.

Even so, there has been a notable change in comments from Indian officials. 
Where they had previously emphasized price as the sole driver of oil decisions 
they are now beginning to underscore the need for diversity of supply.

Before Trump’s tweet, traders and refiners already expected Russian oil flows 
to India to taper off toward around 800,000 to 1 million barrels a day — half 
of what they were at the peak. Until there’s more detail on what’s actually 
been agreed, there’s no sign that will change.

—Rakesh Sharma, Bloomberg News
