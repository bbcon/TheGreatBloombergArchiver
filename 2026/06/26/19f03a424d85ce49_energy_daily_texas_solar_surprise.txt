Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Mark Chediak looks at the evolving power mix in Texas.

An energy milestone was passed in California earlier this month: Solar is now 
consistently surpassing <https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67784> 
natural gas in the power mix.

That’s not surprising for a state that flaunts its green ambitions. What might 
come as a shock is that Texas could follow the same path.

The Lone Star State prides itself on its oil and gas production, and its 
lawmakers have tried to make it more costly and difficult to build solar.

Despite those efforts, the economics of solar power combined with battery 
storage — which keeps the power flowing when the sun goes down — are now so 
compelling that they accounted for the bulk of new capacity on the state’s grid 
last year.

That trend is forecast to continue. BloombergNEF sees solar and storage meeting
the majority <https://www.bnef.com/insights/37509/view> of new electricity 
demand in Texas in the coming years.

To be clear, natural gas will remain the backbone of the state’s grid for some 
time. It accounted for an average of 44% of power generation from 2021 to 2025,
according <https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67685> to the US 
Energy Information Administration. Solar’s share increased from 4% to 12% over 
the same period.

Texas enjoys a growing abundance of cheap shale gas, which is being tapped to 
power new data centers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/microsoft-chevron-sign-20-year-power-deal-for-texas-data-center?cmpid=BBBXT062626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5974>
. State lawmakers could yet succeed in passing anti-renewables legislation, and 
in the meantime they’ve established a fund for new gas-fired power. The Trump 
administration remains largely hostile to green energy.

Nevertheless, BloombergNEF expects renewables in Texas, including wind and 
storage, to overtake total gas generation in the next few years. Itsees 
<https://www.bnef.com/insights/37509/view> solar capacity in Texas more than 
tripling over the next decade to 82 gigawatts while energy storage rises 
fourfold to 40 gigawatts.

That compares to BloombergNEF’s most bullish forecast for new gas additions of 
about 11 gigawatts for a total of 79 gigawatts by 2035.

There are several explanations for renewable energy’s remarkable tenacity in 
Texas, including falling costs and fast installation times for both panels and 
battery packs.

But the advent of large, utility-scale storage to soak up power and overcome 
solar’s intermittency problem is the biggest threat to gas, in the very state 
that produces more of the fossil fuel than any other.

—Mark Chediak, Bloomberg News
