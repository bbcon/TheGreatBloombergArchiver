Full-Employment Paradox

An economy running at “full employment” typically conveys an image of strength 
and dynamism. But that may not always be the case — as US indicators lately 
have begun to suggest.

The Trump administration’s crackdown on immigration 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/trump-immigration-crackdown-could-shrink-us-population-for-first-time?cmpid=BBD021126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260211>
, and its effort to eject thousands if not hundreds of thousands of migrants, 
has undercut the expansion of the American labor force. While it’s impossible 
to know with certainty, this big shift in supply means the US possibly needs to 
create only 25,000 jobs, or fewer, to keep the jobless rate steady.

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell in his press conference last month said 
it’s possible to imagine zero job growth alongside no increase in unemployment. 
“Is that full employment? In a sense it is. If demand and supply are in 
balance, you know, you could say that’s full employment.”

(The January US jobs report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/-consequential-us-jobs-report-will-define-labor-market-slowdown?cmpid=BBD021126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260211>
, due at 8:30 a.m. in Washington, will offer the latest snapshot. Economists 
see the jobless rate holding at 4.4% — higher than recent years’ average, but 
still historically low.)

At the same time, Powell indicated, that’s not the same thing as a “maximum 
employment economy.” Ultimately it’s a “very challenging and quite unusual 
situation,” he said.

One particular challenge is that, with job gains shrinking, there’s less of a 
basis for consumption growth. That risk was on display Tuesday, with the 
unexpectedstalling out 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-10/us-retail-sales-unexpectedly-stagnated-to-close-holiday-season?cmpid=BBD021126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260211>
 of retail sales in December.

“Spending momentum is softening. Why? Because the labor market is softening 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/hassett-sees-leaner-jobs-numbers-ahead-on-less-population-growth?cmpid=BBD021126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260211>
,” said Gregory Daco, chief economist at EY-Parthenon. “This is the key pillar 
to income growth and consumer spending activity.”

He also flagged another Tuesday release: The employment cost index, a broad 
gauge of wages and benefits, showed the smallest advance since 2021 last 
quarter. “The income story is going to be the key story of 2025,” he said on 
Bloomberg TV.

Taking a further step back, “you’ve never been able to maintain this level of 
growth without job growth,” said Kristina Campmany, a senior portfolio manager 
at Invesco. Without net new employment, it’s just hard to see a robust economy 
continuing, she said, also on Bloomberg TV.

So full employment may not feel so full.

Need-to-Know Research

An assessment of the nations that may be most damaged by China’s steady move 
up the global industrial value chain shows that Slovakia, the Czech Republic, 
Hungary and South Korea areparticularly exposed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-01-03/where-china-capacity-lands-is-the-big-question-for-2026-new-economy?cmpid=BBD021126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260211>
.

For that quartet, “more than 70% of their industrial exports are in categories 
where China is growing the fastest,” Artie Lam at Oxford Economics wrote in a 
note Monday. That’s largely because of their major roles in the auto supply 
chain, he said. “Beyond the automotive sector, South Korea is also exposed to 
Chinese competition in the integrated circuits and semiconductors markets.”

As this newsletter has previously highlighted, even while it climbs the 
value-chain ladder, Chinaisn’t vacating 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-29/china-wants-to-dominate-high-and-low-tech-industries-new-economy?cmpid=BBD021126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260211>
 the lower rungs, Lam wrote. In some cases it’s “even deepened its market 
penetration in more traditional, labor-intensive sectors,” which puts it on 
track toward broad-based global industrial dominance, he wrote.
