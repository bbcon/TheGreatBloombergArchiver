The cost of the protracted Iran conflict is mounting around the world. And yet 
the prospect of a deal to end the stalemate seems as remote as ever.

In the US, traders have flipped from expecting the US to cut interest rates 
this year to increasingly seeing theprospect of a rate hike 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/oil-climbs-as-iran-war-shows-no-sign-of-ending-markets-wrap?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
. China’s already weak consumer sentiment is being exacerbated by rising 
commodity prices. Japan and Thailand are planning additional spending to offset 
the impact of the war.

As a result of such inflationary fears, bonds have been selling off, including 
in economies such as Indonesia and India, two countriesheavily reliant 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/indonesian-rupiah-falls-to-record-low-on-oil-as-markets-reopen?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
 on energy imports — like much of Asia.

“Inflation worries have gripped global bond markets,” said Frederic Neumann, 
chief Asia economist at HSBC. “With little prospect of a quick restoration of 
Gulf energy flows, and manufacturing supply chains signaling a relentless rise 
in price pressures, inflation across the world is set to push higher in the 
coming months.”
Trump speaks to members of the media after returning to the White House from a 
trip to China.Photographer: Eric Lee/Bloomberg
There is little sign of relief on the horizon. The US and Iran remain far 
apart on a deal to end weeks of war and reopen the Strait of Hormuz, with 
President Donald Trump expressingrenewed frustration 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/us-and-iran-far-from-hormuz-deal-as-drone-hits-uae-power-plant?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
 with Tehran.

“For Iran, the Clock is Ticking, and they better get moving, FAST, or there 
won’t be anything left of them. TIME IS OF THE ESSENCE!”

Iran’s semi-official Mehr news agency said Washington offered “no tangible 
concessions” while seeking demands it failed to secure during the war, a stance 
the agency said was leading to an impasse.

With Trump’s repeated threats to resume bombing Iran having failed so far, 
it’s unclear what could shift the deadlock. In the meantime, countries are 
increasingly paying the price for a war that they didn’t ask for.

—Richard Frost

What You Need to Know Today

Warning signs are flashing for China’s economy. Retail sales rose just 0.2% in 
April from a year earlier, the latest data shows. That’s the weakest figure 
since December 2022, when China reopened from Covid and mass infections spread.

Car sales plunged 15%, while purchases of home appliances and furniture — 
products that used to be buoyed by government subsidies — declined at a 
double-digit pace. Gold, silver and jewelry sales plummeted 21%.

It’s not just the consumer cutting back. Industrial production increased less 
than expected at 4.1% last month — the weakest in almost three years. 
Fixed-asset investment unexpectedly shrank 1.6% in the first four months of 
2026. No economist surveyed by Bloomberg had predictedas pessimistic a reading 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/china-economy-slows-sharply-as-investment-returns-to-contraction?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
 for retail sales, industry and investment. The poor figures, which come after 
data last week showeddemand shrank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/china-s-credit-growth-misses-expectations-as-new-loans-contract?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
 for new loans, are prompting calls for the government to act.

Japan’s leader is taking steps to offset the impact of rising commodity 
prices. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi said she has called on the finance 
ministry to compilean extra budget 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/japan-s-takaichi-set-to-announce-extra-budget-amid-mideast-shock?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
, after insisting such a measure wasn’t necessary.

For weeks, Takaichi and Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama have denied the need 
for additional funds or fresh bond issuance. But rising oil prices and the 
depletion of funds to pay for relief measures forced their hand. Concern over 
inflation fears has been driving thecountry’s bonds lower 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/japan-10-year-yield-jumps-on-global-inflation-angst-as-oil-rises?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
. On Monday, the 30-year yield surged as much as 20 basis points to the highest 
level since its debut in 1999 before paring some of the gains.

Thailand too is preparing a package 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/thai-growth-beats-estimates-even-as-oil-crisis-weighs-on-outlook?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4582>
 of around 200 billion baht ($6.1 billion) to address a looming cost-of-living 
crisis, Finance Minister Ekniti Nitithanprapas said Monday. “We need to get 
ready for the storm that is building up,” he said.
