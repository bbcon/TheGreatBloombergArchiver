The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 reached all-time highs on April 16, completing a roughly 10% and 14% rally respectively from their March 30 closing levels — one of the fastest such moves recorded since 1950. The advance is analytically unusual: prior recoveries of this magnitude and speed have typically occurred from deeply oversold levels following prolonged bear markets or acute crises. The closest historical parallel, by earnings multiple and timing, appears to be March 2000. Analysts attribute the rally partly to behavioral conditioning from last year's Liberation Day episode, when traders who sold early missed a rapid rebound, making investors more reluctant to hold short positions through the Iran conflict. The structural shift toward passive and retail "buy-the-dip" participants reinforces this dynamic.

Valuations remain stretched by most conventional measures. The 10-year Treasury yield sits at approximately 4.3%, and Robert Shiller's excess CAPE yield — which subtracts the bond yield from the equity earnings yield — implies below-average prospective decade returns. First-quarter earnings estimates have declined during the rally, not risen, making the multiple expansion harder to justify on fundamental grounds.

On monetary policy, the Federal Reserve situation remains unresolved and politically charged. Trump reiterated his threat to dismiss Jerome Powell if he remains as chairman past his May term expiration. The Justice Department continues to pursue its building-renovation probe despite a court ruling characterizing it as without merit. Senator Thom Tillis has blocked Kevin Warsh's confirmation pending resolution of the legal matter. Notably, Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has now signaled twice in two days that he understands the case for the Fed to hold rates steady while awaiting clarity on the war's inflationary impact — a marked shift from his prior advocacy for cuts, and one that implicitly provides political cover for Warsh to avoid an embarrassing dissent on his first FOMC vote. The Fed's Beige Book described an economy in suspension, with no near-term catalyst to move in either direction. Markets are pricing near-zero probability of a June rate change. A federal antitrust jury found Live Nation illegally monopolized the live events industry, setting the stage for a potential structural breakup of the company.
