After moving to Tokyo in late 2009 to lead Bloomberg’s Asia economy coverage, 
I was eager to get a handle on what the data were saying about where the major 
economies were heading, and where they had come from. As someone who derives 
pleasure from analyzing longer-term economic and financial trends, I was pumped 
to discover the Bloomberg Terminal had many data series on Japan going back 
decades.

Several telegraphed deep alarm. Retail sales — unadjusted for inflation — were 
at 1990s levels. Thelabor force 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2011-09-30/japan-s-industrial-output-rises-less-than-expected-weighed-by-strong-yen?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 was at its smallest since the 1980s. Housing starts were at the lowest since 
at least the 1970s. Most troubling, nominal GDP was below a 1997 peak.

It all conditioned me to pay attention when nominal figures drop. Which 
explains why China’s latestretail sales 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-s-first-consumer-spending-drop-since-covid-imperils-growth?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 release, showing the first contraction outside of the pandemic since 1990, 
caught my eye.

This newsletter has from time to time 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2023-06-10/china-s-coming-balance-sheet-recession-new-economy-saturday?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 analyzedsimilarities 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-04-13/bloomberg-new-economy-china-heads-down-japan-s-troubled-macro-policy-path?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 between economic challenges previously encountered by Japan and those now 
facing China. Recent suggestions that China’s “Japanification 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-28/what-is-japanification-and-is-it-happening-to-china?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
” concerns can belaid to rest 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-21/has-china-s-economy-just-shrugged-off-japanification?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 warrant another look.

The latest retreat in Chinese consumer spending speaks to a big Japan parallel 
that still seems to hold: China continues to suffer the effects of a bad-debt 
overhang that followed a credit bubble. Its multiyear property-market slump 
hasn’t hit bottom, weighing on confidence and keeping consumers cautious.

Yet in other ways, China is clearly in much better shape than Japan of the 
later 1990s and early 2000s. Its manufacturing sector continues to roar, taking 
market share from others. Industrial “hollowing out 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2015-03-18/why-the-yen-s-slide-isn-t-bringing-japan-s-factories-jobs-back?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
” is simply not a China story. So the “Japanification” term doesn’t tidily fit 
what’s going on in the world’s No. 2 economy, as discussed below. Nor does the 
“lost decade” that originally applied to 1990s Japan, or the “secular 
stagnation” label used for the weak US growth of the 2010s.

China needs its own moniker. Read on for one proposal.

While there were some statistical asterisks attached to China’s 0.6% 
year-on-year decline in retail sales in May — especially the expiration of 
government-supported trade-in programs for durable goods — the drop still said 
something important. Before Covid, in 2019, sales averaged 8% growth. The past 
three years, they’ve run at just half that pace.

A successfully developing economy such as China, where per-capita GDP remains 
well below advanced nations, should be seeing steady gains, not stagnation. The 
vastly wealthier US sawretail sales 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/us-retail-sales-jumped-in-may-despite-high-gasoline-costs?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 average 3.5% increases in the past three years, practically on par with China. 
There’s something wrong with that picture.

Behind the lackluster Chinese domestic demand is a dynamic similar to what 
happened in Japan.

Under pressure from the Reagan administration, Tokyo in 1985 agreed to a major 
strengthening of the yen. Currency gains then undermined Japanese exporters’ 
competitiveness, in turn spurring Japan to slash interest rates, which added 
fuel to amassive credit boom 
<https://assets.bwbx.io/documents/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/rnSc7a68YyiA/v0>. Lending 
surged roughly 50% from 1985 to 1989, the year the speculation-propelled stock 
market peaked.

When Japan’s bubble burst in the early 1990s, households, companies and the 
banking system were left with a giant amount of bad debt. The cleanup took more 
than a decade and saw a focus on repairing balance sheets rather than growth. 
Policymakers were woefully behind in recognizing the scale of the problem. It 
took two lost decades before sufficientmonetary stimulus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2013-04-04/bank-of-japan-boosts-bond-purchases-at-kuroda-s-first-meeting?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 came to get the economy going again.

China’s credit bubble dates from November 2008, when the government unleashed 
a domestic investment surge to counter the impact of the global financial 
crisis. Local governments across China were given free rein to issue debt and 
spend freely. Property firms competed to raise debt and to accumulate land 
banks for subsequent development.

But way too many projects had zero economic returns. Eventually, President Xi 
Jinping gave license to his lieutenant, Liu He, tocurb credit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2016-05-09/china-s-latest-debt-warning-comes-via-communist-party-mouthpiece?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
, and authorities went on to put “red lines 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-10-08/what-china-s-three-red-lines-mean-for-property-firms-quicktake?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
” around real-estate lending. Property investment has been in continuous 
decline for four years now. Home prices haveplummeted about 30% 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/china-economy-6-charts-explain-why-how-economic-growth-is-slowing-down?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 from their 2021 peak, according to economists’ estimates.

As in Japan, that’s curbed appetite for taking on new credit, at least among 
households and the local authorities with large debt overhangs. Deflationary 
pressures and a demographic shift to population decline have worsened the 
situation. Hiring and wage growth have slowed.

“There are notable parallels between China today and 1990s Japan: an asset 
bubble burst, soft consumption, persistently low inflation or deflation, and an
aging population 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-21/why-china-s-population-is-falling-and-what-it-means-for-its-economy?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
,” Larry Hu and Yuxiao Zhang, economists at Macquarie, wrote in a note earlier 
this month titledChina vs. 1990s Japan.

The giant difference, they highlight, is China’s manufacturing and export 
boom. That’s been helped by an exchange-rate policy that — unlike Tokyo’s — has
kept the yuan weak 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/bessent-urged-by-us-senators-to-push-china-on-undervalued-yuan?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
. From 1990 to the eve of Prime Minister Shinzo Abe taking power on a reflation 
agenda in 2012, the yen appreciated more than 80% against the dollar. But in 
the past decade, China’s yuan is actually down slightly, despite consistently 
large trade surpluses.

China’s Communist leadership also has used a tightening grip on the nation’s 
financial system to ensure that credit flows freely to advanced manufacturing 
and technological innovation. Geostrategic competition with the US has added 
impetus to that push.

Favored Chinese companies get an array of support that was unavailable to 
Japanese firms after Tokyo, under pressure from US trade negotiators in the 
1990s, turned away frommanaged industrial policy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-02-22/world-economy-latest-japan-s-industrial-comeback?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
.

With a supportive exchange rate, subsidies, government procurement aid, 
limited worker protections and non-market based lending, Chinese manufacturing 
has forged a markedly different path than that of Japan.

Hu and Yuxiao argue that, if China’s export and manufacturing engine stalls — 
say, underforeign protectionism 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/china-s-state-media-say-eu-risks-freezing-of-ties-over-trade?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 — “they are likely to pivot to consumption as the growth engine.”

But, as Japan found under Abe, countering a deflationary mindset 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2014-05-19/abe-taps-shiller-insight-on-breaking-japan-s-shrunken-mindset?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 amid trenchant demographic decline isn’t straightforward. Xi is also 
consistent in demonstrating thatwhat he’s interested in 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2024-09-21/bloomberg-new-economy-why-china-s-sluggish-growth-isn-t-rattling-xi-jinping?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 is technological development, not the accumulation of private wealth that 
could drive demand for the kind of sophisticated, services-heavy economies seen 
in advanced nations.

When emerging markets fail to make that sort of transition, economists call it 
the “Middle Income Trap 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-08-16/jokowi-targets-5-2-gdp-growth-as-indonesia-faces-elections?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
.” In China’s case, however, it’s not so much a trap as a policy preference. 
The latest domestic-demand slowdown was even followed byfiscal tightening 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/china-pursues-austerity-with-first-fiscal-gap-cutback-since-2023?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
.

Xi isn’t channeling record trade surpluses 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-14/china-ends-2025-with-1-2-trillion-trade-surplus-as-exports-soar?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260627&utm_content=5639>
 to craft a social safety net that would allow Chinese households to shift from 
saving and balance-sheet repair to spending. Instead, he’s husbanding China’s 
resources for select industrial objectives. No, this isn’t Japanification. 
Perhaps consider it a “middle income preference.”
