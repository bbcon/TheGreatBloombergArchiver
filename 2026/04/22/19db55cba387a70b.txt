Tesla’s wobble

By Michelle Ma 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWTYlfwjEUk/michelle-ma> and Kara 
Carlson <https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AW1MPTNjbc8/kara-carlson>

For years, one aspect of Tesla Inc.’s business has been growing outside the 
public eye, its revenue soaring even as the company’s electric car sales 
sputtered and stalled.

Now analysts wonder if Tesla’s quiet money-maker faces problems of its own 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-22/tesla-s-battery-business-a-quiet-moneymaker-suddenly-stalls?cmpid=BBD042226_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
.

The company’s Megapack batteries — powerful enough to keep factories, data 
centers or the power grid itself humming — have given Tesla a badly needed 
boost. Much smaller Powerwall units, meanwhile, supply individual homes. As the 
company spends heavily to ramp up autonomous taxis, and its Optimus robots seem 
forever a year away, batteries have become an important source of cash.

CATL’s sodium breakthrough

By Annie Lee <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASUCXsQX63g/annie-lee>

With a thick outdoor jacket to protect against -30C (-22F) temperatures and 
bitter winds, Calvin Quek joined car and technology enthusiasts huddled by a 
test track on the outskirts of a picturesque ski region in Inner Mongolia.

They watched Chongqing Changan Automobile Co. electric SUVs and a sleek 
battery-powered coupe effortlessly navigate icy roads and steep snow-dusted 
slopes at the facility in Yakeshi, northeastern China. Yet for the crowd at the 
February demonstration, the most impressive factor wasout of view 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/lithium-rival-sodium-is-making-a-battery-breakthrough-for-evs-energy-storage?cmpid=BBD042226_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
.
A CATL sodium-ion battery model.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
Batteries powering the vehicles used sodium as their key raw material 
component, rather than lithium — the metal that’s become synonymous with 
electrified transportation and the storage of renewable energy. The tests were 
a showcase for one of the world’s first mass-production passenger EVs fitted 
with the alternative technology — an innovation that’s long promised to deliver 
safer and low-cost rechargeable cells.

“I think this signaled a breakthrough moment for sodium-ion battery technology 
in EVs,” said Quek, executive director for nature finance at the Oxford 
Sustainable Finance Group, and a veteran observer of the energy transition.

By the middle of this year, Changan — a state-owned Chinese automaker and a 
local partner of Ford Motor Co. — will begin sales of models fitted with 
sodium-ion technology from Contemporary Amperex Technology Co. Ltd., the 
world’s dominant EV battery manufacturer.

Innovations by CATL have increased the energy density of sodium batteries by 
50%, overcome engineering challenges, and brought the products to a “milestone 
stage,” Chief Technology Officer Gao Huan told reporters Tuesday at an event in 
Beijing. “The era of sodium and lithium shining together has arrived” and the 
supplier will begin mass-production in the fourth quarter of this year, he said.
CATL’s Chief Technology Officer Gao Huan.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
Yet even with recent advances, the sodium sector faces challenges. The 
technology isn’t likely to compete against lithium on energy density — and 
therefore on battery performance — and may only achieve cost parity with 
traditional options around 2030, according to CRU Group, a consultancy. 
Continued improvement in lithium iron phosphate, or LFP, batteries “poses a 
moving target for sodium-ion,” said Sam Adham, head of battery materials at CRU.

Takeaways From Day One at BNEF New York

By Will Wade <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQKkcaEIb6M/will-wade>, Josh 
Saul <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ATn-QRa9KAg/josh-saul>, Lauren Rosenthal
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AXCV-bQx5Dk/lauren-rosenthal> and Michelle 
Ma <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWTYlfwjEUk/michelle-ma>

The BloombergNEF <bbg://screens/BNEF> annual summit in New York kicked off on 
Tuesday as the war in Iran fuels a resurgence of interest in fossil fuel 
alternatives and the US sees a massive push for more electricity to feed data 
centers.

The confluence of those two themes reveals a key shift: Oil and gas are still 
drivers of the global economy, but scaling electricity is quickly becoming just 
as important. TheBNEF Summit New York 
<https://about.bnef.com/events/summit-agenda-newyork-2026/> put these trends in 
context. Here are some takeaways from day one.
Toby Rice, right, and Karen Fang at the BNEF Summit New York on Tuesday.
Electricity is the new oil
Clean energy and batteries aren’t just tools to cut emissions anymore. They’re 
key to ensuring energy security and powering the economy of the 21st century, 
saidRebecca Kujawa <https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/5760427>, chief 
executive officer of Zerra Partners. To ensure sustained economic growth, 
countries can’t rely on fossil fuels as they have in the past. Renewable and 
nuclear energy, as well as more battery storage, are needed to ensure there’s 
enough electricity flowing across the grid, Kujawa said.

“We’re going to start to look at electricity as critical to security and 
geopolitical stability as we think about oil and gas today,” she said. “The 
change has been dramatic.”

AI needs a lot more power, and fast
BNEF is forecasting 106 gigawatts of new demand in the next decade will be 
needed for data centers, said Katrina White, a clean energy analyst, during an 
afternoon presentation.

Earlier in the day, Toby Rice 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/18495401>, the chief executive 
officer of natural gas producerEQT Corp. <bbg://securities/EQT%20US%20Equity>, 
also said as much as 100 gigawatts of additional power may be required for 
artificial intelligence computing facilities and other large power users.

“That’s the energy equivalent of adding over 20 New York Cities,” Rice said. 
“And we need to add it quickly.”

Flexible data centers get priority
For decades, utilities have been slow to grant power connection requests from 
large users like data centers. But the game is changing, according toVarun 
Sivaram <https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/20201021>, chief executive 
officer and founder of software companyEmerald AI 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/2579865D:US?cmpid=BBD042226_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
. Data centers that can curtail power use on demand in response to signals from 
the grid are getting prioritized.

Road to COP31

By John Ainger <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AS5AOj_Ky2g/john-ainger>

Turkey will use its role as host of this year’s COP31 climate summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/turkey-demands-decisive-climate-action-ahead-of-new-global-talks?cmpid=BBD042226_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 to boost clean energy adoption, according to the event’s incoming president, 
with the Iran war showing how important it is to diversify away from fossil 
fuels.
Demonstrators at COP30.Photographer: Marina Calderon/Bloomberg
Murat Kurum, Turkey’s environment minister who will oversee the talks, said in 
an interview that even countries whose economies rely on producing fossil fuels 
will need to shift to greener technologies. That includes nations in the Middle 
East that have historically blocked efforts to move away from emissions-heavy 
energy sources, he said.

“The world must transition away from fossil fuels, for sure,” Kurum told 
Bloomberg on the sidelines of the Petersberg Climate Dialogue in Berlin. “The 
recent crises have indeed showed us how crucial to have energy diversity. You 
should not be dependent on one single energy source.”
