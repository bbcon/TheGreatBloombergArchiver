# US Daily Briefing — February 4, 2026

**Markets and Tech Earnings in Focus.** US equity futures traded mixed ahead of Alphabet's results after the close, with S&P 500 futures up 0.14% and Nasdaq 100 futures roughly flat. The 10-year Treasury yield ticked up to 4.28%. The session's most consequential moves were idiosyncratic: AMD fell 7.3% in pre-market after a sales forecast that disappointed investors hoping for a clearer AI payoff, while Chipotle declined 6.2% on a weak outlook. On the upside, Silicon Labs jumped 33% on a Financial Times report that Texas Instruments is in advanced talks to acquire the chip designer for roughly $7 billion, and Enphase rallied 21% on a revenue beat. The day's earnings docket also includes AbbVie, Eli Lilly, Uber, Qualcomm, and Snap.

**Value Rotation Gains Traction.** The Russell 1000 Value Index has advanced 8.6% since early November, outperforming its growth counterpart by 14 percentage points as software stocks have come under pressure on concerns AI may erode incumbent business models. Apollo's John Zito framed the question directly: "Is software dead?" The shift bears watching, though prior value-over-growth episodes of comparable magnitude (2022 and 2001) coincided with broader market drawdowns rather than durable regime change. Whether this rotation reflects healthy breadth or defensive positioning remains to be seen.

**Fed Transition and Fiscal Backdrop.** Kevin Warsh's incoming challenge as Fed Chair will be building consensus among colleagues, while Governor Stephen Miran has stepped down from his White House role. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent testifies before the House today. The partial government shutdown ended after President Trump signed a funding deal negotiated with Senate Democrats.

**Affordability Pressures Persist.** Despite real wage gains—average hourly earnings rose 3.8% in December, well above inflation—consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in over a decade. Goldman research highlights housing as the binding constraint: the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160-200% of income, from 90-120% in 2000. The disconnect between aggregate income data and household sentiment continues to carry political weight.
