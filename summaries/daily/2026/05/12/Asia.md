South Korea's equity market experienced its sharpest single-session dislocation in recent memory when presidential policy chief Kim Yong-beom floated, via a Facebook post, the idea of a "citizen dividend" funded by taxes on AI profits. The Kospi shed more than $300 billion in market capitalization within 97 minutes, falling as much as 5.1% before recovering a portion of losses after Kim clarified he meant tapping excess tax revenues rather than imposing a new windfall levy on corporate profits. The episode nonetheless exposed fragility beneath a rally that had lifted the index roughly 86% year-to-date through Monday. The underlying tension is genuine: Samsung Electronics posted a 755% profit increase in Q1 2026, its market capitalization recently crossed $1 trillion after shares more than quadrupled over the past year, and the company is forecast to generate 330 trillion won in operating profit this year — a figure that would rank it second only to Nvidia globally. Samsung's labor union is threatening an 18-day strike from May 21, demanding 15% of operating profit for chip-division employees. The convergence of redistribution politics and labor unrest around AI-driven windfall profits appears likely to persist.

The Trump-Xi summit scheduled for Thursday in Beijing adds a consequential layer to the Asia picture. Trump will arrive under domestic strain, having characterized the US-Iran ceasefire as on "massive life support," and the US moved to sanction a dozen entities over Iranian oil sales to China in the days immediately preceding the meeting — a gesture of economic pressure that complicates the diplomatic atmosphere. The consensus expectation is modest: a possible extension of the 12-month trade-war ceasefire agreed at October's South Korea summit, with bilateral investment accommodation representing the principal upside surprise scenario.

Elsewhere, Japan's finance minister confirmed close coordination with US Treasury Secretary Bessent on foreign exchange policy, with Bessent characterizing excess FX volatility as undesirable — a tacit endorsement of Japan's recent market intervention. Japanese household spending fell for a fourth consecutive month, and the Bank of Japan signaled a possible rate increase in June.
