Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Oil Trading Team Leader Serene Cheong explores the workarounds that could 
help tankers mitigate threats from renewed fighting in the Middle East.

The war in the Middle East has delivered some hard lessons for the oil 
business. As fighting erupts again, that new knowledge could be helpful in 
cushioning global markets from the next potential blow to supply.

It didnтАЩt take long for observable tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz 
to grind toa near standstill  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/hormuz-traffic-grinds-to-a-near-halt-as-ceasefire-under-threat?cmpid=BBBXT070926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6372>
after the US resumedstrikes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/us-military-launches-strikes-on-iran-for-second-straight-day?cmpid=BBBXT070926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6372>
 against Iran this week. That followed a spate of Iranian attacks on vessels in 
the waterway.

The 20 or so commodity carriers that crossed Hormuz in either direction on 
Wednesday marked the lowest daily total since before an interim peace deal in 
mid-June. The true total could be higher, given the tendency of vessels to stop 
broadcasting their locations at moments of heightened risk, making tracking 
more difficult.

President Donald Trump said the peace agreement might now be тАЬoverтАЭ and the US 
could resume its blockade of Iranian shipping. Fears of such a deterioration in 
the situation тАФ while justified тАФ are reckoning without the creative 
workarounds developed during months of disruption in the worldтАЩs most vital 
energy conduit.

Version 2.0 of the Hormuz crisis might be a little different.
Tankers anchored in the Strait of Hormuz off the coast of Qeshm Island, Iran,

For one, Abu Dhabi, one of the biggest producers within the Persian Gulf, has 
worked withgutsy shipowners 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-05/the-supertanker-tycoon-making-millions-on-hormuz-shuttle-runs?cmpid=BBBXT070926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6372>
 to get its oil out to global markets. Using so-called dark shuttle transits тАФ 
ships that cross the strait without broadcasting their location тАФ the emirate 
has developed a method that others may try to follow.

Secondly, the use of convoys тАФ not least the crossing on the Iranian side of 
the strait by numerousJapanese ships  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/japan-linked-ships-escaping-hormuz-in-convoy-through-iran-route?cmpid=BBBXT070926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6372>
in clear view with their transponders turned on тАФ showed that an orderly exit 
of the Gulf may still be possible even as tensions persist.

With most oil-laden vessels having now left the region, only those shipowners 
with a high tolerance for risk will be tempted to re-enter. The end result may 
be a niche trade with the resilience to thrive in the most challenging 
circumstances.

For every shipowner wary of re-entering 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/shipowners-assess-hormuz-transit-risk-as-us-iran-deal-crumbles?cmpid=BBBXT070926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6372>
, there will be others who lean on their recent success in crossing Hormuz for 
a handsome profit.

These workarounds, of course, are no substitute for safe passage. The market 
still needs pipelines, alternative supply routes and other structural fixes to 
feel reassured. But shipping at least has more solutions than it did before the 
war began.
