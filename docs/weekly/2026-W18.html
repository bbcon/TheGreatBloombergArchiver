Weekly Brief

Apr 27 – May 03, 2026

US

  • The Fed's April meeting — Powell's last as chair — produced four dissents, the most fractious vote since October 1992, with futures markets now pricing no cuts in 2026 and roughly 50% odds of a hike by April 2027.
  • Mega-cap earnings delivered a split verdict: Alphabet surged more than 7% on Wednesday after beating estimates, while Meta fell roughly 7% in after-hours trade despite strong ad revenue, after raising 2026 capex guidance by $10 billion to potentially above $145 billion.
  • Apple delivered a stronger-than-expected third-quarter revenue forecast on Thursday, gaining approximately 2.8%, capping what appears to have been the best monthly performance for US stocks since late 2020.
  • The Philadelphia Semiconductor index extended its record winning streak to 18 consecutive positive sessions, trading at roughly 60 times trailing earnings — a valuation that prices in substantial margin expansion whose durability remains to be tested.
  • Kevin Warsh's path to the Fed chair cleared after Senator Tillis dropped his blockade of the nomination, following the Justice Department's decision to end its criminal investigation of Jerome Powell.

Fed Leadership and Policy

The Federal Reserve's April meeting will be remembered primarily for its institutional texture rather than its rate decision, which was, as widely anticipated, a hold. Four FOMC members dissented — three regional presidents (Beth Hammack, Neel Kashkari, and Lorie Logan) objecting to any residual easing bias in the forward guidance, and Stephen Miran on separate grounds — marking the most divided vote since October 1992. Two-year Treasury yields jumped 11 basis points on Wednesday, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, while 30-year yields reached 5% for the first time this year. Futures markets have fully removed any 2026 easing from their base case, with roughly 50% odds now assigned to a hike by April 2027. The hawkish tilt reflects a committee increasingly focused on the inflationary consequences of sustained elevated energy prices, a dynamic that sits in direct tension with the rate cuts the White House has sought. The leadership transition adds a further layer of complexity. Powell announced he will remain on the board as a governor after Warsh assumes the chair role, until the Justice Department's building-renovation investigation is resolved. Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized this as a violation of Fed norms, though the precedent set by Marriner Eccles somewhat undercuts the framing. Warsh will inherit a deeply divided committee, an unresolved inflation picture, and the unusual presence of his predecessor as a sitting board member — circumstances that may constrain his ability to shift policy direction quickly, whatever his inclinations.

Equity Markets and Earnings

The week's earnings slate served as a meaningful stress test for the AI-driven valuation narrative. Alphabet's Wednesday results were received as a genuine positive, with shares gaining more than 7% as its custom TPU chip architecture was interpreted as a credible competitive challenge to Nvidia in AI inference workloads — adding $421 billion to market capitalization in a single session, the second-largest single-day market cap gain on record for any company. Amazon's AWS unit posted 28% revenue growth, its fastest in over three years. Meta's reaction, by contrast, was negative: despite advertising revenue of $56.3 billion, the company's decision to raise 2026 capex guidance by $10 billion to potentially above $145 billion was read by investors as prioritizing long-run AI buildout over near-term returns. The combined 2026 capex envelope for the four hyperscalers now approaches $725 billion. Microsoft's Azure growth was viewed as underwhelming, with shares slipping modestly. Apple's above-consensus third-quarter revenue forecast on Thursday provided a constructive bookend to the week. NXP Semiconductors surged 16% on Wednesday premarket on an upbeat revenue forecast, and Seagate rallied 18% on strong AI-related storage demand. The Philadelphia Semiconductor index extended to 18 consecutive positive sessions — the longest winning streak in the index's history. That streak reflects genuine earnings momentum: with slightly over a third of S&P 500 companies having reported, the beat rate stands at approximately 80% against a historical average of 77%, while the aggregate earnings surprise of roughly 12% materially exceeds the typical 7% premium. Societe Generale estimates 12-month forward profits are now approximately $600 billion higher than four months ago, though semiconductors alone account for roughly 43% of that revision — a concentration that amplifies both the upside and the tail risk.

Monetary Policy and Fiscal Dynamics

The Fed's hawkish hold creates an environment in which the bond market may increasingly act as the economy's primary tightening mechanism. Thirty-year Treasury yields pressing toward and briefly touching 5% on Thursday carry direct consequences for mortgage rates, corporate refinancing costs, and the fiscal burden on a government running structural deficits. Fitch separately warned that the US debt burden remains far above other AA-rated sovereigns, a reminder that fiscal sustainability concerns have not been resolved by strong nominal growth. Congressional Republicans are advancing proposals to increase Pentagon spending by 44% — a rare act of intra-party friction with the White House — and to lower capital gains taxes, a combination that complicates the budget arithmetic further. In credit markets, investors showed early signs of fatigue after a $300 billion AI-related debt binge, with demands emerging for better pricing and stronger covenant protections. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to 49.8 in April, its lowest reading since data collection began in 1978, a reminder that the macro backdrop outside equity markets remains more fragile than headline index levels suggest. ---

Bank of Japan: Hawkish Hold and Currency Pressure

The Bank of Japan's April meeting produced a 6-3 vote to hold its benchmark rate at 0.75% — the most divided outcome under Governor Ueda's tenure — with three board members, including the consensus-leaning Junko Nakagawa, voting for an immediate hike. Bloomberg Economics now assigns high probability to a move at the June meeting. Despite the explicitly hawkish signal, the yen remained stubbornly weak near 160 per dollar through much of the week, reflecting investor skepticism about how far normalization can proceed under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, who has appointed two dovish board members and publicly criticized a trade minister for suggesting rate hikes could strengthen the currency. Spring Shunto wage negotiations produced a third consecutive year of approximately 5% wage increases, and five-year inflation breakevens have reached 2.7%, suggesting bond markets believe the deflationary era is genuinely over — yet the currency's weakness persists, creating a tension between fundamentals and market pricing that is likely to require resolution. Japan intervened in currency markets on Thursday for the first time since 2024, purchasing yen and selling dollars after issuing a "final" warning to investors. The move triggered intraday strengthening of as much as 0.7%, with dollar-yen reaching 155.5 before partially retracing to around 156.5. Analysts cautioned that intervention can stabilize markets only briefly: Japan's structural dependence on imported energy — now made substantially more expensive by the Strait of Hormuz disruption — continues to exert downward pressure on the currency through the current account. Japan's top currency official also indicated readiness to intervene in crude oil futures markets, an unusual extension of currency-management tools into commodity markets.

South Korea: Record Performance and Concentration Risk

South Korea's equity market has emerged as the year's strongest performer among major indices, with the Kospi rising approximately 60% and total market capitalization surpassing the UK's at $4.04 trillion. Samsung Electronics reported a 755% year-on-year profit increase in the March quarter, and Samsung and SK Hynix together now represent 42% of Kospi capitalization, up from 28% two years ago. That concentration amplifies both momentum and tail risk. Labor tensions are building: thousands of Samsung employees rallied over the weekend demanding higher wages, with the union threatening an 18-day strike beginning May 21. South Korea's April export data showed continued semiconductor strength, providing some offset to the regional drag from elevated energy costs.

China: Growth, Governance, and Technology Control

China's April PMI data presented a bifurcated picture. Manufacturing remained in expansion territory, supported by resilient export demand, while services and construction slipped back into contraction, with building activity registering its worst performance since the early stages of the pandemic. The divergence reflects a familiar structural tension: external demand is sustaining factory output while weak domestic consumption and a deepening property downturn weigh on the broader economy. The Politburo pledged to counter external shocks and enhance energy security, citing better-than-expected first-quarter growth, and Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable. Beijing's handling of the Meta-Manus transaction represents a significant escalation in China's approach to technology governance. Authorities ordered the two companies to unwind the completed $2 billion acquisition within weeks, barred Manus co-founders from leaving the country, and instructed several private firms via the NDRC to reject US-origin capital unless explicitly approved. The stated rationale centers on preventing strategically important AI capabilities from flowing to the United States, but the broader signal appears directed at domestic entrepreneurs contemplating offshore restructuring to access US capital. The episode risks reinforcing perceptions of elevated political risk in China's technology sector. Separately, China's tax authorities launched a crackdown on circular invoicing in the metals trading sector, rattling copper, aluminum, and silver traders in Shanghai who rely on such invoices for bank financing. The State Taxation Administration framed the investigation as targeting fraudulent activity that has artificially inflated output measures.

India: Heat, Energy, and Trade

India faces an unusual convergence of pressures. Every one of the world's 50 hottest cities this week was located in India, with temperatures exceeding 40°C across densely populated states. The McKinsey Global Institute estimates heat-related productivity losses could reach $250 billion, with monsoon relief not expected until June. The Federation of Indian Airlines warned that carriers are "on the verge of closing down" without government relief on jet fuel prices, with Brent crude trading near $111 a barrel. Against this backdrop, the trade policy liberalization underway is a structurally meaningful development. India signed a free trade agreement with New Zealand, following earlier deals with the EU and UK, representing a departure from the protectionist posture that saw average tariff rates rise from approximately 13% to 18% between 2017 and 2020. The pivot appears driven partly by urgency to secure alternative market access as the US turns more protectionist, and partly by an acknowledgment that manufacturing's share of GDP has declined from 16% to 13% over the past decade. The liberalization has practical limits — most-favored-nation tariff rates have not been broadly reduced, agriculture remains shielded, and structural regulatory constraints persist — but the directional shift is meaningful. Sun Pharmaceutical's agreement to acquire Organon for approximately $11.75 billion on an enterprise basis is separately notable as one of the largest outbound acquisitions in Indian corporate history. ---

Growth and the Stagflationary Pressure

The eurozone economy unexpectedly decelerated at the start of 2026, with first-quarter GDP growth falling short of consensus estimates as surging energy costs — a direct consequence of the Strait of Hormuz blockade — weigh on industrial output and household purchasing power. Germany's government halved its 2026 growth forecast to 0.5% earlier in the month, while the Ifo Institute's business expectations index fell to its lowest level since 2023. Germany, Italy, and Spain individually outperformed first-quarter expectations in isolation, but the aggregate trajectory into the second quarter depends heavily on whether oil prices stabilize or continue to rise. April PMI data slipped into contraction territory with delivery times and input price inflation rising at their fastest pace since 2022. Euro-area consumer inflation expectations jumped across the board in March, and S&P cut credit ratings for Belgium and Slovakia while placing Finland on negative watch, reflecting fiscal deterioration partly attributable to elevated energy costs. Germany's €500 billion infrastructure stimulus fund may be the primary buffer preventing outright contraction, per Ifo President Clemens Fuest, though the fund's disbursement timeline limits its near-term impact.

Central Banks: The Pivot Toward Tightening

Both the ECB and the Bank of England held rates at their April meetings, in line with consensus expectations, but the accompanying signals were unmistakably hawkish. ECB President Christine Lagarde disclosed that policymakers debated an immediate increase but concluded they had insufficient information to act — language interpreted by those familiar with the deliberations as implying only a narrow path to avoiding a June hike if Hormuz-related energy disruption persists. Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel separately indicated he favors a June move absent a material improvement in the outlook. At the Bank of England, Chief Economist Huw Pill voted for an immediate increase, and Governor Andrew Bailey warned that waiting for second-round inflation effects to materialize before acting would mean responding too late. Market pricing has shifted dramatically: traders now price a June hike as the base case for both institutions, with three ECB increases priced for the year — a dramatic reversal from February's expectations of unchanged rates throughout 2026. The White House's Kevin Hassett publicly characterized potential central bank rate increases as policy errors, an unusual intervention in the affairs of independent institutions. A UK research group warned that a deepening Middle East crisis could tip the UK into recession and force aggressive rate hikes — a tail risk that may be underpriced given the country's energy import exposure.

UK Politics: Washington, Westminster, and Local Elections

The week produced a notable split-screen moment in UK politics. King Charles III completed a four-day state visit to Washington that included an address to a joint session of Congress — remarkable for the pointed political content of a constitutional monarch's appeal to reject isolationism and support NATO and Ukraine commitments — alongside bilateral meetings with President Trump. Trump announced the removal of some Scotch whisky tariffs as a personal gesture to the monarch, a tangible if narrowly scoped trade concession. British officials privately expressed skepticism that the royal visit would meaningfully alter Trump's underlying disposition toward Prime Minister Starmer or accelerate a broader UK-US trade agreement. Simultaneously, Starmer survived a parliamentary vote over his assurances regarding Peter Mandelson's appointment as ambassador to Washington, though the prolonged controversy constituted a costly distraction. Labour is projected to lose approximately 1,850 seats in May 7 local elections, including potentially control of the Welsh Parliament for the first time since its formation in 1999, a performance that would raise credible questions about the Prime Minister's political durability. The UK also raised its national terror threat level to severe following a stabbing in London's Golders Green district.

Corporate and Financial Developments

European bank earnings offered a broadly constructive read. UBS reported first-quarter net income of $3.04 billion, beating all estimates, with revenue rising nearly 30% driven by equities and foreign exchange trading; shares jumped 4.3% in Zurich on Tuesday and management confirmed intentions to add to its $3 billion share buyback program. Deutsche Bank posted higher-than-expected profit and revenue, supported by trading income and stronger contributions from asset management and retail banking. Banco Santander exceeded expectations, with its US expansion anchored by the Webster Financial acquisition providing incremental support. Standard Chartered, BNP Paribas, and Société Générale also reported first-quarter results that exceeded expectations. BP reported adjusted net income of $3.2 billion, more than double the year-earlier figure and ahead of the $2.64 billion analyst consensus, as surging oil prices and market volatility drove strong trading results. Barclays' trading division, by contrast, failed to match the outsized revenues generated by Wall Street peers. Lufthansa cancelled approximately 20,000 flights over the next six months in response to the surge in jet fuel prices, an early and visible sign of the war's operational consequences for European aviation. Adidas gained 8.3% in Frankfurt on strong apparel momentum. Bayer sought a favorable Supreme Court ruling to limit the scope of Roundup litigation that has cost the company more than $10 billion. EssilorLuxottica heir Leonardo Maria Del Vecchio agreed to buy out two siblings' stakes in family holding company Delfin for approximately €10 billion. The Netherlands announced plans to impose a 36% annual tax on unrealized gains from stocks, bonds, and cryptocurrencies effective 2028, drawing immediate concern about capital flight. ---

The Hormuz Standoff: Escalation Risk Rises

The Strait of Hormuz remains the dominant variable in global markets, and the week's developments suggest the conflict may be approaching an inflection point rather than winding down. Brent crude briefly surpassed $126 per barrel on Thursday after Axios reported that President Trump is scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options against Iran, and US Central Command is preparing fresh strike plans. Trump separately confirmed to Axios he will not lift the naval blockade without a nuclear agreement, removing near-term ambiguity about the conditions for resolution. The US also escalated its financial campaign, sanctioning Hengli Petrochemical's Dalian refinery — the first time Washington has targeted a major Chinese private refiner — and threatening secondary sanctions on banks supporting Chinese private refiners purchasing Iranian oil. Kpler estimates Iran has 12 to 22 days of remaining storage capacity at current production rates before output curtailments begin; Treasury Secretary Bessent indicated some well shutdowns may have already commenced. Six to eight supertankers remain clustered near Chabahar port in the Gulf of Oman, just outside the blockade perimeter. A single LNG tanker appeared to traverse the strait on Tuesday, the first such transit since the conflict began, though it remains unclear whether this represents a durable development. Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi traveled to Moscow to coordinate with Putin, and Tehran's latest proposal — conveyed through Pakistan — calls for reopening the strait in exchange for lifting the US port blockade, with nuclear negotiations deferred to a separate track. Washington's formal response to that proposal remains to be seen. Polymarket assigns only 52% odds to reopening by end-June, making a protracted closure the probabilistic base case.

Energy Markets and OPEC's Architecture

The UAE's decision to exit OPEC after 60 years adds a structural dimension to the energy market disruption that extends beyond near-term price dynamics. The departure reflects a strategic imperative to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand, and removes from the cartel's toolkit the spare production capacity that served as its primary price-defense mechanism in 2014 and 2020. The exit blindsided other members and raises questions about the cartel's long-term cohesion. Reports that the UAE is reconsidering participation in the Arab League, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation, and potentially the Gulf Cooperation Council suggest a durable strategic reorientation rather than a tactical maneuver. A Turkey-Saudi axis may emerge as a counterweight, with implications for the region's post-conflict order. Separately, US crude exports reached a record above 6 million barrels per day as importing nations seek alternatives to Middle Eastern supply. US shale producers are pumping near-record volumes of 13.6 million barrels per day, well-positioned at current prices but cautious about overextension given the forward curve's implied reversion toward $80 for later-year delivery. Goldman Sachs raised its fourth-quarter Brent forecast to $90 per barrel — up nearly $30 from pre-conflict projections — though spot prices remain elevated well above that level. Exxon Mobil and Chevron both exceeded profit expectations, underpinned by elevated realized prices.

Macro Transmission: Trade, Inflation, and Supply Chains

The economic ramifications of the Hormuz closure are broadening in ways that are becoming harder to dismiss as transient. Oxford Economics estimates global goods trade will expand just 2.5% in 2026, roughly half the 4.9% pace recorded in 2025, with the oil shock alone potentially subtracting 0.6% to 0.9% from world trade volumes. A Dallas Fed survey found 35% of Texas manufacturers are already feeling direct negative impact from the conflict.
