Monetary Policy

Fed Chair Warsh's congressional appearances this week arrive at a delicate moment. Two-year Treasury yields rose to multi-month highs as renewed Strait of Hormuz disruption pushed Brent crude toward $79/barrel, reinforcing inflation concerns. Bloomberg Intelligence data show S&P 500 companies are expected to report roughly 24% earnings growth in Q2 — among the strongest readings outside post-recession recoveries — yet that optimism is colliding with elevated energy costs and rising rate-hike odds. The FOMC reportedly contemplated a rate increase at its most recent meeting, and June CPI, released Tuesday, will be closely parsed for confirmation of that impulse. Warsh has signaled skepticism toward forward guidance, leaving markets with limited visibility into the Fed's reaction function.

Equities and Corporate

US equity futures declined modestly Monday, with Nasdaq 100 futures off 0.9–1.2%, as the Korea semiconductor selloff weighed on memory-related names — Micron fell 5.6%, Western Digital 6.7%, SanDisk 7.3%. Energy stocks partially offset the damage; Occidental Petroleum gained 2%, Chevron 1.3%. Q2 earnings season opens Tuesday with five of the six largest US banks reporting. Analysts caution that with valuations stretched near record levels, companies face a high bar — consensus already reflects an unusually favorable profit environment, leaving little margin for disappointment. ---

Korean Markets

The MSCI Korea Index has fallen 28% from its June high, and a measure of 60-day price swings has reached its highest level since February 1998 — exceeding even the 2008 financial crisis. Circuit breakers have been triggered seven times this year, against six in the prior 25 years. The proximate catalyst Monday was rotation out of Korean-listed shares into SK Hynix's newly listed US ADRs, combined with concerns about AI spending sustainability. The irony is notable: SK Hynix raised $26.5 billion in what was the largest-ever US listing by a foreign company, and its CEO reiterated that memory-chip shortages will likely persist beyond 2030. Samsung, which surged 19-fold in profits last quarter, fell nearly 11%. Structural factors — single-stock ETF leverage amplification and the outsized combined weight of SK Hynix and Samsung — appear to be magnifying moves beyond what fundamentals alone would justify.

Japan and Capital Flows

The yen's brief strengthening on Finance Minister Katayama's pension repatriation signal reversed after Reuters reported that no formal GPIF allocation overhaul is imminent. Deutsche Bank's strategists argue the yen's persistent weakness reflects the gap between Japanese short-term rates and global peers, with overnight index swaps pricing rates at only 1.2% by year-end. Thirty-year JGB yields already exceed equivalent German bunds despite the ECB having tightened far more aggressively — a tension that may signal continued pressure on the BOJ to accelerate its pace of normalization. Indian equities, by contrast, are benefiting from regional reallocation, drawing $1.3 billion in net foreign buying in the four days through July 9. ---

UK Political Economy

The UK's political transition appears to be accelerating. Burnham's allies are reportedly exploring a land tax, public utility renationalization, and deeper devolution — a materially more interventionist fiscal posture than Reeves pursued. Mansion House this week may function less as a policy announcement and more as a symbolic close to a chapter. The death of Senator Graham, meanwhile, removes a key interlocutor between US allies and the Trump administration at a sensitive moment in Ukraine diplomacy, with potential downstream implications for European defense coordination.

Corporate and Industrial

Akzo Nobel's rejection of Nippon Paint's €7.5 billion approach — made without board engagement — keeps its Axalta merger on track but raises questions about whether Nippon Paint will return with a higher bid or pursue other avenues. Volkswagen's CEO failed to secure supervisory board backing for a structural overhaul, leaving Europe's largest automaker in strategic limbo. The EU is separately developing a solidarity instrument to support supply-chain diversification away from China, reflecting the broader European push for industrial sovereignty that also underpins Germany's €35 billion space defense program announced by Defense Minister Pistorius. ---

Argentina Political Economy

Milei's political capital has eroded meaningfully. Corruption — once weaponized effectively against the Peronist opposition — has become a liability for his own administration, narrowing the political space he previously occupied. With investor focus increasingly shifting toward the 2027 electoral cycle, the sustainability of his reform program is under renewed scrutiny. The IMF's latest World Economic Outlook projects 3% global GDP growth for 2026, but Argentina's idiosyncratic political dynamics mean that external resilience may not translate directly into domestic stabilization. The question of whether a reconstituted Peronist opposition can credibly capture the reform narrative is becoming a live investment consideration.

Regional Context

Latin America's broader macro backdrop is shaped heavily by the Iran conflict's commodity pass-through. Fertilizer price increases of more than 30% earlier this year, if sustained, will weigh on agricultural-export economies including Brazil and Argentina at a critical point in the production cycle. El Niño-related crop yield disruptions pose additional risk, particularly for food-price inflation in economies where food represents a large CPI share and pass-through from global grain markets is significant. ---

Oil and the Strait of Hormuz

The conflict has entered a pattern of persistent, low-level escalation rather than decisive resolution. Kpler data show all six commodity carriers transiting Hormuz on Sunday did so with transponders off — a sign of ongoing disruption even if outright closure has not been fully enforced. Markets appear to be pricing this as a "Tanker Wars" scenario of prolonged but manageable friction rather than a binary supply shock, which may explain the relatively contained price response versus the 2022 energy crisis. The more consequential transmission channel may be fertilizer: urea prices spiked over 30% during the conflict's initial phase, and with El Niño simultaneously weighing on crop yields in Southeast Asia and sub-Saharan Africa, food inflation risks are becoming a genuine macroeconomic concern rather than a peripheral one.

Monetary Policy and Dollar Dynamics

The Iran conflict's inflationary pressure arrives as major central banks navigate already-elevated price levels. Notably, the dollar's rise during the conflict has been modest by historical standards — Treasuries did not surge as a safe-haven destination as they would have in prior cycles. Standard Bank's Steven Barrow argues this reflects the waning of US financial hegemony: a structural shift that may paradoxically enhance global economic resilience by reducing the tightening of financial conditions in the rest of the world during periods of stress. That framework, if correct, helps explain why the IMF's global growth projection remains relatively robust despite concurrent shocks of historic severity.
