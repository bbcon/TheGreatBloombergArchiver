The blocked Strait of Hormuz doesn’t appear 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/trump-tells-axios-he-rejects-iran-hormuz-offer-keeps-blockade?cmpid=BBD043026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3937>
 to be opening anytime soon, hampering not justenergy flows 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/us-iran-standoff-shows-no-sign-of-easing-as-trump-backs-blockade?cmpid=BBD043026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3937>
 but also shipping containers trying to move through Persian Gulf ports like 
Dubai’s Jebel Ali — the world’s busiest outside of Asia.

On land, some relief valves are starting to widen.

According to shipping analyst Lars Jensen, the closure “caused a boom in 
traffic” for Gulftainer’s container terminal in Khor Fakkan in the United Arab 
Emirates.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-april-30?cmpid=BBD043026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3937>

The facility — just outside the strait on the Gulf of Oman — is now handling 
about 6,000 trucks a day compared with about 100 before the conflict, he wrote 
Thursday in aLinkedIn post 
<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/larsjensenvespuccimaritime_the-closure-of-the-strait-of-hormuz-has-caused-activity-7455463085076574208-SOlL/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_ios&rcm=ACoAAAQhrDgB0bYwZGt1gaWdmMK9EIR1HhRFMEU>
.

Pre-war, Khor Fakkan was known more as a transshipment hub, not set up to 
handle a lot of inland-moving cargo. But its operations are adapting quickly, 
according to Jensen.

Overflow Capacity

“This has, amongst others, included the expansion from two to 10 gates in a 
matter of weeks as well as the construction of a large new marshalling yard,” 
said Jensen, the CEO of Vespucci Maritime.

A spokesman for Gulftainer couldn’t be immediately reached for comment.

By truck, Khor Fakkan is about a two-hour drive to DP World’s Jebel Ali and 
Jafza, the industrial free zone connected to the massive cargo facility.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/gulf-trucking-bypass-moves-metal-consumer-goods-around-strait?cmpid=BBD043026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3937>

According to IMF PortWatch, Jebel Ali’s container vessel arrivals 
<https://portwatch.imf.org/pages/d10ddc98bfee43949a09ea4d790a18f8> have dropped 
to about one a day over the past two months, from a daily average of about 12 
before the US-Israeli invasion of Iran began Feb. 28.

As of today, there’s a cluster of seven container carriers waiting outside 
Khor Fakkan with it identified as their next destination, according to shipping 
data compiled by Bloomberg shown in this map:
Source: Bloomberg {MAP <GO>}
The buildup in ground haulage capacity across the Gulf region is getting a 
lift from companies like Trukker, a UAE-based business that started an 
Uber-like service for trucks a decade ago.

In an interview earlier this month, Chief Executive Officer Gaurav Biswas said 
significant investments are being planned for permanent solutions to cargo 
movements, especially from eastern UAE.

“Khor Fakkan is a significantly smaller port than Jebel Ali or Abu Dhabi 
ports, so there’s a big queue of vessels that are waiting to offload containers 
and pick up other containers,” Biswas said. “Trucking is scaling up and down 
based on that volume of containers that it can handle.”
