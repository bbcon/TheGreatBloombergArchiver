The intersection of the US-Iran conflict and global energy markets continues to represent the most consequential near-term macro risk across regions. The source material references "war in Iran" as actively disrupting global energy markets, contributing to gasoline price increases that are feeding into household affordability stress in the United States and, almost certainly, in energy-importing economies elsewhere. The Strait of Hormuz — through which a substantial share of global oil flows — remains a focal point of supply vulnerability, though the precise operational status and risk of escalation remains unclear from available reporting. What is apparent is that energy price volatility is already transmitting into consumer prices and political dynamics with notable speed.

The Pentagon's quiet continuation of climate adaptation work at military installations — rebranded as "resilience" and "readiness" rather than climate response — reflects a broader tension playing out across governments: the gap between political positioning on climate and the practical decisions driven by asset exposure and operational necessity. Close to $400 billion in US federal assets face elevated flood risk; similar pressures apply to infrastructure across coastal Asia and Europe. China and India's recently published climate plans both signal a decade of cautious incrementalism rather than accelerated transition, suggesting that global emissions trajectories are unlikely to shift materially in the near term despite the scale of physical risk accumulating.

On global monetary policy, divergence across major central banks remains a structuring theme, though no fresh policy decisions were signaled this week. The combination of trade-related inflation pressure in the US, export-driven cyclical support in China, and gradually easing inflation in parts of Europe creates an environment in which rate path convergence remains distant — a condition that tends to sustain dollar volatility and complicate capital allocation across emerging markets.
