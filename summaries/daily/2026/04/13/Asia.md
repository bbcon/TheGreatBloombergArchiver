Asian markets absorbed the collapse of US-Iran talks and the subsequent US blockade announcement with moderate rather than acute selling pressure. A gauge of Asian equities fell around 0.8%, while Japan's Topix and South Korea's Kospi pared intraday losses; Taiwan's Taiex notably ended the session higher, supported by Taiwan's export data, which surged to an all-time high as global AI chip demand more than offset supply chain disruptions from the Strait closure.

The more consequential dynamic may involve China. Iran is a major oil supplier to China, and Saudi Arabia's crude shipments to Chinese customers are reportedly set to halve in May — to approximately 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April — as Aramco raised official selling prices to a record. The US blockade raises a direct question about whether US warships will interdict vessels carrying Iranian oil to China, which would constitute a significant escalation in US-China tensions. Former US Ambassador to Saudi Arabia Michael Ratney flagged this risk explicitly. China has thus far condemned the US-Israeli strikes on Iran as "unacceptable" but President Xi has not publicly commented on the blockade. Spain's Prime Minister Sánchez, speaking at Tsinghua University during a Beijing visit, called on China to leverage its influence to end the Iran and Ukraine conflicts. Xi is scheduled to host Trump next month.

In Japan, BOJ Governor Kazuo Ueda signaled a more cautious stance on rate hikes ahead of the next policy meeting, a notable pullback given that markets had anticipated tightening as recently as this month. Japan's 10-year government bond yield climbed to 2.49%, its highest level since 1997, as global bonds sold off on inflation concerns. Supply chain effects are becoming visible in the real economy: toilet manufacturer Toto suspended new prefabricated bathroom orders citing naphtha shortages, joining a growing list of Japanese manufacturers cutting production or raising prices as Persian Gulf oil feedstock supplies tighten.
