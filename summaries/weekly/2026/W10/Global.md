**The US-Iran Conflict: Scope and Uncertainty**
The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike — the most direct US targeting of a foreign head of state in the modern era — defined the week's macro backdrop across every asset class and region. By week's end, the conflict had expanded geographically: NATO air defenses downed a ballistic missile over Turkey, Iranian missiles and drones struck Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Bahrain, Israeli airstrikes continued on Tehran, and a US submarine conducted the first American submarine attack on a surface warship since the Second World War, sinking the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka. US Defense Secretary Hegseth estimated operations could last three to eight weeks. Iran's signals remained contradictory throughout — President Pezeshkian vowed retaliation, security chief Larijani rejected US talks publicly, yet the Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries, and on Wednesday the New York Times reported indirect Iranian contact with Washington exploring a ceasefire, prompting an equity rebound. The endgame remains genuinely opaque.

**Energy Markets**
Brent crude surged approximately 12.5% at Monday's open before partially retracing, then continued higher through the week to settle above $82 per barrel by Thursday, representing an approximately 18% weekly gain — levels last seen in July 2024. US diesel futures were on track for a weekly gain exceeding 40%. The Strait of Hormuz effectively ceased functioning as an oil transit corridor; only two commercial vessel transits were recorded in a 24-hour period by Friday and no oil shipments passed through at all on certain days. More than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs announced withdrawal of war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels. Saudi Arabia suspended operations at its 550,000 barrel-per-day Ras Tanura refinery following a drone strike. Goldman Sachs flagged the plausibility of Brent crossing $100 per barrel if low flows persist for another five weeks; Bloomberg Economics modeled a severe scenario in which prolonged closure lifts Brent to approximately $108. Iran's military commander stated Tehran "does not believe in closing the Strait of Hormuz at all" — a signal worth monitoring carefully, as an explicit blockade would represent the most severe escalation scenario. OPEC+'s modest April production increase, announced Sunday, is of limited utility if tankers cannot safely transit the strait.

**Monetary Policy and Safe-Haven Dynamics**
Central banks globally face a structurally uncomfortable supply-side inflation shock — the fourth major such event in six years following Covid, the Russia-Ukraine war, and mercantilist trade disruptions. The IMF's Georgieva warned a sustained 10% energy price increase would push global inflation up 40 basis points and shave 0.1-0.2% from growth. The monetary policy implications are particularly acute for central banks still managing above-target inflation. Traditional safe-haven assets behaved anomalously throughout the week: gold wavered and was trading at a steep discount in Dubai, while Treasuries sold off rather than rallying, leaving the US dollar as the primary haven vehicle — the DXY broke above its 200-day moving average early in the week and extended gains, on track for its largest weekly rise in over a year. Emerging market currencies came under broad pressure, with the FTSE EM index falling 2% Monday — its worst session in eleven months.

**Geopolitical Realignments**
Russia has emerged as the clearest early beneficiary of the conflict: elevated energy prices replenish Kremlin revenues at a critical juncture when sanctions had been beginning to bite, and India's return to Russian crude purchasing — facilitated by the US Treasury waiver — reinforces the trend. The minerals competition between the US and China adds a further dimension to the week's geopolitical texture: China conducted roughly $200 billion in annual trade with sub-Saharan Africa in 2024, approximately four times the US figure, and controls an estimated 80% of Congo's copper output. The Trump administration's push to secure Congolese copper and cobalt stakes represents strategic intent, though closing that gap without decades of accumulated relationship capital appears a formidable task. A new study published in Geophysical Research Letters provided the first statistically significant evidence of an acceleration in global warming — at approximately 0.35°C per decade since 2015, nearly double the 1970-2015 average rate — adding long-term structural urgency to energy transition debates even as immediate policy attention is consumed by the conflict.
