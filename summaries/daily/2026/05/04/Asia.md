Asian equity markets climbed to fresh record highs, led by AI-linked semiconductor names in South Korea and Taiwan, whose benchmark indices surged 5.1% and 4.6% respectively. The rally reflects strong earnings momentum from chipmakers supplying the global AI infrastructure build-out, and appears to be shrugging off near-term headwinds from the Hormuz disruption. Morgan Stanley's chief Asia economist Chetan Ahya has characterized the dynamic as the beginning of a multi-year industrial super-cycle, arguing that structural forces — AI infrastructure, energy transition, and defense spending — will broaden from a narrow semiconductor rally into wider capital expenditure, job creation, and ultimately consumption growth across the region. Taiwan's first-quarter GDP expansion and South Korea's export boom are cited as early evidence. The cycle is expected to last three to four years, with Taiwan, South Korea, China, and Japan identified as the primary beneficiaries.

China's posture on US sanctions hardened materially over the weekend. Beijing ordered its companies to disregard US secondary sanctions — an explicit act of defiance that represents a meaningful departure from its historically cautious approach of quiet compliance. The immediate trigger was Washington's decision to sanction Hengli Petrochemical, one of China's largest private refiners, over Iranian crude purchases. Energy security appears to be the key threshold: private refiners now account for roughly a third of China's processing capacity and have been integral to maintaining fuel supply during the Hormuz disruption. The directive likely signals that Beijing views energy access as a non-negotiable red line ahead of the Trump-Xi summit expected in approximately ten days.

Japan's Ministry of Finance intervened in foreign exchange markets, spending approximately $34 billion to buy yen after the currency breached ¥160 against the dollar following the Federal Reserve's meeting last week. The intervention produced the yen's largest single-day gain since 2022. However, analysts are skeptical of its durability given that Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi is perceived as holding a structurally dovish fiscal and monetary stance, the Bank of Japan remains cautious about committing to near-term rate hikes, and Japan's dependence on Hormuz-transiting oil continues to weigh on the currency structurally. The Bank of Korea, meanwhile, signaled that rate increases may be warranted, citing stronger-than-expected economic resilience and mounting inflationary pressures.
