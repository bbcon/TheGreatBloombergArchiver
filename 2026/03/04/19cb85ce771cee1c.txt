Asian stock traders could well be forgiven for asking, what did we ever do to 
Donald Trump? Because it’s the region’s financial markets that are seeing the
biggest impact <https://bloom.bg/3P0pLSZ> from the US war with Iran — and Asian 
investors who are losing their shirts.

Take South Korea, where the Kospi Index plunged 12% on Wednesday, the most in 
its history <https://bloom.bg/4l85tmE>, and adding to a 7.2% tumble the 
previous day. Or Thailand: its stock benchmark sankas much as 
<https://bloom.bg/4srFgSw> 8.6%. Or Taiwan, which saw its main gauge slump more 
than 4%.

Perhaps more irking for investors is the limited effect the surprise conflict 
has had on US equities. While MSCI’s Asian index has lost about 9% this week, 
its worst three-day loss since Trump’s “Liberation Day” tariffs were unleashed 
last April, the S&P 500 has dipped less than 1% — and futures for the US 
benchmark suggest a gentle open later Wednesday.

As if to rub it in, Goldman Sachs Chairman David Solomon said he’s been 
surprised by the“benign” reaction <https://bloom.bg/4aNa1v9> in financial 
markets to the Middle East conflict. He was speaking at the Australian 
Financial Review Business Summit in Sydney on Wednesday morning.

Asia is particularly vulnerable <https://bloom.bg/4spDPE1> to a jump in oil 
and gas prices due to the region’s reliance on imported energy, especially 
Korea, Thailand and India — where the rupee fell to a record low on Wednesday. 
A hunt for havens is promptingflows away <https://bloom.bg/4bkTsa6> from Asia 
and into US assets, with the Bloomberg dollar index climbing 1.4% this week.

And the biggest decliners in Asia have been the some of hottest trades this 
year. Korea’s stock market had beenlooking bubbly  <https://bloom.bg/3OKWlIw>
after a 50% rally this year, while the Thai benchmark was up more than 20%.

“Asian markets are choking on a toxic cocktail — surging energy prices, a 
resurgent dollar, and geopolitical tensions that nobody is sleeping through 
anymore,” said Hebe Chen, a senior market analyst at Vantage Global Prime.

With investors in the dark about how the conflict develops — Solomon said it 
will take weeks to understand more about the situation — Asia, which had 
benefited from the AI boom, faces the risk of potentially destabilizing capital 
outflows continuing untilsome clarity <https://bloom.bg/4blkQEO> emerges. 
—Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



More on Korea’s market and its historic move, which few saw coming.

Before this week’s cataclysm, optimism on AI <https://bloom.bg/4aOngvz> and 
the ensuing demand for memory chips was so strong that retail investors were 
piling in with borrowed money and analysts were raising their already-bullish 
calls on Korean equities.

Adding to the fevered trading, President Lee Jae Myung’s administration 
actively encouraged equity investment, seeing a booming stock market as one way 
to relieve economic woes ranging from sluggish consumption to a property 
bubble. The rally helped South Korea’s National Pension Service, one of the 
world’s largest public pension funds, postits strongest-ever 
<https://bloom.bg/4ld7i1O> annual return in 2025.

There were, of course, signs that exuberance was becoming irrational. Margin 
debt as well asinvestor deposits <https://bloom.bg/4sfOi4W> at brokerages 
surged to new highs, while the rally was focused on a handful of big stocks.

“There’s been a lot of buying on credit, especially those heavyweight stocks, 
with investors putting down only 30%-40% in margin deposit,” said Kim Dojoon, 
chief executive and investment officer at Seoul-based Zian Investment 
Management.

But now, the severity of Wednesday’s declines — with Samsung Electronics down 
12%, Hyundai Motor losing 16% and SK Hynix off by about 10% — has broken the 
investment case for what many investors saw was a one-way route to riches.




Spanish Prime Minister Pedro Sánchez said he won’t yield to threats over his 
objections to the US and Israeli war in Iran,extending a dispute 
<https://bloom.bg/4aNqSOn> with Trump, who threatened to cut off trade with the 
Mediterranean country.

“We are not going to be complicit in something that is harmful to the world 
and contrary to our values and interests simply out of fear of someone’s 
retaliation,” Sánchez said in a speech Wednesday.

Trump on Tuesday said the US would “cut off all trade 
<https://bloom.bg/4b8AUJ3> with Spain,” criticizing Spain for denying access to 
its military bases for the American bombing campaign against Iran.
Not backing down: Pedro Sanchez, Spain's prime minister. Photographer: Michael 
Nagle/Bloomberg 



Airlines around the globe are suspending more flights to additional 
destinations across the Middle East as the scope of the war expands during its 
fourth day.

Qatar Airways extended the suspension of services into Friday. The disruptions 
since Iran’s first retaliatory strike arespreading across 
<https://bloom.bg/4l7Unhf> the region, leading to more than 12,300 flights 
being canceled, including at major transfer hubs such as Dubai and Doha, 
according to Flightradar24.

The cancellations will reverberate beyond the current period because they 
leave planes out of position and crew members in the wrong locations. Tens of 
thousands of passengers are stranded, so many carriers are running special 
evacuation flights.




The funeral of Iran’s late Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei, who was 
killed in a US-Israeli airstrike last weekend, will begin on Wednesday, in a 
gathering that’s likely to rally supporters <https://bloom.bg/3MTvPMz> of the 
Islamic Republic amid the war.

The three-day ceremony at the Imam Khomeini Mosalla of Tehran is scheduled to 
start at 10 p.m. local time and will see mourners pay their respects to 
Khamenei’s body, the state-run Islamic Republic News Agency reported. He’ll 
then be buried in the northeastern city of Mashhad.

Let’s not forget about AI. Billionaire Leo KoGuan, who emerged a few years ago 
as one of the biggest individual shareholders of Tesla, said he bought 1 
million shares of Nvidia on Tuesday.

“I am convinced AI is NOT a bubble <https://bloom.bg/4b2EF2i>, it is only the 
beginning,” KoGuan posted on X.

“Plan to buy 1 million more NVDA soon. To show support to nervous market,” he 
followed up in a message to Bloomberg News. Nvidia closed at $180.05, meaning 
the 71-year-old billionaire would’ve paid roughly $180 million. He has a $12.8 
billion fortune, according to the Bloomberg Billionaires Index.




Several Chinese financial firms are scaling back exposure to Middle Eastern 
debt, while regulators are stepping up oversight as the conflict raises 
concerns over the nation’s extensive lending in the region.

One major bank took a rare step <https://bloom.bg/40b99dH> in restricting a 
drawdown on a bilateral loan to one of the Abu Dhabi government’s financial 
entities, a person familiar with the matter said. A Chinese insurer’s asset 
management arm is reducing holdings of sovereign and state-linked bonds, 
including those issued by Saudi Aramco, another said. China has directed 
domestic lenders to examine their financing activities in the region — 
including debt extended to state entities — and report their findings as early 
as this week, separate people have said.




In US politics, Senator John Cornyn and Texas Attorney General Ken Paxton are  
heading for a runoff <https://bloom.bg/4aMLOFg> in the Republican primary for 
the US Senate in Texas, while state Representative James Talarico defeated 
Congresswoman Jasmine Crockett in a Democratic race that was marred by legal 
challenges.

In the GOP primary, Cornyn was leading with a little more than 42% support, 
less than two points over Paxton as of early Wednesday. A third candidate, US 
Representative Wesley Hunt, won enough votes to deny either a majority, sending 
Cornyn and Paxton to a two-person race to be decided in May. The runoff sets up 
more than two months of additional campaigning that will further drain the 
candidates’ coffers as Republican seek to defend their majority in the Senate 
in November.
Senator John Cornyn during a news conference in Austin on Tuesday. 
Photographer: Kaylee Greenlee/Bloomberg 



A liquefied natural gas tanker, sanctioned by the US for carrying blacklisted 
Russian fuel,was attacked <bbg://news/stories/TBCNQ4T96OSJ> in the 
Mediterranean Sea, according to Russia’s Transport Ministry.

Arctic Metagaz was targeted near Malta’s territorial waters by Ukrainian drone 
boats, the ministry said in a post on Telegram. The Armed Forces of Malta said 
it assisted with evacuating the crew on Tuesday after it was informed of a 
distressed situation, according to a post on Facebook. The tanker was likely en 
route to China, which has been the sole buyer of gas from sanctioned Russian 
export projects, according to ship-tracking data.

 For Your Commute 



The same AI model that can help you draft a marketing email or a quick dinner 
recipe has also been used to attack Iran, writes Bloomberg Opinion’s Parmy 
Olson. US Central Command used Anthropic’s Claude AI for “intelligence 
assessments, target identification and simulating battle scenarios” during the 
strikes on the country, according to a report in the Wall Street Journal.

Hours earlier, Trump had ordered federal agencies to stop using Claude after a 
dispute with its maker, but the tool was so deeply baked into the Pentagon’s 
systems that it would take months to untangle in favor of a more compliant 
rival. It was used, too, in the January operation that led to the capture of 
Nicolás Maduro.

But what does “intelligence assessments” and “target identification” mean in 
practice? Was Claude flagging locations to strike or making casualty estimates? 
Nobody has made that disclosure and, alarmingly, no one has an obligation to.
