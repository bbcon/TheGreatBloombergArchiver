# Daily Briefing — May 20, 2026

## Global Cross-Currents: Energy, Bonds, and the Hormuz Tipping Point

**The Hormuz closure approaches an inflection point.** With the Strait shut for nearly three months following the outbreak of the US-Iran conflict, HSBC's global economics team argues that even a swift reopening would now leave durable scars on inflation and growth. The bank's revised base case envisions gradual restart of Hormuz traffic from mid-June, though NATO is reportedly discussing intervention to escort vessels through the waterway if it remains closed by early July. India has finalized plans to send tankers through the strait pending government approval, and a South Korean-flagged supertanker appears to be attempting a similar crossing. WTI traded near $107.77, easing modestly as markets weighed President Trump's renewed threat to resume strikes against Iran — a threat now meeting some resistance within the Republican-led Senate. Barclays' Ajay Rajadhyaksha has lifted his 2026 Brent average to $100; in the options market, one trader paid for a position that would pay up to $129 million on a sharp Brent decline by early next week.

**Monetary policy is tilting hawkish across regions.** Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields has reached its highest level since July 2008, with 30-year US Treasuries hitting 5.19% — a 19-year high — and Japanese long yields approaching 3%. Bank Indonesia surprised with a 50bp hike to 5.25% to defend the rupiah; Iceland also tightened. The ECB is widely expected to hike in June, and HSBC argues a 2026 Fed hike "can't be ruled out." UK inflation, by contrast, fell to a one-year low, prompting markets to pare BoE hike expectations — a rare instance of divergence.

**Energy security is reshaping policy.** Germany's gas storage sits below 30% full with the inverted forward curve discouraging injections; traders increasingly expect Berlin to intervene. The UK quietly loosened Russia sanctions to permit imports of diesel and jet fuel refined from Russian crude in third countries — a pragmatic concession to wartime supply constraints. Indonesia announced state centralization of palm oil, thermal coal, and nickel exports, citing fiscal pressure and currency weakness.

The combination of equity valuations reminiscent of 2000 and bond yields reminiscent of 2007 remains the defining tension, with AI capex the principal counterweight to deteriorating financial conditions.
