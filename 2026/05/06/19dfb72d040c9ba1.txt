Yet Another Record

US stocks are at yet another record. It’s remarkable how much it’s been driven 
by momentum, while investors seem impervious to issues of quality — the 
catch-all term for companies with steady profits and resilient balance sheets.

It might be unfair to call this a dash for trash. But nobody seems to care 
about quality. This is from Bloomberg’s Factors To Watch service, showing the 
results of buying the top 20% of stocks by a factor while shorting the worst 
20%. Since Liberation Day, buying quality stocks has been a one-way ticket to 
losses:

This isn’t just an American phenomenon. If we do the same exercise for 
Bloomberg’s stock universe for the world excluding the US, momentum is far more 
dominant. Of late the liquidity factor — buying the stocks with the greatest 
trading volumes — has been even more impressive:

In general, the stocks doing well are exactly the same ones that had momentum 
behind them before the double shocks of tariffs last year and now the Middle 
East conflict. A gusher of money is continuing to propel them. Despite all the 
geopolitical uncertainty, investors don’t seem prepared to pay anything for the 
extra security that comes with a robust balance sheet.

When a rally is moving like this, it can be dangerous to try to stand in front 
of it — which helps to maintain the momentum. And with governments spending 
money as companies splurge on capex, the environment for equities is 
propitious. None of that changes the fact that this rally is chiefly driven by 
liquidity and animal spirits. That would make it vulnerable to a sharp turn if 
the news worsens.

Earnings Should Give No Quarter

The Trump administration has pressed on with its plan to release companies 
<https://bloom.bg/4wrK1yi> from the burden of reporting quarterly, and for this 
it has to be commended. There are always risks in allowing companies to be less 
transparent, but benefits inthe Securities and Exchange Commission’s proposal 
<https://bloom.bg/4dp0BqW> outbalanced them. It will help the combat against 
short-termism <https://bloom.bg/42iZVO7>.

We can do without quarterly earnings because they in fact have zero impact on 
markets <https://bloom.bg/4ds1XB6>, as Points of Return pointed out way back in 
2019. The quarterly game played by investor relations departments and their 
brokers is a complete wash, creating some interesting and predictable effects 
for traders, but having no impact at all on share prices’ longer-term 
trajectory.

The need to publish regularly, and to avoid negative surprises for the market, 
has encouraged companies to devote time and energy to earnings management 
rather than long-term planning. General Electric’s feat of meeting orexceeding 
earnings forecasts every quarter for a decade <https://bloom.bg/4drJWmw> under 
the legendary CEO Jack Welch looked brilliant at the time, buthits differently 
<https://bloom.bg/3QUE4ti> given the company’s decline since his departure.

Will it make a big difference? Barclays finds that in Europe, which doesn’t 
require quarterly reports, roughly 52% of STOXX companies report every quarter 
anyway. That percentage is fairly steady but has grown from 45% 10 years ago. 
So it’s unlikely that the SEC is about to abolish the quarterly earnings season 
altogether.

Indeed, the regulators seem genuinely open-minded, asking 47 questions and 
floating a number of different alternatives, including making a mandatory 
switch to reporting twice a year, offering the option of semiannual reporting 
only to smaller companies. This suggests to Michael McLean of Barclays that the 
SEC doesn’t have a strong view and actually wants some feedback. It’s testing 
market reaction for the next 60 days, and it would be good if people encouraged 
regulators to carry on with the idea.


Shooting the Messenger

Killing the bearer of bad news, everyone knows, is unfair and achieves 
nothing. But last year’s firing of the Bureau of Labor Statistics’ Erika 
McEntarfer <https://bloom.bg/4w7YjE5>, for the crime of having been appointed 
under President Joe Biden and then making a big upward revision to 
unemployment, appears to have been worse than that. It might even have 
inflicted damage worth $20 billion on the US economy.

The impact of government economic numbers is hard to establish, but they do 
matter; a consumer price index off by as little as 1/10th of a percentage point 
could result in a $1 billion overpayment or underpayment to Social Security 
beneficiaries.

According to a new study <https://bloom.bg/4wcUjSK>, her dismissal overlapped 
with a decline in industrial investment of about $20 billion. “Just that 
reduction in trust from the firing has an economic impact 25 times greater than 
the budget of BLS,” said one of the researchers, Erica Groshen, head of the 
bureau from 2013-17 and now senior labor market adviser at Cornell University’s 
ILR School. “When trust in government statistics is eroded, this is the cost of 
people flying blind, or with a cloudier windshield.”

Even if executives had already been driving blind due to post-pandemic 
problems with information-gathering, sacking McEntarfer made them more 
conscious that they couldn’t rely on the data and less likely to make big 
commitments. Many will say this report is the messenger getting off a 
retaliatory shot, as one of the authors (the others include researchers from 
Stanford University and the American Enterprise Institute) is a BLS alumna. But 
critics still need to prove them wrong by producing their own numbers.
