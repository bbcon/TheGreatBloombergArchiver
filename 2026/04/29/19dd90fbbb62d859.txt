Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
OPEC reporter Grant Smith explores the rationale behind Abu Dhabi’s surprise 
decision to leave the organization after 60 years.

The story of the UAE’s shock exit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/uae-exit-blindsides-opec-and-threatens-to-shake-its-grip-on-oil?cmpid=BBBXT042926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3969>
 from OPEC is ultimately about the twilight of the oil age.

On the surface, the decision by the United Arab Emirates to leave the 
organization 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/uae-to-leave-opec-and-opec-next-month-to-pursue-new-strategy?cmpid=BBBXT042926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3969>
 appears to be a matter of frustration with group leader Saudi Arabia.

For much of this decade, Abu Dhabi’s impatience to use new oil-production 
capacity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-05-02/uae-s-main-oil-company-raises-crude-production-capacity?cmpid=BBBXT042926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3969>
 it’s built up has brought the country into conflict with Riyadh, which urged 
supply restraint to defend prices.

But during the past year, the Saudis have also embraced a much looser stance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-01/saudi-oil-supply-hikes-power-best-economic-growth-since-2022?cmpid=BBBXT042926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3969>
 on output, steering OPEC and its allies to swiftly bring back idled barrels.

The question, then, becomes why the UAE is in such a hurry to slip the 
confines of the Organization of the Petroleum Exporting Countries.

Its public announcement of the departure offers clues, such as allusions to a 
“new energy age.”
UAE Energy Minister Suhail Al-Mazrouei. His ministry’s statement said the 
country will be investing “across the energy value chain.”Photographer: THAIER 
AL-SUDANI/AFP via Getty Images
One person familiar with the country’s policymaking said it’s pursuing a 
strategy to optimize the use of abundant resources while the energy transition 
gathers pace.

Put more simply, the UAE feels an urgency to pump as much oil as possible 
before the world’s pivot away from fossil fuels erodes consumption.

To be sure, that’s not exactly the official line.

In its statement, Abu Dhabi pointed to “sustained growth in global energy 
demand.” The organization it’s just left insists that oil consumption will keep 
rising until at least themiddle of the century 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-10/opec-fortifies-outlier-view-that-oil-demand-will-grow-to-2050?cmpid=BBBXT042926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3969>
.

But views among the wider industry suggest a much more imminent demand 
plateau, perhaps within the next decade.

Predictions have been pushed back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-12/iea-reinstates-bullish-oil-demand-growth-scenario-in-key-report?cmpid=BBBXT042926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260429&utm_content=3969>
 recently. The difficulty of hitting climate targets and the necessity of 
ensuring supplies amid conflicts such as Russia’s war on Ukraine focused minds 
on energy security.

Yet the mere suggestion of a peak on the horizon, whatever the date, 
undermines OPEC’s customary tactic in managing oil markets: securing a better 
price for their production by keeping some barrels in the ground.

The UAE’s move underscores a desire to extract that crude while it still can.

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News
