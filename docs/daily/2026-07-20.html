Daily Brief

Monday, July 20, 2026

US

  • Q2 earnings season is accelerating with consensus expecting 23.4% S&P 500 EPS growth, above what ISM manufacturing and services PMIs alone would historically imply, driven in part by technology outperformance.
  • Fed Chair Kevin Warsh reiterated an inflation-watch posture last week; the week ahead is light on US data releases.
  • Brent crude advanced above $90 intraday on continued US-Iran hostilities, pushing US gasoline above $4/gallon for the first time in a month; the Strategic Petroleum Reserve sits at its lowest since 1983.
  • Big Tech earnings — Alphabet and Tesla on Wednesday, Microsoft/Meta/Apple/Amazon the following week — will be scrutinized for evidence that record capex is generating returns.

Earnings Breadth

Q2 S&P 500 earnings are tracking toward 23.4% year-on-year growth per consensus, a figure that meaningfully exceeds what macro indicators alone would predict. Deutsche Bank's Binky Chadha notes that ex-tech and AI, the "rest" of the S&P 500 is projected to grow earnings 14.3%, adding 9.1 percentage points to aggregate growth — a cyclical rebound supported by the ISM manufacturing index returning to expansionary territory. Consumer discretionary is on track for high-single-digit EPS growth, suggesting US consumers have thus far absorbed 18 months of tariff and energy-driven price pressures. The key risk, per ClearBridge Investments, is a material deterioration in labor markets.

Markets and Valuation

The Magnificent Seven have underperformed the median S&P 500 stock year-to-date, with price leadership rotating into semiconductors — a dynamic some analysts characterize as a rolling rather than a resolving bubble. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index rallied 105% from its March low to last month's peak before entering a technical bear market, falling 20%. Forward S&P 500 multiples remain elevated near 20x, with earnings revisions doing the heavy lifting to justify current levels. Alphabet's capex is projected to more than double this year to $187 billion, illustrating the stakes heading into this week's reports. ---

Asia

  • Hong Kong's Hang Seng Index rallied 2.4% Monday, its strongest session globally, while South Korea's Kospi sank 4.5% to its lowest since late April — the gap between the two is tracking for its widest monthly divergence since the Kospi's 1983 launch.
  • China's Moonshot AI told investors it intends to IPO within six months following the launch of Kimi K3, which has rattled semiconductor valuations globally by reinforcing perceptions that Chinese AI labs are closing the gap on US counterparts.
  • The RBI intervened to support the rupee as the currency approached a record low; New Zealand dollar short positioning among leveraged funds reached a record high in CFTC data.
  • KKR and AEW Capital Management are both seeking to exit Chinese real estate holdings at 40–50% losses, illustrating the continued depth of China's property sector distress.

Equity Rotation

The rotation out of chipmakers and into Chinese internet names accelerated sharply. Samsung and SK Hynix have each declined at least 25% this month, while Alibaba, Xiaomi, and Meituan have surged more than 25%. The shift reflects two reinforcing dynamics: deteriorating investor confidence in the sustainability of AI hyperscaler capex, and fresh evidence from Moonshot's Kimi K3 that Chinese AI development is more competitive than previously assumed. Michael Burry disclosed short positions in Nvidia, Applied Materials, and the SOXX ETF while adding to long exposure in Hong Kong-listed names, characterizing valuations there as attractive relative to global peers.

Policy and Diplomacy

Secretary of State Marco Rubio confirmed that Xi Jinping's planned September visit to the US remains on schedule despite Trump's unsubstantiated claims of Chinese electoral interference — a signal that the bilateral truce, however fragile, is being preserved at the diplomatic level. India's student protests over alleged exam paper leaks represent an emerging domestic political challenge for Prime Minister Modi. Malaysia reported a surprise surge in Q2 GDP growth, with robust services and electronics exports cushioning the impact of Middle East disruptions. ---

Europe

  • Andy Burnham takes office as the UK's seventh prime minister in just over a decade, inheriting tepid growth and a lingering inflation problem; Shabana Mahmood has emerged as frontrunner for Chancellor, seen as a relatively market-friendly appointment.
  • The ECB is widely expected to hold rates at Thursday's meeting, with inflation slowing faster than projected and energy prices near the bank's baseline; September remains live for a further move.
  • Ryanair reported a 34% decline in first-quarter profit, citing Iran-conflict-driven oil prices and weakened demand that forced fare cuts during peak summer travel season; shares fell as much as 6.7%.

UK Political Transition

Burnham has pledged to maintain current fiscal rules and pursue broad devolution, but economic specifics remain sparse — a source of anxiety for gilt investors who are closely monitoring his cabinet choices. Bloomberg Economics warns he has limited time before the next election to shift the growth trajectory, and emphasizes the need to support households within a fiscally credible framework. Reported plans include new North Sea oil and gas licensing, nationalization of Thames Water, and a combined autumn budget and spending review — a significant policy agenda, though its macro impact remains uncertain.

ECB and Corporate Developments

The ECB's decision to pause is consistent with its stated data-dependency: two additional inflation prints, a Q2 GDP reading, and business surveys will arrive before September's meeting, giving Lagarde optionality without committing prematurely. In the banking sector, Danish financial executives convened an emergency meeting to defuse an escalating dispute over the country's $500 billion mortgage market, a reflection of how European banking consolidation pressures are intensifying. Germany's chemical industry association VCI described the sector as remaining in structural crisis, while Chancellor Merz flagged the need to address trade imbalances with China. ---

Regional Macro

The week ahead is relatively data-light for Latin America, with Mexico CPI among the most closely watched releases. The USMCA review timeline, if confirmed, would represent a meaningful near-term anchor for Mexican trade and investment sentiment. The broader regional backdrop remains shaped by elevated global oil prices — a net positive for Colombia and Brazil as producers, but a headwind for oil-importing economies. The Strait of Hormuz disruption continues to affect global shipping costs and refining margins, with secondary effects on fuel prices and inflation across the region. ---

Global

  • The US conducted its ninth consecutive day of airstrikes on Iran, targeting military installations and communications networks; visible tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz has ground to a near standstill, with Iran continuing to strike US bases across the Gulf region.
  • Brent crude breached $90 intraday before retreating to approximately $88 following reports that mediators had proposed a 10-day cessation of strikes; WTI futures rose 2.5% to $84.53 before easing.

Strait of Hormuz

The conflict has entered a structurally more dangerous phase. The shuttle-tanker system that had been quietly sustaining some Persian Gulf oil flows was dealt a significant blow after UAE-linked vessels were struck last week. With the US summer driving season now in full swing, gasoline prices have topped $4/gallon and diesel exceeds $5, while refining crack spreads have hit records. Morgan Stanley warns European diesel inventories are on course for multiyear lows by year-end. The cushions that absorbed the initial February shock — global supply surplus, full strategic reserves, and seasonally weak demand — have all diminished.

Geopolitics and Trade

Russia received diplomatic support from North Korea, with Pyongyang's top diplomat visiting Moscow, while Ukraine launched what its foreign minister described as the largest ballistic missile barrage on Kyiv since the invasion began. China's rare-earth magnet exports to the US remain well below pre-trade-war levels despite the nominal truce, sustaining concerns in Washington about supply chain vulnerability. Pension funds globally are grappling with tail-risk climate scenarios, and the Ebola outbreak in the Democratic Republic of Congo is drawing new travel restrictions that aid organizations warn could hamper containment efforts.
