The economic shockwaves from the US-Iran conflict are landing with particular force across Asia, where dependence on Gulf energy supplies is structurally acute and fiscal buffers vary widely. South Korea has moved to implement crisis-level measures including a fuel-price cap, and its prime minister has explicitly invoked the need to prepare for "worst-case scenarios" — language that signals genuine institutional concern rather than routine contingency planning. Compounding Seoul's calculus is the reported redeployment of US air defense assets from the Korean Peninsula toward the Middle East, raising questions about the credibility of American security commitments in Northeast Asia at a moment of elevated regional tension.

The Philippines presents a starker case. President Marcos has declared a national energy emergency, citing the risk that domestic aviation could be grounded entirely due to jet fuel shortages — a materially different order of concern from the airfare increases flagged by US carriers. The contrast is instructive: richer economies face price shocks, while more vulnerable ones face supply disruption. Marcos's response is geopolitically significant as well. Despite deepening military and diplomatic ties with Washington since 2022 — positioning Manila as a key US partner in the South China Sea — the energy crisis is now prompting Manila to explore collaboration with Beijing on oil and gas development in disputed waters, alongside a broader diplomatic re-engagement with China.

This realignment, however tentative, reflects a wider pattern across the Indo-Pacific. The Iran war appears to be accelerating a reassessment among US allies of the costs and benefits of Washington alignment, particularly given Trump's simultaneous trade pressure, reduced regional security presence, and diplomatic abrasiveness. Hong Kong's re-emergence as a wealth management destination and China's positioning as a stable counterweight to US unpredictability — framed by some observers as Beijing casting itself as the "adult in the room" — suggest capital and political flows in the region may be gradually, if cautiously, reorienting.
