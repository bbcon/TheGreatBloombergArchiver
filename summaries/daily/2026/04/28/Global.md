Oil markets continued to tighten on Tuesday, with Brent crude rising as much as 2.9% to $111.32 per barrel — a seventh consecutive day of gains and the highest level in several weeks. The proximate driver remains the US blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, now approaching two months in duration. Analytics firm Kpler estimates Iran has 12 to 22 days of remaining storage capacity at current production rates before it must begin curtailing output, while US Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent indicated some well shutdowns may have already begun. Six to eight supertankers are clustered near Chabahar port in the Gulf of Oman, just outside the blockade perimeter. A single LNG tanker appeared to traverse the Strait of Hormuz on Tuesday, the first such transit since the conflict began, though it remains unclear whether this represents a durable development or an isolated incident.

Diplomatic activity continues without resolution. Iran submitted a peace proposal that the White House confirmed is under active consideration, though US officials described persistent red lines around Tehran's nuclear program. Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi completed a diplomatic tour that included a meeting with Vladimir Putin in St. Petersburg — a signal not lost on Washington. The contrast with the 2015 nuclear negotiations is sharp: those talks involved career diplomats, multilateral frameworks, and exhaustive document review; the current process is being managed by figures whose backgrounds are in real estate, and has been disrupted twice by military strikes.

The economic ramifications are broadening. Oxford Economics estimates global goods trade will expand just 2.5% in 2026, roughly half the 4.9% pace recorded in 2025, with the oil shock alone potentially subtracting 0.6% to 0.9% from world trade volumes. A Dallas Fed survey found 35% of Texas manufacturers are already feeling direct negative impact from the war. Partially offsetting the macro drag, the oil shock is accelerating EV adoption: European consumers purchased 206,200 electric vehicles in March, a 44% year-on-year increase, while South Korean EV sales more than doubled. Chinese EV and hybrid exports reached a record, rising 140% from the prior year — a dynamic that simultaneously reflects the energy crisis and China's growing competitive position in global auto markets. BlackRock Investment Institute warned that elevated inflation driven by the oil shock means long-term government bond yields are likely to remain structurally higher, and that traditional equity-bond diversification relationships may no longer hold.
