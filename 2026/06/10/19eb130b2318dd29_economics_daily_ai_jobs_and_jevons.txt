Jevons Resurrected

William Stanley Jevons, an economist who theorized in the 1860s that more 
efficient steam engines would increase — not decrease — coal consumption by UK 
factories, is having a makeover in theAI age 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/magnetar-plans-fund-that-replaces-human-analysts-with-ai-bots?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
.

Microsoft CEO Satya Nadella invoked 
<https://x.com/satyanadella/status/1883753899255046301?lang=en> the Jevons 
paradox, as the theory is known, early last year when China’s DeepSeek 
introduced a low-cost AI model, triggering arout 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-01-28/deepseek-ai-platform-brings-a-1-trillion-market-reckoning-on-cost?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
 in tech stocks on concerns that demand for high-end chips and cloud computing 
would sink.

More recently, Jevons was embraced by economists applying his paradox to the 
labor market, arguing that by making tasks faster and cheaper,artificial 
intelligence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-ai-data-center-redesign/?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
 will enable companies to do more, reach out to more clients, create more 
products and expand — creating more jobs in the process.

No one knows how AI will eventually reshape the labor market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/asia-s-largest-outsourcer-to-slow-hiring-as-ai-reshapes-industry?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
. But analysts have been looking into US economic data for any sign that AI is 
destroying jobs at scale. (Not lost on anyone, the very industry at the center 
of the technology boom isshedding jobs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-04/us-tech-sector-announces-most-job-cuts-in-nearly-two-years?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
, as highlighted this week in ourAI Today newsletter 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-08/the-ai-boom-has-a-jobs-problem-tech-workers-are-paying-the-price?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
).

Here are categories often cited as at risk, a mixed picture:

 * Indeed job postings for developers have been volatile in recent months but 
are generally trending higher since the middle of last year.
 * Youth unemployment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-04/us-young-workers-outlook-in-ai-economy-looks-better-than-expected?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
 is down this year.
 * Legal services payrolls are up
 * Financial activities payrolls are down more than 80,000 this year
 * Headcounts are declining in the information sector and in accounting and 
tax preparation bookkeeping.
Apollo chief economist Torsten Slok has referred to the Jevons paradox more 
than half a dozen times in his daily notes since the end of April to argue 
there’sno evidence 
<https://www.apollo.com/wealth/the-daily-spark/zero-evidence-of-ai-related-job-losses?utm_medium=email&utm_source=pardot&utm_id=3a0ecb14cf9c1c51fe9b1b2c83e2dec9&utm_campaign=EXT_Daily+Spark&utm_content=body-textlink-dailyspark-here>
 of overall job losses from AI.

Jeffrey Roach, LPL Financial’s chief economist, said in May that the impact 
will likely be less about the wholesale elimination of jobs and more about the 
reallocation of tasks across workers and companies.

“Demand may increase for workers who can use AI effectively, improve 
workflows, and apply human judgment,” he wrote in a note. “In this sense, AI 
could follow a Jevons-like pattern: greater efficiency may increase the overall 
demand for AI-enabled work.”

Need-to-Know Research

Garment factories in India supplying customers like Uniqlo, Marks & Spencer 
and Tesco aresuffering productivity losses 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/extreme-heat-risks-losses-for-indian-suppliers-to-uniqlo-tesco?cmpid=BBD061026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260610&utm_content=5340>
 of as much as 10% as a result of the nation’s extreme heat, according to a new 
report.

Research at 10 facilities in four regions found high temperatures are 
impacting product quality, delivery reliability and contributing to higher 
employee absenteeism during the peak summer months, thestudy 
<https://bhr.stern.nyu.edu/publication/too-hot-to-ignore-extreme-heat-in-garment-supply-chains/>
 published this week by the NYU Stern Center for Business and Human Rights 
found.

Many suppliers are “just about keeping their heads above water” by 
implementing makeshift fixes to handle heat extremes, saidLucy Siers 
<https://bhr.stern.nyu.edu/author/lucysiers/>, senior research scientist at the 
NYU Stern Center and lead author of the report. “The tipping point is when a 
delivery deadline is missed and suppliers are no longer able to insulate their 
buyers.”
