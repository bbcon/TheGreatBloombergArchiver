Testing investor appetites

How much demand is out there for AI stocks? Investors are about to find out.

Beyond SpaceX’s initial public offering, which could kick off as soon as 
Thursday, Anthropic filed yesterday for its ownblockbuster stock listing <> and 
OpenAI is gearing up to go public this year as well.

Strategist Ed Yardeni figures those three transactions will raise about $200 
billion combined. (And then there’s Alphabet and its$80 billion of offerings 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-01/alphabet-to-raise-80-billion-in-equity-capital-for-ai-spending?cmpid=060226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5114>
.)

“Fears are mounting that the ‘AI-3’ IPOs will suck the oxygen out of the rest 
of the stock market,” he wrote in a note to clients.

Yardeni says he’s not too worried, because of the size of the IPO market and 
enthusiasm among individual investors.

Listings globally raised about $200 billion in the year through April, 
according to data compiled by Bloomberg, while IPOs in 2021 raised $492 billion.

Investors who are worried about an oversupply of stock can take some comfort 
in this: Goldman Sachs’ Ben Snider this week estimated that corporate buybacks 
in the US will hit $1.3 trillion this year, easily outweighing the $675 billion 
in new supply he sees from IPOs, follow-on offerings and other issuance.

But even with the euphoria around AI stocks, investors are likely to be 
discerning about the price they’re willing to pay for unprofitable companies 
that are spending big.

“I think we are certainly going to have a negotiation period on how much 
people are actually willing to pay for those,” Shanti Kelemen of Seven 
Investment Management told Bloomberg TV this morning. “Right now what you are 
seeing is testing the market to see where the demand is, where the valuations 
will go to.” —Phil Serafino

‘Super squeeze’

Commodities are in a “super-squeeze” that will worsen 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/commodities-in-super-squeeze-as-hormuz-risks-build-hsbc-says?cmpid=060226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5114>
 if the Strait of Hormuz remains effectively shut, according to HSBC analysts.

“The longer the strait is closed, the more inventories are run down, the more 
likely it is that we reach ‘tipping points’ in the markets for some 
commodities,” analysts including Paul Bloxham said in a report. Still, knowing 
exactly when that might happen is hard to determine, they added.

Looking ahead, other bullish factors for commodities include rising 
consumption for base metals such as copper, and a looming El Niño weather event 
that may hurt crop supplies, HSBC said. —Yongchang Chin 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUJztO3yV14/yongchang-chin?cmpid=060226_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5114>
