**The Hormuz Stalemate and Its Macro Consequences**

The US-Iran standoff entered its third month without diplomatic resolution, though the week produced more movement than most. President Trump called off planned strikes Monday after Saudi Arabia and Gulf allies requested additional time for diplomacy, sending Brent 1.8% lower to around $110. By Wednesday, Iran's Foreign Ministry confirmed it was reviewing a US response to Tehran's 14-point proposal — which envisions a short-term arrangement reopening the strait in exchange for lifting the port blockade, followed by deeper nuclear negotiations — and Trump told reporters Washington was in the "final stages." Brent fell 5.6% on Wednesday on those signals. The relief proved partial: Supreme Leader comments insisting Tehran retain its uranium stockpile and a dispute over a proposed Iran-Oman toll regime in the strait complicated the picture, and by Friday Brent had recovered to above $105 with Trump reiterating the threat of renewed strikes. Goldman Sachs estimates visible global crude and product inventories drew down at a record 8.7 million barrels per day in May, nearly double the conflict's running average — a rate that, if sustained, points to demand destruction rather than supply restoration as the rebalancing mechanism. Rapidan Energy's assessment that a disruption persisting through August could push Brent toward $130 with macroeconomic consequences comparable to 2008 represents a tail scenario the diplomatic track has not yet foreclosed. Physical workarounds are advancing: the UAE is accelerating pipeline construction to Fujairah to double bypass capacity, Singapore's state LNG buyer has secured non-Middle East cargoes through year-end, and NATO is reportedly discussing vessel escort options if the strait remains closed into July.

**The Bond Market as Policy Signal**

The global sovereign bond selloff deepened through the week before partially stabilizing on Iran diplomatic signals. Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields reached its highest level since July 2008, with US 30-year yields touching 5.19% on Tuesday, Japanese super-long yields hitting post-1999 records following PM Takaichi's fiscal reversal, and UK gilts pricing political risk around a potential Burnham premiership. The drivers extend beyond oil: sticky underlying inflation, AI-related capital expenditure, deteriorating fiscal trajectories, and demographic pressures are reinforcing one another in a way that distinguishes this episode from a simple energy shock. The FOMC minutes released Wednesday showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" preferred dropping the easing bias. Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike on Wednesday to defend the rupiah. The ECB signaled a likely June hike. The RBA has already delivered three hikes this year. TS Lombard's Dario Perkins characterized the environment as potentially heralding "rate hikes everywhere," with 2% targets functioning increasingly as a floor rather than a ceiling. The tension between that tightening bias and Pimco's observation that prior energy shocks have historically culminated in recession remains the defining macro ambiguity heading into summer.

**Gulf Capital Flows and Petrodollar Recycling**

A slower-moving but potentially consequential structural shift emerged more clearly through the week's analysis: the contraction of the petrodollar recycling mechanism. Saudi Arabia's first-quarter budget reflected roughly $17 billion in war-related costs, equivalent to 1.5% of GDP. Qatar faces potential losses of $20 billion from damaged gas infrastructure that could take five years to restore. The PIF had already cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from a prior ambition near 50% before the conflict began; the war is accelerating the inward turn, with LIV Golf preparing for possible bankruptcy after PIF withdrew funding and a $200 million Metropolitan Opera commitment abandoned. Saudi Arabia is now pursuing a $35 billion Aramco privatization to court domestic capital rather than export it. The multi-trillion-dollar investment pledges made to the Trump administration in 2025 look progressively less deliverable. For US markets, the implication is incremental at present: a structurally smaller Gulf bid for Treasuries and US equities at the margin, at a moment when Treasury supply is already elevated. Sovereigns historically supported by Gulf bilateral financing — Egypt, Pakistan, Jordan — face a tighter external environment, and Asian recipients of GCC foreign direct investment, including Indian infrastructure platforms and Southeast Asian data-center projects, may face delayed commitments depending on the conflict's duration.

**Geopolitics and the Russia-China Axis**

Putin's Beijing visit clarified the asymmetry of the relationship. Russia arrived weakened by Sunday's record Ukrainian drone strike on Moscow, with its economy heavily dependent on Chinese demand for hydrocarbons and on Beijing for over 90% of sanctioned technology imports. Xi, fresh from his Trump summit and able to offer or withhold leverage on Power of Siberia 2 pricing, retains the structural upper hand. Both sides will emphasize their "no-limits" partnership publicly; the substantive question of gas pricing terms — the core of Russia's leverage pitch, premised on Middle East supply disruption — remains unconfirmed. The September expiry of the US-China trade truce, China's dominance over rare earth separation underpinning roughly $1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity, and Trump's July 24 tariff deadline represent the principal near-term flashpoints for the bilateral relationship. Whether the Beijing détente forged at last week's Trump-Xi summit proves durable against those pressures remains to be seen.
