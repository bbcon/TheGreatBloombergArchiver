**US Daily Briefing — May 23, 2026**

Coverage relevant to US markets and policy is thin on this Saturday, with Bloomberg's weekend distribution focused primarily on global thematic analysis. Two storylines nonetheless carry implications for US investors.

**Gulf capital recycling under strain.** Bloomberg Economics highlights how the ongoing Iran conflict, now in its third month, is materially altering the petrodollar recycling mechanism that has historically supported US asset prices and Treasury demand. With the Strait of Hormuz largely closed, Gulf sovereigns are confronting simultaneously lower hydrocarbon revenues and higher domestic outlays — Saudi Arabia's Q1 budget reflects roughly $17 billion in war-related costs, equivalent to 1.5% of GDP. The Public Investment Fund had already cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from a prior ambition near 50%, and recent withdrawals from LIV Golf and a $200 million Metropolitan Opera deal suggest a continued pivot inward. For US markets, the implication is incremental rather than acute: a structurally smaller Gulf bid for Treasuries and US equities at the margin, and diminished credibility around the multi-trillion-dollar investment pledges made to the Trump administration in 2025. The magnitude and persistence of the effect remain to be seen, particularly given uncertainty around the conflict's duration.

**Subnational climate policy diverges from federal.** Berkeley, California has become the first US municipality to require fossil-fuel appliance replacement or equivalent green upgrades as a condition of single-family home sale, under its Building Emissions Saving Ordinance effective January. The move is notable less for its direct economic footprint than as a marker of the widening gap between municipal climate action and federal rollback under the Trump administration, which has canceled grant programs including a Groundswell-administered residential energy resiliency initiative in Alabama and Georgia. For homebuilders, appliance manufacturers and residential energy retrofitters, the patchwork of state and local mandates increasingly substitutes for the federal incentive architecture being dismantled.
