Daily Brief

Wednesday, June 24, 2026

US

  • No major US macro data releases on the day; markets were driven by tech positioning and the anticipation of Micron Technology's fiscal Q3 earnings report after the close.
  • The S&P 500 momentum ETF suffered its second-worst session since Liberation Day on Tuesday; futures stabilized Wednesday, with S&P 500 futures +0.1% and Nasdaq 100 futures +0.5% as of early morning.
  • Treasury Secretary Bessent signaled confidence in Fed Chair Kevin Warsh and predicted inflation will continue to retreat; the 10-year yield edged lower to 4.48%.
  • FedEx shares fell 6.7% premarket after reporting earnings that beat consensus on profit but flagged trade-policy headwinds and MD-11 fleet groundings; Cerebras Systems dropped 14% after its annual sales forecast missed elevated investor expectations.

Tech Earnings and Market Positioning

Tuesday's tech-led selloff originated in Seoul but arrived on Wall Street with force. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index recorded its largest single-day decline since June 5, with Micron — now accounting for nearly one-fifth of the S&P 500's year-to-date gain after a roughly 270% advance — falling 13%. Goldman Sachs partner Bobby Molavi flagged the danger explicitly, noting that whether active, passive, hedge funds, or quant strategies, "everyone is effectively getting longer AI by the day," creating co-correlation risk that "feels great on the way up, but can be extremely dangerous on the unwind." The SOX index, despite Tuesday's move, remains more overbought relative to its 200-day moving average than at any point since the dot-com peak. Micron's after-close report carries unusual gravity: consensus EPS for the quarter has risen 645% over twelve months, and options markets are pricing a 10% swing in either direction.

Monetary Policy and Macro Backdrop

Bessent's remarks offered a constructive macro framing: inflation appears to be cooling and the Fed's policy trajectory, under Warsh, retains official confidence. US diesel prices fell below $5 per gallon for the first time since mid-March as oil extended declines, providing a tangible disinflationary impulse that, if sustained, supports the broader narrative of easing price pressures without requiring additional Fed action. ---

Asia

  • SK Hynix fell more than 13% in Seoul on Tuesday after flagging a shift toward general-purpose DRAM, triggering an estimated $6 billion in forced selling by leveraged single-stock ETFs; the Kospi recovered 3.3% Wednesday.
  • SK Hynix separately announced plans to raise approximately $29.4 billion (45.45 trillion won) via Nasdaq depositary receipts, targeting a July 10 listing start — which would rank among the five largest share sales on record.
  • Bank of Japan Governor Kazuo Ueda reiterated that inflation risks remain tilted to the upside and that further tightening is warranted if economic and price conditions hold, reinforcing last week's rate increase to the highest level since 1995.
  • China has effectively halted most tungsten shipments to Japan, with rare-earth magnet flows falling to their lowest since May 2025, in apparent retaliation for Prime Minister Takaichi's Taiwan comments.

Korea: Leverage Amplifies Volatility

The immediate catalyst for Tuesday's global tech selloff was SK Hynix's disclosure that it was shifting focus toward general-purpose DRAM — a lower-margin segment — which traders interpreted as a signal that the premium AI-memory cycle may be maturing. The scale of the reaction was amplified by leveraged single-stock ETFs: Korean regulators, who recently permitted such instruments domestically, publicly expressed regret at having done so. A double-leveraged Hynix ETF traded in Hong Kong would have translated the 13.2% decline into roughly a 26.4% loss for holders. The episode raises structural questions about the role of leveraged products in concentrating and accelerating market dislocations.

Japan and China: Policy and Supply-Chain Tensions

The BOJ's continued hawkish signaling stands in contrast to most major central banks and reflects genuine domestic inflation dynamics rather than policy theater. Meanwhile, China's mineral export restrictions on Japan represent a calibrated escalation short of a full embargo but already sufficient to force Japanese manufacturers into stockpile drawdowns and alternative sourcing. NTT's planned $10 billion multi-currency bond sale — the largest global bond offering by an Asian company this year — illustrates that credit conditions remain accommodative enough for large-scale capital deployment despite the geopolitical backdrop. ---

Europe

  • France recorded its hottest day and hottest night on record as a severe European heatwave deepened, prompting nuclear power curtailments that pushed evening electricity prices to their highest since the 2022 energy crisis.
  • The UK's National Grid issued a rare summer power supply warning as temperatures approached 35°C, adding to energy system stress.
  • Germany's Ifo business climate index brightened slightly in June, easing concerns about Iran-war spillovers into Europe's largest economy, though the reading's magnitude was not specified in available coverage.
  • Rheinmetall shares fell 17% — the sharpest decline in a year — after Germany signaled it would withdraw a major warships contract.

Climate Stress and Energy Infrastructure

The current heatwave, driven by El Niño, is exposing meaningful vulnerabilities in European energy infrastructure. France's nuclear curtailments are particularly significant given the system's reliance on nuclear baseload; the combination of heat-reduced output and surging cooling demand is compressing supply margins continent-wide. The UK's grid warning, issued in summer — historically a period of lower demand risk — underscores how climate-related shocks are broadening the seasonal risk window for energy system operators. The Network for Greening the Financial System published guidance this week warning central bankers that more frequent physical shocks will generate persistent monetary policy trade-offs, as heatwaves and floods can "push output and inflation in opposite directions simultaneously."

Fiscal Framework and Politics

The European Commission's decision to grant modest additional budget flexibility to indebted sovereigns, including Italy, has not provoked market disruption, which the EU frames as a validation of its post-pandemic fiscal surveillance framework. On UK politics, Andy Burnham's path to the Labour leadership appears to be clearing after Darren Jones withdrew from consideration; Burnham's northern-English political base is seen as potentially helpful in stemming voter losses to Reform UK. ---

LatAm

  • Colombia's presidential election was won by conservative Abelardo de la Espriella, backed by Donald Trump, marking a sharp ideological pivot for a country that had positioned itself as a leader in the global energy transition.
  • Keiko Fujimori is poised to win Peru's presidential election, her fourth campaign, with her pro-business platform viewed favorably by investors despite rival claims of irregularities.
  • Petrobras and Pemex announced a non-binding cooperation agreement covering exploration, production, and refining, with Petrobras's deepwater salt-formation expertise potentially applicable to underexplored Gulf of Mexico geology.
  • Brazil's President Lula is entering a difficult reelection environment; the Pemex partnership offers diplomatic optics but limited near-term operational significance.

Colombia and the Regional Political Shift

De la Espriella's victory is the most consequential near-term development. His explicit embrace of expanded fossil fuel production — including fracking — reverses the direction set by outgoing President Petro and aligns Colombia more closely with the broader Latin American populist-right wave that has reshaped the region's political economy. For investors, the policy shift raises Colombia's attractiveness as an oil and gas destination but complicates its ESG positioning. The election also has implications for regional climate diplomacy, where Colombia had played an outsized role relative to its emissions footprint.

Petrobras-Pemex: Strategic Logic, Execution Uncertainty

The cooperation agreement reflects genuine strategic logic — Petrobras's pre-salt deepwater capabilities are among the most transferable in the industry, and the Gulf of Mexico's salt geology may harbor undiscovered reserves. However, the non-binding nature of the agreement and Pemex's deep structural liabilities (crushing debt, declining output) mean execution risk is substantial. Latin America's history of grand energy integration initiatives that failed to materialize — including Venezuela's continental gas pipeline proposal — warrants measured expectations. ---

Global

  • Brent crude fell below $75 per barrel for the first time since the Iran war began, as tankers resumed openly transiting the Strait of Hormuz and US-Iran talks signaled incremental progress toward a broader settlement.
  • Significant unresolved disputes remain between Washington and Tehran: Iran denies agreeing to sweeping nuclear inspections, no limits on uranium enrichment have been agreed, and the two sides contradict each other on the disposition of $12 billion in unfrozen assets.
  • SpaceX sold $25 billion in investment-grade bonds — its debut in the US high-grade market — drawing $89 billion in orders, effectively using Starlink and launch revenue to refinance X and xAI debt at lower cost.
  • The global AI infrastructure buildout is accelerating capital deployment across energy, data centers, and semiconductors simultaneously: SoftBank is pursuing a stake in Tokyo Electric Power for data-center power supply, while a Stack Infrastructure Asia-Pacific portfolio drew interest from BlackRock-backed AIP, Brookfield, and KKR at a potential $30 billion valuation.

Oil Markets and the Iran Interim Deal

The decline in Brent to sub-$75 represents a significant reversal from the late-April peak near $125, and the signal from shipping markets — more tankers transiting with AIS transponders active — suggests confidence is returning incrementally. However, the thorniest structural issues remain unresolved. Iran and the US contradict each other on nuclear inspection scope, uranium enrichment limits, ballistic missile programs, and the administration of Hormuz itself. Lebanon and Gaza remain active flashpoints that Iran has flagged as potential deal-breakers. The market appears to be pricing a degree of normalization that the diplomatic reality does not yet fully support, creating asymmetric downside risk if talks deteriorate.

AI Capital Cycle: Infrastructure Phase

The AI investment cycle is visibly shifting from software and model development toward physical infrastructure — power, data centers, and memory. ByteDance is weighing a $20 billion offshore loan, with up to $70 billion in AI-related capital expenditure planned for the year. The SpaceX bond deal illustrates how AI's financial architecture is becoming increasingly complex, with profitable infrastructure businesses cross-subsidizing cash-consuming AI ventures. The simultaneous crowding of financial capital into semiconductor and data-center plays, against a backdrop of leveraged positioning and stretched valuations, may signal that the risk-reward calculus across the AI trade is deteriorating even as the fundamental demand story remains intact.
