Bonds are a losing bet this year

Two and a half months after the US and Israel kicked off a war against Iran, 
the persistent disruption to the oil market isfueling renewed losses in 
government bonds 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/treasuries-lead-global-bond-yields-higher-on-inflation-angst?cmpid=051526_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4522>
.

Yields on US Treasuries have risen every day this week, pushing the 10-year to 
4.54%, the highest in about a year. Sovereign bonds, as measured by Bloomberg 
indexes, have handed investors losses for the year in the US, the UK, the euro 
area and Japan.

Everywhere, it’s a story of price pressures fed by the war, as shown in this 
week’s stronger-than-expected US inflation readings.(Of course, there are 
country-specific reasons adding to the pressures in the UK and Japan, the 
biggest underperformers.)

Traders increasingly are betting on Fed interest rate hikes to keep prices in 
check.

While oil prices have come way off their highs, they’ve stopped going down. 
The war seems to have devolved into a stalemate that is keeping the flow of 
crude through the Strait of Hormuz choked off. Donald Trump probably didn’t 
help matters when he told Fox News overnight that the US doesn’t need the 
strait open “at all.”

So even if the predictions for $150-a-barrel oil aren’t coming true, there’s 
no sign we’re going back to $70 any time soon. The bond market’s ructions also 
are weighing on stocks, disrupting the euphoria around AI that’s raged from 
Seoul to Silicon Valley.

Bottom line, nothing has yet been resolved in the Middle East, oil is getting 
scarcer and inflationary pressures are rapidly escalating. —Phil Serafino 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ABoP3GGeTtg/phil-serafino>, with Paul Dobson

Stocks up, valuations down

It’s a sign of how powerful the demand for memory chips is: Even as shares of 
Sandisk and Micron Technology soar,the stocks are becoming cheaper 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/these-memory-stocks-are-getting-cheaper-despite-scorching-rally?cmpid=051526_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4522>
.

That’s because expectations for their earnings are rising even faster than 
their stock prices as demand for memory continues to grow and a shortage of 
available chips keeps supply low.Analyst estimates for Sandisk’s 2027 earnings 
are up almost 2,000% in the past year, while Micron’s have climbed 768%.

Sandisk shares are up 482% this year, the biggest gain in the S&P 500, while 
Micron has leaped 172%. Both sell for about nine times estimated earnings, 
compared with the S&P 500 at a PE of about 21.

“All of the high bandwidth memory can be priced ridiculously high because of 
supply and demand,” saidKim Forrest <bbg://people/profile/3758073> of Bokeh 
Capital Partners, who owns Micron shares. “I’m a capitalist. Whenever supply 
and demand works like this, I’m in seventh heaven.” —Carmen Reinicke 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AT8Yc28TTKE/carmen-reinicke>
