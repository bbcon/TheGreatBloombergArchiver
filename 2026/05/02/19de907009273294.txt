From sweaters to T-shirts and back again

By Eric Roston <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQdgGkZhizQ/eric-roston> and 
Emma Court <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUVJA-GbuIc/emma-court>

Dramatic temperature jumps marked this March and April in the US Northeast, 
making it hard to know what to dress for: chilly or sweltering conditions? 
Researchers have concluded that wild temperature swings are becoming more 
common as the world heats up.

ThatтАЩs changing how we experience spring, with plants blooming sooner and 
consumers rushing to buy more warm-weather goods earlier in the year.
Snow piles linger on a warm spring day at the Boston Common, on March 9, 2026.
Photographer: Boston Globe
Andra Garner, a meteorologist and climate scientist at Rowan University in New 
Jersey, was struck by the ups and downs of spring weather a few years ago. 
Finding herself switching back and forth between heating and air conditioning, 
she went on to research the matter.

From 1950 to 2019, New Jersey saw increasing variation in daily high and low 
temperatures in late winter and spring, Garner found in a2024 study 
<https://journals.ametsoc.org/view/journals/apme/63/2/JAMC-D-23-0152.1.xml>. 
She and her co-author, environmental scientist Dan Duran, also of Rowan, 
defined swings as changes from above 60F (16C) to below 32F (0C).

Consider this April 23, when the temperature in New York City hit 73F shortly 
before 4 p.m. тАФ a full 28 degrees higher than it had been 12 hours earlier. On 
April 13, the high was 79 тАФ 30 degrees above the overnight low тАФ while on April 
4 the temperature fell to 42 from 73. Those jumps are bigger than the monthтАЩs 
average daily fluctuation of 17 degrees at Central ParkтАЩs weather station. 
Fluctuations over multiple days are also growing larger.

Springtime weather is naturally wild. But the size of recent temperature 
shifts points to the influence of climate change, experts say.

тАЬItтАЩs not unusual to have such variability in the spring, but the extremes are 
a little more extreme,тАЭ said Marshall Shepherd, director of the University of 
GeorgiaтАЩs atmospheric sciences program. тАЬThatтАЩs always been a hallmark of what 
we expected in climate change.тАЭ

Greenhouse gas pollution, as well as kick-starting spring sooner 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-04-23/cherry-blossoms-are-proof-of-a-planet-going-awry?cmpid=BBD050226_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260502&utm_content=4082>
 in the year, may be adding spikiness to daily highs and lows. Temperatures are 
rising earlier across the EarthтАЩs temperate band; a hotter Gulf of Mexico is 
pushing warm air north; awobblier jet stream 
<https://agupubs.onlinelibrary.wiley.com/doi/full/10.1029/2022JD036717> lets 
more cold Arctic air travel south. In other words, the ingredients are ripe for 
surprises.

More than 60% of the globe has experienced more common, intense and rapid 
<https://www.nature.com/articles/s41467-025-58544-5> temperature flips since 
1961, a trend that is expected to worsen, according to a 2025 study in the 
journalNature Communications. Extreme day-to-day temperature changes тАЬare an 
independent, butlargely ignored 
<https://www.nature.com/articles/s41558-025-02486-9>, aspect of extreme weather 
eventsтАЭ that may grow in frequency and intensity this century by 20%, a 
different team of researchers wrote last year.

тАЬRecent studies suggest these whiplash events are increasingly frequent and 
will become more common in the future,тАЭ said Jennifer Francis, senior scientist 
at the Woodwell Climate Research Center, whose research documents how Arctic 
warming may be affecting Northern Hemisphere weather.
