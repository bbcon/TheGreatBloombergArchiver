**Global Macro Briefing — January 6, 2026**

**Wall Street's 2026 Consensus: Modest Optimism with Embedded Tensions**

The sell-side's compiled outlooks for 2026 — drawing on more than 60 institutions including Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan Wealth Management, and the BlackRock Investment Institute — coalesce around a cautiously constructive base case, though one with notable internal tensions worth flagging for cross-asset positioning.

The dominant thread is that AI-related capital expenditure and accommodative government policy continue to supply growth impulse at what is, by conventional metrics, a late stage of the business cycle. This unusual configuration — fiscal and secular investment tailwinds extending an aging expansion — is the analytical centerpiece of most house views. The corollary, widely acknowledged, is that disinflation may stall short of central bank targets. Sticky services inflation and resilient demand appear to leave less room for monetary easing than rates markets currently price, setting up a potential repricing risk should the data validate the consensus view rather than the curve.

On currencies, the modal call remains for continued dollar depreciation, consistent with expectations of narrowing rate differentials, persistent twin deficits, and ongoing reserve diversification. This view, however, sits in some tension with the sticky-inflation thesis: if the Fed has less room to cut than markets expect, the dollar's path lower becomes less straightforward. Investors should treat the dollar-bearish consensus with appropriate skepticism given this internal inconsistency in the broader narrative.

Private assets are expected to continue gaining share of institutional allocations, extending a multi-year trend, though valuation dispersion and exit-channel constraints remain unresolved questions.

The framing of 2026 as "another rocky year" for global trade — against a backdrop of unresolved tariff regimes and supply-chain reconfiguration — suggests that the macro tape will likely be driven as much by policy shocks as by cyclical data. Coverage today is otherwise light on geopolitical and energy-specific developments; we will revisit oil, Iran, and Hormuz dynamics as the news flow resumes.
