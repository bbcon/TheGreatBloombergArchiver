    Macro Backdrop: Inflation Anxiety Eclipses Easing Narrative

    The dominant US theme this week was the bond market's accelerating repricing of the inflation outlook, as the closure of the Strait of Hormuz approached its three-month mark and the Iran war showed no clear path to resolution. Thirty-year Treasury yields rose to 5.19% on Tuesday, the highest level since June 2007, while 10-year yields touched 4.63% on Monday before easing modestly later in the week. The selloff reflected a convergence of factors: persistent energy-driven inflation concerns, expanding fiscal deficits, and a substantive reassessment of the Federal Reserve's policy path. Fed funds futures, which in late February implied a rate of approximately 2.8% by mid-2027, now price in a rate above 4%, with traders assigning roughly a 70% probability to a quarter-point hike by December.

    Federal Reserve Transition Amid Hawkish Repricing

    Minutes from the April FOMC meeting, released Wednesday, revealed a more hawkish committee than markets had previously assumed. Almost all participants noted that energy prices could remain elevated longer than expected even after the conflict ends, and a majority indicated some policy firming would likely become appropriate if inflation continued to run persistently above 2%. Many preferred removing the easing-bias language from the post-meeting statement altogether. Kevin Warsh is scheduled to be sworn in as Fed Chair on Friday, inheriting a constrained environment in which engineering the rate cuts President Trump has sought would risk exacerbating the bond selloff. Headline CPI rose 3.8% year-over-year in April, the highest reading since 2023.

    Equity Markets: AI Narrative Sustains a Narrowing Rally

    US equities showed remarkable resilience despite the bond market pressures, with the S&P 500 on track for its eighth consecutive weekly gain — the longest such streak since 2023. The S&P 500 declined more than 1% on Friday of the prior week before stabilizing, and futures touched 7,489 on Friday. Bank of America's fund manager survey showed equity allocations rising at the fastest pace on record, a positioning extreme that strategist Michael Hartnett characterized as approaching "bull capitulation." Nvidia reported Wednesday after the close, posting 85% revenue growth and 62% gross margins, alongside an additional $80 billion buyback authorization. The stock dipped modestly in extended trading as the sales forecast drew a lukewarm response, though the results catalyzed a substantial rally in Asian AI-linked names Thursday. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley lead the underwriting syndicate. OpenAI is reportedly preparing its own IPO filing within weeks, with Anthropic potentially following in October.

NATO began discussions on potential intervention to reopen the waterway if it remained closed into early July.     Equity Markets and the AI Rally

    US equity markets entered the week at all-time highs, with the S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 propelled by a semiconductor earnings cycle that analysts characterized as qualitatively different from prior beat-and-raise episodes. The rally began to stall on Friday, with S&P 500 futures off 1.0% and Nasdaq 100 futures down 1.5%, as the inflation and rate-hike narrative displaced AI optimism at the margin.

    Inflation and the Federal Reserve

    Tuesday's CPI print delivered a reading of 3.8% year-on-year, the highest in three years, driven primarily by Hormuz-related energy prices but with meaningful breadth underneath: core inflation ran at 2.8%, sticky prices re-crossed 3%, and the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply. Consumer prices rose faster than average hourly earnings in April, the first such occurrence since 2022. Wednesday's PPI data compounded the concern, rising 6% year-on-year and including a 27% surge in electronics components — the latter ending decades of technology deflation. Oxford Economics now projects the PCE deflator reaching 3.8%, effectively foreclosing near-term rate cuts. By Friday, the two-year Treasury yield reached 4.06% and the ten-year 4.54%, its highest level in approximately a year. Fed futures markets priced roughly a two-thirds probability of a rate hike by December, a repricing that gathered momentum throughout the week as Fed Governor Barr characterized inflation as "the overwhelming risk facing the economy."

    Federal Reserve Leadership

    Kevin Warsh cleared an initial Senate vote Monday and was confirmed as Federal Reserve chair Wednesday by the narrowest margin on record. The timing proved immediately consequential: rather than inheriting the easing cycle markets had anticipated around his appointment, Warsh takes the chair at a moment when the risk profile has shifted toward a harder stagflationary configuration. Bank of America recommended short positions in two-year Treasuries on the basis that rate hikes are underpriced.

Trump's financial disclosures revealed personal equity purchases in Q1, including positions in Nvidia, Oracle, Microsoft, Boeing, and Costco.     China: Economy, Policy, and Markets

April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above consensus, with technology the primary driver. The yuan strengthened to a more than three-year high; Goldman Sachs estimates it remains over 20% undervalued and projects a move to 6.50 within twelve months. Beneath the headline data, independent analysts estimate true non-performing loan ratios closer to 10% versus the official 1.5%, implying approximately $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt. China's ten-year bond yield fell below Japan's — a striking inversion that reflects subdued domestic demand even as export and inflation data improve. China also confirmed it has not approved purchases of Nvidia H200 chips and is actively developing domestic alternatives.

The underlying tension is genuine: Samsung posted a 755% profit increase in Q1 2026, its market capitalization recently crossed $1 trillion, and Goldman economists flagged that AI-driven chip booms in South Korea and Taiwan are on track to push current-account surpluses to record levels. Samsung's labor union is threatening an 18-day strike from May 21, demanding 15% of operating profit for chip-division employees.

Xi's latest Five-Year Plan replaced "peace and development" framing with explicit acknowledgment of geopolitical rivalry as the primary driver of global uncertainty, elevating national security as the central organizing principle for economic policy. For industrial communities in Germany, Japan, and Italy, the Rhodium analysis suggests the adjustment pressure is structural rather than cyclical. ---

Brent crude rose above $107 on Tuesday after Trump rejected Iran's latest peace proposal as "a piece of garbage" — characterising the ceasefire as on "massive life support" — before WTI held near $101 by mid-week and Brent approached $109 by Friday, representing a cumulative rise of more than 60% over three months. A Japanese supertanker and two India-bound LNG cargoes appear to have made covert transits, illustrating that flows are occurring but under conditions of elevated rather than normalised risk. Trump's comment to Fox News that the US does not need the strait "open at all" complicated diplomatic signalling. The Trump-Xi summit produced shared rhetorical commitment to reopening the strait but no operational mechanism. The UAE announced plans to double Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027, a structural adaptation to prolonged disruption.

    Oil Market Dynamics and Price Risk

    The buffers that contained prices through the conflict's initial weeks are visibly eroding. US crude and product exports are running above 14 million barrels per day with emergency stockpile drawdowns at record rates — a pace Morgan Stanley and Mercuria both characterise as unsustainable beyond late June or July. Chinese state refiners are drawing on commercial inventories rather than importing, suppressing seaborne demand at a rate that likewise cannot persist. The IEA reported global inventories falling at a record pace and projected an outright contraction of 420,000 barrels per day in 2026 oil demand — a rare and historically stress-associated outcome — against a pre-war forecast of 1.2 million barrels per day growth. TD Securities warned of a potential surge toward $150 per barrel absent a Hormuz resolution; BloombergNEF placed the upside at $160 if closure persists through July. US jet fuel exports surged to a record 455,000 barrels per day as European and Asian airlines scrambled for supply. Iran is also boosting rail freight to China via Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan, with container rates along the route climbing 40% above typical levels.

    Global Inflation and Bond Markets

    Producer price data from the US, China, and Japan all surprised to the upside this week, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. US PPI accelerated to 6% year-on-year, led by freight costs and energy; electronics components inflated 27% as AI-driven memory chip shortages ended decades of technology deflation. Sovereign bond markets are pricing a coherent inflation narrative across jurisdictions: US ten-year yields reached 4.54% on Friday, UK thirty-year gilts hit 5.80%, and Japan's twenty-year rate touched its highest level since 1996. The correlation of losses across US, UK, eurozone, and Japanese government bond indices this year reflects a common energy-price driver layered over country-specific fiscal and political factors. Investors captured 5% yields on thirty-year Treasuries for the first time in nearly two decades. Fed rate hike expectations for December moved from tail risk toward base-case probability for a meaningful share of market participants — a repricing with implications for equity valuations, credit spreads, and emerging market capital flows that have not yet fully resolved.

    China's Clean Technology Opportunity and Supply Chain Coercion

    China's green technology firms are explicitly leveraging the energy shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third. Chinese manufacturers are targeting Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia as primary growth markets. Simultaneously, Beijing's new regulatory framework for supply chain coercion — characterised by Rhodium Group as a shift from reactive to proactive strategy — creates a direct tension with Western diversification efforts. The prospect that any US-China investment accommodation reached at the summit could prioritise American companies at European expense drew concern in Brussels.

    Infrastructure, Water, and Resource Stress

    The conflict is accelerating awareness of concentrated infrastructure vulnerability beyond energy. Centralized desalination plants supplying tens of millions across the Middle East face exposure analogous to centralized energy nodes, driving commercial interest in decentralized atmospheric water harvesting technology. In the American West, record-low Rocky Mountain snowpack is prompting renewed investment in water storage, with the Chimney Hollow Dam — the largest built in the US in twenty-five years — beginning to fill in April, even as 500,000 Corpus Christi residents face potential shortfalls within a year. Data centers are simultaneously placing significant new demand on already-stressed aquifers, creating a tension between AI infrastructure requirements and physical resource constraints that has no near-term resolution.
