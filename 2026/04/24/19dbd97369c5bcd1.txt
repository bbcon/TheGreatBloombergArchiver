Hi, this is Josh Xiao <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVxUV9dEiUc/josh-xiao>
, fresh off a visit to Kuala Lumpur, where I attended one of Asia’s biggest 
defense and security exhibitions.

The main thing that jumped out at me was that Chinese companies were ramping 
up their presence like never before. This year, 192 such firms sent 
representatives to the event, organizer Defence Services Asia told me. That’s 
the biggest showing ever from China, which is the world’s fifth-largest arms 
exporter in terms of percentage of global shipments.

The firms had a bunch of interesting hardware on display. An amphibious 
transport dock equipped with surface-to-air missiles and loaded with guns. The 
Sky Dragon 100, a medium-to-long-range air-defense missile. Oh, and a robot dog 
marketed by Poly Technologies that paraded around the cavernous hall with four 
man-portable air-defense systems on its back.

Another prominent feature of the defense exhibition was the prevalence of 
China Inc.’s drone and anti-drone tech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2025-09-03/china-parades-top-military-hardware-in-beijing-video?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
.
A Chinese military drone and missiles displayed at an exhibition in Zhuhai in 
2024.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
Aircohn, a Shenzhen company that makes systems to find and disrupt unwanted 
drones, said Mexico placed a big order for their portable UAV detection and 
jamming equipment this year after testing it in 2025.

Chelsea Guo, a sales manager at Aircohn, said this was the company’s first 
appearance at the show and suggested why it was there: “Southeast Asia has a 
lot of potential customers that we would like to explore.”

A few stalls away, the anti-drone firm Bitalltech (Beijing) was also at the 
exhibition for the first time.

“Malaysia is a good entry point into Southeast Asia,” said Donna Du, a sales 
director. “You also have African and Middle Eastern customers.”

The sales pitch for these companies has gotten a bit easier given how drone 
tech has played such a big role in the wars in Ukraine and Iran. Tehran has 
used its relatively cheap Shahed drones tohit targets all over the region 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-10/iran-s-attack-drones-and-missiles-put-us-military-under-unexpected-strain?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
, including oil infrastructure and US bases.

That reach has forced Washington to not only deploy much more expensive 
defenses but also put pressure on the US and Israel to wrap up their campaign. 
The US has even apparently had toask Ukraine for help protecting against the 
Shahed drone 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/ukraine-offers-help-downing-iran-drones-in-exchange-for-patriots?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
, which Russia makes versions of. The Pentagon is also planning tomass produce 
a type similar to the Shahed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/pentagon-plans-to-mass-produce-attack-drone-used-in-iran-war?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/sessions/2026-04-23/live-q-amp-a-can-asia-s-green-transition-survive-the-iran-war-and-climate-pushback>
It should be noted that India acknowledged losing aircraft in the fighting, 
without specifying a number, and has said it also destroyed several Pakistani 
jets, something Pakistan denies.

In any event, the big showing by Chinese companies at the exhibition in Kuala 
Lumpur demonstrates Beijing’s push in Southeast Asia, a region that is one of 
the world’s biggest weapons importers.

This year’s Defence Services Asia event also featured a number of maiden 
appearances from companies based in South Korea, Japan and Russia. Russia’s 
Rosoboronexport displayed its Rus-PE in Southeast Asia for the first time. The 
unmanned aerial weapon loiters in the sky, hunts for targets and then smashes 
into them.

The weapons exhibition also coincided with Japan this week lifting most of its 
restrictions on weapons exports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/japan-scraps-most-weapons-export-limits-in-boost-to-arms-sector?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
 — a move that adds to the points of tension between Beijing and Tokyo.

Japan has already inked a deal to sell advanced warships to Australia, its 
first postwar export of lethal war-fighting equipment. Tokyo wants that sale to 
lead to more weapons sales as companies try to capitalize on growing global 
defense spending by investing in R&D and manufacturing.

It is clear Chinese defense contractors are pushing hard into new markets, 
especially with their drone offerings. They’re likely to have lots of 
competition.

Ready or Not

When the head of a top Chinese streaming company said his Netflix-like 
platform will offer AI versions of celebrities for new shows and movies, the 
bold statement rattled the entertainment industry.

This week, iQiyi officially debuted its Nadou Pro, an AI suite designed to 
handle almost every aspect of filmmaking 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/china-s-netflix-iqiyi-goes-all-in-on-ai-content-in-big-overhaul?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
, from scriptwriting and storyboards to the final cut.

As part of what founder and CEO Gong Yu calls the biggest transition in the 
company’s history, iQiyi also opened an IP library so content creators can tap 
the output of a roster of roughly 100 actors for their films and shows. The 
platform is intended to act as a kind of matchmaking service so performers can 
strike deals with studios on using their likenesses in AI-generated content.

China has been an early adopter of AI entertainment, fueled by a cutthroat 
race to build video-generation models rivaling Silicon Valley’s best, a 
relentless work culture and an embattled film industry grappling with an 
economic slowdown. AI is already heavily used to make so-called micro-dramas — 
minute-long episodes that feature simple storylines and constant cliffhanger 
endings.

Yet the Chinese public, from angry fans on the internet to film critics and 
state media outlets, almost immediately voiced concern about iQiyi’s plan for 
AI productions to go mainstream.

Much of the anxiety stems from the fear that without proper licensing, 
A-listers will becomefuel for sloppy AI content 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-27/china-us-talks-are-back-on-with-xi-in-pretty-strong-position?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
. And where would the boundary between art inspired by the human experience and 
AI movies generated with a few clicks by someone on a laptop fall exactly?

A similar debate is roiling Hollywood 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2025-ai-hollywood/?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
, which isgrappling with widespread layoffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-09-19/getting-a-job-in-hollywood-has-never-been-harder?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
. Still, Lionsgate — the studio behind theJohn Wick movies — has already let 
its film catalog be used to train AI video tools. And through both CGI and AI 
tech, we’ve seen beloved actors brought back to life in films likeAlien: Romulus
 and shows such asObi-Wan Kenobi — for good or ill 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-08-14/alien-romulus-review-a-fun-scary-icky-trip-to-space-ruined-by-ai?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
.
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
Gong, a 16-year veteran of the streaming industry who is worried about iQiyi’s 
weaker revenues in a tough market, wants the bulk of its content produced by AI 
in five years.

Speaking to an audience of producers and directors at iQiyi’s annual content 
showcase in Beijing, he suggested fully human-made work could become 
“intangible cultural heritage” — something akin to ancient art displayed in a 
museum. His company aims to release a commercially successful AI-made film as 
soon as this summer.

So look for that in a theater, err, phone or tablet, near you.

Run Run Run

50 minutes, 26 seconds

That’s the time for a red, humanoid robot in a half-marathon <> Sunday in 
Beijing — about seven minutes faster than the men’s world record. The second 
annual robot race featuring machines running alongside humans showed how rapid 
advances in AI could allow such machines to gain the physical skills to take on 
a range of tasks.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

Tim Cook became a rock star in China during his 15 years as Apple CEO 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-20/apple-names-ternus-as-next-ceo-with-cook-becoming-chairman?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
. As such, he was often swarmed by legions of fans when he visited for a store 
opening or a more low-key trip that might include a meal at a noodle house.

That’s why the company’s announcement that the 65-year-old will be stepping 
down this year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/features/2026-apple-next-ceo/?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
 sent shockwaves through Chinese social media, with many people lamenting the 
departure of someone who has probably done more than anybody inbuilding the 
nation’s tech supply chain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-30/apple-s-reliance-on-china-is-about-far-more-than-labor-costs?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
 and making its manufacturing the behemoth it is today.

Notably, Cook helped establish the vast iPhone City operation in Zhengzhou, in 
central China, when most people associated the country with cheap knockoffs and 
low-margin goods.

“Hello Cook, during your 15 years at Apple, you took over a company at a 
controversial time and made Apple products great again,” wrote one Weibo user, 
responding to a post by Cook thanking customers and fans for their support on 
the social media platform where he has 2 million followers.
CEO Tim Cook at an Apple store in Chengdu, in southwest China, in 2023.
Photographer: VCG/Visual China Group
During his tenure, Apple’s market value soared to $4 trillion from around $350 
billion in 2011. He helped build on the success of the Mac and iPhone by 
pushing into health services, smartwatches and earbuds. Apple had $112 billion 
in net income in the fiscal year ending September 2025, eight times the figure 
in September 2010. The company now touts an active installed base of more than 
2.5 billion devices.

Cook will become Apple’s global ambassador 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/apple-s-tim-cook-takes-on-crucial-new-role-global-ambassador?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
 — a role that is well suited for him given his success in navigating 
Sino-American tensions, while diversifying production into other countries like 
India without alienating China, the company’s biggest single-country market 
after the US.

Still, not all were sad to see him go. Some Chinese netizens complained about 
what they see as the high cost of Apple’s products and a lack of innovation. 
Though the firm’s growth remains strong, it has alsostruggled to catch up in AI 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-05-18/how-apple-intelligence-and-siri-ai-went-so-wrong?cmpid=BBD042426_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260424>
 — tech that promises to transform the way consumers use devices.

“If I had to point out his shortcoming, it would be that Cook is more of a 
manager who maintains the status quo,” Weibo user Crazy Sleepy in the Afternoon 
told his 2.6 million followers.
