US Refiners Feel Squeeze From Middle East Oil Cuts
America’s position as the largest crude producer hasn’t spared it from Middle 
East war repercussions

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporters Nathan Risser and Barbara Powell look at the impact of the Middle 
East war on US refiners.

Almost 7 million barrels a day 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/middle-east-oil-output-cuts-deepen-as-ships-stay-clear-of-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT031126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 of oil supply has been cut in the Middle East due to the effective closure of 
the Strait of Hormuz.

The impact has been global 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/world-races-to-shield-oil-flows-after-iran-war-hits-supplies?cmpid=BBBXT031126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260311>
 and hasn’t spared the US, despite the country’s position as the largest crude 
producer.

A handful of US refiners still rely on oil shipped from the Middle East. On 
the East Coast, two PBF Energy Inc. facilities imported about 85,000 barrels of 
Middle East crude daily last year, while in Texas — the US refining epicenter — 
about 150,000 barrels per day went to several coastal plants.

In California, plants owned by Chevron Corp. 
<bbg://securities/CVX%20US%20Equity> and Marathon Petroleum Corp. 
<bbg://securities/MPC%20US%20Equity> processed about 230,000 barrels from the 
region each day in 2025. The state is particularly exposed to a global oil 
price shock, after two other major refineries there closed in the last 12 
months.

Californian drivers already pay more than $5 a gallon for gasoline, 
considerably more than than national average.

That said, the US has mostly weaned itself off oil from the Persian Gulf over 
the last decade or more, after the shale revolution boosted domestic production 
and turned the country into a net exporter of crude.

Overall, American refineries process 16 million barrels a day of crude. Middle 
Eastern imports account for just a sliver of that total — about half a million 
barrels each day.

Still, those imports highlight the current limits of American Energy 
Dominance, the Trump administration’s doctrine promoting abundance and 
self-reliance focused on fossil fuels.

For the affected refiners, there are alternatives to Middle Eastern cargoes. 
But they face competition. Crude from the Gulf of Mexico, Canada and Alaska has 
already seen price spikes as international buyers bid for the oil in the wake 
of US and Israeli air strikes on Iran.

Instead, US refiners may have to look further south. Access to Venezuelan and 
other Latin American grades should keep US refiners in the clear, Energy 
Aspects founder Amrita Sen said in an interview.

Plants can switch to those sources — especially as high freight rates are 
making it almost impossible to send them East, she said.

—Nathan Risser and Barbara Powell, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Japan is restarting its nuclear reactors at a glacial pace. BloombergNEF <> 
projects capacity will reach 26 gigawatts by 2040 — likely falling short of the 
government’s target for atomic energy to supply 20% of the power mix by then. 
BNEF also estimates that achieving net zero will require even more nuclear 
generation than the government’s goal, meaning the current restart pace will 
remain insufficient.
