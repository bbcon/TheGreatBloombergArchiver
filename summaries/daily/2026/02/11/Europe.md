**European Daily Briefing — February 11, 2026**

**UK politics and markets.** Prime Minister Keir Starmer secured a temporary reprieve from the most acute pressure on his leadership, winning public backing from his full cabinet — including Wes Streeting and Ed Miliband, both viewed as potential successors. The dynamic appears to have strengthened Deputy Prime Minister Angela Rayner's position within Labour's internal hierarchy. However, the relief is likely to prove short-lived: pressures linked to the Epstein files, which reference a key Starmer appointee extensively, continue to weigh, and would-be challengers are widely understood to be biding their time rather than abandoning their ambitions. Separately, Elliott Investment Management has built a stake in **London Stock Exchange Group**, according to a person familiar with the matter, as the exchange contends with AI-related disruption and a sustained decline in listings — a development that may catalyse strategic pressure on management.

**European corporates.** **BP** extended yesterday's slump, falling as much as 7.7% after executives offered no timeline for restoring share buybacks, leaving the UK major increasingly isolated from peers. TotalEnergies trimmed but maintained its buyback, while Chevron, Exxon and Shell held theirs steady. The buyback question will be among the first challenges for incoming CEO Meg O'Neill, who inherits roughly $60 billion in total debt and financial obligations. **Heineken** rose 3.6% in Amsterdam after announcing a roughly 7% reduction in its global workforce in response to softening beer demand. **Dassault Systèmes** fell around 20% in Paris following disappointing results, becoming the latest European software name caught in the broader AI-displacement selloff. **Ford** flagged a $900 million tariff charge but guided to higher 2026 profits.

**Macro and policy.** Norwegian January CPI surprised sharply to the upside, driving 10-year yields to levels last seen in early 2009 and shifting OIS pricing toward a March hike from Norges Bank — a notable divergence from the broader DM easing trajectory. Eurozone yields fell on the day, suggesting markets view the Norwegian print as idiosyncratic rather than a regional signal. The European Parliament moved closer to ratifying the US trade deal, agreeing a 2028 sunset clause that gives Washington six months to reduce steel and aluminium tariffs from 50% to 15%. European Council President António Costa said EU leaders are finally prepared to unify capital markets to compete globally, though execution risks remain considerable.
