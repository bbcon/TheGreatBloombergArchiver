Stalling Momentum

China’s inflation momentum appeared to stall in June 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/china-consumer-inflation-surprises-by-slowing-more-than-forecast?cmpid=BBD070926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6368>
. That’s both good and bad news for the world.

For a global economy facing another tick higher in oil prices as hostilities 
in Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/us-military-launches-strikes-on-iran-for-second-straight-day?cmpid=BBD070926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6368>
 resume, moderation in China’s factory prices is a relief. The country’s 
producer price index historically has a close correlation with its export 
prices, and no one needs the world’s largest exporter adding to inflationary 
impulses right now.

But for China’s domestic outlook, and businesses and economies that tap that 
demand, stalling reflation is less welcome as it signals ongoing sluggishness.

While the effect of a low base from 2025 kept producer inflation on an 
upswing, factory prices declined 0.3% from May on a month-on-month basis, their 
first drop since July 2025. The core CPI, which strips out food and energy 
prices, dipped to 1% in June, a sign demand is weakening even as trade booms.

The central bank, for its part, is showing more concern about the increasing 
divergence in growth following a quarterly meeting of its monetary policy 
committee this month. That’s as a growing number of analysts are estimating 
economic growth slipped below the lower bound of the official target of 4.5% to 
5% in the second quarter.

The next window for policymakers to roll out more stimulus is the upcoming 
meeting of the Communist Party’s decision-making Politburo, which is set to be 
convened in late July. Thursday’s inflation report shows there’s room to act.

Need-to-Know Research

After highlighting economic debate over the implications of reduced inflows of 
people into the labor force in our past twonewsletters 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-07-07/reflections-from-pierre-yared-a-departing-member-of-brainiac-trump-team?cmpid=BBD070926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6368>
, today we flag that Oxford Economics is calculating that the world’s No. 4 
economy, Japan, is just about now approaching its peak labor-force population. 
If so, that means Japanese data in coming quarters and years may prove 
revealing.

While Japan’s overall population has been dropping for years, successful 
efforts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-02/japan-s-dynamic-job-market-rebuts-the-image-of-demographic-decline?cmpid=BBD070926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6368>
 by policymakers to boost the ranks of women in the workforce and to let in 
more foreigners countered the impact on employment. But the capacity to “offset 
the decline in the working-age population” is coming to an end, Norihiro 
Yamaguchi at Oxford Economics wrote in a recent note.

While immigration could in theory keep Japanese employment expanding, Oxford 
Economics believes the large jump inforeign workers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/japan-foreign-worker-record-puts-migration-in-focus-before-vote?cmpid=BBD070926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260709&utm_content=6368>
 seen in recent years “will soon prove unsustainable. Indeed, the government is 
already shifting to limit foreign workers in response to deteriorating public 
sentiment.” Yamaguchi concludes that “a decline in labor force population will 
be inevitable even in the most optimistic scenario.”
