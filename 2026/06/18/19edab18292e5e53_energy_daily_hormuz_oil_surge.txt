 his peace deal with Iran and oil flows are surging through the Strait of

At least 12 million barrels of crude appeared to be making their way 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/two-saudi-oil-tankers-have-appeared-outside-strait-of-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT061826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5679>
 out of the Persian Gulf on Thursday, according to ship-tracking data compiled 
by Bloomberg.

That puts flows at or close to the highest levels since the start of the war, 
although itтАЩs difficult to make a full accounting as many ships turned off 
their location beacons in recent weeks to make so-called dark transits.

Several empty tankers were also heading into the waterway to collect new 
cargoes, something that will be necessary for Middle Eastern countries to begin 
reviving halted production.

The signing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/trump-signs-iran-deal-shifting-focus-to-hormuz-and-60-day-talks?cmpid=BBBXT061826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260618&utm_content=5679>
 of the memorandum of understanding is a huge relief for global markets. But 
for the owners of vessels stuck inside the Persian Gulf, this is just the start 
of a new set of challenges.

Trade groups say they have many unanswered questions, each of which will 
affect the pace and scale of the recovery in energy flows.
The damaged Al-Salmi oil tanker on March 31.Source: Kuwait Oil Tankers Co.
Both the LloydтАЩs Market Association, representing managing agents at the 
London insurance marketplace, and Intertanko, the top trade group for tanker 
owners, said the removal of mines is among the issues that need urgent 
resolution.

Vessels currently traversing the strait are doing so through routes that hug 
the coastlines of Iran or Oman. The main internationally recognized shipping 
lane prior to the war, known as the traffic separation scheme, still isnтАЩt 
guaranteed as safe due to the threat of mines.

The industryтАЩs clarification wish-list goes much further: Who will ensure 
traffic moves safely through the strait, especially in the early stages when 
ships are rushing to get out? What should a ship do if it needs assistance when 
transiting one of the two maritime corridors currently in use?

The memorandum says the waterway will be fully open without tolls for 60 days, 
but what happens then? Tehran has repeatedly indicated its intention to retain 
some level of traffic management.

After months of false dawns, the first phase of reopening is finally underway. 
But these questions will need to be answered before ships can transit Hormuz as 
freely as they did before the war.
