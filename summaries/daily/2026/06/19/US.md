**Monetary Policy**
The most consequential domestic development of the week was the Fed's hawkish turn under Chair Kevin Warsh, which reinforced market expectations for interest rate increases beginning as early as July. Currency traders and leveraged funds responded by aggressively buying dollar call options, producing the greenback's sharpest two-day appreciation in three months. Goldman Sachs cut its year-end gold forecast by $500/oz, explicitly citing the removal of Fed rate-cut expectations for 2026. The move has material spillover effects: as Goldman's Kamakshya Trivedi noted, emerging-market local rates now face a "new adversary" in a hawkish Fed, complicating the risk-reward on EM fixed income.

**Equity and Capital Markets**
US equity markets are closed for Juneteenth, limiting price discovery, though futures softened modestly as Iran-deal optimism faded. The underlying picture remains one of exceptional investor enthusiasm — the $119.2 billion weekly inflow into US equity funds is without precedent in the EPFR dataset, driven by AI capital expenditure narratives and the prior decline in oil prices. The Nasdaq 100 has gained 24% year-to-date and the S&P 500 9.6%, though BofA strategists note that political risks — specifically a potential Senate shift — represent the clearest near-term downside scenario.
