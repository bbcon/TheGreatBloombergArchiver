**Equity Rotation Accelerates Beneath Calm Surface**

The week opened with the Dow Industrials having crossed 50,000 for the first time on Friday, February 6, but the headline masked a more consequential repositioning underneath. The S&P 500 sat just below an all-time high at 6,988 on Monday, trailing world ex-US equities by roughly 7.7 percentage points year-to-date and ranking 69th among 92 global indices tracked by Bloomberg. The rotation out of mega-cap software and into chipmakers, equal-weighted indices, small-caps, and value accelerated through the week, catalyzed by Anthropic's release of new AI work-automation tools that sent the iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF down 8.7% on the week. The AI disruption trade broadened further on Thursday, when US equities fell 1.6% as concerns spread to commercial real estate, insurance brokers, wealth managers, and logistics names. An episode that crystallized the mood: Algorhythm Holdings, a micro-cap former karaoke importer that touted an AI freight platform, helped send the Russell 3000 Trucking Index down 6.6% on Thursday, with C.H. Robinson off 15% and Landstar 16%. Treasury volatility, by contrast, fell to a five-year low, suggesting the rotation remained an equity-market phenomenon rather than a broader risk-off event. With roughly 75% of S&P 500 names reported, Q4 blended earnings growth reached a four-year high near 14.5%.

**Labor Market Surprises, Complicating the Easing Path**

The shutdown-delayed January payrolls report, released Wednesday, showed 130,000 jobs added — the strongest monthly gain in over a year — while the unemployment rate ticked lower, prompting futures markets to push the next anticipated Fed cut to July from June. The print upended an expectations consensus that had been building toward two cuts in 2026. Softer data earlier in the week — stalled December retail sales, a benign Q4 employment cost index, and an NFIB survey showing the share of small firms reporting hard-to-fill openings at a post-pandemic low — had already pushed the 10-year Treasury yield six basis points below its 200-day moving average on Tuesday. Wednesday's reversal was sharp. January CPI, expected Friday, was widely anticipated to show core inflation easing to approximately 2.5%, which would provide the Fed some room to maneuver — though the combination of a tight labor market and a revised-lower labor supply complicates the picture materially.

**Fed Succession and Institutional Questions**

President Trump claimed his Fed chair nominee Kevin Warsh could deliver 15% GDP growth — a figure detached from any plausible potential-output estimate that will sharpen questions about institutional independence should Warsh be confirmed in May. Treasury Secretary Bessent urged the Senate to begin hearings on the nomination despite a Republican senator's pledge to block all such proceedings pending resolution of a criminal investigation involving Chair Powell. Separately, former Fed nominee Judy Shelton met privately with Bessent on central bank reform proposals, including a supply-side rate framework and redeployment of the $2.9 trillion in bank reserves parked at the Fed. A Kevin Warsh-led Fed-Treasury accord proposal also circulated in rates markets, stirring debate about balance-sheet operations and the boundaries of Fed independence.

**Fiscal and Trade Friction**

The Republican-led House passed a bill terminating tariffs on Canadian imports on Wednesday, with six Republicans crossing the aisle — the most significant legislative pushback to the administration's trade agenda to date. The measure is unlikely to survive a presidential veto, but the vote exposed GOP fissures heading into midterm primaries. The administration separately moved to rescind the 2009 EPA endangerment finding underpinning federal greenhouse gas regulation, setting up litigation likely to reach the Supreme Court. A Goldman Sachs study released Friday projected data centers will account for roughly 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation holding near 6% through 2027 — a politically sensitive dynamic heading into November's midterms given cost-of-living pressures.
