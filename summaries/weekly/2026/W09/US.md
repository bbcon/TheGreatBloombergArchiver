**Tariff Regime in Transition**
The week opened with the legal and practical fallout from the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariff architecture. By Tuesday, a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 had entered into force, subsequently raised to 15% by executive action. The legal footing is widely questioned: Section 122 applies specifically to "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath stated plainly the US does not meet, a view echoed by former Treasury official Jay Shambaugh, who noted that a genuine balance-of-payments crisis would be reflected in dollar weakness and capital flight — neither of which is occurring. The administration signaled it intends to slow-walk refunds on overturned levies, framing repayments as "corporate welfare," even as more than 1,500 refund lawsuits have been filed representing an estimated $170 billion in contested duties. Legal challenges appear likely, though courts may move too slowly to constrain tariffs that expire in 150 days under the statute.

**Equity Markets and the AI Disruption Narrative**
The week's dominant equity theme was a rotation away from US software and services names perceived as vulnerable to AI displacement. IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years after Anthropic's Claude Code tool was shown to modernize COBOL, threatening IBM's mainframe franchise. C3.ai tumbled 24%, Trade Desk fell 17% in pre-market, Salesforce slid roughly 4%, Workday dropped 9.2%, and Duolingo fell 24% on below-consensus guidance. Against this, hardware and infrastructure names held up: Dell gained 9.8% on AI server demand, and Block surged as much as 27% after announcing it would cut nearly half its workforce as an AI productivity bet. Nvidia's earnings — with revenue guidance of approximately $78 billion beating consensus — produced a muted after-hours response, encapsulating the increasingly demanding valuation bar AI-adjacent equities now face. The S&P 500 options market reflected the broader caution, with the put-call skew rising to a two-year high and normalized two-month skew approaching the upper end of its five-year range.

**Monetary Policy and the Fed Transition**
The National Association for Business Economics conference devoted its agenda almost entirely to AI's labor market implications, with Fed Governor Lisa Cook suggesting the economy may be approaching the most significant reorganization of work in generations and warning that conventional rate tools cannot offset AI-driven structural unemployment. Richmond Fed President Tom Barkin echoed the caution. The more politically consequential monetary story, however, surrounds Kevin Warsh: Bloomberg Economics analysis confirmed a marked shift from his hawkish 2009–2011 positioning toward dovish messaging since 2025, and Deutsche Bank analysts sketched potential reforms under his expected tenure including revisions to the dot plot and a possible shift toward rolling 12- and 24-month rate horizons.

**The Iran Shock and Domestic Political Risk**
Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities arrived as the week's most consequential late development. For Trump, the political arithmetic is uncomfortable: having campaigned on an America-first, anti-interventionist platform, he has now presided over strikes in Venezuela, Iran, Nigeria, and now a direct assault on Iran's nuclear infrastructure. Polling already suggests Republicans face a difficult path to retaining both congressional chambers. The immediate domestic economic concern is energy: oil has risen nearly 20% year-to-date, and any further spike feeds directly into gasoline prices at a moment of elevated household financial stress. Trump acknowledged in an overnight video address that US casualties are possible, an admission that will intensify scrutiny if losses materialize. On the corporate side, Berkshire Hathaway's PacifiCorp faces approximately $55 billion in wildfire-related claims, and Greg Abel's first annual letter as Buffett's successor inherits a materially complex liability picture as climate-driven risk intensifies across the American West.
