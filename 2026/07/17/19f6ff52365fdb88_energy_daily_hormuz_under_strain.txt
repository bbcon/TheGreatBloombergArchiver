Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Alex Longley looks at how oil markets are weathering the latest 
developments in the Strait of Hormuz.

The Strait of Hormuz isn’t fully shut, but it currently seems less open than 
in previous weeks.

There have been signs over the last few days that some ships are continuing to 
cross 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-16/dark-ships-and-oman-transfers-indicate-hormuz-transits-continue?cmpid=BBBXT071726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6682>
 the waterway, but far fewer than before attacks between the US and Iran 
intensified.

At least four vessels were conducting transfers near Oman yesterday, while 
others popped up in the Arabian Sea after transiting.

They are likely among the few willing to brave a strait where attacks have 
increasingly focused ondark transits 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-05/the-supertanker-tycoon-making-millions-on-hormuz-shuttle-runs?cmpid=BBBXT071726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6682>
 — tankers that move barrels out of the Gulf with their tracking signals 
switched off — which have become the oil market’s main escape valve.

Whether flows remain subdued will be crucial for global energy prices in the 
coming weeks, and many traders are questioning how long the US will continue to 
pressure Iran if oil costs rise further.

For the time being, there have been some gains as a result of the attacks, but 
we’re yet to see a spike of the magnitude seen earlier this year. That’s thanks 
to a surge in flows when the interim peace deal came into effect, flooding the 
market with barrels faster than refiners were able to gobble them up.

But signs of stress in the global oil market are mounting — crude timespreads 
are now heavily backwardated and premiums for refined products are bigger than 
they’ve ever been. Signs of caution from ship owners abound, too: Insurance 
companies say they’re seeingfewer requests to cross 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/hormuz-ship-insurance-demand-drops-as-owners-get-nervous?cmpid=BBBXT071726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6682>
 and premiums have soared.

Some crews have refused to make the journey, another factor which can lower 
flows, though when the cost of shipping is so high, the financial incentive is 
there to find ready replacements.

The result is a muddied picture in Hormuz. Flows haven’t totally stopped, but 
they appear to be much lower than they were a few weeks ago.

The oil market won’t be able to sustain that reduction for long. 

—Alex Longley, Bloomberg News

 is becoming a favored hedge against the risks of sudden U-turns in US 
President Donald Trump’s stance on Iran, a dynamic that has repeatedly 
whipsawed markets in recent months. The strategy involves buying one options 
contract while simultaneously selling another, reducing the upfront premium, 
and can generate attractive profits with well-chosen strike prices. About 400 
million barrels’ worth of $1-wide put spreads have traded since the middle of 
last week as Trump vacillated over next steps in the conflict.
