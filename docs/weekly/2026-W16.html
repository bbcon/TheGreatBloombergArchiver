Weekly Brief

Apr 13 – Apr 19, 2026

US

  • University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to 47.6 on Friday — the weakest reading in the survey's 1978 history — as year-ahead inflation expectations rose a full percentage point to 4.8%.
  • The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 reached all-time highs on Wednesday, April 16, completing rallies of roughly 10% and 14% respectively from their March 30 lows — among the fastest recoveries of that magnitude since 1950.
  • Federal Reserve independence remains politically contested, with Trump reiterating threats to remove Jerome Powell and Senator Tillis blocking Kevin Warsh's confirmation pending resolution of a Justice Department building-renovation probe.

Consumer Sentiment and the Macro Backdrop

The week's most striking domestic data point arrived on Friday, when the University of Michigan's preliminary April consumer sentiment index printed at 47.6 — below every prior trough in the survey's history, including readings during the 1980 hostage crisis, the 1990 Gulf War, and the Covid pandemic. Year-ahead inflation expectations rose to 4.8%, their highest in decades. The political polarization embedded in sentiment surveys provides some analytical comfort — Republican respondents have historically skewed results — but even that cohort reported conditions worse than at any point during the Biden administration. March CPI had already printed at its highest in more than two years ahead of the week, briefly pushing the headline rate back above 3%, and while core and trimmed-mean measures remained below that threshold, all four main CPI components were rising simultaneously — a configuration that appears to preclude near-term Fed rate cuts. Mortgage applications fell more than 10% through March, and existing home sales appear anchored near 4.1 million annualized. The pent-up housing demand that might have driven recovery remains sidelined by financing costs.

Equity Markets: Speed Without Precedent

Against that macro backdrop, the equity market's behavior warrants scrutiny. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 closed at all-time highs on Wednesday, April 16, having recovered all their war-driven losses in a move that several analysts described as one of the fastest recoveries from a comparable drawdown since 1950. The Nasdaq extended its winning streak to twelve consecutive sessions. By Thursday, April 17, the S&P 500 closed above 7,000 for the first time since January, on track for a third consecutive week of gains exceeding 3%. The closest historical parallel by earnings multiple and timing, as several strategists noted, appears to be March 2000. The 10-year Treasury yield sits near 4.3%, and the Shiller excess CAPE yield implies below-average prospective decade returns. First-quarter earnings estimates have declined during the rally rather than risen, making the multiple expansion difficult to justify on fundamental grounds. The Bank of America fund manager survey showed the proportion viewing US equities as overvalued at a seven-year low — a finding that reflects valuation compression from war-related selling but does not resolve the underlying tension. A basket of retail-favored shares is on track for its best month relative to mutual fund favorites since 2020, and the Roundhill Meme Stock ETF gained 54% from late March.

Monetary Policy and Political Uncertainty

The Federal Reserve remains firmly on hold, a posture now implicitly endorsed by Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, who signaled twice in the week that he understands the case for waiting while the war's inflationary impact becomes clearer — a meaningful shift from his prior advocacy for cuts. That framing provides political cover for incoming chairman Kevin Warsh to avoid an embarrassing early dissent, should his confirmation proceed. The Fed's Beige Book described an economy in suspension with no near-term catalyst for movement in either direction. Markets are pricing near-zero probability of a June rate change. Separately, the Justice Department made an unannounced visit to the Federal Reserve in Washington, adding to institutional uncertainty. The White House's Economic Report of the President projected 3.5% growth in 2026 and 10-year yields falling to 3.7% — against current rates of approximately 4.28% and a CBO forecast of 4.4% for the early 2030s. The gap between administration projections and independent forecasts remains substantial.

Earnings and Corporate News

Beyond the headline index moves, corporate results were uneven. Goldman Sachs and Fastenal both disappointed at the open of reporting season on Monday. Netflix shares fell approximately 10% in premarket trading on Friday after the streamer's earnings forecast missed estimates, with co-founder Reed Hastings simultaneously announcing his departure from the board after 29 years. Alcoa disappointed despite elevated metal prices, with operational disruptions and higher costs weighing on results. QVC filed for bankruptcy to restructure more than $5 billion in debt — a casualty of secular retail shifts rather than the current macro cycle. A federal antitrust jury found Live Nation illegally monopolized the live events industry, setting the stage for a potential structural breakup. On BNPL, Klarna has lost more than 60% of its market value since its September IPO, with nearly half of users reporting at least one late installment payment in the past year and usage increasingly concentrated in essentials such as groceries — a sign of financial stress rather than discretionary adoption.

Fiscal, Energy, and Political Dynamics

Treasury Secretary Bessent confirmed that tariff rates may be restored to their pre-Supreme Court levels by July, after the court struck down emergency-powers authority for their original imposition. Atlanta Fed survey evidence suggests that the One Big Beautiful Bill's capex provisions have had limited uptake — only 20% of firms incorporated the legislation into planning, echoing the limited pass-through observed after the 2017 tax cuts. States including California, Colorado, New York, and New Jersey are accelerating large-scale renewable energy procurement ahead of federal investment tax credits expiring on July 4, with Southern California Edison alone targeting 6,000 megawatts of additional capacity. The Trump administration rejected CenterPoint Energy's request to retire a 60-year-old Indiana coal plant. On defense infrastructure, the Pentagon continues to advance large-scale hardening at military installations — rebranded as "operational readiness" rather than climate resilience — with Tyndall Air Force Base being rebuilt with flood-resistant structures following nearly $5 billion in hurricane damage. Former Treasury Secretary Henry Paulson warned on Bloomberg Television that a collapse in Treasury demand remains a tail risk, urging authorities to develop contingency plans. ---

China: Growth Beats, but Structural Imbalances Persist

China's first-quarter GDP print of 5.0% year-on-year, released on Wednesday, April 16, beat consensus expectations and offered policymakers a measure of reassurance. The headline, however, conceals a pronounced divergence: industrial output grew 5.7% in March while retail sales rose only 1.7%, falling short of forecasts. A significant portion of the export surge reflected manufacturers front-running US tariff escalation, a boost that is by definition temporary. The Canton Fair in Guangzhou provided a more granular signal of the headwinds ahead — Middle Eastern buyers, who had increasingly substituted for absent American customers since the pandemic, were conspicuously absent this year due to the conflict and flight disruption, with exporters describing conditions as potentially the worst in memory. March trade data showed export growth slowing sharply while imports surged at the fastest pace since late 2021, as elevated energy commodity purchases reflected the war's disruption to Gulf supply. The two-speed economy — robust supply side, fragile demand side — remains a central policy challenge, and the tariff environment that incentivized front-loading may itself constrain external demand in coming quarters.

Energy Squeeze and the Teapot Refiners

China's independent oil refiners — the so-called teapots, accounting for roughly a fifth of national refining capacity — remain under acute pressure as the primary transmission channel of Hormuz disruption into the domestic economy. Heavily reliant on discounted Iranian crude, the sector is now exposed to both US sanctions enforcement and blockade risk. Floating stockpiles of approximately 38 million barrels of Iranian crude in Asian waters provide a buffer of perhaps two months, and Beijing has released strategic reserves and granted additional import quotas. Saudi Aramco's official selling prices have risen to a record, and Riyadh's crude shipments to Chinese customers are set to approximately halve in May — to around 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April. China's overall oil and gas imports declined in March. Russia's potential to fill part of the gap creates a structural incentive for Beijing to reinforce that relationship, as Xi's meeting with Foreign Minister Sergei Lavrov on Wednesday made clear, with Xi emphasizing that "the stability and certainty of China-Russia relations are particularly valuable." Yuan-denominated crude oil transactions reached a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS during the week.

Geopolitical Positioning and the Xi Diplomatic Offensive

Xi's most diplomatically active week in nearly two years — receiving Spain's Prime Minister Sánchez, Abu Dhabi's Crown Prince, Vietnam's President, and Russia's Foreign Minister — served a deliberate purpose: framing Beijing as the responsible stakeholder in a disordered world. Xi's description of the international order as "crumbling into disarray," delivered in the context of his Sánchez meeting on Tuesday, was notably stronger than his customary characterizations of global turbulence. He proposed a four-point Middle East peace plan and vowed to deepen ties with Moscow. The direct question of whether US warships will interdict vessels carrying Iranian oil to China — a scenario that would constitute a significant escalation in bilateral tensions — remains unresolved, as illustrated by the Rich Starry incident on Tuesday, when a US-sanctioned tanker linked to China transited the Strait without interdiction, challenging the blockade's credibility. A Trump-Xi summit in approximately a month's time looms as the next significant test. China has condemned the US-Israeli strikes on Iran as "unacceptable," but Xi has not publicly commented on the blockade.

Taiwan, Japan, and Korea: AI Tailwinds Meet Macro Headwinds

Taiwan Semiconductor's first-quarter results on Wednesday provided the clearest evidence yet that AI infrastructure investment is sustaining its own momentum largely independent of the broader geopolitical disruption. The 58% profit surge and guidance raise to above 30% full-year revenue growth drove Taiwan's Taiex to a record high on Tuesday, April 14, posting a year-to-date gain of 25%. South Korea's Kospi has rallied approximately 23%, recovering sharply from an 18% two-day decline at the conflict's onset; BlackRock flagged Korean equities as a preferred position. In Japan, the more cautious backdrop is evident: BOJ Governor Ueda's pullback on rate hike signaling on Monday, combined with the 10-year JGB yield reaching 2.49% on the same day — its highest since 1997 — reflects the difficulty of navigating tightening global financial conditions while domestic supply chains absorb naphtha shortages. Toilet manufacturer Toto suspended new prefabricated bathroom orders citing feedstock constraints, illustrative of a growing list of Japanese manufacturers cutting production or raising prices. Japan's Finance Minister Katayama warned of "bold action" to support the yen following bilateral discussions with Bessent in Washington, a statement widely interpreted as an intervention threat that received no pushback from the US side. Macro hedge funds in China suffered notable losses in March, with Bridgewater's All Weather Plus strategy declining 5.6% from late February through early April, and the peer-group average loss of 6.3% described as the worst on record. ---

Hungary: A Structural Shift in European Politics

Viktor Orbán's defeat on Sunday, April 13, represents the most consequential shift in European politics in several years. Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured what appears to be a parliamentary supermajority, reducing Fidesz to a rump opposition after 16 years of what one Macquarie strategist characterized as "unchecked executive power through erosion of institutional pillars." The immediate European dividend is substantial: Orbán's Hungary had repeatedly exercised veto power to block EU measures requiring unanimity, holding up approximately €90 billion in support for Ukraine and repeatedly fracturing EU sanctions consensus. Magyar has pledged to dismantle the illiberal governance structures, reorient toward Brussels and NATO, and supports eventual euro adoption. German Chancellor Merz moved quickly to press for rapid disbursement of the Ukraine loan package, meeting with President Zelensky in Berlin on Wednesday. The forint surged to a four-year high on Monday, April 14, triggering what markets have described as a "euro convergence trade." Orbán called a Fidesz party congress for April 28 to review the defeat. Complications remain: Magyar inherits an economy that has consistently underperformed regional peers Poland and Czechia, is dependent on Russian energy imports, and carries commercial ties to Asian battery manufacturers that will constrain the pace of reorientation. Veto rights over EU decisions may also prove too useful a tool to abandon entirely even under new leadership.

ECB Policy: Genuine Uncertainty

ECB President Lagarde's comments at the IMF Spring Meetings on Tuesday and Wednesday placed the eurozone explicitly between two scenarios without resolving them. The March baseline incorporated two rate increases in 2026; the adverse scenario projects 3.5% inflation this year. Lagarde stated the ECB has no tightening bias while simultaneously acknowledging that "looking through" the energy shock risks undermining price stability credibility — positions in tension that she did not reconcile. ECB policymaker Madis Müller separately called for vigilance on inflation risks. A Bloomberg survey published later in the week pointed to a June hike as increasingly plausible given inflation tracking above earlier projections. Bank of England Governor Bailey struck a similar note of cautious ambiguity, signaling no urgency to move while acknowledging the difficulty of assessing the war's full economic impact. Pimco indicated it sees value in European bonds following the war-driven selloff, suggesting institutional buyers view current yield levels as attractive entry points.

Luxury Shock and Industrial Stress

The week delivered a sharp earnings reality check for European luxury. Hermès shares fell as much as 14% on Wednesday, April 15 — its largest single-day decline on record — after first-quarter sales growth disappointed, with management attributing weakness to the confidence effects of the war alongside broader consumer caution. Kering also reported disappointing figures. LVMH's fashion and leather goods division, home to Louis Vuitton, reported sales declining in the first quarter when it reported on Monday, April 14. The contrast with ASML could hardly be more pronounced: Europe's most valuable company raised its full-year guidance to €36–40 billion on Wednesday, with its CEO noting AI demand means supply cannot meet orders "for the foreseeable future." European chemical producers face acute input cost pressures from surging fossil-fuel feedstock prices, with SKW Stickstoffwerke Piesteritz's COO warning that loss of basic chemical supply chains could trigger broader economic damage. The Port of Antwerp-Bruges, home to major operations for TotalEnergies, ExxonMobil, and BASF, is managing additional disruption from a fuel spill. Alstom fell 36% in Paris on Thursday, April 17 — its steepest single-day decline since 2023 — after withdrawing full-year free cash flow guidance. Lufthansa shares fell 4.4% on Monday following a two-day pilot strike over pensions.

UK Political Turbulence

Prime Minister Starmer's position deteriorated materially on Thursday, April 17, when it emerged that Washington Ambassador Peter Mandelson had received security clearance despite objections from vetting officials — a decision complicated by Mandelson's past associations with Jeffrey Epstein. Starmer's response, dismissing Foreign Office permanent secretary Olly Robbins, has been widely characterized as managing the political optics rather than addressing the underlying judgment question. Calls for resignation are growing. The episode arrives as Starmer was attempting to position the UK as a constructive international actor, co-hosting with France a summit on establishing a naval force to ensure freedom of navigation in the Strait of Hormuz — a project whose European support base remains fractured. Separately, UK household energy costs are tracking roughly £500 higher for a typical household, the RICS homebuyer appetite gauge fell to its lowest since August 2023, and Moody's warned that French elections risk stalling budget consolidation. In French telecoms, three major operators entered exclusive talks to acquire Patrick Drahi's SFR at approximately $24 billion on Thursday, a transaction that would reshape the country's mobile landscape. Italy's Meloni called Trump's remarks about Pope Leo XIV "unacceptable," a signal of growing distance from Washington that reflects wider European discomfort with the conflict's management. ---

The Hormuz Blockade: Strategy, Execution Risk, and Market Pricing

The formal US naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, announced after the collapse of US-Iran talks in Islamabad over the weekend, represents a material escalation in a conflict that has disrupted global energy markets since the February 28 strikes. The strategic logic, as articulated by advisers close to the situation, is to close Iran's revenue mechanism while simultaneously working to reopen the mined half of the Strait through demining, escorts, and insurance backstops — thereby pressuring Tehran to concede politically. The approach carries substantial execution risk: US warships operating in closer proximity to Iranian drone and missile capabilities face a rapid escalation cycle should any vessel be struck. Iran responded on Monday by threatening that "no port in the Persian Gulf and Sea of Oman will be safe" if its own facilities are targeted. The UK confirmed it will not participate in the blockade. The mechanism's credibility was directly challenged on Tuesday when the US-sanctioned tanker Rich Starry — linked to China — transited the Strait without apparent interdiction, and two Iran-linked vessels were later observed testing alternative corridors between Larak and Qeshm islands. By Thursday, only a single Pakistan-flagged tanker had completed a crude cargo exit since the blockade came into force, illustrating the gap between diplomatic tone and operational reality. Trump expressed optimism about a permanent ceasefire on Thursday, claiming Iran had conceded on nuclear ambitions and Hormuz access — assertions Tehran has not publicly confirmed. Gulf Arab and European officials believe a durable agreement is more likely six months away.

Macro Consequences: Growth, Trade, and Supply Chains

The IMF's Spring Meetings in Washington provided a sobering backdrop, with Bloomberg Economics' base case projecting global growth of 2.9% in 2026 — the weakest since 2020 — and a downside scenario of 2.2% if oil averages $170 a barrel. Global trade volume growth is now projected at 2.8% for 2026, below global GDP growth of 3.1%. Qatar's Finance Minister described current conditions as "the tip of the iceberg," and economist Carmen Reinhart argued the world is less prepared to respond to a crisis than at any point in the past two decades, constrained by a $348 trillion global debt burden, central banks still contending with inflation, and a fragmented multilateral order. Global Trade Alert has tracked more than 250 distinct government policy responses since hostilities began — the large majority liberalizing import measures, energy subsidies, or demand-reduction programs. Panama Canal congestion has reached its worst since the 2023–2024 drought, with vessels facing 3.5-day waits and one ship paying $4 million to jump the queue. The Port of Los Angeles chief estimates that even after the Strait reopens, full shipping normalization will take approximately four and a half months. The IEA issued a striking forecast: global oil demand is expected to decline in 2026 for the first time since the pandemic, as the price surge offsets underlying demand growth.
