Great Expectations

Central banking across major developed markets right now seems to have just as 
much to do with psychology as it does economics.

Scarred by their post-Covid policy failure, where a bet that a 
supply-shock-induced inflationary wave would prove тАЬtransitoryтАЭ turned out to 
be terribly wrong, monetary authorities arenтАЩt taking risks this time around.

As we explore in todayтАЩs Big Take 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-05-21/iran-war-upends-inflation-bets-in-50-trillion-debt-market-safe-haven?cmpid=BBD052226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4720>
, thereтАЩs a battle to keep inflation expectations in check as the Iran War and 
closure of the Strait of Hormuz drag on. The danger is that workers respond to 
higher pump prices with demands for pay hikes while companies react to rising 
costs with plans to pass those on.

While such concerns have been bubbling away for months now, all of a sudden 
the bond market has taken notice. Long-term yields in the $50 trillion-plus 
market for Group of Seven sovereign bonds hit a two-decade high this week.

тАЬYou just have this series of never-ending supply shocks, and these big

entering a different phase of the monetary cycle,тАЭ he says. тАЬItтАЩll be rate 
hikes everywhere.тАЭ

Against this near-term battle to keep inflation expectations
