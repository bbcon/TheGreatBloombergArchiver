## 2. Shape of the Economy Entering the Crisis

Going into the Hormuz closure (late February 2026), the global economy was in a fragile but broadly stable configuration — characterized by four structural features:

**Monetary easing as the baseline.** All major central banks were either cutting or widely expected to cut through 2026. The Fed had already delivered 75bp of reductions from the 2024 peak. Markets were pricing two further Fed cuts for 2026. The BOJ was the outlier — normalizing from negative rates — but even Tokyo was moving cautiously. This easing impulse underpinned asset prices and credit spreads globally.

**AI-driven productivity premium.** The technology sector, particularly the AI-semiconductor complex, had generated the most concentrated earnings growth in S&P 500 history. TSMC, SK Hynix, and Samsung were collectively rerating the equity risk premium across Asia. Business investment in AI infrastructure was accelerating rather than peaking. This created a two-speed dynamic: real economy consumers under pressure, capital markets pricing a productivity breakout.

**Tariff regime uncertainty already embedded.** The IEEPA tariff architecture was under legal challenge before the Hormuz shock. Supply chain rerouting — particularly from China — had been underway since mid-2025. Multinationals had already partially adjusted sourcing. The trade policy uncertainty index was elevated, but most corporations had adapted operationally. It was not the dominant macro risk entering February.

**Consumer fragility in advanced economies.** Despite equity market strength, household balance sheets were deteriorating. US savings rate had fallen to 4.5% (from 6.4% in January 2024). Buy-now-pay-later usage was shifting toward groceries. University of Michigan sentiment was already weakening before any energy shock. European consumers were more constrained: German industrial stagnation, French fiscal consolidation, and the residual cost-of-living squeeze from 2022–2024 inflation had compressed discretionary spending.

**The pre-crisis baseline in one sentence:** Monetary policy support was buying time for an AI productivity cycle to validate elevated valuations, against a consumer sector that was running down savings and a trade environment that had already absorbed substantial tariff uncertainty — leaving little macro buffer for an exogenous shock.

## 3. The Hormuz Shock: Transmission to the Macro Outlook

The Strait of Hormuz closure (late February 2026) delivered an energy shock with no modern precedent in speed of price impact. Within three weeks:

- Brent crude peaked above **$118/barrel** (+60% from pre-crisis levels)
- **Jet fuel surged to >$215/barrel** — an all-time high, up ~80%
- Global shipping rates spiked as routes rerouted around the Cape of Good Hope
- **Lufthansa cancelled ~20,000 short-haul flights** within the first month; airlines globally shifted to fuel hedging and network rationalization

Monetary policy reversed within weeks. Easing cycles were suspended or reversed; the Fed's final Powell meeting (April 2026) produced the most divided vote since 1992, with markets moving to price zero cuts in 2026 and a 50% probability of a hike by April 2027. The IEA authorized the largest strategic reserve release in its history (400 million barrels) to cap price escalation.

**Downside scenario:** If Hormuz remains closed through end-Q2 (oil at $170/barrel), the Dallas Fed models a **-2.9 percentage point drag on global GDP in the next quarter alone** — equivalent to pulling the IMF baseline from 2.9% to ~2.0%, near-recessionary for advanced economies.

## 5. Implications for Airline Industry Strategic Partners

The macro backdrop creates a specific set of operating conditions for airlines and their strategic partners across three dimensions:

**Demand side — bifurcated resilience.** Business travel, particularly premium and long-haul, has proven more durable than leisure short-haul in prior energy shocks. AI-sector expansion (hyperscaler capex approaching $725B combined) continues to generate high-margin executive travel demand. However, consumer-facing leisure travel faces the double compression of energy-cost pass-through (higher fares) and weakening household sentiment — US consumer confidence at a historic low of 47.6 and savings rates declining. Airlines with premium network exposure are better insulated.

**Cost side — fuel as the defining variable.** At $215/barrel jet fuel (vs ~$115/barrel pre-crisis), a wide-body long-haul aircraft burns approximately $35,000–$45,000 more per transatlantic round trip. This is unhedgeable at scale within months. Airlines with higher hedging ratios (Southwest, Ryanair historically) have a transitory cost advantage; unhedged carriers face margin compression that cannot be absorbed through yield alone without volume loss. MRO partners and lessors face accelerated retirement discussions as older, fuel-inefficient widebodies become economically marginal.

**Route network — geography is reshaping flows.** The Hormuz closure has materially altered traffic patterns:
- *Middle East transit hubs (Dubai, Doha, Abu Dhabi)* face structural demand disruption from rerouted flights and regional travel depression
- *Southeast Asian hubs (Singapore, Hong Kong)* are absorbing rerouted flows from Cape of Good Hope-adjusted itineraries
- *Atlantic routes* face capacity pressure as European carriers pull back short-haul to protect long-haul economics
- India — a key growth market with 6%+ GDP growth — faces a specific demand risk from heat stress and currency weakness, moderating its near-term expansion potential for international carriers

**Macro tail risk for partners:** The critical monitor is the Section 122 tariff expiry (~late July 2026) and the US-China Geneva truce expiry (September 2026). A tariff escalation cycle resuming in Q3 — potentially coinciding with still-elevated energy costs — would amplify the demand compression in trade-exposed manufacturing corridors (trans-Pacific, trans-Atlantic freight). For cargo partners, the Q3 2026 window is the highest-risk period in the current planning horizon.
