Rate Response

The world’s top central banks convene this week facing a renewed inflation 
threat from the war in Iran and the possibility that they’ll be forced to delay 
interest-rate cuts and in some cases consider hikes.

Changes aren’t imminent yet: The Federal Reserve 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/fed-to-hold-interest-rates-steady-as-iran-war-scrambles-the-economic-outlook?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
, theEuropean Central Bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/economists-see-ecb-holding-rates-until-2028-in-split-from-market?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 and theBank of England 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/uk-inflation-risk-looks-more-2011-than-2022-for-bank-of-england?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 are all expected to keep borrowing costs steady as they assess how much 
surging energy costs will feed through to consumer prices and growth.

But for them and the other 18 central banks that are about to set policy — 
overseeing about two-thirds of the global economy in total — the tone will turn 
more cautious as they acknowledge the risk of another inflation shock.

Much hinges on how long the conflict lasts — something markets are struggling 
to gauge. Investors wary of stagflation have been whipsawed by oil-price swings 
and uncertainty over Trump’s next move, raising questions about how quickly 
central bankers would react to fresh price pressures.

What’s clear is that policymakers around the globe 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/rba-to-deliver-back-to-back-hikes-as-iran-war-refuels-inflation?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 — still counting the cost ofUS tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/trump-tariffs-tracker/?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 and grappling with a fragmenting geopolitical landscape — are reluctantly 
preparing to step back in should events in the Middle East reignite consumer 
prices, derail economic growth or hijack their currencies.

“Central banks can set interest rates — they can’t reopen the Strait of 
Hormuz,” saidTom Orlik <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/APScahppEq4/tom-orlik>
, Bloomberg Economics’ chief economist. “Expect Powell, Lagarde, Bailey & Co to 
stay on hold, signal vigilance, and hope the Iran war ends before it lands them 
with another inflation problem they’re ill equipped to solve.”

Like then, it’s tough to estimate the duration of the fighting

Trump has flip-flopped from saying the war could end “very soon” to claiming

 — a choke point for energy shipments — effectively closed.

For now, reductions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/economists-see-two-fed-2026-rate-cuts-reveal-worries-over-chair-nominee-warsh?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>

inflation risks

While investors are no longer fully pricing in a cut in 2026, they’re still 
leaning toward easing — making the US the outlier among its Group of Seven

Need-to-Know Research

While the Mideast war has significantly disrupted the global energy supply 
chain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/oil-market-set-for-tumultuous-week-as-kharg-attack-raises-stakes?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 and prompted a jacking up of 2026 projections for oil and gas prices, the 
conflict is unlikely to provoke the sort of broader economic supply shock the 
world saw in 2021-22, according to Goldman Sachs economists.

“Our analysis suggests that the major risk to global supply and inflation is 
mostly confined to energy,” Joseph Briggs and Megan Peters wrote in a note 
Sunday. That “limits the risk that the severe supply chain disruptions (and 
associated surge in inflation) and large second-round inflation effects 
observed in 2021-2022 will re-emerge.” They cited three reasons, reviewed below.

Non-energy trade with the Gulf is limited, at just 1% of global commerce, with 
major economies “generally even less exposed.” By contrast, post-Covid 
shutdowns in east Asia affected 20% of world trade. Second, though the region 
accounts for a large share of some chemical and andmetal goods 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/bahrain-starts-cutting-output-at-world-s-top-aluminum-smelter?cmpid=BBD031626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
, these aren’t as vital as the likes of semiconductors, whose shortage proved 
crippling in 2021-22. Finally, the region isn’t a big re-exporter of most 
traded goods, so the disruption to global shipping costs should be limited, the 
Goldman duo said.
