The defining macro development of the moment remains the Iran war and its cascading effects on energy markets. Brent crude's prompt spread — the difference between its two nearest futures contracts — widened above $10 per barrel in backwardation over the past week, the most acute reading since the conflict began and surpassing peaks reached following Russia's invasion of Ukraine in 2022. Dated Brent, the benchmark for physical market transactions, briefly surged above $140 per barrel ahead of Easter, the highest level since 2008. OPEC+ warned Sunday that damage to Middle Eastern energy infrastructure will produce prolonged supply disruption extending well beyond any eventual ceasefire, a supply-side assessment that should be taken seriously given the group's direct visibility into regional production capacity.

The geopolitical situation deteriorated meaningfully over the Easter weekend. Iran downed two US warplanes; US forces rescued the surviving aircrew after a two-day operation; the US destroyed Iran's largest bridge; and Iranian forces escalated attacks on Gulf energy facilities. President Trump's ultimatum — demanding the Strait of Hormuz reopen by Tuesday or face strikes on Iranian power plants — did little to suggest imminent de-escalation. Prediction markets assign roughly even odds to a ceasefire by end-June; a protracted conflict beyond that horizon would present a more severe challenge to global growth and financial stability.

Global M&A recorded its strongest first quarter on record at approximately $1.3 trillion in transaction value, roughly 20% above the prior year, suggesting corporate strategic agendas remained intact through the conflict's early weeks. However, deal values have declined around 15% since the war began, indicating a meaningful frontloading effect and growing caution. Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon characterized the outlook as cautiously constructive but explicitly flagged that a protracted war or additional exogenous shock could alter sentiment materially. Private credit markets — a critical funding source for leveraged transactions — are showing signs of stress, adding a further constraint on deal financing conditions if inflation forces the Fed toward tightening. The war's ultimate impact on the global dealmaking cycle, as with so much else, remains contingent on developments in Iran.
