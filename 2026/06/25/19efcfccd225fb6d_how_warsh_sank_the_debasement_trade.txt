The Un-Debasement Trade

Precious metals’ remarkable rally to start this year seems very distant. Gold 
has given up all its gains and is now down by about 8%. Wednesday saw it drop 
below $4,000 per ounce for the first time in eight months.

That reversal has played out thanks to gold’s loss of haven appeal 
<https://bloom.bg/4etXXRt>, even as three months of fighting in the Middle East 
gave investors powerful need of somewhere to shelter. It’s also driven by a 
broader unwinding of the debasement trade that had probably started to fray 
before the war. Kevin Warsh’s nomination as Federal Reserve chair in January 
(over candidates seen as beholden to President Donald Trump’s more dovish 
agenda) likely marked the shift in expectations that ultimately undermined the 
trade.

The value of the S&P 500 when denominated in gold, a classic indicator of the 
confidence in whether growth is being driven by true economic strength or by 
debasement, turned upward in dramatic fashion three months ago:


How far this trade unravels largely depends on whether it’s more about 
feckless fiscal policy or irresponsible monetary policy. Despite the spotlight 
on Warsh, Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution argues that the debasement 
trade is primarily about the fear that policymakers (in the US and elsewhere) 
will inflate away unsustainable debt burdens:

So it starts with fiscal and then monetary provides the assist. When you 
inflate away debt, you turn on the printing presses. So monetary policy is an 
accessory. But the root cause is bad fiscal policy. That’s why you have to turn 
on the printing presses.

This explains why the markets parsed the announcement of Jay Powell’s 
successor in unusual detail. Warsh was nominated amid persistent calls from 
Trump for lower interest rates (which should help the gold price), but his 
nomination immediately rattled investors. Gold tumbled as much as 13% from an 
all-time high that day, its steepest decline in more than four decades, while 
its putative modern replacement, Bitcoin, subsequently collapsed. The dollar, 
meanwhile, found a bottom after a prolonged slide. Its surge has been given 
extra force by the end of the war, and the DXY broad dollar index closed 
Wednesday at a 14-month high:

Warsh’s reputation as an inflation hawk sowed doubt about whether rates would 
continue to fall under his leadership. Any lingering fear that the Fed might 
ignore renewed inflation pressures was quickly dispelled by the new chairman’s
maiden press conference <https://bloom.bg/4aPM2uM>, where he emphasized price 
stability above all else. But the narrative of a new hawkishness may be 
overblown. The energy shock is dissipating quickly in the wake of the peace 
deal, and that has brought down inflation expectations even more sharply. If 
Warsh wants more room to cut rates, this should give it to him:

The issue here is that prevailing inflation wasn’t driven solely by the 
closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Even as thewaterway reopens 
<https://bloom.bg/4oJAv5X>, price pressures increasingly appear to extend 
beyond energy. That helps to explain the Fed’s hawkish tilt. Vincent Deluard of 
StoneX Financial argues that investors who celebrate the slaying of the 
inflation dragon before it’s dead will be disappointed:

The Iran peace deal should ease one source of stress. Oil prices should not 
spike to $200, and US inflation should not revisit its 2022 peak if the Straits 
of Hormuz remain open. However, inflation will not return to the 2% target and 
will remain stuck at a 3.5%-4% plateau until the next inflationary shock.

Apollo Global’s Torsten Slok points to a counterintuitive scenario in which 
lower oil prices could act like a tax cut, and pump more demand into an already 
overheating economy. That could spur higher inflation, as seen in the chart 
below:

Driven by the strong April CPI, hot May non-farm payrolls, and a hawkish Fed, 
Slok argues that there is a new market narrative, in which reopening the Strait 
overheats the economy and forces the Fed to raise interest rates soon. And 
indeed, Bloomberg’s World Interest Rate Probabilities function shows rate 
expectations increasing when the crude price rose, but then climbing further as 
it fell:

Such projections support the dollar’s renewed strength. Standard Chartered 
Bank’s Steven Englander suggests that real and nominal rate differentials have 
been the greenback’s main drivers since early May. He expects the Fed to remain 
on hold and theEuropean Central Bank <https://bloom.bg/4oQJOkL> to cut once in 
the first half of next year, favoring the dollar. The move in forecasts has 
been dramatic in recent weeks:

A stronger dollar won’t be popular in the White House, and could exacerbate 
Bitcoin’s woes. The dollar index has climbed to its highest in more than a 
year, while the cryptocurrency is hovering near its lowest price since 2024. 
Given Bitcoin’s longstanding inverse relationship with the dollar, this isn’t a 
modest tailwind:

Absent a return to inflation (which certainly can’t be ruled out), betting on 
renewed debasement is increasingly difficult to justify. At this point, the 
trade is on its last legs.

—Richard Abbey and John Authers

An Unburst Bubble

The great bubble in semiconductor stocks was within microns of bursting — but 
then Micron Technology Inc.’s results came to the rescue. Trading in Micron, 
once more a $1.4 trillion company if the latest after-hours price holds up, has 
been anything but normal. Volatility like this cannot endure. Yet all’s well 
that ends well, and we can assume that the trade will survive for a while 
longer:

Micron cleared the high bar that had been set for it, raising revenue 
forecasts further than expected while also delivering a positive surprise on 
earnings. In the space of only two quarters, 12-month trailing earnings have 
quadrupled:

Before the announcement, Micron’s stock had closed trading at 9.5 times 
expected earnings — barely even half the multiple for the average S&P 500 
company. The question will continue to be about the sustainability of the 
immense sums that are now being spent on Micron’s products, rather than on 
valuation put on its stock. These earnings, and the projections to accompany 
them, brook no argument <https://bloom.bg/4gFlUXf>.

Early trading in Asia also seemed to surf the latest confirmation that people 
are spending big money on chips. SK Hynix Inc. had sparked the selloff with 
Tuesday’s news that it was planning to concentrate more on lower-margin DRAM 
memory chips. On Wednesday, it unveiled a plan for a $29 billionUS equity 
issuance <https://bloom.bg/3QEb21m>, which would normally weigh on the share 
price. When insiders are selling on a scale like that, it’s generally a warning 
sign to investors; but Micron seems to have created just thetailwind that Hynix 
needed <https://bloom.bg/4gFVOTX>:

This doesn’t mean that everything in the garden is rosy. Larry McDonald of 
Bear Traps Report points out that volatility like this, with companies gaining 
and losing more than $100 billion in a matter of hours, is rare. Generally, it 
is only ever seen close to major market tops or bottoms.

Korea’s roller-coaster caused such alarm on Wall Street in large part because 
of the evidence that massive silicon-built fortunes were causing social unrest. 
Korea is a cohesive society, and news there of proposals totax the massive 
unrealized gains <https://bloom.bg/4f04N14> made in chips sounded very bad in 
New York. So did Korean regulators’ concern that they shouldn’t have permitted 
trading in single-company ETFs.

BCA Research recommended ending a remarkable trade that has more than doubled 
investors’ money this year. The idea is to go long EM semiconductors and short 
the companies paying for them — the Magnificent Seven hyperscalers — and it’s 
hard to see how it can carry on much longer. BCA notes that theKospi index 
<https://bloom.bg/3QBSiQ6>’s one-month implied volatility has surpassed 
previous peaks that historically “coincided with equity bear market troughs, 
not all-time highs,” and takes this as a signal of a top driven by a “magnified 
expression of… speculative forces”:

Other reasons for concern: McDonald points out that we’re approaching the end 
of a month, a quarter, and a long US holiday weekend — a combination that lends 
itself to big switches and summer swoons. The scale of the IPOs now underway 
will be hard for the market to process, and implies that insiders think the top 
is in. 

Evasive action doesn’t necessarily mean getting out of the market altogether 
and hoping to get the timing right. In 2000 as the dot-coms imploded, 
industrials and value stocks logged gains. This time around, McDonald points 
out that the Vanguard Industrials ETF, whose biggest holdings are Caterpillar 
Inc. and General Electric Co., has slightly outperformed the Magnificents over 
the last 12 months:

A few deliberate steps to limit exposure to chips probably make sense, 
particularly now that Micron has provided a good opportunity to sell high.
