• OPEC+ agreed to another modest production quota increase — ratifying the return of more than 900,000 barrels per day since the war began — but faces a potential oversupply problem as Middle East exports recover faster than Asian buyers require; WTI fell to $68.30.

**Post-War Monetary Policy Shock**
Bloomberg Economics' assessment constitutes the clearest quantification to date of the Iran war's lasting macro footprint. The Federal Reserve — previously expected to cut rates by a full percentage point by mid-2027 — is now projected to deliver only a single 25-basis-point reduction over that horizon. The ECB is anticipated to hike to a level half a point above its prior trajectory before subsequently easing. Bloomberg Economics' chief global economist attributes central banks' hawkish persistence to post-pandemic institutional scarring: having been embarrassed by transitory inflation misjudgments, policymakers are reluctant to ease prematurely even as oil prices recede from war-driven peaks. The implication is that the energy shock's second-order effect — tighter financial conditions — will compound its direct cost-of-living impact for households and businesses through at least 2028.

**Oil Market Structure**
OPEC+'s internal cohesion has deteriorated materially. The UAE's departure in May and Iraq's signaled willingness to follow have left Saudi Arabia increasingly isolated as the residual swing producer. The return of suppressed capacity — particularly from the UAE, which has significant unused production headroom — threatens to overwhelm demand recovery if Riyadh cannot coordinate credible restraint across a fractured membership. Forecasters already see production outpacing consumption in parts of Asia. Whether Saudi Arabia will accept a disproportionate burden-sharing arrangement to stabilize prices remains, at this juncture, an open question.
