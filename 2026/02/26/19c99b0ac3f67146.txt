 Bull case for Bitcoin 



The pessimism around Bitcoin these days seems unrelenting, as we noted in 
yesterday’s newsletter. Yet theinstitutional scaffolding is still standing 
<https://bloom.bg/4kYFFcr>.

In 2022, the infrastructure collapsed alongside the price. FTX, Celsius, 
BlockFi, Three Arrows — were all dominoes. This time, the exchanges are 
running, the custodians are solvent and a slew of US banks are building crypto 
products.

The ETF outflows that dominated headlines? Most assets have stuck around. 
Institutional buyers, except perhaps hedge funds, are hanging in.Circle’s 
bullish earnings <https://bloom.bg/4u1EL3e> sharpened the point that the crypto 
moment is real.

Put another way, for all the negative vibes out there, the buying capacity of 
long-term holders has grown over the past year, thanks to big improvements in 
the market plumbing.



Bitcoin’s freely tradable supply has also shrunk. That changes the physics of 
a potential recovery: If sentiment turns, buying pressure travels through a 
narrower pipe. And narrower pipes can produce more force.

“The current Bitcoin price action is a mere crisis of confidence. Nothing 
broke, no skeletons will show up,” wrote Gautam Chhugani, senior analyst of 
global digital assets at Bernstein. “The Bitcoin bear case is the weakest in 
its history.” He expects the token to reach $150,000 this year. —Sid Verma 
<https://bloom.bg/47tPKrD> with Vildana Hajric <https://bloom.bg/3DSeO0i> and 
Isabelle Lee <https://bloom.bg/4gRZU8q>

 Hedging a drop 



With the S&P 500 stuck in a range for the better part of four months, a 
sentiment measure in the options market is showing so much pessimism 
<https://bloom.bg/4cPGwdr> towards US stocks that some strategists see a reason 
to buy.

The put-call skew, which measures the cost of buying downside protection 
compared to placing upside bets, jumped to a two-year high last week. 
Normalized two-month skew on the S&P 500 is now near the upper end of its 
five-year range.

Generally, when sentiment moves so far in one direction, strategists start to 
sense a turn the other way. —Bernard Goyder <https://bloom.bg/3YnYyeR>
