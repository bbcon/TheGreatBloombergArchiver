Japan’s exports grew at a slower pace in February, as tariffs weighed on car 
shipments to the US and as demand in China slumped due to the Lunar New Year 
holidays.

Exports to the US fell 8% in February from a year earlier, the Finance 
Ministry reported Wednesday, led by a 14.8% retreat in car shipments by value. 
On a unit basis, exports of cars fell at a slower pace, indicating that 
Japanese manufacturers continue to protect market share in the US with price 
cuts as they contend with tariffs. (Click here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/japan-s-takaichi-visits-trump-as-hormuz-warship-standoff-simmers?cmpid=BBD031826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 for the full story fromYoshiaki Nohara 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/16419149>.)

The data release comes as Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi faces a diplomatic 
balancing act at a summit this week with Donald Trump in Washington, as 
Japanese companies cope with the fallout from US tariffs — and the widening 
conflict in the Mideast.

“The war in Iran, its impact on Japan’s economy and US demands for allied 
military support are likely to dominate,” said Bloomberg Economics’ Adam Farrar 
and Taro Kimura.

“Pressure will also be high for Japan to show it is delivering on its $550 
billion investment commitment,” Farrar and Kimurawrote Tuesday <>.

The US and Japan are in the midst of implementing last year’s trade deal that 
capped levies on US imports of Japanese goods at 15% in exchange for a pledge 
from Tokyo to boost investment in the US to help revitalize American 
manufacturing.

Last month Tokyo announced the first tranche of investments, saying it would 
channel up to a combined $36 billion into US oil, gas, and critical mineral 
projects. A second batch of projects may include next generation nuclear power 
projects, according to local media reports.

Meanwhile, exports to China declined 10.9% in February, with drops in 
semiconductor manufacturing equipment, plastics and scientific optical 
equipment driving that decline even as shipments of semiconductors and other 
electronic components continued to surge on AI-related demand.

The value of overall exports rose 4.2% in February from a year earlier, after 
a big jump in the previous month. The result beat the median analyst forecast 
of a 1.9% rise. Imports rose 10.2%, a little below the consensus estimate, as 
the trade balance swung to a surplus of ¥57.3 billion on an unadjusted basis.

“The decline in exports to China was due to the Lunar New Year,” said 
Shinichiro Kobayashi, chief economist at Mitsubishi UFJ Research and 
Consulting. “Still, overall exports grew because the global economy remained 
fundamentally sound despite some signs of slowing down in the US.”

 | Demand is disappointing, and inventories are piling up, even as factories 
enter the busy period that follows the Lunar New Year. Stockpiles of primary 
aluminum have shot above 1.3 million tons, the highest since 2020. The 
lackluster performance of a widely used industrial material could be a warning 
sign for China’s broader economy, which rebounded in the first two months of 
the year but now faces the fallout from higher energy costs and other 
disruptions due to the conflict in the Middle East.
