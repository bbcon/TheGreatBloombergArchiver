# Trade War: Key Developments & Outlook
**Prepared:** 2026-05-27
**Sources:** Monthly summaries (Mar–Apr 2026), Weekly summaries (W18–W21), Raw Bloomberg emails

---

## 1. IEEPA Struck Down — What Replaced It

**The ruling:** The Supreme Court invalidated Trump's IEEPA-based tariff architecture, removing the legal basis for the broad tariff regime. The Court of International Trade subsequently ordered $160–166 billion in refunds to importers, and a federal trade court in early May separately declared the administration's residual 10% global tariff unlawful. The administration is appealing and has sought a court-ordered pause pending that appeal.

**The replacement:** On the first Tuesday of March, the White House imposed a **15% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974** — a balance-of-payments provision. The legal footing is widely questioned. Section 122 requires a "fundamental international payments problem" (dollar weakness, capital flight); former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath said plainly the US does not meet that threshold. Critically, **Section 122 has a statutory 150-day limit**, placing the expiry around **late July 2026**. Treasury Secretary Bessent has publicly stated tariff rates may be restored to pre-Supreme Court levels by July, suggesting the administration intends to find new legal vehicles before Section 122 lapses.

**Fiscal gap:** The CFRB estimates the IEEPA invalidation reduces projected federal revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036; Section 122 recovers only ~$925 billion — a $775 billion hole driving urgency to plug the legal gap.

**What's next:** The administration filed fresh **Section 301 investigations** against 12+ economies — China, EU, Mexico, India, Taiwan — with a public hearing set for May 5. Section 232-style investigations are also queued to replace struck-down levies. These are the likely successors to both IEEPA and Section 122, with Trump's **July 24 tariff deadline** serving as the next hard marker. A new round of duties this summer is widely expected to collide with gasoline near $4.50 and midterm affordability concerns.

## 2. US–China: Beijing Summit and the Trade Truce

**The trade truce** agreed in Geneva (pre-March) reduced average US levies on Chinese goods from ~32% to ~24% (Morgan Stanley). That truce has a **September 2026 expiry date** — the next hard cliff in the bilateral timeline.

**Trump-Xi Beijing Summit (May 14–15)** was the first US presidential visit to China since 2017. The concrete output was thin:
- China renewed import licenses for hundreds of US beef processing plants
- Commitment to **$17 billion in annual US agricultural purchases through 2028**
- A "trade investment board" covering roughly **$30 billion in non-critical flows**, with potential for limited Chinese capital access to non-sensitive US industries

The summit is increasingly characterized as a **"placeholder" event** — cooperative optics with limited operational substance. Xi warned that mishandling Taiwan could push the relationship into a "highly dangerous situation" while the two leaders were still in the room. Trump subsequently confirmed China has not approved Nvidia H200 chip purchases and intends to develop domestic alternatives.

**China's structural position:** Beijing's rare earth dominance — underpinning ~$1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity — and its new supply chain coercion regulations remain the asymmetric leverage cards in Beijing's hand. China's April exports rose 14% YoY (vs. 8% consensus) and its trade surplus hit a record $214 billion, meaning the structural imbalance driving the conflict is widening, not narrowing.

## 3. US–EU: July 4 Deadline and the Trade Deal Text

The EU was given until **July 4** to ratify a trade deal or face auto tariffs rising to 25%. Negotiators **finalized the deal text** in the week of May 19:
- EU export tariffs capped at **15%**
- In exchange: removal of EU duties on US industrial goods
- 25% auto tariff threat averted for now

This is near-term relief but formalizes a materially worse arrangement than the pre-tariff baseline — eurozone exports to the US were running 37% below year-ago levels in March. The deal requires formal ratification. Eurozone March shipments to the US were down 37% YoY, illustrating the stakes.

---

## 4. EU–China: A Separate but Interconnected Front

The EU-China relationship is under separate strain. China's clean tech export surge — EVs up 112% YoY in April, solar panels doubling month-on-month — is hardening European industrial defensiveness. Germany's machinery association described China's five-year plan as generating "unprecedented corporate anxiety" over competitiveness. The EU is weighing additional defensive measures ahead of a **June EU-China leaders' summit**, with the Nuctech subsidy investigation as a likely flashpoint. Beijing has warned of retaliation.

---

## 5. Outlook

The structural picture is one of **tariff regime instability rather than resolution**:

| Date | Event |
|------|-------|
| Late July 2026 | Section 122 statutory 150-day limit expires |
| July 4 2026 | US-EU deal ratification deadline |
| July 24 2026 | Trump tariff deadline (Section 301/232 successors) |
| September 2026 | US-China Geneva truce expiry |
| June 2026 | EU-China leaders' summit (Nuctech flashpoint) |

**US-China:** September truce expiry is the next hard date. The Beijing summit bought goodwill but resolved nothing structural. Fresh Section 232/301 duties targeting China in the July–September window are a live risk.

**US-EU:** The July 4 deal text averts the immediate escalation, but ratification is incomplete and the new 15% baseline is permanently worse than the pre-tariff regime.

**EU-China:** June summit is a flashpoint. Beijing's coercion toolkit has expanded and its clean tech export surge is accelerating European defensiveness.

**Wild card:** Hormuz resolution could lower oil prices, ease the inflation constraint, but also reduce the political cost of tariff escalation for an administration entering midterms.
