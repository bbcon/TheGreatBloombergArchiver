US markets experienced modest volatility on March 17 as renewed Iranian strikes on Gulf energy infrastructure pushed Brent above $100 a barrel, though the S&P 500 managed a 1.01% gain in the prior session before S&P 500 futures slipped 0.2% in early trading. The more consequential story remains the Federal Reserve's policy calculus heading into this week's FOMC meeting — one that is unusually complicated by the confluence of an oil-driven supply shock, a weakening labor market, and an imminent leadership transition.

The bond market's reaction has been notable for its restraint. Five-year/five-year inflation breakevens have barely moved despite a roughly 46% surge in US gasoline futures since hostilities began on February 28, and two-year real rates are declining rather than rising. Markets appear to be treating the oil shock as a growth headwind rather than an inflation catalyst — consistent with historical central bank practice of looking through energy price moves until they feed into core inflation. Fed funds futures have nonetheless repriced, with expectations for two 25-basis-point cuts this year falling to one, reflecting the deteriorating growth outlook rather than inflation fears.

The political backdrop adds further complexity. February payrolls unexpectedly declined, reinforcing the dovish case. Fed chair nominee Kevin Warsh has signaled a preference for easing, and President Trump — watching gasoline prices rise toward the November midterms — has strong political incentives to push for cuts. Jerome Powell chairs only one more FOMC meeting after this week. The Federal Reserve is also expected to launch revised bank capital proposals that would free approximately $200 billion in capital for the largest lenders, a potentially stimulative move that carries its own overheating risks at an uncertain moment. US diesel topping $5 a gallon for the first time since December 2022 adds to consumer price pressures that complicate any simple policy narrative.

Nvidia's annual developer conference provided a constructive counterpoint: CEO Jensen Huang projected that the company's AI processors would help generate $1 trillion in chip sales through 2027, though his commentary favoring copper interconnects over fiber optics weighed on photonics names in premarket trading.
