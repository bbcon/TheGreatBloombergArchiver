# Global Daily Briefing — May 22, 2026

**Iran-US Standoff Keeps Energy Markets on Edge**

Iran said the latest US proposal "partly bridged the gap" in negotiations, but the diplomatic opening was immediately complicated by Supreme Leader comments insisting Tehran retain its uranium stockpile, and by a dispute over a proposed Iran-Oman permanent toll regime in the Strait of Hormuz. President Trump reiterated threats of renewed strikes if talks collapse. Brent rose 2.6% to above $105 a barrel, with WTI near $98, reflecting two-way risk pricing rather than directional conviction. Rapidan Energy warned that if Hormuz disruptions persist through August, Brent could approach $130, with macroeconomic consequences comparable to 2008. The conflict has also reportedly cost US forces more than two dozen MQ-9 Reaper drones — close to $1 billion in equipment — underscoring that escalation risk remains material.

**Inflation Risk Reprices the G7 Bond Complex**

Long-dated yields across the $50 trillion-plus G7 sovereign market reached two-decade highs this week as investors demanded greater compensation for inflation persistence. Memories of the post-Covid "transitory" misjudgment are shaping central bank reaction functions: the RBA has already delivered three hikes this year, and ECB officials including Lagarde have signaled vigilance, with at least one policymaker leaning toward a hike. TS Lombard's Dario Perkins characterized the environment as potentially heralding "rate hikes everywhere," with 2% targets increasingly functioning as a floor rather than a ceiling. Pimco's Daniel Ivascyn cautioned that prior energy shocks have historically culminated in recession — a tension worth monitoring as tightening bias collides with demand destruction risk.

**Divergence and Second-Order Effects**

Japan provides a counterpoint: core inflation slowed to a four-year low, complicating the BOJ's tightening path even as PM Takaichi announced a ~$19 billion stimulus package with revived energy subsidies. India's RBI is reportedly revisiting its 2013 taper-tantrum toolkit as the rupee nears 97/USD. The shock is rippling unevenly through real economies — Southeast Asian fishing fleets are idling on diesel costs, while European governments from the UK to Poland eye windfall taxes on oil majors' trading profits, a move that may further erode regional upstream investment.

**Rare Earths: A Slower-Moving Constraint**

China's dominance over rare earth separation and magnet production — underpinning roughly $1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity — appears likely to persist well into the next decade despite roughly $10 billion in global public funding for alternatives this year. The asymmetry will continue to shape US-China engagement ahead of the September trade truce expiry.
