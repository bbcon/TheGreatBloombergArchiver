Well-Earned

Is the stock market right to “look past” the conflict in Iran? It’s a popular 
question with Brent crude back to $108 per barrel, and there’s ample reason to 
fear that the dangers of the blockade of theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://bloom.bg/4cLxdcR> have been understated. But there’s an equal and 
opposite question: Would the market be right to look past a growth in corporate 
earnings that is beginning to look historic?

According to Societe Generale SA’s chief quantitative strategist Andrew 
Lapthorne, “12‑month forward profits are now around $600 billion higher than 
they were just four months ago — a 12% increase, and the largest upward 
revision on record outside of a post‑recession recovery.”

War in the Middle East over the last two months did nothing to stop this. With 
first-quarter earnings <https://bloom.bg/4eN6jUC> now in from a little over a 
third of the companies in the S&P 500, the fallout from the conflict, which 
broke out two-thirds of the way through the quarter, has barely been visible. 
It’s hard to chalk this up to complacency, because analysis of earnings-call 
transcripts shows mentions of the war have surged this quarter:

For business managers, there’s a sense that, as Boeing’s Kelly Ortberg 
recently told investors, this too shall pass. Regarding aviation, he argues 
that there have been moments like this before, whether a recession, pandemic, 
or conflict. “The resilience of our industry has always led to a recovery and 
return to growth trends,” he adds. Plenty of executives share that confidence 
in their ability to withstand the fallout, and the market seems to agree.

Wednesday afternoon will be a critical moment, when four hyperscalers announce 
their results, but to date, both the share of companies beating estimates and 
the magnitude of those surprises are running above historical averages.

As John Butters of FactSet notes, more than 80% of companies have topped 
estimates, compared to the average of roughly 77%. In aggregate, earnings are 
about 12% above expectations, well ahead of the historical average of around 
7%. Of course, investor relations departments manipulate expectations to 
generate surprises, but that’s their job. When companies deliver a particularly 
strong rate of surprise, that will help the market.

The boom in profits is very much centered on technology and the unprecedented 
AI data-center buildout. Other sectors are playing catch-up. As this SocGen 
chart shows, the rise is profits is concentrated almost entirely in the prime 
beneficiaries of thiscapital intensity <https://bloom.bg/4e9rSOY>:

Evidently, this helps justify the near-hyperbolic rise of the Philadelphia 
Stock Exchange Semiconductors index <https://bloom.bg/4cPs2sC>, up by almost 
50% in April alone. But the picture becomes less reassuring once you unpack it 
to look at concentration risk. As Lapthorne notes, about 43% of the increase in 
2026 consensus profit comes from semiconductors alone. Add IT hardware and 
energy, he says, “and close to 98% of this year’s extraordinary profit upgrade 
is explained.”

This cannot continue indefinitely. When the momentum ends will depend on the 
forces still driving the rally, though gauging how much fuel remains is a 
challenge. Margins could be critical. At present, there’s a belief that overall 
S&P margins, already in record territory, can continue to increase, as this 
chart from Peter Berezin of BCA Research illustrates:

Doug Peta, also of BCA Research, points out that the US economy’s natural 
tendency is to expand, and so corporate earnings’ natural tendency is to grow. 
That said, it’s not common for profit margins to widen as impressively as they 
have lately. Over time, they’ve tended to revert to a norm; that tendency 
appears to have been suspended, and the latest earnings forecasts suggest that 
margins can keep growing:

As long as the multiple that investors are willing to pay for these earnings 
doesn’t decline, Peta argues that equities’ natural tendency is to appreciate. 
But there are limits. Earnings tend to move in cycles, and this one is already 
five years old. Peta demonstrates that projected growth (shown with the dashed 
line) would imply taking it far further than its two predecessors in this 
century:

Current earnings forecasts look very, very stretched, then. But they’re not 
totally unrealistic. As Peta says:

20% earnings growth is a lot to ask of an expansion heading into its seventh 
year. It hasn’t been done before, though a bull (or even a neutral observer) 
could argue that past cycles haven’t had a gale-force AI-capex wind at their 
back.

Party (Gasp) Like It’s 1999???

Comparisons to 1999 must be handled carefully when writing about markets. 
Rather like comparing politicians to Hitler, they can be cheap and exaggerated. 
Just as leaders can be bad without being heinous genocidal lunatics, so markets 
can be overvalued without inflicting anything like the damage caused by the 
crash at the turn of the millennium.

At a more scientific level, there’s quite a range of bubbles in the literature 
now, if you’re prepared to range across assets and geographies, but there are 
very few 1999s. For a market so large and central to the global economy to get 
quite so overextended is very rare. The Great Crash of 1929 might be the only 
other name on the list.

That said, the great melt-up that culminated in the bursting of the dot-com 
bubble <https://bloom.bg/45WmA4H> in March 2000 does have some notable points 
in common. Then as now, stocks already looked pretty expensive after a long 
run. A new technology began to gain critical weight and created huge excitement 
(the internet then, AI now), and the market suffered a brief moment of 
existential dread. In the fall of 1998, the meltdown of theLong-Term Capital 
Management <https://bloom.bg/4cODAfp> hedge fund briefly threatened a disaster 
as bad as the Global Financial Crisis 10 years later, and this spring the US 
and Israel chose to attack Iran in a move that many had long dreaded as a 
catastrophic mistake.

Back then, the Federal Reserve under Alan Greenspan came to the rescue. This 
time, a ceasefire plus President Donald Trump dialing down the rhetoric that he 
was prepared to escalate and wipe out Iranian civilization led to similar 
relief.

The SOX’s outperformance in the last three weeks is on a par with the 
information technology sector’s surge in the wake ofGreenspan’s surprise 
inter-meeting rate cut <https://bloom.bg/42xOul3>. In the lead-up to both 
events, the S&P had been behaving as though it was running out of juice after a 
long expansion.

We know what happened next, when tech stocks melted up and  
<https://bloom.bg/4bP8GVu>partied like it was 1999 <https://bloom.bg/4bP8GVu>, 
but here it is in graphical form:

If there is fear of missing out, it’s reasonable. Value managers who sagely 
sat out the tech bubble a quarter-century ago regretted it. By 2002, their 
clients were definitely better off for it — but most of them had already pulled 
out their money during the bubble. Professional money managers who avoided the 
bubble were punished for it.

More points of similarity: the enthusiasm was about to be tested by IPOs. In 
1998, theglobe.com somehow rose 900% in its first hour of trading, sending the 
bubble into its next and final phase. Over the remainder of this year, 
companies like SpaceX and OpenAI are due to test the market’s enthusiasm.

A look at sentiment shows a sudden swing toward bullishness, but it actually 
had been somewhat subdued since the initial euphoric reaction to Trump’s 
election in 2024. Self-described bulls now exceed bears in the American 
Association of Individual Investors’ weekly survey for the first time in 
months. The big snapback toward optimism is reminiscent of the resurgence of 
animal spirits after LTCM in 1998:

Other factors might also help a 1999-style melt-up. Then as now, there is 
support from the credit market. Concerns about private credit and“cockroaches” 
<https://bloom.bg/3R45tca> are widespread, but have yet to contaminate what 
people will pay for investment-grade or high-yield credit. Spreads in terms of 
the yields they pay compared to similar Treasury bonds are close to their 
tightest since the Global Financial Crisis, despite everything:

The final stage of the madness in 1999 was driven by yet more injections of 
liquidity as the Fed worriedly tried to guard against possible disruption from 
the Y2K computer bug. In hindsight, that looked like a mistake. Outright rate 
cuts into a market that was already surging had a predictable effect. That 
might be something for the Federal Open Market Committee to consider as its 
last meeting under Jerome Powell gets underway.

The sample size is far too small to prove anything. But the experience we do 
have suggests that a melt-up is quite possible. While that’s on, it’s difficult 
to leave the market. It will generate its own momentum.



 Survival Tips 



It’s good to have an excuse to write about Prince, because the 10th 
anniversary of hisuntimely death at the age of 57 <https://bloom.bg/4vUpG4l> 
came last week. It’s very hard to believe that he’s been gone so long. He was 
stunningly productive, launching 39 albums (all reviewed here 
<https://bloom.bg/4cLx9d7>). If you haven’t heard 1999 
<https://bloom.bg/4cSn26u>, you should. He could make a guitar gently weep 
<https://bloom.bg/4s8HoP7>. He could put on a heck of a live show 
<https://bloom.bg/4n0sf0Q>. My favorites that aren’t totally part of the canon 
might beDiamonds and Pearls <https://bloom.bg/42QJ71c> and I Wanna Be Your Lover
 <https://bloom.bg/4eeoFh3> (my wife’s favorite). Candidates for most-Prince 
song ever (there are many) includeCream <https://bloom.bg/4n59OrF> or Head 
<https://bloom.bg/4eeoGS9>. Prince songs he wrote for others include Manic 
Monday <https://bloom.bg/4czNh2G>, Nothing Compares 2U 
<https://bloom.bg/4t1hgFB>, I Feel for You <https://bloom.bg/4mVhSv7>, and 
Sugar Walls <https://bloom.bg/4n59Tvt>. He also wrote songs for Celine Dion 
<https://bloom.bg/4eHWVBG> and Martika <https://bloom.bg/4eM74x5>, and helped 
outKate Bush <https://bloom.bg/4mXi2C3>. And many others. And between “1999” and
When Doves Cry <https://bloom.bg/4cCIayO>, he’s been a godsend when trying to 
put a fun spin on a financial topic more times than I can count.
