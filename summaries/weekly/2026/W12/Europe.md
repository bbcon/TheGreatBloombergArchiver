**Central Banks: A Hawkish Pivot**
The week's most consequential European macro development was the unmistakable shift in central bank posture. The ECB held at 2.0% on Thursday — a rate in place since last June — but the surrounding communication had changed materially. The ECB dropped its description of policy as being in a "good place," and Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel told Bloomberg on Friday that a hike as early as April is conceivable if energy-driven inflation risks materialize. The Bank of England voted unanimously to hold, but Governor Andrew Bailey explicitly noted that the prior expectation of scope to cut has changed, stating the BoE "stands ready to act." Sweden's Riksbank held similarly. The direction of travel across European central banks is now unmistakably toward tighter policy. Bloomberg Economics estimated March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections, and analysts drew explicit parallels to the post-Ukraine 2022 inflation episode. UK gilt volatility subsequently became a focal point for global fixed income investors, compounded by February borrowing data that came in above forecast due to surging debt-interest costs.

**Energy Exposure and Partial Cushions**
Europe entered the crisis with more resilience than during the 2022 energy shock, though that resilience has limits. Rabobank estimated electricity prices would be approximately one-third higher absent the growth in solar and wind capacity added since 2022, with France's nuclear fleet also operating more reliably than during that earlier episode. European natural gas futures had already risen more than 60% since hostilities began before Thursday's jump of as much as 35% following the Ras Laffan strike, pushing benchmark prices beyond double their pre-war levels. Dubai crude contracts for Gulf-loaded oil are up over 80% in March. Evening power price spikes in several countries point to ongoing vulnerability, and analysts warn that seasonal solar support will fade heading into autumn. Germany's domestic response — restricting petrol stations to one price hike per day and tightening antitrust oversight — is a political gesture rather than a market-moving intervention.

**Politics and Defense**
At the EU Brussels summit, leaders called for a moratorium on strikes against energy infrastructure, with Italian Prime Minister Meloni described as particularly animated about the severity of the situation. Trump explicitly warned that the US "will remember" how NATO allies responded to the crisis, adding that insufficient support would be "very bad for the future of NATO." Most European governments — including the UK, Germany, and France — declined to participate militarily, citing both legal constraints and skepticism about escalation. European officials are simultaneously navigating fatigue from US-related crises, and there are nascent signs governments are reconsidering their posture toward China as a more predictable trade partner. Germany's €600 billion defense spending program is generating tangible economic stimulus, with defense suppliers reporting record order books, but Chancellor Merz's broader political standing remains precarious — fewer than a quarter of Germans hold a positive view of him. In the UK, Prime Minister Starmer faces mounting pressure from Labour's left flank ahead of anticipated May local election losses, with Reform UK continuing to lead in national polls despite emerging fragmentation from newer far-right parties backed by Elon Musk.

**Corporate Developments**
UniCredit's $40 billion bid for Commerzbank, announced Monday, would allow the Italian lender to increase its stake beyond the 30% threshold and represents the most significant cross-border European banking consolidation attempt in years. Unilever's exploration of a food division sale — with McCormick among the early bidders at a valuation of approximately €28–31 billion — would represent the largest restructuring in the company's near-century history. HSBC's reported review of approximately 20,000 roles as part of an AI-driven restructuring of its middle and back offices is among the first concrete signals of how the technology may reshape large financial institution workforces at scale. Logitech fell 5.7% in Zurich on a UBS downgrade; HelloFresh dropped 9.6% in Frankfurt on guidance materially below expectations.
