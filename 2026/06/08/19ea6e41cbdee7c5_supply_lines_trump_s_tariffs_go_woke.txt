President Donald Trump has shown few signs in his second term that he cares 
much about the future of the Amazon rainforest. Nor are there any indications 
that ending either corruption overseas or the use of forced labor are White 
House priorities.

Yet in recent weeks all three of those things have become justifications for 
proposed tariffs put forward by the Trump administration. So what should we 
make of that? Are TrumpтАЩs economic wars suddenly embracing a new humanitarian 
and environmentalist agenda? Or, just maybe, are we seeing a clever strategy 
unfolding that may make TrumpтАЩs tariffs more politically durable?

If there is a core argument for TrumpтАЩs trade wars it is that economic 
security is national security. So just about anything can qualify as a target 
for tariffs and other economic weapons, which leads to peculiar demarcations. 
Imported kitchen cabinets and bathroom vanities currently face national 
security tariffs. Semiconductors do not.

Until the Supreme Court in February declared illegal his use of a 1977 law to 
impose tariffs for any reason he choses, Trump had even more latitude. But as 
they rebuild his tariff wall in the wake of that decision, TrumpтАЩs trade team 
has to go through a process that includes investigations and public comment 
periods and requires defensible motives. And the justifications they are 
picking are interesting and curiously un-Trumpian. Perhaps even тАФ in a 
description that would likely make Trump bristle тАФ woke.

It should be said that were another country to level the same charges against 
the US, Washington might have trouble defending its position. Trump last year 
instructed the Justice Department to stop prosecuting cases under the Foreign 
Corrupt Practices Act which bars US companies from bribing foreign officials. 
He has also, of course, called climate change a hoax and pulled the US out of 
international talks, axed financing for wind farms andjust funded

 the first US coal-fired power plant in a generation. And when it comes to 
forced labor thereare reports

The suspicion is, of course, that TrumpтАЩs team is just cynically rolling out 
excuses for tariffs rather than setting out to end corruption, deforestation or 
forced labor.

But there could also be something else afoot. 

For many years Democrats worked hard in Congress to force any US trade 
negotiations to include labor and environmental standards. This was one price 
Democrats demanded back when big trade bills moved through Congress and the 
legislature took its Constitutional prerogative over trade seriously. At the 
time, big trade negotiations depended on Congress delegating its authority to 
presidents on the understanding that legislators could shape the agenda of the 
talks by mandating priorities.

Republicans typically argued at the time that including things like 
environmental standards just polluted talks aimed at greasing the flow of 
commerce between nations. The cost they demanded was strict intellectual 
property protections for pharmaceutical companies and legal mechanisms to 
protect US companies from being targeted by rogue governments or having their 
overseas assets seized amid disputes.

Politically Durable

That all seems quaint now. Congress hasnтАЩt really asserted that authority for 
most of the past decade. The last big trade bill passed through Congress in 
2015. And none of TrumpтАЩs current trade deals, whether they be with the EU or 
China or other economies, are likely ever to require a congressional vote.

What may be clever, though, is the political branding underway with an eye on 
the future.

TrumpтАЩs tariffs are currently unpopular, polls show. Democrats have been 
actively including tariffs in their case for TrumpтАЩs economic mismanagement 
ahead of this NovemberтАЩs midterm elections. But look beyond those votes and to 
a future in which Democrats control either Congress, the White House or both 
and ask yourself: Are Democrats dependent on support from labor unions and 
environmentalists ever likely to eliminate tariffs purporting to be targeting 
forced labor? Or corruption? Or the deforestation of the Amazon?

The consensus among trade lawyers is that TrumpтАЩs use of Section 301 of the 
trade act of 1974 to reconstruct his tariff wall is more legally durable. But 
with the justifications they have chosen so far the Trump administration may 
also be making them more politically durable.

This isnтАЩt without costs, of course. For the rest of the world watching on and 
growing yet more suspicious of Washington, this is yet another reason to 
complain about American hypocrisy and a government that cares more about 
tariffs than it does about the issues it is using as pretexts to impose them.

TrumpтАЩs tariffs may be woke. But few people actually believe he is embracing 
the issues he cites. And thatтАЩs yet another reason for everyone else to be 
suspicious of what the US is up to. Which adds to the mountain of reasons 
already there and potentially will have consequences for American power and 
trust in it for many years to come.
