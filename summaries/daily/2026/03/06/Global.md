The US-Israel military campaign against Iran entered its seventh day with no evident de-escalation, reshaping energy markets, financial flows, and geopolitical alignments simultaneously. Iranian missiles and drones struck at least five countries across the Gulf region, including Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Bahrain, while Israeli airstrikes continued on Tehran. The Strait of Hormuz has effectively ceased functioning as an oil transit corridor — only two commercial vessel transits were recorded in the preceding 24-hour period and no oil shipments passed through at all. Goldman Sachs flagged the plausibility of Brent crossing $100 per barrel if low flows persist for another five weeks; Brent has already rallied approximately 18% on the week, with US diesel futures on track for a weekly gain exceeding 40%. The Trump administration has ruled out deploying Treasury to trade oil futures to suppress prices, at least for now, while the temporary waiver on Indian purchases of Russian crude represents a tactical release valve that simultaneously benefits Moscow's war finances at a moment when sanctions were beginning to bite meaningfully.

The conflict is forcing central banks globally to confront another supply-side inflation shock in what has become a structurally shock-prone decade — Covid, the Russia-Ukraine war, mercantilist trade disruptions, and now a Middle East regional war have each required difficult monetary policy judgments under uncertainty. The IMF's Kristalina Georgieva warned that a sustained 10% increase in energy prices would push global inflation up 40 basis points and shave 0.1-0.2% from growth. Traditional safe-haven assets have behaved anomalously: gold has declined and is trading at a steep discount in Dubai, while Treasuries have also sold off, leaving the dollar as the primary haven vehicle.

Geopolitically, Russia has emerged as the clearest early beneficiary of the conflict. India's return to Russian crude purchasing — with buyers paying premiums for stranded cargoes — replenishes the Kremlin's revenue stream at a critical juncture. The UAE and Gulf financial hubs face reputational and operational pressure as their image as stable commercial anchors is tested. Meanwhile, a new scientific study published in Geophysical Research Letters provides the first statistically significant evidence of an acceleration in global warming, with the planet warming at approximately 0.35°C per decade since 2015, nearly double the average rate between 1970 and 2015 — a finding that adds long-term structural urgency to energy transition debates even as immediate policy attention is consumed by the conflict.
