The Iran conflict, which began with US and Israeli strikes on February 28, continues to reverberate through global energy markets. Oil and gas prices have risen materially since the onset of hostilities, with considerable volatility accompanying each new operational development. Even as the prospect of a Strait of Hormuz reopening provides intermittent relief to markets, prices remain elevated relative to pre-conflict levels, and the transmission to local economies — particularly across Asia — is becoming increasingly visible. The energy-cost surge is already compressing purchasing power in import-dependent economies and complicating the policy calculus for central banks navigating the growth-inflation tradeoff.

The structural implications may prove more durable than the near-term price moves. Bloomberg's David Fickling argues the conflict may reshape Asia's energy systems over decades, potentially accelerating the reorientation of supply chains, LNG contracting, and energy infrastructure investment across the region. How quickly and completely the Strait reopens — and whether any resolution proves stable — remains a central uncertainty for commodity markets.

On the climate and financial innovation front, prediction markets for weather and climate events are gaining traction, with trading volume on short-term weather contracts demonstrating measurable growth. Research by Patrick Brown at Interactive Brokers found that prediction markets outperformed US National Weather Service forecasts on at least some near-term weather events, lending credibility to the commercial application of these instruments. Institutional use cases — including reinsurance companies seeking better medium-term cyclone or El Niño forecasts through bespoke expert markets — represent a more targeted and arguably more defensible deployment of the prediction market framework. Analysts caution, however, that the complexity of longer-horizon climate projections may limit the applicability of popular market-based mechanisms beyond near-term meteorological events.
