**Bond Markets and the Rate Outlook**

The week's defining story in US markets was the continued repricing of the long end. The 30-year Treasury yield reached 5.19% on Tuesday, a level last seen in June 2007, while 10-year yields touched 4.63% on Monday — the highest since February. The drivers were mutually reinforcing: war-driven inflation, with April headline CPI at 3.8% year-on-year, the firmest since 2023; persistent fiscal supply; AI-related capital expenditure; and continued labor market resilience. Citigroup's Jim McCormick flagged 5.5% as the next technical focal point. Barclays' Ajay Rajadhyaksha argued that only weaker macro data was likely to arrest the move absent a Hormuz reopening, and HSBC characterized a 2026 Fed hike as a tail risk that "can't be ruled out." The effect on equity markets was bifurcated: large-cap AI names remained broadly bid, while options activity in the Russell 2000 showed the first meaningful pickup in hedging demand in six weeks. BofA's fund manager survey, released mid-week, showed a record monthly jump in equity allocations and the sharpest cash drawdown since February 2024 — readings strategist Michael Hartnett characterized as near-complete "bull capitulation," with early June flagged as a potential inflection.

**The New Fed Chair**

Kevin Warsh's swearing-in on Friday transferred the chairmanship at an awkward moment. Wednesday's FOMC minutes — Powell's final meeting — showed a committee whose consensus had shifted materially beneath the incoming chair, with a majority signaling some firming may be appropriate if inflation runs persistently above 2%. Futures now price fed funds above 4% next summer, against 2.8% pricing in late February. UBS's Jonathan Pingle suggested Warsh may lean on trimmed-mean inflation and unit labor costs to frame a delay in hikes, and ABN Amro analysts floated the possibility of changes to the SEP cadence and press conference schedule within six to twelve months. Jamie Dimon, speaking to Bloomberg Television during the week, warned rates "could be much higher than they are today." The realistic ceiling on Warsh's dovishness appears to be holding, rather than cutting.

**Corporate Developments**

Nvidia's Wednesday print — 85% revenue growth, 62% gross margins, $78.9 billion in revenue — met but did not exceed elevated expectations, and the stock's after-hours dip illustrated the challenge of sustaining momentum at a Shiller CAPE of 39.6. Hyperscaler capex projections for 2026 and 2027 were revised to $812 billion and $968 billion respectively, up 89% year-to-date. SpaceX's filing, reportedly led by Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley, disclosed a $4.94 billion net loss alongside the revenue figure, a super-voting structure, and performance milestones tied to a Mars colony. NextEra's $67 billion Dominion acquisition — announced Monday — positions the combined entity to capture data-center load growth in Florida and Virginia with battery storage central to the strategy. Google and Blackstone announced a $5 billion initial equity commitment for an AI cloud venture, scaling to roughly $25 billion including leverage, which weighed on CoreWeave, Nebius and Applied Digital. Meta reassigned 7,000 employees to AI roles ahead of broader cuts. Separately, Brookings data showed 82% of Los Angeles County businesses reported negative impacts from ICE enforcement operations, with concentrated damage to small firms a potential early indicator of broader policy-driven disruption.

**Fiscal and Trade**

The EU finalized the text of its US trade deal on Tuesday, capping EU export tariffs at 15% in exchange for removal of EU duties on US industrial goods — averting a threatened rise to 25% on European automobiles ahead of the July 4 deadline. The post-summit US-China framework remains thin: $17 billion in annual agricultural purchases through 2028 is the most concrete commitment, with Section 232-style investigations queued to replace tariffs struck down in February. A fresh round of duties this summer could collide with gasoline prices near $4.50 and affordability concerns ahead of the midterms. Trump reversed course and announced 5,000 additional troops for Poland on Friday, while the Pentagon confirmed a separate permanent reduction of roughly 4,000 troops from Europe announced earlier in the week.
