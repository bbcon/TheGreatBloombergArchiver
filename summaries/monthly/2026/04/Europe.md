**Macro Conditions and the Stagflationary Trap**

Europe's economic position deteriorated visibly across April, converging toward a configuration that several analysts characterized as stagflationary: energy-driven inflation building precisely as growth momentum fades. Euro-area inflation jumped to 2.5% in March — the steepest monthly acceleration since Russia's Ukraine invasion in 2022 — though the reading came in below consensus at 2.6%, and critically, core and services inflation softened relative to February, suggesting the energy pass-through remains the primary driver rather than a broadening of underlying pressure. Preliminary April PMI data showed the eurozone economy unexpectedly contracting for the first time since late 2024, driven by a steep services decline as war-driven energy costs and eroding consumer confidence fed through. Germany's government halved its 2026 growth forecast to 0.5%, the ZEW investor expectations index collapsed to -17.2 in April from -0.5 in March — its lowest level in more than three years and well below consensus — and the Bundesbank estimated only slight first-quarter expansion. The Ifo Institute's business expectations index fell to its lowest since 2023. Germany's leading economic institutes warned that potential growth may approach zero in the medium term. The Ifo president characterized the country's €500 billion infrastructure stimulus fund as potentially the primary buffer preventing outright contraction, a sobering assessment of how quickly the cyclical picture has shifted since Merz took office. TS Lombard raised the risk of a "Trichet 2.0" scenario in which the ECB tightens into a weakening economy.

**Energy Supply Stress**

Europe's energy position deteriorated on multiple fronts simultaneously during the month. Diesel futures for the ICE Gasoil benchmark broke above $200 per barrel in the second week, the highest since 2022, as Hormuz disruptions compressed refined product flows. Tankers that loaded diesel in UK waters diverted cargoes as far as Australia while vessels originally heading toward Europe turned toward Africa as Asian buyers outbid European counterparts. Jet fuel in Europe surged to all-time highs equivalent to over $215 per barrel at one point, and Brent briefly surpassed $126 per barrel late in the month following reports of fresh US military planning against Iran. European natural gas futures spiked nearly 18% on a Monday open in the final week before partially recovering. Compounding the imported shock, Norway emerged from its driest winter in decades, with snow reserves at their lowest in two decades and an estimated 25 terawatt-hour energy deficit equivalent to nearly a fifth of the country's total hydropower output last year. Norwegian electricity exports to the UK fell approximately 50% year-on-year and to Germany by around 40%, while Nordic prices surged to more than four times 2025 levels in parts of the region. UK household energy costs tracked roughly £500 higher for a typical household. The EU Emissions Trading System adjustment proposals, EU resident guidance to work from home and reduce driving, and EDF's €240 million commitment to accelerate heat pump and electric truck adoption together reflect a policy response that is reactive rather than structural.

**Central Banks: Holding Under Pressure**

The ECB and Bank of England held rates at their late-April meetings, in line with consensus, but the decisions represent a holding pattern rather than a settled direction. Lagarde disclosed that policymakers debated an immediate increase but concluded they had "insufficient information" to act — language that implies a narrow path to avoiding a June move if Hormuz disruption persists. Bundesbank President Nagel separately indicated he favors a June hike. At the Bank of England, Chief Economist Pill voted for an immediate increase, and Governor Bailey warned that waiting for second-round effects to materialize before acting would mean responding "too late." Market pricing shifted dramatically across the month: traders moved to price a June hike as the base case for both institutions, with three ECB increases priced for 2026 — a dramatic reversal from February's expectation of unchanged rates. UK gilt markets came under particular strain, with mortgage rates above 5% in March according to RICS data and homebuyer demand falling to its lowest since August 2023. The structural rigidity of the UK — regulated energy pricing and inflation-linked wages — leaves it comparatively exposed relative to euro-area peers. The White House's Kevin Hassett publicly characterized potential ECB or Fed hikes as "policy errors," an unusual intervention in the affairs of independent central banks that drew little immediate policy response.

**Political Developments: Hungary and the UK**

Hungary's Sunday election in the third week of April produced the month's most consequential European political development. Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured a supermajority, ending Viktor Orbán's 16-year tenure and removing a persistent obstruction to EU cohesion — Orbán's Hungary had held up approximately €90 billion in EU support for Ukraine and exercised veto power to fracture sanctions consensus. The forint surged to a four-year high following the result, and German Chancellor Merz moved quickly to press for disbursement of the €90 billion EU loan for Ukraine. Magyar has pledged to reorient Hungary toward Brussels, supports eventual euro adoption, and has signaled a swift transition of power. That said, Hungary's dependence on Russian energy and existing commercial ties to Asian battery manufacturers will constrain the pace of reorientation, and the economy has consistently underperformed regional peers Poland and the Czech Republic. The EU also finalized its €90 billion loan to Ukraine and Europe-wide Russian LNG import ban took effect — poorly timed given current energy market stress, though strategically consistent with the bloc's longer-run posture. Bulgaria's presidential election produced a victory for former President Radev, who opposes Russian sanctions and military aid to Ukraine, adding a complicating element to European political cohesion that partially offsets the Hungarian development. In the UK, Starmer survived a parliamentary vote on the Mandelson vetting controversy but faces local elections in early May where Labour is projected to lose approximately 1,850 seats — a performance that would raise credible questions about his political durability. The Mandelson episode consumed considerable political bandwidth at precisely the moment the government needed to project competence on energy, defense, and diplomatic coordination.

**Corporate Earnings**

European corporate earnings reflected the bifurcation between sectors exposed to energy-cost shock and those riding AI-driven structural demand. ASML raised its full-year sales forecast to €36–40 billion from €34–39 billion, driven by AI-related semiconductor demand, with its CEO noting supply cannot meet demand for the foreseeable future — a constructive signal for European technology manufacturing. UBS reported first-quarter net income of $3.04 billion, beating all estimates with revenue rising nearly 30% on equities and foreign exchange strength. BP reported adjusted net income of $3.2 billion, more than double the year-earlier figure. Adidas gained 8.3% in Frankfurt on strong apparel momentum. SAP gained 7.3% on strong cloud revenue. By contrast, Hermès fell as much as 14% on its worst single day on record after first-quarter sales disappointed, LVMH reported fashion and leather goods sales declining in the first quarter, Alstom fell 36% after withdrawing its free cash flow guidance, and Lufthansa cancelled approximately 20,000 short-haul flights over the next six months in response to the jet fuel surge. S&P cut credit ratings for Belgium and Slovakia and placed Finland on negative watch, reflecting fiscal deterioration partly linked to elevated energy costs. Universal Music surged 11% after Bill Ackman proposed a deal valuing the company at approximately $65 billion. The UniCredit-Commerzbank standoff continued without resolution despite Orcel having accumulated just under 30% of Commerzbank and tabling a €35 billion offer. Greece received an MSCI upgrade to developed-market status effective May 2027.
