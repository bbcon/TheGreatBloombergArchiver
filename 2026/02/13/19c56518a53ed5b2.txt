The growing Jeffrey Epstein scandal among America’s elite has claimed another 
high-profile corporate role and raised questions about who may be the next to 
go.

Goldman Sachs’ top lawyer Kathy Ruemmler is leaving the firm 
<https://bloom.bg/4aqRpQ5> this summer after Department of Justice documents 
showed the extent of her links with the sex offender.

Ruemmler was well-known for her time as a White House counsel in Barack 
Obama’s administration, before returning to private practice at Latham & 
Watkins. After David Solomon rose to the top of Goldman, he installed Ruemmler 
as Goldman’s top lawyer, giving her oversight of how the firm responds to all 
legal and regulatory matters.

Recently released Epstein files show that in the years before Ruemmler became 
the bank’s general counsel she accepted tens of thousands of dollars in gifts 
from Epstein. She sometimes called him “Uncle Jeffrey <https://bloom.bg/4kA8onS>
,” described him as like “another older brother <https://bloom.bg/4ahlkeU>” and 
addressed him in an exchange about sex crimes as “sweetie.”

Despite this, Ruemmler continued to have the support of Solomon in recent 
months,triggering unease <https://bloom.bg/4ah7lWu> among the bank’s top 
executives. In November, the CEO called her “an excellent lawyer” when asked if 
he expected further fallout from her past with Epstein. More recently, a 
Goldman Sachs spokesperson said the bank had done due diligence about her ties 
to Epstein and “was satisfied.”

In a statement, Solomon said that he respects her decision to resign and that 
she would be “missed.”

As Epstein’s connections to senior figures across global politics, academia, 
finance and tech havecome to light <https://bloom.bg/3ZAhuY1>, an increasing 
number are being ensnared, including Jes Staley, a formertop executive 
<https://bloom.bg/4kJDGbV> at JPMorgan Chase and Barclays, Peter Mandelson and 
attorney Brad Karp, who recentlystepped down <https://bloom.bg/4aucXv5> as head 
of elite law firm Paul Weiss. The list is likely to get longer as pressure 
grows for redacted names in emails to be made public.—Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



The jittery mood in financial markets continues as the glittering AI boom 
times morph into the glitchy AI scare trade. Asianshares tumbled 
<https://bloom.bg/4auAexf> and U.S. stock index futures were down, signaling 
further losses after the S&P 500 slumped 1.6% <https://bloom.bg/4kJDDwL> 
overnight. The MSCI All Country World Index is set for its first back-to-back 
weekly losses this year.

At the heart of the reversal is widening concern that the latest AI tools 
released by Google, Anthropic and others are good enough to threaten a broad 
range of companies. Software firms have been particularly hard hit. “Software 
stocks are now trading like banks in 2008,” said Nick Ferres, chief investment 
officer of Vantage Point Asset Management in Singapore.

But fear has  tainted stocks <https://bloom.bg/4kCwiiw> in industries far from 
the realm of tech. Commercial real estate stocks nosedived in the US on 
Thursday as traders worried about risk <https://bloom.bg/4bNxzAZ> to demand for 
office space from higher use of AI tools. Others include private credit 
companies, insurers, wealth managers and logistics firms.

Asia has been relatively unscathed so far, but the region’s stock markets are 
unlikely to be immune from contagion if the Wall Street selloff deepens.

“The level of paranoia is  Category 5 <https://bloom.bg/4qCaW6j>,” said Joseph 
Shaposhnik, portfolio manager at Rainwater Equity. “It’s not something that 
we’ve seen in quite a long period of time.”
From rave to ravine: Tech stocks plunge. Photographer: Michael Nagle/Bloomberg 



In another throwback to a past age, Chinese firms are splurging on overseas 
acquisition sprees <https://bloom.bg/3Mwmvhf> in a way not seen since Beijing 
slammed the door shut a decade ago.

Last month alone, the volume of outbound mergers and acquisitions from Greater 
China approached $12 billion, the most for January since 2017. The shopping 
list includedhigh-profile names <https://bloom.bg/4kFmIeW> such as German 
sports brand Puma and Canadian miner Allied Gold.

Brutal rivalry and slowing business domestically is prompting companies to 
look overseas, aided by Beijing’s blessing to pursue strategic assets and M&A 
in key industries.

“We expect to see more Chinese companies buying assets overseas in markets 
with lower regulatory hurdles and industries that are less sensitive, including 
consumer and retail,” said Nancy Zheng, a partner at Bain in Shanghai.




The Trump administration is said to be working to narrow its broad tariffs on 
steel and aluminum products that companies finddifficult to calculate 
<https://bloom.bg/4kwFqoK> and the European Union wants reined in as part of 
its pending trade deal with the US.

The US Trade Representative’s Office is scrambling to resolve complications 
spawned last year by the Commerce Department’s efforts to rush out President 
Donald Trump’s tariff agenda, a person familiar with the matter said. Trump 
last year imposed a 50% levy on foreign steel and aluminum in a measure aimed 
at Chinese overcapacity. The step wound up hitting other major trading partners 
hard, including Canada, the EU, Mexico and South Korea.




Malaysia is investigating allegations surrounding anti-graft chief Azam Baki, 
days after Bloomberg News reported he had shareholdings that were higher than 
allowed limits <https://bloom.bg/3O6J8JT> for public officials. The cabinet  
has directed <https://bloom.bg/4crJmFk> a special committee to look into the 
matter, Communications Minister Fahmi Fadzil said. Malaysia’s Public Service 
Department said it’s reviewing rules for public officials, including those on 
stock ownership, some of which have been in place for more than 20 years.
Azam Baki, chief commissioner of the Malaysian Anti-Corruption Commission, 
left, and Prime Minister Anwar Ibrahim at an MACC dinner in February 2025.
Photographer: ZULFADHLI ZULKIFLI 



Hong Kong is said to be considering a plan to allow a broader range of 
companies to file for IPOs confidentially, as the city seeks to cement its lead 
among global listing venues.

The proposal would expand the privilege of private filings beyond technology 
<https://bloom.bg/4av7VhU> and biotech to include traditional sectors, 
according to people familiar with the matter. The change is expected to be part 
of a wide-ranging market consultation paper to be published by Hong Kong 
Exchanges as soon as the end of this month.




The US and Taiwan finalized a trade agreement to cut tariffs, boost market 
access for American products in Asia and channel billions of dollars into US 
energy and technology projects.

Under the terms of the deal, Taiwan pledged to purchase 
<https://bloom.bg/4kwRhDf> more than $44 billion worth of liquefied natural gas 
and crude oil from the US and open its market further to American goods, 
including meat products, dairy, wheat, medical products and automobiles. Taipei 
also committed to buying around $15 billion in American civil aircraft and 
parts and invest roughly $25 billion in power-generation equipment by 2029.




Sanrio, the Japanese company behind Hello Kitty, surged by its daily limit 
after postingstronger-than-expected <https://bloom.bg/4qCb063> earnings and 
lifting full-year guidance. The performance exceeded the company’s plan as 
popularity climbed further for characters including “Kuromi” and “My Melody,” 
Sanrio said. The stock rallied 15%, the most since August.
