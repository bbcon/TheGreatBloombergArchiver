Daily Brief

Wednesday, July 15, 2026

US

  • US CPI fell in June — headline down month-on-month for the first time in six years, with core CPI flat, surprising nearly all economists and coming in well below consensus expectations.
  • Fed Chair Warsh declined to declare "mission accomplished" in Congressional testimony, signaling inflation remains too elevated despite the benign print.
  • Major Wall Street banks posted record or near-record trading revenues in Q2, with JPMorgan and Goldman Sachs leading; IBM suffered its largest single-day decline in history, falling more than 25%.

Inflation

June CPI delivered the most broadly encouraging inflation print in years. Headline prices fell month-on-month for the first time since 2020, while core CPI — excluding food and energy — was flat, a reading described by Oxford Economics as reflecting the absence of all three forces the Fed was most alert to: tariff pass-through, AI-driven pricing, and oil. The Fed's supercore services measure declined, the Atlanta Fed sticky-price gauge fell to its lowest since the pandemic, and the Cleveland Fed trimmed mean dipped below 3%. Labor-intensive services linked to immigrant workers remained an exception, with home healthcare costs up 10.7% year-on-year.

Monetary Policy

Despite the encouraging data, Fed Chair Warsh used his House Financial Services Committee testimony to push back firmly against premature optimism. He declined to signal rate cuts and notably framed a "family fight" among policymakers over the "extent and timing" of any future tightening — his closest approach yet to acknowledging a rate hike remains on the table. Futures pricing for a July hike collapsed following the CPI release, marking the second-largest post-CPI repricing of Fed meeting odds since 2008. Capital Economics maintained that the AI investment boom and rebounding consumer demand will keep core inflation above target, and that a rate hike remains a question of when, not if.

Markets and Earnings

The S&P 500 gained 0.38%, a muted response reflecting residual uncertainty over oil and the Hormuz situation. Q2 earnings season opened strongly: JPMorgan reported its highest-ever quarterly profit, while Goldman Sachs posted a record $7.42 billion in quarterly trading revenue. IBM fell more than 25% — the largest single-day decline in its history — after disappointing results attributed to customers redirecting spending toward chips and servers. ---

Asia

  • China's Q2 GDP grew 4.3% year-on-year, the slowest pace since at least 1992 outside the pandemic, missing consensus and prompting Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, and ANZ to cut full-year forecasts.
  • South Korea's Kospi surged 6.2% on Wednesday after the soft US CPI print, but remains the worst-performing major index globally this month following a 25% peak-to-trough decline.
  • SK Hynix's US-listed ADRs trade at a 51% premium to Seoul shares, illustrating dangerous structural distortions driven by leveraged ETFs and margin concentration.

China: Growth Slowdown

China's second-quarter GDP expansion of 4.3% year-on-year represented the weakest reading in more than three years and came in below consensus. Property investment fell 18% in the first half from a year earlier — its steepest decline on record — while fixed-asset investment contracted 5.7%, worsening from the 4.1% decline recorded through May. Moody's Analytics described domestic demand as "the economy's weakest link," and Bloomberg Economics argued that higher oil prices from the US-Iran conflict represent only a "ripple" for China given its lower import dependency and currency stability, leaving deflation as a structural domestic demand problem requiring a domestic demand cure.

South Korea: Volatility and Leverage Risks

Korean President Lee Jae Myung acknowledged that the market was "quite unstable" following a historically rapid surge that itself created the conditions for a sharp correction. Leveraged ETFs tracking SK Hynix and Samsung have been identified as amplifiers of volatility, with regulators directed to prepare follow-up measures. Smart money outflows preceded the retail-driven collapse: tech funds recorded $9.3 billion in weekly outflows through late June, and BlackRock's iShares MSCI South Korea ETF suffered a record $2.7 billion monthly outflow in May. The ADR premium for SK Hynix — at 51% above the Seoul-listed price — reflects structural impediments to arbitrage rather than fundamental divergence. ---

Europe

  • ASML raised its full-year net sales guidance for the second time in 2026, now targeting €43–45 billion versus analyst consensus of €39.3 billion, driven by accelerating AI-related chip equipment demand.
  • Incoming UK Prime Minister Andy Burnham's transition team is publicly divided over the Chancellor appointment, signaling early political fragility before he takes office.

ASML and Industrial Outlook

ASML's guidance upgrade — approximately 12% above the prior analyst consensus midpoint — provided the clearest market signal yet that AI-driven semiconductor capital expenditure is running well ahead of earlier projections. CEO Christophe Fouquet noted customers were not only increasing capex but accelerating all their plans, a distinction that implies demand durability rather than a pull-forward effect. Intel's adoption of ASML's most advanced lithography equipment for volume production was cited as evidence of broader commercial readiness. Shares rose 4% in Amsterdam, with Applied Materials and Lam Research also gaining in early US trading.

UK Political Transition

Burnham's advisers are split on whether Energy Secretary Ed Miliband should be appointed Chancellor, with left-wing supporters favouring the appointment and others strongly opposed. The OECD and Bank of England Governor both called for fiscal discipline on the same day, framing the Chancellor question as consequential for sovereign credibility. Germany's Rhine River water levels have fallen to their lowest for the time of year since 1990, threatening to disrupt manufacturing supply chains in southern Germany and Switzerland at a moment when Europe's largest economy remains fragile. ---

LatAm

  • Chile is flirting with recession despite having elected a pro-market president to revive growth, as regional competition for investment intensifies amid a rightward political shift across Latin America.

Chile: Growth Reality Check

Chileans who voted for a market-oriented administration to accelerate economic recovery are confronting a more difficult reality. Growth has stalled sufficiently to raise recession concerns in one of Latin America's historically most stable economies, while the broader regional shift toward pro-market governments has increased competition for foreign direct investment. The dynamic illustrates a recurring tension in the region: structural reform agendas that are credible in design can still be undermined by adverse external conditions, including elevated global rates, softer commodity demand, and geopolitical uncertainty constraining capital flows.

Brazil-US Trade

Brazil's government held its fifth high-level meeting with US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer, reflecting sustained diplomatic engagement as both sides navigate the tariff environment. No specific outcomes were disclosed. The frequency of contact suggests Brazil is actively seeking to differentiate its trade relationship with Washington, though the broader US tariff architecture continues to create uncertainty for Brazilian exporters. ---

Global

  • Trump abandoned the proposed 20% Hormuz cargo fee within 24 hours under Gulf state pressure, replacing it with a pledge to seek trade and investment deals; the US simultaneously resumed its naval blockade of Iranian shipping.
  • Brent crude traded above $86 per barrel, having surged 11% over two sessions, as Trump threatened additional strikes on Iranian power plants and bridges.
  • China's Q2 GDP miss, the Hormuz disruption, and AI-driven capex represent three simultaneous structural forces reshaping global trade, energy, and monetary policy simultaneously.

Strait of Hormuz

The US completed a seven-hour wave of strikes against targets near the strait and along Iran's coast immediately before resuming its naval blockade. Only a handful of vessels transited the waterway on Wednesday, including a US-sanctioned Iranian oil tanker and several commercial carriers. The IMO Secretary General described the passage as remaining unsafe. Two Iranian tankers were signaling Pakistan as their destination, suggesting they were seeking holding positions. Asian refiners are accelerating purchases of US crude as Persian Gulf supply becomes effectively inaccessible near-term; South Korea is absorbing record volumes of Canadian crude, a substitution that analysts say may outlast the conflict itself.

Energy and Monetary Policy Divergence

The tension between the June US CPI print — which offered temporary relief on the inflation outlook — and resurging oil prices creates an analytically uncomfortable juxtaposition. One month of benign data is unlikely to be sustained if Brent remains above $85. US natural gas, meanwhile, is decoupling from heat-driven power demand as solar and battery storage account for 91% of new US generation installed in Q1, with gas now supplying less than 40% of US power — four to five percentage points below a year ago. Globally, the Ebola outbreak in the DRC is accelerating, with the WHO warning true case counts may be two to four times the official figure.
