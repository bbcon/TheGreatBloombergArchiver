Weekly Brief

May 04 – May 10, 2026

US

Equity Market Resilience

US equity markets sustained record highs through much of the week, with the S&P 500 approaching 7,400 by Wednesday despite an increasingly bifurcated underlying economy. The advance remains narrowly concentrated: factor analysis reveals that quality stocks have persistently underperformed since Liberation Day, while momentum and liquidity factors continue to dominate. Goldman Sachs strategists flagged "yellow flags" around the thinness of the rally, and the composition — AMD surging roughly 17% on raised guidance, Super Micro gaining 19%, Pinterest beating expectations, while Cloudflare fell as much as 17% on weak guidance and Trade Desk sank 15% — illustrates that selective execution rather than broad re-rating is driving the index higher.

AI Capital Expenditure Cycle

The AI investment thesis remains the primary structural support. First-quarter data show real spending on information-processing equipment rising 64% and software up 46%, even as all other capex declined 17%. Alphabet raised nearly $17 billion in debt on Tuesday to fund data center buildout, and S&P 500 companies announced $665 billion in authorized buybacks in the first four months of the year — the highest pace on record — providing a structural floor beneath indices. Nvidia's 9% decline over six sessions and its status as the worst performer in the Philadelphia semiconductor index year-to-date suggests competitive chip architectures from Alphabet, Amazon, and Intel are registering in investor sentiment, even if market share loss remains limited in practice.

K-Shaped Consumption and the Energy Tax

The energy shock's asymmetric incidence is becoming well-documented. US gasoline prices topped $4.50 per gallon for the first time since July 2022 by Wednesday. New York Fed research shows households earning under $40,000 reduced real gasoline consumption 7% even as nominal spending rose 12%, while higher-income cohorts barely adjusted. Kraft Heinz's CEO warned that lower-income shoppers are "literally running out of money at the end of the month." Spirit Airlines' cessation of operations offered a pointed illustration of the shock's asymmetric impact on businesses serving price-sensitive consumers. Pantheon Macro estimates the energy shock may translate into roughly a 1% annualized decline in real after-tax income in Q2, with Bank of America characterizing the full-year "gas tax" at approximately 0.5% of GDP.

Monetary Policy and Fiscal Tensions

The Federal Reserve left rates unchanged, with internal divisions deepening. Two-year inflation swaps crossed 3% — their highest since 2022 — and 30-year Treasury yields briefly breached 5% on Thursday. Fed Governor Collins aligned herself with recent dissenting easing language, suggesting the FOMC has not resolved its internal debate. The self-reinforcing dynamic between the administration's military posture in the Gulf and its publicly stated desire for lower interest rates represents a material policy inconsistency. On fiscal matters, the CBO projects federal debt reaching 108% of GDP by 2030. Jeffrey Gundlach disclosed he is repositioning for an extreme restructuring scenario, joining Apollo's Marc Rowan and Bridgewater's Ray Dalio in unusually direct warnings, though five-year CDS-implied default probabilities remain below 1%. A federal trade court additionally declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful, and the administration began processing refunds for $166 billion in previously vacated tariff collections.

Corporate Developments

GameStop proposed a $56 billion all-cash-and-stock acquisition of eBay on Monday, offering a roughly 20% premium to Friday's close — a striking bid from a company with a $12 billion market capitalization. Amazon's launch of Amazon Supply Chain Services, packaging its freight, trucking, air, ocean, and last-mile capabilities into a single commercial offering, weighed on FedEx, UPS, and Expeditors, though Bloomberg Intelligence analysts characterize the near-term competitive threat as incremental. The April nonfarm payrolls report, expected Friday to show approximately 62,000–65,000 jobs added, is the week's remaining data event of consequence. ---

Asia

Semiconductor Rally and the AI Super-Cycle

Asian equity markets delivered one of their more striking weekly performances. South Korea's Kospi surged more than 14% on the week, with Samsung Electronics rallying 14–16% on Wednesday to cross a $1 trillion market capitalization — only the second Asian company to achieve that threshold. SK Hynix rose 30% over the week. Goldman Sachs raised its Kospi target for the fourth time this year, projecting an additional 20% gain and citing forecast earnings growth of 300% for 2026 — characterizing it as the strongest such figure for any Asian market since the 1999 post-crisis recovery. Taiwan's benchmark gained approximately 7% and Japan's Nikkei 225 rose 5.8% to a record high, recovering sharply from Monday's modest pullback as Strait of Hormuz diplomacy shifted sentiment. Morgan Stanley's chief Asia economist Chetan Ahya framed the broader dynamic as the beginning of a multi-year industrial super-cycle driven by AI infrastructure, energy transition, and defense spending.

China: Sanctions Defiance and Financial Maneuvering

Beijing's posture on US secondary sanctions evolved in nuanced and consequential ways across the week. The week opened with Beijing ordering private companies to disregard US secondary sanctions — a deliberate departure from its historically cautious approach of quiet compliance — following Washington's sanctioning of Hengli Petrochemical over Iranian crude purchases. By Thursday, however, China's largest banks had been quietly directed to halt new lending to five recently sanctioned refiners, leaving existing loans intact. The juxtaposition reveals a calculated approach: projecting defiance on sovereignty grounds while shielding state-owned financial institutions from dollar-access risks. JPMorgan and Citigroup are simultaneously being sued in Chinese courts after freezing payments tied to a subsequently sanctioned firm, illustrating the narrowing corridor available to global banks caught between competing legal jurisdictions.

Japan: Intervention, Deployment, and Diplomatic Positioning

Japan's Ministry of Finance deployed approximately $30 billion in estimated interventions across the week to support the yen near ¥160 per dollar, producing short-term moves of 1–2% on multiple days but achieving limited durable effect. Analysts broadly characterize the operations as increasingly ineffective level-targeting absent a meaningful shift in Bank of Japan policy, given elevated energy import costs and still-accommodative monetary settings. On the geopolitical front, Japan's deployment of 1,400 combat troops to the Philippines for joint Balikatan drills — including a ship-sinking exercise in the South China Sea on Wednesday — marks the first such deployment since World War II. Prime Minister Takaichi's overseas visits to Vietnam and Australia, alongside a ruling-party delegation to Taiwan, underscore a deepening regional security posture whose contrast with limited China engagement was conspicuous.

Regional Differentiation: Oil-Exposed vs. AI-Leveraged

The bifurcation between AI-leveraged and oil-exposed economies is becoming a defining feature of the regional growth landscape. Indonesia's first-quarter GDP expanded 5.61% year-on-year — a three-year high — even as the rupiah set a record low, authorities tightened foreign currency purchase limits to $25,000, and the Philippines registered its worst consumer spending data since the pandemic. The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a third consecutive rate hike in an 8-1 vote on Tuesday, unwinding all of last year's easing and leading developed-market peers in the hawkish response to the energy shock. Toyota issued a notably cautious earnings outlook on Friday, projecting ¥3 trillion in operating income against a consensus of ¥4.6 trillion, citing raw material cost pressures from the Iran conflict — the stock fell as much as 3.5%. ---

Europe

Political Fragmentation in the UK

UK local elections on Thursday produced results that will complicate Prime Minister Keir Starmer's already-strained position. With counts approaching completion in a third of English councils, Reform UK had secured a net gain of 275 seats while Labour's tally fell by 204 — the party losing roughly half of the seats it was defending. Starmer ruled out stepping aside despite the results. The outcome suggests meaningful fragmentation of Britain's traditional two-party structure, with Reform on the right and the Greens on the left gaining simultaneously. Whether this represents durable realignment or mid-term protest vote remains genuinely uncertain, but the breadth of Reform's gains gives Nigel Farage a credible platform ahead of the national electoral cycle running to 2029.

Gilt Markets and Fiscal Pressure

UK 30-year gilt yields reached 5.78% on Wednesday — the highest level since 1998 — reflecting a combination of the country's negative net international investment position, twin current account and fiscal deficits, and a perception that the Bank of England faces a more constrained policy path than the Federal Reserve. The spread of UK gilts over US Treasuries widened beyond levels seen at the peak of the 2022 Liz Truss episode. Bank of England officials joined private forecasters this week in questioning the reliability of recent UK economic data, adding further complexity to rate-setting at a moment when overnight index swaps imply materially more BoE tightening than markets priced before the Iran conflict began.

Transatlantic Tensions and European Strategic Posture

European assets underperformed relative to US markets through the first half of the week, with the Euro Stoxx 50 falling 1.7% on Monday, reflecting the continent's greater structural exposure to the Hormuz disruption. Two US actions compounded that vulnerability: President Trump announced a 25% tariff on European automobiles, and the Pentagon confirmed a troop drawdown from Germany beyond the initial 5,000 disclosed — both moves appearing linked to Chancellor Merz's public criticism of US Iran policy. The EU has reiterated retaliatory options and given Washington until July 4 to ratify a trade deal or face consequences. Separately, dozens of European leaders gathered in Yerevan for the European Political Community summit, with UK Prime Minister Starmer announcing Britain's intention to join the EU's €90 billion Ukraine loan plan — a notable post-Brexit alignment on security matters.

Corporate Earnings

European corporate results were mixed. UniCredit delivered a record quarterly profit of €3.22 billion on Tuesday, up 16% year-on-year and well ahead of the €2.68 billion consensus. Shell reported stronger-than-expected first-quarter earnings as elevated energy prices and trading volatility offset production declines. Novo Nordisk surged approximately 9% on Wednesday after its new Wegovy pill drove quarterly sales growth. Offsetting these, HSBC shares fell 5.9% in London on Tuesday after profits missed consensus estimates, weighed by an unexpected UK fraud-related charge and Middle East provisions. BMW earnings declined on intense China pricing competition. In Switzerland, lawmakers convened to begin a legislative process that could impose up to $20 billion in additional capital requirements on UBS, with meaningful implications for European banking sector capital allocation. ---

Global

The Hormuz Arc: Escalation, Diplomacy, and Partial De-escalation

The Strait of Hormuz remained the week's dominant cross-cutting fault line, with the conflict entering its tenth week and the operational and diplomatic picture shifting materially across the period. The week opened with the breakdown of the month-old ceasefire: US warships escorted American-flagged vessels through the strait on Monday, prompting an Iranian missile and drone attack that caused a large fire at the UAE's Fujairah port. President Trump then suspended "Project Freedom" on Wednesday, citing a request from mediator Pakistan and what he described as "great progress" toward a deal. On Thursday, a one-page memorandum of understanding circulated publicly, proposing that Tehran reopen the strait in exchange for a lifting of the US port blockade and a uranium enrichment moratorium, with broader nuclear questions deferred. Brent crude fell sharply on the news before paring losses after Tehran indicated a deal was not imminent. By Friday, US forces struck Iranian missile and drone launch sites near the strait following attacks on three American warships, illustrating that the ceasefire's resilience remains deeply uncertain.

Energy Markets: Price Action and Supply Adaptation

Oil prices traversed a wide range during the week. WTI touched $112 per barrel in intraweek trading before falling nearly 10% on Wednesday as diplomacy advanced, then recovering on Friday's fresh clashes. Brent futures for end-2026 crossed $90 per barrel — their highest since the conflict began. Prediction markets assigned less than 50% odds to a return to normal Hormuz traffic by end of June, down from roughly 90% two weeks prior. US shale producers are responding: Diamondback Energy's CEO signaled an inflection point, with the Permian Basin expected to add as many as 30 rigs, while Exxon plans a 12% production increase. US crude exports have exceeded 250 million barrels over the past nine weeks. The New York Fed's global supply chain pressure index reached its highest reading since July 2022 in April.

Geopolitical Architecture: Trump-Xi Summit and Caucasus Competition

The Trump-Xi Beijing summit, scheduled for May 14–15, is the week's principal forward-looking geopolitical event. Senior executives from Blackstone, Citigroup, Nvidia, Apple, Exxon, and Boeing are expected to accompany Trump in what would be the first visit by a sitting US president to China since 2017. Trade, Taiwan, and Iran are all on the agenda, with expectations for substantive deliverables appropriately low given the breadth of bilateral divergence. China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi hosted Iran's Abbas Araghchi this week and publicly urged Tehran to reopen the strait — positioning Xi Jinping as a potential peacemaker while simultaneously reflecting Beijing's dependence on Middle Eastern oil for approximately two-fifths of its imports. In a separate theater, the US signed a 99-year agreement with Armenia for management of a transit corridor linking Azerbaijan to Nakhchivan, directly challenging Russian influence in a region Moscow has historically dominated.

Dollar Architecture and De-Dollarization Dynamics

De-dollarization dynamics, long discussed but slow-moving, accumulated observable momentum across the week. Iran's reported collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin, China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US secondary sanctions, Gulf sovereign wealth funds reassessing US asset allocations, and central banks across emerging markets increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves represent individually modest but collectively notable stress points on dollar-centric financial infrastructure. At the Milken Institute conference in Los Angeles, EQT's Per Franzén — overseeing roughly €270 billion — noted clients actively reassessing historical overweights to dollar-denominated assets. The more salient risk, as multiple participants framed it, is not displacement by a rival currency but erosion through sustained US policy inconsistency. Project mBridge, the BIS-coordinated cross-border settlement system developed alongside China, Hong Kong, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Thailand, remains nascent in volume but is developing under conditions that could accelerate adoption faster than baseline projections assume.
