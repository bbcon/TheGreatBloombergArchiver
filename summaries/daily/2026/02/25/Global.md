President Trump's Iran warnings dominated geopolitical attention on Wednesday. In his State of the Union address, Trump accused Iranian officials of pursuing "sinister ambitions" to reconstitute their nuclear program following US airstrikes last year, while simultaneously reiterating interest in a diplomatic resolution. The dual messaging — military threat and negotiating posture — reflects a deliberate ambiguity, though it adds to mounting evidence of a US military buildup in the Middle East. NATO's AWACS surveillance aircraft stationed in Turkey have reportedly shifted their operational focus from Russia to Iran. The prospect of an armed escalation appears to be influencing energy markets: supertanker earnings have risen to their highest level in years, with traders pricing a non-trivial risk that conflict could disrupt oil flows through the Strait of Hormuz. Trump's special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner are scheduled to hold fresh talks with Iranian officials in Geneva on Thursday, keeping a diplomatic channel open even as military preparations appear to be advancing.

On the energy transition, Bloomberg Opinion's Javier Blas argued that "net zero" has effectively been abandoned as a political commitment, noting that mentions have collapsed in rich-nation energy communiqués while global demand for oil, gas, and coal stands at all-time highs. His projection — that emissions may stabilize in the 25,000-30,000 million metric ton range by 2050, well above zero — represents an implicit acknowledgment that the 2050 target is no longer operationally credible under current policy trajectories. Renewable electricity capacity is nonetheless expected to continue expanding; the divergence between clean power growth and overall emissions reduction illustrates the core tension in the transition narrative.

China's Belt and Road Initiative appears to be entering a new expansionary phase after a period of retrenchment. Data cited by advisory firm Teneo shows total BRI-related engagements reached an all-time high of $214 billion in 2025, with both average deal size and geographic reach at record levels, concentrated in central Asia, energy, metals, and technology. The "small-but-beautiful" era declared in 2021 appears to have been a Covid-era artifact rather than a structural recalibration.

UBS raised its worst-case private credit default scenario to 15%, up from 13% issued less than a month ago, citing "rapid, severe AI disruption" as a clearer catalyst than previously modeled. The revision underscores the degree to which AI disruption risk has migrated from equity market narratives into credit analysis, with implications for the $1.8 trillion private credit complex.
