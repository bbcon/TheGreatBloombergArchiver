It’s been many decades since Vietnam had a leader with global visibility. Now, 
President To Lam, one of the most powerful figures to emerge from the Southeast 
Asian nation since Ho Chi Minh, is using his clout to remake it into a global 
economic power.

During his decade-long ascent — capped in April 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/vietnam-s-to-lam-secures-presidency-tightening-grip-on-power?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 when he secured the presidency — Lam has stacked the government and ruling 
Communist party with allies. And while pushing the boundaries of Vietnam’s 
traditional collective leadership model on the domestic front, he’s engaged in 
a tempo of foreign travel aimed at raising the global profile of a nation long 
known for its historic Cold War victory over the US.

Lam’s diplomatic schedule in recent months included visits to the White House, 
Beijing and New Delhi, ahigh-profile speech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-29/vietnam-s-to-lam-says-military-power-alone-can-t-ensure-security?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 at an annual security conference in Singapore and the hosting of leaders of 
Japan and South Korea — two of Vietnam’s biggest foreign investors.
To LamPhotographer: Dario Pignatelli/Bloomberg
Lam has had a strong story to tell: one of sustained, rapid economic growth 
combined with the kind of political stability thatstands out 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/the-bad-bet-that-sealed-a-crushing-defeat-for-thai-reformists?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 in a region where fiercedomestic infighting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/why-was-philippine-vp-sara-duterte-impeached-again-what-happens-next?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 is common. The latest GDP reading, out last week, showed Vietnam clocking in 
at 8.4% growth, a pace thattrounced expectations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-03/vietnam-s-quarterly-growth-surges-june-trade-beats-estimates?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
. The World Bank justbumped the country up 
<https://documents1.worldbank.org/curated/en/099063026190549947/pdf/BOSIB-8ff63426-3c40-412d-bad2-cc20a4ebea81.pdf>
 to “upper-middle” income status.

But Lam’s vision is even grander. He’s seeking to supercharge what’s already 
one of the world’s fastest-growing economies, targeting an almost 12% expansion 
in the second half of this year. His government’s planning horizon extends well 
beyond its own lifetime. Last week, it officiallyunveiled 
<https://en.baochinhphu.vn/ha-noi-unveils-100-year-master-plan-111260629145745311.htm>
 a $2.5 trillion, 100-year “master plan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/vietnam-plans-2-5-trillion-overhaul-of-hanoi-with-goal-to-remake-red-river?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
” for the nation’s capital, Hanoi.

If anything, the biggest risk may be Lam is thinking too big.

Vietnam’s trajectory 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2015-03-22/asia-s-about-to-spawn-a-new-tiger-economy-good-morning-vietnam?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 over the past two decades has been “striking,” JPMorgan Chase & Co. economist 
Mahmoud Harb wrote in a note to clients this month. As recently as 2008, the 
World Bank still classified it as “low income,” he said. “There are few, if 
any, comparable cases which have climbed the development ladder this quickly 
without a commodity windfall.”

Rather than benefiting from oil or mineral wealth, the Southeast Asian nation 
has followed the same export and manufacturing-led model pioneered by Japan 
after World War II and adopted by China and others.

But Lam, 69, wants this newfound middle power to be more than just a factory 
hub. Eyeinghigh-income status 
<https://blogs.worldbank.org/en/opendata/who-moves-up-and-why--a-closer-look-at-the-new-world-bank-group->
 by 2045 — when Vietnam commemorates the centenary of its declaration of 
independence — he wants the nation to sustain double-digit expansion rates. 
Much of that will come via state-led investment, as laid out inResolution 168 
<https://baochinhphu.vn/cap-nhat-kich-ban-tang-truong-va-giai-phap-trong-tam-trong-cac-quy-con-lai-102260628071913834.htm>
, the government’s latest growth missive.
Demolition is a common sight in Vietnamese cities these days, including in the 
capital of Hanoi, where the government has plans for a series of major 
development projects.Photographer: Francesca Stevens/Bloomberg
Lam’s plan includes grand ambitions to transform the banks of the Red River, 
which winds through the heart of Hanoi, into a vast urban corridor of parks, 
cultural venues and modern housing. Vietnam’s commercial hub, Ho Chi Minh City, 
is witnessing its own mini-wave ofmega-projects 
<https://vietnamnet.vn/en/hcm-city-launches-us-9-6-billion-infrastructure-projects-for-50th-anniversary-2531316.html>
, launched this month to mark 50 years since its name was changed from Saigon.

Ambitious targets have been assigned to every ministry and province, putting 
pressure on officials who’ve already endured some of the biggest cuts to the 
bureaucracy in decades.

Amid the dust and demolition from construction sites dominating Vietnam’s 
cities these days, there are concerns about the scale of financing required and 
whether resources are being put to the best use. Vietnam’scredit-to-GDP ratio 
<https://data.worldbank.org/indicator/FS.AST.PRVT.GD.ZS?locations=VN-ID-PH-IN> 
is already among the highest in Asia’s emerging markets, and bouts of tight 
liquidity have highlighted pressures on the banking sector.

Policymakers have made accommodations. The central bank has come up with a 
special credit line to allow banks to lend to three of the big developers — 
Vingroup, Sun Group and Masterise — to fund certain projects (with the debt 
kept off of official credit quota books).

The central bank itself has signaled some concern, warning of pressure on 
monetary policymakers as they seek to balance a 10% economic growth target with 
keeping the dong exchange rate stable andinflation under control 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/vietnam-central-bank-warns-on-inflation-acts-to-stabilize-dong?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
. The State Bank of Vietnam’s credit growth target is a hefty 15% for this 
year, but it’s repeatedly advised that this could be adjusted if necessary.

“The current policy mix remains supportive” for growth in the second half of 
this year,” Pham Luu Hung, chief economist at SSI Securities Corp., wrote in a 
note last week. “But rising leverage, greater credit concentration and 
structural funding pressures warrant close monitoring.” His counterparts at 
Oxford Economics have cited “overheating risks.”
Xi Jinping, left, and To Lam during a welcome ceremony outside the Great Hall 
of the People in Beijing in April.Photographer: Shen Hong/Xinhua/AP
Meantime, on the international front, Vietnam pitches itself as a nation 
outside of geopolitical fights, welcoming investment from all. Even as 
companies based in developed nations set up shop in Vietnam as a 
“China-plus-one” destination, Chinese enterprises aremoving operations there 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-24/china-s-investments-in-southeast-asia-snarl-us-plans-on-supply-chains?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 to help get around restrictions on direct exports from China. Registered 
foreign investment in Vietnam at the end of June totaled $34.65 billion, up 61% 
from a year earlier.

It’s a strategy of multidirectional diplomacy and multidirectional outreach, 
says Ted Osius, a former US ambassador to Vietnam and now a Ho Chi Minh 
City-based investor and adviser: “Stay open, stay engaged economically with as 
many partners as possible.”

That may be harder than it sounds. President Donald Trump has repeatedly 
called out Vietnam’s surging trade surplus with the US. The latest American 
data, for May, showed Vietnam weighing in with thesecond-largest <> monthly 
imbalance. Hanoi is still trying to wrap up a trade deal with Washington, ahead 
of Lam’s next planned visit to the US, for the United Nations General Assembly 
in September.

As for ties with another superpower, China, for all Lam’s bonhomie 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/xi-tells-vietnam-s-lam-keeping-socialist-path-is-top-priority?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 with President Xi Jinping in April, relations aren’t without tension. Vietnam 
has beenexpanding its maritime outposts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-09/vietnam-expands-south-china-sea-outposts-as-beijing-widens-lead?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260711&utm_content=6284>
 in the South China Sea, where China has been aggressively contesting various 
territorial claims.

As the saying goes, with great power comes great responsibility. Now that Lam 
has secured his rule over Vietnam, making good on his grand pledges may prove a 
high-wire act.
