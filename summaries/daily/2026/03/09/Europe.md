European markets are confronting a dual shock: soaring energy prices threatening to reignite inflation, and a deteriorating growth backdrop that was already fragile. Interest rate markets moved sharply; swaps now imply two full 25 basis point ECB rate hikes in 2026, compared with one priced as recently as Friday. The first hike is fully priced by June. For the Bank of England, money markets shifted from pricing cuts to a 70% probability of a hike by year-end, as estimates from ING and RSM UK suggest UK inflation could rise to more than double the 2% target if current energy price levels persist. The ECB and BOE face a genuinely uncomfortable position: easing into an energy-driven inflation shock risks credibility loss, while tightening risks amplifying a growth slowdown.

German economic data released Monday added to the difficult backdrop. Both industrial production and factory orders declined at the start of the year, tempering expectations for a cyclical recovery in Europe's largest economy. Germany's chemical industry faces a structural crisis that predates the Iran conflict — higher energy costs following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, rising carbon prices, new Chinese capacity, and softening demand have already forced plant closures — but the war materially intensifies pressure. European chemical producers are estimated to pay approximately €3 billion more annually in natural gas costs if the post-war price surge persists. The Leuna chemical park in eastern Germany, exposed through its dependence on energy and raw material imports, has become a test case.

In politics, Chancellor Friedrich Merz suffered a notable setback as his CDU lost the Baden-Württemberg regional election to the ruling Greens — a state the CDU had been projected to reclaim after 15 years of Green governance. The result compounds Merz's domestic political difficulties and reflects broader voter unease. In the UK, Prime Minister Starmer pledged support for households facing energy cost pressures. German bund futures slid to an almost 15-year low, with Italian fiscal credibility also identified as at risk from a prolonged Middle East conflict by Scope Ratings.
