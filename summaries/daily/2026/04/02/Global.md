The defining market development of April 2 is the Brent crude price breaking above $107 per barrel following Trump's speech, representing a gain of more than 40% since the Iran conflict began approximately five weeks ago. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed to normal commercial traffic, and Trump's pledge of fresh military action over the next two to three weeks — absent any defined exit condition — has extinguished near-term expectations for a reopening. Even in an optimistic scenario where hostilities de-escalate, damaged infrastructure and residual security risks would likely delay a return to normal flow for weeks thereafter. Options markets reflect deep uncertainty about the endgame, with positioning spanning downside hedges to speculative bets on oil reaching $450 per barrel — a distribution that speaks to the absence of conviction rather than directional consensus.

The IRGC's management of Hormuz has taken on commercial characteristics: tanker operators report receiving offers of safe passage contingent on re-flagging vessels under friendly-nation registries, with payments demanded in yuan or cryptocurrency. The UAE has formally called on the United Nations to authorize measures — including force — to reopen the waterway, reflecting the acute economic pressure on Gulf states whose export infrastructure remains inside the affected zone. Emirates Global Aluminium has halted operations at its Al Taweelah smelter following missile and drone strikes.

The global diesel scramble is reshaping commodity trade routes in ways that carry significant second-order costs. US naphtha exports reached a single-month record of approximately 15 million barrels in March, as Japanese buyers substituted US Gulf Coast supply for unavailable Middle Eastern cargoes. UNCTAD now projects global merchandise trade growth of 1.5–2.5% for 2026, a sharp deceleration from 4.7% last year. Iran's President Pezeshkian published an open letter addressed directly to the American public on Thursday, arguing that Iran did not initiate hostilities and questioning whether the conflict serves US or Israeli interests — a move that appears calibrated to exploit divisions within Trump's political base rather than to advance formal diplomatic channels. Iran's strategic posture remains effectively controlled by the IRGC, which has shown no inclination toward ceasefire discussions.
