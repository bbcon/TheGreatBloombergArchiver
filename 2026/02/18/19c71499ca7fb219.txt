Ethiopia’s EV revolution

By Fasika Tadesse 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVQgwR12Lio/fasika-tadesse> and Akshat Rathi 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUdOobfNzkQ/akshat-rathi>

Yuma Sasaki likes a challenge. His mantra is that the harder it is to do 
business in a country, the better his chances.“Those are the good markets,” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-18/ethiopia-ev-boom-sparked-by-ice-car-import-ban?cmpid=BBD021826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 he said, because there isn’t that much competition.

Three years ago, Sasaki, who was born in Japan, founded an electric 
two-wheeler startup called Dodai in Addis Ababa. The Ethiopian capital didn’t 
have any two wheelers on the streets because the government had banned them in 
2019 out of security concerns. (The city is the headquarters of the Africa 
Union and often hosts heads of state and other high-ranking officials.)
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-18/ethiopia-ev-boom-sparked-by-ice-car-import-ban>
A delivery rider for logistics company Klik.Photographer: Amanuel 
Sileshi/Bloomberg
Sasaki saw an opportunity. The average Ethiopian earns about $1,000 each year, 
and most of its cities don’t have great public transportation. Addis Ababa’s 
metropolitan population hovers around 10 million, and Sasaki calculated that 
its gig workers could use at least 150,000 two-wheelers.

He started lobbying the city’s government to allow motorbikes again. “I 
convinced them that, without two wheelers, it’s very difficult for e-commerce 
to grow and create enough jobs,” he said. Sasaki also agreed that he would only 
sell two-wheelers to those who agreed to have their vehicles located by the 
government in case of security issues.
 <https://omny.fm/shows/zero/ethiopias-electric-car-boom-shames-rich-countries>
Sasaki knew the economics made sense. In his visits to Ethiopia, he had seen 
firsthand that gasoline was expensive and, because the government didn’t have 
enough foreign reserves to import fuel, there often wasn’t enough of it. Even 
today, there are long lines at gas stations.

Electricity, on the other hand, was cheap and plentiful. Mainly made up of 
renewables in the form of hydropower, solar and wind. Plus the country was in 
the midst of building the Grand Ethiopian Renaissance dam, completed last year, 
that would provide a surge of power and lower costs.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-18/ethiopia-ev-boom-sparked-by-ice-car-import-ban>
Queues at a gas station.Photographer: Amanuel Sileshi/Bloomberg
At the time, the Ethiopian government was also considering a radical policy 
that would transform the nation’s automotives market. In January 2024, 
officials implemented a ban on importing fossil fuel-powered vehicles and 
drastically cut the nation’s high tariffs on electric vehicles.

Addis Ababa is set to host the United Nations climate summit COP32 in 2027. 
But the change in vehicle policy was driven by financial pressure, rather than 
climate goals. The country imports all the oil it consumes, with the bill 
reaching $4 billion in 2022. It defaulted on its sovereign bonds in 2023 and 
got a $3.4 billion bailout from the International Monetary Fund.

The result has been a surge in EV sales. Unsurprisingly, most are cheap 
Chinese models. In less than two years, government figures indicate that the 
country’s EV penetration has increased from less than 1% to more than 6%. 
That’s above the global average of 4%.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-18/ethiopia-ev-boom-sparked-by-ice-car-import-ban>
EV technicians at an EV assembly factory in Ethiopia.Bloomberg
For Dodai, the policy change was a blessing. Its electric two-wheelers are 
assembled in an Addis Ababa factory using mostly imported Chinese parts. In 
three years, it has sold 1,800 motorbikes and is now setting up 
battery-swapping stations around the city.

“The Ethiopia story is fascinating,” said Colin McKerracher, head of clean 
transport at BloombergNEF. “What you’re seeing in places that don’t make a lot 
of vehicles of any type, they’re saying: ‘Well, look, if I’m going to import 
the cars anyway, then I’d rather import less oil. We may as well import the one 
that cleans up local air quality and is cheaper to buy.’”

ESG pay targets quietly disappear

By Ben Elgin <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AP8O5gRPysw/ben-elgin> and 
Jeff Green <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AD8PxHnr0Mk/jeff-green>

Two years ago, many of America’s largest companies began stripping diversity 
targets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-06-18/best-buy-chipotle-carnival-downplay-dei-executive-pay-goals?cmpid=BBD021826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 out of executive pay packages. Now, environmental measures—including goals 
tied to climate emissions—are beginning to face a similar fate.

Apple Inc. <https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/AAPL:US> quietly dropped a 
so-called “ESG modifier” from its 2025 pay packages for Chief Executive Officer 
Tim Cook and other top executives, according to a corporate filing last month. 
The provision, in place since 2021, had allowed Apple’s board to adjust annual 
bonuses up or down by as much as 10% depending on the company’s performance on 
a variety of measures, including greenhouse-gas reductions and renewable energy 
use among suppliers.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-18/apple-quietly-unlinks-environmental-performance-from-pay-packages>
Illustration: 731;Bloomberg
Apple’s move follows similar decisions at dozens of companies, including 
Starbucks Corp. <https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/SBUX:US>, Salesforce Inc. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/CRM:US>, Mastercard Inc. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/MA:US> and Procter & Gamble Co. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/quote/PG:US>, which have recently weakened or 
severed ties between environmental performance and the size of their 
executives’ paychecks.

The shift is beginning to show up in the numbers. The share of S&P 500 
companies tying executive compensation to environmental metrics fell to 46.7% 
in 2025, down from a peak of 52.6% two years earlier, according to figures from 
The Conference Board and analytics firm ESGAUGE.

Gates’ climate tech halt

By Akshat Rathi <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUdOobfNzkQ/akshat-rathi> 
andCoco Liu <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AU-LNn94_HY/coco-liu>

Breakthrough Energy, the Bill Gates-backed group that bankrolls climate tech, 
hasstopped making new investments 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/bill-gates-key-climate-tech-fund-halts-new-investment-lays-off-workers?cmpid=BBD021826_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 from one of its key funds to scale up nascent green technologies, just as the 
market for these startups is being squeezed by President Donald Trump’s 
assaults on climate policy.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/bill-gates-key-climate-tech-fund-halts-new-investment-lays-off-workers>
Bill GatesAFP
Launched in 2021, Breakthrough’s Catalyst fund raised more than $1 billion to 
finance the demonstration and scale-up of first-of-their-kind green solutions, 
which often struggle to get off the ground due to their unproven technologies 
and hefty costs. After supporting 10 startups and spending “high hundreds of 
millions of dollars,” a Breakthrough Energy spokesperson said, it has now 
suspended new investment from the fund.
