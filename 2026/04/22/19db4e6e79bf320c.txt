Fed’s ‘Statecraft’ Role

Tucked away in Kevin Warsh’s 2 ½ hour Senate confirmation hearing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/warsh-says-fed-needs-a-new-framework-for-dealing-with-inflation?cmpid=BBD042226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 Tuesday was a brief exchange over the Federal Reserve’s international role, 
when the nominee to become the next US central bank chief offered some brief 
thoughts.

“There are risks to the US position in the world, including economic,” Warsh 
said. That’s why “the economic statecraft agenda” led by Treasury Secretary 
Scott Bessent and Secretary of State Marco Rubio is important, he said.

“The Fed will play a supporting role in ensuring that the financial system is 
as safe as it can be and work with them, because it’s outside of the conduct of 
monetary policy,” said Warsh – who more thantwo decades ago 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-15/trump-fed-chair-contender-kevin-warsh-ready-to-cut-interest-rates?cmpid=BBD042226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 served on the White House’s National Economic Council, which coordinates 
policy.

While Warsh didn’t reference it, one of the Trump administration’s 
international economic policy innovations has been a fresh approach toward 
using dollar swap lines. Usually, those arrangements are handled at the Fed, 
with the US central bank offering dollar liquidity to one of its peers in 
exchange for the other nation’s currency.

But Bessent last year used the Treasury’s capacity to prop up Argentina’s 
currency 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-17/why-argentina-turned-to-trump-for-a-financial-rescue?cmpid=BBD042226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
. And Tuesday, President Trump said a currency swap with the United Arab 
Emirates isunder consideration 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/trump-says-currency-swap-with-uae-is-under-consideration?cmpid=BBD042226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
.

The Treasury chief was specific in an interview 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-08-11/treasury-secretary-bessent-on-tariffs-deficits-trump-s-economic-plan?cmpid=BBD042226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 with Bloomberg last July in viewing dollar liquidity lines as an instrument 
for “locking in dollar supremacy.” He said that the “Treasury can do swap lines 
just like the Fed – I think you’re going to see us doing swap lines with a 
whole cohort of new countries that the Fed doesn’t have swap lines with.”

“One of my goals to create a big dollar funding market in the Middle East,” 
Bessent said last year. One big challenge, however, is that the Treasurylacks 
the Fed’s capacity 
<https://home.treasury.gov/policy-issues/international/exchange-stabilization-fund>
.

Warsh gave no indication at all that the Fed could get involved in any major 
expansion ofdollar swap lines 
<https://www.federalreserve.gov/monetarypolicy/bst_liquidityswaps.htm> when he 
described the central bank as needing to “ensure the US is on its front foot 
and in a position of strength during this period of rivalry between the US and 
another nation around the world.” (A clear reference to China.)

Asked what’s most important to support the dollar, he answered, “Deliver 
stable prices.” But inflation-fighting isn’t usually thought of as a tool of 
“statecraft.” Making this area one among many to watch in the coming months.

Need-to-Know Research

Morgan Stanley analysts flag that, with the November US congressional 
elections little more than six months away, investors are starting to pepper 
them with questions on the implications. Led by Ariana Salvatore, head of US 
public policy research, they offered a number of thoughts in a note on Tuesday.

Among them is a “myth-busting” section, where Morgan Stanley warned not to 
count on any Democratic victory curbing Trump’s ability to inject uncertainty. 
A number of areas will remain under his control, including the Iran situation 
and other foreign policies, tariffs, deregulation and immigration. Most 
instances of elevated policy uncertainty since early 2025 have been “tied to 
executive, rather than legislative” action, the analysts highlighted.

While many assume that divided government means legislative gridlock, Morgan 
Stanley analysts also said it’s possible to see some areas of compromise 
between the White House and a Congress where at least one chamber is held by 
the Democrats. Among them are industrial policy moves to promote US AI 
competition against China, and a regulatory framework for the nascentcrypto 
industry 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-08/how-bitcoin-ether-are-rallying-as-trump-embraces-crypto?cmpid=BBD042226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
. Still, the bar for momentum here “is high,” they said.
