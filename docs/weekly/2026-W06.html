Weekly Brief

Feb 02 – Feb 08, 2026

US

  • Tech rotation deepens as software names shed more than 17% YTD on AI disruption fears; breadth remains constructive with equal-weighted S&P hitting a record mid-week.
  • Amazon guided to $200 billion in 2026 AI capex on Thursday, pushing aggregate hyperscaler spending toward $650 billion and intensifying investor scrutiny of return timelines.
  • Bitcoin fell below $70,000 on Wednesday—down more than 42% from its October peak—while gold held above $5,000/oz, reinforcing bullion's role as the primary safe-haven asset.
  • Fed transition dynamics and questions over institutional independence added a low-level but persistent risk premium to rates markets through the week.
  • Affordability constraints and a record-weak consumer confidence reading underscored the gap between aggregate income data and household sentiment.

Equity Markets and the AI Rotation

The week's dominant US equity theme was a sharp factor rotation away from software and momentum names toward value, culminating Wednesday in the largest value-over-momentum outperformance since the November 2020 vaccine catalyst—notable precisely because no comparable discrete trigger was identifiable. The S&P 1500 Software index fell more than 17% year-to-date, with the term "SaaSpocalypse" gaining currency among traders. Thomson Reuters fell as much as 20% on Tuesday on concerns that Anthropic's legal-workflow tool threatens its core franchise. AMD slid 7.3% in Monday pre-market after disappointing AI-related guidance, then extended losses to 17% post-earnings. Alphabet beat fourth-quarter revenue estimates but guided 2026 capex to as much as $185 billion versus consensus near $120 billion; shares fell 3.3% in Wednesday pre-market. Amazon fell approximately 8% in Thursday pre-market after guiding to $200 billion in AI-related capital expenditure for 2026. Qualcomm declined 9% and Arm 6.6% on softer outlooks citing potential memory-component shortages. Against this, breadth remained resilient: roughly two-thirds of S&P 500 constituents advanced Wednesday, the equal-weighted index closed at a record, and Silicon Labs jumped 33% Monday on a report that Texas Instruments is in advanced talks to acquire the chip designer for roughly $7 billion.

Macro Backdrop and Consumer Dynamics

The macro underpinning remained firmer than the equity tape suggested. Q3 GDP was revised to a 4.4% annualized pace, the strongest in two years, and Bank of America flagged potential for front-loaded 2026 growth, citing roughly $135–$140 billion in OBBBA fiscal stimulus and a normalization boost from the partial government shutdown's resolution. Average hourly earnings rose 3.8% in December, well above inflation—yet consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in more than a decade. Goldman research pointed to housing as the binding constraint: the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160–200% of income from 90–120% in 2000. This disconnect between aggregate and distributional data carries continued political weight.

Fed Transition and Fiscal Policy

Kevin Warsh's incoming tenure as Fed Chair carries an internal consensus-building challenge that markets are only beginning to price. President Trump indicated Monday he would not have nominated Warsh had he signaled a preference for rate increases, adding an unusual layer of political commentary around the succession. Governor Lisa Cook reiterated the importance of central bank credibility. Senate Republicans raised concerns about the Justice Department's investigation into outgoing Chair Jerome Powell. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent testified before the House on Monday and the Senate on Wednesday, navigating questions about the administration's fiscal trajectory. The partial government shutdown ended after Trump signed a funding deal negotiated with Senate Democrats, removing one near-term tail risk.

Corporate and Trade

Stellantis took $26 billion in charges on Thursday tied to unwinding its EV strategy, with shares falling as much as 24% in Milan—a move with direct implications for North American manufacturing given its Jeep, Ram, and Chrysler exposure. Molina Healthcare plunged 33% in Friday pre-market after guiding 2026 profit to less than half of consensus, dragging Centene and Oscar Health lower in a sharp managed-care de-rating. The administration moved to seed a $12 billion critical-minerals stockpile, extending its industrial policy footprint. French customs data illustrated the tariff regime's bilateral costs: Q4 2025 spirits exports to the US fell 47%, wine 39%, and cosmetics 25%—a reminder that tariff escalation carries measurable retaliatory feedback. ---

Asia

  • Chinese tech approached bear-market territory on the Hang Seng mid-week as the global software rotation swept through Asian trading sessions.
  • China's 2026 growth target appears set to be cut to 4.5%–5% at the March NPC, marking the first downward revision in three years; Citi treated its previously baseline stimulus package as a ceiling rather than a floor.
  • Beijing escalated its Panama port dispute, instructing state firms to halt new project discussions and intensifying customs inspections on Panamanian goods—underscoring infrastructure's role as a geopolitical lever.
  • The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, with markets pricing a further increase amid persistent services inflation.
  • Japan's PM Takaichi appeared set to secure a comfortable lower-house majority on Sunday, with Topix gains increasingly underpinned by governance reform rather than macro tailwinds alone.

China: Policy, Markets, and Geopolitics

China's macro picture was defined by a tension between firming services activity and accumulating structural headwinds. January services PMI picked up, and Beijing reiterated its commitment to building a unified national market to unlock domestic consumption—a recurring priority that has yet to produce a durable demand-side rebound. More than a dozen provinces lowered their 2026 growth targets through the week, and consensus coalesced around a national target of 4.5%–5% for the NPC in March, the first downward revision in three years. Citi revised its stimulus baseline downward, treating its previously expected package—roughly one trillion yuan in fiscal support, a 20-basis-point rate cut, and a 50-basis-point RRR reduction—as a ceiling rather than a floor. Against this backdrop, Baidu announced its first-ever dividend alongside a $5 billion buyback on Wednesday, a notable capital-return shift from large-cap Chinese internet names that partially offset the broader tech selloff. President Trump and President Xi spoke by phone during the week, covering trade and Taiwan; the tone appeared constructive, but substantive details remained opaque. On geopolitics, Beijing's response to a Panamanian court decision voiding a CK Hutchison port contract escalated materially. By mid-week, Chinese authorities had instructed state-linked firms to halt new project discussions in Panama and intensified customs inspections on Panamanian goods. The episode illustrates how port and logistics infrastructure has become a primary arena for US-China strategic competition, with downstream political risk for Chinese-operated maritime assets globally and potential acceleration of divestment discussions at COSCO and Hutchison-related entities.

Japan

Polling through the week suggested Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's LDP-led coalition was on track to comfortably clear the 233-seat majority threshold in Sunday's lower-house election. The yen whipsawed on Takaichi's comments favoring a weaker currency, which she partially walked back. More durably, the Topix rally appeared underpinned by genuine governance reform—rising dividends, accelerating buybacks, and a re-rating of long-discounted sectors such as non-ferrous metals, with Sumitomo Electric having tripled since the US election. The 30-year JGB yield's surge through the week is best read as a catch-up to G7 peers rather than incipient fiscal stress, though Takaichi's expansionary fiscal instincts warrant continued monitoring.

Australia and Southeast Asia

The RBA's rate hike on Wednesday positioned Australia at the leading edge of major central bank tightening in 2026, with economists and futures markets pricing a further increase amid services-driven inflation persistence. The divergence from eurozone disinflation appears supportive for AUD on widening rate differentials, though housing-sensitive consumption represents a meaningful offset. In Southeast Asia, Moody's cut Indonesia's outlook to negative on fiscal risk concerns; Jakarta's finance ministry pushed back in unusually pointed terms, and the government's public criticism of a Citigroup economist raised questions about market communication under the Prabowo administration. The Philippines posted its weakest non-pandemic growth in 15 years. India's RBI held rates while citing improved clarity from a prospective US trade framework; reporting late in the week suggested a surprise bilateral deal could materially reset US-India ties, with Indian IT and pharmaceutical exporters among the most directly exposed beneficiaries.

Metals and Capital Flows

Chinese retail flows meaningfully amplified the metals cycle's volatility. Shanghai copper futures recorded their highest-ever monthly volumes in January. China's top four gold ETFs saw nearly $1 billion in outflows on Tuesday. Silver fell as much as 17% intraday on Wednesday to roughly $74/oz, taking its five-day decline to nearly 40%; trader Bian Ximing's large net-short silver position on the Shanghai Futures Exchange reportedly generated roughly $300 million in paper gains. The episode reinforced Beijing's challenge: with property and equity returns subdued, household savings continue to seek speculative outlets, testing official tolerance for retail risk-taking. ---

Europe

  • Novo Nordisk shares plunged as much as 20% in Copenhagen on Monday on sharply weaker sales guidance, resetting consensus assumptions on GLP-1 pricing power.
  • The Bank of England held rates in a notably close 5-4 vote on Wednesday; Governor Bailey subsequently endorsed market pricing of roughly 50% probability of a March cut.
  • UK Prime Minister Starmer's political position deteriorated materially over his ties to Peter Mandelson, with parliament voting to compel disclosure of related documents.
  • Stellantis announced €22 billion in EV-related charges on Thursday, its largest restructuring action in recent memory, joining a broader European auto industry retreat from electrification timelines.
  • German factory orders rose at their fastest pace in two years, reinforcing tentative signs of manufacturing recovery in the eurozone's largest economy.

Monetary Policy: ECB and Bank of England

Both major European central banks held policy rates unchanged during the week, though the context differed meaningfully. The BOE's Wednesday decision was a 5-4 vote in favor of holding—a more divided committee than the pre-meeting consensus framing implied—and Governor Bailey's subsequent endorsement of a roughly 50% probability of a March cut shifted the market interpretation toward a resumption of easing. The MPC's growing confidence that UK inflation has been contained has shifted the policy risk function toward the labour market, where softening signals have accumulated; sterling and short sterling rates will remain sensitive to incoming wage and employment data. The ECB's hold reflected continued patience as policymakers assessed whether ongoing disinflation is durable. Euro-area HICP sank further below the 2% target through the period, sharpening the policy debate, while ECB Governing Council member Stournaras downplayed concerns about recent euro appreciation. The asymmetric question of how the Council's reaction function handles target undershoots—versus the overshoots of 2022–23—remains an open and meaningful one for markets.

UK Political Risk

Prime Minister Starmer's position weakened materially through the week. Labour MPs were described as being in a "febrile, mutinous mood" over his ties to Peter Mandelson, whose appointment as US ambassador carries complications given Mandelson's longstanding association with Jeffrey Epstein. Parliament voted to compel disclosure of related documents, a significant development in its own right. Sterling came under pressure and gilt yields reflected a renewed political risk premium, though rivals have so far refrained from a formal leadership challenge. How long Starmer can maintain his position absent a credible challenger—or a political recovery—remains genuinely uncertain.

Macro and Corporate

German factory orders posting their fastest two-year rise offered a constructive datapoint for an economy that closed 2025 with stronger-than-expected growth. Chancellor Merz separately pursued Middle Eastern energy suppliers to reduce German reliance on US LNG, a notable strategic pivot given transatlantic frictions. On the corporate side, the week was eventful. Novo Nordisk's Monday plunge—among the largest single-day declines in its history—carried index-level implications for Denmark and reset sell-side assumptions around GLP-1 competitive dynamics. Shell reported adjusted net income of $3.26 billion in Q4, down 11% year-on-year, on softer crude, weak oil trading, and chemicals losses; it maintained its $3.5 billion quarterly buyback. UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat, underscoring capital return confidence post-Credit Suisse integration. HSBC moved toward a more Wall Street-style compensation model, preparing minimal or zero bonuses for underperformers. The proposed Rio Tinto–Glencore tie-up collapsed within 24 hours over price disagreements on Thursday; Glencore shares fell roughly 11%, leaving the company's strategic direction uncertain with Ivan Glasenberg remaining a key shareholder. Equinor cut its 2026 buyback to as much as $1.5 billion from $5 billion in 2025 after Q4 profit missed on lower commodity prices.

Trade and Auto Sector

French customs data released during the week provided concrete evidence of US tariff pass-through: Q4 2025 spirits exports to the US fell 47%, wine 39%, and perfumes and cosmetics 25%—meaningful declines for export-sensitive French consumer and luxury categories. Stellantis's €22 billion in EV-related charges on Thursday—exceeding analyst expectations and covering model cancellations and supplier compensation—extended the auto sector's retreat from electrification timelines that has now touched Ford, GM, Porsche, and Stellantis. The scale of the writedown carries implications for European auto supply chains and raises further questions about the continent's path toward 2035 combustion-engine phase-out targets. ---

Global

  • Silver fell as much as 17% intraday on Wednesday to roughly $74/oz, its five-day decline approaching 40%, as speculative positioning built since Citigroup's $150 target call was aggressively unwound.
  • US-Iran tensions kept a bid under crude, with the US Navy shooting down an Iranian drone in the Arabian Sea on Monday; Muscat nuclear talks were subsequently confirmed for later in the week.
  • China's 543 GW of power capacity additions in 2025 increasingly appear to confer a durable AI infrastructure advantage, as US data-center grid-connection backlogs stretch to seven years in parts of Virginia.
  • The GCC-Asia trade corridor expanded at 15% annually from 2021–24, emerging as a bright spot against the broader backdrop of tariff-driven global trade fragmentation.
  • Chinese and Russian sanctioned crude is accumulating unsold, with structural cracks appearing in the black market from ample legitimate supply at competitive prices rather than enforcement alone.

Commodity Volatility and Speculative Dynamics

The week's most dramatic cross-asset moves occurred in precious and base metals, where the unwind of speculative positioning—much of it channeled through Shanghai-listed instruments by mainland retail investors with limited alternative investment outlets—produced extreme intraday swings. Silver's fall of as much as 17% on Wednesday, bringing its five-day decline to nearly 40%, was the most visible manifestation. Gold declined as much as 3.5% on the same day before stabilizing above $5,000/oz later in the week as dip-buyers returned. Shanghai copper futures recorded their highest-ever monthly volumes in January, and China's top four gold ETFs saw nearly $1 billion in outflows on Tuesday. The structural drivers underpinning metals demand—central bank gold accumulation, dollar diversification, and industrial demand tied to grid, renewables, and AI infrastructure—appear intact, but near-term positioning had clearly overextended. The Trump administration's announcement of a $12 billion critical-minerals stockpile, framed explicitly as a response to demonstrated Chinese rare-earth leverage, adds a policy dimension to what had been a primarily speculative and structural story.

Energy Geopolitics and the Strait of Hormuz

Oil oscillated through the week as traders balanced geopolitical risk against fundamental signals. Brent advanced to around $68 on Monday and Tuesday after the US Navy shot down an Iranian drone heading toward an American aircraft carrier in the Arabian Sea. Iran subsequently confirmed nuclear talks with the US were scheduled in Muscat, and oil declined for the first time in three days on Wednesday as diplomacy appeared to temper the risk premium—though the American military build-up in the region continued. Bloomberg Opinion's John Authers argued through the week that markets are under-pricing a major oil shock, a view that sits uncomfortably with current term-structure pricing and suggests asymmetric risk should hostilities escalate toward the Strait of Hormuz. Some VLCC operators were reported to be accelerating transits through the chokepoint. Separately, the sanctioned-crude complex showed signs of structural stress: Iranian and Russian barrels were accumulating unsold, driven less by enforcement than by ample availability of legitimate alternatives at competitive prices—a development with meaningful implications for the marginal price of oil and for the discount structures available to remaining buyers, principally China.

Monetary Policy Divergence

The global monetary policy landscape continued to splinter. Australia's RBA delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, while the ECB and BOE both held and signaled residual easing bias. In the US, the Fed succession dynamic introduced questions about the institution's reaction function under incoming Chair Warsh. The divergence argues for sustained FX volatility: AUD appears supported on rate differential grounds, while sterling faces competing pressures from a dovish BOE and domestic political risk. Gold's persistence above $5,000/oz while Bitcoin fell to roughly half its October peak reinforces that bullion—not crypto—is absorbing the safe-haven and dollar-diversification flows tied to US fiscal and policy concerns.

Trade Fragmentation and the Gulf Nexus

Against the backdrop of US tariff escalation reshaping traditional trade corridors, Gulf Cooperation Council economies emerged as a notable beneficiary. GCC trade expanded at 15% annually from 2021–24 versus a 9% global rate; Asia-Middle East flows are projected to reach $1.5 trillion by 2030 from $905 billion in 2024. The UAE imported approximately 560,000 Chinese passenger vehicles in 2025—third globally—reflecting its role as a transshipment hub for Chinese manufactured goods navigating Western tariff exposure. Britain signaled a GCC free-trade deal was imminent. Competition between DP World and ADQ-owned Khalifa Port for Gulf logistics primacy is intensifying, with France's CMA CGM joining MSC as joint-venture partners at Khalifa. The durability of Gulf trade dynamism ultimately rests on whether energy-funded fiscal expansion can be sustained, as Saudi Arabia has already scaled back the more ambitious elements of Vision 2030.
