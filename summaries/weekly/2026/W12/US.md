• The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.5%–3.75% on Wednesday, with Chair Powell signaling cuts are increasingly distant and colleagues raising the possibility of a hike — generating the S&P 500's largest Fed-day decline since 2024.
• US gasoline averaged $3.70 per gallon as of Sunday March 16, up roughly 27% since hostilities began February 28, with diesel surpassing $5 per gallon for the first time since December 2022.
• Futures markets eliminated all expectations for 2026 rate cuts by Friday, a dramatic reversal from the two cuts priced at the week's open.
• Super Micro tumbled 22% on Friday after federal prosecutors charged a co-founder with illegally diverting Nvidia-powered servers to China; FedEx climbed roughly 10% after raising its earnings forecast.
• The Fed's institutional standing came under pressure on multiple fronts: a prolonged Powell succession dispute, a Justice Department challenge to his authority, and a Supreme Court-linked tariff refund process still unresolved.

**Monetary Policy**
The Federal Reserve's Wednesday meeting was, by some margin, the most consequential in years. The FOMC voted 11-1 to hold the benchmark rate at 3.5%–3.75%, but the surrounding communication was unmistakably hawkish. Powell acknowledged "meaningful" internal movement toward fewer cuts and explicitly noted that some colleagues had raised the possibility of a rate hike — a formulation that rattled markets. The Fed's median year-end headline inflation estimate was revised upward to 2.7% from 2.4%. Core PCE was running at an estimated 3% in February, a full percentage point above target, and February producer price data showed core PPI rising to a three-year high. As Carl Weinberg of High Frequency Economics observed, the pipeline was already accelerating before the Strait of Hormuz blockade added a separate inflationary layer. By Friday, futures markets had fully eliminated expectations for any rate reductions this year. The 10-year Treasury yield rose to approximately 4.30% by week's end.

**Institutional Uncertainty**
The Fed's policy challenge was compounded by an unusually fraught succession picture. Powell confirmed he will not vacate his board seat — which runs to January 2028 — until a Justice Department investigation into the Fed's building renovation project concludes. Nominee Kevin Warsh faces a Senate confirmation hold from Senator Thom Tillis on the same grounds. The result is a protracted, politically charged transition at the central bank at precisely the moment when clarity of purpose is most valuable. Separately, top Justice Department officials are rallying behind a US attorney's challenge to Powell's authority, with the White House declining to oppose the effort — a development whose implications for central bank independence remain to be seen but warrant close monitoring.

**Energy and the Consumer**
The transmission from oil prices to household budgets is now clearly visible in the data. SMBC Nikko Securities estimates the gasoline price increase could reduce the personal saving rate by 60 basis points to approximately 3.9%. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to a three-month low in the March preliminary reading. January consumer spending was essentially flat even before the energy spike, and households no longer hold pandemic-era savings buffers. Oxford Economics cautioned that higher energy costs threaten to suppress spending decisions materially in coming months. The political arithmetic is uncomfortable: gasoline at levels last seen in 2023, tariff-driven price pressures still in the pipeline, and a midterm election cycle approaching. President Trump called for an emergency rate cut — a move associated historically with acute crises — while Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent floated lifting sanctions on approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian crude already at sea, a measure analysts regard as operationally limited given virtually all such supply flows to Chinese refiners.

**Corporate Developments**
The week produced a notably mixed corporate tape. Nvidia's annual developer conference was constructive, with CEO Jensen Huang projecting $1 trillion in AI processor sales through 2027, though his preference for copper interconnects over fiber optics weighed on photonics names. Meta gained roughly 3% in premarket Monday on reports of a $27 billion cloud infrastructure deal and a planned workforce reduction exceeding 20%. Lululemon fell approximately 2% after forecasting a second consecutive year of profit declines. Micron Technology, whose market capitalization has risen from roughly $100 billion at end-2024 to approximately $520 billion, reported after Wednesday's close. On Friday, Goldman Sachs's trading desk noted clients who anticipated a quick resolution to the Iran conflict are increasingly doubtful, with some positioning for either a sharp correction or a prolonged 2022-style grind lower.
