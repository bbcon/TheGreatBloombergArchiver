The European Central Bank moved to the forefront of the monetary policy debate as the Iran conflict reshapes the eurozone's inflation and growth calculus. ECB Governing Council member Peter Kazimir stated explicitly that the war risks forcing the central bank to raise rates sooner than previously anticipated, while colleague François Villeroy de Galhau struck a slightly more measured tone, noting that next week's meeting is not the right moment for action but that policymakers will not permit inflation to become entrenched. ECB President Christine Lagarde separately pledged that the bank would act to prevent the conflict from inflicting the same inflationary pain on the eurozone as Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine — a signal that the institution is monitoring energy pass-through effects closely, even as the precise monetary response remains undetermined.

The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 1.13% as markets processed renewed disruptions to Middle Eastern shipping. Rheinmetall shares declined 4.5% after the German defense contractor guided below analyst sales expectations for 2026, a notable miss given the broader European defense spending cycle. In the automotive sector, Porsche rose 2.1% after incoming CEO Michael Leiters outlined plans to introduce models above the 911 to support margins — though the broader picture for German automakers in China remains challenging, with Porsche's China sales slumping as domestic electric vehicle manufacturers capture an increasing share of the premium segment. Mercedes-Benz separately unveiled a luxury electric van targeting Chinese consumers, illustrating the competitive pressure incumbents face in their most important growth market.

In the UK, London Underground commuters face twelve days of strike action beginning later this month, with the RMT union opposing Transport for London's proposal to allow drivers to volunteer for a four-day working week. Chancellor Rachel Reeves declined to speculate on the fiscal implications of the Iran war, while the Bank of England confirmed that historical figures on banknotes — including Winston Churchill and Jane Austen — will be replaced by native British wildlife.
