We seem to be plagued by conflict and geopolitical tensions. 

Pakistan Defense Minister Khawaja Asif declared “open war 
<https://bloom.bg/4aRP4ho>” with Afghanistan after both sides carried out 
cross-border strikes overnight that killed dozens. Pakistan’s military targeted 
“defense locations” in the Afghan capital Kabul as well as in the border 
provinces of Kandahar and Paktia, killing more than 130 Afghan Taliban 
“operatives,” according to Islamabad’s Information Minister Attaullah Tarar.

Tensions have steadily risen between the neighbors since the return of the 
Afghan Taliban to power in 2021, with Islamabad blaming Kabul for hosting 
militant groups that planattacks in Pakistan <https://bloom.bg/4sg50kB>. The 
Taliban deny the accusation. The sides have failed to extend a truce brokered 
by Qatar and Turkey in October last year.

Meanwhile, US and Iranian officials ended the latest round of nuclear talks in 
Geneva on Thursday by agreeing to reconvene as soon as next week, opening the 
door to further diplomacy even as President Donald Trumpmasses military forces 
<https://bloom.bg/4kTIqM4> in the region.

Oil rose. While some of the heat has come out of the oil market this week, 
prices remain higher <https://bloom.bg/3ZZnTMK> this year as concerns about a 
potential US strike on Iran help to offset broader glut expectations. Traders 
will be keenly watching a scheduled OPEC+ supply meeting on Sunday, as conflict 
risks cloud the outlook.—Stephanie Phang <https://bloom.bg/4gs6Wkp>



 What You Need to Know Today 



In less visceral shocks, Hong Kong’s proposal to pull HK$150 billion ($19 
billion) from afund used to defend the currency’s peg <https://bloom.bg/4aRP6Wy>
 with the greenback unsettled some officials and raised concern over the 
precedent it sets. There was widespread surprise in Hong Kong on Wednesday when 
Financial Secretary Paul Chan unveiled the plan to transfer cash from the 
city’s Exchange Fund to finance a giant technology hub project and other 
infrastructure works. It would be the first withdrawal since 1984.




The yuan ended its longest winning streak since 2010 as China moved to rein in 
the currency’s rise <https://bloom.bg/4cAMZsT> by scrapping an extra fee for 
betting against it in the derivatives market. The People’s Bank of China said 
it will remove the reserve requirement of 20% on foreign-currency forward 
contracts from March 2, lowering the cost for market participants to bet 
against the yuan via derivative contracts with banks. Even so,bets on a 
stronger yuan <https://bloom.bg/3ZWpFyb> are picking up in currency-option 
markets, with traders positioning for a move toward 6.50 per dollar by year end.
Chinese one-hundred yuan banknotes. Photographer: SeongJoon Cho/Bloomberg 



Chinese President Xi Jinping has a dilemma. America’s most formidable AI rival 
and home to the world’s largest labor force, China can’t afford to hobble 
itself in the race with Washington for AI capabilities that will underpin 
advanced manufacturing and technology critical to military advancement. At the 
same time, the Communist Party needs to ensure enough job creation to prevent 
social unrest.Read more here <https://bloom.bg/3OzpM09>. 




Japan’s top spokesperson condemned foreign influence operations, as alarm 
heightened <https://bloom.bg/4aT412R> after OpenAI reported a China-linked 
attempt to discredit Japan’s leader online. The alleged influence operation 
surfaced in mid-October, after Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi 
criticized human rights conditions in Inner Mongolia, according to the report.
Sanae Takaichi, Japan's prime minister. Photographer: Kiyoshi Ota/Bloomberg 



Mizuho Financial Group is planning to replace about 5,000 administrative jobs 
in Japan with AI over the next 10 years, as the country’s third-largest lender 
tries to boost productivity. “We are planning to enhance our earnings 
capabilities by shifting human resources to our focus areas byfully utilizing AI
 <https://bloom.bg/3N21WcV>,” Mizuho said. “It is not a headcount reduction.” 




The son of a reclusive oil merchant who dominated Indonesia’s fuel trade for 
decades received aprison sentence <https://bloom.bg/4ciD0bl> as part of a $17 
billion corruption case centered on the country’s state-owned energy firm. The 
sentencing is the most high-profile conviction yet in a sprawling graft probe 
into Pertamina and associated businesses. Muhamad Kerry Adrianto Riza was 
sentenced to 15 years in jail on Thursday by a panel of judges at the Central 
Jakarta District Court. He must also pay a fine and compensate the state for 
losses totaling 2.9 trillion rupiah ($173 million) or face his assets being 
seized.
Muhamad Kerry Adrianto Riza arrives for a sentencing hearing in Jakarta this 
week.Photographer: BAY ISMOYO/AFP 



Some good news: South Korea’s National Pension Service posted its 
strongest-ever annual return <https://bloom.bg/4ci7IkS> in 2025 as a 
semiconductor- and AI–driven stock rally boosted performance. One of the 
world’s largest public pension funds, it managed 1,458 trillion won ($1.02 
trillion) as of end-2025, returning 18.82% for the year to mark a third 
straight year of record gains. That surpassed its previous high-water mark of 
15% set a year earlier and the best result since its establishment in 1988.

 For Your Commute 



There aren’t many foods more synonymous with New York City than bagels. The 
chewy, dense texture — of authentic, well-made ones — and plethora of schmears 
and fillings are powering some ofthe Big Apple’s longest lines 
<https://bloom.bg/4aZj1wd> these days. It’s an obsession that’s spread from Los 
Angeles toLondon <https://bloom.bg/3MKfEB9> and back to Wall Street 
<https://bloom.bg/4tZzJUO>. Now, bagels have found a new, loyal fan base a half 
a world away in Singapore, where they’ve gone from curiosity to a fast-growing 
business model, drawing crowds, commanding premium prices and spawning a wave 
of locally adapted versions. Dive into this trend with Rachel Phuahere 
<https://bloom.bg/4r1ItXN>.
