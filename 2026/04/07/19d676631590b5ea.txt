Sunday’s ballot looks just as significant for the Kremlin.

After 16 years of Orban, polls indicate that voters have lost faith 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-03/hungary-s-orban-faces-new-setback-as-more-whistleblowers-emerge?cmpid=BBD040726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
 in their populist premier, a totemic figure for Donald Trump’s MAGA movement 
that shares an anti-immigration, EU-skeptic slant.

It’s one reason why this is also among the most consequential elections 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-05/serbia-s-vucic-says-explosive-found-at-gas-pipe-near-hungary?cmpid=BBD040726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
 the European Union has witnessed.

Orban has exposed the limits of the 27-nation bloc’s consensus-driven 
decision-making, regularly impeding member states from advancing a united 
agenda.
Orban and Russian President Vladimir Putin in Moscow in 2025.Photographer: 
Alexander Nemenov/AFP/Getty Images
Then there’s Russia’s war against Ukraine.

Orban’s government is obstructing all policies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/orban-and-his-rival-amass-supporters-for-pre-election-showdown?cmpid=BBD040726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
 favorable to Kyiv, including a critical €90 billion ($104 billion) loan. His 
election campaign portrays President Volodymyr Zelenskiy as an enemy of the 
state, and majors on bashing Brussels.

Last month, authorities seized currency that was being transferred from 
Austria to Ukraine via Hungary, which continues to import Russian energy while 
the EU phases it out.

Orban has long been seen as Europe’s most Russia-friendly leader. Relations 
may go beyond simply drawing from the Kremlin’s electoral playbook.

As we report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/orban-discusses-us-russia-summit-with-putin-call-transcript?cmpid=BBD040726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
, Orban told Vladimir Putin in October that he was willing to help Moscow in 
any way he could, including by hosting a summit to help settle the war in 
Ukraine.

In a brief call with the Russian president, he recalled a children’s story 
popular in Hungary. The Aesop fable involves amouse freeing a lion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/viktor-orban-offered-to-help-vladimir-putin-call-transcript-shows?cmpid=BBD040726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
 caught in a net after it had earlier spared the rodent’s life.

The revelation follows reports that Hungary’s foreign minister promised his 
Russian counterpart to help remove sanctions on the sister of a Russian 
billionaire, and regularly shared information about EU discussions with Moscow. 
He has dismissed the allegations.

As Hungary’s campaign enters its final stretch, there’s a lot resting on the 
outcome.

The head of Taiwan’s main opposition, the Kuomintang, started a six-day visit 
to China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/taiwan-opposition-head-touts-china-talks-on-first-trip-in-decade?cmpid=BBD040726_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>

bureaucracy since taking up the party chief post in 2024, cutting red tape and 
removing barriers to investment as the country seeks to sustain economic growth.
