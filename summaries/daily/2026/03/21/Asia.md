Japan and South Korea appear to be consolidating a diplomatic and economic rapprochement that would have seemed implausible as recently as a few years ago. Current South Korean President Lee Jae Myung and Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi — the latter a conservative nationalist by background — have prioritized pragmatic cooperation over historical grievances, a posture that analysts describe as driven by a convergent set of external pressures. China's economic coercion, including restricted Japanese access to rare earths and other critical inputs, has reinforced the logic of bilateral solidarity. The growing China-Russia-North Korea military alignment poses overlapping security challenges for both capitals.

The Trump administration has also served as an indirect catalyst. Washington's transactional posture — including tariffs on both allies, large investment pledge demands ($550 billion from Japan, $350 billion from South Korea), and public questioning of US security commitments — has, somewhat paradoxically, pushed Seoul and Tokyo closer together. As one analyst framed it, a more unpredictable United States in the Indo-Pacific has made an active Korea-Japan partnership a strategic necessity rather than a political choice. Last week, the two countries' finance ministers agreed to expand bilateral economic cooperation, including a review of their currency swap arrangement, currently sized at $10 billion.

Public opinion appears to be supportive of the new trajectory. Korean favorability toward Japan recently surpassed 56%, described as a record high, while a majority of Japanese hold positive views of South Korea. That symmetry reduces the domestic political cost of sustained engagement for both governments.

Separately, China began 2026 with its fastest pace of government spending growth since 2022, suggesting Beijing is leaning on fiscal stimulus to sustain economic momentum. Samsung meanwhile announced plans to deploy approximately $73 billion this year across chip capacity and research, underscoring continued investment intensity in the semiconductor sector even as the broader technology supply chain remains subject to geopolitical disruption.
