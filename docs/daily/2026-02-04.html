Wednesday, February 04, 2026

US

Markets and Tech Earnings in Focus

US equity futures traded mixed ahead of Alphabet's results after the close, with S&P 500 futures up 0.14% and Nasdaq 100 futures roughly flat. The 10-year Treasury yield ticked up to 4.28%. The session's most consequential moves were idiosyncratic: AMD fell 7.3% in pre-market after a sales forecast that disappointed investors hoping for a clearer AI payoff, while Chipotle declined 6.2% on a weak outlook. On the upside, Silicon Labs jumped 33% on a Financial Times report that Texas Instruments is in advanced talks to acquire the chip designer for roughly $7 billion, and Enphase rallied 21% on a revenue beat. The day's earnings docket also includes AbbVie, Eli Lilly, Uber, Qualcomm, and Snap.

Value Rotation Gains Traction

The Russell 1000 Value Index has advanced 8.6% since early November, outperforming its growth counterpart by 14 percentage points as software stocks have come under pressure on concerns AI may erode incumbent business models. Apollo's John Zito framed the question directly: "Is software dead?" The shift bears watching, though prior value-over-growth episodes of comparable magnitude (2022 and 2001) coincided with broader market drawdowns rather than durable regime change. Whether this rotation reflects healthy breadth or defensive positioning remains to be seen.

Fed Transition and Fiscal Backdrop

Kevin Warsh's incoming challenge as Fed Chair will be building consensus among colleagues, while Governor Stephen Miran has stepped down from his White House role. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent testifies before the House today. The partial government shutdown ended after President Trump signed a funding deal negotiated with Senate Democrats.

Affordability Pressures Persist

Despite real wage gains—average hourly earnings rose 3.8% in December, well above inflation—consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in over a decade. Goldman research highlights housing as the binding constraint: the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160-200% of income, from 90-120% in 2000. The disconnect between aggregate income data and household sentiment continues to carry political weight.

Asia

Australia leads global tightening cycle

The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered the first interest-rate increase among major central banks this year, positioning the country at the leading edge of a "splintering global policy outlook." Economists and markets are already pricing in a further hike, suggesting underlying inflation pressures — likely services-driven — have proven more persistent than the RBA had anticipated. The divergence is notable against a backdrop of disinflation in the euro area, where headline prices have sunk further below the ECB's 2% target, and points to potential support for AUD on widening rate differentials, though the implications for housing-sensitive consumption bear watching.

China — services firm, but tensions accumulate

Services activity picked up in January, and Beijing reiterated its commitment to building a "unified national market" to unlock domestic consumption — a recurring structural priority that has yet to translate into a durable demand-side rebound. Separately, a Panama court decision voiding CK Hutchison's contract for two ports drew a sharp response from Beijing, which warned of a "heavy price" to be paid. The episode underscores how port and logistics infrastructure has become a contested arena in US-China strategic competition. Domestically, a speculative gold rush — amplified by bullion's move back above $5,000/oz after a sharp recent correction — is testing official tolerance for retail risk-taking, with authorities mindful of any threat to social cohesion.

Selective decoupling, not full retreat

New NBER research from Alfaro and Chor finds China's share of US imports has fallen to roughly 9%, back to pre-WTO accession levels, with Vietnam, Mexico and Taiwan the principal beneficiaries. The reallocation has largely been a reshuffling among existing top-20 partners rather than a broader deglobalization — a meaningful nuance for Southeast Asian exporters positioning around tariff arbitrage.

Australia critical minerals

Canberra is weighing a price floor for rare earths and other critical minerals to counter Chinese dominance and attract investment — a policy lever whose effectiveness will depend on coordinated demand-side commitments from allied buyers.

Europe

Novo Nordisk shock dominates corporate tape

Shares of the Danish pharmaceutical giant plunged as much as 20% in Copenhagen after the company forecast a steep decline in sales, providing concrete evidence of an intensifying price war in the obesity drug market. The guidance suggests competitive dynamics in GLP-1s are deteriorating faster than the sell-side had modeled, with implications for the broader European healthcare complex and for Denmark's equity index, where Novo carries outsized weight. The scale of the move — among the largest single-day declines in the company's history — points to a meaningful reset of consensus assumptions around pricing power in the category.

UBS delivers shareholder return signal

UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat, reinforcing the constructive narrative around large-cap European banks. The capital return underscores management's confidence in integration progress following the Credit Suisse absorption, though investors will want to assess underlying revenue quality and the trajectory of cost synergies in subsequent disclosures.

Disinflation continues to run ahead of the ECB

Euro-area inflation sank further below the ECB's 2% target, sharpening the policy debate as the Governing Council weighs its next move. The undershoot complicates the recent communication that easing is largely complete, and markets will likely lean toward pricing additional accommodation if the trend persists into the spring projection round. The data sit uneasily alongside firmer activity signals elsewhere, and the Council's reaction function around target undershoots — versus overshoots in 2022-23 — remains an open question.

Energy diversification and Equinor

Chancellor Friedrich Merz is courting Middle Eastern suppliers to diversify German energy sources and reduce reliance on US LNG, a notable strategic pivot given recent transatlantic frictions. Separately, Equinor scaled back its buyback program to as much as $1.5 billion this year from $5 billion in 2025 after Q4 profit missed estimates on lower oil and gas prices — a reminder that European energy majors' capital return capacity remains highly sensitive to commodity prints.

Global

Middle East tensions keep a bid under crude, but markets remain sanguine

Brent advanced to around $68 a barrel for a second consecutive session after the US Navy shot down an Iranian drone heading toward an American aircraft carrier in the Arabian Sea. Oil swung between gains and losses through the session as traders balanced geopolitical risk against a reported sharp drawdown in US inventories. Notably, Bloomberg Opinion's John Authers argued that markets are under-pricing the probability of a major oil shock — a view that sits uncomfortably with current term-structure pricing and suggests asymmetric risk should hostilities escalate toward the Strait of Hormuz. Separately, discounts on Russian Urals to Indian refiners have widened to more than $10 below Brent over the past 10 days, testing New Delhi's commitment to a US trade agreement predicated on lower Russian purchases.

Monetary policy divergence is widening

The Reserve Bank of Australia has delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, with markets and economists positioning for a further increase — the first tightening cycle of the year globally. The contrast with the euro area is stark: HICP has sunk further below the ECB's 2% target, sharpening the debate over the next move. In the US, Kevin Warsh's challenge as incoming Fed chair will be building internal consensus, while Governor Stephen Miran has stepped down from his White House role. The splintering policy backdrop argues for sustained FX volatility and continued cross-market dispersion.

Gold extended its rebound

above $5,000/oz for a second day as dip-buyers returned following the recent collapse, while Michael Burry warned of a self-reinforcing "death spiral" risk in Bitcoin, down roughly 40% from October highs. The divergence reinforces the thesis that bullion — not crypto — is absorbing safe-haven flows tied to deficit and policy concerns.

Climate-energy transition

Germany's Merz is courting Middle East suppliers to dilute LNG dependence on the US, and Australia is weighing a critical-minerals price floor to counter Chinese dominance — both incremental but directionally meaningful for supply-chain realignment.
