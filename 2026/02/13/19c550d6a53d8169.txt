Tensions With Japan Have Chinese Skiers Slaloming on Home Slopes
Skiers are hitting runs closer to home due to tensions with Japan

Hi, this is Karoline Kan in Singapore, where many people are planning a ski 
trip to Japan during the upcoming Lunar New Year holiday.

Normally, Chinese tourists would also be making plans to hit Hokkaido for ski 
season. But this year is a bit different.

After Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi said a Chinese invasion of Taiwan 
could trigger a military response by Tokyo, Beijing warned people against 
traveling to her country. Read aboutChina-Japan ties after Takaichi’s big win 
here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/xi-faces-dilemma-over-japan-after-takaichi-wins-historic-mandate?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

That call has resonated with many people 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-20/chinese-visitors-to-japan-slump-45-in-december-amid-dispute?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 in China. “As an ordinary Chinese person, I can’t do much but can at least 
cancel my skiing trip to protest,” said Chen Yang, a 42-year-old who with her 
family has often traveled from the southern province of Guangdong to Japan for 
skiing.

She’s not alone. Bookings to Sapporo are expected to slide 62% in the first 
two months of 2026, according to China Trading Desk, a marketing and research 
firm. Beidahu, a top resort area in the northeastern province of Jilin, saw 
hotel reservations jump as much as 70%.

The shift plays into Beijing’s long-term strategy. In 2014, Chinese leader Xi 
Jinping pledged to involve 300 million of the nation’s people in winter sports, 
treating ice and snow asan economic opportunity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/chinese-factory-deflation-eases-again-but-consumer-prices-cool?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

The push accelerated after the 2022 Winter Olympics in Beijing, backed by 
heavy state investment and policy support. (By the way,check out our Milano 
Cortina Winter Olympics medal tracker here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/milano-cortina-2026-winter-olympics-medal-count/?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.)

Three decades ago, China had just nine ski venues and roughly 10,000 skiers. 
Today, the industry is on track to top 1 trillion yuan ($140 billion) in size — 
about four times its 2015 level. The country has built roughly 900 ski resorts 
and venues, including 66 indoor slopes, largely in the past decade.

Beidahu and Songhuahu, also in Jilin, each attracted more than 1 million 
visits in the 2025 season for the first time. That milestone has been achieved 
by only a small fraction of resorts around the world, most of them in the Alps 
and North America. Read moreabout China’s ski scene here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/china-s-ski-slopes-pull-big-crowds-as-japan-tensions-run-high?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

Behind the surge is heavy state backing. Xi himself is one of the most visible 
champions of winter sports, linking them to public health, culture and economic 
growth. “Ice and snow are as valuable as gold and silver mountains,” he has 
said, echoing an earlier message about environmental protection.

The economic logic is straightforward. Skiers tend to splash more cash and 
stay longer than other tourists. They also often travel in groups, amplifying 
their spending power.

“Ski tourism stimulates multiple sectors, from hotels and dining to transport 
and sightseeing,” said Zhibin Lu, director of Summitski Tour, a travel platform.

But China has some work to do if it wants to match other top ski destinations. 
For one thing, its resorts are often overcrowded, causing long lines and 
crowded bunny slopes.

Runs are so busy “you don’t even want to ski and couldn’t if you tried,” one 
social media user named Crystal wrote on the Instagram-like Xiaohongshu 
platform after a trip to a resort in Jilin.
Snowboarders at a resort in Zhangjiakou, Hebei province, in 2022.Photographer: 
Andrea Verdelli/Bloomberg
Perhaps most importantly, China lacks the natural snowfall that gives Hokkaido 
its famed powder — “Japow <https://www.skiing-hokkaido.com/why-japow/>” — 
forcing most resorts to rely heavily on artificial snow. That’s a big turnoff 
for dedicated winter sports types.

“In the long run, serious skiers will demand more,” said Jiang Shenli, founder 
of Xueyanshe, a platform that connects tens of thousands of customers with 
skiing-related services.

Takaichi’s historic election victory 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-08/historic-win-pits-takaichi-s-vision-of-japan-against-the-markets?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 has put her in a strong political position for likely years to come, meaning 
she can continue to ignore Beijing’s demand to retract her comments about 
Taiwan. Beijing may eventually have a reason to seek a détente with her but 
that’ll likely take some time.

Until then, expect Chinese skiers to hit the home slopes on future holidays 
like this Lunar New Year.

“I cannot say for sure what I will do next year,” said Chen, the Guangdong 
resident. “What I know is I am sure there will be more young Chinese people 
skiing, and the majority of them will ski at home.”

Slimming Down

US Treasuries have long been regarded as a beacon of stability in a troubled 
world. But as far as China — once the world’s biggest holder of such debt — is 
concerned, that may be less true than in the past.

Beijing has quietly halved its holdings of Treasuries over the past 
decade-plus, and itrecently advised financial institutions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-02-10/why-china-is-urging-banks-to-limit-us-treasury-holdings-video?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 to rein in their holdings of US sovereign debt. Citing concern over 
concentration risks and market volatility, regulators also instructed banks 
with high exposure to pare down their positions, according to people familiar 
with the matter. Read moreabout that move by Chinese officials here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-09/china-urges-banks-to-limit-holdings-of-us-treasuries-citing-market-volatility?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

The guidance seems to reflect growing wariness among officials that large 
holdings of US government debt may expose banks to sharp swings, the people 
said. The institutions held about $298 billion worth of dollar-denominated 
bonds as of September. It’s unclear how much of that was in Treasuries.

The move was framed around diversifying market risk rather than anything to do 
with geopolitical maneuvering or a fundamental loss of confidence inUS 
creditworthiness 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-19/us-lost-moody-s-aaa-credit-rating-what-s-at-stake-for-markets?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

Still, it comes with China and the US at odds over a range of issues, testing 
a one-year trade truce 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/xi-holds-phone-call-with-trump-xinhua-reports?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
 reached by their leaders in October.

“The whole idea of lending to the government of your primary adversary should 
no longer be welcomed in Beijing,” said Stephen Jen, co-founder of London-based 
Eurizon SLJ Capital.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-02-10/why-china-is-urging-banks-to-limit-us-treasury-holdings-video>

Global investors are also increasingly questioning Washington’s fiscal 
discipline 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-02/what-caused-the-2026-government-shutdown-what-are-its-effects?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
, President Donald Trump’s commitment to a strong dollar and thecontinued 
independence of the Federal Reserve 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-12/why-fed-independence-matters-for-interest-rates-and-the-us-economy?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

Recently a $25 billion Danish pension fund announced it was exiting the $100 
million it had held in US Treasuries, while a Dutch fund said it had cut its 
holdings by about €10 billion to €19 billion in the six months through 
September. Outside Europe, India’s holdings have dropped to a five-year low, 
while Brazil’s long-term holdings have also declined.

Even as some investors talk about selling America 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-02/sell-america-trade-why-investors-are-questioning-us-assets?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
, there’s little sign of panic in the market over a wave of Treasuries sales by 
foreigners or a widespread loss of confidence in the traditional safe haven 
asset. A measure of Treasury volatility, for instance, has fallen to a 
five-year low.

And in explaining why Treasuries recovered after some initial selling, traders 
took the Chinese guidance as the continuation of a trend that started around 
2013, the year Xi became China’s president.

The nation’s stockpile fell to $683 billion in November, the lowest since 
2008. Once the world’s largest creditor to the US, China was overtaken by Japan 
in 2019 and by the UK last year. Read moreabout China’s long retreat from 
Treasuries here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/china-s-years-long-retreat-from-us-treasuries-flags-bigger-risks?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

Beijing may well continue reducing its Treasury holdings but investors don’t 
expect it to do so aggressively.

“The bond market is taking the view that China won’t do this,” said Kathleen 
Brooks, research director at XTB, “and if they do reduce the size of their 
Treasury holdings they will do this in a slow and gradual way.”

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

A blond, smirking boy wizard from J.K. Rowling’s popular Harry Potter stories 
is making a comeback in China as an unlikely mascot for the upcoming Lunar New 
Year.

The sneering face of Draco Malfoy, a spoiled bully and antagonist in the 
fantasy franchise, has surprisingly popped up on festive decorations as China 
prepares to usher inthe Year of the Horse 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/chinese-dining-chain-shuts-ahead-of-lunar-new-year-shares-slump?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
.

The craze is due to some creative play on the name Malfoy. In Mandarin, it 
works out to: “Ma er fu” — the characters for “horse” “you” and “fortune.” 
Taken together, it basically means: “In the Year of the Horse, you shall all be 
blessed.”

The Harry Potter franchise of movies and books has been hugely popular in China
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-02-11/warner-bros-plans-harry-potter-attraction-in-shanghai-in-2027?cmpid=BBD021326_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260213>
, selling millions of books and producing box office hits. It has attracted a 
fan base that spans generations.

That helps explain why Chinese social media users have shared snapshots of 
their homes and offices adorned with red signs featuring Malfoy’s slightly 
mischievous grin.
A Lunar New Year poster of Draco Malfoy in a shopping mall in Henan province 
on Feb. 5.Photographer: AFP/Getty Images
On the nation’s e-commerce sites, quick-thinking entrepreneurs have rushed to 
sell Malfoy-themed New Year merchandise like fridge magnets, keychains and 
phone cases. Some depict the baddie riding a cartoon horse.

Tom Felton, who played Malfoy in the Harry Potter films 
<https://www.imdb.com/list/ls066314338/>, seems to be getting a kick out of his 
character’s return to the stage in China. He reposted an Instagram story 
hailing Malfoy as a “symbol of Chinese New Year in China,” complete with a 
photo of him beaming from a giant banner in a shopping mall.

China’s internet users have also been amused. “Does Hogwarts celebrate Chinese 
New Year too?” one user quipped on Xiaohongshu, referring to the school for 
witchcraft and wizardry in the franchise.
