The first major heat wave 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/deadly-heat-wave-breaks-may-records-across-northwest-europe?cmpid=BBD052626_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4843>
 of the season has broken temperature records across northwest Europe, 
triggered water shortages in the UK and has been linked to several deaths in 
France.

Driven by a persistent high-pressure heat dome, the scorching conditions are 
expected to continue today before easing tomorrow, according to meteorologists 
and weather models.

The UK and France recorded their hottest May days on Monday, with temperatures 
in London and Paris forecast to potentially reach 35C and 33C on Tuesday, 
according to the UK Met Office and Météo-France.
 <https://x.com/metoffice/status/2058939633832985032?s=20>
In France, the heat wave has been directly linked to at least two deaths, 
government spokeswoman Maud Bregeon said. At least five people died from 
drowning, while others died from heat-related causes during sporting events, 
she said.

Hourly power prices in the country traded below zero 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/french-power-prices-jump-as-hot-weather-spurs-nuclear-concerns?cmpid=BBD052626_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4843>
 around 1 p.m. today on Epex Spot due to an abundance of solar power. But 
concerns that scorching summer temperatures will lead to lower hydro 
availability and potential nuclear curtailment weighed on month-ahead prices, 
which jumped as much as 11.7% today, to the highest level since late March, 
according to data from EEX.

The weather system has pushed away cloud cover across a wide swath of the UK, 
leading to unusually sunny skies that have further intensified the heat while 
also boosting solar power generation. At its peak around midday on Sunday, 
solar met almost half of the UK’s electricity demand — the highest-ever, 
according to data from NESO.

Amber alerts for high temperatures are in effect for western Spain, where 
temperatures could reach 36C today, according to Aemet. Between Wednesday and 
Friday, the southern basins of Guadiana and Guadalquivir are forecast to reach 
between 36C and 38C, while the Ebro basin could locally hit 40C.

In India, dangerously torrid daily heat has long been a climate warning and a 
concern. Now the country is increasingly dealing withsweltering nights too 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/india-s-scorching-nights-strain-power-grid-and-threaten-health?cmpid=BBD052626_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4843>
, as global warming and poor urban planning put millions at risk.

For eight of the past 10 years, average minimum temperatures during March to 
May — a proxy for nighttime heat over the pre-monsoon season — have been higher 
than the long-term average, according to data from the India Meteorological 
Department. Only 2020 and 2023 bucked the trend. Earlier this month, the IMD 
warned this summer would again see above-normal minimum temperatures across 
India.

“Sustained high temperatures through the night are becoming a bigger cause of 
heat exhaustion than the day-time heat,” said Rohit Magotra, a director at New 
Delhi-based think-tank Integrated Research and Action for Development, or 
IRADe, who advises the Indian government on heat mitigation. “Especially for 
people without access to adequate water and consistent, affordable cooling.”

But hotter nights are also the consequence of so-called urban heat islands, 
when concrete in densely packed neighborhoods absorbs daytime solar radiation 
and slowly releases the trapped heat at nights, pushing the indoor temperatures 
above the outside readings. In the poorest areas of India’s cities, inhabitants 
often work outdoors by day — and now struggle to recuperate in the evening.

“Climate change is driving baseline temperatures higher, but the way we build 
our cities is what traps that heat,” said Vishwas Chitale, team lead for 
climate resilience at the New Delhi-based Council on Energy, Environment and 
Water. “More than half of extreme heat in India cities is driven by rapid 
concretization and expanding road networks.”

Clean power ripe for ‘smart money’

By Ishika Mookerjee 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUJMYcOXODs/ishika-mookerjee?cmpid=BBD052626_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4843>

Renewables are poised to attract fresh, large-scale investment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/clean-energy-trade-ripe-for-smart-money-singapore-envoy-says?cmpid=BBD052626_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260526&utm_content=4843>
 as energy security and supply chain concerns drive an increase in demand and 
revenue for the sector, according to Singapore’s chief climate diplomat.

Growing global demand for inputs into the clean energy industry could cause 
price increases in the coming years, “unless industrial capacity keeps pace,” 
Climate Action Ambassador Ravi Menon told Bloomberg News on Friday.
Ravi Menon, Singapore’s ambassador for climate actionPhotographer: Lionel 
Ng/Bloomberg
“You need a price signal and then capital and investments will flow into this 
and the supply will then start to catch up. In fact, the smart money should 
already be investing in that now, knowing that there’s going to be demand and 
prices are going to go up,” the former central banker said. ‘Smart money’ 
typically refers to institutional investors and hedge funds.

Renewables demand is set to surge as fossil-fuel dependent countries scramble 
to boost their energy independence against a backdrop of prolonged oil and gas 
disruptions due to the Iran war. Already, South Korea and the Philippines are 
among those looking to fast-track such projects, and the S&P Global Clean 
Energy Transition Index has climbed 37% this year, outperforming the 30% gain 
in the S&P Global Oil Index.
