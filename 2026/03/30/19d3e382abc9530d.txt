Worried about growth

In the bond market, some investors are saying the surge in yields has gone far 
enough. They’re shifting their focus from the threat of inflation from the Iran 
war tothe prospect of an economic slowdown 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-29/jpmorgan-pimco-say-bond-market-is-underestimating-slowdown-risk?cmpid=033026_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260330>
.

Money managers at Pimco, JPMorgan Chase and Columbia Threadneedle Investments, 
among others,are preparing for an economic hit that will eventually trigger a 
bond-market rebound and cause yields to come sliding back down.

The sudden shift in sentiment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-30/bonds-everywhere-are-rallying-as-global-slowdown-fears-intensify?cmpid=033026_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260330>
 comes after the war’s oil price shock pushed the yield on the 10-year US 
Treasury note above 4.4% last week. In emerging markets, too, some fund 
managers arestepping in to buy bonds 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-29/emerging-markets-rout-lures-contrarians-betting-on-rate-cuts?cmpid=033026_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260330>
 on the view that central banks, rather than raise interest rates, will instead 
have to cut them to stave off a growth shock.

“Every day that this conflict persists brings us closer to the market being 
forced to consider the more negative implications for growth, which should 
ultimately push Treasury yields lower,” said Kelsey Berro at JPMorgan Asset 
Management. “Yields broadly have risen enough to be attractive.” —Michael 
MacKenzie <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVgVy7_JF34/michael-mackenzie> and 
Ye Xie <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AOaIfVMEEws/ye-xie>
