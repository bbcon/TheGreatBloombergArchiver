**Energy and Shipping**
The Houthi strikes on Saudi tankers in the Red Sea represent a meaningful escalation, extending the Iran conflict's disruption from the Strait of Hormuz — the primary crude chokepoint — to Bab el-Mandeb, through which container traffic flows to the Suez Canal. Bloomberg Intelligence estimates a sustained Red Sea blockade could lift energy shipping rates by at least 30%. Container lines including Maersk and Hapag-Lloyd, which had begun cautiously restoring Red Sea routing, may be forced back onto the longer Cape of Good Hope diversion. DP World's concurrent announcement of new deepwater terminals on the UAE's Gulf of Oman coast — explicitly designed to bypass Hormuz — illustrates how infrastructure investment is beginning to adapt to a structurally more dangerous maritime environment.

**Monetary Policy Divergence**
The global rates picture continues to diverge in ways consistent with the "new macro regime" described by Moody's: Japanese two-year yields at their highest since 1995, US 30-year yields at their longest sustained run above 5% since 2007, and UK 30-year yields near multi-decade highs. The common thread is structurally higher inflation expectations and elevated investment demand, which is narrowing fiscal space and compressing the room governments have to absorb future shocks. The ECB's hold today, against this backdrop, reflects caution rather than confidence that the cycle is complete.
