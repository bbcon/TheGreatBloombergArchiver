India occupied the foreground of Asia-Pacific developments this week, hosting two major international gatherings that together illustrated the country's growing ambitions — and the tensions embedded within them. The fourth annual global AI summit concluded in New Delhi, the first such event held in the Global South. With attendance from OpenAI's Sam Altman, DeepMind's Demis Hassabis, and French President Macron, among others, the summit underscored India's convening power and its attractiveness as a market, given that more than 100 million Indians use ChatGPT weekly, second only to the United States. Prior to the summit, India had secured approximately $5 billion in venture capital commitments and $140 billion in AI infrastructure pledges; by its conclusion, those figures had risen to at least $17 billion and $270 billion respectively — a material step toward the government's AI sovereignty agenda.

India's AI sovereignty strategy, articulated by Electronics Minister Ashwini Vaishnaw, rests on several pillars: development of small language models calibrated to the Indian market, construction of shared compute facilities for startups and students, domestic semiconductor design and eventual manufacturing, and active attraction of foreign capital for data center infrastructure. The underlying concern driving these ambitions is strategic dependency — the risk that foreign control over cloud and AI infrastructure could be interrupted, whether by corporate decision or government directive.

Separately, India held its first major national climate conference in Mumbai, where former US Secretary of State Hillary Clinton urged the country to assume greater global leadership on emissions reduction in the absence of US federal engagement under the Trump administration. The call comes at a moment of genuine ambiguity: India remains the world's third-largest emitter, coal accounts for nearly three-quarters of power generation, and the Modi government missed its 2025 UN deadline to submit updated emissions-cutting plans. Investment in renewables is accelerating, but policy clarity remains elusive, and proposals to sanction new coal plant construction through 2047 sit uneasily alongside the government's 2070 net-zero target.
