Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Robert Tuttle looks at the implications of competing oil pipeline 
projects in North America.

US-Canada relations have become fraught during President Donald Trump’s second 
term. But when it comes to oil, the countries appear to be growing more 
interdependent than ever, much to the chagrin of the Canadian government.

Last week, Trump signed a presidential permit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/trump-signs-permit-for-pipeline-from-canada-echoing-keystone-xl?cmpid=BBBXT050826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4281>
 authorizing the Bridger Pipeline expansion. The project is designed to 
eventually carry about 1.1 million barrels a day of Canadian crude to Wyoming, 
very much like the infamous and now defunct Keystone XL pipeline, which was 
canceled by President Joe Biden five years ago.

Though the US is the world’s largest oil producer, its refineries still rely 
on shipments from north of the border to meet all of their needs. If the 
Bridger project gets built, it would lock in supplies from the Canadian oil 
sands for years, if not decades, cementing the two countries’ energy 
integration.

However, it would also undermine the viability of a rival pipeline planned by 
oil-rich Alberta to carry 1 million barrels a day over the Rockies to Canada’s 
Pacific coast.

America’s imports of energy from Canada are exempt from trade tariffs Trump 
imposed last year. Still, Prime Minister Mark Carney wants to boost exports of 
all kinds to countries other than the US, and he’s backing the Alberta pipeline 
plan.
A heavy hauler truck drives through the Athabasca oil sands near Fort 
McMurray, Alberta.Photographer: Ben Nelms/Bloomberg
Even before Bridger’s permit, the Canadian project already faced significant 
challenges.

It’s opposed by some Indigenous communities in British Columbia and the 
province’s premier. Also at issue are concessions Alberta made in return for 
Carney’s support: a higher industrial carbon tax and the roll-out of carbon 
storage in the oil sands.

That’s not gone over well with some domestic oil producers. Carbon storage 
amounts to an expense with little benefit for the companies, while the carbon 
tax will drive investment to other countries, Jon McKenzie, the boss of 
Calgary-based Cenovus Energy Inc., said recently.

The Canadian pipeline isn’t necessarily doomed, according to Heather 
Exner-Pirot <bbg://people/profile/25428555>, director of energy, natural 
resources and environment at the Macdonald-Laurier Institute. But it may get 
delayed until new oil sands capacity comes online.

“I still think, eventually in the 2030s, we’ll need this pipeline,” she said. 
“We just pushed it back four years.”

—Robert Tuttle, Bloomberg News
