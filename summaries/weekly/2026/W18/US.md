**Fed Leadership and Policy**
The Federal Reserve's April meeting will be remembered primarily for its institutional texture rather than its rate decision, which was, as widely anticipated, a hold. Four FOMC members dissented — three regional presidents (Beth Hammack, Neel Kashkari, and Lorie Logan) objecting to any residual easing bias in the forward guidance, and Stephen Miran on separate grounds — marking the most divided vote since October 1992. Two-year Treasury yields jumped 11 basis points on Wednesday, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, while 30-year yields reached 5% for the first time this year. Futures markets have fully removed any 2026 easing from their base case, with roughly 50% odds now assigned to a hike by April 2027. The hawkish tilt reflects a committee increasingly focused on the inflationary consequences of sustained elevated energy prices, a dynamic that sits in direct tension with the rate cuts the White House has sought.

The leadership transition adds a further layer of complexity. Powell announced he will remain on the board as a governor after Warsh assumes the chair role, until the Justice Department's building-renovation investigation is resolved. Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized this as a violation of Fed norms, though the precedent set by Marriner Eccles somewhat undercuts the framing. Warsh will inherit a deeply divided committee, an unresolved inflation picture, and the unusual presence of his predecessor as a sitting board member — circumstances that may constrain his ability to shift policy direction quickly, whatever his inclinations.

**Equity Markets and Earnings**
The week's earnings slate served as a meaningful stress test for the AI-driven valuation narrative. Alphabet's Wednesday results were received as a genuine positive, with shares gaining more than 7% as its custom TPU chip architecture was interpreted as a credible competitive challenge to Nvidia in AI inference workloads — adding $421 billion to market capitalization in a single session, the second-largest single-day market cap gain on record for any company. Amazon's AWS unit posted 28% revenue growth, its fastest in over three years. Meta's reaction, by contrast, was negative: despite advertising revenue of $56.3 billion, the company's decision to raise 2026 capex guidance by $10 billion to potentially above $145 billion was read by investors as prioritizing long-run AI buildout over near-term returns. The combined 2026 capex envelope for the four hyperscalers now approaches $725 billion. Microsoft's Azure growth was viewed as underwhelming, with shares slipping modestly. Apple's above-consensus third-quarter revenue forecast on Thursday provided a constructive bookend to the week.

NXP Semiconductors surged 16% on Wednesday premarket on an upbeat revenue forecast, and Seagate rallied 18% on strong AI-related storage demand. The Philadelphia Semiconductor index extended to 18 consecutive positive sessions — the longest winning streak in the index's history. That streak reflects genuine earnings momentum: with slightly over a third of S&P 500 companies having reported, the beat rate stands at approximately 80% against a historical average of 77%, while the aggregate earnings surprise of roughly 12% materially exceeds the typical 7% premium. Societe Generale estimates 12-month forward profits are now approximately $600 billion higher than four months ago, though semiconductors alone account for roughly 43% of that revision — a concentration that amplifies both the upside and the tail risk.

**Monetary Policy and Fiscal Dynamics**
The Fed's hawkish hold creates an environment in which the bond market may increasingly act as the economy's primary tightening mechanism. Thirty-year Treasury yields pressing toward and briefly touching 5% on Thursday carry direct consequences for mortgage rates, corporate refinancing costs, and the fiscal burden on a government running structural deficits. Fitch separately warned that the US debt burden remains far above other AA-rated sovereigns, a reminder that fiscal sustainability concerns have not been resolved by strong nominal growth. Congressional Republicans are advancing proposals to increase Pentagon spending by 44% — a rare act of intra-party friction with the White House — and to lower capital gains taxes, a combination that complicates the budget arithmetic further. In credit markets, investors showed early signs of fatigue after a $300 billion AI-related debt binge, with demands emerging for better pricing and stronger covenant protections. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to 49.8 in April, its lowest reading since data collection began in 1978, a reminder that the macro backdrop outside equity markets remains more fragile than headline index levels suggest.
