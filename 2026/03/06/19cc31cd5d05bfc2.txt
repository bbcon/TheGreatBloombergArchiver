Supply-Shock Decade

This has become the decade of supply shocks, from Covid to the Russia-Ukraine 
war to mercantilist trade actions to, now, a Middle East regional war. With the
global energy market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/iran-war-how-oil-prices-are-surging-as-hormuz-shipping-production-disrupted?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 upended, central banks are confronted again with the question of whether to 
mind the inflation or the growth impact.

Policymakers are united on one thing: It will all depend on how long the war, 
and the constriction of oil and gas shipments it’s caused, will last. But that 
hasn’t stopped people from making bets. Investors havesold off 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/us-treasuries-extend-fall-as-iran-war-fuels-global-bond-rout?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 government securities on the risk of faster inflation andtighter monetary 
policy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/traders-no-longer-price-second-boe-interest-rate-cut-this-year?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
, net-net.

Perhaps no surprise, Germany’s central bank chief was plain in making his call 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/ecb-s-nagel-says-inflation-more-worrying-than-growth-on-iran-war?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 on the matter Thursday: “At the moment, I would say there’s more impact coming 
on the inflation front” than on growth, Joachim Nagel said.

In Japan, policymakers are still on track 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/boj-is-said-to-keep-april-hike-on-table-with-eyes-on-iran-crisis?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 to continue raising borrowing costs, with even an April move not off the 
table, Bloomberg reported Thursday. A former official put theodds of it 
happening 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/boj-april-rate-hike-odds-are-50-50-after-iran-ex-official-says?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 at 50/50. Meanwhile interest rate-cut betsare unraveling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/rate-cut-bets-are-unraveling-across-developing-asia-on-oil-surge?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 across developing Asia.

As for the Federal Reserve, it’s still being run by Jerome Powell, who’s 
record shows a “wait-and-see” bias. That’s how the US central bank reacted to 
the steep tariff hikes last year, as well as — in retrospect mistakenly — to 
the initial jump in consumer prices in 2021.

As long as energy-price increases 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-5?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 don’t morph into a broader inflationary impulse and start reshaping public and 
investor expectations, the Iran war impact will prove — wait for it — 
transitory.

Anna Wong <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVZEA8jmBvs/anna-wong>, chief US 
economist at Bloomberg Economics, sees the crisis in time imposing an 
“asymmetric easing bias.”

In the short term, headline inflation will of course be bumped up from the 
higher energy costs, but that then means “consumers buy less of everything 
else,” Wong says. And that leads to “less hiring for those everything-else 
sectors — which should push theunemployment rate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/us-companies-added-63-000-jobs-in-february-adp-data-show?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 up.” (The latest USjobless rate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-28/us-job-growth-seen-moderating-after-robust-january?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 reading is due at 8:30 a.m. in Washington Friday.)

But for now, Wong sees the Fed’s hawks as “calling the shots.”

Come June, with a new chair in place, things could be different. Philip Marey, 
senior US strategist at Rabobank, says Kevin Warsh could start pushing for rate 
cuts in his first Federal Open Market Committee meeting, in June (assuming he’s 
confirmed by then.)

“Inspired by the limited tariff inflation, both in size and duration, he is 
likely to encourage the FOMC to look through the temporary effects of oil 
prices on inflation,” Marey wrote in a note Thursday.

Need-to-Know Research

The Trump administration’s move to replace its invalidated tariffs with a 10% 
rate will go some — but not all — of the way to replace the lost fiscal 
revenue, according tocalculations 
<https://www.crfb.org/blogs/how-much-will-trumps-new-10-or-15-tariffs-raise> by 
the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget.

Assuming that refunds 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/us-to-pay-interest-if-ordered-to-pay-importers-tariff-refunds?cmpid=BBD030626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 for the illegal levies are given, the Supreme Court’s ruling against the use 
of “IEEPA” tariffs will reduce government revenue by $1.7 trillion through 
2036, the CRFB estimated. If the 10% replacement levy were kept in place over 
that period — something that would take further action, as the current measure 
has a 150-day deadline — that would bring in $925 billion.

The White House order 
<https://www.whitehouse.gov/presidential-actions/2026/02/imposing-a-temporary-import-surcharge-to-address-fundamental-international-payments-problems/>
 included a raft of exceptions for the 10% level now in place. If Trump follows 
through on plans to boost that to 15%, that would bring in $1.3 trillion over 
the years through 2036, the CRFB said. That would still leave a gap of $400 
billion relative to what was in place prior to the Supreme Court ruling. Under 
any scenario, the federal debt load continues to climb.
