**ECB Policy: A Genuine Dilemma**

The ECB's policy path crystallized into its most difficult configuration of the current cycle this week. Two Governing Council members, including Isabel Schnabel, called for a rate hike at the June meeting, and President Lagarde confirmed the bank will revise its inflation projections upward. The case for tightening was reinforced Thursday when CPI accelerated in France, Spain, and Italy to the fastest rates since 2024 — largely reflecting energy price pass-through from the Middle East conflict. Yet France's Q1 GDP contraction, reported the same day, presents the precise scenario that most complicates the ECB's task: inflation overshoot alongside weak growth. The conditionality in the Governing Council's language — a hike "unless a durable peace deal is reached" — introduces genuine two-way risk to the rate path. Bund yields and inflation expectations fell sharply Monday on thin holiday volumes as Hormuz deal optimism briefly dominated, before partially reversing as fresh US strikes on Iranian targets pushed energy prices back toward $97. Governing Council member Villeroy's observation that second-round inflation effects have not yet materialized sits in tension with Schnabel's more hawkish framing, and the ECB's semi-annual Financial Stability Review warned Wednesday that asset prices remain "stretched by historical standards," with geopolitical risks appearing underestimated by investors.

**UK Political Economy**

The UK's political situation continues to deteriorate in ways that carry medium-term economic significance. Commentary from Westminster suggests the Starmer administration is entering a period of effective policymaking paralysis as the leadership question remains unresolved, with Andy Burnham's potential candidacy drawing attention from fixed-income investors alert to any leftward fiscal shift during a leadership contest. The more immediate economic concern is consumer price pressure: over 80% of British food and drink manufacturers surveyed indicated they plan to raise prices, with energy cost pass-through from the Hormuz disruption the primary driver. A government-commissioned review warned of a "lost generation" of young unemployed workers, attributing the deterioration to surging employer national insurance contributions, worsening youth mental health, and AI-driven displacement of entry-level roles — structural drivers unlikely to self-correct through the cycle. Reform UK's position as Britain's most popular party by polling adds a longer-horizon policy risk: the party's deputy leader has outlined plans to dismantle net-zero commitments, which would create material discontinuity risk for UK renewable energy investments and carbon market instruments if translated into electoral power.

**Carbon Markets and EU Industrial Policy**

A Bloomberg investigation found that companies in at least nine European countries — including BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, EDF, ExxonMobil, and OMV — purchased carbon credits from Chinese projects in the EU's upstream emissions reduction scheme that appear to have been fraudulent or non-existent. German authorities have voided credits from 30 projects claiming 2.1 million tons of CO₂ savings. No criminal penalties are expected for the purchasing companies, as acquisitions were made in good faith, but the episode raises fundamental questions about the governance architecture of European carbon markets ahead of the EU's planned expansion of credit-based mechanisms toward its 2040 emissions target. The European Commission's rebooted Chips Act, targeting €120 billion in investment by 2035 in public-private partnership, reflects a strategic response to the semiconductor race — and specifically to Huawei's LogicFolding disclosure — that Brussels regards as a structural competitive threat requiring industrial policy intervention rather than market-driven adjustment alone. Oxford Economics published analysis quantifying a further upside risk to ECB inflation forecasts: severe climate-driven food supply disruptions could raise eurozone headline inflation by up to 0.6 percentage points annually, a supply-side risk that conventional monetary tools are poorly designed to address.
