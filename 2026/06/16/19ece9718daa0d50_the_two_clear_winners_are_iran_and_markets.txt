Unconditional <https://bloom.bg/4vIOs6C> Surrender <https://bloom.bg/4aKM7jh>

Three months ago, President Donald Trump announced that conflict with Iran 
could only end with“unconditional surrender.” <https://bloom.bg/4uEwBN9> Few 
thought that it would be the US, not Iran, waving a white flag, but that 
appears to have happened.

The full terms of the memorandum of understanding <https://bloom.bg/4v7VwJX> 
agreed under the auspices of Pakistan have not been revealed yet, and there are 
many points of detail that could matter. But the direction of travel is clear. 
Jean Ergas of Tigress Financial Partners chose to begin every item in his 
Monday morning macro snapshot with the same words:

Iran! — Peace to end all peace! Iran regime wins!

As Ergas points out, Iran forced the US to negotiate (a victory in itself), 
refused to move tonet-zero nuclear fuel <https://bloom.bg/4veJdMc>, won an 
agreement for the gradual release of blocked funds and possibly even funding to 
help with post-bombardment reconstruction, forced Washington to drop any talk 
of regime change, and avoided collapsing into a failed state. It’s hard to see 
what the American escapade has achieved, beyondkilling a very old man 
<https://bloom.bg/4vPy3gA>.

The Strait of Hormuz will reopen for the duration of the 60-day period in 
which the sides move on to the hardest subjects to do with thwarting an Iranian 
nuclear weapon. There doesn’t appear to be anything to stop Iran fromcharging a 
toll <https://bloom.bg/4vkGfG4> for the use of the Strait (which it will 
control with Oman) once the 60 days have elapsed.

The longer-term consequences could be profound. Tina Fordham of Fordham Global 
Foresight pointed out that the game theory of the situation had changed, 
because the US has now revealed that it’s not prepared to resort to risking 
American lives:

Perhaps the gravest consequence of the terms agreed is the inevitable 
conclusion that the US will be unwilling to back up the deal with a credible 
threat of force, a crucial element of diplomacy. Tehran understands that the US 
will not want to resume hostilities ahead of November midterms.

Countries that enjoy some form of chokepoint in the global economic system 
<https://bloom.bg/4vfm6RD> have the green light to exploit it. China’s control 
of rare earths and Iran’s power over Hormuz have both now forced a US climbdown 
within the space of 12 months. The cost of trade will continue to rise, 
probably through paying explicit tolls to use the Strait, while everyone 
attempts to move toward self-sufficiency (or “autarky”).

In an environment like this, autarkic policies <https://bloom.bg/4vc3aTA> make 
sense, but globalization took hold because it helped to limit prices. Moving to 
self-sufficiency can happen, but will come with a cost.
Next up, autarky. Photographer: Majid Saeedi/Getty 
For recent examples, Europe headed off a total energy crisis after Russia’s 
invasion of Ukraine <https://bloom.bg/3QrDeo3> four years ago, and the 
developed world has managed to eke out enough from its existing oil inventories 
to avoid major shutdowns during the closure of the Strait. This has been costly 
— and also created great profits for oil refiners.

It can all be treated as part of a gradual deglobalization that has been the 
base case for many investors since Trump’s first election. There are doubts 
over the future of trade, and over the details of the peace plan, which for the 
moment is only a memorandum. But what matters for markets is that there is 
little to no doubt that the Strait will soon reopen for business. To quote 
Viktor Shvets of Macquarie:

There is no doubt that the proposed MoU will be signed on Friday, as the 
alternative of another major escalation is highly unlikely, even as a 
negotiating tactic. Escalate to de-escalate tactics seldom work, especially 
against resourceful and determined foes.

Iran appears to have prevailed, but the point for investors now is to work out 
the consequences for the energy markets. As these are likely to be very 
positive, with the industry ready to spend the next 60 days bringing oil 
inventories to overflowing, global markets reacted with a big rebound.

How Markets Bounced Back

The most immediate impact was in oil, where crude prices dropped to their 
lowest since the first week of the war, while futures for delivery a year from 
now also fell. Both remain significantly higher than before the war started:

Lower oil prices, and less risk of further rises in future, had a direct 
impact on inflation expectations. Swaps over one and two years for the US are 
now back to where they were on the day after Trump’s election. They rose 
sharply after he imposed the shocks, first of tariffs, then the war, but both 
times he appears to have stood down before serious damage was done.

Perhaps surprisingly, the shift hasn’t yet had much impact on expectations for 
central banks, with the Federal Reserve still seen as likely to hike by the end 
of the year. With Kevin Warsh due to make his debut press conference as 
chairman Wednesday — a hotly anticipated event in which he could take a number 
of directions — futures traders decided against any significant moves:

Monday’s biggest winners in the US were the stocks that had been the greatest 
beneficiaries of risk appetite and plentiful liquidity, rather than anyone 
particularly hurt by the conflict. Thus the single biggest contributor to a 
record close for the Nasdaq 100 was the chipmaker Micron Technology Inc., which 
gained 10.75%. The correction in semiconductor stocks that started two weeks 
ago is already over, with the PSE SOX index back at an all-time high. 
Meanwhile, the great AI-driven shift out of software stocks and into 
semiconductors was never interrupted by the war:

Internationally, the same picture repeated. With risks to liquidity appearing 
to diminish, money flowed straight back into the chip sector, perceived these 
days as close to a certain bet. Korea’s KOSPI index, home to the freshly minted 
trillion-dollar companies SK Hynix and Samsung, gained 5.2%.

In foreign exchange markets, the news from the Strait weakened the dollar 
(which generally benefits from times of low risk appetite), and helped emerging 
markets. While EM stocks are still lagging thanks to the power of the AI 
phenomenon in the US, their currencies have regained virtually all their losses 
from early in the war:

George Saravelos, chief currency strategist at Deutsche Bank AG, suggested 
that oil importing currencies still had scope to rally: “To put it simply, the 
more supertankers can start moving through the Strait of Hormuz full of oil and 
the quicker they do so, the more these currencies should rally.”

That points to Sweden’s krona, an energy importer that tends to be correlated 
to global growth — meaning it should now be ready to outperform the rest of 
Europe this year; South Africa’s rand (might benefit from a rebound in gold, 
which has sold off during the conflict); the Chilean peso (exposed to copper); 
and particularly India’s rupee, where the central bank has been intervening.

The greatest sense, however, is that investors are glad for any excuse to move 
on from months of agonizing over a narrow Middle Eastern waterway. Jordan 
Rochester of Mizuho Group suggested that the new focus would be:

Less Truth Social posts and Hormuz-dipping headlines. Instead, it’ll be much 
more data watching, central bank commentary, the AI super cycle theme, British 
politics, Japan’s fiscal spend expectations, and whatever else isn’t the Strait 
of Hormuz.

It may well be too soon to to try to put the issue behind us. The events of 
the last three months could reverberate for years. But the desire to move on is 
wholly understandable. For now, traders will continue as if nothing had 
happened.



 Survival Tips 



Following on from songs about mountains — I could have mentioned Katie Melua’s 
Half Way Up the Hindu Kush <https://bloom.bg/4vUI9Nq>, Inspiral Carpets’ 
Biggest Mountain <https://bloom.bg/43DGhwj>, the Marshall Tucker Band’s Fire on 
the Mountain <https://bloom.bg/3Qm6cFS>, Mountain Song 
<https://bloom.bg/4gncQGk> by Jane’s Addiction, King of the Mountain 
<https://bloom.bg/4ecWkrb> by Midnight Oil or Ozzy Osbourne’s Over the Mountain 
<https://bloom.bg/4orBh7v>, but the really critical omission was early 1970s 
rockersMountain <https://bloom.bg/4v6nq9g>. Their song Nantucket Sleighride 
<https://bloom.bg/4vNXAGZ> is a setting to music <https://bloom.bg/4vVsb5M> of 
the struggles of Captain Ahab andMoby Dick <https://bloom.bg/3Qm6gW8>, which 
has its own analogies to monetary policy. (A “Nantucket sleighride” happens when
a harpooned whale turns the tables and drags the boat <https://bloom.bg/4eca19O>
 behind it.) And — thank you very much to Steve Sosnick of Interactive Brokers 
for pointing this out — I should have known this because it was also the theme 
song for the 1970s ITVcurrent affairs show Weekend World 
<https://bloom.bg/4gnvy0x>. So the tune is inextricably linked in my mind with 
26% inflation and intractable industrial disputes. Good times. Any more I 
missed?
