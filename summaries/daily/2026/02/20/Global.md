The dominant cross-cutting theme remains the US-Iran standoff and its implications for energy markets. President Trump stated Iran has 10 to 15 days at most to reach a nuclear agreement, with the US having assembled a substantial military presence across the Middle East. The Wall Street Journal reported the administration is evaluating a limited early strike designed to pressure Tehran toward negotiations. Oil rose to near a six-month high on the back of these developments, with WTI trading around $66 per barrel. The situation remains fluid: the UK has reportedly held off granting the US access to Diego Garcia — a critical Indian Ocean base — for potential operations against Iran, complicating military planning. How Iran responds to the deadline, and whether diplomatic channels remain viable, is the single most consequential near-term variable for global energy markets.

The fracture within the International Energy Agency crystallized this week at its Paris ministerial summit. US Energy Secretary Chris Wright assailed the IEA's climate focus, arguing that emissions policies have suppressed European growth and energy access. The US threatened to exit if the agency does not abandon net zero framing — a significant escalation given the IEA was co-founded by Washington in the 1970s. The meeting ended without the customary joint ministerial communiqué, with only a Netherlands-authored summary released. Members did find limited common ground on critical minerals cooperation and energy security, but the institutional fracture appears genuine and its longer-term ramifications remain unresolved.

The Supreme Court's tariff ruling carries significant global trade implications. By invalidating the emergency-powers legal basis for reciprocal tariffs, the decision removes — at least temporarily — the single largest source of trade policy uncertainty for US trading partners. The US-Indonesia trade deal, the US-Taiwan import shift, and India's tariff concessions all reflect ongoing reconfiguration of supply chains that will continue regardless of the court's ruling. Separately, the EPA's own cost-benefit analysis of its endangerment finding rollback suggests costs could exceed $1.4 trillion under higher-end scenarios — materially complicating the administration's $1.3 trillion savings claim and likely to figure in ongoing legal challenges.
