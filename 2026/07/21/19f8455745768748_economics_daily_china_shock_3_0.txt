Moonshot Moment

China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-21/china-vows-to-achieve-annual-growth-target-after-economy-weakens?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
 is closing the AI gap, with profound implications for the world economy.

Echoing the Deepseek shock of January 2025 that rattled global markets, 
today’s Moonshot moment is being spurred by angst over China’s advances on two 
different fronts:

 * AI startup Moonshot on Friday released Kimi K3 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-17/china-s-powerful-new-moonshot-ai-model-closes-gap-with-us-rivals?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
, touting benchmarks comparable to some of the US labs’ best offerings.
 * China’s largest DRAM maker, ChangXin Memory Technologies Inc. is preparing a
blockbuster IPO 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-16/cxmt-s-blockbuster-ipo-212-times-covered-for-retail-investors?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
 to fund a major capacity expansion.
Let’s look at the new model first. Moonshot 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/chinese-ai-sensation-moonshot-s-gamble-on-big-models-pays-off?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
 claims it performs on par with some of the top-tier platforms from OpenAI and 
Anthropic PBC. Unlike the closed US models, Kimi is open weight, which means 
its parameters are available for users to download and customize. Entrepreneur 
and investor Kai-Fu Lee used mobile phones as an analogy, saying OpenAI and 
Anthropic are like the iPhone and Chinese models are like Android, meaning “the 
American closed models will make the most money but the Chinese models will get 
more market share.”

That push for market share is in line with President Xi Jinping’s AI ambitions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-17/xi-vows-to-make-ai-for-all-in-debut-at-china-s-top-tech-summit?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
. Xi used his appearance at the World AI Conference in Shanghai on Friday to 
urge an inclusive approach. “AI development should not be a solo performance by 
a single country, but a symphony of international cooperation,” Xi said.

On the chips front, China is gaining ground on the big three memory producers, 
Samsung Electronics, SK Hynix and Micron Technology. Fourth placed CXMT aims to 
deploy the IPO proceeds to expand capacity of its wafer fabrication lines and 
to upgrade its DRAM technology.

To Peter Alexander, founder of Z-Ben Advisors, that means investors should 
look beyond the China Shock 2.0 now rattling German automakers and start 
preparing for the China Shock 3.0 that’ll reshape the semiconductor market.

“A new competitor doesn’t need to produce the world’s best technology,” 
Alexander wrote in asubstack <https://substack.com/@plalexander/p-206788151> 
late last week. “It only needs to produce acceptable technology, at scale and 
at a lower cost. This is exactly how CXMT can become a massive disruptive 
force.”

The process would start at home, with Chinese buyers snapping up CXMT chips 
and thereby removing a major source of demand for the current big three.

“As China Shock 2.0 is now highlighting, technological superiority alone does 
not guarantee market dominance. The critical factor is the relationship between 
performance and price,” Alexander argues.

And China is rarely beaten on price, leaving the Korean and Taiwanese AI-chip 
booms we’ve explored inrecent newsletters 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-07-03/how-the-ai-boom-is-carving-a-k-shape-into-asian-economies?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
 vulnerable. As for China’s own economy, the tech advance is proving abuffer 
but no panacea 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/ai-is-new-engine-keeping-chinese-economy-from-harder-landing?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>

Need-to-Know Research

A standard explanation of falling developed-world fertility rates says that 
this pattern is tied to women having greater agency, thanks to economic 
independence and perhaps social acceptance, to pursue their own paths. That 
fails to capture that men are abig part of the problem 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/nobel-laureate-goldin-says-male-duds-are-weighing-on-birth-rates?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
, new analysis suggests.

“The real obstacle is the need for husbands and fathers to demonstrate their 
commitment in advance” that they’ll share in the burden of child-rearing, Nobel 
laureate Claudia Goldin, a Harvard University economist, wrote in a recent
working paper <https://www.nber.org/papers/w35425> for the National Bureau of 
Economic Research. “The more that men can credibly signal they will be 
dependable ‘dads’ and not disappointing ‘duds,’” the more likely educated women 
are to have babies, she wrote.

Goldin also said that countries that have had some of the largest fertility 
declines, such as Japan, Italy and Greece, suffered from a gap between the pace 
of development and the rate of social change. While women gained more education 
and job opportunities, they were still expected to handle the bulk of housework 
— making it harder for them to raise children. These nations “didn’t have 
sufficient time for generations toadjust their traditions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/japan-s-world-cup-cleanup-praise-meets-skepticism-at-home?cmpid=BBD072126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6763>
,” she said.

Correlation between fertility “and the gender difference in household and 
caring hours is strong,” Goldin concluded.
