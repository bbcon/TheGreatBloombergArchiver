US President Donald Trump sounds confident the Strait of Hormuz will reopen to 
shipping starting Friday. At the Group of Seven leaders summit in France this 
week, it’s clear European alliesdon’t share 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/us-at-odds-with-allies-over-how-easy-it-is-to-reopen-hormuz?cmpid=BBD061626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5491>
 his optimism.

Washington and Tehran still need to decide on the final steps for the interim 
peace deal that’s also being called a memorandum of understanding, set to be 
signed in Switzerland on Friday. Neither side has released a text.

As Bloomberg’s Oliver Crook reports 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-16/us-europe-disagree-on-how-easy-it-is-to-reopen-hormuz?cmpid=BBD061626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5491>
 from the G-7 summit in Evian, European diplomats say it’s not realistic to 
think the strait will see energy, commodities and goods trade flowing normally 
in just a few days. Also unclear is whether vessels will be charged tolls in 
the future.

.
Dalia Fahmy, director of international relations and diplomacy and associate 
professor of political science at Long Island University,told Bloomberg 
Television 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-16/fahmy-trump-wants-to-reshape-the-middle-east-video?cmpid=BBD061626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5491>
 that it’s “only the beginning” of a series of negotiations, with the reopening 
of Hormuz just one of several things to discuss.

In the 12 months leading up to the Feb. 28 start of the war, an average of 21 
container ships a day were trackedtransiting Hormuz 
<https://x.com/bloombergtv/status/2066645096062189748?s=46&t=SvNTluN3W4uqumhv6PE4KQ>
 daily in both directions. Since then, a total of 23 have made the crossing and 
none since June 8, according to data compiled by Bloomberg.

Here’s a rundown of what some other stakeholders and analysts are saying:

 * Germany’s Hapag-Lloyd, the world’s fifth-biggest container carrier, said in 
an emailed statement Tuesday that its four vessels in the Persian Gulf will be 
ready to cross Hormuz this weekend but “will only proceed when it it safe to do 
so.”
 * No. 2 Maersk said it’s too early to say what comes next. “At this stage, 
there are no changes to our operations in the region,” the Danish logistics 
company said Monday.
 * Mitsui OSK Lines said close coordination 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/shipowners-seek-clarity-on-hormuz-deal-as-600-vessels-eye-exit?cmpid=BBD061626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5491>
 with governments and insurance firms would be essential before it could send 
ships through the strait again.
 * Jeff Currie, executive co-chairman at Abaxx Markets, told Bloomberg 
Television 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-16/currie-hormuz-flows-may-not-normalize-until-year-s-end-video?cmpid=BBD061626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5491>
 that “the uncertainty remains quite high” and that Hormuz flows may not 
normalize until the end of the year.
 * According to Windward Maritime, said operational conditions were “unchanged 
and the maritime picture will only shift once the deal’s terms are confirmed 
and implemented.”
 * Judah Levine of Freightos said in a note Tuesday that “a full return to 
normal traffic and an oil market recovery are expected to happen only gradually 
over the coming months.”
 * Fertilizer flows 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/fertilizer-ships-face-long-backlog-even-if-hormuz-reopens?cmpid=BBD061626_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260616&utm_content=5491>
 from the Gulf are unlikely to resume quickly, as shipowners await more safety 
assurances.
 * According to Bloomberg Intelligence, port throughput <> growth is set to 
split through 2026 as war-driven shipping rerouting pressures Gulf-exposed 
operators such as DP World. Carriers such as CMA CGM and Hapag-Lloyd are 
avoiding choke points like the UAE’s Jebel Ali and Fujairah and using gateways 
such as Salalah and Jeddah instead via overland routes.
 * But just when one shipping headache in the region looks to be ending, a 
recentBimco report says 
<https://www.bimco.org/news-insights/bimco-news/2026/06/11-somali-piracy/> 
there’s been a uptick in Somali pirate activity, with seven hijackings this 
year. Part of the reason: Naval patrols in the Red Sea, Gulf of Aden and Somali 
Basin have been “more stretched than usual” given the Iran war.
