Weekly Brief

May 11 – May 17, 2026

US

  • April CPI rose to 3.8% year-on-year on Tuesday — the highest reading in three years — as Hormuz-driven energy costs generated second-order pass-through into core services.
  • Kevin Warsh was confirmed as Federal Reserve chair on Wednesday by the narrowest margin on record, inheriting a policy framework where rate hikes have moved from tail risk to plausible base case.
  • The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 reached successive all-time highs through midweek before retreating Friday, with 10-year Treasury yields touching 4.54% and futures markets pricing roughly two-thirds probability of a December rate hike.
  • Cerebras Systems debuted Friday as the year's largest IPO, surging 68% to a $67 billion market cap, while Nvidia approached a $6 trillion valuation — both reflecting continued AI-driven momentum even as macro headwinds intensified.
  • The administration sought a pause on a federal court ruling that declared the president's 10% global tariffs unlawful, while processing more than $35.5 billion in tariff refunds following an earlier Supreme Court decision.

Monetary Policy and Inflation

The week's most consequential domestic development was Tuesday's CPI print, which showed headline inflation at 3.8% year-on-year — the highest reading since 2023 and above the Federal Reserve's upper tolerance band. The Strait of Hormuz closure remains the primary driver through elevated energy costs, but the composition of the report was more troubling than the headline: core inflation ex-energy ran at 2.8%, sticky prices re-crossed 3%, and the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply, suggesting the oil shock is generating second-order wage and price effects rather than operating as a purely relative-price disturbance. Consumer prices rose faster than average hourly earnings in April, the first such occurrence since 2022. Wednesday's PPI report reinforced the picture, rising 6% year-on-year — its highest reading since 2022 on an ex-pandemic basis — led by energy and freight, with electronics components inflating 27% year-on-year as AI-driven memory shortages ended decades of technology-sector disinflation. Oxford Economics now projects the PCE deflator reaching 3.8%, effectively foreclosing near-term rate cuts. The Fed's policy horizon shifted accordingly: Bank of America explicitly recommended short positions in two-year Treasuries, characterizing rate-hike risk as underpriced, while Fed Governor Barr described inflation as "the overwhelming risk facing the economy."

Federal Reserve Leadership Transition

Kevin Warsh's Senate confirmation on Wednesday — by the narrowest margin on record — represents one of the more consequential central bank leadership changes in decades, delivered at a particularly difficult moment. Rather than the rate-cutting cycle that markets initially priced around his appointment, Warsh inherits a policy environment where the probability distribution for the fed funds rate has shifted decisively toward tightening. Whether he will defend institutional independence against an administration that has historically expressed preferences on monetary policy remains to be seen.

Equity Markets and AI Concentration

US equity markets spent most of the week threading a narrow path between AI-driven earnings momentum and rising rate anxiety. The S&P 500 and Nasdaq 100 set fresh records through Thursday before retreating Friday on PPI data and oil price pressure. The concentration dynamic sharpened further: the top ten S&P 500 constituents account for approximately 40% of index market capitalization, an all-time high, while the SOX semiconductor index has outperformed the Magnificent Seven since the ChatGPT launch. Alphabet closed the week with a market cap of $4.8 trillion against Nvidia's near-$6 trillion, having gained 34% in April alone. The simultaneous rise in the VIX alongside equity prices — a historically uncommon configuration — may signal that investors are hedging aggressively into the rally rather than expressing unqualified conviction. Critics including Jeremy Grantham and Michael Burry publicly flagged valuation concerns, though earnings revisions at semiconductor names continue to outpace price appreciation, with Sandisk and Micron trading at roughly nine times forward earnings despite gains of 482% and 172% respectively year-to-date.

Fiscal and Policy Developments

The administration's legal exposure on trade policy intensified this week. A federal trade court declared the president's 10% global tariffs unlawful, and the government responded by requesting a pause pending appeals while processing over $35.5 billion in tariff refunds from a related Supreme Court ruling. The FEMA leadership situation drew scrutiny ahead of the June 1 hurricane season: newly nominated director Cameron Hamilton previously authored a memo advocating the agency's abolition, and roughly 5,000 staffers have departed since January. Trump's financial disclosures separately revealed personal equity purchases in Q1 including Nvidia, Oracle, Microsoft, Boeing, and Costco — a detail unlikely to recede from political circulation with midterms less than six months away. ---

Asia

  • The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing on Thursday and Friday produced a Chinese commitment to purchase 200 Boeing aircraft and a renewed trade truce framework, though no actionable Iran mechanism emerged and Xi issued his bluntest Taiwan warning to date.
  • South Korea's Kospi fell 6.1% on Friday — its steepest single-session decline in months — as elevated rate expectations repriced a market that had risen roughly 86% year-to-date.
  • China's April CPI rose to 1.2% and PPI to 2.8% year-on-year, its fastest pace since July 2022, with analysts declaring an exit from deflation though partly attributable to imported energy inflation.
  • The Bank of Japan signaled a possible June rate increase as 20-year JGB yields touched their highest level since 1997, while a board member called for tightening "as early as possible."
  • China's new supply chain coercion regulations — characterized by Rhodium Group as a shift from reactive to proactive economic statecraft — represent a structural escalation in Beijing's industrial policy posture.

Trump-Xi Summit

The week's geopolitical centrepiece was the Trump-Xi summit in Beijing on Thursday and Friday, the first US presidential visit to China in nearly a decade. The delegation's composition was itself a signal: Jensen Huang of Nvidia joined Tim Cook, Elon Musk, Kelly Ortberg, and Goldman Sachs' David Solomon, thrusting AI chip access into the heart of the agenda alongside trade, Taiwan, and Iran. Xi greeted Trump with a full state welcome at the Great Hall of the People, and both leaders struck cooperative public tones. China renewed import licenses for hundreds of US beef plants as an early gesture, and Treasury Secretary Bessent outlined a potential board of investment allowing Chinese capital into non-sensitive US industries, alongside possible tariff relief on roughly $30 billion of non-critical trade. Pre-summit working-level talks between Bessent and Vice Premier He Lifeng had begun in Seoul on Wednesday. The most pointed moment came when Xi warned that mishandling Taiwan could push the bilateral relationship into a "highly dangerous situation" — language published by Chinese state media while the two leaders were still in the room. On Iran, both sides expressed shared interest in reopening Hormuz but produced no operational mechanism. Trump subsequently confirmed that China has not approved purchases of Nvidia H200 chips and intends to develop domestic alternatives — a reminder that the semiconductor competition underlying the summit cordiality remains unresolved.

South Korean Market Dislocation

South Korean equities experienced two distinct episodes of volatility this week. On Monday, the Kospi shed more than $300 billion in market capitalization within 97 minutes — falling as much as 5.1% — after presidential policy chief Kim Yong-beom floated a "citizen dividend" funded by AI-profit taxes via a Facebook post, before recovering partially following a clarification. By Friday, the index fell a further 6.1% in its steepest single-day decline since March. The underlying tension is genuine: Samsung Electronics posted a 755% profit increase in Q1 and its labor union is threatening an 18-day strike from May 21 demanding 15% of operating profit for chip-division employees. The convergence of redistribution politics, labor unrest, and speculative positioning — retail borrowing is at record levels — around AI-driven windfall profits appears likely to persist. Goldman Sachs economists noted that AI-chip export booms in South Korea and Taiwan are on track to push both economies' current-account surpluses to record levels, likely pressuring their central banks toward rate increases later this year.

China: Inflation, Credit Quality, and Strategic Posture

China's April inflation data marked a meaningful inflection: PPI rising to 2.8% year-on-year — its fastest since July 2022 — alongside a 1.2% CPI reading prompted analysts to declare a formal exit from the deflationary spiral that had weighed on domestic pricing for the better part of two years. The drivers are partly imported, however, with elevated energy and raw material costs from the Hormuz closure contributing materially; Chinese crude imports fell 20% year-on-year in April as state refiners drew on commercial inventories. More structurally concerning, independent analysts estimate true non-performing loan ratios in the Chinese banking system at approximately 10% of total credit — against an official reading of 1.5% — implying roughly $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt. Regulatory forbearance has so far contained systemic risk but is visibly slowing loan growth and trapping capital in unproductive enterprises. China's April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above the 8% consensus, with technology the primary driver — even as 10-year domestic bond yields remain below 2%, now trading below Japanese government bond yields, a striking inversion that reflects the dichotomy between external competitiveness and subdued domestic demand.

Japan, India, and Regional Currency Stress

Japan's 20-year JGB yields touched their highest level since 1997 this week, driven by energy-import inflation feeding domestic price pressures. A Bank of Japan board member called for tightening "as early as possible," and Finance Minister Katayama confirmed close coordination with Bessent on foreign exchange policy, with Bessent characterizing excess FX volatility as undesirable — a tacit endorsement of recent intervention. Japanese household spending fell for a fourth consecutive month, illustrating the demand compression accompanying the cost-of-living shock. Corporate goods prices rose at the fastest pace in 12 years. In India, Prime Minister Modi doubled gold and silver import tariffs to approximately 15%, pairing earlier appeals for citizens to curb bullion purchases with formal policy tightening to defend the rupee and shore up foreign exchange reserves. The Philippines, India, and Indonesia are all burning FX reserves to defend weakening currencies as oil import costs mount, with shrinking import cover pushing policymakers toward rate hikes. Thailand convened an emergency meeting between the prime minister and leading business figures to address converging crises of inflation, unemployment, and energy costs. ---

Europe

  • UK 30-year gilt yields reached their highest level since 1998 and the 10-year touched 5.14% on Friday, as political uncertainty around Prime Minister Starmer's tenure compounded oil-driven inflation pressures.
  • Four junior ministers resigned in an attempt to force Starmer's departure, with Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham emerging as frontrunner to challenge him for the Labour leadership by Friday.
  • The ECB's policy stance shifted materially, with Bundesbank President Nagel indicating rate hikes are "increasingly likely" and Governing Council member Kazaks warning of stagflation risks.
  • UK Q1 GDP rose 0.6%, its strongest quarter in a year, though virtually all forecasters expect near-stagnant growth over the remainder of 2026.
  • German industrial production fell unexpectedly for a second consecutive month in March, and France's unemployment rose to its highest level in five years.

UK Political Crisis

The week opened with Prime Minister Starmer delivering a reset speech following Labour's sweeping local election defeat, only to face an accelerating internal revolt. By Tuesday, he was publicly defying cabinet-level calls to set a departure timetable, telling ministers that "the country expects us to get on with governing" — a response that failed to stabilize investor sentiment. Home Secretary Mahmood and Health Secretary Streeting were among those privately or publicly pressing for transition; four junior ministers resigned in an attempt to force the issue. By Friday, Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham had secured a credible path to mount a leadership challenge, with the MP for Makerfield standing aside in his favour, making Burnham the frontrunner in what markets now price as a greater-than-50% probability of a change in leadership before year-end. The pound fell on the news, gilt yields surged, and Schroders disclosed it is actively avoiding UK bonds, citing the risk that a successor could pursue a more expansionary fiscal stance. Jamie Dimon added an external dimension, warning that any post-Starmer government raising taxes on banks would cause JPMorgan to abandon plans for a multi-billion-pound Canary Wharf headquarters. The King's Speech, meanwhile, represents a near-term test of whether Starmer can project governing authority through the noise.

UK Economy and Gilt Markets

Beneath the political turbulence, the UK's economic data produced a genuine positive surprise: Q1 GDP rose 0.6%, the strongest quarterly growth in a year, with the IT sector contributing meaningfully. The reading suggests the economy absorbed the early weeks of the Iran conflict without contracting. However, the sustainability of that pace is widely questioned — Bloomberg Economics projects near-stagnant growth over the remainder of 2026 as higher energy costs, tighter borrowing conditions, and political uncertainty compound. The gilt market has increasingly reflected this tension: 30-year yields reached levels last seen in 1998, and the spread between gilts and Treasuries has tracked the oil price since the outbreak of hostilities, suggesting Iran may be a more proximate driver of UK funding costs than domestic politics alone, even if the two reinforce one another. The UK simultaneously halved its pledge to the Green Climate Fund, reducing its 2024–2027 contribution from £1.6 billion to £815 million, a further withdrawal from multilateral climate commitments. British employers continued shifting toward temporary hiring in April, with permanent-role hiring falling at its fastest pace in three months.

Eurozone: ECB Pivot and German Fragility

The ECB's policy outlook underwent a meaningful shift this week. Bundesbank President Nagel told Handelsblatt that rate hikes are increasingly likely — a notable pivot given that the dominant expectation at the start of the year was for continued easing. Governing Council member Kazaks went further, warning that data are pointing toward stagflation risks and that a hike would be warranted if oil price increases feed through to inflation expectations. A Bloomberg survey now shows the ECB expected to hike twice in 2026, though Kazaks also described a June move as "less obvious" than imminent — reflecting the genuine tension between above-target inflation and softening growth that Oxford Economics estimates could add 0.6 to 0.7 percentage points of additional inflation pass-through given elevated public inflation awareness. Germany's underlying position remains fragile: industrial production fell unexpectedly for a second consecutive month in March, Chancellor Merz's approval ratings have fallen to historic lows, the AfD has extended its polling lead, and a six-hour coalition working session produced few concrete agreements. France compounded the eurozone picture, with unemployment rising unexpectedly to its highest level in five years.

European Political and Structural Themes

European political fragility extended well beyond the UK. Romania's government fell, Latvia's prime minister resigned over a drone-related controversy, and trade union protests greeted the German chancellor. Italy under Meloni stands as a notable exception to this pattern of leadership attrition. At the EU level, the Commission reached unanimous political agreement to sanction violent West Bank settlers — its first such consensus since the Gaza crisis — while reporting suggests von der Leyen's centralized management style is generating internal friction and diluting focus on the single market agenda. An analysis of London commercial property found that up to 78% of Westminster offices and 71% of City offices will fail to meet Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards expected in the early 2030s, raising stranded-asset risk across the sector. In eastern European bond markets, Hungary's borrowing costs fell below Poland's for the first time since 2020, reflecting investor optimism around new Prime Minister Magyar's EU alignment agenda. ---

Global

  • Brent crude rose from approximately $103 to around $109 across the week as the Strait of Hormuz remained largely closed, entering its eleventh week of disruption, with TD Securities warning of a potential surge toward $150 and BloombergNEF projecting $160 if closure persists through July.
  • The IEA reported global oil inventories falling at a record pace and projected outright demand contraction of 420,000 barrels per day in 2026 — a reading historically associated with severe economic stress.
  • US, UK, eurozone, and Japanese sovereign bond markets sold off in concert, reflecting a common energy-inflation driver layered over country-specific fiscal and political factors, with US 10-year yields at 4.54% and UK 10-year gilts at 5.14% by Friday.
  • The Trump-Xi summit produced shared rhetoric on Hormuz but no operational mechanism, while Trump's comment that the US does not need the strait open "at all" complicated the diplomatic signal.
  • China's new supply chain coercion regulations, characterized by Rhodium Group as a shift to proactive economic statecraft, represent a structural escalation with implications for Western industrial policy.

The Iran Conflict and Hormuz

The Strait of Hormuz closure — now in its eleventh week — remains the dominant cross-cutting variable in global markets. Monday opened with Trump rejecting Iran's latest peace proposal, sending Brent crude up as much as 4.6% toward $106 before settling above $103. By Tuesday, with WTI near $98.93 and Trump characterizing the ceasefire as on "massive life support," the diplomatic outlook deteriorated further. Satellite imagery confirmed that Iranian crude exports from the Kharg Island terminal had halted for several days — the first sign of a prolonged stoppage since the conflict began. BloombergNEF estimates disruption across 27 Middle Eastern oil fields has reached 9 million barrels per day. A Japanese supertanker appeared to make a covert Hormuz transit, two India-bound LNG cargoes crossed under elevated-risk conditions, and Qatar directed vessels near Ras Laffan to disable their AIS transponders — at least nine tankers went dark from Monday. The asymmetric warfare dimension remains central: Iran's deployment of midget submarines has sustained the stalemate despite significant US naval superiority. Friday's Brent price of approximately $109 per barrel represents a rise of more than 60% since the conflict began in late February. The UAE announced plans to double its Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027, a structural adaptation to what some planners are now treating as a prolonged-disruption scenario.

Oil Market Buffers and Their Limits

Morgan Stanley and Mercuria both characterized the short-term buffers preventing a more severe price spike as unsustainable. US crude and product exports are running above 14 million barrels per day with emergency stockpile drawdowns at record rates; simultaneously, Chinese state refiners have reduced crude purchases by drawing on commercial inventories. Neither dynamic can persist indefinitely. The IEA's assessment that global inventories are falling at a record pace and will continue declining for months implies continued upward price pressure absent resolution. The US EIA now projects global oil demand growth of only 200,000 barrels per day in 2026, against a pre-war forecast of 1.2 million; the IEA projects outright demand contraction of 420,000 barrels per day. Hapag-Lloyd reported approximately $250 million in additional monthly operating costs since February 28, though its CEO noted that Asian export volumes outside the Persian Gulf have been "surprisingly" resilient. The jet fuel market is under particular strain, with US jet fuel exports surging to a record 455,000 barrels per day as airlines in Europe and Asia scrambled for supply. OPEC+ is targeting completion of quota restoration by end-September, though the relief that provides depends substantially on Hormuz conditions.

Trade Flows and Energy Infrastructure Adaptation

The Hormuz disruption is reshaping physical trade routes in ways that may prove semi-permanent. Iran has boosted rail freight to China via Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan, with train frequency rising from roughly one per week to one every three to four days and container rates along the route climbing 40% above typical levels. China's clean technology manufacturers are explicitly leveraging the energy shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third, with Chinese firms actively targeting Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia. The commercial logic is clear — sustained high fossil fuel prices improve the economics of electrification across every geography simultaneously. At the same time, Beijing's new supply chain coercion regulations — allowing China to retaliate against foreign governments or firms that assist in actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial security — are calibrated to deter the sourcing diversification that Washington and Brussels have been encouraging. Rhodium Group warns of a self-reinforcing dynamic in which China's rising manufacturing share erodes competitor margins, reducing Western firms' capacity to reinvest in R&D, while private capital increasingly avoids sectors with entrenched state-backed Chinese competition.

Global Bond Markets and Inflation Propagation

The synchronized selloff in sovereign bond markets across the US, UK, eurozone, and Japan this week reflects a common inflation driver propagating through different institutional contexts. US 10-year yields closed Friday at 4.54%, UK 10-year gilts at 5.14%, and Japan's 20-year rate at its highest level since 1996. Producer price indices in the US, China, and Japan all surprised to the upside this week, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. Investors captured 5% yields on 30-year US Treasuries for the first time in nearly two decades — a notable level for long-duration pricing globally. The broader consequences of a world in which all major central banks face simultaneous inflationary pressure — limiting their ability to buffer growth slowdowns with easing — remain to be fully absorbed in equity valuations, credit spreads, and emerging market capital flows. Asian central banks are already responding: the Philippines, India, and Indonesia are burning FX reserves to defend weakening currencies, while the Bank of Japan is signaling an accelerated tightening path. The coherence of losses across global bond indices this year represents a meaningful change from the post-GFC environment of divergent monetary cycles, and its full implications for asset allocation have not yet resolved.
