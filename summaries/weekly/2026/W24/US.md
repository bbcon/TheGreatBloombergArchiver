**Labor Market**
The May payroll print of 170,000 — meaningfully above consensus — marked the third consecutive month of solid job creation and effectively negated the Sahm Rule recession signal that had briefly emerged in 2024–2025. The strength removes the Fed's primary justification for easing, though the labor market's resilience also reflects genuine underlying demand rather than mere statistical noise. NFIB small business optimism, however, told a more nuanced story: the May reading slipped below expectations, erasing all post-election gains, with hiring plans deteriorating and capital expenditure falling to its lowest since 2009. The divergence between large-cap resilience and small-business stress is widening.

**Monetary Policy**
Markets repriced aggressively over the course of the week. Two-year Treasury yields surged to their highest level in over a year by Tuesday; overnight index swaps shifted from pricing cuts to pricing meaningful probability of hikes; and Goldman Sachs formally abandoned its 2026 rate-cut call. May CPI topping 4% YoY on Wednesday reinforced the hawkish pivot, though core's below-consensus reading kept a near-term hike off the table. Incoming Fed Chair Kevin Warsh — facing his first FOMC meeting next week — confronts persistent political pressure from President Trump, who told NBC that raising rates would be "the wrong thing to do," even as the bond market signals the opposite. Consensus now places the Fed on hold well into mid-2027.
