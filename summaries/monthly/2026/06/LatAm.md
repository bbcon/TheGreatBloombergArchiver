**Regional Political Realignment**
De la Espriella's Colombian victory — built on a pro-Trump, law-and-order platform that effectively dismantled the traditional Uribista conservative movement — completes a rapid hemispheric realignment toward US-aligned governments. His platform reversing Petro's direction, reopening Colombia to oil and gas exploration including fracking, is likely to be received as market-constructive at the margin, though limited governing experience raises genuine implementation questions. Brazil's President Lula, facing reelection in October against Flávio Bolsonaro, may emerge as the last significant counterweight to comprehensive US hemispheric alignment — a contest whose outcome will shape regional political economy into 2027. The Brazilian Senate leader from Lula's party resigned following a police investigation linking him to failed lender Banco Master, narrowing the administration's legislative coalition credibility at a sensitive moment.

**Trade Exposures and Energy**
Mexico faces a structurally acute challenge: average tariffs on Mexican auto exports stand at approximately 19%, above rates applied to some Asian competitors, while Trump's stated intent not to reauthorize USMCA sets up a protracted renegotiation with material implications for a manufacturing sector central to the country's export model. Brazil simultaneously faces a 12.5% forced-labor levy and a separate 25% unfair trade practices rate, with President Lula reframing the threat politically as "TariFlávio" ahead of elections.
