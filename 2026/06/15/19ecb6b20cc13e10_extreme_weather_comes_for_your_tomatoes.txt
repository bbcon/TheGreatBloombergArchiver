Hotter planet, higher prices

By Emma Court 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUVJA-GbuIc/emma-court?cmpid=BBD061526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5547>

Juicy tomatoes are a summer staple often slipped into burgers or sliced atop a 
salad. But weather disruptions in two major growing regions have driven up 
prices for the vegetable, and consumers shouldnтАЩt expect much relief for their 
wallets in the months ahead.

тАЬSupply is going to level out, prices are going to tweak down. But weтАЩre not

US tomato prices rose by about 40% between January and April, the biggest 
three-month increase since 2006, consumer price index data shows. While prices 
eased slightly last month, they remain more than 30% higher than a year ago. 
Some Mexican states, meanwhile, are reporting price increases of more than 100% 
from the prior year.

Weather has long affected grocery store prices, but increasingly frequent 
bouts of extreme heat, drought and flooding are putting household budgets on 
the frontlines of climate change. As global warming intensifies, economists are 
expecting food-price shocks to become more common, raising the risk that higher 
grocery costs become a more enduring source of inflation.

Tomatoes are the latest example of this, joining the ranks of staples like 
coffee and beef. The price hikes come at a time when spiraling anxiety about 
the cost of living is colliding with accelerating inflation, largely driven by 
higher energy costs related to the war with Iran.

Other factors, too, are fueling the ongoing run-up in tomato prices. US duties 
on Mexican imports and an appreciating peso have led farmers there to plant 
less tomato acreage. But back-to-back weather extremes are playing a major role.

The US depends on imports for much of its tomato supply, and most of those тАУ 
some 90% тАУ come from Mexico. Drought conditions left less water for irrigation 
during the October and November planting season in Sinaloa, a key growing 
region. Then unseasonal rains in January exposed plants to early blight, fungal 
disease that thrives in wet conditions.

winter storms in late December and January that the stateтАЩs agriculture 
commissioner called тАЬone of the most damaging freeze events for Florida 
agriculture in history.тАЭ An estimated 80% of the stateтАЩs tomato production 
suffered losses, resulting in some $164 million in projected damages.

As a result, wholesale prices for Roma tomatoes тАФ which are largely grown in 
Mexico тАФ and the mature green tomatoes often grown in Florida reached the

record тАФ more than two months.

тАЬItтАЩs not every year that you have weather events impacting both regions in 
the same month,тАЭ he says.

Catastrophe models for war

By Gautam Naik 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVyoWbluWhQ/gautam-naik?cmpid=BBD061526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260615&utm_content=5547>

As Wall Street races to incorporate war into its risk scenarios, the same 
people modeling natural catastrophes are now adapting their methodology to help

Since 2008, the number of countries engaged in external conflicts has nearly

Wars are upending the finance industryтАЩs ability to predict everything from 
the price of oil to the cost of a mortgage, and Wall Street has had to

Verisk, which is best known for its work on natural catastrophe models for

Smoke rises from Jebel Ali port after an Iranian missile attack in Dubai, 
United Arab Emirates, in March.Photographer: Christopher Pike/Bloomberg
The firmтАЩs Predictive War Index, released to clients in late May, uses a 
machine learning algorithm to forecast the likelihood of war occurring in a 
country over the next 12 months. It was trained on political, economic, and 
social datasets from 1995-2022 and therefore doesnтАЩt take the current Iran war 
into account. Even so, back-testing showed that had the model been ready in 
early January, it would have shown a 66% probability of war breaking out in 
Iran 1 1/2 months later, according to Verisk.

The firmтАЩs other new model, the Geopolitical Relations Index, tracks the 
changing level of tension between pairs of countries. It looks at parameters 
such as whether theyтАЩve had military clashes in the past, how similar their 
styles of government are, or whether theyтАЩre geographically close enough to 
project power.

A separate Verisk model, launched in October 2023, has in the period since 
then correctly predicted six out of seven government collapses, including the 
ouster of Bashar al-Assad in Syria in 2024, and the sudden removal of 
VenezuelaтАЩs Nicolas Maduro in January.

In the case of MaduroтАЩs removal, тАЬthere were economic issues combined with a 
past history of political instability that increased the risk,тАЭ said Chris 
Boylan, a data science expert at Verisk Maplecroft.
