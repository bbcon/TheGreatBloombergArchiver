 Maturing in 2126 



Money managers are placing wagers today that Google and other businesses owned 
by Alphabet will still be going concerns a century from now.

Alphabet is  selling a 100-year bond in the UK <https://bloom.bg/4r6KAu1>, 
part of a clutch of debt sales it kicked off this week to fund itsambitions in 
artificial intelligence <https://bloom.bg/4r6KFxP>. It’s the first century bond 
issued by a technology company since Motorola sold one in 1997 
<https://bloom.bg/3O523Vi>. 

Pension funds and insurers love ultra-long debt because it matches with their 
long-term liabilities.

But if you’re buying a 100-year bond, you want to be pretty confident the 
issuer will be around for 10 decades to keep paying the coupons and then 
ultimately to repay the principal.

That’s why the securities are mostly issued by governments and institutions. 
Austria sold one in 2020 to lock in interest rates pushed below 1% by the 
pandemic. In the UK, the University of Oxford and the charitable foundation 
Wellcome Trust Ltd. have issued the debt.

Such bonds aren’t for the faint of heart: The combination of long maturities 
and low coupons make them extremely sensitive to changes in interest-rate 
expectations. The Austrian debt, for example, is trading below 30 cents on the 
euro, a collapse from a peak of nearly 140 cents in late 2020.

And such bonds issued by corporations carry the risks of technological 
obsolescence, competition and outdated business models.Department store 
retailer J.C. Penney Co. filed for bankruptcy in 2020, just 23 years after 
issuing a century bond.

Motorola still exists but, as hedge fund manager Michael Burry noted on X, it’s
 no longer a dominant, household name <https://bloom.bg/4rFxS5x>, having been 
beaten by Nokia, and then Apple, in mobile phones. It’s still paying the coupon 
on that 100-year note though. —Phil Serafino <https://bloom.bg/42HUmtw> and 
Tasos Vossos <https://bloom.bg/4n9Zajb>, with Greg Ritchie

 Memory squeeze 



The relentless surge in memory chip prices over the past few months has driven 
a vast divide between winners and losers <https://bloom.bg/45UTp1Q> in the 
stock market, and investors don’t see any end in sight.

Companies from game console maker Nintendo to big PC brands and Apple 
suppliers are seeing shares slump because their costs for memory have gone up. 
Memory producers, meanwhile, are soaring to unprecedented heights.

Money managers and analysts are now assessing which firms can best navigate 
the squeeze by locking in supplies, raising product prices or redesigning to 
use less memory.

“What remains underappreciated is the risk around duration — current 
valuations largely factor in that the disruption will normalize within one to 
two quarters,” said Vivian Pai at Fidelity International. “We believe industry 
tightness is likely to persist,” possibly through the rest of the year, she 
added. —Winnie Hsu <https://bloom.bg/3G9AMxv>
