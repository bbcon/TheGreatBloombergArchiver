US equity markets remained under pressure as S&P 500 futures extended their losing streak into a fifth consecutive week, the longest such run since 2022, reflecting the compound strains of Middle East-driven inflation anxiety, a weakening labor market, and fiscal uncertainty. The index would need to fall an additional 7.7% to reach the 50% Fibonacci retracement level from April's low, a level technical analysts are watching closely.

On the monetary policy front, three Federal Reserve officials expressed growing concern over the economic outlook, with at least one policymaker explicitly characterizing the oil price spike as elevating inflation risks above labor market deterioration in the hierarchy of concerns. That framing matters: it signals the Fed may be moving toward a posture where easing is harder to justify even as growth softens — a stagflationary bind the institution has limited tools to resolve cleanly. Weekly jobless claims fell to their lowest level in nearly two years, offering a modest counterweight, though February's 92,000 decline in nonfarm payrolls remains an overhang.

The housing market is exhibiting visible stress heading into the spring selling season. Thirty-year fixed mortgage rates have climbed back to 6.4%, a six-month high, reversing the sub-6% readings seen as recently as late February. KB Home lowered full-year guidance, citing the conflict as a contributing factor, and industry contacts described conditions at a JPMorgan homebuilder conference as "fragile." The deterioration reflects both the mechanical channel — Treasury yields rising on inflation repricing — and the confidence channel, as households facing rising energy costs and equity portfolio losses pull back.

On the fiscal front, the Senate passed legislation to fund most of the Department of Homeland Security, advancing a resolution to the partial government shutdown that has produced some of the worst airport congestion in recent memory. The bill must still clear the House and receive presidential signature, leaving the outcome uncertain. Trump separately indicated he would issue an executive order to pay TSA officers, though the legal authority to circumvent congressional appropriations remains questionable.
