Hi, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai, where a group of US senators kicked off a 
visit to China this week.

Such events are typically a diplomatic nicety aimed at drumming up business 
for the states the lawmakers represent. But this one took on added importance 
because it comes ahead of Donald Trump’shighly anticipated summit with Xi 
Jinping in Beijing next week 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/world-s-most-important-bromance-revived-as-trump-heads-to-china?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 — the first by a US leader since 2017.

During his meeting with Shanghai Mayor Gong Zheng at an Art Deco hotel in a 
swanky part of the financial hub, Trump ally Steve Daines said he was “pleased 
to see that things have somewhat deescalated” in recent months.

The better vibes between China and the US that the Republican from Montana 
highlighted remain in placedespite moves by both sides 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-05-06/trump-administration-s-pentagon-blacklist-misstep-roils-us-china-tech-ties?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 that in the past may have sparked a diplomatic tempest.

For example, Trump has been trying to rebuild the tariff wall struck down by 
US courts; aiming to curb Chinese influencein the Western Hemisphere 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-05-05/trump-s-military-buildup-in-panama-puts-latin-america-on-edge?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
; andlaunching attacks against Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/trump-s-summit-with-xi-is-on-despite-china-s-iran-concerns?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 and Venezuela, two of Beijing’s friends and sources of cheap oil for its 
export-reliant economy.
 <>
China has held its own in this regard since Xi and Trump negotiated a one-year 
trade truce in South Korea last fall. Beijing has maintained tight control of 
its exports of the rare earthsvitally important to the US economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/xi-s-has-1-2-trillion-rare-earths-leverage-ahead-of-trump-visit?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
, tried to undercut the dollar’s dominance by promoting trade in the yuan and 
made it hard for Nvidia to sell its advanced chips in the country.

It sought to unwind Meta’s purchase of the Chinese startup Manus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-05/china-blocked-meta-s-manus-deal-what-now-for-ai-startups?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
, a deal that could extend America’s lead in AI. And has directed companies not 
to abide by US sanctions on private refiners linked to the Iranian oil trade, 
though it has also advised the largest banks totemporarily suspend new loans to 
five plants 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/china-asks-banks-to-pause-new-loans-to-us-sanctioned-refiners?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
.

Oh, and the Taiwan question remains a lingering worry for both sides. 
Illustrating how the issue remains front of mind, this week Secretary of State 
Marco Rubio saidneither nation wants it to destabilize Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-05/rubio-says-neither-us-china-want-asia-destabilized-over-taiwan?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
.

Then there is Iran.

The war seems to be taking on a new sense of urgency for Beijing. For example, 
this week top Chinese diplomat Wang Yi told Iranian counterpart Abbas Araghchi 
thatthe Strait of Hormuz should reopen and quick 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-06/iran-s-top-diplomat-meets-wang-yi-in-first-china-trip-since-war?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
.

Fixing that problem would end a crisis that is a mounting worry for the 
world’s No. 2 economy given the Middle East accounts for two-fifths of its oil 
imports. And it would make Xi look like a peacemaker before the world during 
Trump’s visit, boosting the Chinese government’seffort to advance its  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-04/china-to-russia-us-and-eu-chase-trade-mineral-stakes-in-caucasus?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
diplomatic clout. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-04/china-to-russia-us-and-eu-chase-trade-mineral-stakes-in-caucasus?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>

In fact, the stability of bilateral ties serves both China and the US well 
right now. Despite recent signs of a pickup in the Chinese economy, including 
booming AI exports and signs that deflation is ending, Xi stillhas on his plate 
the property crisis 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/wall-street-dares-to-ask-if-china-s-property-turnaround-is-close?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
, stubbornly high youth unemployment anda running battle against corruption in 
the military 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/china-announces-suspended-death-sentences-for-two-ex-ministers?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 that has gutted its top leadership.

Trump has worries of his own. The war in Iran is unpopular back home, not only 
becauseit has pushed up gas prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-07/war-s-economic-damage-to-us-spreads-evening-briefing-americas?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 but because many of the people who voted for him did so precisely because they 
wanted America to avoid entanglements like this.

While there are ample signs that Trump remains the dominant force in his 
party, Republicans seem to be in fora tough time in November’s midterm elections
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-07/affordability-concerns-rise-as-trump-gop-s-biggest-midterm-headache-grows?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 – a result that could damage his ability to govern.

Trump’s big meeting with Xi may be short on deliverables that could ease the 
pain by showing American voters he’s getting results for them. And there 
doesn’t seem to be much wiggle room between the two sides on Taiwan, tech curbs 
or even Hong Kong’s jailing offormer media mogul Jimmy Lai 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-05/trump-says-he-will-bring-up-hong-kong-s-jimmy-lai-in-xi-summit?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
.

Henry Huiyao Wang, the founder and president of the Center for China and 
Globalization in Beijing, said that turbulence around the world meant it was 
important for Xi and Trump to meet now.

“President Trump needs China to be on the side of global security,” he said. 
“And of course, China would like to see all these conflicts end because 
economically, it is a partner with many of these countries and wants to see 
global stability.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/audio/2026-05-05/big-take-asia-china-halts-meta-s-2-billion-ai-deal-podcast>
Trump will likely have to be satisfied with some easy wins in Beijing. Think a 
nice photo op with Xi in the Great Hall of the People. A pledge to buy American 
planes and agricultural and energy products would also be nice. Daines himself 
hinted atthe desire for more Boeing purchases 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/us-senator-thanks-china-for-iran-efforts-calls-for-boeing-deal?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
 when his trip took him to Beijing to meet top Chinese officials.

And Xi is very likely to play along. For all the headaches that the US 
president has created for China, another point that came up during Senator 
Daines’s visit to Shanghai stands out: Xi sees Trump as someone with whom he 
can work.

For the Chinese leader, that’s a welcome contrast to the Biden administration, 
which forged regional alliances such as the Quad to constrain China’s ability 
to project power, more openly backed Taiwan and placed heightened importance on 
tech curbs.

And it’s a big reason to expect a summit heavy on style and less so on 
substance next week.

Workers 1, AI 0

As anxiety mounts everywhere over the threat AI poses to jobs, a Chinese court 
delivered a somewhat reassuring message for workers: companies cannot fire them 
just to use the new tech.

A court in the eastern city of Hangzhou — home to some of China’s most 
prominent AI startups — ruled that a company illegally dismissed a 35-year-old 
after he refused to take a demotion with a 40% pay cut when his job checking 
the accuracy of output by large language models was automated.

The ruling, upholding a lower court decision, underscores the growing tension 
in Beijing’s AI push 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-27/xi-s-ai-ambitions-collide-with-china-s-fragile-employment-market?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
. Even as China races to develop frontier technologies and embed them across 
the economy, authorities remain wary of the social and political risks of 
disruption in the world’s largest labor market.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-04-27/why-deepseek-v4-impresses-despite-lack-of-wow-factor-video>
That concern is already informing policy. In January, the human resources 
ministry said it was drafting guidance to address AI’s impact on jobs. And 
Zhang Yunming, vice minister of industry and IT, has warned that employment 
pressures from AI were “inevitable.”

Those pressures are coming into sharper focus as China’s labor market 
unexpectedly deteriorates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-22/unemployment-spikes-for-key-chinese-age-group-as-ai-use-spreads?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
, especially the key demographic of early-career workers. Official data show 
the jobless rate for people aged 25 to 29 climbed to 7.7% in March, the highest 
since the government revamped its employment data more than two years ago. The 
unemployment rate for people aged 16 to 24 remains stubbornly high, too.

Young job seekers remain attractive for employers because they are generally 
smart, educated and motivated. But they are facing growing competition from the 
rapid spread of AI tools — a transformation that Citi estimates could 
eventually displace an estimated 70 million workers in China.

For now, the nation’s courts and labor arbitration bodies — operating under 
the control of the ruling Communist Party — are emerging as an early line of 
defense for workers displaced by AI.

Last year, arbitrators in Beijing sided with a dismissed worker whose employer 
had replaced his role manually gathering data with AI. They ruled that 
substituting AI for human labor was a profit-driven business decision, not an 
uncontrollable external event, and therefore did not provide lawful grounds to 
terminate an employment contract.

A court in the southern city of Guangzhou reached a similar conclusion in 
2024, ruling that a company’s decision to replace a graphic designer with AI 
did not qualify as a change in “objective circumstances” under labor law, 
making the dismissal unlawful.

Such moves will be welcome news for the tens of millions of graduates entering 
China’s labor market this summer, even if they know that AI is here to stay.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

How can this still be happening here?

That is the question many people in China were asking after an explosion at a 
factory making fireworks killed some 37 people and injured dozens more.

The blast occurred in Liuyang, a city in south-central China that calls itself 
the pyrotechnics capital of the world, during the Labor Day holiday.

Underscoring how Beijing is keen to show it is taking the disaster seriously, 
Xi ordered officials to figure out what went wrong. Authorities say they are 
still investigating the cause.
Firefighters at the scene of the deadly fireworks plant blast in Liuyang, 
Hunan province.Photographer: China News Service/China News Service
In recent years, China has become a country that prides itself on its economic 
and tech achievements 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-05/china-shows-it-s-going-all-in-on-technologies-of-the-future?cmpid=BBD050826_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4241>
. It has also become much, much safer, especially regarding street crime. 
Pickpockets and bike thieves who used to plague city streets are mostly a thing 
of the past.

And despite having the world’s largest industrial workforce, the fatality rate 
from industrial accidents has tumbled over the past decade. It now has fewer 
deaths per 100,000 workers than the US but still trails the figure in many 
European nations, according to various estimates.

Nevertheless, two industries remain problematic. Coal mining, which 
authorities have tried to make safer in part by encouraging the sector to 
consolidate and automate.

And fireworks.

That explains why sorrow over the latest accident quickly yielded to anger on 
social media, where users wondered how the plants making stuff like bottle 
rockets and sparklers can continue to have such accidents.
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
“On June 16 last year, an explosion occurred at a fireworks factory in 
Changde, Hunan, and injured more than 20 people,” Weibo user Today’s Sharp View 
told his 1.2 million followers, referring to an incident that also killed nine.

“Less than a year later, a fireworks factory explosion occurred again and the 
impact was even worse,” the person wrote, arguing that lessons were going 
unlearned.

Liuyang has a long tradition of pyrotechnic production. Guinness World Records 
says thatthe first accurately documented firework 
<https://www.guinnessworldrecords.com/world-records/446403-first-documented-firecracker>
 can be linked to a monk who lived near Liuyang during the Tang dynasty 
(618–907).

Many people on social media expressed pride in that tradition and indicated 
they hoped it would continue, pointing to the jobs it creates and the money it 
makes.

But as one influencer with some 4.5 million followers said on Weibo, the 
disasters have to stop “and safety standards must be upgraded so that tragedies 
cannot be repeated.”
