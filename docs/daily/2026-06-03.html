Daily Brief

Wednesday, June 03, 2026

US

  • April JOLTS job openings surged to their highest pre-pandemic level, with vacancies again exceeding the number of unemployed workers — a clear beat relative to recession fears.
  • The Trump administration proposed new tariffs of at least 10% on imports from roughly 60 trading partners, including the EU, UK, Canada, and Mexico, and 12.5% on China, Japan, and others, reviving the protectionist agenda the Supreme Court had struck down.
  • The S&P 500 extended its winning streak to nine consecutive sessions, driven almost entirely by AI-related technology names.
  • Real personal income has declined 0.6% over the past year, a pace Goldman Sachs describes as rarely seen outside of recession, even as headline labor market data remains resilient.

Labor Market

April JOLTS data released Tuesday showed job openings surging back to their highest pre-pandemic level, with the US once again registering more than one vacancy per unemployed worker. The print stands in stark contrast to recession-adjacent signals elsewhere in the data. Real personal income has fallen 0.6% over the past year on Goldman Sachs estimates — a threshold historically associated with downturns — while gasoline costs have risen roughly 44% since late February, adding approximately $200 per month to household fuel bills. Treasury Secretary Bessent acknowledged the burden while arguing it is transitory, a framing that will face scrutiny at Senate and House hearings Wednesday and Thursday. The May payrolls report, due Friday, is forecast to show average hourly earnings growth of 3.4% year-over-year, below prevailing inflation readings.

Trade Policy

The administration's new tariff proposal, structured under a Section 301 forced-labor investigation, calls for a 10% levy on most major partners and 12.5% on China, India, Japan, South Korea, Brazil, and Switzerland. Bloomberg Economics estimates the effective US tariff rate would rise by only 0.5 percentage point from its current 10.7% level if implemented after the expiring Section 122 tariffs lapse in late July — a relatively modest increment. A second 301 probe targeting industrial overcapacity is expected shortly, with analysts flagging potential stacking of duties as the key risk.

Equity Markets

The S&P 500's nine-session winning streak is attributable almost entirely to the technology sector, which has outperformed the ex-tech S&P 500 by 33% over the past three months — the strongest relative quarter for tech on record. Marvell Technology extended its recent rally in premarket trading following Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang's prediction the company would reach a $1 trillion valuation. SpaceX separately announced plans to raise $75 billion in what would be the largest IPO on record, pricing shares at $135 ahead of any formal roadshow. ---

Asia

  • Indonesia's Jakarta Composite Index fell as much as 5.7% to its lowest level since May 2021 after prosecutors raided the headquarters of President Prabowo Subianto's flagship free meals program.
  • Bank of Japan Governor Ueda reiterated that interest rates need to keep rising to contain inflation, reinforcing the BOJ's tightening trajectory.
  • Japan's fertility rate fell to a fresh record low in 2025, adding long-run structural pressure to the economy.
  • Foreign investors have sold nearly $70 billion of Korean equities year-to-date while directing $73.6 billion into Japanese stocks, even as Goldman Sachs lifted its targets for both the Kospi and Taiwan's benchmark index.

Indonesia

Indonesia dominated Asian market news. The benchmark Jakarta Composite fell as much as 5.7% intraday, touching its lowest since May 2021, after anti-corruption prosecutors raided the operational headquarters of President Prabowo Subianto's free meals program — a signature campaign pledge. The raid followed the abrupt removal of the agency's chief amid food-poisoning incidents and operational failures. The rupiah weakened approximately 0.6% against the dollar, leading regional currency losses. The episode raises reputational risk for Prabowo's administration and may complicate investor confidence in a market already under pressure from the global commodity shock.

Capital Flows and Japan

Foreign investors have rotated sharply away from Korea and Taiwan despite those markets' AI-driven gains, redirecting capital toward Japan. Overseas selling of Korean equities stands at nearly $70 billion year-to-date, with Taiwan flows also turning negative, while Japan attracted $73.6 billion of inflows through late May. The preference for Japan reflects its market depth, breadth, and corporate governance reforms rather than superior earnings momentum. India separately moved toward reducing taxes on foreign bond investors and removing ownership caps on long-tenor sovereign notes, a potential catalyst for incremental inflows into its fixed income market. ---

Europe

  • The Stoxx Europe 600 declined 0.4%, weighed by fresh US tariff proposals and rising oil prices.
  • The EU is preparing to warn citizens and companies about a potential trade conflict with China as the bloc considers new restrictive measures against Beijing.
  • Akzo Nobel shares fell 19% in Amsterdam after Nippon Paint and Sherwin-Williams abandoned acquisition efforts; Inditex gained 5.7% in Madrid on stronger second-quarter sales momentum.

Trade and Policy

The Trump administration's new tariff proposal — imposing at least 10% duties on EU imports — poses a direct challenge to European exporters, even if Bloomberg Economics estimates the near-term increment to effective tariff rates is modest. Brussels is simultaneously bracing for a separate trade confrontation with China, with the EU weighing new restrictive measures and preparing to communicate the risks to businesses. The EU also confirmed it will allow fiscal flexibility for green energy investments, providing some offset to the broader tightening of trade conditions.

Markets and Corporate News

European equities came under modest pressure as rising oil — Brent near $98 — squeezed import-dependent economies, with the continent's deep exposure to Persian Gulf energy a persistent vulnerability. The corporate picture was mixed. Akzo Nobel's 19% decline was among the sharpest single-session moves in European equities this year after two potential acquirers walked away simultaneously. Inditex offered a contrasting signal: the Zara owner's acceleration in second-quarter sales growth suggests consumer spending in its key markets retains some resilience despite the inflationary backdrop. UBS raised $1.5 billion in Additional Tier 1 capital — its first such issuance since Switzerland paused plans to tighten AT1 rules — signaling selective reopening of bank capital markets. ---

LatAm

  • The US proposed 25% tariffs on Brazil specifically for unfair trade practices, hours after the broader 12.5% proposal covering most major emerging markets was announced.
  • Brazilian President Lula moved immediately to weaponize the tariff threat politically, branding it "TariFlávio" in a direct attack on electoral rival Flávio Bolsonaro.
  • Mexican teachers threatened to disrupt World Cup matches over wage demands, adding operational risk to a tournament already facing logistical scrutiny.

Brazil

Brazil faces a dual tariff exposure: the 12.5% levy proposed under the forced-labor investigation and a separate, more punitive 25% rate tied to a distinct unfair trade practices finding. President Lula's rapid political reframing of the tariff threat — linking it to Flávio Bolsonaro's recent White House meeting — illustrates how trade policy has become embedded in Brazil's pre-election dynamics. The episode adds uncertainty to an already complex macro picture in which energy cost inflation, driven by the Hormuz closure, is pressuring the current account.

Mexico

Mexico faces the 10% baseline tariff under the new US proposal, a meaningful development for an economy whose export sector is deeply integrated with US supply chains. Separately, the domestic political environment is generating its own noise, with teachers' unions threatening disruptions to World Cup host-city operations. On a constructive note, investment in Mexico's electricity sector is showing early signs of revival, with new deals in power generation, renewables, and grid infrastructure offering support to President Sheinbaum's modernization agenda — a potential offset to near-term trade headwinds if momentum is sustained. ---

Global

  • The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed for the fourth consecutive month; US and Iranian forces exchanged strikes Tuesday, with Iran launching ballistic missiles toward Kuwait and Bahrain.
  • Brent crude rose for a third consecutive session to approximately $98 per barrel, with WTI at $95.37, as ceasefire prospects on prediction markets continued to fade.
  • The OECD warned that if Middle East hostilities persist into 2027, global growth could slow to 1.8%, pushing several economies into or near recession.
  • The Trump administration's new tariff proposals affecting 60 trading partners add a second structural shock to a global economy already absorbing the energy disruption.

Hormuz and Energy

The Strait of Hormuz has been effectively closed to most tanker traffic for approximately three months, and Tuesday's exchange of strikes — including Iranian ballistic missiles targeting Kuwait and Bahrain, intercepted by US and allied defenses — diminished already-faint hopes for a near-term resolution. Saudi Arabia and the UAE are maximizing pipeline bypass capacity, but those routes face binding physical constraints and remain within range of Iranian missiles and drones. Iraq, Kuwait, Qatar, and Bahrain lack viable alternatives. The capacity gap means the supply disruption is structural rather than temporary for as long as hostilities persist. A MSC container vessel was separately struck at Iraq's Um-Qasr port, illustrating the widening geographic footprint of maritime risk.

Global Growth and Trade

The OECD's warning of 1.8% global growth under a prolonged conflict scenario frames the macro stakes clearly: the energy shock is already transmitting through inflation and real income compression, while the simultaneous re-escalation of US trade barriers threatens to compound the demand drag. Bloomberg Economics estimates China's potential deployment of rare-earth supply restrictions could put $4.4 trillion in global GDP at risk — a latent vulnerability that US-China trade tensions keep relevant. The interaction between an energy supply shock, renewed tariff uncertainty, and tightening monetary policy across Asia represents the dominant risk cluster for global growth in the second half of 2026.
