 ‘Policy juice’ 



Some bulls are betting we’re at that point in the market cycle where bad news 
for the economy isgood news for stocks <https://bloom.bg/4ao9G0u>. 

Today’s January employment report is likely to show the economy added only 
65,000 jobs last month, and revisions to past data will probably revealan even 
sharper slowdown in hiring <https://bloom.bg/4ksg7Ex> than previously thought.

But that may be positive for stocks, says Jim Paulsen, a strategist who writes 
the “Paulsen Perspectives” newsletter.That’s because history shows that periods 
of weak job growth are good times to buy equities, on the view that the Fed 
will support the economy by lowering interest rates.

Interest-rate traders are coalescing around a bet <https://bloom.bg/4tvxhFi> 
that would pay off if policymakers cut rates two or three times this year. 
Treasuries surged yesterday, sending yields to their lowest levels of the past 
month, aftera weak retail sales report <https://bloom.bg/3Mf1w2x>.

“Once policy juice arrives, whether or not the economy eventually suffers a 
recession, the stock market begins looking toward a renewed economic recovery 
and investors started bidding stock prices higher,” Paulsen wrote. —Natalia 
Kniazhevich <https://bloom.bg/4h3iYAl>

 Rolling selloff 



After a couple of years of AI-driven euphoria, investors now are focusing on 
the downside of the technology — products, companies and whole industries that
seem to be threatened with extinction <https://bloom.bg/4a9FqaO>.

In the latest upheaval, a tax-strategy tool from a little-known startup sent 
shares of brokers Charles Schwab and Raymond James down by 7% or more yesterday.

The rolling selloff has included:

 * Software stocks. The first group to get hit, starting last year, and the 
declines are broadening. Dassault Systemes is tumbling 20% in Paris today after 
the French maker of industrial design software reported disappointing results 
<https://bloom.bg/4qqDaRv>. 
 * Insurance brokers. Willis Towers Watson, Arthur J Gallagher and Aon slumped 
Monday after the online marketplace Insurify unveiled an AI tool to compare 
rates on auto policies <https://bloom.bg/4aJ4gOX>. 
 * Legal services and financial services. New tools from Anthropic PBC last 
week sparked routs in stocks ranging from Thomson Reuters and Legalzoom.com 
<https://bloom.bg/3Mf1mIt> to FactSet Research and S&P Global 
<https://bloom.bg/4cmdIJe>. 
To money manager Ross Gerber of Gerber Kawasaki, the angst is premature.

“We can try to extrapolate out what the world will look like in five years 
with AI, but we just don’t know,” he said. —Carmen Reinicke 
<https://bloom.bg/4i6roIa> and Rebecca Torrence <https://bloom.bg/3O1Gjd0>
