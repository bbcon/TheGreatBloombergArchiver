Economics of Slimming

Weight-loss drugs known as GLP-1s 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-24/weight-loss-drugs-are-changing-what-to-know-about-glp-1-medications?cmpid=BBD072226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6766>
 have been around for several years now. But only recently have studies begun 
to emerge looking at their potential economic impact. Initial findings indicate 
it may well prove to be significant.

Across the bulk of member nations of the OECD — a group mainly of developed 
nations across Europe, the Americas and the Asia-Pacific region —almost a fifth 
<https://www.oecd.org/en/publications/health-at-a-glance-2023_7a7afb35-en/full-report.html>
 of adults were obese as of 2021. The incidence of diabetes, closely linked 
with obesity, was 7%. In the US alone, one study has found a total estimated 
cost of diagnosed diabetes of$413 billion 
<https://pubmed.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/37909353/> for the year 2022.

Figuring out the extent to which GLP-1s affect the economic participation of 
those who take them requires tracking those individuals over a period of years, 
and comparing their experience with those who didn’t take the drugs. That can 
be tricky if a significant share of your study group discontinues treatment.

A new working paper <http://www.nber.org/papers/w35475> from the National 
Bureau of Economic Research spotlights a study done in Denmark, where the 
health system provides levels of benefits that make it easier for patients to 
keep going with the drugs. After four years, some 75% of patients were still on 
GLP-1s, compared with US rates of 40% to 60%.

The five economists behind the paper conclude, “GLP-1 treatment leads to a 
meaningful and persistent reduction in long-term sickness leave. This effect 
grows over time and is accompanied by declines in emergency department 
encounters and cardiovascular medication use, consistent with sustained 
treatment exposure and gradual improvements in underlying health.”
Source: NBER working paper by N. Meltem Daysal, Camille JH. Fredrickson, Ida 
L. Kristiansen, Mircea Trandafir and Jonathan Zhang via Bloomberg Intelligence
Keeping in mind that Denmark 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-23/danish-economy-ministry-raises-gdp-forecast-due-to-pharma-sales?cmpid=BBD072226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6766>
 has a universal, comprehensive publicly funded health system, this improvement 
was associated with “meaningful” positive fiscal spillovers, the economists 
wrote. The figure was valued at 1.3% to 1.5% of annual labor income per 
employed individual.

“In settings where work-limiting illness is less fully insured, similar health 
improvements could instead appear more directly in earnings, hours, or job 
retention,” the authors N. Meltem Daysal, Camille JH. Fredrickson, Ida L. 
Kristiansen, Mircea Trandafir and Jonathan Zhang wrote in their paper.

A separate study recently indicated that obesity drugs can help kick-start 
careers for those who otherwise struggled to get a job.

A paper 
<https://www.nber.org/papers/w35387?utm_campaign=ntwh&utm_medium=email&utm_source=ntwg18>
 by Harvard University economics professor Rebecca Diamond found that the 
employment rate for women who weren’t working before taking the medicine rose 
27 percentage points after about a year and a half. (A limited sample size for 
men in the database Diamond drew on meant the paper, which has yet to be 
peer-reviewed, focused on women.)

As time goes on, data will allow for further, more comprehensive studies. But 
at least some initial analysis suggests GLP-1s have the potential to aid 
employment, incomes and fiscal balances.

Need-to-Know Research

For all the hype about AI and the gigantic amounts being invested in it, 
economists have largely argued that benefits in terms of productivity have yet 
to show up. A fresh analysis by Barclays economists reinforces that stance.

“Our bottom line is that AI adoption appears gradual and steady rather than 
rapid and transformative, with most households and businesses still reporting 
limited exposure to the technology” so far, economists including Jonathan 
Millar and Marc Giannoni wrote in a note Tuesday.
Note: Chart produced by Barclays research
The pickup in productivity in recent years comes in part from an outsized role 
for cyclical swings, the team wrote. Employers hoarded labor after the 
difficulty of finding workers during Covid, and that weighed on productivity 
during the 2022-24 period, they said. More recently, there’s been a 
normalization back to trend, reducing the number of hours per worker and 
boosting productivity.

There’s little reason to believe recent productivity gains “are already 
leading to cost savings that will help tame inflation, which argues against 
moves to ease monetary policy on such grounds,” the Barclays economists wrote.
