Saudi Arabia, the world’s top oil exporter, is feeling the squeeze 
<https://bloom.bg/3NXm1S9> from a war that’s choking off one of global energy’s 
most critical arteries. Shipments to China and India are set to drop next 
month, said traders familiar with the matter, as disruptions in the Strait of 
Hormuz forced Aramco to reroute some flows via its Red Sea pipeline, a 
workaround that only partly offsets the hit. The result:tighter supplies 
<https://bloom.bg/3PtE7vy> for Asia, higher costs <https://bloom.bg/4bIMA5j> 
for importers, and a growing sense that markets may be underestimating just how 
quickly this conflict is spilling into the real economy.

That complacency is worrying some of the biggest names on Wall Street. 
BlackRock President Rob Kapitowarns investors <https://bloom.bg/4bIMAlP> are 
mispricing the risks from the Iran war, arguing the hit to growth and inflation 
could linger even if the fighting stopped tomorrow. With supply chains snarled, 
he said oil could still spike toward $150 a barrel, while Apollo’s Jim Zelter 
flagged rising risks for a US recession and strain on the credit cycle from a 
prolonged conflict. Markets, in other words, may be bracing for a quick end, 
even as the shock is only just beginning to bite.



 What You Need to Know Today 



Oil is climbing again as mixed signals from Washington and Tehran cloud the 
outlook <https://bloom.bg/40UGMAU> for any ceasefire, with the near-total 
shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz driving one of the biggest supply shocks in 
decades. Brent is now on track for its largest monthly gain since 1990, as 
millions of barrels of daily output remain offline and energy markets swing 
between hopes of diplomacy and the reality of ongoing attacks. Trump 
administration officials aregame-planning extreme scenarios 
<https://bloom.bg/4uNRHKp> like $200 oil, according to people familiar with the 
matter. The risks to energy flows were underscored by asuspected undersea attack
 <https://bloom.bg/4bIMACl> on a crude tanker in the Black Sea.




Germany is quietly preparing a playbook for a potential future clash 
<https://bloom.bg/4bRedta> with the US under President Trump, people familiar 
with the effort told Bloomberg. Officials have begun mapping out 
vulnerabilities in American supply chains that Europe could exploit, from 
targeting Big Tech and AI infrastructure to squeezing pharmaceutical flows. The 
goal is to build a unified EU strategy that can push back against US pressure, 
reflecting a broader shift in Europe’s thinking as transatlantic ties grow more 
transactional and policymakers increasingly view economic leverage as a two-way 
street, even at the risk of escalation.




Iran pushed back against US-led diplomacy, with its ambassador to Japan 
warning that Tehranwon’t accept <https://bloom.bg/40QqPvz> any unilateral peace 
plan and insisting it will set its own terms for ending the conflict. While 
signaling openness to mediation from countries like Japan, the comments 
underscore the wide gap between the two sides — and the limited prospects for a 
near-term deal even as the war continues to disrupt energy markets.




ChangXin Memory Technologies more than doubled revenue to about $8 billion in 
2025 <https://bloom.bg/4bIMB9n>, according to people familiar with the matter, 
driven bybooming demand <https://bloom.bg/47JjUbt> for AI-driven memory chips 
ahead of its planned IPO. The performance underscores Beijing’s push to build a 
self-reliant semiconductor ecosystem, with CXMT emerging as a key domestic 
challenger to global heavyweights like Samsung and SK Hynix, even as it 
prepares to invest heavily in next-gen chip development.




Former Goldman Sachs CEO Lloyd Blankfein is warning that private markets may 
be sitting on abuildup of hidden risk <https://bloom.bg/4bwNCme>, as piles of 
unsold assets on investor balance sheets raise the odds they’re overvalued. He 
likens the situation to dry tinder accumulating in a forest: the longer markets 
go without a correction, the greater the chance that a single spark could 
trigger a broad and sudden markdown, forcing investors to finally confront what 
those assets are really worth.




Moonshot AI is weighing a potential Hong Kong IPO as it looks to capitalize 
<https://bloom.bg/4bIMBpT> on surging investor appetite for artificial 
intelligence stocks, people familiar said, though plans remain preliminary and 
may not materialize. The Kimi chatbot maker has held early talks with advisers 
including CICC and Goldman Sachs, while also exploring raising up to $1 billion 
in private funding, underscoring how China’s top AI startups are riding a wave 
of strong valuations and market momentum.




Sky-high airfares are fast turning summer travel into a luxury, with prices on 
key Asia–Europe routessurging as much as 560% <https://bloom.bg/3O2sWcL> as 
war-driven disruptions, fuel spikes and airspace closures upend global 
aviation. With tens of thousands of flights canceled and Gulf transit hubs 
squeezed, airlines are passing on higher costs. Tens of thousands of travelers
remain stranded <https://bloom.bg/4c5b6Pw> and left scrambling for costly, DIY 
routes home with little airline support. Even if the conflict ends soon, 
travelers should brace for elevated fares well into the fall.

 For Your Commute 



There may be no sport more gruesome in the realm of travel than scoring a 
reservation at Tokyo’s most famous sushi establishments, writes my colleague
Brandon Presser <https://bloom.bg/40svR1G>. With just six to eight seats — and 
one or two rounds of service per night — they’re the ultimate get on a holiday 
in Japan.

Their desirability has only skyrocketed as international tourism to Japan has 
grown to an all-time high of over 42 million arrivals in 2025 (a 15.8% increase 
from the year before). As a result, a set menu meal in Tokyo that was 20,000 
yen ($130) in 2024 is now over 35,000 yen ($220).

There are over 5,000 sushi restaurants in the city, from cheap conveyor-belt 
eats to $1,000 meals showcasing the rarest, most premium ingredients.Here’s 
<https://bloom.bg/4uT6Mua> how to navigate them.
