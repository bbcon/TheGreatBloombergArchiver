The dominant macro theme of March 25 is the tension between a diplomatic opening on Iran and the continued prosecution of the war. The US 15-point ceasefire plan, delivered via Pakistan, has been met publicly by Tehran with dismissal and continued missile strikes on Gulf Arab states and Israel. Brent crude fell more than 6% toward $97 a barrel on the diplomatic headlines, but the move appears to reflect relief at reduced tail risk rather than conviction that an end to the conflict is imminent. Iran's statement that foreign ships may transit the Strait of Hormuz provided they comply with Tehran's regulations — including, reportedly, transit fees on some commercial vessels — stops well short of a full reopening and amounts to a form of de facto toll extraction on global shipping.

The commodity spillovers are broadening. Urea, the world's most widely used nitrogen fertilizer, has risen to its highest level in more than three years as energy-linked production costs surge and export restrictions proliferate. The risk of a feedback loop from energy into food prices is real, with governments across Asia restricting fuel and fertilizer exports in ways that may deepen rather than resolve shortages — a dynamic with precedent in the 2008 rice price crisis.

Gold's behavior continues to challenge conventional safe-haven narratives. The metal fell 27% from its January intraday peak to its trough earlier this week — its worst multi-week stretch in over a decade — before recovering modestly on ceasefire hopes. The selloff appears to reflect a confluence of factors: the asset was heavily overbought entering the conflict, the dollar has strengthened as an alternative haven, and the shift in rate expectations has undermined the "debasement" trade thesis. The correlation between gold and real yields that anchored its valuation framework through the post-GFC era has broken down; whether this is a structural regime change or a temporary dislocation remains genuinely uncertain.
