 Price shock 



The Iran war is rekindling inflation concerns across financial markets, 
sapping the outlook for global bonds <https://bloom.bg/406Opns> after their 
best start to a year since the pandemic.

Traders are offloading government debt as they game-plan how a prolonged 
conflict may ramp up oil and supercharge inflation. A sustained oil price shock 
of $20 a barrel could raise global inflation by as much as 1 percentage point, 
according to Societe Generale strategists.

Those concerns are eroding the haven appeal of fixed-income assets, with 
sovereign debt from the US, Japan, Australia, New Zealand, South Korea and 
Indonesia all posting losses.

There are few signs of a near-term respite. Traders increasingly are paring 
their bets on interest rate cuts from central banks inthe UK 
<https://bloom.bg/4rKFeoB>, Hungary and Turkey <https://bloom.bg/4cZgDYF>, 
among others.

“Contrary to popular wisdom, a shock emanating from the Middle East that puts 
energy flows at risk generally causes global bond yields to rise not fall,” 
said Gareth Berry of Macquarie Bank. “That’s especially true at a time like 
this when monetary easing has been priced in ahead of time, which now suddenly 
looks less likely to materialize.” —Ruth Carson <https://bloom.bg/42kcWY2>

 Energy crisis 



Oil and gas traders are watching as a worst-case scenario unfolds for global 
energy supplies <https://bloom.bg/3OVISgW>. Tanker traffic through the Strait 
of Hormuz has all but ground to a halt. Iranian missile and drone attacks 
forced the closure of both the world’s biggest LNG facility and Saudi Arabia’s 
largest oil refinery.

The near-unprecedented scale of the disruption means that an energy crisis is 
looming closer the longer the war drags on.

Yet so far, traders are betting the conflict will be relatively brief. Prices 
are surging <https://bloom.bg/3OKgb6G>, but the moves have been far smaller 
than in previous crises. Brent crude is only at its highest since June, while 
thespike in European natural gas <https://bloom.bg/4rIQ0Mb> barely registers 
compared with the moves of the 2021-22 energy crisis.

Donald Trump may have more latitude in the Middle East than his predecessors 
given changes to the global energy map. But the length of the crisis is still a 
key factor.

“We’ve had a somewhat muted response but the longer it carries on, the more 
risk there is of higher prices,” said Gary Ross at Black Gold Investors LLC. —
Jack Farchy <https://bloom.bg/4leedI0>, Alex Longley <https://bloom.bg/4slyGN5>,
