**Peru Between Washington and Beijing**

Keiko Fujimori's election last month appeared to offer the Trump administration a natural ally given her US education and pro-business platform. Her refusal to endorse automatic alignment with Washington, however, reflects the structural reality of Peru's economic relationships: China is Peru's largest trading partner and a dominant investor in mining, energy, and infrastructure, most visibly through the $1.3 billion Chancay megaport inaugurated by President Xi Jinping. The port has become a focal point of US-China rivalry in the Pacific, and the incoming Fujimori government's pragmatic neutrality suggests Lima intends to maximize leverage from both powers rather than accept conditionality from either. This dynamic illustrates a broader pattern south of Colombia where Beijing's economic penetration limits Washington's ability to enforce geopolitical alignment.

**Regional Rate Policy**

Peru's tenth consecutive hold at 4.25% reflects a central bank that remains cautious despite a modest inflation uptick, consistent with a regional pattern of monetary authorities in hold mode after aggressive tightening cycles. The decision implies that the Banco Central de Reserva del Perú views current price pressures as manageable within the existing policy stance, though any sustained pass-through from fuel price volatility linked to the Hormuz disruptions could complicate that assessment.
