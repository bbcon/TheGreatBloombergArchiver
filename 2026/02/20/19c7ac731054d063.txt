 Rate-cut skeptics 



The bond market consensus is that the Fed will cut interest rates at least 
twice more this year. Some big money managers aren’t buying it 
<https://bloom.bg/4as7rdV>.

US growth looks too sturdy to build in much more Fed easing, the skeptics say. 
They point to the increase in employment last month, which beat projections, 
and the flood of money that companies are pouring into the economy to invest in 
AI. And some Fed policymakers already are consideringthe potential need to hike 
rates <https://bloom.bg/4c57JIU>.

For Rob Waldner, fixed-income chief strategist at Invesco, the base case is 
one cut this year. But it’s “increasingly likely there are no cuts” given the 
strength of recent economic data, said Waldner, whose firm is underweight 
Treasuries.

At Carmignac in Paris, Guillaume Rigeade is short US Treasuries and sees the 
10-year yield drifting toward 4.5% in the coming months from about 4.07% now.

Rigeade is looking in part to November’s US mid-term elections. With Donald 
Trump’s job approval ratings waning, the administration may push for more 
spending to benefit households and businesses, adding to headwinds for bonds, 
he said.

Skepticism on rate cuts is starting to get priced in to the currency market 
too, with the dollar poised to notchits best week in four months 
<https://bloom.bg/4qLeYJs> as traders pare back their expectations for easing. 

The debate underscores how tough it is for investors to assess whether 
inflation or employment will be the Fed’s biggest challenge in 2026, not to 
mention how monetary policy will evolve after Chair Jerome Powell’s term ends 
in May. —Alice Gledhill <https://bloom.bg/3GFCE0u>, Michael MacKenzie 
<https://bloom.bg/422rgV4>, and Will Standring <https://bloom.bg/4qMxy4c>

 Calling the top, for now 



It’s not even March yet and strategists are saying this week’s record high for 
European stocks isas good as things will get for investors this year 
<https://bloom.bg/4aJrl2W>.

The Stoxx Europe 600 Index will finish 2026 little changed from Wednesday’s 
all-time high of 630 points, according to the median of 17 forecasts in a 
Bloomberg survey.

Supportive tailwinds have largely already played out, while expectations for 
double-digit increases in company earnings appear demanding, strategists said.

The Stoxx 600 is up 6% this year, adding to last year’s 17% rally and 
trouncing the S&P 500. But eight consecutive months of gains and steadily 
increasing earnings estimates have pushed valuations to their highest level in 
over five years. —Michael Msika <https://bloom.bg/447dEJu>
