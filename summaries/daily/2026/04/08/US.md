US equity futures staged a sharp rally on April 8 as markets responded to the US-Iran ceasefire announcement, with S&P 500 futures gaining 2.6% and Nasdaq 100 futures up 3.3% in early trading. The immediate beneficiaries were technology and semiconductor stocks — Micron rose nearly 10% and Western Digital gained 8.3% on expectations that ceasefire conditions would help unlock helium supplies critical to chip manufacturing — while energy names moved sharply in the opposite direction, with Exxon Mobil down 5.8% and Occidental Petroleum falling 8.1% in premarket. Fertilizer stocks, which had risen on supply disruption fears, also gave back gains. Levi Strauss provided a brighter note, with shares rallying 12% on better-than-expected results driven by direct-to-consumer strength.

On the monetary policy front, Federal Reserve Vice Chair Philip Jefferson acknowledged that the Iran conflict will push near-term US inflation higher while characterizing current policy as appropriate. The Fed's March meeting minutes are due later Wednesday. The more pressing near-term data catalysts arrive Thursday and Friday, with PCE for February followed by the March CPI print. Both are expected to confirm headline inflation has risen materially, with gasoline's year-on-year increase moving from roughly zero to more than 25% in barely a month, likely pushing the headline rate back above 3%. The ISM non-manufacturing survey offered an additional caution signal: more than 70% of purchasing managers reported rising input prices, the largest monthly increase in 13 years, which some analysts characterize as increasingly consistent with stagflationary conditions, though strong new orders provide a partial offset.

Falling oil prices have revived rate-cut expectations, with bond markets rallying and the 10-year Treasury yield declining to 4.24%. Longer-term inflation expectations remain relatively anchored — the five-year/five-year breakeven rate sits below 2.2% — suggesting markets are treating the energy shock as transitory. Kevin Warsh's confirmation as incoming Fed chair remains pending in the Senate, with an expected bias toward rate cuts if inflation data permit.
