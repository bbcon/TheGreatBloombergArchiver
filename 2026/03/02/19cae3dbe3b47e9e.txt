Iran faces a choice between another hardliner or a more moderate figure.

Even as missiles and drones fly 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-28/iran-strikes-key-things-to-know-about-the-us-israel-conflict-with-tehran?cmpid=BBD030226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 across the Middle East, the future of Iran’s autocratic Islamic Republic is 
about to be decided by an election.

With Supreme Leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei killed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-28/ali-khamenei-iran-s-supreme-leader-killed-in-us-israel-strikes?cmpid=BBD030226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 in a US-Israeli strike on Tehran, the task of choosing his successor falls to 
the Assembly of Experts, a group of 88 senior clerics who’ve only performed the 
task once before, in 1989.

Grainy footage <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=NWtAjWz3B9M&t=73s> from the 
time shows Khamenei silently bowing his head as the result of the vote becomes 
clear.

He became the only head of state that many Iranians have ever known, and the 
polarizing legacy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-01/iran-switches-to-survival-mode-after-killing-of-khamenei?cmpid=BBD030226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
 of his 36-year iron rule was on full display in the parallel reactions to his 
death. On social media, footage showed celebrations and dancing in the streets; 
on state TV, demonstrators mourned and vowed vengeance.

Foreign Ministry spokesman Esmail Baghaei said he “hopes” there will be a vote 
“within the next few days,” though it’s unclear how the process would work 
during an active conflict.

The basic choice is between hardliners, who would signal continuity of all the 
contentious foreign and domestic policies that have brought us tothis point 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-28/us-iran-relations-a-visual-timeline-of-their-fraught-ties?cmpid=BBD030226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, or a more pragmatic figure who’s prepared to compromise.

The former camp has a much longer list of contenders, notably Khamenei’s son 
Mojtaba. The latter might include moderate former presidents Mohammad Khatami 
or Hassan Rouhani, but they’ve been ostracized from politics and won’t placate 
a public that’sdemanding wholesale change 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-25/iran-protests-brutal-crackdown-kills-thousands-spreads-fear?cmpid=BBD030226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
.

All the while, the challenge of dealing with an unpredictable American 
administration remains. President Donald Trump has made numerous, sometimes 
contradictory statements on the goals of the attack, including that it could 
end in a few days or last four to five weeks.

He’s said the ultimate aim is the freedom of Iranians and also that he could 
cut a deal with the Islamic Republic.

Hanging over all of this is the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/why-iran-s-fate-lies-in-hands-of-revolutionary-guards-after-us-israel-strikes?cmpid=BBD030226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260302>
, whose power and profit rose more than any other group under Khamenei’s 
patronage.

They’re unlikely to give up their privileges without a fight. — Patrick Sykes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUpqNrs7eAk/patrick-sykes>
