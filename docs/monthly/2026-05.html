Monthly Brief

May 2026

US

  • April CPI rose to 3.8% year-on-year — the highest since 2023 and above the 3.5% consensus — as Strait of Hormuz-driven energy costs generated second-order pass-through into core services, effectively closing the door on near-term Fed easing.
  • The Fed's April meeting produced four dissents, the most divided vote since October 1992; by month's end, futures markets had fully removed 2026 rate cuts from their base case and were pricing roughly 70–75% odds of a hike by early 2027.
  • The S&P 500 closed May up 4.9%, on pace for a ninth consecutive weekly gain, driven almost entirely by AI semiconductor names; Goldman Sachs raised its year-end target to 8,000 with the SOX index contributing roughly 369 of the index's 672-point year-to-date advance.
  • Kevin Warsh was confirmed and sworn in as Federal Reserve Chair by the narrowest margin on record, inheriting a committee whose consensus had already shifted hawkish under Powell's final meetings.
  • University of Michigan consumer sentiment hit its lowest reading since the survey's 1978 inception, a sharp and widening disconnect from equity markets at successive all-time highs.

Monetary Policy and Inflation

The Federal Reserve's April meeting will be remembered primarily for its institutional texture rather than its rate decision. Four FOMC members dissented — regional presidents Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan objecting to residual easing bias in the forward guidance, and Stephen Miran on separate grounds — producing the most divided vote since October 1992. Two-year Treasury yields jumped 11 basis points on the day of the decision, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, while 30-year yields briefly touched 5% for the first time in the year. By the final week of May, with the 30-year touching 5.19% — its highest since June 2007 — the market had fully repriced the Fed's path. Futures assigned roughly 70–75% probability to a hike by late 2026 or early 2027, a complete reversal from the easing expectations that prevailed in late February. The CPI print at 3.8% year-on-year, released in the third week of the month, was the most consequential single domestic data point of the period. Headline energy was the proximate driver, but the report's composition was more troubling: core inflation ex-energy ran at 2.8%, sticky prices re-crossed 3%, and the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply, suggesting the oil shock is generating second-order wage and price effects. Consumer prices rose faster than average hourly earnings in April — the first such occurrence since 2022. The PPI report reinforced the picture, rising 6% year-on-year with electronics components inflating 27% as AI-driven memory shortages ended decades of technology-sector disinflation. Oxford Economics revised its PCE deflator projection to 3.8%, effectively foreclosing near-term easing. The FOMC minutes from Powell's final meeting, released mid-month, showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" preferred dropping the easing bias entirely. April nonfarm payrolls, released late in the month and expected at 62,000–65,000, had not yet fully registered the energy shock's pass-through — the labor market's durability into a tighter financial conditions environment represents an unresolved question heading into summer.

Federal Reserve Leadership Transition

Kevin Warsh's path to the chairmanship cleared early in the month when Senator Tillis dropped his blockade following the Justice Department's decision to end its criminal investigation of Jerome Powell. Warsh was confirmed by the narrowest margin on record and sworn in on the final Friday of May, inheriting a policy framework materially more constrained than the one implied by his initial appointment narrative. Rather than the rate-cutting cycle originally anticipated, he faces a committee where a majority signals firming may be appropriate if inflation runs persistently above 2%. Powell announced he will remain on the board as a governor until the building-renovation investigation resolves — a detail Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized as a violation of Fed norms, invoking the Marriner Eccles precedent somewhat selectively. Whether Warsh will lean on trimmed-mean inflation measures to justify delay, as some analysts suggest, or move more aggressively to establish anti-inflation credibility, remains to be seen. His first substantive test will arrive with summer data.

Equity Markets and AI Concentration

The month's equity performance was remarkable in both magnitude and concentration. The S&P 500 sustained record closes through much of May, approaching 7,400 mid-month and closing the period with a ninth consecutive weekly gain on course. The advance was driven overwhelmingly by the semiconductor complex: the SOX index extended to 18 consecutive positive sessions — the longest winning streak in its history — with the Philadelphia Semiconductor index trading at roughly 60 times trailing earnings, a valuation that prices in substantial margin expansion. Micron Technology reached a $1 trillion market capitalization in 48 trading days, the fastest doubling on record, underpinned by projected earnings growth exceeding 1,200% through fiscal 2027. Alphabet gained 34% in April alone following its earnings beat, adding $421 billion in market capitalization in a single session — the second-largest single-day gain on record for any company. Nvidia approached a $6 trillion valuation and reported 85% revenue growth for the quarter, though shares dipped after hours on guidance that merely met elevated expectations. AMD surged approximately 17% on its raised sales forecast; Dell gained 40% in premarket trade on AI server demand that far exceeded consensus. The earnings backdrop was genuinely supportive: approximately 80% of reporting S&P 500 companies beat estimates against a historical average of 77%, with aggregate earnings surprises of roughly 12% materially exceeding the typical 7% premium. Société Générale estimated 12-month forward profits are now approximately $600 billion higher than four months ago, though semiconductors alone account for roughly 43% of that revision — a concentration that amplifies both the upside and tail risk. Goldman Sachs strategists noted "yellow flags" in the advance's composition: quality stocks were consistent underperformers since Liberation Day, while momentum and liquidity factors dominated. Household equity exposure exceeded even 2000 dot-com peak levels by month's end, a dynamic BofA's Michael Hartnett characterized as "bubbly," noting compressed volatility alongside retail mania. The simultaneous rise in the VIX with equity prices — historically uncommon — may signal aggressive hedging into the rally rather than unqualified conviction. Meta fell roughly 7% after-hours following its earnings despite $56.3 billion in ad revenue, as investors read the company's $10 billion raise in 2026 capex guidance — to potentially above $145 billion — as prioritizing AI buildout over near-term returns. Microsoft's Azure growth disappointed. Apple delivered an above-consensus third-quarter revenue forecast late in the month, providing a constructive bookend. Combined 2026 capex for the four hyperscalers approaches $725 billion, with 2027 projections revised to $968 billion — up 89% year-to-date. Snowflake surged approximately 36% on a $6 billion AWS agreement, while Salesforce fell roughly 3% on a disappointing revenue forecast, illustrating uneven AI monetization across the software stack. Cerebras Systems debuted as the year's largest IPO, surging 68% to a $67 billion market cap. SpaceX filed its S-1 targeting up to $75 billion in proceeds, disclosing $18.7 billion in 2025 revenue alongside a $4.94 billion net loss and a super-voting structure. NextEra announced a $67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy — the largest US utility deal on record — driven by data-center power demand across Florida and Virginia.

Fiscal, Trade, and Consumer Stress

The fiscal and legal backdrop grew more complex through the month. A federal trade court declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful in the final week; the administration sought a pause pending appeals while processing over $35.5 billion in tariff refunds following an earlier Supreme Court ruling. Congressional Republicans advanced proposals to increase Pentagon spending by 44% and lower capital gains taxes — a combination that complicates budget arithmetic — while the Senate rebuffed Trump's $1.8 billion "weaponization" account request, a notable intra-party setback. The CBO projects federal debt reaching 108% of GDP by 2030; Fitch warned the US debt burden remains far above other AA-rated sovereigns. Jeffrey Gundlach placed the probability of a US debt restructuring at below 30% but rising, joining Apollo's Marc Rowan and Bridgewater's Ray Dalio in issuing unusually direct fiscal warnings. At the household level, the energy shock's K-shaped transmission sharpened through the month. Gasoline prices exceeded $4.50 per gallon by late May, with New York Fed research showing households earning under $40,000 cut real gasoline consumption 7% even as nominal spending rose 12%. Spirit Airlines ceased operations. Kraft Heinz's CEO warned lower-income shoppers are "literally running out of money at the end of the month." BofA estimates the full-year energy "gas tax" amounts to roughly 0.5% of GDP — approximately equal to the projected boost from either the One Big Beautiful Bill or AI capital expenditure — a structural offset that complicates the administration's growth narrative and sits in widening contrast to an equity market pricing continued expansion. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi surged approximately 73% year-to-date through May, driven almost entirely by Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix — both crossing $1 trillion in market capitalization — while Taiwan revised its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 9.6% from 7.7%, citing AI-driven semiconductor demand.
  • China's April activity data broadly missed consensus: retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year (weakest since December 2022), industrial production grew 4.1% (weakest in nearly three years), and fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% year-to-date, reigniting the domestic stimulus debate.
  • The Bank of Japan held rates at 0.75% in its most divided vote under Governor Ueda — 6-3 — with a June hike as the base case; Japan intervened in currency markets, deploying an estimated $30 billion to defend the yen near ¥160.
  • The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a third consecutive rate hike in an 8-1 vote, unwinding all of last year's easing, leading developed-market peers in the hawkish response to the energy shock.
  • Huawei unveiled its "LogicFolding" chip architecture, claiming 1.4nm-equivalent performance by 2031 without EUV lithography — a direct challenge to the effectiveness of US export controls and a development that sent SMIC and Hua Hong shares sharply higher.

Semiconductors and the AI Super-Cycle

Asian equity markets produced their most extraordinary performance in recent memory, with South Korea and Taiwan at the epicenter of a global AI infrastructure repricing. The Kospi posted a 24% monthly gain in May alone — its strongest monthly performance in recent memory — with Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix together crossing $1 trillion market capitalizations and the two names accounting for roughly 42% of Kospi capitalization, up from 28% two years earlier. Samsung reported a 755% year-on-year profit increase in the March quarter; SK Hynix controls 57% of the high-bandwidth memory market, a component for which demand is expected to outstrip supply through at least 2027. Korean chip exports for the first twenty days of May rose 202% year-on-year, and Goldman Sachs raised its Kospi target for the fourth time in the year, projecting an additional 20% gain with forecast earnings growth of 300% for 2026 — a characterization Morgan Stanley's chief Asia economist situated within a multi-year industrial super-cycle spanning AI infrastructure, energy transition, and defense spending. Taiwan's revision of its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 9.6% — above even the most optimistic pre-year estimate — reflects an island economy that has become the physical backbone of global AI buildout. Taiwan's equity market overtook India in total market capitalization during the final week of May, driven primarily by TSMC. Taiwan's tech firms are borrowing a record $14.5 billion to fund expansion. The concentration of gains, however, introduces tail risk: some funds were forced to sell positions that breached internal concentration limits, and South Korea's retail margin debt reached a record ₩36.5 trillion ($24 billion) at its peak. An episode early in May illustrated the sensitivity: the Kospi shed more than $300 billion in market capitalization within 97 minutes after a presidential policy aide floated an AI-profit tax via social media, before partially recovering on a clarification. The index fell a further 6.1% in a single session later in May as elevated rate expectations repriced stretched valuations. Samsung averted a threatened 18-day general strike late in May by reaching an agreement with its largest union providing AI-linked bonuses averaging approximately $340,000 per chip employee — a settlement that resolves near-term production risk but sets a precedent for future labor negotiations in an industry generating extraordinary profits. Huawei's disclosure of its LogicFolding architecture at a chip conference in China was the month's most consequential technology-policy development. Presented by semiconductor chief He Tingbo, the approach seeks to improve chip performance by shortening internal data-transmission distances rather than shrinking transistors — explicitly designed to circumvent China's inability to procure ASML's EUV machines. The company targets 1.4nm-equivalent production by 2031, roughly three years behind TSMC's current roadmap. Material engineering hurdles remain around heat dissipation and manufacturing scale, but the market reaction was immediate and the strategic implication — that export controls may be accelerating Chinese semiconductor self-sufficiency rather than halting it — has become harder to dismiss.

China: Growth Disappointment, Governance, and Diplomacy

China's economic trajectory through May presented a persistent gap between external strength and domestic weakness. April PMI data showed manufacturing holding in expansion territory, supported by resilient export demand, while services and construction slipped into contraction. April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above the 8% consensus, with technology the primary driver. Yet April activity data released in the final week constituted a broad miss: retail sales rose just 0.2% year-on-year, with auto sales down 15% and jewelry off 21%; industrial production grew 4.1%, the weakest in nearly three years; and fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% year-to-date — a result no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated. The Politburo pledged to counter external shocks and cited better-than-expected first-quarter performance; Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable. Nonetheless, independent analysts estimate true non-performing loan ratios at approximately 10% of total credit against an official 1.5% — implying roughly $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt — with regulatory forbearance slowing loan growth and trapping capital in unproductive enterprises. Domestic 10-year bond yields remain below 2%, now trading below Japanese government bond yields — a striking inversion that reflects the dichotomy between external competitiveness and subdued domestic demand. Chinese crude imports fell 20% year-on-year in April as state refiners drew on commercial inventories, with approximately two-fifths of China's oil imports historically transiting Hormuz. The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing in the third week of May was the geopolitical centerpiece of the month. China renewed import licenses for US beef plants as an early gesture, and Treasury Secretary Bessent outlined a potential investment board and tariff relief on roughly $30 billion in non-critical trade. The summit produced at least $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchase commitments through 2028 and renewed trade truce language. Yet the "placeholder summit" characterization resonated: Xi's Taiwan warning — that mishandling could push the relationship into a "highly dangerous situation," published by Chinese state media while the leaders were still in the room — was the summit's sharpest moment, and no operational Iran mechanism emerged. Trump subsequently confirmed China has not approved Nvidia H200 purchases and intends to develop domestic alternatives, a reminder that semiconductor competition underlying the summit's cordiality remains unresolved. Beijing's governance posture throughout May signaled escalating economic statecraft. The Meta-Manus AI acquisition was ordered unwound weeks after completion, with Manus co-founders barred from leaving the country and several private firms instructed via the NDRC to reject US-origin capital absent explicit approval. China's new supply chain coercion regulations — characterized by Rhodium Group as a shift from reactive to proactive economic statecraft — allow Beijing to retaliate against foreign governments or firms assisting actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial security. Tax authorities launched a crackdown on circular invoicing in metals trading, rattling copper, aluminum, and silver traders in Shanghai who rely on such invoices for bank financing. Beijing's crackdown on cross-border equity trading was the most forceful in recent years, costing Futu Holdings founder Leaf Li an estimated $1.7 billion in a single session; China also began restricting overseas travel for top AI professionals at firms including Alibaba and DeepSeek. The yuan nonetheless ended May on course for a sixth consecutive quarterly gain against the dollar — its longest winning streak since 2013.

Japan, Southeast Asia, and Regional Policy Divergence

Japan's monetary and fiscal positions evolved in tension through the month. The BOJ's April meeting produced a 6-3 hawkish hold, with three board members voting for an immediate hike and a June move widely anticipated. Spring Shunto wage negotiations delivered a third consecutive year of approximately 5% increases, and five-year inflation breakevens reached 2.7%, suggesting bond markets believe the deflationary era is genuinely over. Japan intervened in currency markets — deploying an estimated $30 billion — pulling the yen from above ¥160 toward ¥155–157, but analysts broadly characterized the effort as increasingly ineffective level-targeting absent a BOJ rate hike commitment. Japan's structural dependence on imported energy, made substantially more expensive by Hormuz, continues to exert downward pressure on the currency through the current account. The structural tension is reflected in JGB markets: 20-year yields touched their highest level since 1997, and Prime Minister Takaichi reversed course and instructed the finance ministry to compile a supplementary budget of roughly $19 billion including revived energy subsidies — a move that pushed 30-year JGB yields to fresh post-1999 highs. Japan's Q1 GDP expanded an annualized 2.1%, accelerating from a revised 0.8%, but household spending fell for a fourth consecutive month. Toyota issued a notably cautious earnings outlook, projecting ¥3 trillion in operating income for fiscal 2027 against a ¥4.6 trillion consensus, citing raw material cost increases from the Iran conflict. In Southeast Asia, Indonesia faced simultaneous currency and political stress: the rupiah hit a record low, Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike to 5.25% — double consensus — and President Prabowo announced state centralization of palm oil, coal, and nickel exports through Danantara, a move that blindsided senior officials and pushed the Jakarta Composite's year-to-date decline to roughly 26%, the worst among major global benchmarks. A Moody's warning on centralization risks followed promptly. India's pressures compounded through May: every one of the world's 50 hottest cities was located in India at peak, with temperatures exceeding 40°C and McKinsey estimating heat-related productivity losses of $250 billion. The rupee touched nearly 97 per dollar, and the RBI is reportedly weighing its 2013 taper-tantrum toolkit. India signed a free trade agreement with New Zealand, following earlier deals with the EU and UK — a structural departure from the protectionist posture that saw average tariff rates rise from approximately 13% to 18% between 2017 and 2020 — while Sun Pharmaceutical's approximately $11.75 billion acquisition of Organon represents one of the largest outbound acquisitions in Indian corporate history. ---

Europe

  • The eurozone composite PMI fell to 47.5 in May — marking the sharpest contraction in two and a half years — while French and Spanish CPI accelerated to the fastest pace since 2024, above consensus, presenting the ECB with a direct stagflationary tension that is increasingly difficult to resolve.
  • UK 30-year gilt yields reached 5.78% — the highest since 1998 — and UK political stability deteriorated sharply, with four junior ministers resigning to force Prime Minister Starmer's departure; Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham emerged as frontrunner to challenge for the Labour leadership.
  • The ECB held rates in April but shifted toward a June hike as base case, with Bundesbank President Nagel stating hikes are "increasingly likely"; ECB President Lagarde confirmed the bank will revise inflation projections upward.
  • The EU finalized its US trade deal text in the final week, averting an increase in auto tariffs from 15% to 25% ahead of Trump's July 4 deadline — though the pact formalizes a less favorable arrangement than the pre-tariff status quo.
  • European bank earnings were broadly constructive: UBS reported Q1 net income of $3.04 billion beating all estimates; BP reported adjusted net income of $3.2 billion, more than double the prior year; UniCredit posted a record quarterly profit of €3.22 billion, up 16% year-on-year.

Growth and the Stagflationary Pressure

The eurozone economy unexpectedly decelerated at the start of 2026, with first-quarter GDP growth falling short of consensus estimates as Hormuz-driven energy costs weighed on industrial output and household purchasing power. Germany's government halved its 2026 growth forecast to 0.5% earlier in the month, and the Ifo Institute's business expectations index fell to its lowest level since 2023. Germany, Italy, and Spain individually outperformed first-quarter expectations in isolation, but the aggregate trajectory into the second quarter depends heavily on whether oil prices stabilize. The composite PMI's fall to 47.5 in May — its sharpest contraction in two and a half years — confirmed deterioration is broadening. France's PMI reading fell to its lowest since 2020 and its Q1 GDP contracted — the two data points arriving on the same day in the final week, reinforcing the stagflationary characterization. Germany was broadly stable relative to peers but its industrial production fell unexpectedly for a second consecutive month in March. France's unemployment rose unexpectedly to its highest level in five years. S&P cut credit ratings for Belgium and Slovakia and placed Finland on negative watch, reflecting fiscal deterioration partly attributable to elevated energy costs. Germany's €500 billion infrastructure stimulus fund may be the primary buffer preventing outright contraction — per Ifo President Clemens Fuest — though the fund's disbursement timeline limits its near-term impact. Oxford Economics estimates the Hormuz disruption could add 0.6 to 0.7 percentage points of additional inflation pass-through, given elevated public inflation awareness. Eurozone exports to the US fell 37% year-on-year in March, underscoring the stakes of the transatlantic trade negotiations.

ECB and Bank of England: The Pivot Toward Tightening

Both the ECB and Bank of England held rates at their April meetings in line with consensus, but the direction of travel shifted materially through May. ECB President Lagarde disclosed that policymakers debated an immediate increase at the April meeting but concluded they had insufficient information to act. By mid-May, Bundesbank President Nagel indicated hikes are "increasingly likely," Governing Council members including Isabel Schnabel and Pierre Wunsch called explicitly for a June move, and Lagarde confirmed the bank will revise inflation projections upward. Governing Council member Kazaks warned the data are pointing toward stagflation risks, while Villeroy's observation that second-round effects have not yet materialized sits in tension with the broader hawkish chorus. Market pricing consolidated toward three ECB hikes for 2026 — a dramatic reversal from February's expectations of unchanged rates throughout the year. The conditionality in Council language — a hike "unless a durable peace deal is reached" — introduces genuine two-way risk: Bund yields fell sharply on thin holiday volumes in the final week of May on Hormuz deal optimism, before recovering partially on renewed US strikes. ECB semi-annual Financial Stability Review warned that asset prices remain "stretched by historical standards" with geopolitical risks appearing underestimated. At the Bank of England, Chief Economist Huw Pill voted for an immediate increase at the April meeting, and Governor Bailey warned that waiting for second-round inflation effects before acting would mean responding too late. UK CPI data released mid-month fell to its lowest reading in more than a year — a divergence from euro-area dynamics that temporarily led traders to pare BoE hike expectations — but over 80% of British food and drink manufacturers surveyed indicated plans to raise prices, suggesting the disinflation reading may be transitory. The UK's energy import exposure creates a direct transmission channel from Hormuz to domestic consumer prices that monetary policy alone cannot offset. Overnight index swaps implied significantly more BoE tightening than was priced before the Iran conflict.

UK Politics: An Accelerating Crisis

The UK's political situation deteriorated sharply through May in ways that carry medium-term economic significance. The week of the English local elections saw Labour lose approximately 1,850 seats and control of the Welsh Parliament — its worst local election result in decades — and Reform UK secured a net gain of 275 seats, displacing Conservative support in traditional strongholds and overtaking the Conservatives as Britain's most popular party by polling. Prime Minister Starmer survived a parliamentary confidence vote over the Peter Mandelson appointment controversy in late April but the political damage was cumulative. By mid-May, Home Secretary Mahmood and Health Secretary Streeting were among those privately or publicly pressing for transition; four junior ministers resigned in an attempt to force a departure timetable. By the final week of May, Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham secured a credible path to mount a leadership challenge, with markets pricing greater than 50% probability of a leadership change before year-end. The pound fell and gilt yields surged on the news, with Schroders disclosing it was actively avoiding UK bonds, citing the risk that a successor could pursue more expansionary fiscal policy. Burnham's mid-week commitment to preserve existing borrowing limits offered some reassurance. Jamie Dimon warned that any post-Starmer government raising taxes on banks would cause JPMorgan to abandon plans for a multi-billion-pound Canary Wharf headquarters. King Charles III's state visit to Washington — including an address to a joint session of Congress with notably pointed political content — coincided with Trump's removal of some Scotch whisky tariffs as a personal gesture, though British officials privately expressed skepticism the visit would meaningfully alter Trump's underlying disposition toward Starmer.

Gilt Markets and Fiscal Stress

UK 30-year gilt yields reached 5.78% — the highest since 1998 — during the third week of May, with the spread over US Treasuries having widened beyond levels seen at the peak of the Liz Truss episode in 2022. The spread has tracked oil prices closely since the outbreak of hostilities, suggesting the Hormuz disruption may be a more proximate driver than domestic politics alone — though the two reinforce one another. Chancellor Reeves navigated a complex fiscal week in the final days of May: abandoning a planned fuel-duty increase, preparing cost-of-living measures including free summer bus travel, concluding an FTA with the Gulf Cooperation Council, and closing a corporate tax loophole projected to yield £300 million annually. The Foreign Office simultaneously announced cuts that would roughly halve its Iran-related team — a resource decision that drew sharp comment. Reform UK deputy leader Tice's public warning against participation in the offshore wind AR7 auction introduces a meaningful policy risk premium for UK green infrastructure assets that warrants pricing by institutional investors.

European Corporate and Structural Developments

UBS reported first-quarter net income of $3.04 billion, beating all estimates, with revenue rising nearly 30% driven by equities and FX trading; shares gained 4.3% on the day and management confirmed expansion of its $3 billion buyback. Deutsche Bank beat on profit and revenue, Santander exceeded expectations, and Standard Chartered, BNP Paribas, and Société Générale all reported Q1 results above consensus. HSBC, by contrast, fell 5.9% in London after missing estimates due to an unexpected UK fraud charge and Middle East provisions. UniCredit's record quarterly result of €3.22 billion contrasted favorably with peers. BP reported adjusted net income of $3.2 billion — more than double the year-earlier figure and ahead of the $2.64 billion consensus — as elevated oil prices and trading volatility drove outperformance. Lufthansa cancelled approximately 20,000 flights over the next six months in response to jet fuel prices, an early and visible operational consequence of the conflict for European aviation. Novo Nordisk surged approximately 9% on Wegovy pill quarterly sales momentum. In Switzerland, lawmakers advanced legislation that could impose up to $20 billion in additional capital requirements on UBS. Publicis rose 2.4% on its approximately $2.5 billion LiveRamp acquisition. Germany formally launched Uniper's privatization, potentially Europe's largest utility transaction of the year. The EU rebooted its Chips Act with a framework requiring an estimated €120 billion in public-private investment by 2035, a direct response to the semiconductor race being driven by US and Chinese capital and specifically to Huawei's LogicFolding disclosure. ---

Brazil: Bilateral Friction and Fiscal Risk

The US designation of Brazil's two largest criminal organizations as terrorist entities is the most consequential Latin American development of the period. The move gives US law enforcement expanded tools to pursue international financial flows linked to the PCC and CV, but carries significant diplomatic cost: Lula has consistently resisted framing Brazilian organized crime through a counterterrorism lens, and perceptions of US interference in domestic security matters are politically toxic in Brasília. The relationship between the Trump and Lula administrations was already strained by trade policy and ideological divergence; this designation adds a new source of bilateral friction at a moment when Brazil is deepening commodity export relationships with China. The macro implications are indirect but real — political turbulence in Brazil historically widens sovereign spreads and weakens the real, though the magnitude of any market reaction depends on whether the designation produces measurable policy escalation from either side. The fiscal backdrop complicates the picture further. Bloomberg Economics characterizes Lula's pre-election stimulus as a deliberate effort to boost activity ahead of the vote. While the near-term demand impulse may support growth data, the strategy risks widening Brazil's already elevated fiscal deficit and adding to inflationary pressures at a point when the Banco Central do Brasil has been working to anchor expectations. The tension between electoral fiscal expansion and monetary credibility is a recurring dynamic in Brazilian political economy, and markets will likely price additional risk premium into Brazilian rates if the stimulus trajectory extends beyond what primary balance targets can accommodate. The wider global environment — US rates moving toward tightening, the Hormuz energy shock compressing emerging market fiscal space, and China's growth disappointment weighing on commodity demand at the margin — represents a less forgiving external backdrop than existed during prior episodes of Brazilian pre-electoral stimulus.

Mexico: Sovereign Downgrade and Trade Exposure

Moody's downgrade of Mexico to one notch above junk in the final week of May introduces meaningful credit and trade exposure concerns for an economy deeply integrated with the United States. The downgrade reflects deteriorating fiscal dynamics, institutional erosion, and energy sector challenges — long-standing concerns that the Hormuz-driven commodity environment has not resolved and may be compounding through higher import costs. Mexico's near-shoring narrative, which attracted substantial foreign direct investment over the preceding two years on the premise of US supply chain diversification, faces questions about durability in an environment where the US trade policy framework remains contested in the courts and the administration is simultaneously pursuing tariff escalation against European automobiles. The interaction between the sovereign downgrade, elevated US rates, and peso vulnerability represents a risk cluster that warrants monitoring, particularly given Mexico's dollar-denominated financing needs.

Venezuela, Colombia, and Andean Economies

Machado's signal of willingness to negotiate on a path to presidential elections represents a meaningful shift in tone, suggesting Washington's pressure campaign may be producing marginal movement. The context is an economy in severe distress: inflation running near 600% has produced majority public support for full dollarization — a measure that would eliminate the inflation tax but remove the government's ability to monetize deficits, a constraint Venezuela's fiscal structure has historically been unable to absorb without substantial external support. The broader regional picture is shaped by the commodity cycle: industrial metals' best monthly performance since January provides genuine terms-of-trade relief for Chile, Peru, and Colombia, offering some offset to the tighter global financial conditions implied by higher US benchmark rates and the Hormuz-driven energy shock. China's commercial outreach in the Western Hemisphere is simultaneously broadening — Beijing's diplomatic rapprochement with Canada, including reduced EV tariffs exchanged for lower agricultural levies, is a reminder that Latin American producers of agricultural commodities and resources face evolving competition dynamics in key export markets. Colombia's approaching presidential election, with a leftist candidate polling as frontrunner, adds to a cluster of emerging market political risk events that Oxford Economics' quarterly survey identified as contributing to the sharpest deterioration in EM conditions since late 2022. ---

Global

  • The UAE's shock exit from OPEC after 60 years removed the cartel's primary spare production capacity buffer and signaled a durable reorientation of Abu Dhabi's strategic posture; US crude exports concurrently reached a record above 6 million barrels per day.

The Hormuz Conflict: Escalation, Costs, and Tentative Diplomacy

The Strait of Hormuz closure — entering its third consecutive month — remained the single most consequential variable in global markets throughout May, with the diplomatic and military situation oscillating in ways that produced sharp intraday moves across oil, equities, and fixed income. Brent briefly surpassed $126 per barrel mid-month after Axios reported President Trump was scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options against Iran and US Central Command was preparing fresh strike plans — a level representing a rise of more than 60% since the conflict began in late February. The conflict's military dimension intensified through the month: Iran attacked three American warships and struck the UAE's Fujairah oil terminal; US forces struck Iranian missile and drone launch sites near the strait on multiple occasions; and Iran deployed midget submarines that have sustained the stalemate despite significant US naval superiority. The cumulative supply loss since the conflict began approached one billion barrels. The final week of May produced the month's most significant diplomatic development. President Trump called off planned strikes after Saudi Arabia and Gulf allies requested additional time for diplomacy, sending Brent approximately 7% lower in thin holiday trading. By Thursday, a tentative 60-day ceasefire extension framework had been reported, with a Hormuz reopening proposed in exchange for a moratorium on uranium enrichment and lifting of some sanctions. WTI fell nearly 10% from its weekly peak on Wednesday following the diplomatic signals before recovering partially as US forces struck additional Iranian targets and Iran's Supreme Leader declared that US military bases in the region "will no longer be safe." Republican hawks argued the emerging terms concede too much. Secretary Rubio acknowledged talks could take several more days. Trump has not yet formally approved the terms, and Iran's Foreign Ministry earlier in the week stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent." Polymarket assigns below 50% odds to normal Hormuz traffic by end-June. The IEA reported global oil inventories falling at a record pace — Goldman Sachs estimated visible crude and product inventories drew down at 8.7 million barrels per day in May, nearly double the conflict's running average — a rate that, if sustained, points toward demand destruction rather than supply restoration as the rebalancing mechanism.

Energy Architecture in Transition

The UAE's exit from OPEC after 60 years, announced in early May, adds a structural dimension to the energy disruption that extends beyond near-term price dynamics. The departure reflects Abu Dhabi's strategic imperative to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand and removes from the cartel's toolkit the spare production capacity that served as its primary price-defense mechanism in 2014 and 2020. Reports that the UAE is reconsidering participation in the Arab League, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation, and potentially the Gulf Cooperation Council suggest a durable strategic reorientation. OPEC+ agreed a modest symbolic increase of 188,000 barrels per day for June — largely a signaling exercise — while ADNOC simultaneously announced $55 billion in accelerated project awards and plans to double Fujairah pipeline bypass capacity by 2027, treating a prolonged disruption as a planning assumption rather than an outlier. US crude exports reached a record above 6 million barrels per day as importing nations sought alternatives, with domestic shale production near-record at 13.6 million barrels per day. Goldman Sachs raised its fourth-quarter Brent forecast to $90 per barrel — up nearly $30 from pre-conflict projections — though spot prices remained well above that level through most of the month. The oil shock is simultaneously accelerating structural energy transitions. European EV purchases rose 44% year-on-year in March, South Korean EV sales more than doubled, and Chinese EV and hybrid exports reached a record, rising 140% from the prior year. China's clean technology manufacturers explicitly leveraged the shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third. Green bond issuance in emerging markets rose 53% year-on-year to $24 billion in Q1 as energy-import-dependent economies accelerated renewables deployment. The strategic logic is clear — sustained high fossil fuel prices simultaneously improve the economics of electrification across every geography and reinforce the security case for domestic clean energy investment.

Global Bond Markets and the Synchronized Tightening Impulse

The synchronized selloff in sovereign bond markets across the US, UK, eurozone, and Japan through May represents a meaningful departure from the post-GFC environment of divergent monetary cycles. US 30-year yields touched 5.19% on the third Tuesday of the month — a level last seen in June 2007; UK 10-year gilts reached 5.14%; and Japan's 20-year rate hit its highest level since 1996. Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields reached its highest since July 2008 at mid-month before partially stabilizing on Iran diplomatic signals in the final days. Producer price indices in the US, China, and Japan all surprised to the upside through the month, suggesting pipeline inflation pressure has not peaked. The common driver is the energy shock, but reinforcing factors — AI-related capital expenditure, deteriorating fiscal trajectories, demographic pressures, and sticky underlying inflation — distinguish this episode from a simple commodity price impulse. All major central banks simultaneously face inflationary pressure that limits their ability to buffer growth slowdowns with easing. The Reserve Bank of Australia has already delivered three hikes, unwinding all of last year's easing. The ECB and Bank of England are converging toward June increases. Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50 basis point hike. TS Lombard's Dario Perkins characterized the environment as potentially heralding "rate hikes everywhere," with 2% targets functioning increasingly as a floor rather than a ceiling. The tension between that tightening bias and the historical precedent of energy shocks culminating in recession remains the defining macro ambiguity as markets enter summer.

Dollar Architecture, Geopolitical Realignment, and the AI Supercycle

A slower-moving but potentially consequential structural development accumulated visibility through May: observable stress points in dollar-denominated financial architecture. Iran's collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin represents a de minimis but symbolically notable bypass of dollar payment infrastructure. China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US secondary sanctions directly challenges the enforcement architecture that gives dollar primacy its coercive utility. Gulf sovereign wealth funds — led by Saudi Arabia's PIF, which cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from near 50% — are turning materially inward, with war-related costs equivalent to roughly 1.5% of Saudi GDP in the first month compressing the petrodollar recycling pool that has historically supported global asset prices. Central banks across emerging markets are increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves; gold peaked above $4,490 in the early weeks of the month before shedding nearly 20% from its late-January peak as rising real yields and speculative position unwinds dominated.
