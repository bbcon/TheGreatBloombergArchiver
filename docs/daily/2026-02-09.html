US

Equity market leadership in flux

Friday's recovery — which lifted the Dow Industrials above 50,000 for the first time — masked a more consequential repositioning underneath. The rotation out of mega-cap software and into chipmakers, equal-weighted indices, small-caps, and value has accelerated, with broader market measures hitting all-time highs even as the S&P 500 and Magnificent Seven sit below recent peaks. The catalyst appears to be Anthropic's release of new AI work-automation tools, which sent the iShares Expanded Tech-Software ETF down 8.7% on the week and prompted reassessments of incumbent software business models. Friday's bounce was thin: volume ran roughly 13% below the five-day average, implied volatility remained elevated, and a 9% rally in Goldman's most-shorted basket suggests short-covering rather than fresh conviction. Goldman's trading desk warned of further selling pressure this week from CTA unwinds. S&P 500 futures were down 0.29% in early trading; 10-year Treasury yields edged up 2bp to 4.23%.

Fed leadership and institutional questions

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent urged the Senate to begin hearings on Kevin Warsh's nomination to chair the Federal Reserve, despite a Republican senator's pledge to block all such nominations pending resolution of a criminal investigation involving Chair Powell. Separately, Warsh floated reviving a Fed-Treasury accord to constrain bond-market intervention — a proposal that has stirred debate across rates markets given its implications for Fed independence and balance-sheet operations.

China trims Treasury exposure; market reaction muted

Chinese regulators have verbally instructed large domestic banks to pare US Treasury holdings, citing concentration and volatility concerns. The market response was negligible, reflecting that Chinese official and private holdings have declined steadily for over a decade to roughly $683 billion (about 7% of foreign-held Treasuries) and have been more than absorbed by other foreign buyers.

Asia

Japan: Takaichi's Landslide Reshapes the Outlook

Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's snap election gamble produced the Liberal Democratic Party's largest postwar single-party victory, delivering a two-thirds lower-house supermajority — an outcome prediction markets had priced at roughly even odds just two weeks ago. The Nikkei 225 surged nearly 4% to a record high, with investors pricing in fiscal stimulus, accelerated corporate governance reform, and the prospect of multi-year policy continuity. The yen and JGBs were notably calmer than the January rout had suggested possible, helped by Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama's assurance that a proposed two-year consumption tax cut on food would not require additional bond issuance. Still, the yen drifted to 157.76 against the dollar, with 160 widely viewed as the intervention threshold. The central tension remains unresolved: Takaichi's fiscal expansion sits awkwardly against Japan's ~230% debt-to-GDP burden, and her well-documented opposition to BOJ rate hikes complicates the inflation response. Evercore ISI frames the risk as a "fat right tail" — a Japanese analogue to the Truss episode — should political authority override fiscal restraint.

China: Treasury Guidance and Lunar New Year Liquidity

Chinese regulators have verbally instructed major banks to limit US Treasury purchases and trim concentrated positions, citing volatility and concentration risk. The directive excludes state holdings, and markets shrugged — 10-year yields rose only two basis points — reflecting that Chinese diversification has been gradual and well-absorbed by other foreign buyers. Separately, the PBOC injected $86.4 billion via 14-day repos, with Industrial Securities forecasting over $500 billion in liquidity provision ahead of Sunday's holiday. The PBOC also extended its gold-buying streak to 15 months. In Hong Kong, the 20-year sentence handed to Jimmy Lai under the national security law will test Western capitals' appetite to press Beijing while pursuing stabilization.

Europe

UK political crisis intensifies

Prime Minister Keir Starmer's position has come under serious strain following the resignation of Morgan McSweeney, his chief of staff, amid the fallout from the appointment of Peter Mandelson as ambassador to Washington. People familiar with the matter indicate a group of cabinet ministers may privately urge Starmer to step down, though allies expect him to survive the week. Markets are positioning for further weakness: hedge funds have built short positions in sterling against the euro, citing the combination of political turmoil, expectations of further BOE rate cuts, and elevated options demand. Gilts declined as the political pressure intensified, though separate UK labour market data suggested the jobs sector is beginning to stabilise as firms emerge from a period dominated by tax speculation. UK Q4 GDP and industrial production are due Thursday.

Global

US-Iran Talks Lower Geopolitical Premium, But Not by Much

The first round of US-Iran nuclear negotiations in Oman concluded on a constructive note, with President Trump describing the talks as "very good" and Iranian President Pezeshkian calling them "a step forward." A second round is scheduled this week. Brent crude opened lower in response, but remains up more than 10% year-to-date, suggesting traders are unwilling to fully discount the geopolitical premium. As SEB's Bjarne Schieldrop noted, "the Iranian risk premium cannot be fully defused as long as US warships are located where they are." Meaningful divergence persists between the two sides: Iran insists negotiations remain confined to its nuclear program, while Secretary of State Rubio has signaled Washington wants a broader framework covering ballistic missiles and proxy financing. Trump has indicated he could accept a narrower deal, which would offer a politically expedient win ahead of midterms — though prior negotiations with Tehran ended with US participation in Israel's 12-day strike campaign, a reminder that the path is not linear. Netanyahu travels to Washington Wednesday to press for tighter constraints on Tehran.

Monetary Policy Divergence: Japan Takes Center Stage

PM Takaichi's two-thirds lower-house supermajority — the LDP's largest postwar single-party mandate — reshapes the global fiscal-monetary backdrop. The Nikkei rallied nearly 4% to record highs, while the yen and JGBs held steadier than feared after Finance Minister Katayama clarified that a proposed two-year food consumption tax cut would not require additional debt issuance. The tension remains structural: planned fiscal expansion against a 230% debt-to-GDP backdrop, combined with implicit pressure on the BOJ to delay further hikes, sits awkwardly with a yen trading near 157 and approaching levels markets associate with intervention. Evercore ISI flags a "fat right tail" risk of a Truss-style bond episode should fiscal restraint erode.

Cross-Currents: China Trims Treasuries, Gold Rebounds

Chinese regulators have verbally instructed banks to reduce US Treasury holdings on concentration and volatility concerns. Market reaction was muted — 10-year yields rose only two basis points — reflecting that Chinese diversification has been underway for a decade. Gold reclaimed $5,000/oz, recovering roughly half its late-January drawdown, with Bessent attributing recent volatility partly to Chinese trading activity. Separately, Trump removed the 25% tariff surcharge on Indian goods tied to Russian oil purchases, a step that may halve India's Russian crude imports and redirect flows toward US and Venezuelan barrels.
