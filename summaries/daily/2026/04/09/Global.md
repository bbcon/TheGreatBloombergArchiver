The central market event of the week remains the fragile US-Iran ceasefire announced Tuesday, which has delivered a sharp but already wavering relief rally. World equities gained 3.26% on Wednesday; oil fell 12.1% — its largest single-day decline since April 2020 — before rebounding approximately 4% on Thursday as Tehran alleged ceasefire violations and Israeli strikes in Lebanon intensified. Vice President Vance will lead US-Iran talks in Islamabad on Saturday, and Trump has pledged to keep US forces in the Persian Gulf pending a final agreement.

The Strait of Hormuz remains functionally closed. Only three vessels transited on Wednesday following the ceasefire announcement. Physical crude markets remain acutely stressed: Dated Brent struck a record above $140 per barrel on Tuesday before falling to around $120, still commanding a spread of more than $20 above benchmark for North Sea grades. The EIA estimates the conflict has removed approximately 314 million barrels from global supply to date — roughly 80% of the IEA's record emergency reserve release. Goldman Sachs has flagged Brent averaging above $100 through 2026 if the Strait remains closed for another month. Japan is reportedly considering releasing the equivalent of twenty days of consumption from strategic reserves.

The structural consequences of the episode are becoming clearer across several dimensions. First, the economics of modern warfare have shifted materially: cheap attritable drones costing $20,000 are being neutralized by $4 million interceptors, creating an unsustainable cost asymmetry that is drawing US missile inventories away from Indo-Pacific theaters and raising concerns about a security vacuum in Asia — a point explicitly raised by Japan's defense minister. Second, the energy transition is being accelerated by necessity rather than policy, with Asian economies now facing urgent pressure to reduce Middle East import dependence; China's position as the dominant clean-technology supplier makes it a structural beneficiary of this reorientation. Third, emerging market capital flows, which had reached near two-decade highs earlier in 2026, suffered approximately $60 billion in equity outflows during the crisis — a concentrated rather than systemic shock, differentiated primarily by energy import dependence. Wednesday's rally partially reversed those outflows, but the durability of the recovery hinges on whether the Strait reopens in practice, not merely in principle.
