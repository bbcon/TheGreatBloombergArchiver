Is This It? <https://bloom.bg/4gCOUik> 

Tech stocks are in the throes of a big selloff. It can’t be blamed on 
geopolitics, with oil markets calming down as a messy compromise takes shape in 
the Middle East. And there’s no big new news — barring, possibly, imminent 
results from Micron Technology Inc., these days a $1.2 trillion company. So is 
this the moment the bubble bursts?

Probably not, but it’s a mighty interesting juncture. And it’s also important 
to keep two different cycles separate. First, there are the fundamentals of 
what tech companies are making and selling — they’re undeniably growing fast, 
but that makes it much harder to gauge what they’re worth. Second, there are 
what market people call the technicals — the waves of mass psychology that show 
up in charts, and which plainly show that we reached the point of 
over-excitement a while ago.

The events of the last 24 hours are purely to do with the latter. The spark 
that lit the flameappears to have come from Korea <https://bloom.bg/4w59JaL>. 
SK Hynix Inc. — another freshly minted trillion-dollar chipmaker — had nothing 
significant to announce. But one news item attracted attention: The company 
said it was“shifting its focus to the general-purpose DRAM market.” 
<https://bloom.bg/3StuWN0> Memory chips are less profitable, and this suggested 
to some traders that maybe the company wasn’t quite as superb a license to 
print money as they had thought.

There was a lot of room to rethink, because Hynix’s shares have been taken on 
a breakneck race that makes even the 2000 dot-com bubble pale by comparison:

The Korean market had been growing more volatile, so a brutal drop of more 
than 10% wasn’t so surprising:

The risk is that trading activity could feed on itself. People have started 
holding leveraged exchange-traded funds that hold only one or two companies, 
and they’re very popular.This is what has happened to assets 
<https://bloom.bg/4aNk6Yu> in the CSOP double-leveraged SK Hynix ETF 
<https://bloom.bg/3Slsh8d>, traded in Hong Kong, which offers to double each 
day’s returns, positive or negative, in the South Korean chipmaker:

Korean regulators recently allowed similar single-company ETFs in Korea — and 
spooked the market this week bypublicly regretting doing so 
<https://bloom.bg/44cM6kR>. It’s hard to see why anyone would ever want a 
double-leveraged ETF <https://bloom.bg/4esWGtU>, particularly in a company 
already prone to big movements. Leverage might help add juice to a sure-fire, 
low-return investment to make it worthwhile — but making an already risky bet 
even more volatile is plain dumb. In this case, if the ETF is working according 
plan, holders should have seen yesterday’s 13.2% fall for Hynix translated into 
a 26.4% loss. No wonder Korea’s regulators are kicking themselves for allowing 
them, while investors in the rest of the world are alarmed that the plunge 
could feed on itself.

Once an ETF is launched, people will tend to use it. The financial industry 
will take every opportunity to sell it — but if your investment case relies on 
new suckers being lured in to buy ETFs, it’s probably not a good one. For a 
recent case in point, Bitcoin — once the ultimate anti-establishment investment 
— went on a massive rally after big institutions persuaded the Securities and 
Exchange Commission to license spot Bitcoin ETFs. That is now well and truly 
over:

The events in Korea predictably had their greatest impact on US momentum 
stocks, which have been winning of late. The ETF tracking the S&P 500 momentum 
index had its second-worst session since Liberation Day last year, but that has 
to be viewed in the context of the stunning rally that had preceded it:

The SOX semiconductor index, even after Tuesday’s selloff, remains far ahead 
of its 200-day moving average. Indeed, it’s still more overbought than at any 
time since the peak of the dot-com boom:

Good news: So far, this isn’t even a correction. Worse news: That leaves much 
space for over-levered investors to suffer losses that they cannot afford. That 
was the risk that preoccupied traders from Seoul to Manhattan. And as the 
market is over-extended, bringing with it the risk of a big reaction to bad 
news, it’s not surprising that people wanted to get out ahead of the next 
opportunity for genuine news on the fundamentals. Expectations have gone 
through the roof for Micron’s announcement after the market Wednesday of 
earnings for its fiscal third quarter:

Micron may well clear its bar. The rally can easily surge on for a while 
longer. But the risk is clear. Even if investors are broadly right that the AI 
story is transformative, and will make big profits for chipmakers, the market 
is so over-extended and speculative that it opens the risk for an accident. And 
we still need to ask whether the fundamental story is right…

Les Software Bleus

The narrative that the software industry will be a prime casualty of 
artificial intelligence hasn’t gone away. In eight months, the S&P 1500 
Software Index has shed nearly a third of its value. Its weakness predates the 
Iran conflict.

Apollo Global Management’s Torsten Slok argues that the sector’s troubles stem 
from AI disrupting incumbents’ business models, while rates that have been 
higher-for-longer than expected put a strain on the debt that financed them. 
Explosive growth in direct lending to Software as a Service (SaaS) firms, which 
rose from $8 billion in 2015 to $538 billion in 2025, leaves much capital 
exposed to both forces:

Concerns over Anthropic’s Mythos and Fable models, which prompted the US to 
impose export restrictions <https://bloom.bg/44r4qGN>, add to the fears. 
Whether software companies truly face an AI “SaaSpocalypse” or investors are 
overreacting, S&P’s software benchmark relative to the SOX shows the scale of 
the mismatch: 

Has semiconductors’ gain come directly at software’s expense, or have 
investors genuinely turned bearish on the sector? Morningstar’s Dan Romanoff 
points out that fund flows into companies tied to AI capital-spending suggest 
that the former is at least partly true. As investors crowd into the 
infrastructure plays, software loses both capital and attention. Does this
signal the end of SaaS <https://bloom.bg/3OzVCKu>? For now, Romanoff notes that 
software groups are reporting solid quarters, but data-center supply chain 
companies’ profits are phenomenal:

Surely that is siphoning at least some investment dollars away from software. 
We do not speak to many clients who actually think that software companies are 
going to evaporate.

In the long run, it’s even arguable that the sector could benefit from AI. 
D.E. Shaw shows productivity growth has risen above 5% in the industries most 
exposed to AI, including software. That’s roughly double the economy-wide pace, 
suggesting that the technology may already be delivering measurable gains in 
output per worker. That would be potentially fantastic news for economic 
growth, inflation and interest rates. It would also mean that software’s death 
had been much exaggerated.

Even after semis’ mini-crash, Bank of America Corp.’s Vivek Arya argues that 
“memory chip shortages and price inflation remain the critical moving pieces” 
to support the sector. BofA now expects US chip demand to reach $2.7 trillion 
by 2030, up from their previous estimate of $2.3 trillion, implying annual 
growth of 28% between 2025 and 2030. The upgrade is driven mainly by stronger 
demand for memory chips and AI data-center hardware.

But with AI becoming cheaper to deploy and investors increasingly questioning 
the valuations underpinning the ecosystem, BNY’s Bob Savage questions whether 
the urgency to build at the current pace can last:

For equity investors, the central question is shifting from whether AI demand 
exists to how efficiently that demand translates into sustainable returns 
across the technology value chain. Recent market performance reflects both 
cyclical influences, including energy price volatility and geopolitical 
disruptions, and structural forces tied to AI adoption.

There might yet be another fresh surge in AI adoption. In particular, agentic 
AI could push demand beyond expectations even as models become more efficient. 
Such fundamentals will ultimately decide the future of the industry. In the 
shorter term, however, those factors are so hard to gauge in real time that 
more volatility seems more or less a given.

—Richard Abbey


India: Innocent Bystander-in-Chief

Few if any innocent bystanders have suffered as much collateral damage from 
the Iran conflict asIndia <https://bloom.bg/4w5AX0Q>. An energy importer 
situated close to the war zone, and emerging from a period when many investors 
began to think they’d overvalued Indian assets, the nation has taken a serious 
pummeling. With peace apparently now breaking out, it’s the natural candidate 
to profit the most from any rebound.

MSCI’s India index brutally lags the rest of the world:

Over the last few months, as the country has had to deal with the blocked 
Strait of Hormuz, that has been expressed almost entirely in a collapse in its 
relative valuation:

It’s plain the war really had an adverse effect, and plenty of good underlying 
reasons to buy into its stock market remain. Ajay Rajadhyaksha of Barclays Plc 
argued that this was a compelling opportunity:

The economy grew 7.7% in the fiscal year ending in March, with the final 
quarter printing at 7.8% — ahead of both the Reserve Bank’s and the market’s 
expectations. And yet, foreign investors have sold more Indian equity in the 
first five months of 2026 than across 2025. India’s weight in the MSCI Emerging 
Markets index has collapsed from 20% at its 2024 peak to under 12%... A 
disconnect of this magnitude between fundamentals and positioning does not come 
along often.

India enjoyed steady net investment inflows for years, making their reversal 
as private equity operators and public portfolio managers took profits 
particularly difficult. There are early signs of inflows returning, as Societe 
Generale SA illustrates:

The war inflicted surprisingly little broad financial damage, and has thus 
left disappointingly few opportunities in its wake. Indian equities could just 
be the big exception.
