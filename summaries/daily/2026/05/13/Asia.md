The Trump-Xi summit in Beijing dominates the regional agenda. Trump arrived Wednesday evening for a 36-hour meeting that encompasses trade, tariffs, critical minerals, Taiwan, and the Iran war. The delegation's composition is itself a signal: Jensen Huang of Nvidia was a last-minute addition to a group that already included Apple's Tim Cook, Tesla's Elon Musk, Boeing's Kelly Ortberg, and Goldman Sachs' David Solomon. Huang's presence thrusts AI and semiconductor access into the heart of the agenda — he has characterised the Chinese market as a $50 billion opportunity. Pre-summit working-level talks between Treasury Secretary Bessent and Vice Premier He Lifeng began in Seoul, the first face-to-face contact since Paris two months ago.

One structural outcome receiving attention ahead of the meeting is the possibility of a currency accord — what TS Lombard characterises as a "Beijing Accord" under which the yuan would be allowed to appreciate. Goldman Sachs estimates the yuan is more than 20% undervalued; a stronger currency would serve China's domestic demand agenda while satisfying Washington's manufacturing competitiveness objectives. The question is whether either side has sufficient trust to formalise such an arrangement.

China's economic data continue to present a contradictory picture. April exports rose 14% year-on-year, well above the 8% consensus, with technology the primary driver. At the same time, the bond market reflects subdued domestic growth and inflation expectations, with 10-year yields below 2% — now below Japanese government bond yields, a striking inversion. One analyst estimates hidden non-performing loans in the banking system at roughly 10% of total credit, implying approximately $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt.

Elsewhere in the region, India more than doubled gold and silver import tariffs to approximately 15%, pairing Prime Minister Modi's appeal for citizens to curb bullion purchases with formal policy tightening in an attempt to defend the rupee and shore up foreign exchange reserves. The move adds to pressure on already weak Indian equity markets. In Japan, 20-year JGB yields touched their highest level since 1997, driven by elevated energy import costs feeding domestic inflation. SoftBank reported a surprise jump in quarterly profit, largely attributable to valuation gains on its OpenAI stake.
