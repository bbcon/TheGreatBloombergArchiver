Asia is bearing the sharpest economic costs of the Strait of Hormuz disruption. Saudi Aramco has been forced to reroute some flows through its Red Sea pipeline, a partial workaround that still leaves China and India facing reduced crude shipments from the kingdom next month. India, where over 330 million households rely on LPG for cooking and roughly 90% of supply transits the Middle East, has seen delivery times stretch from five to 25 days in some regions, triggering a run on electric induction stoves with online sales rising as much as 30-fold. Across Southeast Asia, consumers are shifting toward BYD electric vehicles, while Pakistan stands to save an estimated $6.3 billion this year by substituting solar for oil and gas at elevated prices — a structural response that the war may be accelerating beyond what the energy transition alone would have achieved.

Governments across the region have shifted to emergency energy footing, cutting work weeks, switching off street lighting, and limiting petrol station hours. The IMF is actively modelling which economies may require fresh financing if the conflict extends, with energy-dependent emerging Asia the primary area of concern. China's small and medium-sized enterprises are already reporting surging input costs, with some reducing headcount. BlackRock President Rob Kapito warned that oil could still spike toward $150 a barrel even if the fighting stopped tomorrow, while Apollo's Jim Zelter flagged rising recession risk and credit cycle strain from a prolonged conflict.

On a more constructive note, the Trump-Xi summit has been confirmed for May 14-15 in Beijing, a development that provides some stabilization to US-China relations after weeks of additional uncertainty. Meanwhile, China's semiconductor ambitions advanced: ChangXin Memory Technologies reportedly more than doubled revenue to approximately $8 billion in 2025, driven by AI chip demand, as Beijing continues its push toward domestic self-sufficiency in advanced semiconductors. Moonshot AI, maker of the Kimi chatbot, is exploring a Hong Kong IPO alongside up to $1 billion in private funding, reflecting continued investor appetite for Chinese AI assets.
