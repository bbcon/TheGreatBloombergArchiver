China Angst

Europe is facing a new cycle of Chinese rivalry heralded by its latest roadmap 
for economic advancement,as we explain here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-07/european-industry-is-in-crosshairs-of-china-s-new-five-year-plan?cmpid=BBD050726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260507&utm_content=4204>
.

Beijing’s five-year plan issued in March articulates aspirations for 
technological sophistication that amount to another big warning to the region’s 
businesses: the same economy whose rise Germany and other manufacturing hubs 
long benefited from is now, more than ever, a threat to prosperity.

China’s goals include the continuing modernization of traditional industries 
like chemicals and machineries, on which big European companies built their 
riches. But it also aims to cultivate emerging and frontier technologies like 
robotics, biomedicine and nuclear fusion energy, while catching up with western 
spending levels on research and development.

The angst is particular intense in Germany, which has the biggest trade volume 
with China among European Union nations. Claudia Barkowsky, the Beijing 
representative of machinery industry group VDMA, says the latest five-year plan 
has sparked unprecedented interest.

“Companies want to know what the plan means for the future,” she said. “It 
definitely means more competition, because China is now shifting massively from 
scale toward quality, efficiency, and sustainability.”

Such threats have prompted soul searching on how to respond, and on whether 
the EU’s commitment to free trade can be sustained. Crafting a common position 
has been difficult.

The European Commission has proposed to support domestic manufacturing by 
giving it a preference in public procurement, though the bill was repeatedly 
delayed amid internal divisions. Further debate will probably follow when it’s 
discussed among governments and in the EU’s parliament.

Need-to-Know Research

The surge in US gasoline prices since the Iran war broke out has deepened the 
so-calledK-shaped divide 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-24/how-the-k-shaped-economy-is-hurting-everyone-but-the-rich?cmpid=BBD050726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260507&utm_content=4204>
 seen across the US economy, weighing disproportionately on lower-income 
households, according to an analysis published Wednesday by the Federal Reserve 
Bank of New York.

With the average price of a gallon of gas climbing above $4 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/where-are-us-gas-prices-hurting-most-nashville-raleigh-top-list?cmpid=BBD050726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260507&utm_content=4204>
 by late March, low-income Americans begancutting back 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/rising-gas-prices-from-iran-war-push-us-consumers-to-find-ways-to-save?cmpid=BBD050726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260507&utm_content=4204>
 on their consumption of gasoline, while those on the higher end of the income 
spectrum kept buying at roughly the same pace, the researchers wrote. “A 
K-shaped pattern in gasoline consumption emerged.”

Low-income households, or those earning less than $40,000 a year, saw a 12% 
increase in their nominal gas spending and a 7% drop in real gas consumption, 
the New York Fed researchersfound 
<https://libertystreeteconomics.newyorkfed.org/2026/05/same-shock-different-roads-a-k-shaped-pattern-at-the-pump/>
. Households making more than $125,000 a year, which the researchers defined as 
high income, increased their spending on gas by 19% in large part because they 
barely reduced their consumption.
