Daily Brief

Thursday, July 16, 2026

US

  • June PPI came in softer than expected on core measures, with PPI ex-food and energy rising 4.7% YoY; headline PPI declined sharply due to a 12% drop in gasoline prices, though core pipeline pressures remain elevated.
  • Fed Chair Warsh testified before Congress, downplaying AI-driven inflation fears and maintaining the central bank's independence from political pressure.
  • US equities gained on the session despite continued chipmaker weakness; top six US bank shares have risen an average of ~80% since the 2024 election.
  • TSMC's 77% quarterly profit surge and raised capex outlook bolstered AI sentiment, but its ADRs fell ~3.8% in premarket as semiconductor valuation concerns persist.

Inflation and Monetary Policy

June producer price data presented a mixed picture. The headline PPI declined materially, driven largely by a 12% drop in gasoline prices—a reflection of oil's mid-year retreat rather than broad disinflation. Stripping out energy, however, core PPI components continued to trend higher, a distinction that will matter considerably if crude prices remain elevated following renewed US-Iran hostilities. Fed Chair Warsh used his congressional testimony to characterize AI as "inflationary before it becomes disinflationary," while affirming the Fed's operational independence. The minutes from last month's FOMC meeting had signaled a hawkish lean, but the softer headline inflation prints appear to have moderated rate expectations, with Treasury yields narrowing their premium over global peers—a development that carries negative implications for near-term dollar strength.

Markets and Earnings

US equities finished higher despite persistent selling in chipmakers, with broad market gains partly supported by another strong quarter from major banks—the top six institutions collectively generated over $100 billion in profits this year. Sentiment, however, is complicated by the BofA global fund manager survey, conducted July 2–9, which found cash allocations falling below 4%—a level BofA historically treats as a contrarian sell signal—even as geopolitical risks were already re-escalating. United Airlines fell 2.3% premarket on a weaker earnings outlook; Netflix faces its own reckoning after the close, with shares down 45% from their June 2025 peak. ---

Asia

  • TSMC reported a 77% YoY jump in quarterly profit, beat consensus, and raised both its full-year sales and capex outlook to as much as $64 billion, affirming AI demand visibility through at least 2029.
  • The Bank of Korea raised interest rates for the first time since 2023, citing AI-driven demand as a structural inflationary force and flagging persistent won weakness.
  • South Korea's Kospi fell 6.4% as regulators halted new listings of leveraged single-stock ETFs tied to Samsung Electronics and SK Hynix amid a volatility spiral.
  • China's internet majors—Alibaba, Tencent—gained after regulators approved Apple Intelligence for the Chinese iPhone market using Alibaba's AI model, signaling a partial policy thaw toward Big Tech.

Monetary Policy and Macro

The Bank of Korea's decision to hike rates marks a meaningful policy inflection. Governor Shin Hyun Song explicitly linked the move to the AI semiconductor boom, describing it as a structural demand surge that is keeping broader price pressures alive. The won's persistent weakness—counterintuitive given South Korea's record export earnings—reflects a dynamic identified by Societe Générale: semiconductor export revenues are being recycled into foreign assets via equity outflows rather than repatriating, limiting currency appreciation. A similar pattern appears to be at work in Taiwan. Both dynamics suggest the AI boom's macro transmission is narrow, generating output and profits without yet producing the wage and consumption gains needed for sustained domestic reflation.

Technology and Corporate

TSMC's results were unambiguously strong, but markets looked through the beat. The CFO's statement that capital expenditure "in the next three years will be significantly higher than in the past three years" underscored confidence in secular AI demand—yet TSMC's ADRs still declined ~3.8% premarket, reflecting investor concern about semiconductor valuations rather than fundamentals. Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang's Tokyo visit—courting Kioxia, Shin-Etsu Chemical, Tokyo Electron and others over an izakaya dinner—and a subsequent robotics partnership with Fujitsu, Fanuc, Kawasaki and Yaskawa illustrates the broadening of the AI supply chain strategy into Japan's industrial base. ---

Europe

  • UK GDP unexpectedly grew in May, printing above consensus and suggesting greater economic resilience than feared ahead of incoming Prime Minister Andy Burnham's government.
  • Reports that Burnham will appoint centrist Shabana Mahmood as Chancellor—over soft-left favourite Ed Miliband—drove sterling to its strongest level in a year on a trade-weighted basis.
  • France faces a fiscal gap widening to nearly 7% of GDP by 2030 without corrective action, per a Finance Ministry-commissioned report, requiring €126 billion in savings over five years.

UK Politics and Macro

The stronger-than-expected May GDP print offers Burnham a marginally better starting point than markets had assumed, though the durability of that resilience remains uncertain given ongoing trade disruptions and elevated energy costs. The Chancellor speculation is already moving markets: sterling's sharp appreciation on the Mahmood reports reflects investor preference for centrist fiscal management over a more expansionary left-leaning approach. The decision appears not yet final, however, and the Labour left's consternation introduces a degree of political uncertainty about the incoming government's cohesion. British Steel's move into public ownership may be an early signal of the industrial policy direction regardless of who holds the Chancellor brief.

Eurozone Fiscal Pressures

France's fiscal trajectory stands out as a medium-term risk. The Finance Ministry's own analysis projects the deficit widening to nearly 7% of output by 2030 under current policy, driven primarily by rising debt-service costs. The report urges a combination of spending restraint and tax measures—a politically difficult combination ahead of presidential elections. Separately, Italian Prime Minister Meloni's coalition suffered a shock parliamentary defeat on electoral reform, though she subsequently secured lower-house approval, highlighting the fragility of her governing majority ahead of next year's general election. ---

LatAm

  • The US announced 25% tariffs on selected Brazilian imports effective July 22, following an unfair trade practices investigation—a direct escalation that threatens to disrupt bilateral trade flows.
  • Brazil's tariff exposure arrives amid broader commodity market turbulence linked to Hormuz disruptions, adding pressure to an already uncertain external environment.

Trade and Policy

The 25% tariff on Brazilian goods represents the most concrete bilateral trade action the US has taken against a major Latin American economy since reimposing the Iran blockade reshaped global supply chains. The goods targeted have not been fully specified in available reporting, but the move follows a formal Section 301-style investigation into alleged unfair trade practices. The timing is awkward for Brazil: commodity markets are already under stress from Hormuz-related fertilizer disruptions—a dynamic that contributed to Syngenta delaying its planned Hong Kong IPO—and the additional tariff burden risks compressing export margins precisely when external support is most needed. How Brasília responds, and whether retaliatory measures are considered, will be an important signal for regional trade dynamics in coming weeks. ---

Global

  • Brent crude traded near $84–$85/barrel after the US struck a sanctioned supertanker deep inside the Persian Gulf near Iran's main oil export terminal—the first such vessel strike since reimposing the blockade.
  • Prediction markets now assign less than 50% probability to normal Hormuz traffic resuming by year-end, a sharp deterioration from earlier in July.
  • Asian spot LNG prices climbed to their highest level since March as traders priced in prolonged corridor disruption.
  • A Pew Research Center survey found more countries view China favorably than the US for the first time in nearly 20 years, reflecting a collapse in US favorability linked to tariffs, Greenland demands, and the Iran conflict.

Hormuz Escalation

The US decision to strike a supertanker deep within the Persian Gulf—well beyond the Strait of Hormuz itself—may signal a meaningful expansion of enforcement scope. The operation near Kharg Island, Iran's primary crude export terminal, raises the operational stakes considerably: prior interdiction activity had been concentrated near the strait's mouth, and this strike suggests US forces are now prepared to contest Iranian shipping throughout the Gulf. India's subsequent order barring its seafarers from Hormuz transits illustrates the cascading effects on the human infrastructure of global trade. The IEA's executive director warned the global economy faces a renewed challenge if the disruption extends beyond several weeks—a threshold that prediction markets suggest is increasingly likely to be breached.

Macro Implications of Prolonged Disruption

The tension between backward-looking June data—which showed moderating inflation and a cooling labor market—and the forward trajectory of energy prices is the central analytical challenge across asset classes. June PPI's headline improvement was heavily energy-driven; with Brent now 33% above its start-of-year level and prediction markets pricing persistent disruption, the question of whether businesses must eventually pass higher input costs to consumers reasserts itself. Fund managers surveyed by BofA in early July adopted a "no landing" base case and reduced cash to levels that historically trigger contrarian sell signals—a positioning established on assumptions that may prove too optimistic if the Hormuz situation deteriorates materially. The dollar's trajectory adds a further layer of complexity: structurally overvalued by broad consensus, supported near-term by geopolitical haven flows, but increasingly undermined by narrowing yield differentials.
