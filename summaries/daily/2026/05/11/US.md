US equity markets continue to trade at all-time highs, with the S&P 500 blowing through first-quarter earnings estimates in a fashion that appears qualitatively different from typical beat-and-raise cycles. The semiconductor sector is the primary driver: demand from AI-related data center buildout is surging while supply remains tighter than anticipated. Brokers now forecast that next year's earnings for the SOX semiconductor index will approach double last year's levels. The broader market concentration story has intensified alongside this rally — the cap-weighted S&P 500 is widening its gap over the equal-weighted version, and the SOX index has now outperformed the Magnificent Seven since the ChatGPT launch. Alphabet, meanwhile, is closing on Nvidia as the world's largest company by market cap, with a market capitalization of $4.8 trillion against Nvidia's $5.2 trillion following a 34% gain in Alphabet shares in April alone.

The macroeconomic backdrop is more complicated. April non-farm payrolls rose 115,000 and the unemployment rate held steady, a reading that most analysts characterized as Goldilocks-adjacent but consistent with a labor market entering what Macquarie's Viktor Shvets described as an "unstable equilibrium" — no hiring but also no firing. The more pressing concern is inflation. Tuesday's CPI print is expected to show headline inflation approaching 4%, driven by elevated oil prices stemming from the Hormuz closure. Oxford Economics pushed its next Fed cut forecast from June to December, and futures markets price the Fed on hold into the second half of 2026 at the earliest. The combination of strong earnings and restrictive-for-longer monetary policy represents an awkward but, for now, equity-supportive equilibrium. Separately, a federal trade court declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful, a fresh legal setback for the administration's trade agenda, though the practical market implications remain to be seen given ongoing appeals.
