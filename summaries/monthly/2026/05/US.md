• April CPI rose to 3.8% year-on-year — the highest since 2023 and above the 3.5% consensus — as Strait of Hormuz-driven energy costs generated second-order pass-through into core services, effectively closing the door on near-term Fed easing.
• The Fed's April meeting produced four dissents, the most divided vote since October 1992; by month's end, futures markets had fully removed 2026 rate cuts from their base case and were pricing roughly 70–75% odds of a hike by early 2027.
• The S&P 500 closed May up 4.9%, on pace for a ninth consecutive weekly gain, driven almost entirely by AI semiconductor names; Goldman Sachs raised its year-end target to 8,000 with the SOX index contributing roughly 369 of the index's 672-point year-to-date advance.
• Kevin Warsh was confirmed and sworn in as Federal Reserve Chair by the narrowest margin on record, inheriting a committee whose consensus had already shifted hawkish under Powell's final meetings.
• University of Michigan consumer sentiment hit its lowest reading since the survey's 1978 inception, a sharp and widening disconnect from equity markets at successive all-time highs.

**Monetary Policy and Inflation**

The Federal Reserve's April meeting will be remembered primarily for its institutional texture rather than its rate decision. Four FOMC members dissented — regional presidents Hammack, Kashkari, and Logan objecting to residual easing bias in the forward guidance, and Stephen Miran on separate grounds — producing the most divided vote since October 1992. Two-year Treasury yields jumped 11 basis points on the day of the decision, the largest Fed-day move since January 2022, while 30-year yields briefly touched 5% for the first time in the year. By the final week of May, with the 30-year touching 5.19% — its highest since June 2007 — the market had fully repriced the Fed's path. Futures assigned roughly 70–75% probability to a hike by late 2026 or early 2027, a complete reversal from the easing expectations that prevailed in late February.

The CPI print at 3.8% year-on-year, released in the third week of the month, was the most consequential single domestic data point of the period. Headline energy was the proximate driver, but the report's composition was more troubling: core inflation ex-energy ran at 2.8%, sticky prices re-crossed 3%, and the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply, suggesting the oil shock is generating second-order wage and price effects. Consumer prices rose faster than average hourly earnings in April — the first such occurrence since 2022. The PPI report reinforced the picture, rising 6% year-on-year with electronics components inflating 27% as AI-driven memory shortages ended decades of technology-sector disinflation. Oxford Economics revised its PCE deflator projection to 3.8%, effectively foreclosing near-term easing. The FOMC minutes from Powell's final meeting, released mid-month, showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" preferred dropping the easing bias entirely. April nonfarm payrolls, released late in the month and expected at 62,000–65,000, had not yet fully registered the energy shock's pass-through — the labor market's durability into a tighter financial conditions environment represents an unresolved question heading into summer.

**Federal Reserve Leadership Transition**

Kevin Warsh's path to the chairmanship cleared early in the month when Senator Tillis dropped his blockade following the Justice Department's decision to end its criminal investigation of Jerome Powell. Warsh was confirmed by the narrowest margin on record and sworn in on the final Friday of May, inheriting a policy framework materially more constrained than the one implied by his initial appointment narrative. Rather than the rate-cutting cycle originally anticipated, he faces a committee where a majority signals firming may be appropriate if inflation runs persistently above 2%. Powell announced he will remain on the board as a governor until the building-renovation investigation resolves — a detail Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized as a violation of Fed norms, invoking the Marriner Eccles precedent somewhat selectively. Whether Warsh will lean on trimmed-mean inflation measures to justify delay, as some analysts suggest, or move more aggressively to establish anti-inflation credibility, remains to be seen. His first substantive test will arrive with summer data.

**Equity Markets and AI Concentration**

The month's equity performance was remarkable in both magnitude and concentration. The S&P 500 sustained record closes through much of May, approaching 7,400 mid-month and closing the period with a ninth consecutive weekly gain on course. The advance was driven overwhelmingly by the semiconductor complex: the SOX index extended to 18 consecutive positive sessions — the longest winning streak in its history — with the Philadelphia Semiconductor index trading at roughly 60 times trailing earnings, a valuation that prices in substantial margin expansion. Micron Technology reached a $1 trillion market capitalization in 48 trading days, the fastest doubling on record, underpinned by projected earnings growth exceeding 1,200% through fiscal 2027. Alphabet gained 34% in April alone following its earnings beat, adding $421 billion in market capitalization in a single session — the second-largest single-day gain on record for any company. Nvidia approached a $6 trillion valuation and reported 85% revenue growth for the quarter, though shares dipped after hours on guidance that merely met elevated expectations. AMD surged approximately 17% on its raised sales forecast; Dell gained 40% in premarket trade on AI server demand that far exceeded consensus.

The earnings backdrop was genuinely supportive: approximately 80% of reporting S&P 500 companies beat estimates against a historical average of 77%, with aggregate earnings surprises of roughly 12% materially exceeding the typical 7% premium. Société Générale estimated 12-month forward profits are now approximately $600 billion higher than four months ago, though semiconductors alone account for roughly 43% of that revision — a concentration that amplifies both the upside and tail risk. Goldman Sachs strategists noted "yellow flags" in the advance's composition: quality stocks were consistent underperformers since Liberation Day, while momentum and liquidity factors dominated. Household equity exposure exceeded even 2000 dot-com peak levels by month's end, a dynamic BofA's Michael Hartnett characterized as "bubbly," noting compressed volatility alongside retail mania. The simultaneous rise in the VIX with equity prices — historically uncommon — may signal aggressive hedging into the rally rather than unqualified conviction.

Meta fell roughly 7% after-hours following its earnings despite $56.3 billion in ad revenue, as investors read the company's $10 billion raise in 2026 capex guidance — to potentially above $145 billion — as prioritizing AI buildout over near-term returns. Microsoft's Azure growth disappointed. Apple delivered an above-consensus third-quarter revenue forecast late in the month, providing a constructive bookend. Combined 2026 capex for the four hyperscalers approaches $725 billion, with 2027 projections revised to $968 billion — up 89% year-to-date. Snowflake surged approximately 36% on a $6 billion AWS agreement, while Salesforce fell roughly 3% on a disappointing revenue forecast, illustrating uneven AI monetization across the software stack. Cerebras Systems debuted as the year's largest IPO, surging 68% to a $67 billion market cap. SpaceX filed its S-1 targeting up to $75 billion in proceeds, disclosing $18.7 billion in 2025 revenue alongside a $4.94 billion net loss and a super-voting structure. NextEra announced a $67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy — the largest US utility deal on record — driven by data-center power demand across Florida and Virginia.

**Fiscal, Trade, and Consumer Stress**

The fiscal and legal backdrop grew more complex through the month. A federal trade court declared Trump's 10% global tariffs unlawful in the final week; the administration sought a pause pending appeals while processing over $35.5 billion in tariff refunds following an earlier Supreme Court ruling. Congressional Republicans advanced proposals to increase Pentagon spending by 44% and lower capital gains taxes — a combination that complicates budget arithmetic — while the Senate rebuffed Trump's $1.8 billion "weaponization" account request, a notable intra-party setback. The CBO projects federal debt reaching 108% of GDP by 2030; Fitch warned the US debt burden remains far above other AA-rated sovereigns. Jeffrey Gundlach placed the probability of a US debt restructuring at below 30% but rising, joining Apollo's Marc Rowan and Bridgewater's Ray Dalio in issuing unusually direct fiscal warnings.

At the household level, the energy shock's K-shaped transmission sharpened through the month. Gasoline prices exceeded $4.50 per gallon by late May, with New York Fed research showing households earning under $40,000 cut real gasoline consumption 7% even as nominal spending rose 12%. Spirit Airlines ceased operations. Kraft Heinz's CEO warned lower-income shoppers are "literally running out of money at the end of the month." BofA estimates the full-year energy "gas tax" amounts to roughly 0.5% of GDP — approximately equal to the projected boost from either the One Big Beautiful Bill or AI capital expenditure — a structural offset that complicates the administration's growth narrative and sits in widening contrast to an equity market pricing continued expansion.
