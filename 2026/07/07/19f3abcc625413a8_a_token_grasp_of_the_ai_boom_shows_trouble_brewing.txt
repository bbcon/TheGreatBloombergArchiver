More Than a Token <https://bloom.bg/4eSsC9K> 

Amid the ever-growing array of metrics of the artificial-intelligence boom, 
one relatively useful, straightforward measure tracks how much is being spent 
on large language models. Why not just count the total number oftokens 
<https://bloom.bg/3QZN8gM> — the tiny packets of data that are the building 
blocks of AI models — people buy to use them? It’s as close to anAI 
<https://bloom.bg/4f0ocO7> currency as we currently have, and while it might 
not ultimately prove the best gauge of the technology’s trajectory, it’s a 
real-time gauge ofAI companies <https://bloom.bg/4f3WSP6>’ pricing power. On 
this basis, the Silicon Data Token Expenditure Index <https://bloom.bg/4vbCIc1>
’s plunge from record highs offers signs of trouble brewing beneath the surface:

Bloomberg News colleagues Jan-Patrick Barnert and Michael Msika point out  here
 <https://bloom.bg/4vcyUXV> that this doesn’t mean that AI is getting cheaper. 
As the gauge blends prices and usage, a dip could reflect either falling list 
prices, a shift in demand toward cheaper models, or a softening in how much 
buyers are willing to pay. Only weeks ago, the index was trading at twice its 
level from late last year, even as the price of a single token had plunged by 
more than 90% since 2023.

For now, veteran bullish technology investor Louis Navellier argues that high 
token prices could be pushing users toward lower-cost alternatives, 
particularly Chinese offerings, and dampening enthusiasm for all things AI. 
What does this signal for the broader AI trade in the near-term? Not a great 
deal, he argues:

I do not want investors to worry about the selloff in AI and data 
center-related stocks. In my opinion, the big news is that the order backlogs 
for AI and data center-related companies surged to nearly a three-year backlog, 
so the data center boom is expected to persist at least through 2029.

Meanwhile, Navellier’s expectations align with the sense that investors 
looking to find AI winners will ultimately gravitate toward hyperscalers such 
as Microsoft Corp. (currently down 28% from the all-time high it set last 
October), Amazon.com Inc., and Meta Platforms Inc., given the strength of their 
core businesses. As Bank of America Corp.’s Savita Subramanian demonstrates in 
this chart, the AI arms race of the last two years has had an extraordinary 
effect on their free cash flows. Virtually all of the cash these companies 
produce is now going to capital expenditures (meaning chips):

As investors have grasped that these companies now depend on capital as much 
as old-line industrials, eliminating a key perceived advantage that the tech 
sector has enjoyed for decades, so their share have stalled. Attention has 
naturally turned to the massive sums they have to pay to the chipmakers of the 
SOX semiconductors index:

Morgan Stanley’s Michael Wilson argues that chips’ momentum will fade as 
investors grow increasingly wary of their ballooning valuations, rising 
competition, and potential overcapacity — and remember the potential payoff 
from massive AI investments for hyperscalers:

I expect the hyperscalers now to stabilize; that’s what’s been going on in the 
last couple of weeks, and the semiconductor stocks are going to correct… That’s 
a good development. You can’t have this divergence continue; it’s not 
sustainable.

The likelihood remains that AI will positively affect productivity, which 
should ultimately drive the technology’s attractiveness — even if competition 
pushes down prices for cost-sensitive consumers. A recent Boston Consulting 
Group paper by Matthew Kropp and his team analyzed token consumption at more 
than 100 public technology companies with more than $500 million in trailing 
12-month revenue. It concluded that tokens multiply what knowledge workers can 
produce, much as the assembly line did for manufacturing:

The firms pulling ahead are redesigning their processes around AI, with people 
applying expertise to direct and improve the system. These companies operate at 
a pace and scale that human-only organizations cannot match.

Ultimately, they find that if the winners get it right, they’ll create 
workflows that improve organically as AI becomes better, faster, and cheaper, 
and the rewards could be significant. The following chart shows that the 
pattern amongBCG’s survey <https://bloom.bg/4gpvhdM> is already consistent with 
productive token use translating into an advantage:

For all AI’s promise, the path to realizing significant productivity 
improvements isn’t straightforward. Citadel’s Frank Flight argues that the 
journey will be more selective and cost-conscious than markets once assumed. 
That is relevant for asset prices:

The key variable is not productivity alone, but the elasticity of demand for 
the tasks and services whose costs are falling. Where demand is elastic, 
AI-enabled efficiency gains can expand output enough to raise demand for 
complementary factors of production, such as labor.

Over the medium term, Flight notes that improvements in model efficiency and 
power infrastructure can offset some of today’s physical constraints. He argues 
that markets, however, should avoid anchoring themselves to a world in which AI 
is ubiquitous, frictionless, and immediate — even though that is close to what 
eventually happened after the introduction of the internet.

Ultimately, a more plausible path is one of uneven diffusion: Frontier 
deployment concentrated where the returns justify the computing power, broader 
adoption shaped by cost and capacity, and asset prices periodically forced to 
reconcile ambition with physical constraints. Put differently, the period of 
vertical ascent is probably over.

—Richard Abbey

French Encore

French politics is just beginning to appear on the horizon. Last week,  the 
nation set the date <https://bloom.bg/4gpvlKy> for next year’s presidential 
election as April 18, with a second round — needed in the almost-certain event 
that nobody tops 50% — taking place two weeks later. Predictions of aPax Macron 
<https://bloom.bg/3QD0rnu> era fizzled years ago, but for all the incumbent’s 
unpopularity at home, international investors might well prefer him to his 
successors.

Emmanuel Macron’s political authority has never recovered from his disastrous 
decision two years to call snap legislative elections. The same is true of 
French assets. Those elections created a parliamentary logjam with three blocs 
each commanding about a third of the seats. French stocks underperformed 
dramatically during that political crisis. They have more quietly continued 
their slide over the last year:

A similar pattern is visible in bond markets, where the yield on French OATS 
continues to inch higher, and in the process restrict the fiscal room for 
maneuver. Perceived political risk, viewed in the extra yield on OATS compared 
to German bunds,leapt when Macron called the election <https://bloom.bg/4vj1ZkH>
 and has stayed high during the period of deadlock that followed:

Driving the bond market is France’s troubled fiscal situation 
<https://bloom.bg/4wzu5Jz>. The political impasse has stymied any attempts to 
make significant improvements. Morgan Stanley’s Jean-Francois Ouvrard and Jens 
Eisenschmidt suggest that the central government balance for the first five 
months of this year shows a deterioration from 2025, while there are also risks 
from local governments and social security. It now forecasts a public deficit 
of 5.2% of gross domestic product for the year (up from 5.1%). This compares 
with 5.1% last year, and the government’s 2026 cut target of 5.0%.

Today, the “public finances alert committee” (comite d’alerte des finances 
publiques) will meet, and Budget Minister David Amiel <https://bloom.bg/4gZ14Cm>
 set the scene by commenting that all indicators were “flashing red” and France 
was“sitting on a barrel of gunpowder.” <https://bloom.bg/4eWQtVT> A budget 
needs to be presented to parliament in October, in a process that will be 
difficult.

If that is the concrete effect of political stasis, next year might bring a 
drastic departure in a direction that the market detests. After Marine Le Pen 
of the far-right Rassemblement National ran Macron close in 2017 and 2022, 
there appears a strong chance that the party might finally win power next year. 
It’s not clear whether Le Pen will be allowed to run, but the combined 
prediction market odds for her and party leaderJordan Bardella 
<https://bloom.bg/4f0ofth> show that they’re seen with a solid chance of about 
one in three, better than any other single party:

A fractured left includes a number of candidates, such as the far-left veteran 
Jean-Luc Melenchon, who would also alarm investors, while it is hard to see 
voters endorsing anyone promising to continue with Macron’s hugely unpopular 
agenda.

The market is (relatively) calm about the prospect of an RN victory for two 
broad reasons. First, French markets have steadily been pricing in higher 
political risk for a while, so there’s less need for some sudden discontinuous 
lurch once the result is known. Second, experience elsewhere suggests to all 
and sundry that hard-right parties can and should be more pragmatic once in 
office.

 Brexit in the UK <https://bloom.bg/4fqi2HF> makes leaving the EU altogether 
look far less attractive. In Italy, the anti-European and populist coalition of 
the populist-left Five Star movement and populist-right League, which came to 
power in 2018, crashed and burned after it confronted the EU over budget rules. 
Giorgia Meloni, prime minister of the Fascist-descended Brothers of Italy, has 
moderated the tone and overseen a period of stability that eliminated the 
country’s perceived political risk compared to France:

Put all these factors together, and markets are treating France’s intractable 
political difficulties as reason to withdraw steadily, rather than flee in 
fright. The odds are that events between now and next spring’s elections will 
test thatsang-froid.



 Survival Tips 



Some more Americana songs, not necessarily in honor of Folarin Balogun: 
Jefferson Airplane <https://bloom.bg/49W1kym>’s “Volunteers of America,” Grand 
Funk Railroad <https://bloom.bg/4ws01iT>’s “We’re an American Band,” Neil Young 
<https://bloom.bg/4f5WTCk>’s “Home on the Range,” C.W. McCall 
<https://bloom.bg/4f2q9tB>’s “Convoy,” Frank Sinatra <https://bloom.bg/3R2DJ8m>
’s “The House I Live In,”Lenny Kravitz <https://bloom.bg/4gIkpYD>’s “American 
Woman,”The Cult <https://bloom.bg/4f0qRY9>’s “American Gothic,” Stephen Wilson 
Jr <https://bloom.bg/4gZ14SS>.’s (totally different) “American Gothic,” Charlie 
Haden <https://bloom.bg/4aDQKMj>’s “Going Home” (his take on Dvorak’s “New 
World Symphony”) and theVillage People <https://bloom.bg/3HiCDAo>’s “Go West.”

Meanwhile, as it’s impossible not to think about the World Cup at present, 
songsFIFA should have borne in mind <https://bloom.bg/4bcObRr> when changing 
the rules on the fly might include the Human League’sI Am the Law 
<https://bloom.bg/4w7YJtP> and Livin’ Joy’s Follow the Rules 
<https://bloom.bg/4eWQwkx>. Any more? And for a prescient take on the way this 
glorious celebration of international diversity and competition could so 
quickly become an ugly international incident, try reading George Orwell’s The 
Sporting Spirit <https://bloom.bg/4gjN4D0>, published in 1945 after a Soviet 
football team had played a number of English clubs in a goodwill tour. “Sport 
is frankly mimic warfare,” he said, and football is “a game in which everyone 
gets hurt and every nation has its own style of play which seems unfair to 
foreigners.” Painfully true and eternally worth reading.
