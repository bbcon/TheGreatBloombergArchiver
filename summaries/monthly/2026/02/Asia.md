• The MSCI Asia Pacific Index rose to a record, posting its strongest start relative to the S&P 500 this century — up roughly 13% year-to-date against 1.4% for US equities by late February, with January EM portfolio inflows approaching $100 billion, the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades.
• Japan's Prime Minister Takaichi secured a two-thirds lower-house supermajority — the LDP's largest postwar single-party mandate — sending the Nikkei 225 up nearly 4% to a record high on the following Monday and reshaping Japan's fiscal-monetary configuration.
• China's 2026 growth target appears set to be cut to 4.5%–5% at the March NPC, the first downward revision in three years, while regulators moved to end the auto-sector price war and the January factory-gate deflation reading moderated.
• Indonesia's institutional credibility deteriorated sharply through February: MSCI placed its emerging-market classification under review, FTSE Russell followed, Moody's cut the credit outlook to negative, and the rupiah weakened to levels last seen nearly three decades ago.
• India's AI Impact Summit concluded with infrastructure pledges rising from $140 billion to at least $270 billion over the course of the week, establishing AI sovereignty as the country's defining strategic economic theme.

**Japan: Mandate, Fiscal Ambition, and Monetary Tension**

Prime Minister Takaichi's snap-election landslide — delivering a result that prediction markets had assigned roughly even odds just a fortnight prior — was the month's single most consequential political event in the region. The Nikkei 225 surged nearly 4% to a record on the Monday following the election result and added 2.8% on Tuesday, while JGB yields, which had spiked sharply in January, edged lower and the yen steadied; by mid-week of that period, the yen had strengthened to ¥153 from near ¥160 in January, and 30-year JGB yields had fallen roughly 40 basis points from their January highs. Finance Minister Katayama's assurance that a proposed two-year food consumption tax cut would not require additional bond issuance provided an important initial anchor. The structural tension, however, remains unresolved. Planned fiscal expansion sits against a debt-to-GDP ratio of approximately 230%, and Takaichi's well-documented skepticism toward BOJ rate hikes creates an awkward configuration at a time when domestic inflation has not fully receded. Evercore ISI characterized the downside as a "fat right tail" — a Japanese analogue to the UK's 2022 Truss episode — should political authority erode fiscal restraint. Fourth-quarter real GDP expanded just 0.2% annualized, far below the 1.6% consensus, with consumer spending rising only 0.1%; the weak data reinforces the case for fiscal support while simultaneously complicating the BOJ's normalization path. BOJ board nominations for the seats vacated by dovish member Asahi Noguchi and consensus voter Junko Nakagawa are expected as soon as February 25, and given Takaichi's historical association with reflation, the appointments will be parsed closely by JGB and yen markets. Takaichi's $36 billion US investment commitment — anchored by a large natural gas facility in Ohio — serves multiple strategic functions: visible progress on the $550 billion bilateral trade framework with Washington, energy security diversification, and a potential buffer against tariff escalation. The China relationship adds a further complication: Takaichi's comments on a potential Taiwan military response have cooled Beijing's posture, with measurable consequences in tourism flows as bookings to Sapporo were projected to fall 62% in January-February. On the corporate side, Mizuho Financial fell roughly 5% on Monday and 3.8% on Tuesday following disclosure of a regulatory investigation into suspected insider trading at its brokerage unit. The Topix rally more broadly appeared underpinned by genuine governance reform — rising dividends, accelerating buybacks, and a re-rating of long-discounted sectors including non-ferrous metals, with Sumitomo Electric having tripled since the US election.

**China: Policy Signals, Market Bifurcation, and Geopolitical Friction**

China's macro picture through February was defined by a tension between modest activity improvement and accumulating structural headwinds. January services PMI picked up, and January factory-gate deflation moderated more than expected — though Morgan Stanley's Robin Xing cautioned there is "no decisive reflation yet," with improvement leaning heavily on the global metals rally. More than a dozen provinces lowered their 2026 growth targets through early February, and consensus coalesced around a national target of 4.5%–5% for the March NPC — the first downward revision in three years. Citi revised its stimulus baseline downward, treating its previously expected package — roughly one trillion yuan in fiscal support, a 20-basis-point rate cut, and a 50-basis-point RRR reduction — as a ceiling rather than a floor. The PBOC injected $86.4 billion via 14-day repos ahead of the Lunar New Year holiday, with Industrial Securities forecasting over $500 billion in total liquidity provision for the holiday period. Foreign positioning nonetheless improved materially: Goldman Sachs and BNP Paribas surveys pointed to accelerating allocations, Qube's China long-only fund grew more than tenfold year-on-year to $2 billion, and Eight Roads reversed plans to sell approximately $400 million in Chinese tech stakes — a reversal reflecting improved valuations and a lower temperature in US-China relations following a constructive phone call between Presidents Trump and Xi, though substantive trade details remained opaque. Xi's call for domestic demand to serve as China's "main driver" of economic growth represents rhetorical reinforcement of a long-stated objective; the IMF's Article IV assessment noted household consumption has hovered near 40% of GDP essentially unchanged since 2005, underscoring the implementation gap. On geopolitics, Beijing's response to a Panamanian court decision voiding a CK Hutchison port contract escalated materially mid-month: Chinese authorities instructed state-linked firms to halt new project discussions in Panama and intensified customs inspections on Panamanian goods, illustrating how port and logistics infrastructure has become a primary arena for US-China strategic competition. The Pentagon's brief — and quickly reversed — decision to add Alibaba, BYD, and Baidu to its list of companies deemed to be aiding the Chinese military, which sent Alibaba more than 3% lower in Hong Kong before the designation was withdrawn, served as a reminder of how abrupt and unpredictable US-China tech friction can be. Moonshot targeted a $10 billion valuation in a new funding round, more than doubling the $4.3 billion mark set weeks earlier; robotics firms attracted buying interest following their Spring Festival gala showcase. The abrupt closure of all Shanghai outlets of the premium restaurant chain Xiaonanguo days before Lunar New Year underscored ongoing consumption fragility.

**South Korea, Indonesia, India, and the Broader Region**

South Korea's KOSPI gained approximately 30% year-to-date by late February, supported by AI hardware exposure, shareholder-return reforms, and relatively attractive valuations. Former President Yoon Suk Yeol was sentenced to life in prison for insurrection following his late-2024 martial law declaration — formally closing the most severe constitutional crisis the country has faced in decades, though an appeal is expected. Indonesia presented a sharply contrasting picture: the replacement of technocrats with loyalists, a family member appointed as Bank Indonesia deputy governor, and a flagship school lunch program costing an estimated $28 billion annually combined to produce a currency under severe pressure, with the rupiah weakening to levels last seen nearly three decades ago. MSCI placed Indonesia's emerging-market classification under review, FTSE Russell followed, and Moody's cut the credit outlook to negative — compounding deterioration that Jakarta's finance ministry pushed back against in unusually pointed terms. Bank Indonesia held rates at 4.75% as expected but faces a familiar central bank dilemma: rate cuts are warranted by domestic inflation dynamics but precluded by currency weakness. India presented the most constructive regional picture outside Japan: the AI Impact Summit demonstrated genuine convening power with pledges rising from $140 billion to $270 billion over the course of the week, the country formally joined a US-led critical minerals initiative, HSBC's flash survey showed activity accelerating in February, and GQG Partners continued adding to a $24 billion India position. Malaysia's exports rose at their fastest pace in over three years in January. Australia's BHP delivered earnings at the upper end of expectations, with copper contributing more than half of group profit for the first time; the RBA's February minutes confirmed its rate hike reflected a downgraded view of potential output, making it the first major developed-market central bank to tighten in 2026, with markets pricing a further increase amid services inflation persistence. DRAM prices rose 75% from December to January, with Samsung and SK Hynix among the principal beneficiaries as AI accelerator demand crowds out conventional buyers. Indonesia ordered Weda Bay Nickel — the world's largest nickel mine — to cut permitted output to 12 million tons from 42 million, an aggressive supply-side intervention with immediate implications for stainless steel and battery material markets.
