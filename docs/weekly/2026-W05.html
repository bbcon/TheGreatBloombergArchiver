Weekly Brief

Jan 26 – Feb 01, 2026

US

Fed Chair Nomination Dominates the Week

The week's defining development arrived on Friday when President Trump named Kevin Warsh as his nominee to succeed Jerome Powell when Powell's term concludes in May. The choice generated immediate friction between the administration's stated preferences and market interpretation: Trump has consistently pressed for lower rates, yet Warsh carries a reputation as a relative hawk, having criticized accommodative policy during and after his prior tenure as a Fed governor. Warsh has moved to align himself publicly with the president's monetary policy views, but his institutional record and historical concerns about asset-price distortions suggest any pivot toward easier policy would be constrained rather than immediate. Senate confirmation appears probable given the Republican majority, though it is not guaranteed. The substantive question — how Warsh balances political pressure against the Fed's institutional credibility — remains unresolved and is likely to dominate monetary policy discourse through the spring.

Markets React to a Dual Headwind

Friday's session reflected the compounding effect of the Warsh nomination alongside a hotter-than-expected PPI print, which reinforced concerns about sticky upstream inflation even as the Fed's tightening cycle appeared to be in its terminal phase. US equities declined on the day, with rate-sensitive sectors bearing the most pressure. The combination of a perceived hawkish successor and firmer producer prices effectively narrowed the market's window for early Fed easing — a narrative that had provided meaningful support to risk assets through much of January. The S&P's intraday reaction illustrated the degree to which monetary policy expectations remain the primary valuation anchor for US equities in the current environment.

Precious Metals Reverse Sharply

Gold and silver fell sharply on Friday, unwinding a portion of the safe-haven trade that had accumulated through January. The reversal was notable in its timing: both metals had earlier in the week approached or established record levels, fueled by geopolitical risk premia tied to Trump's renewed demands for US control over Greenland and the associated "Sell America" positioning that periodically surfaced through 2025. The speed of Friday's pullback suggests the trade had become crowded, with the Warsh nomination and PPI print providing the catalyst for a positioning squeeze rather than a fundamental reassessment of the underlying geopolitical environment.

Advisor Positioning Tilts Toward Value

Against a backdrop of elevated volatility, Bloomberg's wealth adviser survey revealed a measured rather than defensive response. Few managers counseled moving meaningfully into cash; instead, the tilt was toward value equities and selective international exposure, with Japanese equities cited favorably as a relative value alternative to stretched US mega-cap technology. The posture is consistent with a view that current market turbulence reflects repricing rather than regime change — though the Warsh nomination introduces enough policy uncertainty to keep that judgment provisional through the confirmation process and the May transition. ---

Asia

Fed Nomination Carries Significant Regional Implications

The week's most consequential development for Asia-Pacific markets originated in Washington. President Trump's Friday nomination of Kevin Warsh to succeed Jerome Powell introduced a layer of monetary policy uncertainty whose effects, if sustained, would reverberate across the region. Wall Street's initial characterization of Warsh as a relative hawk — however provisional — implies a trajectory of prolonged dollar strength and elevated US real rates, a combination that historically tightens financial conditions across emerging Asia, pressures local currencies, and reduces the policy space available to regional central banks. The Bank of Japan's already delicate normalization path would face additional complications if dollar strength persists, as yen depreciation pressure would re-emerge precisely when the BoJ is attempting to communicate a credible exit from its ultra-accommodative framework. The PBoC, for its part, would find its room to ease further constrained by the risk of accelerated capital outflows should the renminbi come under renewed pressure.

Tension Between Trump's Preferences and Warsh's Record

The uncertainty is compounded by the unresolved tension between Trump's stated demand for easier monetary policy and Warsh's historical instincts. If political pressure ultimately prevails and Warsh accommodates a more dovish stance post-confirmation, the implications for Asia reverse meaningfully: dollar softness and lower US rates would ease the regional burden, provide the PBoC with additional easing latitude, and support capital flows into higher-yielding Asian credit. Markets are unlikely to resolve this ambiguity quickly, and the period between the Warsh nomination and any concrete signal from the new chair will likely sustain elevated uncertainty in Asian FX and rate markets.

Precious Metals Reversal and Regional Equities

Gold and silver fell sharply on Friday following the Warsh announcement and a hotter-than-expected US PPI print, unwinding a portion of the safe-haven accumulation that had built through January. The reversal may dampen flows that had supported regional gold-linked equities and commodity-adjacent trades, particularly in markets with meaningful precious metals exposure. The pullback also illustrates the degree to which the recent metals rally reflected positioning and geopolitical risk premia — including friction over Trump's Greenland rhetoric — rather than a durable fundamental rerating.

Japan as a Relative Value Destination

Japanese equities received favorable mention in Bloomberg's wealth adviser survey on allocation preferences, with managers citing value characteristics amid a broader risk-off tilt in US markets. The observation is consistent with a theme that has built gradually through late 2025 and into 2026: Japan as a credible diversification destination relative to stretched US mega-cap technology valuations. While this does not constitute a market-moving catalyst on its own, sustained foreign interest in Japanese equities would provide a supportive flow backdrop for the Nikkei, provided yen volatility remains contained.

Europe

Cross-Atlantic Monetary Policy Spillovers

The week's most significant development for European markets originated in the United States. President Trump's Friday nomination of Kevin Warsh as Fed chair-designate introduces a meaningful source of cross-Atlantic uncertainty. The initial Wall Street read — characterizing Warsh as a relative hawk — carries direct implications for the ECB's policy calculus. A sustained hawkish signal from the incoming Fed chair would likely support the dollar against the euro, tightening imported financial conditions for the eurozone at a moment when the ECB's easing cycle has not fully run its course. Should euro weakness prove durable, the Governing Council faces the uncomfortable prospect of accommodative domestic policy partly offset by rising import prices — a reversal of the disinflationary tailwind that supported the 2024–2025 easing trajectory. Bund-Treasury spreads are worth monitoring in the sessions ahead as the market prices the Warsh confirmation path and any early signals about his monetary policy orientation.

ECB Policy Tension Remains Unresolved

The tension in the Warsh nomination — between Trump's demand for easier policy and Warsh's historically hawkish instincts — creates a scenario in which European rate markets may reprice multiple times as the confirmation process unfolds and the new chair's actual posture becomes clearer. If Warsh ultimately accommodates political pressure and proves more dovish in practice, the dollar would likely soften and the ECB's room to maneuver would expand. Markets are unlikely to resolve this ambiguity quickly, and European duration positioning may remain unsettled through the May transition. Greenland and Transatlantic Alliance Cohesion

Trump's renewed demand for US control over Greenland continued to generate geopolitical noise through the week, contributing to the "Sell America" positioning that helped drive gold to record levels earlier in January before Friday's sharp metals reversal. For European investors, the Greenland dispute carries direct relevance: Denmark's sovereignty over the territory is an EU-adjacent matter, and the underlying question of transatlantic alliance reliability has obvious implications for European defense spending, NATO credibility, and the region's strategic posture. Today's source material offers no fresh detail on Copenhagen's response or broader EU positioning on the dispute — a gap worth flagging given the issue's longer-term relevance to European political risk pricing.

Global

The Warsh Nomination as a Global Inflection Point

The dominant development of the week, with implications that cascade across every major asset class and region, was President Trump's Friday nomination of Kevin Warsh to succeed Jerome Powell as Federal Reserve Chair when Powell's term concludes in May. The appointment crystallizes a tension that will likely define global macro discourse through mid-2026: Trump has persistently demanded lower US interest rates, yet Warsh's institutional record — as a former Fed governor critical of accommodative policy and attentive to inflation credibility — suggests a more orthodox disposition. Warsh has moved to publicly align himself with the president's views, but market participants are understandably skeptical that political alignment translates mechanically into dovish outcomes, particularly given the FOMC composition Warsh would inherit and the structural constraints on unilateral rate action. Senate confirmation appears probable, leaving the operative uncertainty not in the outcome of the vote but in the substance of policy once Warsh takes the chair.

Dollar, Rates, and Global Monetary Policy Divergence

If the initial market interpretation of Warsh as hawkish holds, the implications for global monetary policy divergence are considerable. The dollar would likely remain well-supported, compressing the policy space available to the ECB as it seeks to extend its easing cycle, complicating the Bank of Japan's normalization timeline, and tightening financial conditions across emerging markets and Asia more broadly. The scenario in which Trump's political pressure ultimately shapes Warsh's posture — producing a more accommodative Fed — would carry the opposite implications and represents a meaningful alternative path. The difficulty for global portfolio managers is that neither scenario can yet be assigned a high-confidence probability, leaving rate differentials, currency positioning, and duration allocation in a state of elevated uncertainty through the spring.

Geopolitical Risk Premia and Metals Volatility

Gold and silver fell sharply on Friday following the Warsh announcement and a hotter-than-expected US PPI print, marking a notable reversal from the record-level moves seen earlier in the week. The safe-haven bid that drove precious metals higher through January reflected a confluence of geopolitical risk premia: Trump's renewed demands for US control over Greenland, the associated "Sell America" positioning, and residual anxiety about global alliance structures. Friday's unwind illustrates how crowded the trade had become, with a single catalyst sufficient to trigger a sharp positioning correction. The underlying geopolitical drivers have not materially changed, suggesting the metals market may remain volatile as risk appetite oscillates.
