US customs authorities are increasingly turning to AI to catch tariff dodgers 
and identify illicit goods before cargo even reaches the US.

New York-based Tru Identity <https://www.truregister.com/company>, a 
trade-compliance automation platform, is working with Customs and Border 
Protection to develop ways to streamline the goods-entry process and bolster 
national security, Bloomberg’s Laura Curtisreports Thursday 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/us-customs-ramps-up-ai-investment-in-push-to-sharpen-enforcement?cmpid=BBD062526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5745>
.

“For a long time, the private sector was ahead and got used to it,” Tru CEO 
Hugo Pakula said. “Now the status quo has flipped, and people need to realize 
that it’s time to level up or be considered high risk by CBP.”

Under the partnership, CBP gains insights into the advanced screening tools 
that Tru’s commercial users rely on. On the commercial side, businesses using 
Tru’s tools gain confidence in their declarations to CBP because the agency is 
using the same universe of data and interpretation of the rules.

Customs Compliance

While President Donald Trump sought during the first year of his second term 
to streamline the federal bureaucracy in the private sector’s favor, his 
unpredictable tariff policies have heaped additional back-office burdens and 
costs on companies trying to comply.

“Importers have to be extremely flexible, and their systems have to pivot very 
quickly,” said Janet Labuda, senior global trade and customs consultant at 
Maersk, the world’s No. 2 container carrier.

Hundreds of thousands of importers are responsible for roughly 4.4 million 
shipments cleared by CBP each day. The agency is now transitioning from a model 
where vastly more enforcement resources were spent on agents inspecting the 
contents of boxes than on determining which ones to open.

“This kind of specialized AI is the definitive edge CBP needs to effectively 
police the flood, Pakula said.
