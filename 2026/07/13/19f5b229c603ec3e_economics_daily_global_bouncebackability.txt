The ‘Bouncebackability’ Factor

Jacking up US tariff rates to the highest level since the 1940s 
<https://budgetlab.yale.edu/research/state-us-tariffs-april-2-2026>. Launching 
a war with Iran that chokes off more energy supply than the 1970s crises. Had 
economists been given these scenarios at the start of last year, most arguably 
wouldn’t have thought the global economy would be in the shape it is today.

In its latest World Economic Outlook update, the International Monetary Fund 
projected a 3%global GDP gain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/imf-sees-ai-surge-offset-war-oil-shock-and-keeps-growth-outlook?cmpid=BBD071326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6408>
 for this year — not hugely different from the 3.5% average recorded over the 
previous two years.

Part of the resilience, of course, is the enormous artificial intelligence 
investment boom that’s been unfolding alongside the inflaming of geopolitical 
tensions. The fund pointed to that in its new forecast.

But Steven Barrow, who’s been analyzing global markets and economics for four 
decades, argues there’s another key factor. Writing in a note last week, the 
head of G10 strategy at Standard Bank in London pinpointed “the 
‘bouncebackability’ of financial asset prices,” and especially equities.

Despite the tariff drama last year, the MSCI World Index of stocks soared 21%. 
And it’s up more than 10% so far this year. Again, AI is part of the rationale 
there — all part of the“A-pillar” foundation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-11-10/the-three-a-s-in-the-trump-economy-ai-asset-prices-and-the-affluent?cmpid=BBD071326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6408>
 for growth this newsletter flagged in November (AI investment, asset prices 
and affluent consumers.)

There’s also something more structural, says Barrow: the waning of demand for 
US safe assets.

It used to be, during times of global stress, the dollar would surge, along 
with US Treasuries. That would effectively draw money out of risk assets, such 
as stocks. That would also make things particularly hard fordeveloping nations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2023-01-06/what-s-happening-in-the-world-economy-emerging-markets-turn-optimistic?cmpid=BBD071326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6408>
 with large amounts of dollar debt, forcing them to raise interest rates to 
protect their exchange rates — all hurting their growth.

But Treasuries didn’t see any big surge during the hottest part of the Iran 
conflict, which continues to simmer. The dollar actually fell during the 
biggest period of tariff stress last year, “hence financial conditions 
elsewhere did not tighten” as they might have in the past, Barrow says. “Even 
the war in Iran has produced a rise in the dollar that is modest relative to 
history.”

“The decline in the US’s role as the global financial hegemon” means the 
potential for stress to ripple through the world in the way it used to is 
diminishing, Barrow said in an email exchange last week.

Others are also picking up on a shift. The IMF earlier this year pointed to US 
Treasurieslosing some of their premium 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/imf-says-treasuries-losing-premium-warns-us-on-debt-management?cmpid=BBD071326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6408>
, which in turn means smaller spreads for corporate borrowers.

The debate over the dollar’s dominance in the world’s financial system has 
long focused on when “king dollar 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-30/dollar-fears-are-flaring-as-trump-rekindles-debasement-trade?cmpid=BBD071326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6408>
” will lose its crown, not so much on what that actually means. Greater global 
economic resilience may be one potential consequence worth considering.
