Hungarians made history 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-12/orban-concedes-hungary-election-loss-opposition-leader-says?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 on April 12 when they ousted Viktor Orbán following his increasingly 
authoritarian 16 years in power. A classic David versus Goliath story, it also 
brought a sense of déjà vu for anyone in Budapest that day, with jubilant young 
people taking to the streets chanting “Ruszkik haza!” or “Russians, go home!”

The moment felt like 1989 all over again — when Hungary and other Soviet-bloc 
nations ditched Communism for the hope of a more prosperous Western course. And 
it was almost equally surprising. It’s hard to overstate how much the odds were 
stacked against opposition leader Peter Magyar. Orbán’s state media juggernaut 
deployed what Magyar called a “North Korea-like”level of propaganda 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/magyar-tells-hungary-state-media-its-news-coverage-to-go-off-air?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 against him and his Tisza party.

But Magyar and Tisza prevailed, and in decisive fashion, with the effects of 
their overwhelming victory stretching far beyond a nation of fewer than 10 
million people. Orbán’s defeat can be viewed as a smaller version of the Soviet 
Union’s collapse and its reverberations beyond Eastern Europe. Some 35 years 
ago, that geostrategic earthquake helped trigger grand visions, from the 
reunification of Germany to the Maastricht Treaty — which formally established 
the European Union, a powerful force behind the economic and political reforms 
that followed.

Orbán’s tenure didn’t just feature constraints on Hungary’s once free-market 
economy, but it also stymied the whole of the EU, which was unable to move 
ahead with €90 billion ($106 billion) in support for Ukraine as it battles to 
defend itself against Russia.

With Magyar, 45, now moving to improve relations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-04-16/hungary-voters-sent-orban-packing-the-eu-must-seize-the-opportunity?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 with the bloc, restore Hungary’s political checks and balances and mobilize 
investment, there’s a chance to reinvigorate the entire European project in the 
face of severe challenges from Moscow — and, since the return of Donald Trump 
to the White House — Washington.

While in Budapest this week, I found myself reminded of 1989 as I witnessed 
young Hungarians who have known no other leader but Orbán tearing down posters 
of his Fidesz party and gleefully stomping on them. In nearby Poland, I 
witnessed similar scenes as a boy, watching my countrymen topple a monument to 
Felix Dzerzhinsky — founder of a predecessor to the KGB and afearsome symbol 
<https://history.blog.gov.uk/2017/12/20/whats-the-context-20-december-1917-formation-of-the-cheka-the-first-soviet-security-and-intelligence-agency/>
 of the Communist era.

Ironically, Orbán rose to prominence as a young student activist calling on 
Soviet troops to go home. While no one would accuse him of instituting anything 
close to a totalitarian state, he managed to build what he himself 
characterized as an illiberal one.
Viktor Orbán casts his vote in the the Hungarian general election in Budapest 
in 1994.Photographer: Tibor Illyes/MTI/AFP/Getty Images
It featured state control of the media, what critics said was the 
gerrymandering 
<https://www.unav.edu/web/global-affairs/hungary-s-electoral-system-reforms-gerrymandering-and-strategic-voting-in-2026>
 of the electoral system and a redistribution of economic assets. Orbán’s 
Hungary was an “economic model of an unchecked executive power through erosion 
of institutional pillars” including the bureaucracy and judiciary, Viktor 
Shvets, a global strategist at Macquarie, wrote in a note this week.

The result: “Since 2010, Hungary has consistently underperformed the EU, and 
especially its closest comparables” in the form of Poland and the Czech 
Republic, Shvets wrote.

Beyond Hungary’s borders, Orbán eventually emerged as one of the closest 
allies of both Kremlin leader Vladimir Putin and Trump. For the US president, 
Orbán was the role model for the “MAGA” movement and a focal point for the 
forces of right-wing nationalism the US seeks to promote in the EU.

Trump gave “total endorsement” to Orbán in the campaign and dispatched Vice 
President JD Vance to Budapest just days before the vote. Vance alleged — 
without irony — that “Brussels bureaucrats”were meddling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/vance-picks-fight-with-europe-over-orban-in-election-endorsement?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 in Hungary’s election.

For Putin, Orbán saw himself as a mouse ready to help 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/viktor-orban-offered-to-help-vladimir-putin-call-transcript-shows?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 Russia’s mighty lion. He held up the EU’s sanctions against the Kremlin and 
sought to block its €90 billion lifeline to Ukraine. His party’s ubiquitous 
election poster had the face of Ukrainian President Volodymyr Zelenskiy next to 
that of Magyar, while Orbán’s propaganda machine falsely accused the opposition 
leader of planning to send Hungarian boys to fight in Ukraine.

It all backfired spectacularly. In the end, the bread-and-butter issues of 
economic mismanagement became Orbán’s undoing. Magyar, a former official in 
Orbán’s party, will command a super-majority in the next parliament, with 
Fidesz reduced to a rump of an opposition.

Magyar has a clear mandate for systemic change, though it would be unwise for 
his supportersto be starry-eyed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-17/tearing-down-orban-s-entrenched-system-is-magyar-s-job-one?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
, given the breadth of challenges he faces. He’s unlikely to abandon the 
Russian oil and gas Hungary depends on, or perhaps even the veto power over key 
EU decisions that Orbán weaponized. And the economy won’t suddenly diversify 
beyond the Asian battery-makers on which Orbán bet so heavily.
Peter MagyarPhotographer: Denes Erdos/AP Photo
Nevertheless, Magyar’s vision offers Hungary, and Europe by extension, a 
markedly different path in coming years. The EU is likely to finally row in the 
same direction on a host of issues, making it harder for countries like the US, 
Russia and China to exploit divisions fostered by Orbán. The euro also gets a
fresh burst 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-14/lagarde-welcomes-new-hungarian-leader-s-plan-to-join-euro?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 of vigor, after Magyar said his government will work towardadopting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/magyar-says-hungary-should-eventually-adopt-the-euro?cmpid=BBD041826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260418>
 the single currency.

It’s a lot to deliver for the relatively young and inexperienced Magyar. But 
If the EU helps him turn Hungary around — using the same spigot of aid and 
development that turned Poland into a global success story — it could showcase 
a renewed relevance and energy for the entire European project.—Piotr 
Skolimowski <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/APqJUXfpK74/piotr-skolimowski>
