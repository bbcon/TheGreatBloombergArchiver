Misunderstanding and Reflexivity

Long remembered for the storming of the Bastille <https://bloom.bg/44pajED>, 
it appears that henceforward July 14 may also be known for theblocking of the 
Strait of Hormuz <https://bloom.bg/4pjemvH>. The US has announced that it will 
hold up traffic through the crucial waterway starting Tuesday, and levy a 20% 
charge on anyone using its protection to get through. The move amps up 
uncertainty, and markedly adds to theupward pressure on materials prices 
<https://bloom.bg/4h9MYy2>.

Last month’s Memorandum of Understanding to pause the war between the US and 
Iran, always ambiguous over exactly who could control the Strait and on what 
terms, appears now to be a dead letter. The failure has been immediately felt 
in oil prices, with Brent crude for December delivery now more expensive than 
when President Donald Trump signed the MOU:

Escalating hostilities over the last few days have already choked off the flow 
of oil through the Strait. It had never returned to normal following the 
tentative deal. Recent experience clearly shows that Hormuz isn’t a tap that 
can readily be turned open and closed:

A steady decline in global oil inventories will make it progressively harder 
to maintain the blockade. The US drew heavily from its strategic reserve during 
the early stages of the war in Ukraine, and total stocks (controlled by both 
public and private sectors) are at depths unseen since 1984:

Markets themselves are a player. Not simply responding to the facts on the 
ground, they can also create them. Last month, an over-rapid decline in oil 
prices relieved the pressure on both sides, and increased their temptation to 
misbehave. To quote Marko Papic of BCA Research:

The irony of this situation is that oil prices are an independent variable, 
not a dependent one. Both Iran and the US synchronize their kinetic activity 
with a particular sensitivity to oil prices themselves. When they are low, 
kinetic [military] activity ramps up. When oil prices rise, suddenly 
negotiations are back on the table.

Tina Fordham of Fordham Global Foresight said another period of sustained high 
oil prices might be needed to persuade the US that Iran can’t be bombed into 
capitulating, “so in that sense the absence of a major energy shock has 
provided space for this conflict to re-escalate markedly.”

If the US is serious about charging a 20% toll on tankers — likely to be 
extremely unpopular with Gulf allies — markets might create the strongest 
opposition. Fordham said:

This new US proposal of blockading the Iranian blockade runs the risk not only 
of spooking overly complacent markets but sparking a widening of the conflict.

The most likely scenario now is an ongoing tension between markets and the 
combatants’ military strategies. Neither wishes to cede control of the Strait; 
when prices rise, they will be more pragmatic, but cheaper oil will prompt both 
sides to see what they can get away with. That’s what’s happening now and it 
will probably take further rises in oil prices, if not a return to recent 
highs, to get them to desist.

Reflexive Bonds

Bond investors may have even more power than oil markets. Surging yields 
persuaded Trump last year to turn back from the initial, most damaging version 
of his Liberation Day tariffs.Real yields <https://bloom.bg/4pjeprn> have now 
surpassed those highs:

This is in part a response to the deterioration in the Gulf, but it also owes 
much to the inflationary outlook. Christopher Waller, the Fed’s deputy 
governor, gavea startlingly hawkish speech <https://bloom.bg/4fA0b1W> Monday, 
ending with a clear statement that a rate hike at the Federal Open Market 
Committee meeting later this month is a real possibility:

If we get another hot reading on core inflation this week, then the FOMC will 
need to consider tightening monetary policy in the near term. As always, we 
need to avoid making the mistake of fighting the last war and reacting too soon 
to tighten inflation, merely because we waited too long last time. But we also 
must avoid repeating the same mistake we made in 2021 and 2022 by waiting too 
long to respond.

The market heard him:

A July hike is now priced as close to a 50/50 shot, while two hikes by 
year-end are seen as probable. Both are the highest they’ve been this year.

These numbers could easily move sharply in either direction soon after you 
receive this, as Tuesday morning sees June inflation data at 8:30 a.m. Eastern 
Time, followed byKevin Warsh’s maiden congressional testimony 
<https://bloom.bg/4w0AsFD> 90 minutes later. A soft inflation number could 
negate worries about a hike this month; if Warsh really wants this to be an 
option, he’s likely to make that clear.

Waller’s concerns went beyond energy. He is primarily concerned with core 
inflation, which excludes gas prices and has been rising ever since the war 
started. Economists surveyed by Bloomberg expect it to drop from 2.9% to 2.8% 
for June. Thus there’s every possibility that the worry about a near-term hike 
could soon dissipate.

But whatever the June number, concerns about price rises aren’t going away, 
particularly if oil exporters have to pay a 20% protection fee. The collapse in 
inflation expectations after the MOU always looked overdone, and some 
proponents are admitting defeat. On June 24, Bob Elliott of Unlimited Funds
launched a trade <https://bloom.bg/4f10tin> betting on rate expectations to 
fall via taking a very long position in two-year Treasuries. Headmitted defeat 
<https://bloom.bg/4aNjYZ9> on Monday, saying: 

Probably should have hedged it with oil given just how volatile the policy 
environment has been, but such is life. Better to take the loss on another 
unexpected policy reversal than try to hold on and hope this reverses given the 
uncertainties around escalation. I’ve got no edge in predicting policy shifts 
(and neither does anyone else).

He’s right about that. Conditions seem to be conspiring to mess up any 
strong-conviction macro trade for quite a while to come.


Ar-Gen-Tina! 

Twenty-six years ago, Argentina was mired in economic turmoil, sinking into a 
great depression. Its capital markets were widely considered uninvestable, 
while the currency plummeted after the 2001 decision to abandon its one-to-one 
peg to the US dollar. That national malaise followed La Albiceleste — the White 
and Sky Blue — to the 2002 World Cup in Japan and South Korea, where theylost 
to England <https://bloom.bg/4hc6DgR> and failed to advance beyond the group 
stage, while archrivalBrazil won <https://bloom.bg/4bmE3FQ> their record fifth 
title.

Today, much like its defending world champion soccer team, Argentina’s economy 
is in far better shape while Brazil struggles to make an impression. Progress 
under libertarian firebrand President Javier Milei has been torturous, and last 
year required a highly contentious lifeline from the Trump administration. But 
markets’ performance since Milei’s election in 2023 suggests rather greater 
confidence. MSCI’s index of Argentine stocks has outperformed even the 
Magnificent Seven tech platforms, in dollar terms, since he won:

It’s a volatile market recovering from some very bad times, of course, but 
this does seem exaggerated, even if macro indicators are headed in the right 
direction. The peso, helped bythe US bailout <https://bloom.bg/4aN5YPc>, isn’t 
out of the woods. Last week’srepayment of $4.3 billion 
<https://bloom.bg/4prONc0> on its dollar-denominated bonds should bolster 
confidence, but Capital Economics’ Kimberley Sperrfechter argues that this 
doesn’t ease concerns about overvaluation:

The dilemma the government faces is that allowing the peso to depreciate – 
which is needed to improve the competitiveness of Argentina’s non-energy 
exports and temper import demand – would threaten to fuel price pressures and 
jeopardize the Milei administration’s goal of bringing inflation down to single 
digits. That is something the government will be keen to avoid ahead of next 
year’s elections.

For now, an energy export boom props up the peso, keeping the international 
reserves at relatively healthy levels. The big devaluation when Milei entered 
office is, just about, having the desired effect:

For all the signs of economic spring, Milei’s popularity is flagging, with 
approval ratings near the lowest of his tenure.Disapproval likely stems 
<https://bloom.bg/4pnGrlB> from his failure fully to rein in inflation, weak 
employment, and low wage growth:

Milei’s remarkable victory in last October’s midterm elections 
<https://bloom.bg/4aSZrCu> trounced the pollsters and sets the stage for a 
closely contested general election next year. Prediction markets are betting 
he’ll win, but it’s on a knife-edge:

Few challengers have come forward so far; one leading contender is Axel 
Kicillof, the two-term governor of Buenos Aires province. As Kicillof served as
economy minister during Argentina’s sovereign default saga 
<https://bloom.bg/4f22s5S> a decade ago, he might not be well placed to 
capitalize on voters’ discontent. A Milei victory would be a powerful 
endorsement of his economic program. Bloomberg Economics’ Jimena Zuniga argues 
that it would:

Offer the clearest assurance that Argentina will stick to a path toward 
macro-economic stability and growth. That is our baseline scenario. A less 
benign growth-inflation outlook, combined with recent corruption scandals, 
increases the risk of other outcomes.

Overall the outlook, as for the nation’s footballers in the World Cup 
semifinals <https://bloom.bg/4pjBpGw>, warrants cautious optimism. Zuniga 
points out that, after recent credit-rating upgrades, there is scope for upside 
if policymakers can deliver more positive surprises. A key catalyst would be 
renewed access to international capital markets, possible in the coming months, 
which would ease concerns over debt repayments due in 2027.

With Lionel Messi <https://bloom.bg/4phbcZk>, arguably the greatest-ever 
footballer and the World Cup’s top scorer, still leadingLa Albiceleste, a title 
defense could be a powerful symbolic boost. Lasting economic gains will be 
tougher, but after years of financial turmoil, confidence itself can be a 
valuable asset.

—Richard Abbey
