The Iran strikes carry immediate implications for Asian economies through two channels: energy import costs and regional security spillovers. Asia's largest economies — China, Japan, South Korea, and India — are all major importers of Middle Eastern crude, much of which transits the Strait of Hormuz. Iran has threatened to close the strait, and Bloomberg Economics estimates that a full closure could push oil to $108 per barrel. For Japan and South Korea, which have limited domestic energy production, such a scenario would represent a significant terms-of-trade deterioration. India, which had resumed meaningful purchases of discounted Iranian crude, faces both supply disruption and price risk simultaneously.

China's position is particularly complex. Beijing is Iran's largest oil trading partner and has maintained economic ties that circumvent Western sanctions. The US-Israel strikes place China in a diplomatically awkward position: condemnation risks straining relations with Washington at a moment when trade tensions are already elevated, while silence signals acquiescence to unilateral military action that Beijing has consistently opposed on principle. The People's Bank of China this week was reportedly taking steps to stem yuan appreciation, a reminder that China's record $1.2 trillion trade surplus continues to generate currency pressure even as President Xi publicly advocates for a more prominent global role for the renminbi.

On the reserve currency question, Xi's reiterated ambitions for the yuan as a global reserve asset face structural barriers that the Iran shock does nothing to resolve. Foreign ownership of Chinese government bonds has declined to just under $400 billion, compared with $9.3 trillion of foreign-held Treasuries, reflecting the persistent impediments of capital controls, limited market liquidity, and a banking sector that holds rather than trades fixed income. The ECB's new repo facility for foreign central banks and euro-zone governments' rhetorical support for a broader euro role similarly face fragmentation constraints, with German Bund annual trading volumes representing less than two weeks of equivalent US Treasury turnover.
