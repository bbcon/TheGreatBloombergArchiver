The most consequential single variable in global markets at present may be the trajectory of US-Iran tensions. Brent crude surged 4.3% on Wednesday to re-cross $70 per barrel and extended gains Thursday, reflecting growing concern that a military confrontation is no longer a remote possibility. Prediction market participants now assign roughly 70% probability to a US strike. President Trump stated the US is "ready, willing, and able to rapidly fulfill its mission, with speed and violence, if necessary"; Iran responded it would "defend itself and respond like never before." US Vice President Vance described the latest round of nuclear talks as having gone "well in some ways" while acknowledging Iranian negotiators had not accepted the red lines set before them. The US also announced visa restrictions on Iranian officials for suppressing domestic protests and restricting internet access. Iran's capacity to administer a meaningful oil supply shock — through the Strait of Hormuz or direct targeting of regional energy infrastructure — is not in question, and the risk of a financial accident remains material even if broader equity markets appear, for now, to be pricing the situation with unusual equanimity.

The IMF released its annual Article IV assessment of China, calling once again for a rebalancing toward domestic consumption and away from investment and export-led growth. The Fund noted China's large current-account surplus has produced "adverse spillovers to trading partners." The prescription is identical to guidance the IMF has issued for two decades — Chinese household consumption remains near 40% of GDP, essentially unchanged since 2005 — though officials have recently signaled the forthcoming 15th five-year plan may give consumption a more prominent role, driven in part by the realization that trade barriers make continued export reliance increasingly untenable.

OpenAI is close to finalizing the first phase of a funding round exceeding $100 billion, which would value the company above $850 billion — among the largest private financings on record. Amazon, SoftBank, Nvidia, and Microsoft are expected to anchor the round, with sovereign wealth funds and venture capital to follow. The scale of capital deployment reflects a broader dynamic in which AI infrastructure spending has become a geopolitical as well as commercial competition, with India's AI summit serving as the most recent venue for governments and corporations to signal strategic commitment.
