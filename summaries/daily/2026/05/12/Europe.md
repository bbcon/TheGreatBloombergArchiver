The UK's political situation deteriorated materially on May 12, with Prime Minister Keir Starmer publicly defying cabinet-level calls for him to set a departure timetable. Home Secretary Shabana Mahmood is among those privately urging him to consider a transition, according to people familiar with the matter. Starmer's response — telling cabinet that "the country expects us to get on with governing" — did little to stabilize investor sentiment. Thirty-year gilt yields rose as much as 12 basis points to 5.80%, the highest level since 1998, while the pound fell 0.5% to $1.35. Schroders disclosed it is actively avoiding UK bonds, citing the risk that Starmer's replacement could pursue a more expansionary fiscal stance. The market reaction reflects a well-established pattern: UK assets are sensitive not just to who governs, but to the uncertainty of transition itself, and Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham is already being discussed as a potential successor.

In Germany, Chancellor Friedrich Merz convened coalition talks between the CDU-led conservatives and the Social Democrats aimed at advancing tax and pension reform, with public support for his government having declined sharply since taking office. Investor confidence readings did improve marginally, though Germany's fiscal watchdog cautioned against excessive spending, illustrating the tension between the reform ambition and the consolidation constraints the new government has imposed on itself.

At the European Commission level, reporting suggests Ursula von der Leyen's management style — relying on a tightly centralized group of advisers while sidelining broader institutional structures — is generating internal friction and diluting focus on the Commission's core mandate of strengthening the single market. The EU did reach unanimous political agreement to sanction violent settlers in the West Bank, its first such consensus since the height of the Gaza crisis.
