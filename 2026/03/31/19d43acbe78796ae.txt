But the biggest-ever disruption to physical supplies has yet to become fully 
apparent in one surprising place: the price of the main crude benchmarks.

Sure, Brent futures have risen at an unprecedented pace 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-30/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-31?cmpid=BBBXT033126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 this month, but theyтАЩre still hovering in the low $100s per barrel тАФ a level 
the world coped with pretty comfortably in non-crisis times such as the early 
2010s.

If this is one of the worst oil crunches in history, then why arenтАЩt we 
staring at record prices?

For one, the world began the year chock-full of supplies, with US shale wells 
and deepwater platforms in Brazil and Guyana filling up tankers faster than 
buyers could unload them.

And this month, as the war unfolded, the International Energy Agency agreed to 
release arecord volume 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/iea-confirms-huge-release-of-emergency-oil-stockpiles?cmpid=BBBXT033126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 of oil from emergency reserves to keep prices in check.
Crude hasnтАЩt risen as much as might be expected, despite signs of growing

Some traders are also afraid to be caught pushing prices too far in the wrong 
direction. At times, a single tweet by US President Donald Trump has been 
enough to send the market crashing, only for attacks to then escalate further.

Some say they donтАЩt dare hold positions into the weekend, when some of the 
biggest strikes have taken place.

Fuel prices, however, have risen far faster because of strict specifications 
that limit market size and liquidity.Jet fuel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-27/air-travel-crisis-risks-spreading-on-iran-war-s-jet-fuel-squeeze?cmpid=BBBXT033126_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260331>
 in Europe, for example, has already surged to all-time highs, equivalent to 
more than $215 a barrel.

This disconnect between the cost of crude and fuel is unlikely to last 
forever. We might soon reach the point where desensitized traders no longer 
react to messages from the White House aimed at talking down prices.

The bigger issue is that even if the war were to end tomorrow, the energy 
disruptions wonтАЩt. Repairs to damaged infrastructure will take weeks or months, 
even years. Changes to Middle East shipping norms could last even longer.

The oil market may still be trading hopes of a ceasefire, but the physical 
world is already pricing the aftermath. Crude can ignore reality for only so 
long.
