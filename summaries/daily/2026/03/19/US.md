The Federal Reserve's March meeting delivered no policy change — the FOMC voted 11-1 to hold the benchmark rate at 3.5%-3.75% — but the press conference that followed generated meaningful market volatility. Chair Jerome Powell made clear that rate cuts remain contingent on demonstrated progress on inflation, a bar that appears increasingly distant given both the persistence of tariff-driven price pressures and the rapidly escalating Middle East energy shock. The Fed's preferred core inflation gauge ran at an estimated 3% in February, a full percentage point above target. February producer price data reinforced the concern: core PPI rose to a three-year high, with goods prices continuing to climb since Liberation Day. As Carl Weinberg of High Frequency Economics noted, the pipeline was already accelerating before the Strait of Hormuz blockade.

The signal from futures markets is stark. Expectations have shifted from three cuts over the next 18 months to one, and that single cut is now essentially a coin flip. Powell acknowledged "meaningful" internal movement toward fewer cuts, and explicitly noted that colleagues had raised the possibility that the next move could be a hike. Bond yields rose and equities sold off as he spoke, with the S&P 500 recording its largest Fed-day decline since 2024.

Powell's remarks on his own tenure compounded market uncertainty. He confirmed that if Kevin Warsh is not confirmed before his term expires in May, he will remain as chair pro tempore. More significantly, he stated he will not vacate his board seat — which runs to January 2028 — until the Justice Department investigation into the Fed's building renovation project is "well and truly over." The succession is now a prolonged open question, with Senator Thom Tillis blocking Warsh's confirmation vote until the probe concludes and prosecutor Jeanine Pirro having signaled she will not abandon the inquiry. Markets appear increasingly uncomfortable with the prospect of a protracted, politically charged transition at the central bank.
