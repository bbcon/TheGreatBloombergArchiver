European markets opened softer, with the Stoxx Europe 600 declining 0.24%, as the continent grappled with the implications of a reshuffled US tariff regime. The European Parliament's trade chief Bernd Lange moved to freeze ratification of the EU-US trade agreement pending clarification from Washington on its revised policy framework — a significant step that signals the fragility of arrangements negotiated under the now-invalidated IEEPA tariffs. ECB President Christine Lagarde warned that Trump's latest moves risk disrupting the EU-US trade "equilibrium" and could pose a fresh headwind to the eurozone economy.

German business confidence brightened more than anticipated, providing some offset to the broader uncertainty. Chancellor Friedrich Merz is visiting China, where he signaled a notably sharper line than his predecessors, warning German business leaders against deepening investments there and indicating that government backing would not be forthcoming if those investments soured. The shift reflects a broader reassessment of Germany's China exposure, though it sits in some tension with the economy's ongoing need for export markets.

In France, President Macron appointed David Amiel as budget minister in a cabinet reshuffle that drew criticism from opposition parties. Macron separately wrote to Trump requesting the lifting of sanctions on French and EU officials, including former Commissioner Thierry Breton, while France summoned US Ambassador Charles Kushner over comments related to a domestic violent incident. On corporate news, Johnson Matthey shares fell 17% after Honeywell reduced its offer price for the company's Catalyst Technologies business. JD Sports rose 4.4% after announcing a £200 million shareholder buyback. A significant governance failure at the VZB pension fund — a Berlin-based professional fund for dentists — has drawn scrutiny to Germany's Versorgungswerke system, which manages roughly €300 billion in assets, after an estimated €1.1 billion in losses from concentrated illiquid exposures.
