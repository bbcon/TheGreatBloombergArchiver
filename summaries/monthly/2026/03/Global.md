• The US-Israel military campaign against Iranian nuclear facilities, launched late in the final week of February, and the resulting Strait of Hormuz closure — effectively shutting down approximately 20% of global oil flows — was the dominant event shaping all markets, central banks, and geopolitics throughout March.
• Brent crude surged from approximately $70 per barrel at month's open to above $118 intraday in the final week — a gain of roughly 63% since hostilities began — before partially retracing toward $108–$113 following tentative de-escalation signals.
• The IEA announced a record emergency release of 400 million barrels from member-country stockpiles, its largest in the agency's history, with Japan separately releasing approximately 80 million barrels beginning mid-month.
• Gold posted its worst weekly decline since 1983 in the final week — down approximately 10.5% — a counterintuitive move reflecting a dash for liquidity and dollar strength as real rate expectations surged; from its intraday peak above $5,000 per ounce to its trough, the metal declined approximately 27%.
• Global debt reached $348 trillion at end-2025, with $29 trillion added in the year — the fastest accumulation since the pandemic — providing the fiscal backdrop against which sovereign governments are absorbing an oil shock they have limited room to cushion.

**The Iran Conflict: Escalation, De-escalation Signals, and Endgame Uncertainty**
The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike represents the most consequential geopolitical rupture since Russia's invasion of Ukraine. Iran's retaliation expanded to at least five countries across the month — missile strikes on US bases in Qatar, Bahrain, the UAE, and elsewhere; drone strikes on Gulf Arab energy infrastructure; and a US submarine sinking of the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka in the second week, the first American submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II. NATO air defenses downed a ballistic missile over Turkey. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field in the third week — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities in the conflict — and Iran responded by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City, causing extensive damage and sparking fires, with two LNG trains expected to remain offline for up to five years. By the final week, Prime Minister Netanyahu stated Israel would no longer target energy infrastructure and said the war would end faster than people expect, while Iran's foreign minister signaled that recent restraint was deliberate. The Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries, and a New York Times report of indirect Iranian-Washington contact prompted a temporary equity rebound. Whether these signals constitute a durable de-escalation or tactical repositioning remains genuinely uncertain. Prediction markets placed a 52% probability on the conflict extending beyond April 30 at month's end. Iran's ceasefire conditions — permanent non-aggression pledges from both the US and Israel — appear incompatible with either government's stated positions, leaving the diplomatic path narrow. Iran's new supreme leader, Mojtaba Khamenei, vowed to keep Hormuz effectively closed and threatened additional fronts if strikes continued, and Iran's parliament is reportedly drafting legislation to impose passage fees on Hormuz transit — an arrangement that would amount to de facto toll extraction on global shipping.

**Energy Markets: Scale and Complexity**
The IEA has characterized the cumulative supply disruption as equivalent to the combined effect of the two 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis — a framing delivered by its executive director in Canberra. Brent crude surged approximately 18% in the first full week of conflict, peaked above $118 intraday in the third week, and held in the $108–$113 range through the final week, on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990. US diesel futures gained more than 40% in a single week at peak; European natural gas rose more than 50% in one week and ultimately more than doubled from pre-war levels. Qatar's Ras Laffan facility, which produces roughly 20% of global LNG supply, sustained damage potentially taking years to repair. Saudi Arabia's Ras Tanura refinery suspended operations following a drone strike; Iraq agreed to resume pipeline exports via Turkey, contributing to price relief in the final days. The binding near-term constraint on shipping proved to be the insurance market rather than an explicit blockade: more than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs withdrew war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels, causing commercial shipping to self-suspend. Only two commercial vessel transits were recorded in a 24-hour period at the closure's peak, with no oil shipments passing at all. US naval escort and insurance guarantees provided partial mitigation, though ING analysts noted escorts risk becoming targets and individual ship captains ultimately determine whether to sail. BloombergNEF's base case sees Brent averaging $106.70 in April; a delayed Hormuz reopening until mid-June would push that to $154.40; Macquarie warned of a path toward $200 in a prolonged closure scenario. Dallas Fed modeling suggests a closure lasting through June would reduce global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points in the next quarter. Paradoxically, Iran appears a net financial beneficiary, reportedly earning hundreds of millions of dollars in incremental oil revenue as the only major exporter able to utilize the strait freely.

**Global Monetary Policy: A Collective Pivot**
The macro implications of the oil shock are particularly uncomfortable for central banks globally that had been in or approaching easing cycles. A sustained $20-per-barrel shock could add up to one percentage point to global inflation according to Société Générale; the IMF's Georgieva estimated a sustained 10% energy price increase would push global inflation 40 basis points higher and shave 0.1–0.2% from growth. The week of the most concentrated central bank calendar — covering approximately two-thirds of the global economy across 18 policy meetings — produced a historically unusual outcome: the Reserve Bank of Australia became the first major central bank to hike in response to the energy impulse; the Fed, ECB, and Bank of England all held but shifted tone materially toward caution. By month's end, traders had unwound rate-cut expectations across the US, UK, Turkey, and Hungary simultaneously, and the cumulative repricing — from an easing trajectory to a prospective tightening one within the space of three weeks — represents one of the more significant collective monetary policy pivots of the current cycle. Sovereign bonds sold off alongside equities, leaving the dollar as the dominant haven vehicle. Bloomberg Economics estimates March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections. The OECD, in its first formal revision, left its 2026 global growth projection unchanged at 2.9% while trimming 2027 by 0.1 percentage point and raising G20 average inflation to 4.0% from 2.8% in December — a forecast that likely understates the left-tail risk given the conflict's unresolved trajectory.

**Commodity Contagion and Alliance Fractures**
The conflict's second-order commodity effects accelerated through the month. Urea prices rose 25% since hostilities began, reaching levels last seen during Russia's Ukraine invasion, as Iran accounts for approximately 5% and 11% of global urea and ammonia trade respectively. Aluminum briefly hit a near four-year high. Coal prices reached their highest level in over a year on gas substitution effects. Over 46,000 flights were canceled in the conflict's third week alone. Economy-class round-trip fares from Sydney to London increased more than 80% through the month. Gold's collapse — down roughly 10.5% in its worst weekly decline since 1983, with Turkey selling approximately $8 billion in reserves to defend its currency — represents a significant dislocation in a traditional safe-haven asset. Reports that Iran is permitting Hormuz passage for vessels paying in yuan prompted Deutsche Bank to flag the conflict as a potential catalyst for petrodollar erosion. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on pace for its largest monthly gain since July at approximately 2%. Alliance management has been a persistent source of friction: the UK, Japan, Germany, and South Korea each declined to participate militarily; Trump publicly abandoned the request for allied warships after being rebuffed; and China's state media were openly dismissive of US appeals. Russia has emerged as a clear early economic beneficiary, with elevated energy revenues and the discount on Urals crude versus Brent nearly eliminated. Environmental consequences are also accumulating: Israeli drone strikes on refineries near Tehran triggered toxic black rain over a metropolitan area of approximately 18.5 million people, and analysts have identified over 300 incidents carrying environmental risk. The crisis is simultaneously accelerating structural energy transition dynamics — EV queues in the Philippines, rooftop solar uptake in India — in ways that may prove durable well beyond the conflict's resolution.
