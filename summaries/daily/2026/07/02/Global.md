**Hormuz and Energy Markets**
The divergence between oil and LNG markets illustrates the uneven character of the post-conflict normalization. Crude flows through the Strait of Hormuz have recovered meaningfully under US military protection, pushing WTI below $68 per barrel — close to pre-war levels. LNG is a different story. Qatar, responsible for approximately one-fifth of pre-conflict global LNG supply, has extended force majeure clauses on shipments to Asia and Europe, while continuing to sublease vessels — signals that QatarEnergy does not anticipate a rapid resumption of full operations. A fire at Ras Laffan during a plant restart, combined with insurance and crew-safety concerns following the targeting of Qatari-flagged vessels, compounds the uncertainty. Shell has warned that sustained Hormuz disruption could produce the first annual decline in global LNG exports in over a decade, with significant downstream consequences for European gas storage refill targets and Asian economies reliant on LNG for power generation.

**AI Emissions and Climate**
The tension between AI investment and sustainability commitments sharpened this week as Amazon disclosed a 16% increase in greenhouse gas emissions in 2025 and Google reported an 18% rise, both attributing the deterioration primarily to data center expansion. Google's electricity consumption rose 37% year-on-year. Both companies maintain net-zero targets — 2040 for Amazon, 2030 for Google — but the trajectory of emissions growth appears inconsistent with those timelines absent a step-change in clean energy procurement or efficiency gains.
