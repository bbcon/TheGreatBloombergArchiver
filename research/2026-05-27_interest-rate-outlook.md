# Interest Expectations Revised Up as Inflation Pressures Emerge
**Prepared:** 2026-05-27
**Purpose:** Strategic context for airline industry partner briefing

---

## The Reversal: From Easing to Tightening

The Hormuz shock executed the sharpest reversal in global monetary policy expectations since the 2021–2022 inflation surge — within three weeks of the closure, every major central bank's trajectory had been repriced from easing to hold-or-hike.

**Before the crisis (January–February 2026):**
- Fed: two 25bp cuts fully priced for 2026; easing bias intact
- ECB: rates expected unchanged through year-end; inflation on glide-path to 2%
- BoJ: gradual normalization underway at 0.75%; markets comfortable with slow pace

**After the Hormuz shock:**
- Fed: zero cuts priced; ~65% probability of a December 2026 hike; 50% probability of hike by April 2027
- ECB: three 2026 hikes repriced — a dramatic reversal from February
- BoJ: normalization paused; super-long yields hit post-1999 records under fiscal and inflation pressure

---

## Inflation: The Second-Order Problem

The initial price shock was mechanical — Brent above $118/barrel, jet fuel >$215/barrel. What changed the monetary calculus was evidence of second-order pass-through into core inflation, indicating the shock was not self-contained:

- **US April CPI: 3.8% YoY** — highest in three years; core (ex-energy) ran at 2.8%; the Fed's supercore services measure rose sharply; consumer prices rose faster than wages for the first time since 2022
- **US April PPI: +6.0% YoY** — led by energy and freight; electronics components +27% YoY as AI-driven memory shortages ended decades of technology-sector disinflation
- **Oxford Economics** projects the PCE deflator reaching 3.8%, foreclosing near-term rate cuts
- **OECD** raised its US 2026 inflation forecast to 4.2%; G20 average CPI lifted to 4.0% from 2.8%
- **Eurozone:** March inflation jumped to 2.5% (steepest monthly acceleration since the Russia-Ukraine invasion in 2022); services inflation softening, but energy pass-through is clearly the driver
- **Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields** reached its highest since July 2008 in the week of May 18–22

## Central Bank-by-Bank Outlook

### Federal Reserve — From Pivot to Pause to Potential Hike

The Fed's April meeting — Powell's last as chair — produced the most divided FOMC vote since October 1992, with four dissents. The committee held rates unchanged but the tone was unambiguously hawkish. FOMC minutes released in May showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation and "many" favored dropping the easing bias entirely.

Kevin Warsh, confirmed as successor by the narrowest margin on record, inherits a policy framework where rate hikes have shifted from tail risk to plausible base case. His publicly stated preferences — trimmed-mean inflation gauges, narrower mandate, smaller balance sheet — suggest a structural tightening bias. The 30-year Treasury touched **5.19%** in mid-May, a level last seen two decades ago. Bank of America has recommended short positions in two-year Treasuries, characterizing hike risk as underpriced.

**Baseline:** No cuts in 2026. First hike — if inflation does not recede — probable in H1 2027. Upper tail: hike in Q4 2026 if energy remains elevated and core inflation stays above 3%.

### European Central Bank — A Trichet Scenario Re-emerges

The ECB held at its April meeting but disclosed that policymakers debated an immediate increase — language that implies a June hike is the most likely next move. Bundesbank President Nagel explicitly favors a June move. Market pricing shifted from unchanged-through-year-end to **three hikes in 2026** — the most dramatic intra-quarter ECB repricing since 2022.

TS Lombard has raised the risk of a **"Trichet 2.0" scenario** — named for the ECB's 2008 and 2011 rate hikes into weakening growth — where the central bank tightens into an economy already heading into stagnation. Eurozone PMI fell to 47.5 in May (contraction territory); Germany's ZEW collapsed to -17.2; the Bundesbank estimated only "slight" Q1 expansion. The stagflation risk is structural: energy costs are supply-driven and wage-indexed contracts are feeding second-round effects.

**Baseline:** June hike (25bp). Two to three additional hikes in H2 2026 if Hormuz remains closed and wage data deteriorate. Downside: a sharp growth contraction forces an early halt, deepening the stagflationary trap.

### Bank of Japan — Normalization on Hold

The BoJ delivered a **6-3 vote to hold at 0.75%** — the most divided outcome under Governor Ueda — reflecting genuine uncertainty between the inflationary impulse (Spring Shunto delivered a third consecutive year of ~5% wage increases; five-year breakevens at 2.7%) and the growth drag from energy costs and global slowdown risk. Japan intervened directly in currency markets when dollar-yen approached 160, its first intervention since 2024.

Japanese super-long yields hit **post-1999 records** in the week of May 18, driven partly by Prime Minister Takaichi's fiscal reversal (accelerated defense and infrastructure spending) and structurally rising term premia. This creates a particular tension: the BoJ is holding short rates to avoid accelerating yen weakness and growth contraction, while long-end yields are rising regardless on fiscal and inflation dynamics.

**Baseline:** BoJ holds through summer; first hike toward 1.0% unlikely before Q4 2026 at earliest. The risk is that a disorderly yen move (above 165/$) forces an emergency tightening that collides with the growth outlook.

## Implications for the Airline Industry

The repricing of global rates from "easing" to "hold or hike" has direct and indirect consequences for airlines and their strategic partners across four transmission channels:

**1. Cost of capital and fleet financing.** Aircraft acquisition is capital-intensive. A wide-body order commonly involves $15–30 billion in financing across a long-haul carrier's fleet program. A 150bp upward shift in long-term rates — consistent with the current 30-year Treasury repricing — raises the annual carrying cost of a $20 billion fleet program by approximately $300 million. For airlines considering fleet modernization, the higher discount rate also compresses the NPV advantage of more fuel-efficient next-generation aircraft, weakening the economic case for accelerated retirement of older frames.

**2. Aircraft leasing rates.** The major lessors (AerCap, Air Lease, BBAM) fund themselves in investment-grade bond markets. Higher rates lift their cost of funds, which are passed through into lease rates at renewal. Airlines with a high proportion of leased capacity — Ryanair (~30%), IAG (~40%), most LCCs at >50% — face incremental lease cost pressure on fleet renewals, on top of elevated fuel costs.

**3. Demand-side compression from rate transmission.** Mortgage rates above 5% (UK), elevated US consumer borrowing costs, and higher corporate financing costs collectively reduce household and business travel budgets. The historical pattern from 2005–2007 is instructive: the last period of combined high energy costs and rising rates compressed leisure travel growth in Europe and North America within 12–18 months of the rate cycle peak. Premium cabin demand — less elastic to rate cycles — is partially insulated, but volume-dependent, yield-managed short-haul networks carry more exposure.

**4. Currency dynamics.** A higher-for-longer Fed relative to a potentially earlier-pausing BoJ and an ECB tightening into stagnation creates a dollar-strengthening environment in the near term. Dollar strength depresses international ticket revenue when translated back to USD for US carriers and complicates hedging economics for non-US airlines with significant dollar-denominated cost bases (fuel, aircraft financing, maintenance). Emerging-market carriers — particularly in South Asia (rupee approaching 97/$) and Africa — face the most acute pressure: depreciating local currencies against the dollar inflate costs while local revenue bases are in weakening currencies.

## Summary

| Central Bank | Pre-Crisis Pricing | Current Market Pricing | Key Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| **Fed** | 2 cuts in 2026 | 0 cuts; ~65% probability of Dec hike | Core inflation staying above 3% → hike in Q4 |
| **ECB** | Unchanged through 2026 | 3 hikes in 2026 | Trichet 2.0: tightening into stagnation |
| **BoJ** | Slow normalization to ~1% | On hold; long yields rising regardless | Disorderly yen forcing emergency tightening |

For airline strategic planning, the salient shift is not the level of rates per se — it is the direction and the speed of reversal. A world that expected easing throughout 2026 provided a low-cost-of-capital buffer that partially offset fuel and demand pressures. That buffer has been removed. The airlines best positioned are those that locked in long-term fixed-rate financing before February 2026, have high fuel hedge ratios, and maintain premium network exposure relatively insulated from consumer rate sensitivity.
