The jury is still out on the effectiveness of the Trump tariffs to deliver on 
their core promise: a reindustrialization of America and the return offactory 
jobs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/us-industrial-production-rises-by-most-in-over-a-year?cmpid=BBD060126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=4998>
. But you can certainly make the case that Team Trump is recording wins in the 
intellectual battle to reshape how the world views trade and tariffs, and that 
one man is increasingly responsible.

At the very least, that’s what US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer was 
projecting last week as he embarked on a two-part victory tour of globalist 
institutions.

The first stop came in a long conversation 
<https://www.cfr.org/event/a-conversation-with-jamieson-greer> with predecessor 
Mike Froman at the Council on Foreign Relations, a think tank with globalist 
DNA. The second emerged inan essay 
<https://www.imf.org/en/publications/fandd/issues/2026/06/straight-talk-economics-for-the-real-economy>
 in the International Monetary Fund’s house magazine in which Greer not only 
laid out the historical case for tariffs but why he thinks economists and the 
bastion of orthodoxy that is the IMF have it all wrong.

Greer has become Trump’s unquestioned general on all things trade. The idea 
floated for a time that Commerce Secretary Howard Lutnick would oversee the 
portfolio fizzled amid the chaotic policymaking of 2025. Treasury Secretary 
Scott Bessent may have nominal leadership of theChina brief 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/world-imbalances-trouble-g-7-in-wake-of-trump-s-china-encounter?cmpid=BBD060126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=4998>
 but he defers to Greer on all things trade.

Greer has grown into his role by being very good at presenting a cogent 
argument for tariffs. You may disagree with his conclusions but Greer’s case is 
built on history and substance, and it’s generally delivered calmly and with 
good humor.

Lawyers vs Economists

Greer is not an ideological warrior like Peter Navarro or Stephen Miller. He’s 
a respected trade attorney who, like his mentor Robert Lighthizer, Trump’s 
first-term USTR, believes the US took a wrong turn at the end of the Cold War. 
He is out to bring back a world in which global commerce is shaped by trade 
lawyers guarding national interests rather than economists chasing a 
theoretical — or even theological — belief in the absolute power of efficiency.

“As the global economy changes, so too must the economics profession,” Greer’s 
essay in the IMF’s Finance and Development magazine concludes pointedly. 
“Economists may be dismal scientists, but they have no reason to live with 
their heads in the sand.”

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-31/china-factory-activity-worsens-in-warning-sign-for-economy?cmpid=BBD060126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=4998>

To be clear, Greer’s victory lap may be premature. Manufacturing has lost more 
jobs <https://fred.stlouisfed.org/series/MANEMP> than it has gained since Trump 
returned to office. As Froman pointed out in their conversation and his own
written retort 
<https://www.cfr.org/articles/evaluating-trumps-trade-policy-on-trumps-criteria>
, manufacturing’s share of US GDP also hasn’t risen. And Froman and other 
globalists aren’t conceding defeat.

“Globalists are not dead. Indeed, they’re winning,” Froman wrote, pointing 
cheekily to the fact a horse named Globalist overcame 32-1 odds to win a recent 
race at Kentucky’s fabled Churchill Downs.

Also, the boom areas 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-30/us-jobs-report-set-to-reveal-solid-growth-steady-unemployment-rate?cmpid=BBD060126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=4998>
 of the US economy now are those like AI that have intentionally been excluded 
from tariffs. Or ones like healthcare that depend more on the delivery of 
services than goods.

Policy ‘Poaching’

But it’s hard to deny Trump’s tariffs are shaping not only patterns of trade 
but the policy discussions in other economies. If the European Union is taking 
a harder line on China and increasingly concerned about the effects of the 
Chinese model, that’s a consequence of US policy.

Greer says when he reads articles and reports by his EU counterpart Maroš 
Šefčovič these days he often thinks “I wrote it,” he told the CFR session. “I’m 
like: ‘Oh, he just poached everything I think.’”

Greer doesn’t lack a grand vision or a capacity for Trumpian grandiosity. 

In an op-ed last year 
<https://www.nytimes.com/2025/08/07/opinion/trump-trade-tariffs.html> he 
declared the arrival of the Turnberry system, named after the Trump golf club 
at which the US and EU announced their now-fragile trade deal. One valid 
criticism since has been that the system he outlined looks ad-hoc and 
temporary. The US under Trump has been better at dismantling an order than 
building a new one, the critique goes.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-31/the-aluminum-shock-hitting-the-global-economy-video?cmpid=BBD060126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=4998>

Greer now argues the US is creating a new global order through its actions and 
their consequences. The EU’sangst over China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-30/china-pledges-to-retaliate-if-eu-imposes-trade-restrictions?cmpid=BBD060126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=4998>
 would look different “if the United States had continued to be the consumer of 
last resort for all Chinese goods,” Greer told CFR last week. “So whether 
there’s a formal international system around it or not, there is a new order. 
And it is really being keyed off of what America is doing.”

China and other countries also have power, Greer went on. Which the Trump 
administration is comfortable with. The main achievement of a recent 
presidential trip to Beijing was stability, he said. The US is no longer trying 
to change China, Greer said, but dealing with it as it is. And “I get to keep 
tariffs on China. Which is pretty awesome.”

All of which marks a return to the power-driven version of history that came 
before the era of hyper-globalization that Greer and his intellectual kin 
disliked so much.

“We’re just seeing a reversion to the mean,” Greer told the CFR audience. 
Whether that turns out to be good for the US and world economies remains to be 
seen. But in the short-term Greer and his fellow travelers are at least driving 
the intellectual battles.
