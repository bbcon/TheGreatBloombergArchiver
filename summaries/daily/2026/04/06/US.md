US equity futures edged higher early Monday, gaining around 0.4% on the S&P 500, lifted by an Axios report that the US, Iran, and regional mediators are exploring terms for a potential 45-day ceasefire. Trading remained thin, with European and several Asian markets closed for Easter Monday, and the durability of the move appeared contingent on news flow ahead of a Trump press conference scheduled for 1 p.m. Washington time.

The geopolitical backdrop remained deeply unsettled. Over the weekend, President Trump posted an explicit ultimatum demanding Iran "Open the Strait" by Tuesday or face attacks on power plants and bridges — following the destruction of Iran's largest bridge and the rescue of an F-15E weapons officer after two days of special forces operations. Prediction markets on Polymarket marked down the probability of a ceasefire by end-June to roughly 50/50, a significant deterioration from last week's more optimistic positioning.

Against this backdrop, Friday's non-farm payrolls print — released during the Good Friday holiday — offered a modestly constructive data point. March payrolls rose 178,000, the strongest gain since 2024, and the Sahm Rule shows no current recessionary signal. However, the report warrants caution: wage growth is slowing across multiple measures, JOLTS data show hiring near post-GFC lows, quit rates are declining, and average unemployment duration has re-extended above six months. The picture is one of a labor market that is softening gradually rather than collapsing — consistent with a pre-recessionary plateau rather than clear resilience.

The Federal Reserve's March meeting minutes, due Wednesday, may offer insight into how policymakers are weighing the Iran-driven inflation shock against these mixed labor signals. Economists expect March CPI — also due this week — to show a roughly 1% month-on-month increase, the sharpest reading since 2022, driven primarily by the approximately $1-per-gallon surge in gasoline prices. That combination of slowing labor markets and accelerating inflation creates a genuinely uncomfortable policy dilemma for the Fed, with rate cuts harder to justify and rate hikes difficult to defend absent clearer demand-side inflationary pressure.
