US

Trump Nominates Warsh to Chair the Fed

President Trump named Kevin Warsh as his nominee to succeed Jerome Powell when Powell's term concludes in May. Warsh, a former Fed governor passed over for the chairmanship in 2017, has publicly aligned himself with the president's views on monetary policy, though his policy record suggests a more orthodox stance than the dovish posture Trump has demanded. Initial Wall Street reaction characterized the pick as relatively hawkish — a notable tension given the administration's persistent pressure for lower rates. Confirmation by the Republican-controlled Senate appears likely but is not assured. The practical implications for the rate path remain uncertain: Warsh would inherit a committee whose median view he cannot unilaterally dictate, and his historical concerns about asset-price distortions and inflation credibility could constrain any rapid pivot toward easier policy. As Jonathan Levin observed in Bloomberg Opinion, Trump "demanded a dove [and] picked a hawk" — a characterization that, if it holds, complicates the narrative of imminent Fed capitulation to political pressure.

Markets React to Fed News and Hotter PPI

US equities declined following the Warsh announcement, with the move compounded by a hotter-than-expected PPI print that reinforces concerns about sticky upstream inflation. The combination — a perceived hawkish nominee alongside firmer producer prices — pressured rate-sensitive sectors and contributed to a sharp reversal in precious metals, with gold and silver both falling sharply after recent record-setting moves. The metals decline marks a notable unwind of the safe-haven trade that had built through January amid geopolitical strains, including renewed friction over Trump's Greenland posture that had earlier revived "Sell America" flows.

Positioning Backdrop

Wealth advisers surveyed by Bloomberg generally counsel against elevated cash allocations despite the risk-off tone, with several rotating toward value equities and selective international exposure, including Japan. The advice reflects a view that current volatility, while elevated, does not warrant defensive capitulation.

Global

Trump Names Warsh to Succeed Powell at the Fed

President Trump selected Kevin Warsh as his nominee to chair the Federal Reserve following the May expiration of Jerome Powell's term. The choice is notable for its tension with prior expectations: Trump has consistently pressed for lower rates, yet Warsh is broadly viewed on Wall Street as a relative hawk, having criticized accommodative policy during and after his prior tenure as a Fed governor. Warsh has publicly aligned himself with the President's monetary policy views, though institutional constraints and FOMC composition suggest any pivot would be gradual rather than immediate. Markets responded with a risk-off tone: US equities declined, gold and silver sold off sharply amid broader volatility in precious metals, and the reaction was compounded by a hotter-than-expected PPI print. The juxtaposition — a hawkish nominee against a White House demanding easier policy — leaves the trajectory of US monetary policy unusually difficult to forecast, with implications for global rate differentials, dollar positioning, and the relative attractiveness of duration outside the US. Senate confirmation is expected given the Republican majority, but the substantive question of how Warsh balances political pressure against the Fed's institutional preferences remains open.

Among wealth managers surveyed by Bloomberg, the response to the current environment has tilted toward value equities and selective international exposure, with Japanese equities cited favorably — consistent with a view that US exceptionalism is being repriced at the margin.
