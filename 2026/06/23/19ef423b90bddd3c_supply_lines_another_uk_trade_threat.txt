Starting July 1, European Union consumers face a new €3 surcharge on cheap 
e-commerce items purchased mostly from China, but UK retailers like John Jones 
in Hereford, England, are worried they’ll suffer, too.

That’s because by the middle of next week, the EU will end its de minimis 
exemption allowing tariff-free flows of products under €150 ($172) — a 
temporary move in a broader modernization through 2028 of a customs regime 
swamped bynearly 6 billion 
<https://ec.europa.eu/commission/presscorner/detail/en/ip_26_735> e-commerce 
packages last year. An additional handling fee estimated to be €2 per item is 
expected to kick in by Nov. 1.

The goal in Brussels, following a similar end 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-25/the-de-minimis-loophole-why-cheap-imports-are-getting-pricier-and-slower?cmpid=BBD062326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260623&utm_content=5747>
 to de minimis by the Trump administration last year, is to level the playing 
field for domestic retailers and improve oversight of product safety in 
business-to-consumer packages sold online through Chinese-owned sites likeShein 
andTemu.
 
<https://www.fedex.com/content/dam/fedex/eu-europe/downloads/FedEx_DeMinimis_Customer_Guide_EU.pdf>

But with Britain’s £135 ($179) tariff-free cap for low-value imports remaining 
in place 
<https://www.gov.uk/government/consultations/reforming-the-customs-treatment-of-low-value-imports-into-the-united-kingdom/reforming-the-customs-treatment-of-low-value-imports-into-the-united-kingdom>
 for a few more years, local retailers and other merchants are bracing for 
sticker-shock disruptions from customers across the channel. Trade diversion is 
another concern, with an estimated 1 billion such packages already flooding 
into the UK annually.

“If they’re doing this in Europe, you can imagine that the sellers will say, 
’well, we must target the UK because it will be a lot cheaper again to dump 
product in the UK than it will be into Europe,” said Andrew Goodacre, CEO of the
British Independent Retailers Association. “We all fear another big rush of 
cheap product.”

All this creates challenges for Jones, managing partner with Philip Morris & 
Son, a small department store in Hereford, a county town in western England. 
The shop and itswebsite 
<https://www.philipmorrisdirect.co.uk/?srsltid=AfmBOoq_oAFb9UoF8jWyZ6sKhfFC9s4GZP17hRYo4FfJoJD7pkpcfL0_>
 sell everything from Barbour tote bags and handheld butter churns to duck 
calls for hunters.

Before Britain left the EU, 10% to 15% of Jones’ business was international. 
After Brexit, that share fell to 5%. His online sales to the US have all but 
dried up since President Donald Trump imposed import taxes on goods from most 
trading partners.

Now Jones is worried about what happens after July 1 and whether a customer 
base in the EU will disappear, too. “Our high street is being eroded day by 
day,” he said in an interview.

Supply ‘Bottlenecks’

Parcelhero, a courier service in the UK, indicated in a statement last week 
that a coalition of international parcel carriers contacted EU finance 
ministers urging a “phased approach” to the bloc’s new customs rules, “warning 
that key elements of the framework are not ready for the 1 July 2026 start 
date.”

“The carriers warned of supply chain bottlenecks and an impact on the 
availability of some medical supplies,” according to Parcelhero, which also 
nodded to another layer of complexity — how therules apply 
<https://www.niassembly.gov.uk/globalassets/documents/raise/publications/2022-2027/2026/proactive/3526.pdf>
 to goods moving between Northern Ireland and Ireland.

“Companies based in Northern Ireland and shipping from there to European Union 
destinations will not be impacted by the new flat customs duty,” the Parcelhero 
statement said, “as Northern Ireland effectively remains inside the European 
Union as part of the Windsor framework agreed between the UK and EU.”

On the 10-year anniversary of the Brexit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-06-09/what-rolling-back-brexit-vote-would-mean-for-the-uk-economy?cmpid=BBD062326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260623&utm_content=5747>
 vote, business group Logistics UK said Tuesday that misalignment with trading 
partners has cost the British economy an estimated £12 billion.

According to an analysis 
<https://logistics.org.uk/media/press-releases/2026/june/trade-led-growth-could-unlock-12-billion-for-the-u>
 by MDS Transmodal for Logistics UK, total goods trade with all countries and 
in both directions is down by almost 10% in the decade since the Brexit 
referendum. Blame lies with “increased processes and friction when crossing 
borders,” Logistics UK said.
