**Equity Markets and the AI Rotation**
US equities experienced a pronounced factor rotation during the week, with the S&P 1500 Software index declining more than 17% year-to-date as concerns mounted that AI tools are disrupting rather than augmenting incumbent software business models. The term "SaaSpocalypse" entered market parlance. AMD fell 7.3% in pre-market Monday before closing down 17% post-earnings, while Thomson Reuters dropped as much as 20% on concerns over Anthropic's legal workflow tool. Alphabet beat Q4 revenue estimates but guided 2026 capital expenditure to as much as $185 billion against a consensus near $120 billion, with shares falling 3.3% pre-market Wednesday. Qualcomm declined 9% and Arm 6.6% on softer outlooks. Amazon tumbled approximately 8% Thursday after disclosing $200 billion in planned 2026 AI-related capex. The aggregate implied spend from Alphabet, Amazon, Meta, and Microsoft now approaches $650 billion — a scale with few historical analogs. On the upside, Silicon Labs surged 33% on reports of a $7 billion Texas Instruments acquisition approach, and Enphase rallied 21% on a revenue beat. Breadth remained constructive: roughly two-thirds of S&P 500 constituents advanced Wednesday, the equal-weighted index hit a record, and Value outperformed Momentum by the widest margin since the November 2020 vaccine data. Nvidia's earnings later this month loom as a critical test of whether the software selloff represents rational repricing or overreaction.

**Fed Transition and Fiscal Developments**
The partial government shutdown ended after President Trump signed a funding deal negotiated with Senate Democrats. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent testified before the House Tuesday and the Senate Wednesday in contentious sessions. President Trump indicated he would not have nominated Kevin Warsh to the Fed chair role had Warsh signaled a preference for rate hikes — a comment that drew public pushback on institutional independence grounds, with Governor Lisa Cook reiterating the importance of central bank credibility. Senate Republicans separately raised concerns about the Justice Department's investigation into incumbent Chair Jerome Powell. Former Vice Chair Richard Clarida also weighed in publicly on the Warsh nomination. The succession dynamic, combined with questions about institutional independence, has been cited as a contributing factor in this year's demand for gold and dollar-diversification vehicles.

**Macro Backdrop and Affordability**
Q3 GDP was revised to a 4.4% annualized pace, the strongest in two years. Bank of America projects front-loaded 2026 growth, citing roughly $135–140 billion in fiscal stimulus equivalent to approximately 0.6% of disposable income, plus a 0.5–1 percentage point boost from post-shutdown normalization in Q1. AEI and Brookings researchers estimate the US may have experienced net negative immigration in 2025, with 2026 projections ranging from a 185,000 gain to a 925,000 decline — a development with potential labor-supply and trend-growth implications. Consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in over a decade despite real wage gains of 3.8% year-on-year in December. Goldman research highlights housing as the binding constraint: the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160–200% of annual income from 90–120% in 2000. Separately, the administration set aside $12 billion to establish a critical-minerals strategic stockpile, extending its industrial policy footprint into materials security and carrying downstream implications for domestic mining and processing equities. Western utilities — PG&E, Edison International, and Xcel Energy — remained in focus as a La Niña-driven warm, dry winter elevated wildfire risk; Xcel executed preventative shutoffs in Colorado affecting more than 50,000 customers, and Edison faces hundreds of lawsuits tied to last year's Eaton fire.
