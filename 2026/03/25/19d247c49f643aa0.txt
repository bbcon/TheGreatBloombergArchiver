Day trader fatigue

Retail investors, the most reliable dip buyers in the US stock market, might 
be turning into sellers.

Mom-and-pop traders on Monday notched their first day of net selling of single 
stocks since 2023, dumping $20.6 million worth of shares, data from Vanda 
Research shows. That came as the S&P 500 rallied after President Trump eased 
back on his Iran rhetoric.

Demand from this segment had been subsiding steadily as the Iran conflict 
drags on. And though they were back to buying on Tuesday, the hint of ebbing 
enthusiasm from a group that’s pounced on every pullback over the past few 
years is worrisome.

Another data point: A Citadel Securities gauge of retail’s risk appetite has 
declined sharply from February highs.

“The trend since the start of March has been one of gradually receding retail 
participation,” said Ruta Prieskienyte of Vanda.

Retail’s buying power has helped stabilize markets during bouts of volatility 
across the three-year bull market. Cooling interest from the investor group 
could spell trouble considering its growing influence on Wall Street. —

US rate hike bets

Investors have lifted odds that the Fed is on the verge of raising interest 
rates. But a fragile labor market and the risks to economic growth from surging 
oil pricesmake that a long shot 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/investors-are-betting-on-an-interest-rate-hike-that-s-really-not-that-likely?cmpid=032526_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
, at least for now.

On March 19, federal funds rate futures showed a 6% chance of an April hike, 
and have stayed in positive territory since. It’s the first time since 2023 
that investors saw a hike at the next central bank meeting as more likely than 
a cut.

The shift illustrates the enormous uncertainty for the US economy. But 
economists who closely follow the central bank say a rate hike in the near term 
remains highly unlikely.

“Of course the oil shock is a new inflation risk, but it’s also, if anything, 
a negative growth shock and maybe negative for employment,” said Citigroup’s 
Veronica Clark.

What’s going with futures? It’s worth noting that these markets are more than 
a simple measure of expectations. Molly Brooks of TD Securities said some of 
the trades are less predictions and more like insurance policies against an 
unlikely but damaging outcome.

Meanwhile, some investors sense opportunity. Pimco says central banks are 
unlikely to match the market repricing, creating an opening toinvest against 
the prevailing narrative 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/pimco-takes-contrarian-view-as-market-bets-on-global-rate-hikes?cmpid=032526_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260325>
. It favors exposure to interest-rate sensitive global bonds. —Maria Eloisa
