Oil in Troubled Waters

Washington’s U-turns in the Gulf have sent oil prices swinging wildly in 
recent weeks, but US stocks have moved in only one direction: up. So the 
market’s reaction toTuesday’s reversal <https://bloom.bg/42o96fF> of the US 
decision to provide escorts for selected vessels through the Strait of Hormuz, 
and then an Axios story saying a peace deal was close, should not have been 
surprising. Brent crude’s plunge didn’t last long, and it pared some losses 
when Tehran suggested a deal wasn’t imminent:

At the peak of hostilities, the S&P 500 and oil prices moved inversely, but 
that relationship is long gone:


Beyond oil’s latest retreat lies the question of how seriously Trump’s 
“promising” peace deal should be taken. It’s not clear that markets are pricing 
in a truly durable agreement that restores transit through the Strait without 
the risk of renewed disruption. Market-based inflation expectations, however, 
suggest confidence that the energy shock will unwind:

Such hopes have been dashed before. Still, Eric Golson of the University of 
Surrey argues that, given Tehran’s tolerance for economic pain, it could 
plausibly hold out for longer. In his view, the energy shock has yet to fully 
play out:

They’ve got the world backed into a corner and, at the moment, there’s no 
advantage for them to surrender before the world feels the pain. They have 
every incentive to keep holding on until they get the best possible deal they 
can.

For markets, reopening the Strait matters most. Brad Conger of Hirtle 
Callaghan points out that investors are less focused on parts of the deal 
relating to denuclearization; that raises the possibility of a narrower deal to 
restore transit flows first, leaving broader issues until later:

The world economy does not actually care whether Iran has a nuclear weapon or 
is developing a nuclear weapon. And so even a bad deal for the United States 
is, in the market’s view, good because it takes pressure off of all of the 
intermediates, in addition to fuel, fertilizers, plastics, and all the things 
that are starting to be in shortage.

Equities seem assured that some deal lies ahead and are looking beyond the 
shock, drawing support from robust high-tech investment. For them, the latest 
developments were a pure positive, and the stocks that were already leading 
performed best:

If a durable breakthrough does emerge, the beneficiaries might include travel 
and luxury stocks, among the hardest hit by the energy shock and the collapse 
in consumer confidence surrounding it. Conger notes that the most consequential 
shift may be in Europe, which has borne the brunt of the disruption. A 
sustained reopening could help the continent’s industrial backbone, potentially 
lifting sectors ranging from aerospace and autos to broader manufacturing.

—Richard Abbey

Private Credit Where It’s Due

The troubles in private credit aren’t going away, and they’re a reason for 
concern. But there’s a growing consensus that they don’t pose a systemic risk 
to the broader financial system. If true, that’s a great relief. The asset 
class was born out of the re-regulation after the Global Financial Crisis as the
Dodd-Frank Act <https://bloom.bg/4drK0mg> tightened capital requirements on 
traditional banks. A period of strain may be a healthy step — the disaster 
scenario, which everyone wants to avoid, would be to reveal that tightening the 
rules had merely moved risks out of banks and into a different place.

As private credit remains untested by a prolonged economic downturn, signs of 
trouble warrant close monitoring. A new report from the Financial Stability 
Board points to growing interconnectedness between private credit funds and 
banks, insurers, and private equity firms:

Many large alternative asset managers operate both private equity and private 
credit strategies under the same roof, leading to further integration and 
potential synergies, while potentially raising conflict-of-interest concerns. 
Private credit and private equity funds often draw capital from the same 
limited partners, such as pension funds and insurance companies.

Since JPMorgan’s CEO Jamie Dimon drew his infamous analogy to cockroaches 
<https://bloom.bg/3R91smS> last year, scrutiny has intensified, with the 
Federal Reserve’s governor and former vice chairman for supervision, Michael 
Barr, this week warning that“psychological stresses” <https://bloom.bg/3R91sDo> 
could spill over into broader credit markets.

Gauging the extent of the risk, however, requires assessing underlying credit 
quality and banks’ exposure to the asset class. On credit quality, the FSB says 
that the measures available to them are scarce and apply only to a subset of 
borrowers. With data limited, markets are relying on private ratings, often 
from smaller, lesser-known agencies.

This opacity can lead to  informational contagion <https://bloom.bg/4wboRnV>, 
which can in turn amplify credit issues. The available data suggest that 
private credit issuers are concentrated in single B-rated companies (not the 
most speculative), while leveraged loans and high-yield bonds are in a much 
wider range of credit quality:

Fed Chair Jerome Powell’s comments at Harvard University 
<https://bloom.bg/4nhZ8pM> in March that the central bank was watching the 
sector very closely has offered the greatest reassurance on private credit’s 
spillover risks. That’s backed by the FSB’s findings that banks’ lending to 
private credit is relatively limited. In aggregate, reported bank exposures are 
less than 0.5% of their total assets, where private credit lending could be 
separately identified:

That might seem to put contagion concerns to rest — but not so fast. The FSB 
warns that the complexity of funding structures may create spillover risks to 
the banking system during stress events. For example, private credit funds rely 
on bank credit lines for liquidity. That could shift pressures to banks, 
especially if corporate borrowers are also drawing on revolving credit lines.

The Middle East conflict has kept inflation risks alive, making it less likely 
that lower interest rates will ease any credit stress.Marcello Estevao 
<https://bloom.bg/4drK4Cw> of the Institute of International Finance argues 
that the issue isn’t scale, but the interaction between tighter financing 
conditions, borrower quality, and the slower recognition of stress. Fears that 
AI could unravel software companies’ time-tested business models have had a 
brutal impact on their share prices. That adds to the pressure on private 
credit, which remains concentrated in a handful of sectors. It’s certainly 
exposed to software, but this arguably has been exaggerated. This chart from 
Nomura Capital Management breaks down exposure by sector:
Source: Nomura Capital Management 
Nomura’s Matthew Pallai plays down the exposure to software, arguing that the 
problems currently involve isolated cases unlikely to cause broader contagion, 
outside of corporate direct lending-focused strategies. He’s still assessing 
possible routes by which the stress could yet become more widespread:

Refinancing constraints could emerge if capital flows slow, limiting the funds 
available to roll loans. Market infrastructure could tighten, reducing 
deployable capital. Finally, software stress could be an early signal of 
broader fundamental pressure in corporate debt, as history suggests the weakest 
segments show strain first.

—Richard Abbey


Starmergeddon?

The UK heads to the polls today to elect new mayors and councillors. 
Unusually, this local affair could have international repercussions. That’s 
because of the country’s excruciating exposure to the current geopolitical 
risks, and the political trouble for thehapless Labour prime minister, Sir Keir 
Starmer <https://bloom.bg/3R91riO>.

Rate expectations in the overnight index swaps market show that the oil shock 
is expected to force theBank of England <https://bloom.bg/3ONj5In> to tighten 
far more than the Fed. Before the Iran war, the two central banks were thought 
to be on much the same trajectory. Not any more:

Beyond monetary issues, the UK also has a fiscal problem. Ever since the 
disastrous tenure of Liz Truss in 2022, the focus has been on whether the gilts 
market will tolerate the borrowing that the government likely needs to jolt the 
economy back into life. With the spread of gilt over Treasury yields now higher 
than at the worst of the Truss crisis, the UK bond market — described as a bed 
of nitroglycerine by the bond investor Bill Gross more than a decade ago — 
looks ever more deadly:

As Jim Reid of Deutsche Bank points out, the UK has a negative net 
international investment position <https://bloom.bg/4tr3psp> (meaning it 
invests far more abroad than foreigners invest in Britain) and runs current 
account and budget deficits. This means, he says, that “it’s reliant on the 
kindness of strangers and has limited buffers against external shocks.”

Starmer’s parlous position shouldn’t affect this. Labour has a huge majority 
and needn’t face a general election until 2029. The talk is of ejecting him as 
leader, not a change of government. But it matters becausethe most plausible 
alternative candidates <https://bloom.bg/3R91uv0> are at least somewhat to 
Starmer's left, and would likely need to promise to borrow more. To quote Reid:

Markets have been paying attention, because the expectation is that a new 
Labour leader would face pressure to ease the fiscal rules, and hence raise 
gilt issuance. So on days when the pressure has mounted against Starmer, gilts 
have usually struggled as well.

It’s hard for Labour to appease the electorate without enraging the even more 
terrifying bond market.

Meanwhile, the issue of Europe is back, with those who opposed leaving the 
European Union increasingly emboldened to argue for a return as the Brexit 
referendum’s 10th anniversary next month approaches. Starmer is the UK’s sixth 
prime minister since then and none has yet delivered any positive results from 
the decision to leave.

The foreign exchange market’s view has been startlingly constant. Sterling 
crashed on referendum night. Those were chaotic conditions, but a decade later, 
the markets have never felt any need to reassess the judgment that Brexit would 
damage the pound:

It’s just possible that British voters might now be prepared to change their 
mind. But it will be difficult without clearer political leadership than 
currently seems on offer, and with a bond market that doesn’t trust the 
government to spend much more. Starmer may well go down as the UK’s sixth 
failed premiership in a decade, but it’s hard to see how any successor is going 
to do much better.
