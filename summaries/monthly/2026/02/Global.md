• Brent crude oscillated between approximately $63 and $70 through February, caught between escalating US-Iran tensions — including an Iranian drone intercept, a US ultimatum, and confirmation of Muscat and Geneva nuclear talks — and a building global supply glut that kept the structural downward pressure intact.
• Gold held above $5,000/oz through most of the month, peaked mid-month on safe-haven and geopolitical demand before a sharp Friday reversal, then stabilized; Bitcoin fell to roughly half its October peak, reinforcing bullion rather than crypto as the dominant store-of-value trade.
• January non-resident EM portfolio inflows approached $100 billion — the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades — as the dollar softened and investors rotated deliberately away from concentrated US exposure.
• The IEA's annual power outlook projects global electricity demand growing roughly 50% faster over the next five years than the prior decade, with consumption set to outpace GDP — a structural break with significant implications for inflation, capex, and the energy transition.
• The IEA Paris ministerial ended without a joint communiqué as the US threatened to exit unless the agency abandoned its net zero framing, marking a significant fracture in the institution Washington co-founded in the 1970s.

**Oil Markets and Iran Diplomacy**

The US-Iran dynamic was February's most consequential global risk variable. The month opened with the US Navy shooting down an Iranian drone heading toward an American aircraft carrier in the Arabian Sea on the first Monday, sending Brent to approximately $68. Iran subsequently confirmed nuclear talks scheduled in Muscat, and oil declined for the first time in three days as diplomacy appeared to temper the risk premium — though the American military build-up in the region continued. A second diplomatic track opened in Geneva in the final week, with US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi holding sessions both sides described as constructive, with Iran expected to return detailed proposals within two weeks. The tone shifted materially when President Trump stated Iran has ten to fifteen days to reach an agreement and that the US is "ready, willing, and able to rapidly fulfill its mission, with speed and violence, if necessary." Iran responded it would "defend itself and respond like never before," the Wall Street Journal reported the administration was evaluating a limited early strike to pressure rather than preempt, and the UK reportedly withheld access to Diego Garcia for potential operations. WTI moved from near $63 at the month's open toward $66 by the final Friday. The trajectory of negotiations — and whether Iran's expected response accepts core US red lines covering ballistic missiles and proxy financing, or whether the diplomatic track narrows — is the single most consequential near-term variable for global energy markets. Bloomberg Opinion's John Authers argued through the month that markets are under-pricing a major oil shock, a view that sits uncomfortably with current term-structure pricing and implies asymmetric risk should hostilities escalate toward the Strait of Hormuz. Iranian and Russian sanctioned crude accumulated unsold through the period, driven less by enforcement than by ample availability of legitimate alternatives at competitive prices — a development with meaningful implications for the marginal price of oil and for discount structures available to remaining buyers, principally China. South Korea's Sinokor group accumulated roughly 120 VLCCs over two months, helping push benchmark VLCC earnings above $120,000 per day — a more than fourfold increase over the month.

**Monetary Policy Divergence**

February produced some of the sharpest cross-country monetary policy divergence observed this cycle. The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, while the ECB, Bank of England, and Bank Indonesia all held. Norway's January CPI surprise drove 10-year sovereign yields to levels not seen since early 2009, shifting OIS pricing toward a March Norges Bank hike — treated by markets as idiosyncratic given the energy-heavy economy's inflation dynamics rather than a regional signal, as eurozone yields fell on the same session. A senior BOJ hawk signaled that conditions for Japan's next rate hike could align by spring. Russia's central bank delivered an unexpected rate cut; South Africa's Reserve Bank is now expected to cut three further times; Peru held steady. India's RBI held while citing improved clarity from a prospective US trade framework. In the US, the January payrolls beat pushed the expected first Fed cut from June to July, with the FOMC minutes subsequently revealing members had considered the possibility of rate increases. The MOVE index continued its steady decline from its April Liberation Day peak, suggesting bond markets are treating the institutional uncertainty around the Fed succession as manageable — though that complacency may face its first real test once Warsh arrives in May. The dollar's trajectory through the month was itself contested: dollar weakness accelerated in portions of the final week on tariff ruling and rotation dynamics, before the hawkish FOMC minutes triggered the dollar's best week in four months. Gold's persistence above $5,000/oz while Bitcoin fell to roughly half its October peak reinforces that bullion — not crypto — is absorbing safe-haven and dollar-diversification flows tied to US fiscal and policy concerns.

**AI Sovereignty and the Capital Expenditure Cycle**

February established AI sovereignty as a defining strategic theme for non-US, non-Chinese economies. India's AI Impact Summit, the first such gathering held in the Global South, crystallized a common structural concern across middle-power nations: the most consequential AI systems are controlled by a small number of US and Chinese entities, and access can in principle be interrupted. The 2024 UniSuper incident, in which a Google cloud misconfiguration cut off Australian pension fund members, served as a reference point for technology dependency risk. India's approach combines domestic small language models, shared compute facilities, semiconductor design ambitions, and active capital attraction. OpenAI's near-finalized funding round exceeding $100 billion — valuing the company above $850 billion, with Amazon, SoftBank, Nvidia, Microsoft, and sovereign wealth funds among anchors — underscores that AI infrastructure deployment has become a geopolitical as well as commercial competition. The BofA global fund manager survey showed for the first time in a generation that a majority of money managers fear corporate overinvestment rather than underinvestment, a direct consequence of $600 billion-plus in hyperscaler capex projected for 2026. Goldman Sachs economists projected data centers will account for 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation near 6% annually through 2027. China's ability to add generating capacity at a pace materially exceeding the US build-out — 543 GW of power capacity additions in 2025 — positions it on a more favorable power-cost curve for AI infrastructure, even as US export controls constrain its chip position. China's CO2 emissions fell 0.3% in 2025 — the first non-pandemic annual decline, as renewables absorbed all incremental power demand — while the US moved in the opposite direction, rescinding the 2009 endangerment finding and advancing a proposed 9.2-gigawatt gas plant in Ohio. The IEA fracture appeared genuine: the Paris ministerial ended without a joint communiqué, with only a Netherlands-authored summary published after Energy Secretary Chris Wright assailed the agency's net zero framing. The policy divergence between the two largest emitters appears increasingly structural and will shape investment flows, trade relations, and geopolitical competition through the decade.

**Capital Flows and Trade Corridor Realignment**

The global capital-flow picture reflected a deliberate reappraisal of US asset concentration risk. Non-resident portfolio inflows into emerging markets approached $100 billion in January — the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades per IIF data — with allocations broad-based across debt, equity, China, and ex-China. Hong Kong single-family offices, 25% more numerous at end-2025 than a year prior, reported among the highest reallocation intentions surveyed, with 19% planning reduced US exposure; China's official Treasury holdings fell to $683 billion in November, their lowest level in years. Against the backdrop of US tariff escalation reshaping traditional trade corridors, Gulf Cooperation Council economies emerged as a notable beneficiary: GCC trade expanded at 15% annually from 2021–24 versus a 9% global rate, and Asia-Middle East flows are projected to reach $1.5 trillion by 2030 from $905 billion in 2024. Britain signaled a GCC free-trade deal was imminent. The UAE imported approximately 560,000 Chinese passenger vehicles in 2025, reflecting its role as a transshipment hub for Chinese manufactured goods navigating Western tariff exposure. Port of Los Angeles export volumes fell 8% year-on-year in January, with soybean shipments to China down 80% as Argentine and Brazilian suppliers locked in multi-month contracts, effectively displacing US exporters for at least another agricultural cycle. The durability of these corridor shifts ultimately rests on whether the geopolitical and tariff environment that has accelerated them proves stable — a question that, as with much of February's macro landscape, remains genuinely unresolved.
