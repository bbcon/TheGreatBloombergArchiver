Bessent’s Banquet

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent likes to say that 2025 was about setting the 
table for the US economy — putting all the policy pieces in place, from the 
sweeping tax-cut act to deregulation to investment deals with countries around 
the world and a tariff wall to encourage domestic output.

This year was supposed to be the “banquet.” But a party pooper 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/iran-war-raises-us-gas-prices-above-3-40-testing-trump-on-inflation?cmpid=BBD031026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 has arrived in the dining hall, threatening to disrupt the feast: The Iran 
war, and specifically its shuttering of the Strait of Hormuz chokepoint, which 
has propelled gasoline and other energy costs higher.

President Donald Trump’s Monday declaration of the conflict as being “very 
complete 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/trump-hints-at-early-end-to-iran-war-easing-oil-shock-concerns?cmpid=BBD031026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
” notwithstanding, until tankers start passing through the strait in number, a 
return to sub-$70 a barrel crude prices is hard to envision. For its part, 
Saudi Arabia on Monday joined those looking to cut backcrude production 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/saudi-arabia-starts-oil-output-cuts-as-shut-hormuz-fills-storage?cmpid=BBD031026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
.

This hit to the pocketbook comes just as Americans were supposed to be 
cheering the arrival of enlarged tax refunds, from Trump’s “One Big Beautiful 
Bill” — a key serving of the 2026 “banquet.”

On average, households will see around $800 extra in tax refunds this year, 
according toAnna Wong <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AVZEA8jmBvs/anna-wong>, 
chief US economist at Bloomberg Economics. But if crude settles at $80 — it was
fluctuating around $91 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-10?cmpid=BBD031026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 early on Tuesday — that poses a $650 increase in their bill. In other words, 
the implicit energy tax hike cancels out most of the OBBB benefit.

If oil surges to and stays at $110 a barrel, that would impose a cost of 
around $1,960 per household, Wong says — more than sweeping away the entire tax 
benefit. Oil at about $83 a barrel is where the OBBB bump gets wiped out.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/democrats-seize-on-iran-driven-gas-prices-in-appeals-to-voters?cmpid=BBD031026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 are regressive, hitting lower-income households hardest. These consumers have 
already shown signs of retrenching, with much of the growth in US spending 
thought to have come from the wealthy in recent years. The top 20% of income 
earners accounted for over 59% of personal outlays as of the third quarter, 
according to Mark Zandi at Moody’s Analytics.

The longer the war and supply disruptions go one, the more reliant US spending

Need-to-Know Research

The old cliche says that history doesn’t repeat but it can rhyme. If so, 
there’s a curious rhyming going on when it comes to the US consumer price 
index, as pointed out by Jim Reid, Deutsche Bank’s global head of macro 
research and thematic strategy.

It shows that the course of US inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-07/us-inflation-gauges-likely-diverged-before-war-in-iran?cmpid=BBD031026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260310>
 over the past decade has been tracking reasonably — if not disturbingly — well 
what happened in the late 1960s to 1970s. That was when escalated US fiscal 
spending combined with major oil-price shocks sent inflation skyrocketing. As 
shown in the chart above, the next phase for CPI from 1978 was another surge, 
thanks to the 1979 Iran crisis.

“There are important differences today,” Reid highlighted in a note Monday. 
“Long-term inflation expectations have been remarkably stable, even through the 
2022-23 inflation spike. Economies are much less energy-intensive today, and 
labor markets have far lower unionization and wage indexation, reducing the 
risk of a 1970s-style wage-price spiral.” But the parallels so far are “eerie.”
