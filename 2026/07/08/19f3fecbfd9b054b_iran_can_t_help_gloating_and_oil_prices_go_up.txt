Mission Still Unaccomplished

Iran is back in the news. Following Iranian attacks on tankers in the Strait 
of Hormuz, the US renounced the waivers that had allowed the country to start 
selling its oil once more, and then announced afresh wave of military strikes 
<https://bloom.bg/4y8GtSf>.

Coming late in the East Coast afternoon, the news had a predictable effect on 
markets <https://bloom.bg/4y6cz17>, pushing bond yields up. The 10-year 
Treasury gained 8.2 basis points to hit 4.55%, while crude had its greatest 
rise since June 1, with Brent gaining 3.01%. This punctured optimism that 
inflation had been licked — although it was surprising that there was such 
confidence in the first place. Brent prices for December delivery, and the 
average price paid by US consumers at the pump, had been declining but remained 
some 25% higher for the year:

That calm reflected a belief that flows would resume, and were based on 
confidence that the US would not escalate the war into a broader land conflict. 
That assumption looks well-founded, and makes it harder for the US to threaten 
retaliation against moves that close the Strait of Hormuz. As the Bloomberg 
News Trends count of stories on the terminal from all sources shows, mentions 
of the Strait had on Monday had dropped to their lowest since the war started:

This was premature. Tankers are indeed moving through the Strait, but 
Bloomberg’s count of crossings shows that traffic is still far below normal and 
had begun to dwindle again: 

Market-based inflation forecasts had come down sharply; one-year swaps are now 
at 2.23%, compared to 2.5% on the eve of the war. But consumers feel 
differently. The New York Federal Reserve’s regular consumer survey 
<https://bloom.bg/3SOgcIS> shows they are braced for 3.67% inflation over the 
next year, the highest since 2023, and higher than at any point in the decade 
leading up to the pandemic:

Jean Ergas of Tigress Financial Partners suggested that the victory 
celebrations had always been premature. “Crude futures are falling because we 
are getting rid of one-off excess capacity. But not everyone wants to send 
their ships through Hormuz.”

He suggested that VAR (Value at Risk) metrics should be replaced by OAR (Oil 
at Risk), as tanker operators felt it necessary to cross the Strait in convoys 
and pay for the privilege. Meanwhile, Iran made a show of strength with the 
funeral of former Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, which demonstrated the regime 
remained intact and unchanged.

Iran seems really keen to show the world that the  US has lost 
<https://bloom.bg/4eQrfK5>. That more or less guarantees that volatility will 
continue, and markets will need to readjust.

Bad Times for Big Mo

The switchback for oil came as markets continued a startling reverse over the 
key trade in artificial intelligence and the chips needed to build it. Samsung 
Electronics Co. announced spectacularly strong earnings 
<https://bloom.bg/4voGOxD>, to be greeted by a selloff that brought its 
price/earnings multiple down to its biggest discount to the MSCI World on 
record:

Semiconductors are traditionally regarded as a cyclical business, which means 
low multiples, but returning to the lowest p/e ratio since 2019 suggests an 
overreaction in sentiment.

All of this has created much pain for the quantitative investing community, 
who make their bread and butter from momentum — the tendency for winning stocks 
to keep winning while losers keep losing. It’s prone to occasional sharp 
reversals, and it’s suffering a serious one now. This is how the 
exchange-traded funds for the S&P 500 Momentum index (including the winners) 
has fared compared to the S&P 500 Equal-Weight index, a measure of the average 
stock. This has been brutal:

What’s unusual is the lack of an external trigger. The last big momentum 
shocks were triggered by the Covid-19 shutdowns, and then the arrival of the 
vaccines that allowed a return to normality. Nothing so dramatic is happening 
at present.

There are various ways to measure momentum, but they agree this is a big 
event. This is how a “long the winners/short the losers” trade in technology 
stocks has fared over the last decade. (If on the terminal, open this in GP to 
view it on a log scale.):

Breaking the trade into its component parts, the nerves afflicting the winners 
among the semiconductor stocks are obviously important, but the baskets kept by 
Goldman Sachs show that the rise in the losers has also been problematic for 
the quants:

Globally, Societe Generale SA’s Andrew Lapthorne shows that it’s the losers’ 
recovery that’s really creating difficulties. While this is primarily a US 
phenomenon, the revival of previous losers in Japan and Europe has also caught 
out the quants:

Source: Societe Generale SA 
Lapthorne commented that such periods “inevitably trigger accusations of 
‘quant unwinds’— when the laggards rally, momentum strategies tend to be caught 
on the wrong side of the move, and this often exacerbates the situation.” When 
there are numerous overlapping themes, and all involve recovery for stocks that 
had been doing badly, it will be tough on quants. As these portfolios mostly 
are not very leveraged, there shouldn’t be serious systemic consequences, but 
the situation could easily have further to roll.


US vs. MCA, Round Two

Sadly the US <https://bloom.bg/4ePkhFd>, Mexico <https://bloom.bg/4y5odtb> and 
 Canada <https://bloom.bg/4y9jVkd> are no longer in the wonderful World Cup 
they’re hosting. All perished in the round of 16. That frees them to focus on 
last week’s news from theUS Trade Representative <https://bloom.bg/4yfYGh0> 
announcing that the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement (USMCA) won’t be renewed. From 
now on, terms of trade between the three nations of North America will be 
renegotiated each year.

That’s not great, but the relatively muted market reaction suggests the 
decision wasn’t entirely shocking, especially given Washington’s hardline 
attitude on tariffs. The Mexican and Canadian currencies barely budged. 
Bloomberg Economics’ indexes of trade policy uncertainty — based on news 
coverage — stayed low:

The six-year-old pact, <https://bloom.bg/48sY2Su> successor to the North 
American Free Trade Agreement (NAFTA), was due for a joint review beginning 
July 1. It governs roughly $2 trillion annually in goods and services. US 
imports from Mexico have surged since it was signed, helping to deal with the 
sharp fall in imports from China:

The deal’s future was always going to face pushback from Washington. Under the 
terms signed during Donald Trump’s first term, the countries can agree to 
extend it for another 16 years, or put it on a 10-year path toward expiration. 
The US could also withdraw with six months’ notice. While a catastrophic exit 
seems unlikely, what lies ahead is unclear. The ambiguity, Rafael Ch of Signum 
Global Advisors argues, was deliberate:

By not specifying that it is selecting option two – that is, put the deal on a 
10-year track toward sunsetting, with formal annual reviews in the meantime, 
the Trump administration is implicitly suggesting that it may still pursue 
option three (full termination) if its demands are not met.

That keeps Mexico and Canada under pressure from both Washington and their own 
domestic stakeholders, who will push their governments toward concessions to 
restore certainty. Ch points out that Mexican headlines frame the US decision 
as Mexico’s failure.

A potent pain point for Canada and Mexico is the risk that uncertainty will 
hold businesses back from making investments. This isn’t good for the US, 
either. Oxford Economics’ Michael Pearce argues:

It’s not clear to me that Canada and Mexico’s loss is necessarily America’s 
gain, but the logic of the administration will be that it works in that way, in 
that it prevents firms from investing with that long-term certainty. We’ve seen 
that reflected in the recent decision by Toyota to relocate some truck 
production from Mexico to a plant in Texas, which will be used as evidence of 
that.

With the auto industry alone accounting for almost 18% of US trade with its 
neighbors, according to the American Automotive Policy Council, concerns that a 
protracted review could present headwinds for carmakers are justified. Ongoing 
negotiations between the US and Mexico underscore the importance of the auto 
industry to the core of this agreement.
Vehicles awaiting export from the Port of Veracruz. Photographer: Brett 
Gundlock/Bloomberg 
Rules of origin <https://bloom.bg/4vKylps>, which determine where a product is 
made and whether it qualifies for preferential tariff treatment, are another 
thorny issue. The USMCA currently requires 75% of the “regional value content” 
for passenger vehicles and light trucks to be sourced from North America.

Pearce notes that the administration wants to raise that threshold to 82%, 
with 50% of a vehicle’s value produced in the US. Paradoxically, that could 
encourage automakers to build outside the US using the least expensive 
components they can find to reduce the value of imported vehicles to a point 
where paying tariffs is a better deal than complying with USMCA rules — and run 
counter to Trump’s quest to reestablish America as a manufacturing powerhouse. 
The one certainty: These neighborly negotiations will be fraught.

—Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



To win an election, the new popular strategy is to present yourself as a 
victim of the “establishment.”Marine Le Pen <https://bloom.bg/4vTLXyT> seems 
intent on doing this now that a French court has given her a suspended prison 
sentence for embezzlement, allowing her to campaign wearing an electronic 
monitor. In Britain, Nigel Farage responded to corruption allegations by 
proclaiming himself a target of a stitch-up and resigning his seat in 
Parliament so that he can win a new mandate. “This will be a 
people-versus-the-establishment byelection,” he says.“It’s a chance to stick 
two fingers up <https://bloom.bg/4y7nozM> to the entire establishment.’’ 

Except the establishment won’t show up. All the major parties dismissed it as 
a transparent stunt and refused to put up a candidate. Farage might want 
attention, but he’ll have to settle for a battle of ideas with perennial 
contenderCount Binface <https://bloom.bg/4eQoZT7>, which is about what he 
deserves. For Farage and Le Pen,this song many of you nominated to commemorate 
Trump’s intervention in the World Cup <https://bloom.bg/4y147Qy> seems 
appropriate.
