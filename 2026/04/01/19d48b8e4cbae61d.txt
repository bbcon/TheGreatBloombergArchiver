Eyes on Equities

Much of the focus on US consumer resilience since the Iran war broke out has 
been on Americans’ ability to cope with higher gasoline prices. Evidence 
suggests it’s better now than in past decades. Where the vulnerability may 
instead lie is the stock market.

Spending on motor fuels in January hovered near a record low as a share of 
disposable income at just 1.6%, the latest figures show. That offers some 
cushion for the recent surge in gas pricespast $4 a gallon 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/us-gasoline-price-rises-above-4-as-war-sends-fuel-costs-surging?cmpid=BBD040126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
.

What’s also come down: household savings rates, most recently at 4.5%, 
compared with 5.1% a year before and 6.4% in January 2024. Diminishing 
firepower from incomes showcases the importance of accumulated wealth as a prop 
for further consumption.

And on that score, corporate equities at market value accounted for more than 
a quarter of household net worth at the end of 2025 — marking a record high in 
some 80 years of data, the Fed’slatest quarterly report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/us-household-net-worth-climbs-2-2-trillion-to-fresh-record?cmpid=BBD040126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 on US balance sheets shows.

Tuesday’s surge in the S&P 500 on optimism about the potential for a 
negotiated solution to the US-Israeli conflict with Iran still left the 
benchmark down 5% for March, the worst monthly performance in a year.

“One risk to the US economy is the wealth effect from equity prices, which our 
work shows has been an important tailwind for consumer spending,” said Matthew 
Martin, a senior US economist at Oxford Economics.

Martin said in a note last week that “a more significant correction in the 
stock market could drive a sharper slowdown in consumer spending from 
higher-income households, which could prompt a bigger hit to the labor market.”

The war’s impact on spending will take time to emerge. A snapshot of US 
consumer health on the eve of the energy crisis comes Wednesday, with retail 
sales for February.

Economists have clashed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/how-k-shaped-us-economy-is-fueling-consumer-spending-debate?cmpid=BBD040126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 over the extent to which top earners account for the recent rise in personal 
consumption. But few would disagree that a deterioration in wealth would 
compound fears about the war’s hit to the US economy. The March slump in 
consumer sentimentwas particularly pronounced 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-27/us-consumer-sentiment-slips-as-inflation-outlook-worsens-on-war?cmpid=BBD040126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 among upper-income households, underscoring the importance of keeping an eye 
on markets.

Need-to-Know Research

An even more ominous observation accompanied a new economic outlook for 
Germany on Wednesday from the country’s leading institutes that showed growth 
this year will beless than half 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/german-growth-forecast-cut-in-half-on-iran-war-advisers-say?cmpid=BBD040126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 the pace they predicted in 2025.

The projections point to the prospect that even hundreds of billions of euros 
in debt-fueled stimulus currently flowing from the government may fail to avert 
a future where stagnation for Europe’s biggest economy becomes the new norm.

“Potential growth will come to a standstill in the medium term,” they wrote. 
That’s down from the current already meager rate of 0.2%.

In addition to the demographic decline in the working-age population, a 
decreasing number of working hours per employed person is contributing to this 
trend, the institutes noted.
