US equity markets continued their recovery, with the S&P 500 recording its seventh consecutive day of gains — the longest winning streak since October — as the Iran ceasefire appeared to hold heading into weekend peace talks in Islamabad. US junk bond spreads have returned to pre-war levels, and the Bloomberg US Corporate High Yield Index closed at a record high, reflecting a meaningful easing of financial conditions. The market's durability through a turbulent geopolitical period has been notable, though investors remain alert to weekend headline risk.

The day's macro focus shifted to the March CPI release, which analysts expect to show the fastest monthly headline increase in nearly four years, driven by the energy price surge following the US-Iran conflict. That inflationary impulse is complicating the Federal Reserve's path, with Bloomberg Economics now projecting only two cuts this year, down from four forecast in January. The outlook was further complicated by an unusual joint action from Fed Chair Jerome Powell and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, who summoned Wall Street bank CEOs to an urgent meeting over concerns that Anthropic's new AI model, Mythos — released to a limited set of firms — could enable a new generation of cyberattacks. Officials stressed that banks need to harden their defenses against AI-enabled threats, a signal that financial sector regulators are increasingly treating advanced AI as a systemic risk issue.

In corporate developments, an index of SaaS stocks fell 4.8% on Thursday to its lowest level in over three years, extending a roughly 40% decline in 2026, as Anthropic's release of its Claude Managed Agents product and a new Meta model renewed fears that AI agents will erode the competitive moats of incumbent software vendors. Optical component maker Lumentum rose nearly 5% in premarket trading on accelerating demand from large technology companies, while Nike slipped after a downgrade citing athleisure market saturation. First-quarter S&P 500 earnings growth is now estimated at 12.5%, up from 10.9% prior to the war, with results from Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase due next week.
