The overarching theme across global markets is the uncertainty created by the collapse of Trump's IEEPA tariff framework and the contested legal legitimacy of its Section 122 replacement. The practical consequence is a reconfiguration of relative tariff burdens: countries such as China, India, and Brazil, which faced elevated reciprocal levies, now converge toward the new 15% global rate, while prior beneficiaries — notably the UK and Australia — lose their preferential 10% arrangements. The British Chambers of Commerce estimates UK exporters face up to £3 billion in additional costs, affecting some 40,000 companies. Indonesia signed a bilateral deal with the US just last Thursday, leaving its status uncertain within days of the agreement.

Oil markets remain focused on the Iran risk premium. Brent crude has gained approximately 18% year-to-date, trading above $70 per barrel, driven in part by fears of US military action against Iranian nuclear infrastructure. Trump stated last week that Iran had 10–15 days to reach a deal, while also noting he is considering limited strikes. US-Iran nuclear talks are scheduled to resume Thursday in Geneva, with Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi meeting US envoy Steve Witkoff. Analysts and traders caution that a sustained oil price rally requires an actual supply disruption — prior episodes of elevated tension, including last June's Israeli strikes, produced short-lived price moves. US forces in the region are at their largest deployment in over two decades, widening the range of potential outcomes, but the base case among commodity market participants remains diplomatic engagement rather than kinetic escalation.

Gold moved toward its highest level of the month as investors sought haven assets amid trade and geopolitical uncertainty. Bitcoin fell more than 2%, trading near $66,000, extending a decline of more than 40% from its peak and raising questions about the asset's investment thesis at a time of otherwise supportive institutional and regulatory conditions. Private equity returned distributions equivalent to just 14% of net asset value in 2025 — the second-lowest since 2008 — with $3.8 trillion in unsold assets weighing on the industry, according to Bain. Blue Owl Capital's decision to gate one of its private credit funds has intensified scrutiny of the $1.8 trillion sector, with some observers drawing comparisons to pre-2008 credit market dynamics.
