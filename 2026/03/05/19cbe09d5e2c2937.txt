Energy Dominance Won’t Stop US Fuel Costs Pushing Higher
Costlier fuel could pose risks for Republicans at the midterms in November.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Will Kubzansky looks at US fuel costs — long seen by President Donald 
Trump as a barometer of his success in office — as the Middle East conflict 
sends oil prices higher.

The Iran war may be happening on the other side of the world, but US consumers 
are already starting to see the effects.

Average gasoline prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/gasoline-prices-surge-amid-iran-attack-a-liability-for-trump?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 jumped to $3.25 a gallon on Wednesday, according to data from the American 
Automobile Association — up by 25 cents this week.

Diesel, often referred to as a workhorse of the global economy because of its 
wide use in freight, power and heating,has soared 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/global-diesel-prices-surge-higher-as-iran-war-disrupts-supplies?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 almost 30% since the conflict began, outpacing gains in crude oil.

Any increase is unwelcome news for President Donald Trump, who has 
consistently touted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-05-11/trump-s-thirst-for-cheap-oil-irks-an-industry-he-loves-to-praise?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 lower pump prices as a measure of his success.

Rising electricity bills have already emerged as a major political issue 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-ai-data-centers-electricity-prices/?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
. Now, costlier fuel could pose even more risks for Republicans at the midterm 
polls in November.

To be sure, the US is well-positioned to absorb some of the supply shock 
caused by disruption to energy exports through the Strait of Hormuz.

The shale revolution elevated the country to its current position as the 
biggest oil and gas producer, significantly reducing its dependence on Middle 
Eastern crude supplies.

Under his so-called energy dominance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-17/here-s-trump-s-record-on-gas-prices-electricity-costs-energy-production?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 agenda, Trump has pursued even higher production as well as low prices. The US 
is drilling record volumes of oil and gas. It’s also a net exporter of gas and 
petroleum, an advantage not enjoyed by manyother countries 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/india-households-face-a-looming-fuel-crunch-as-iran-crisis-drags?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
.

The benefits of that privileged position have been on display this week as the 
disruption to Middle Eastern energy exports sends European and Asian natural 
gas buyers scrambling. US natural gas prices remain little changed by 
comparison.

But unlike gas, the oil market is truly global. Being the No. 1 driller isn’t 
enough to fully insulate the US from the effects of the war.

Trump has already acknowledged the price impact, saying that the US would 
providenaval escorts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/trump-says-us-will-escort-insure-oil-tankers-amid-iran-war?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 to ensure safe passage for tankers through Hormuz and get energy shipments 
moving again. More measures may follow.

—Will Kubzansky, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

It’s never been more expensive to ship crude from the US to Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/us-to-asia-oil-shipment-costs-hit-a-record-some-deals-falter?cmpid=BBBXT030526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
, though some deals are starting to fall through already with rates sky-high. 
As of Wednesday it cost just over $29 million to hire a supertanker to take 2 
million barrels from America’s Gulf Coast to China, according to the Baltic 
Exchange in London. That’s about double the cost from two weeks ago.

A deepening energy crunch across Asia is affecting all corners

as deals for new solar projects stalled, according toBloombergNEF <>. Wind drew
