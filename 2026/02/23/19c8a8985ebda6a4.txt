A Sustained Oil Rally Rests on Iran Tensions Hitting Supply
A sustained increase in crude prices will require an actual impact on flows.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, oil 
reporter Yongchang Chin looks at the prospects for the market as traders brace 
for a potential US move on Iran.

Oil has had the strongest start to a year since 2022, in large part thanks to 
fears of afresh conflict 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-19/iran-s-window-for-diplomacy-is-closing-un-nuclear-watchdog-says?cmpid=BBBXT022326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 in Iran that have offset concerns about a looming glut.

But it will take an actual supply disruption, something that has been absent 
from so many past conflicts, to keep the rally going.

Geopolitical concerns have helped drive an 18% gain in benchmark Brent crude 
so far this year, with futures now above$70 a barrel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-19/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-feb-20?cmpid=BBBXT022326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
.

And the dangers are real. The US is stationing a vast array 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-19/pentagon-amasses-mideast-force-as-trump-threatens-iran-on-talks?cmpid=BBBXT022326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 of forces in the Middle East — the most in more than two decades — and 
President Donald Trump said Thursday that Iran had 10 to 15 days to strike a 
deal over its nuclear program.

The last time the US deployed anything like this was before the invasion of 
Iraq.

Traders are preparing for further moves in the market, with Brent skews in a 
deep bias towardbullish call options 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/oil-traders-rush-to-hedge-iran-risk-after-wild-start-to-year?cmpid=BBBXT022326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
, which benefit buyers when prices rise. Volatility has surged to an 
eight-month high, showing crude’s erratic swings aren’t likely to let up.
A protest against war with Iran outside the White House in Washington, DC, 
last June.Photographer: Aaron Schwartz/Bloomberg
But it’s unclear how much further oil can go from here until there is a major 
development, refiners and traders say — likely one that goes beyond the Israeli 
strikes last June.

Any attack, whether a heavy assault or a limited warning to push Tehran to 
compromise, will come with a knee-jerk reaction, of course. But last year’s 
experience suggests that sustaining higher prices at a time of ample supply 
requires actual oil flows — from Iran or the wider region — to be affected.

When Israel fired missiles and tension escalated 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-14/israel-attacks-tehran-as-iran-fires-another-missile-salvo?cmpid=BBBXT022326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 over several days last year, prices did rise, but the surge faded quickly. 
There’s much more Iranian oil on the water now, too.

Arguably, the universe of possibilities is wider this time. With two aircraft 
carriers, fighter jets and refueling tankers, Trump hasleft his options open 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/trump-won-t-rule-out-limited-iran-strike-as-forces-gather?cmpid=BBBXT022326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
. He and other administration officials have not made their aims clear, either.

But a lasting surge in prices from this point requires more than probability 
or chance. It will require fact.

—Yongchang Chin, Bloomberg News
