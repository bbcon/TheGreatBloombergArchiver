What Might Have Been

Just for a moment, imagine an alternative world where US President Donald 
Trump opted against attacking Iran, sparing consumers and businesses from the 
prospect of another war-induced inflation shock.

That glimpse is what the OECD offered 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/war-hits-global-economy-with-us-inflation-seen-by-oecd-at-4-2?cmpid=BBD032626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
 on Thursday alongside new forecasts that try to assess the fallout stemming 
from the conflict.

The Paris-based club of rich nations described how the Middle East crisis 
arrived just as the global economy was picking up tailwinds from investment in 
artificial intelligence, an easing of US tariff rates and supportive monetary 
and fiscal policies.

Without the conflict, the OECD reckons it could have revised up its global 
growth forecast by 0.3 point for 2026. Instead, it left that prediction 
unchanged at 2.9% and trimmed its figure for 2027 by 0.1 point to 3%.

The bigger shock of course, given the disruption to energy supplies in and 
around the Strait of Hormuz, is to consumer prices.

“Before the conflict, inflation was on a downward path,” OECD chief Mathias 
Cormann told reporters. “Higher energy prices have reversed that trajectory.”

In its updated outlook 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-26/uk-faces-worst-hit-from-war-among-major-economies-oecd-warns?cmpid=BBD032626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
, the OECD sharply increased its forecasts for inflation in major economies. It 
now sees the average rate for the Group of 20 this year jumping to 4% rather 
than the 2.8% it predicted in December.

For the US, the organization expects inflation to jump to 4.2%, up from 2.6% 
last year. Its price outlook for 2026 is 1.2 percentage point higher than in 
December.

“The breadth and duration of the conflict are very uncertain,” the OECD said. 
“But a prolonged period of higher energy prices will add markedly to business 
costs and raise consumer price inflation, with adverse consequences for growth.”

The organization 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-24/war-knocks-global-economy-with-dual-shock-to-growth-and-prices?cmpid=BBD032626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260326>
 is the first of the major international economic institutions to formally 
update forecasts. The International Monetary Fund’s own version is due next 
month as its meetings in Washington begin.

Indicators such as business surveys have already begun to point to a 
synchronized global shock of weaker activity and rising prices. Policymakers 
meanwhile are pivoting away from rate cuts, and toward tightening.

“Central banks need to remain vigilant and ensure that inflation expectations 
stay well anchored,” the OECD said. “Monetary policy adjustments may be needed 
if price pressures broaden or if growth prospects weaken substantially.”

Need-to-Know Research

One feature of the oil crises of the 1970s was missteps by policymakers that 
made things worse, such as the Nixon administration’s imposition of price 
controls thatcreated shortages 
<https://energyhistory.yale.edu/the-oil-shocks-of-the-1970s/> and long lines at 
gas pumps rather than letting the full force of the market change demand and 
supply dynamics.

At least one economist is now advocating a full-force type of approach. 
“Governments are trying to cap prices, but this is counterproductive — we need 
the demand destruction that higher prices bring. Higher prices will help adjust 
demand to the market,” Eric Golson, an economics professor at the University of 
Surrey, said in a note Thursday.

And this isn’t just for gasoline, he added. US and European regulators should 
“immediately allow airlines to cancel and consolidate flights without penalty 
or loss of slots, to reduce demand for jet fuel.” In all, about 8 million 
barrels a day of energy “demand destruction is needed” in the second quarter, 
he said.
