Daily Brief

Wednesday, April 29, 2026

US

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell's final FOMC press conference headlines a consequential afternoon for markets, with the rate decision arriving concurrently with first-quarter results from Alphabet, Meta, Microsoft, and Amazon — the combined weight of these events representing a significant near-term test for equity valuations. Ahead of the decision, concern over whether AI capital spending is translating into returns briefly dragged the Nasdaq 100 down 1% after a report suggested OpenAI missed internal targets for user acquisition and sales, though the company pushed back on that characterization. Tech sector earnings are nonetheless expected to have grown roughly 41% in the first quarter — the strongest gain among all S&P 500 sectors — providing some fundamental underpinning to recent market resilience. On fiscal policy, key congressional Republicans appear poised to break with the White House over a proposed 44% increase to the Pentagon budget, a rare act of intra-party defiance that may signal the administration's softening grip as midterm elections approach. Separately, Republican lawmakers are also pushing to lower taxes on capital gains. Both dynamics complicate the fiscal arithmetic at a moment when 30-year Treasury yields are pressing toward 5%, with bond traders pricing the inflationary consequences of sustained elevated oil prices. Corporate news was broadly constructive. NXP Semiconductors surged 16% premarket on an upbeat revenue forecast, suggesting the auto semiconductor cycle may be turning. Seagate rallied 18% on strong AI-related storage demand. Starbucks gained after raising its sales outlook. On the downside, Robinhood fell nearly 10% after revenue missed estimates and management flagged an additional $100 million investment requirement for its "Trump account" initiative. Brown-Forman and Pernod Ricard abruptly ended merger talks, removing a potential transatlantic deal from the table. Meanwhile, Kearney's annual Reshoring Index remained in negative territory for 2025, suggesting that while tariffs have redirected some sourcing away from China, a meaningful revival of domestic manufacturing capacity remains years away.

Asia

The Bank of Japan delivered a "hawkish hold" at its April meeting, keeping rates unchanged on a 6-3 vote — a record number of dissents — while signaling its intention to raise rates further, likely in June, contingent on avoiding serious escalation in the Middle East. Despite the explicit forward guidance, the yen remains stubbornly weak near 160 per dollar, reflecting persistent investor skepticism about how far normalization can proceed under Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi, who has historically favored a weak yen to support exporters and appointed two dovish committee members last month. Takaichi recently criticized a trade minister for suggesting rate hikes could strengthen the currency, keeping political pressure on the BOJ visible. Spring Shunto wage negotiations produced a third consecutive year of approximately 5% wage increases, and five-year inflation breakevens have reached 2.7%, suggesting bond markets believe the deflationary era is genuinely over — yet the yen's weakness persists, creating a tension between the data and market pricing. In China, regulators have suspended the issuance of new autonomous vehicle licenses after dozens of Baidu Apollo Go robotaxis abruptly stopped operating in Wuhan last month, stranding passengers. Authorities called for a full safety review by local governments, marking a meaningful setback for an industry estimated to reach 83 billion yuan by 2030. Goldman Sachs staff in Hong Kong have separately lost access to Anthropic's Claude AI model, a location-specific restriction affecting primarily software engineers. Hong Kong Exchanges reported record first-quarter net income of HK$5.2 billion, up 27%, driven by a surge in IPOs and robust trading volumes. Hong Kong also ran its widest trade deficit in at least 74 years, a likely spillover from Hormuz-related supply disruptions. In India, major airlines warned of potential service suspensions unless the government reduces jet fuel prices, with the Federation of Indian Airlines describing the sector as "on the verge of closing down." As the world's third-largest oil consumer, India is absorbing disproportionate pressure from the Hormuz closure, with Brent trading near $111 a barrel following a 2.8% gain on Tuesday.

Europe

European bank earnings delivered broadly constructive results. UBS reported first-quarter net income of $3.04 billion, beating all estimates, with revenue rising nearly 30% driven by equities and foreign exchange — shares jumped 4.3% in Zurich and management confirmed intentions to add to its $3 billion share buyback program. Deutsche Bank posted higher-than-expected profit and revenue, supported by trading income and stronger contributions from asset management and retail banking. Banco Santander's profit also beat expectations, with its US expansion — anchored by the recent Webster Financial acquisition — providing incremental support. Adidas gained 8.3% in Frankfurt on strong apparel momentum. In the UK, a notable split-screen moment unfolded: King Charles III addressed the US Congress with an unusually direct appeal to reject isolationism, support NATO, and maintain the Ukraine commitment — remarkable in its pointed political content for a constitutional monarch. Simultaneously, Prime Minister Keir Starmer survived a parliamentary vote related to the Peter Mandelson appointment controversy, underscoring how managing the relationship with Washington continues to consume significant bandwidth in Downing Street. A UK research group separately warned that a deepening Middle East crisis could tip the UK into recession and force aggressive rate hikes, a tail risk that may be underpriced given the country's energy import exposure. In the Netherlands, the government is advancing a plan to impose a 36% annual tax on unrealized gains from stocks, bonds, and cryptocurrencies, effective 2028. Critics warn of capital flight and reduced domestic investment appetite. The proposal adds the Netherlands to a growing list of Western governments pursuing wealth-tax measures, though the political and economic pushback appears more acute given the country's historical reputation for stable economic policymaking. Separately, the EU is considering adding conditionality to its €90 billion Ukraine loan package, linking some disbursements to an unpopular business tax reform.

Global

The most consequential development of the day is the US decision to prepare for an extended naval blockade of the Strait of Hormuz, with President Trump reportedly concluding this represents a lower-risk path than either resumed bombing or disengagement. The implication is a prolonged period of neither active hostilities nor resolution — a structural supply shock rather than an acute one. Brent traded above $114 a barrel, with December futures reaching new highs, signaling that markets are now pricing the blockade as a durable condition rather than a temporary disruption. The US also threatened secondary sanctions on banks supporting Chinese private refiners purchasing Iranian oil, adding financial pressure on Beijing at a moment when the planned Trump-Xi summit in May was already navigating tensions over technology and trade. The UAE's shock exit from OPEC after 60 years adds a further destabilizing dimension to energy markets. The decision appears driven less by frustration with Saudi Arabia's near-term output policy — Riyadh has itself embraced looser supply management recently — than by a strategic imperative to maximize production before the energy transition erodes long-run demand. Abu Dhabi's urgency to pump reflects a view that the demand plateau may arrive within the next decade, undermining OPEC's core price-defense mechanism. The departure blindsided other members and raises questions about the cartel's long-term cohesion. Chip supply chains are emerging as an underappreciated casualty of the conflict. PGMEA, a photoresist chemical derived from naphtha and critical to semiconductor fabrication, has become harder to source since the war began. Shin-Etsu Chemical, Japan's largest chemical company, withheld its full-year guidance, citing naphtha-derived supply constraints; its most recent quarterly operating income fell 13% and missed estimates. Analysts note that photoresist disruptions take months to propagate through complex supply chains and are difficult to reverse — though for now, the broader semiconductor industry appears to have sufficient inventory buffers to absorb near-term pressure. The Bloomberg agriculture spot index has also hit a two-year high, up 13% over three months, as fertilizer supply disruptions and harvest uncertainty compound food price pressures globally.
