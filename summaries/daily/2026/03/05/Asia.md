Asia-Pacific markets are navigating both the immediate shock of a widening conflict and China's annual policy setting, producing a complex and unsettled backdrop. Asian equities recovered partially on Thursday after sharp Wednesday losses, though the rebound was uneven. The most dramatic single-session move came in South Korea, where the Kospi surged 10% after a two-day decline of 18%, supported by President Lee Jae Myung's invocation of the country's 100 trillion won ($68.2 billion) market stabilization program. The swings reflect Korea's acute vulnerability as a major energy importer with elevated retail investor participation.

The conflict's escalation into Indian waters commands particular attention. A US submarine sank the Iranian warship IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka's coast — the first US submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II — as the ship returned from an Indian-hosted multilateral fleet review. The Indian rupee fell to a record low against the dollar, and Indian equity benchmarks have in dollar terms erased all gains since last April's Liberation Day market turmoil. The incident has created a delicate diplomatic position for Prime Minister Narendra Modi, who called for "dialog and diplomacy" while facing uncomfortable questions about sovereignty in India's own maritime neighborhood.

China's National People's Congress delivered the session's most consequential policy signals. Premier Li Qiang set a GDP growth target of 4.5% to 5% — the lowest since 1991 — in an explicit acknowledgment that the country's economic model is under strain. Li described the transition to new growth drivers as "formidable," citing an imbalance between strong supply and weak demand. Fiscal stimulus is being modestly dialed back. Notably, Beijing is doubling down on technology: the accompanying five-year plan emphasizes AI, brain-computer interfaces, pharmaceutical innovation, and technological self-reliance, signaling that the rivalry with the US in advanced sectors will intensify. China also instructed its largest oil refiners to suspend diesel and gasoline exports immediately, reflecting precautionary energy conservation as Gulf crude flows are disrupted. On Hong Kong markets, mainland Chinese investors sold a record HK$27.7 billion worth of shares in a single session, the largest net selling on record, underscoring diminished risk appetite for assets perceived as geopolitically exposed.
