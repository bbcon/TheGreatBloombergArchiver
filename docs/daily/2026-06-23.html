Daily Brief

Tuesday, June 23, 2026

US

  • Federal Reserve policy remains a dominant market theme: Chicago Fed President Goolsbee stated that "too-high inflation is going the wrong way," reinforcing the hawkish tilt signaled at last week's FOMC, where half of members indicated at least one rate hike this year.
  • Global tech selloff accelerates: Nasdaq 100 futures fell 2.5% as AI spending skepticism and Fed rate concerns combined to reverse the year's dominant trade.
  • SpaceX shed approximately $600 billion in market value over three sessions and dropped below its first-day opening price of $150, while the company simultaneously launched a ~$20 billion investment-grade bond sale.
  • Oracle disclosed it cut 21,000 jobs over the past 12 months, attributing a portion of the reduction to AI-enabled productivity gains.

Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve remained at the center of market attention on June 23, with Chicago Fed President Austan Goolsbee adding to the hawkish tone established at last week's FOMC meeting. Goolsbee expressed concern that above-target inflation appears to be moving in the wrong direction, language that reinforces the signal from the June FOMC, where half of the committee penciled in at least one rate hike in 2026. Treasury Secretary Bessent, separately, invoked the legacy of the late Alan Greenspan — who died Monday at age 100 — to argue for Fed forbearance, drawing parallels between the mid-1990s technology productivity boom and today's AI cycle. Current Fed Chair Kevin Warsh has similarly cited that episode, though the analogy remains contested.

Equity and Credit Markets

The AI-driven rally that has defined 2026 faced its sharpest test yet. Nasdaq 100 futures slumped 2.5%, with chipmakers including Micron, Intel, and Sandisk down 6–10% in premarket trading. SpaceX, which lost approximately $600 billion in market value over three sessions, pressed ahead with a roughly $20 billion investment-grade bond offering rated Baa1 by Moody's despite sustained negative free cash flow — an unusual extension of credit market trust. IBM bucked the trend, gaining 3.9% on a JPMorgan upgrade. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi plunged 10% from a record high — triggering a 20-minute circuit breaker suspension — the largest single-session decline since the outbreak of the Iran conflict in March, led by Samsung and SK Hynix both falling over 12%.
  • A gauge of Asian technology stocks fell 6%, the steepest drop since March 9, as AI-related selling spread across the region.
  • Iran's 60-day US sanctions waiver triggered immediate outreach to refiners in India, Japan, and South Korea, with Iranian sellers contacting buyers before the license was formally granted.
  • Thailand's central bank governor signaled plans to curb buy-now-pay-later credit growth, citing risks to an economy already carrying one of Asia's highest household debt burdens.

Equity Markets

The AI trade, which has been the primary driver of Asian equity performance in 2026, went into sharp reverse. The Kospi's 10% decline was the most consequential move, given South Korea's role as a bellwether for global semiconductor demand. Samsung and SK Hynix — the two largest memory chip producers globally — each fell more than 12%, raising questions about whether the hyperscaler AI spending cycle that underpinned their re-rating is approaching a plateau. Separately, an SK Hynix ETF listed in Hong Kong saw assets swell to $17 billion, reflecting the scale of retail positioning now at risk. Chinese equities in Hong Kong entered bear market territory.

Iran Oil and Regional Dynamics

Iran's receipt of a 60-day US sanctions waiver set off an immediate scramble to re-engage Asian buyers. Sellers contacted Indian, Japanese, and South Korean refiners ahead of the formal license grant, though obstacles remain: EU and UK restrictions complicate insurance, dark-fleet vessels face port access issues, and Trump could revoke the waivers at any time. China's private refining sector — historically the dominant buyer of Iranian crude — is operating at a nine-year low utilization rate, limiting the immediate demand uplift for Tehran. ---

Europe

  • Eurozone composite PMI for June came in at 49.5, above the 48.5 consensus and up from 48.5 in May, though still below the 50 expansion threshold for a third consecutive month, suggesting continued contraction at a moderating pace.
  • UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer resigned; Andy Burnham is the near-certain successor after winning the Makerfield by-election and Wes Streeting's withdrawal from contention.
  • The EU's de minimis customs exemption on e-commerce packages under €150 ends July 1, with a €3 surcharge taking effect; UK retailers fear trade diversion toward Britain.
  • A heat wave struck Europe with temperatures reaching 43.3°C in central France on Monday, threatening power systems and disrupting Rhine shipping.

Economic Data

Euro-area business activity contracted for a third consecutive month in June but at a slower pace than anticipated. The composite PMI rose to 49.5 from 48.5 in May, beating consensus and offering tentative evidence that the region is absorbing the dual headwinds of the Iran-war-related inflation shock and broader confidence deterioration. The data does not yet indicate a recovery — the index remains in contraction territory — but the direction of travel provides modest reassurance. Rhine water levels fell sharply amid the heat wave, forcing barges to run at below half capacity and disrupting fuel shipments to parts of western Germany and the Netherlands, a supply-side risk that bears monitoring.

UK Political Transition

Starmer's resignation on the tenth anniversary of the Brexit referendum marks the sixth prime ministerial departure since 2016. Burnham's swift emergence as successor — enabled by a decisive by-election win and the clearing of parliamentary hurdles — appears to have contained market disruption. Gilts and sterling showed no signs of acute stress, and analysts at Gavekal noted that the absence of a drawn-out leadership contest reduces the risk of unfunded fiscal commitments. Burnham nonetheless inherits constrained fiscal space; the gilt market's reaction to Liz Truss in 2022 remains the binding constraint on any new government's ambitions. ---

Political Economy

The most consequential LatAm development of the period is structural rather than data-driven. The standoff in Bolivia's Chapare region has lasted over 18 months and disrupted economic activity through sustained road blockades; the decision to deploy security forces represents an escalation that could either resolve the disruption or intensify it. Separately, Cuba remained in focus as opposition leader Rosa María Payá outlined plans for post-regime governance, drawing on Trump administration support — a dynamic with potential implications for US-Caribbean policy and regional migration flows, though near-term market impact appears limited. ---

Global

  • The US issued a 60-day sanctions waiver permitting Iran to sell oil on international markets, the most significant shift in Iran sanctions policy in nearly a decade, as negotiations toward a permanent deal continue.
  • Brent crude fell 1.3% to approximately $72.89, and is up only 7% since before the conflict began, suggesting markets had largely priced in a temporary disruption.
  • Global tech equities suffered their steepest single-day decline since the Iran war began in March, with the AI rally under pressure from both valuation concerns and a more hawkish Fed.
  • Alan Greenspan died at age 100; his legacy — and particularly the debate over whether forbearance during a technology boom is vindicated by history — is directly shaping current Fed and Treasury rhetoric.

Energy and the Strait of Hormuz

The US sanctions waiver for Iran represents a meaningful de-escalation signal, but the downstream effects on global inflation remain unresolved. Brent's modest 7% gain since the conflict began reflects markets' prior assumption of a contained, temporary disruption — meaning the upside from a resolution was always limited. More consequential may be the lagged pass-through still working through the system: natural gas prices typically take three to four months to reach household bills, jet fuel surcharges have yet to be fully passed on to airfares, and fertilizer costs — elevated by both Strait disruption and higher gas-based production costs — have yet to fully feed through to food prices. A fifth of global ammonia-based fertilizer exports originate in the Gulf. Emerging market central banks, already operating in an environment where the FOMC has tilted hawkish, cannot yet declare the inflation episode over.

Monetary Policy Divergence

The Fed's hawkish shift is reverberating globally. EM central banks face a familiar dilemma: domestic inflation elevated by the energy shock, a stronger dollar environment if the Fed hikes, and limited room to ease. Japan's finance minister held talks with Treasury Secretary Bessent, providing only a brief boost to the yen, which remains near four-decade lows — raising the risk of intervention. The ECB's Lagarde called for any G7 currency discussions to include China, underscoring the degree to which currency misalignment has become a systemic concern alongside the energy shock.
