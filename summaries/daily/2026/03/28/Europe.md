Europe enters the weekend confronting a familiar but newly intensified energy vulnerability. The continent's previous exposure to Russian gas, substantially reduced since 2022 through a painful and costly transition toward US LNG and other alternatives, has inadvertently created a new form of dependence — this time on Washington's goodwill. Trump's envoy to the EU, Andrew Puzder, made that leverage explicit this week, warning that American LNG could be redirected to other buyers if Brussels fails to accelerate progress on a trade deal, or that terms "may not be as favorable." The European Parliament approved the trade agreement on March 26, though the longer-term implications of energy supply being instrumentalized as diplomatic leverage remain unsettling for European policymakers.

Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Jan Hatzius quantified the inflation risk in a "severely adverse" oil scenario, estimating that euro area headline inflation could rise by nearly 3 percentage points over the next year — the largest projected impact among major economies, driven by the dual exposure to crude oil and natural gas prices. The OECD separately concluded that the UK faces the largest output hit among G7 nations this year, a sobering assessment for a government already managing sluggish growth. Hatzius's analysts added a notable caveat: their estimates "may underestimate the nonlinear impact" of a severe gas supply shock — an acknowledgment that tail risks are not fully captured in central forecasts.

Germany is responding with a characteristically structural approach, drafting contingency plans to counter US pressure — including the potential targeting of White House-aligned tech firms in any future trade dispute — while simultaneously implementing domestic energy support measures. German economist Isabella Weber has drawn a pointed historical parallel, comparing the current moment to the early weeks of 2020, when the Wuhan outbreak had signaled catastrophe but its full dimensions remained unclear. Whether other European economies have sufficient fiscal capacity to replicate Germany's support measures is an open and consequential question.
