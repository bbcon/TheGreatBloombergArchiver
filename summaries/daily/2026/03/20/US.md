US equity futures were modestly lower Friday, with S&P 500 futures off 0.6%, as the Iran war's implications for monetary policy continued to weigh on risk appetite. The most significant development for US markets was the Federal Reserve's posture: Chair Jerome Powell offered no indication that rate hikes are on the table but gave equally little comfort to those expecting cuts, emphasizing that the inflationary impact of tariffs needs to dissipate before the Fed can ease. Futures markets have now fully repriced the 2026 path, eliminating expectations for any rate reductions this year after previously pricing two cuts. Separately, Fed policymakers revised their median year-end headline inflation estimate to 2.7% from 2.4%.

On the legal and institutional front, top Justice Department officials are rallying behind a US attorney's challenge to Fed Chair Powell's authority, with the White House declining to oppose the effort — a development with potentially significant implications for central bank independence that warrants close monitoring.

In corporate news, FedEx climbed roughly 10% in premarket trading after raising its earnings forecast, providing a modest counterpoint to the broader market anxiety. Super Micro tumbled 22% after federal prosecutors charged a co-founder with illegally diverting Nvidia-powered servers to China, a high-profile case that highlights ongoing concerns around AI technology export compliance. Options expiration Friday threatened to amplify volatility in an already turbulent tape; Goldman Sachs's trading desk noted that clients who had anticipated a quick resolution to the Iran war are increasingly doubtful, with some now anticipating either a market correction or a prolonged 2022-style grind lower. The US 10-year Treasury yield rose to approximately 4.30%, with the bond selloff reflecting a global reassessment of the rate path as energy-driven inflation concerns mount.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent floated the idea of lifting sanctions on Iranian crude already at sea — roughly 140 million barrels by his estimate — as the administration sought tools to cap oil prices. The practical impact appears limited, however, as virtually all Iranian crude is purchased by Chinese refiners, and broader sanctions infrastructure remains intact.
