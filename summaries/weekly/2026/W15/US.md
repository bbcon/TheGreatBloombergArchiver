**Labor Market and Inflation Dynamics**

Friday's March non-farm payrolls, released during the Good Friday holiday, showed a gain of 178,000 — the strongest print since 2024 — and the Sahm Rule currently shows no recessionary signal. The underlying picture is more nuanced, however. Wage growth is slowing across multiple measures, JOLTS hiring is near post-GFC lows, quit rates are declining, and average unemployment duration has re-extended above six months. The composite portrait is one of a labor market softening gradually rather than collapsing, consistent with a pre-recessionary plateau rather than durable resilience. Against that backdrop, March CPI — due Friday — is expected to show a roughly 0.9–1.0% month-on-month headline gain, the sharpest reading since June 2022, driven principally by gasoline's move above $4 per gallon for the first time since 2022. Retail diesel has risen nearly 50% to $5.62 per gallon, and the Logistics Manager's Index climbed to 65.7 in March, its highest since May 2022. The ISM non-manufacturing survey reinforced the concern, with more than 70% of purchasing managers reporting rising input prices — the largest monthly increase in 13 years.

**Federal Reserve and Monetary Policy**

The Fed's March meeting minutes, released Wednesday, offered insight into how policymakers are weighing the energy shock against mixed labor signals, though the core policy dilemma remains unresolved. Fed Vice Chair Philip Jefferson acknowledged the Iran conflict will push near-term inflation higher while characterizing current policy as appropriate. Bloomberg Economics now projects only two cuts in 2026, down from four forecast in January, and Fed funds futures have effectively priced out easing entirely. The five-year/five-year inflation breakeven remains below 2.2%, suggesting markets tentatively treat the energy shock as transitory, but that assessment is fragile and contingent on whether the Strait of Hormuz reopens in practice. Kevin Warsh's confirmation as incoming Fed chair remains pending in the Senate. The February core PCE data point — due Thursday and expected to show a 0.4% monthly gain keeping the year-on-year rate above 3% — will provide an additional read on whether the inflation impulse is broadening beyond energy.

**Equity Markets and Corporate Developments**

US equities navigated a turbulent week with notable resilience. The S&P 500 oscillated sharply around the ceasefire announcement — futures gained 2.6% on Wednesday and the index recorded its longest winning streak since October — while sector rotation was pronounced. Health insurers rallied sharply Monday after CMS proposed a 2.5% Medicare Advantage rate increase for 2027, with Humana gaining 11% and UnitedHealth rising 7%. Semiconductor and technology names benefited disproportionately from the ceasefire, with Micron rising nearly 10% and Western Digital gaining 8.3% on expectations of restored helium supply chains. Energy names moved in the opposite direction, with Exxon Mobil down 5.8% and Occidental falling 8.1% on ceasefire day. An index of SaaS stocks fell 4.8% on Thursday to its lowest in over three years, extending a roughly 40% decline in 2026, as Anthropic's Claude Managed Agents release and a new Meta model renewed fears that AI agents will erode incumbent software competitive moats. FactSet projects S&P 500 first-quarter earnings growth of approximately 12.5% year-on-year, with Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase results due next week. SpaceX has begun investor meetings for a potential IPO that could value the company above $2 trillion, and Morgan Stanley launched a Bitcoin-tracking ETF under the ticker MSBT, underscoring the asset class's institutional entrenchment even as Bitcoin trades near $71,000, roughly 40% below its October peak.
