President Donald Trump sent what might be construed as a chilling message to 
the thousands of American importers applying this week for refunds for his
illegal tariffs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/tariff-refunds-face-delays-over-slow-importer-opt-in-us-says?cmpid=BBD042226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
: In his eyes, you might fare better if you don’t.

“It’s brilliant if they don’t do that,” Trump said Tuesday on CNBC when asked 
whether companies should request repayments on import taxes that the Supreme 
Court struck down in February.

That ruling set the stage for a complicated task that could become the largest 
repayment by the US government in its history. (Click here 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-21/trump-encourages-companies-not-to-seek-tariff-refunds?cmpid=BBD042226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 to read the full story.)

Trump was asked if he’d be offended if companies such as Amazon.com and Apple 
end up seeking refunds. “If they don’t do that, I’ll remember them,” he said.

Despite capturing 40 cents of every dollar spent online in the US, the 
e-commerce giant’s tariff exposure is relatively limited compared with large 
retailers that pay tariffs on bulk shipments from overseas in order to stock 
store shelves.

Amazon doesn’t own most of the inventory it sells on its online marketplace. 
About 60% of all the products sold come from independent merchants who are 
responsible for covering the duty bill in addition to paying Amazon commissions 
on each sale, as well as fees for advertising and logistics services.

Apple’s Exemptions

Apple, meanwhile, has been diversifying its supply chain away from China over 
the past few years, though it still makes the bulk of iPhones in the country. 
The company alsowon exemptions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-06/trump-plans-100-tariff-on-chips-with-carveout-for-apple-others?cmpid=BBD042226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260422>
 from Trump in his first administration, and more recently with pledges to 
boost US investments. Still, in its fourth-quarterearnings call <> last year, 
the company indicated $1.4 billion in tariff-related costs.

Trump made his tariff refund comments a day after US Customs and Border 
Protection launched a web portal for importers to file requests for refunds 
that could total more than $160 billion. Yet, the process is still fraught with 
uncertainty.

Companies are facing pressure from consumers who have in some cases already 
filed their own lawsuits, seeking reimbursement for higher prices and 
surcharges they’ve had to pay over the past year because of Trump’s now-voided 
tariffs.

UPS Customers

“Importers and authorized brokers should anticipate that valid IEEPA refunds 
will generally be issued within 60-90 days following acceptance of the CAPE 
Declaration, unless a compliance concern requires further CBP review,” CBP said 
Tuesday in aFAQ post  <>on social media. “This timeframe includes 45 days for 
CBP review plus additional time to process the refund through the Treasury 
Department.”

Parcel carrier UPS said in a statement recently 
<https://www.ups.com/us/en/shipping/international-shipping/tariffs/tariff-refunds>
 that it would pass refunds to customers who paid tariffs via the courier, but 
only after the money is received from the government.

“There is no need for those customers to contact UPS. After we receive the 
funds from CBP, we have established a process to issue refunds to the payors,” 
the company said.

Earlier this month, Walmart Executive Vice President and CFO John David Rainey 
said the refund process would be complex and the timing uncertain.

“We’ll certainly avail ourselves of the opportunity that we have to get a 
refund,” Rainey said, according to atranscript <> of the discussion during a 
JPMorgan forum. “But when that happens remains to be seen.”

expectations index by the ZEW institute declined to -17.2 in April from -0.5 in 
March. That’s worse than anticipated by all but three economists in a Bloomberg 
survey, which had a median estimate of -5.8. A measure of current conditions 
also fell.
