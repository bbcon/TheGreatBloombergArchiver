Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
OPEC reporter Grant Smith analyzes the group’s latest policy move and looks at 
how shifts in the oil market could alter its calculus in the months ahead.

OPEC+ is emerging from the Iran war into an altered landscape.

When the conflict first shut 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-01/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-2?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
 the Strait of Hormuz and forced Persian Gulf producers toslash oil output 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/uae-oil-production-is-down-by-almost-half-amid-hormuz-closure?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
, it seemed inevitable that consumers would want them to ramp back up as fast 
as possible.

Instead, the biggest oil supply disruption in history is giving way to a 
surplus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-04/oil-s-stunning-reversal-rekindles-fears-of-a-global-glut?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
 inAsia and elsewhere 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-30/growing-oil-glut-spurs-asian-refiners-to-offer-cargoes-to-the-us?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
 as Middle East exports bounce back quicker than many buyers really need.

Even if this gush of barrels proves transitory, some forecasters see 
production outpacing demand. And that would present the Organization of the 
Petroleum Exporting Countries and its partners with tough choices.
OPEC headquarters in Vienna.Photographer: Christian Bruna/Getty Images Europe
On Sunday, key members agreed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-05/opec-agrees-another-modest-oil-output-quota-hike-for-august?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
 on the latest in a series of modest quota increases that would allow them to 
bolster output as Hormuz normalizes. The nominal hikes are starting to add up: 
They’ve ratified the return of more than 900,000 barrels a day since the war 
began, though much of this may not materialize.

Whether there’s actually a need to open the taps is another matter.

Group leader Saudi Arabia may be compelled to slow-walk the recovery of its 
production to prevent global markets becoming saturated, or even try to 
co-ordinate new groupwide cutbacks. The alternative would be to risk descending 
into a price war as members battle over market share.

But choreographing supply curbs, never an easy task, has only grown harder.

The organization is coming out of the conflict weaker, after long-standing 
member theUnited Arab Emirates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/uae-exit-blindsides-opec-and-threatens-to-shake-its-grip-on-oil?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
 quit in May. Iraq alsomounted a challenge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/iraq-might-leave-opec-if-its-production-quota-isn-t-raised?cmpid=BBBXT070626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260706&utm_content=6244>
, suggesting it could consider an exit in the future unless permitted a higher 
production limit.

The UAE, while pledging to act responsibly toward its former partners, has 
significant amounts of unused capacity to activate and its desire to do so was 
central to its departure.

But it would be a tall order to persuade the likes of Iraq — financially 
squeezed by its wartime export losses — or Kazakhstan, which pumps far more 
than it’s allowed, to make way for Abu Dhabi.

The responsibility for keeping markets balanced may land, as it often does, on 
Saudi Arabia. The question is: Would Riyadh be willing to shoulder that burden?

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Renewable energy investment in Africa has grown almost fivefold over the past 
decade, according to BloombergNEF. Last year’s figure represented a slight drop 
from the $19 billion peak reached in 2024, with a 42% slump in South African 
investment weighing on the total. Still, growth in Namibia, Cameroon and Kenya 
— mainly from small-scale solar — limited the decline.
