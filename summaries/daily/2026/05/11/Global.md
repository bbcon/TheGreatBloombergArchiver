The central tension in global markets remains the Iran conflict and the sustained closure of the Strait of Hormuz, now entering its tenth week. Donald Trump rejected Iran's latest peace proposal, sending Brent crude up as much as 4.6% toward $106 a barrel before settling above $103. Bond yields rose globally on renewed inflation fears, though equity markets showed limited reaction to the diplomatic impasse. Morgan Stanley warned that the oil market is in "a race against time" — the buffers that contained prices through the initial weeks of the conflict, including elevated US crude exports and a demand slowdown in China, may not hold if the strait remains closed into June. In a counterintuitive development, physical oil cargo prices fell sharply as buyers retreated from last month's bidding frenzy, though traders cautioned this calm may be short-lived.

The Hormuz disruption is reshaping trade and energy flows in structural ways. Iran is boosting rail freight to China via Kazakhstan and Turkmenistan, with train frequency rising from roughly one per week to one every three to four days and container rates along the route climbing 40% above typical levels. A Qatari LNG tanker made a rare passage through Hormuz to Pakistan — the result of Islamabad's mediation between Washington and Tehran — but this remains an exception rather than a trend, with Qatari export volumes still far below pre-conflict levels. China's clean technology manufacturers are explicitly leveraging the global energy shock as an export opportunity: EV exports rose 112% year-on-year in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third. Chinese firms are actively targeting new markets in Europe, Africa, and Southeast Asia. Against this backdrop, more than 40 nations were convening Monday to plan a European-led naval escort mission for ships transiting Hormuz once a ceasefire is established.
