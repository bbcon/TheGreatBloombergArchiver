**Technology Selloff Spreads to the Region**
The global technology rout that erased close to $1 trillion from an iShares software ETF over seven sessions extended into Asian trading early in the week, with Samsung Electronics tumbling and a Hang Seng gauge of Chinese technology shares approaching bear-market territory. The Nasdaq 100 fell roughly 4.6% over three sessions — its worst run since April — driven by concern over hyperscaler capex and AI disruption of incumbent software models. AMD's 17% post-earnings decline, combined with cautious guidance from Qualcomm and Arm citing potential memory-component shortages, weighed on regional chipmakers. By Friday, Asian equities had steadied, with Bitcoin recovering more than 3% from a low near $60,000 in Asian hours, and precious and base metals finding tentative footing. Against the risk-off backdrop, Baidu's announcement of its first-ever dividend alongside a $5 billion buyback represented a notable shift in capital-return posture from Chinese large-cap internet names.

**China: Growth Targets, Metals Speculation, and Geopolitical Friction**
More than a dozen Chinese provinces lowered their 2026 growth targets during the week, with consensus coalescing around a national target of 4.5%–5% at next month's National People's Congress — the first downward revision in three years. Citi now treats its previously baseline stimulus package (approximately 1 trillion yuan fiscal, 20 basis points of rate cuts, 50 basis points of RRR reduction) as a ceiling rather than a base case. On the geopolitical front, a Panama court decision voiding a CK Hutchison port contract drew a sharp response from Beijing, which instructed state firms to halt new project discussions in Panama and intensified customs inspections on Panamanian goods. Trump and Xi spoke by phone covering trade and Taiwan; tone appeared constructive, though substantive commitments remain opaque. Domestically, mainland retail flows into gold, silver, copper, and tin — driven by limited domestic investment alternatives — meaningfully amplified the metals cycle's recent volatility. China's top four gold ETFs recorded nearly $1 billion of outflows on Tuesday, and Shanghai copper futures registered record January volumes. Near-term positioning has clearly overextended, though structural drivers — central-bank gold accumulation, dollar diversification, and strategic metals demand tied to AI and grid infrastructure — appear intact.

**Japan and Southeast Asian Political Risk**
Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's LDP-led coalition was projected to comfortably clear the 233 seats required for a majority in Sunday's lower-house election, though the result itself constitutes event risk for regional rates and yen positioning given her expansionary fiscal instincts and comments on currency weakness, which she partially walked back. The Topix rally has increasingly been underpinned by governance reform — rising dividends, accelerating buybacks, and re-rating of long-discounted sectors. Elsewhere in the region, Moody's cut Indonesia's outlook to negative, citing fiscal risks, prompting an unusually pointed public rebuke of Citi economists by Jakarta's finance ministry. The Philippines posted its weakest non-pandemic growth in 15 years. India's RBI held rates, citing improved visibility from a developing US trade framework; Indian exporters face an 18% tariff rate once a joint statement is finalized, though a constructive outcome would benefit IT and pharmaceutical names. BYD's Hong Kong shares extended a selloff that has erased over $60 billion in market value since May. Australia's Reserve Bank delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, with markets pricing a further increase as services inflation proved more persistent than anticipated.
