Friday, February 20, 2026

US

The Supreme Court delivered the most consequential domestic policy ruling of the Trump presidency on Friday, striking down in a 6-3 decision the administration's "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs imposed under emergency powers. The ruling invalidates duties on goods from dozens of countries and could cut the US average effective tariff rate by more than half. The court left unresolved whether importers are entitled to refunds, which Bloomberg Economics estimates could total as much as $170 billion — a messy legal fight now remanded to lower courts. The White House indicated tariffs remain available under alternative legal authorities, suggesting the administration will seek to reconstruct its trade architecture on different statutory grounds. Market reaction, while notable, was not a complete surprise given legal challenges had been widely anticipated. The ruling lands against a backdrop of a broader market debate over Federal Reserve policy. A growing minority of money managers are skeptical of consensus expectations for two or more rate cuts in 2026. Invesco's Rob Waldner identifies one cut as his base case, with "no cuts" an increasingly plausible scenario given robust employment data and the surge of AI-driven capital expenditure. Carmignac's Guillaume Rigeade holds a short US Treasury position and projects the 10-year yield drifting toward 4.5% from roughly 4.07% currently. The dollar is on track for its best week in four months as rate-cut expectations are pared. Complicating the outlook further: Fed Chair Jerome Powell's term expires in May, adding institutional uncertainty to an already complex policy calculus. On fiscal dynamics, new CBO projections show net interest payments rising from $1 trillion this year to $2.1 trillion by 2036, reaching 4.6% of GDP — a ratio that alone exceeds Treasury Secretary Bessent's 3% total deficit target for 2029. The CBO does not anticipate a disorderly bond market response, projecting 10-year yields moving from 4.1% to only 4.4% over the decade, though analysts note the sheer scale of outstanding debt may constrain future administrations' fiscal flexibility. S&P 500 futures were little changed heading into the session. ---

Asia

Asian equity markets edged lower in early trading, with the Hang Seng declining 1.1% as the broader risk environment was weighed by rising geopolitical tensions and uncertainty around Iran. Against that backdrop, a notable bifurcation emerged within Chinese equities: shares of generative AI startups Zhipu and MiniMax Group soared as Hong Kong markets reopened after the Lunar New Year holiday, with investors rotating into pure AI plays and out of traditional internet giants. Robotics firms also attracted buying interest following their prominent showcase during China's Spring Festival gala, though the broader market remained under pressure. Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi used her parliamentary address to set out a broad economic agenda, pledging to "hit the button" on growth through strategic investment, active fiscal policy, and assertive diplomacy. Her comments reflect the difficult balance she must strike: stimulating growth without triggering the bond market volatility that has historically accompanied aggressive Japanese fiscal expansion. Separately, Japan's manufacturing PMI remained in expansionary territory for a third consecutive month, suggesting firmer corporate sentiment. Japan's cultural economy also drew attention, with Bloomberg identifying how intellectual property rights tied to Hello Kitty, Demon Slayer, and other franchises have minted a new class of billionaires — with pop culture now the country's second most valuable export category. India presented several constructive signals. HSBC's flash survey showed economic activity accelerating in February, partly attributed to improved sentiment following US tariff reductions on Indian goods. Prime Minister Modi's hosting of OpenAI's Sam Altman and Anthropic's Dario Amodei at the India AI Impact Summit catalyzed a roughly $4 billion combined rally in data center-related stocks. India also formally joined a US-led critical minerals supply chain initiative. Elsewhere in the region, Malaysian exports rose at their fastest pace in over three years in January, driven by electronics shipments. The US also finalized a trade agreement with Indonesia, with Jakarta committing to facilitate an estimated $33 billion in American goods purchases in exchange for lower US tariffs — a significant development for Southeast Asian trade architecture. ---

Europe

European equities outperformed on the day, with the Stoxx Europe 600 gaining 0.5% to 628, near the all-time high of 630 reached Wednesday. Strategists are already signaling caution, however: the median of 17 Bloomberg survey forecasts suggests European stocks will finish 2026 little changed from current levels, as eight consecutive months of gains and valuations at five-year highs leave limited room for further multiple expansion. Luxury names were a notable bright spot, with Moncler rallying 13% on results described by Barclays as significantly ahead of estimates, pulling LVMH and Hermès higher. The euro area flash PMI surprised to the upside, with manufacturers recording their best performance since 2022, driven by an unexpected improvement in Germany. Q4 negotiated wages rose 3%, a data point the ECB will monitor carefully as it balances disinflation progress against residual services-sector price pressures. Separately, the UK recorded its largest budget surplus on record, boosted by surging capital gains tax receipts and lower debt servicing costs — a brief fiscal respite for a government navigating difficult spending decisions ahead. The arrest of Prince Andrew on suspicion of misconduct in public office — on his 66th birthday — marks a significant escalation in the broader Epstein fallout, with the investigation set to scrutinize years of alleged ties to the convicted sex offender. The development adds pressure to the monarchy at a moment when public confidence in institutions is already fragile. Separately, Primark faces structural headwinds as sales flatlined at £9.5 billion and the company remains without a permanent CEO; parent AB Foods is weighing a potential spin-off, though analysts caution this would require performance improvement first. On climate policy, UK Energy Secretary Ed Miliband defended his clean energy agreement with California Governor Newsom despite criticism from President Trump, framing it as consistent with British national interest. ---

Global

The dominant cross-cutting theme remains the US-Iran standoff and its implications for energy markets. President Trump stated Iran has 10 to 15 days at most to reach a nuclear agreement, with the US having assembled a substantial military presence across the Middle East. The Wall Street Journal reported the administration is evaluating a limited early strike designed to pressure Tehran toward negotiations. Oil rose to near a six-month high on the back of these developments, with WTI trading around $66 per barrel. The situation remains fluid: the UK has reportedly held off granting the US access to Diego Garcia — a critical Indian Ocean base — for potential operations against Iran, complicating military planning. How Iran responds to the deadline, and whether diplomatic channels remain viable, is the single most consequential near-term variable for global energy markets. The fracture within the International Energy Agency crystallized this week at its Paris ministerial summit. US Energy Secretary Chris Wright assailed the IEA's climate focus, arguing that emissions policies have suppressed European growth and energy access. The US threatened to exit if the agency does not abandon net zero framing — a significant escalation given the IEA was co-founded by Washington in the 1970s. The meeting ended without the customary joint ministerial communiqué, with only a Netherlands-authored summary released. Members did find limited common ground on critical minerals cooperation and energy security, but the institutional fracture appears genuine and its longer-term ramifications remain unresolved. The Supreme Court's tariff ruling carries significant global trade implications. By invalidating the emergency-powers legal basis for reciprocal tariffs, the decision removes — at least temporarily — the single largest source of trade policy uncertainty for US trading partners. The US-Indonesia trade deal, the US-Taiwan import shift, and India's tariff concessions all reflect ongoing reconfiguration of supply chains that will continue regardless of the court's ruling. Separately, the EPA's own cost-benefit analysis of its endangerment finding rollback suggests costs could exceed $1.4 trillion under higher-end scenarios — materially complicating the administration's $1.3 trillion savings claim and likely to figure in ongoing legal challenges.
