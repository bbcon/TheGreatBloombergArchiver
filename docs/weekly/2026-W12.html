Weekly Brief

Mar 16 – Mar 22, 2026

US

  • The Federal Reserve held rates at 3.5%–3.75% on Wednesday, with Chair Powell signaling cuts are increasingly distant and colleagues raising the possibility of a hike — generating the S&P 500's largest Fed-day decline since 2024.
  • US gasoline averaged $3.70 per gallon as of Sunday March 16, up roughly 27% since hostilities began February 28, with diesel surpassing $5 per gallon for the first time since December 2022.
  • Futures markets eliminated all expectations for 2026 rate cuts by Friday, a dramatic reversal from the two cuts priced at the week's open.
  • Super Micro tumbled 22% on Friday after federal prosecutors charged a co-founder with illegally diverting Nvidia-powered servers to China; FedEx climbed roughly 10% after raising its earnings forecast.
  • The Fed's institutional standing came under pressure on multiple fronts: a prolonged Powell succession dispute, a Justice Department challenge to his authority, and a Supreme Court-linked tariff refund process still unresolved.

Monetary Policy

The Federal Reserve's Wednesday meeting was, by some margin, the most consequential in years. The FOMC voted 11-1 to hold the benchmark rate at 3.5%–3.75%, but the surrounding communication was unmistakably hawkish. Powell acknowledged "meaningful" internal movement toward fewer cuts and explicitly noted that some colleagues had raised the possibility of a rate hike — a formulation that rattled markets. The Fed's median year-end headline inflation estimate was revised upward to 2.7% from 2.4%. Core PCE was running at an estimated 3% in February, a full percentage point above target, and February producer price data showed core PPI rising to a three-year high. As Carl Weinberg of High Frequency Economics observed, the pipeline was already accelerating before the Strait of Hormuz blockade added a separate inflationary layer. By Friday, futures markets had fully eliminated expectations for any rate reductions this year. The 10-year Treasury yield rose to approximately 4.30% by week's end.

Institutional Uncertainty

The Fed's policy challenge was compounded by an unusually fraught succession picture. Powell confirmed he will not vacate his board seat — which runs to January 2028 — until a Justice Department investigation into the Fed's building renovation project concludes. Nominee Kevin Warsh faces a Senate confirmation hold from Senator Thom Tillis on the same grounds. The result is a protracted, politically charged transition at the central bank at precisely the moment when clarity of purpose is most valuable. Separately, top Justice Department officials are rallying behind a US attorney's challenge to Powell's authority, with the White House declining to oppose the effort — a development whose implications for central bank independence remain to be seen but warrant close monitoring.

Energy and the Consumer

The transmission from oil prices to household budgets is now clearly visible in the data. SMBC Nikko Securities estimates the gasoline price increase could reduce the personal saving rate by 60 basis points to approximately 3.9%. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to a three-month low in the March preliminary reading. January consumer spending was essentially flat even before the energy spike, and households no longer hold pandemic-era savings buffers. Oxford Economics cautioned that higher energy costs threaten to suppress spending decisions materially in coming months. The political arithmetic is uncomfortable: gasoline at levels last seen in 2023, tariff-driven price pressures still in the pipeline, and a midterm election cycle approaching. President Trump called for an emergency rate cut — a move associated historically with acute crises — while Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent floated lifting sanctions on approximately 140 million barrels of Iranian crude already at sea, a measure analysts regard as operationally limited given virtually all such supply flows to Chinese refiners.

Corporate Developments

The week produced a notably mixed corporate tape. Nvidia's annual developer conference was constructive, with CEO Jensen Huang projecting $1 trillion in AI processor sales through 2027, though his preference for copper interconnects over fiber optics weighed on photonics names. Meta gained roughly 3% in premarket Monday on reports of a $27 billion cloud infrastructure deal and a planned workforce reduction exceeding 20%. Lululemon fell approximately 2% after forecasting a second consecutive year of profit declines. Micron Technology, whose market capitalization has risen from roughly $100 billion at end-2024 to approximately $520 billion, reported after Wednesday's close. On Friday, Goldman Sachs's trading desk noted clients who anticipated a quick resolution to the Iran conflict are increasingly doubtful, with some positioning for either a sharp correction or a prolonged 2022-style grind lower. ---

Asia

  • China's January–February industrial production rose 6.3% year-on-year — the fastest pace since September — though analysts caution the Hormuz closure poses a material threat to sustaining that momentum.
  • Asian buyers scrambled for alternative crude supplies, with US oil purchases for April loading reaching approximately 60 million barrels — the highest single-month volume since April 2023.
  • South Korea's Kospi surged 5% on Wednesday after authorities restricted value-dilutive subsidiary listings; Samsung Electronics rose 7% on signals it is exploring multi-year memory chip contracts.
  • The Indian rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to a record low by Friday, with Nomura projecting it could reach 96 by end-June; Goldman Sachs projected 95 over the next year.
  • The Bank of Japan held rates unchanged at its Thursday meeting, with Governor Ueda citing Middle East uncertainty, while Japan's February exports to the US fell 8% year-on-year ahead of Prime Minister Takaichi's White House visit.

China: Resilience Under Pressure

China entered the week with the most encouraging domestic data of any major Asian economy. Industrial production expanded 6.3% year-on-year in January–February, retail sales exceeded forecasts, and fixed-asset investment turned positive after a period of contraction. Chinese equities regained some footing, and Jefferies' Christopher Wood argued that mainland equities are the most defensible in the current environment, citing robust domestic renewable capacity, battery storage, and reserve buffers as partial insulation from the energy shock. Beijing also began 2026 with its fastest pace of government spending growth since 2022, suggesting fiscal stimulus is being deployed to sustain momentum. The risk, as Gavekal Research flagged, is that rising commodity prices compress manufacturer margins — particularly where cost pass-through to consumers is limited — potentially triggering further investment pullback and necessitating additional easing. China's 2026 growth target of approximately 5% relies heavily on exports to non-US markets, making an oil-driven global slowdown a material threat.

China: Geopolitical Positioning

China remains the central geopolitical pivot of the Iran conflict. Chinese state media responded with undisguised scorn to US appeals for help reopening the Strait of Hormuz, and the structural leverage Washington once held through tariff threats has diminished, partly via Supreme Court rulings and partly due to China's commanding position in rare earth supply chains. Beijing appears likely to engage on its own terms and timetable, if at all. The Trump-Xi summit, originally planned for late March, was postponed to approximately mid-May — a delay Beijing reportedly views with quiet relief, given the diplomatic complications of hosting Trump while Iran war operations continue. A Paris meeting between senior officials appeared constructive, and the trade truce remains intact, though a pending $14 billion US arms package for Taiwan represents a significant latent irritant. Industry consultant FGE NexantECA indicated Beijing is close to drawing on its estimated 1.4 billion barrels of commercial oil reserves as the Hormuz disruption persists. Alibaba and Tencent combined lost approximately $66 billion in market value after results from both disappointed investors seeking a clearer path to AI monetization; Alibaba's quarterly net income fell 67%.

Japan and South Korea

Japan faces a specific supply chain vulnerability from tightening naphtha supplies, with multiple petrochemical firms announcing production cuts. The Bank of Japan held rates at its Thursday meeting but maintained a conditional tightening bias, with analysts noting a potential move as soon as April if inflation data cooperate. Prime Minister Takaichi characterized her White House meeting as "extremely difficult": Japan's pacifist constitution forecloses overseas military deployments, February exports to the US fell 8% year-on-year, and car exports by value declined 14.8%. Takaichi is expected to offer pledges of additional US investment, potentially in nuclear energy, as a diplomatic offset. South Korea's governance reform story was the week's most constructive equity narrative — the Kospi surged 5% on Wednesday and now trades above 5,900, with Samsung's multi-year contract signaling a potential structural improvement in memory market dynamics.

Emerging Asia

India occupies a particularly exposed position. The rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to a record low, Prime Minister Modi secured safe passage for LPG tankers through Hormuz — placing India alongside China and Russia — while managing US relations and preparing for April state elections that will test his handling of the energy crisis. Indonesia's central bank delivered a hawkish hold while tightening foreign-exchange regulations to defend the rupiah, which is down approximately 14% in March. The Philippine peso fell past 60 per dollar. Pakistan's airstrikes on Kabul, reported to have killed at least 400 people at a drug treatment facility, represent a separate and serious escalation risk that global markets have not yet fully priced. ---

Europe

  • The ECB held its key rate at 2.0% on Thursday but dropped language describing policy as in a "good place"; money markets are now pricing two quarter-point hikes this year.
  • UK 10-year gilt yields rose to their highest level since 2008 following the Bank of England's Thursday statement that it "stands ready to act" against inflation, as a unanimous hold was accompanied by a sharp hawkish shift in tone.
  • European natural gas futures jumped as much as 35% on Thursday after Iran struck Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex, driving benchmark prices to more than double their pre-war levels.
  • UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank, potentially clearing the path toward a full acquisition of Germany's second-largest lender.
  • Unilever confirmed it is evaluating a sale of its food division and received an offer from McCormick, valued by Barclays at approximately €28–31 billion.

Central Banks: A Hawkish Pivot

The week's most consequential European macro development was the unmistakable shift in central bank posture. The ECB held at 2.0% on Thursday — a rate in place since last June — but the surrounding communication had changed materially. The ECB dropped its description of policy as being in a "good place," and Bundesbank President Joachim Nagel told Bloomberg on Friday that a hike as early as April is conceivable if energy-driven inflation risks materialize. The Bank of England voted unanimously to hold, but Governor Andrew Bailey explicitly noted that the prior expectation of scope to cut has changed, stating the BoE "stands ready to act." Sweden's Riksbank held similarly. The direction of travel across European central banks is now unmistakably toward tighter policy. Bloomberg Economics estimated March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections, and analysts drew explicit parallels to the post-Ukraine 2022 inflation episode. UK gilt volatility subsequently became a focal point for global fixed income investors, compounded by February borrowing data that came in above forecast due to surging debt-interest costs.

Energy Exposure and Partial Cushions

Europe entered the crisis with more resilience than during the 2022 energy shock, though that resilience has limits. Rabobank estimated electricity prices would be approximately one-third higher absent the growth in solar and wind capacity added since 2022, with France's nuclear fleet also operating more reliably than during that earlier episode. European natural gas futures had already risen more than 60% since hostilities began before Thursday's jump of as much as 35% following the Ras Laffan strike, pushing benchmark prices beyond double their pre-war levels. Dubai crude contracts for Gulf-loaded oil are up over 80% in March. Evening power price spikes in several countries point to ongoing vulnerability, and analysts warn that seasonal solar support will fade heading into autumn. Germany's domestic response — restricting petrol stations to one price hike per day and tightening antitrust oversight — is a political gesture rather than a market-moving intervention.

Politics and Defense

At the EU Brussels summit, leaders called for a moratorium on strikes against energy infrastructure, with Italian Prime Minister Meloni described as particularly animated about the severity of the situation. Trump explicitly warned that the US "will remember" how NATO allies responded to the crisis, adding that insufficient support would be "very bad for the future of NATO." Most European governments — including the UK, Germany, and France — declined to participate militarily, citing both legal constraints and skepticism about escalation. European officials are simultaneously navigating fatigue from US-related crises, and there are nascent signs governments are reconsidering their posture toward China as a more predictable trade partner. Germany's €600 billion defense spending program is generating tangible economic stimulus, with defense suppliers reporting record order books, but Chancellor Merz's broader political standing remains precarious — fewer than a quarter of Germans hold a positive view of him. In the UK, Prime Minister Starmer faces mounting pressure from Labour's left flank ahead of anticipated May local election losses, with Reform UK continuing to lead in national polls despite emerging fragmentation from newer far-right parties backed by Elon Musk.

Corporate Developments

UniCredit's $40 billion bid for Commerzbank, announced Monday, would allow the Italian lender to increase its stake beyond the 30% threshold and represents the most significant cross-border European banking consolidation attempt in years. Unilever's exploration of a food division sale — with McCormick among the early bidders at a valuation of approximately €28–31 billion — would represent the largest restructuring in the company's near-century history. HSBC's reported review of approximately 20,000 roles as part of an AI-driven restructuring of its middle and back offices is among the first concrete signals of how the technology may reshape large financial institution workforces at scale. Logitech fell 5.7% in Zurich on a UBS downgrade; HelloFresh dropped 9.6% in Frankfurt on guidance materially below expectations. ---

Global

  • Brent crude rose above $118 per barrel on Thursday after Iran struck Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — the world's largest — before pulling back to approximately $108–110 on Friday following signals of potential restraint from both Israel and Iran.
  • The Strait of Hormuz remained effectively closed to commercial shipping throughout the week, with approximately 40,000 seafarers stranded on either side and vessel insurance premiums reaching roughly 5% of hull value.
  • Gold fell approximately 7% on the week — its largest weekly decline since March 2020 and on pace for its worst monthly performance since 2008 — as global monetary policy repricing eroded the appeal of non-yielding assets.
  • The Reserve Bank of Australia hiked rates, becoming the first of eighteen central banks to meet this week to move, signaling at least some inflation-targeting frameworks are treating the oil shock as requiring active tightening.
  • Tungsten prices have more than doubled this year amid Chinese export controls and rising military demand, illustrating how the conflict is amplifying pre-existing commodity tensions.

The Strait of Hormuz: Escalation and Tentative Signals

The Iran war entered its most dangerous phase this week before showing tentative signs of restraint by Friday. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities — prompting Iran to inflict significant damage on Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City on Thursday, sparking fires and causing damage that Qatar indicated could keep two LNG trains offline for up to five years. Additional strikes hit a gas facility in Abu Dhabi, set ablaze two Kuwaiti oil refineries, and caused damage near a Saudi refinery. Tehran designated energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE as "legitimate targets." Brent surged above $118 on Thursday. By Friday, Prime Minister Netanyahu stated Israel would no longer target energy infrastructure and said the war would end faster than people expect; Iran's foreign minister signaled that recent restraint was deliberate. Whether this constitutes a durable de-escalation or tactical repositioning remains genuinely uncertain. Prediction markets put the probability of a ceasefire by May 31 below 50% as of early in the week.

Energy Markets: A Fragmented Supply Landscape

The price signals across different crude grades and delivery points illustrate the geographic severity of the disruption. WTI traded near $96 on Thursday, Brent at $115, and Middle Eastern grades above $150 — a fragmentation that reflects a shortage most acute for Asian importers dependent on Gulf barrels. Ras Laffan supplied roughly a fifth of global LNG before the war; a restart now expected to take months rather than weeks is pressuring gas prices globally, with downstream effects on fertilizer production, power generation, and semiconductor manufacturing costs. Saudi Arabia appears to have rerouted some exports to above half of normal volumes via contingency infrastructure, offering partial relief. Russia has benefited materially, accelerating crude loading to capture elevated prices while a US sanctions waiver window remains open, with revenues from its key export blend hitting a record for weekly income since the Ukraine invasion began. Trump waived the Jones Act for 60 days, allowing foreign-flagged vessels to transport commodities between US ports, and the administration has ruled out banning oil and gas exports.

Central Banks and Macro Divergence

The week's central bank calendar was historically dense, covering approximately two-thirds of the global economy across eighteen policy meetings. All major central banks held rates, but the signal embedded in forward guidance shifted meaningfully toward caution. The Reserve Bank of Australia moved first, hiking on Tuesday in a split decision — an indication that at least some policymakers are treating the energy shock as warranting active tightening rather than patient observation. The Fed held Wednesday, the ECB and Bank of Japan on Thursday, with all three communicating heightened vigilance. Goldman Sachs economists offered a partially reassuring view, arguing that broader supply-chain disruption comparable to 2021–22 is unlikely, as non-energy Gulf trade represents only 1% of global commerce. However, the energy shock alone is sufficient to complicate deliberations materially. Bloomberg Economics estimated March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections, and BofA's global fund manager survey found institutional investors executing the largest overweight rotation into commodities since the 2022 Ukraine invasion.

Alliance Fractures and Geopolitical Reconfiguration

Trump's appeals for allied military support produced little tangible commitment across the week. Australia ruled out deploying vessels, Japan's defense minister cited constitutional constraints, and Germany, France, and the UK all declined to participate militarily. Europe's largest naval powers stated that reopening the Strait during active hostilities would require a deeply complex multinational capability not currently feasible. China's state media were openly dismissive. The episode has exposed meaningful fissures in the Western alliance architecture. Simultaneously, the transactional character of US demands — overlapping with tariffs and large investment pledge requirements — appears to be accelerating the formation of alternative bilateral arrangements among US allies. The Japan-South Korea rapprochement is the clearest example: both finance ministers agreed to expand bilateral economic cooperation including a review of their $10 billion currency swap, with public favorability in each direction described as at or near record highs. The IPCC faces an institutional challenge following US withdrawal of scientific funding, and New York's proposed rollback of its 2030 climate targets illustrates the political difficulty of sustaining decarbonization commitments when energy affordability pressures are acute.
