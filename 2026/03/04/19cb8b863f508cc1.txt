Long-Awaited Shift

Calls to open up the China market and spur consumption are nothing new.

President Bill Clinton argued China’s entry into the WTO in 2001 would open 
the market for American goods, only to see US trade deficits balloon. The IMF 
for two decades led calls for higher spending to “rebalance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-19/imf-calls-for-china-to-rebalance-toward-consumption-as-trade-barriers-rise?cmpid=BBD030426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
” growth, while Trump has launched two trade wars to try to force the issue.

Yet all the while, China’s leaders largely ignored those calls, pursuing their 
investment and export-led growth model to great effect. To them, consumption is 
a derivative of industrialization and development, not a driver of it.

Now, with the old growth model 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/china-economy-6-charts-explain-why-how-economic-growth-is-slowing-down?cmpid=BBD030426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 challenged by protectionism, overcapacity and a property slump, there are 
signs Beijing may be about to make anhistoric shift 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-02/xi-eyes-consumers-to-lead-new-era-for-china-s-unbalanced-economy?cmpid=BBD030426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
.

In coming days, President Xi Jinping and his officials will unveil the 15th 
Five-Year Plan, including a goal to spur a “significant increase” in 
consumption by 2030 — language more urgent than previous calls on domestic 
spending.

Currently stuck at an estimated 41% of GDP, Bloomberg Economics forecasts 
consumption will grow to make up 46% of the economy by 2030.

“Stronger consumption would not only unlock China’s next growth engine, it 
would help the world tap the full potential of its massive market,” economists
Eric Zhu <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AVK6e_X298w/eric-zhu-economist> and 
Chang Shu <https://bloomberg.com/authors/ATwU2T7qXnU/chang-shu> wrote in a note.

While officials seem more committed in their rhetoric 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-03/what-to-watch-as-china-s-leaders-hash-out-plan-for-economic-path?cmpid=BBD030426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 toward boosting consumption, it’s unclear how they’re going to achieve it. 
Households’ spending on goods is actually largely on par with the level seen in 
advanced economies — it’s just that the goods they’re consuming are often 
cheaper. That leaves making consumers spend more on services central to the 
rebalancing effort.

The BE economists prescribe structural measures including child and elderly 
care subsidies, more efforts to increase the birthrate, and anti-involution 
policies to boost demand and tackle deflation.

But no one expects China to abandon its industrial ambitions. Leaders also 
plan to keep manufacturing’s share of the economy at a “reasonable” level.

“Old habits die hard,” said Robin Xing, chief China economist at Morgan 
Stanley. “We are in a period of profound global change — a multi-polar world 
marked by persistent geopolitical tensions, where security increasingly takes 
precedence over growth. This naturally reinforces the view that manufacturing 
is the foundation of national strength and that industrial chains must be more 
competitive and self-sufficient.”

While economists will pour over the five-year plan and the 2026 targets 
outlined at the National People’s Congress, security wonks will be more focused 
on defense spending, which will also be detailed as part of the national budget.

For the past couple of years, Beijing has announced plans to increase defense 
spending by 7.2% (to about 1.78 trillion yuan, or $257 billion, in 2025), and a 
similar percentage increase can be anticipated this year. But those figures 
likely understate outlays, with Sydney-based think tank theLowy Institute 
<https://www.lowyinstitute.org/the-interpreter/solving-puzzle-china-s-defence-spending>
 forecasting actual spending of $542.7 billion for 2026.

Need-to-Know Research

The concept of rate cuts being a stimulus to the economy is of course well 
understood. But how exactly do they boost consumer spending in particular? A 
quartet of economists took a look at that in a new National Bureau of Economic 
Researchpaper <http://www.nber.org/papers/w34911>.

The main finding: rate cuts generate a significant bump up in consumption due 
to households borrowing against the appreciation in their asset holdings — 
especially house prices. With roughly 40% of US and UK households having a 
mortgage, a somewhat smaller impact comes from reduced debt-service costs, 
Angus Foulis, Jonathon Hazell, Atif Mian and Belinda Tracey calculated.

The economists sifted through individual-level data on mortgages and 
consumption and gauged changes in borrowing based on the effect of rate 
reductions on boosting asset prices. “Overall, monetary policy easing affects 
consumption in large part by raising asset prices, and encouraging household 
borrowing against the greater housing wealth,” they wrote.
