An old maxim of the oil industry is that big fields tend to get bigger. Crude 
tends to be found near already-known deposits. Better technology can access 
deeper or more technically challenging resources at lower cost.

Venezuela, at least for the last 50 years, has consistently bucked this trend. 
While home to by some estimates the world’s largest oil reserves, its 
production has steadily gone down, not up. Output has tumbled almost 70% from 
its peak of 3.7 million barrels a day in the 1970s. This has been devastating 
for the South American nation’s economy, with per-capita GDP falling by almost 
the same percentage over that period.

But everything changed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-05/how-the-us-is-reshaping-venezuela-after-maduro-s-ouster?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
 with the removal of Nicolas Maduro in January, when US President Donald Trump 
ordered a controversial military raid to seize the authoritarian leader. 
Venezuela’s oil production has soared 45% in three months, toalmost 1.2 million 
barrels a day 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-01/venezuela-oil-exports-hit-1-million-b-d-in-post-maduro-era?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
. While far from its heyday, the trajectory – should it continue – may have 
profound implications not just for the nation of 27 million, but the global 
economy and geopolitical competition.

Besides reviving Venezuela’s fortunes, tapping the country’s fossil fuel 
potential would shore up global long-term crude supplies while diminishing 
Middle East leverage over energy prices. A lot could go wrong, of course. Big 
Oil has had a tough history there, with multiple nationalizations. But the Iran 
war is likely reshaping industry perspectives.

Little more than a century ago, skeptics of Venezuela’s oil prospects were 
proven wrong when itsfirst gusher 
<https://www.ebsco.com/research-starters/history/oil-discovered-venezuela> 
blew. It’s worth watching closely again now.

Venezuela has had a rough few decades of soaring inflation, deepening poverty 
and mass emigration. The crisis has beendescribed 
<https://news.harvard.edu/gazette/story/2019/02/harvard-expert-tries-to-make-sense-of-venezuelas-collapse/>
 by Ricardo Hausmann, a Harvard University professor and former Venezuelan 
minister, as the “biggest economic collapse in human history outside of war or 
state collapse.”

In the immediate aftermath of Maduro’s Jan. 3 rendition, not everyone was 
ready to jump in. Companies had little appetite for investing billions — or 
tens of billions — of dollars at the behest of a mercurial American president 
while the world was facing an oil glut.

At the time, Exxon Mobil Corp. Chief Executive Officer Darren Woods memorably 
called Venezuela “uninvestable 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-09/trump-pressures-big-oil-for-100-billion-venezuela-investment?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
” during a White House meeting. “We’ve had our assets seized there twice,” Woods
told <https://www.youtube.com/watch?v=HRWayi2kwzE> Trump. “You can imagine to 
re-enter a third time would require some pretty significant changes.” (It’s 
worth noting Exxon has been busy plowing money into tapping the reserves of 
Venezuela’s neighbor,Guyana 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/exxon-touts-guyana-growth-in-race-for-next-decade-s-oil-fields?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
.)

But six months later, Exxon and ConocoPhillips are among the oil majors in 
talks aboutreturning to Venezuela 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-26/exxon-conocophillips-seek-safeguards-to-return-to-venezuela?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
 — and recovering debts from when their operations were nationalized in the 
2000s. Chevron Corp., the only American player to remain in the country during 
US sanctions, has pledged to raise production 50% by the end of next year.

On the ground, the energy surrounding this turnabout is palpable. Mostly empty 
a year ago,$400-a-night rooms 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-21/venezuela-s-cayena-hotel-in-caracas-is-hub-for-rich-investors-making-deals?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
 at the luxury Cayena Hotel in Caracas now are filled with oil executives, 
billionaires and hedge fund managers seeking new ventures. And American 
Airlines recently began thefirst US-Venezuela nonstop 
<https://news.aa.com/news/news-details/2026/American-Airlines-returns-to-Venezuela-with-first-Miami-to-Caracas-flight-NET-RTS-04/default.aspx>
 in seven years.

So what changed? Maduro’s former No. 2, now-Acting President Delcy Rodríguez, 
moved quickly to work with Trump and make clear Caracas was open for business. 
She quickly reformed the country’s oil and gas laws to encourage new 
investment, and signaled taxes and royalties would be open to negotiation. A
stream of Trump administration officials 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-30/us-embassy-in-venezuela-formally-reopens-as-trump-plan-advances?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
, from the energy and interior secretaries to theCIA director 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-16/venezuela-envoy-meets-us-officials-as-machado-visit-continues?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
, visited the country.
The Cayena Hotel in CaracasPhotographer: Bloomberg
Perhaps equally important, the US-Israel war with Iran triggered the effective 
closure of the Strait of Hormuz, cutting off about 10% to 15% of the world’s 
crude supply from late February. The International Energy Agency called it “the 
largestsupply disruption 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/oil-flows-through-hormuz-creep-higher-as-more-supertankers-exit?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
 in the history of the global oil market.”

While peace talks have proceeded, crude prices remain well above $90 a barrel, 
a level at which most of Venezuela’s reserves would be highly profitable. 
Regardless of when the Middle East conflict ends, it has served as a major 
wake-up call for nations around the world aboutover-reliance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-23/the-world-is-losing-the-gulf-s-petrodollar-stimulus-new-economy?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
 on the Persian Gulf.

“There’s a high premium being placed on diversity of supply,” said Amy Myers 
Jaffe, director of New York University’s Global Energy, Climate and 
Sustainability Lab. “That helps Venezuela.”

If current oil prices are maintained over the long run, Venezuela could add 
more than 900,000 barrels a day by 2035, with most production coming from 
existing fields where operating costs are just $8 a barrel, according to Rystad 
Energy.

The catch, however, is that it will require capital. A lot 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/venezuela-government-announces-debt-restructuring-process?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
. Getting back to Venezuela’s prior peak would require an investment of $100 
billion over a decade, estimated Francisco Monaldi, director of the Latin 
American energy program at Rice University’s Baker Institute for Public Policy. 
Rystad pegs the figure even higher, at $180 billion.

Still, that’s a fraction of what US hyperscalers are throwing into artificial 
intelligence this year alone, and it’s not beyond the capacity of the world’s 
biggest oil companies.

The bigger problem: Venezuela’s long history of being a graveyard for private 
capital. Not because of what’s below the ground, but because of what’s above 
it. But if the changes of the past four months are sustained, then production 
of the world’s most important commodity from the world’s largest reserves may 
soon get a lot bigger.

“There’s a change in the internal culture,” said Blue Water Acquisition Corp. 
IV CEO Joseph Hernandez, who said he’s looking to make deals in Venezuela. 
“They areacting like capitalists 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/venezuela-moves-to-open-power-sector-to-private-investment?cmpid=new-economy&utm_campaign=new-economy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260606&utm_content=5132>
 all of a sudden.”
