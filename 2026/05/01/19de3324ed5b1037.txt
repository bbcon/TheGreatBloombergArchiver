Need-to-Know Research

Economic history shows that successive technology innovation shocks have 
created jobs тАФ on average тАФ and thereтАЩs no reason to think that artificial 
intelligence would be any different, according to Citadel Securities.

Macro strategist Frank Flight wrote this week that AI is more likely to 
complement labor than replace it. His research expanded on a note back in 
February, when he argued that fears then spreading through markets that the AI 
boom would lead to mass layoffs wereprobably overblown 
<https://blinks.bloomberg.com/news/stories/TAZGV6T96OSJ?cmpid=BBD050126_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260501&utm_content=4030>
.

Flight points to a lack of evidence in recent data and earnings calls that AI 
is destroying jobs. For instance, openings on Indeed for software engineers тАФ 
one of the most AI-exposed occupations, have risen in past months even as 
overall vacancies fell. And an analysis of S&P 500 quarterly earnings calls 
show a surge in AI mentions but stability in labor trends, he said.
Citadel Securities
Companies are hiring engineers because of AI, not in spite of it, Flight said. 
Other sectors also are benefiting, such as construction, to build all those 
data centers.

тАЬThe history of technological change is not one of a fixed pie with a 
shrinking share attributable to labor, but instead of a growing pie, in which 
new technologies expand capacity, enlarge the productive frontier and leave the 
economy larger than before,тАЭ Flight wrote.
