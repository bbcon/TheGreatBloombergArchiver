End of the Tunnel

Finally, there’s light at the end of the long tunnel of China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-23/wall-street-banks-push-back-calls-for-china-rate-cuts-during-war?cmpid=BBD032326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
’s property slump.

Bloomberg Economics reckons the drop in new property construction since a peak 
in 2021 is getting close to realigning supply with demand. As with most things 
China, the numbers are staggering, so here’s some context.

Property construction peaked at 1,565 million square meters in 2021 — which 
means in that single year, China built out about 8.5 times the total floorspace 
of all buildings in Manhattan. Things got so out of hand that BE’sEric Zhu 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVK6e_X298w/eric-zhu-economist> reckons 
construction needed to fall by about 55% to realign with what’s actually needed.

It’s no wonder policymakers slammed on the brakes. With exports booming, they 
wagered they could take the steam out of the sector without sparking the kind 
of painful recessions seen in Japan or the US during their property slumps.

Now, BE says the 40% drop in property investment since 2021 suggests the 
adjustment is roughly 70% done, with the remaining 30% to be finished within 
two years, pointing to stabilization by 2027. Indeed, there’s already some 
green shoots, with price declines on average moderating in the first two months 
of the year and even eking out increases in 10 of 70 cities measured.

With real estate making up 60% to 70% of household wealth, stemming the 
downturn is important for stabilizing consumer sentiment and spending.

If BE’s forecasts prove accurate, China’s property downturn will have lasted 
around six years from a 2021 peak to a 2027 tough. That’s roughly the same 
duration as theUS housing downturn 
<https://www.federalreservehistory.org/essays/great-recession-and-its-aftermath#:~:text=Ultimately%2C%20home%20prices%20fell%20by,Liang%2C%20and%20Suarez%202009).>
 from 2006 to 2012 and way shorter than the two-decade downturn seen in Japan. 
So much for the Japanification thesis.

So will economic growth roar higher once the property fog lifts? Not 
necessarily.

As Macquarie Group economist Larry Hu has long argued, Beijing has tailored 
stimulus settings to keep the overall growth rate humming around target as it 
took advantage of theexport boom 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-22/china-premier-pledges-to-promote-balanced-trade-as-exports-surge?cmpid=BBD032326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
 to tighten the screws on property.

In a December report titled “When will housing bottom?” Hu argued the housing 
market wouldn’t level out until policymakers injected significant funding into 
the sector, but they may not do so as long as exports remain strong. And 
judging by the first two months of the year, exports remainred hot 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-10/china-s-exports-surge-tops-forecasts-as-iran-war-threatens-trade?cmpid=BBD032326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
.

BE isn’t calling for a rebound either. Stabilization will see housing’s 
contribution to GDP remain above 15% of GDP next year — still significant, but 
well down from the peak of around 25% in 2015-2018. Meanwhile, high-tech and 
green industries could account for about 20% of GDP in 2026, putting them on 
track to leapfrog property-related sectors for the first time, BE’s Zhu notes.

The Week Ahead

The global economy’s first collective health check since war broke out in the 
Middle East will arrive in the form of business surveys from the US to the euro 
zone. Every purchasing manager index for which Bloomberg collects estimates is 
anticipated to show a decline when initial numbers for March are released on 
Tuesday, according to the median forecast of economists.

Their outlooks point to a synchronized weakening across both manufacturing and 
services. Such results will offer a glimpse of the cumulative economic damage 
already racked up, three weeks after US and Israel attacked Iran.

Elsewhere, inflation data in Japan, Australia and the UK, Chinese industrial 
profits, and central bank decisions from Norway to Mexico may focus investors.

Need-to-Know Research

Economists at the Dallas Fed — which is responsible for a district with a 
large energy industry — estimate that the hit to global growth from a continued 
shutdown of the Strait of Hormuz through June would amount to2.9 percentage 
points 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/dallas-fed-sees-hit-to-global-gdp-if-strait-of-hormuz-stays-shut?cmpid=BBD032326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260323>
 next quarter.

“While our model does not account for all features of the current oil crisis 
(for example, we do not model the disruption of natural gas and fertilizer 
exports from the Persian Gulf), it captures the key economic mechanisms related 
to oil,” the trio of Dallas Fed research staff wrote in anote 
<https://www.dallasfed.org/research/economics/2026/0320> Friday. If the 
shutdown lasts just one quarter, then 2026 world GDP growth would be shaved by 
0.2 percentage points, measured fourth quarter on fourth quarter.
Source: Federal Reserve Bank of DallasSource: Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas
If the Strait’s shutdown lasts three quarters, that would be a 1.3 percentage 
point blow to global 2026 growth, they said. “Today, we are concerned with a 
shortfall close to 20%” of global oil supplies, which makes the current 
geopolitical event three to five times larger in impact than the 1973, 1979-80 
and 1990 oil shocks, they wrote.
