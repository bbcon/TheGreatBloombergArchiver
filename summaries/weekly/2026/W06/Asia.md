**China: Policy, Markets, and Geopolitics**

China's macro picture was defined by a tension between firming services activity and accumulating structural headwinds. January services PMI picked up, and Beijing reiterated its commitment to building a unified national market to unlock domestic consumption—a recurring priority that has yet to produce a durable demand-side rebound. More than a dozen provinces lowered their 2026 growth targets through the week, and consensus coalesced around a national target of 4.5%–5% for the NPC in March, the first downward revision in three years. Citi revised its stimulus baseline downward, treating its previously expected package—roughly one trillion yuan in fiscal support, a 20-basis-point rate cut, and a 50-basis-point RRR reduction—as a ceiling rather than a floor. Against this backdrop, Baidu announced its first-ever dividend alongside a $5 billion buyback on Wednesday, a notable capital-return shift from large-cap Chinese internet names that partially offset the broader tech selloff. President Trump and President Xi spoke by phone during the week, covering trade and Taiwan; the tone appeared constructive, but substantive details remained opaque.

On geopolitics, Beijing's response to a Panamanian court decision voiding a CK Hutchison port contract escalated materially. By mid-week, Chinese authorities had instructed state-linked firms to halt new project discussions in Panama and intensified customs inspections on Panamanian goods. The episode illustrates how port and logistics infrastructure has become a primary arena for US-China strategic competition, with downstream political risk for Chinese-operated maritime assets globally and potential acceleration of divestment discussions at COSCO and Hutchison-related entities.

**Japan**

Polling through the week suggested Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi's LDP-led coalition was on track to comfortably clear the 233-seat majority threshold in Sunday's lower-house election. The yen whipsawed on Takaichi's comments favoring a weaker currency, which she partially walked back. More durably, the Topix rally appeared underpinned by genuine governance reform—rising dividends, accelerating buybacks, and a re-rating of long-discounted sectors such as non-ferrous metals, with Sumitomo Electric having tripled since the US election. The 30-year JGB yield's surge through the week is best read as a catch-up to G7 peers rather than incipient fiscal stress, though Takaichi's expansionary fiscal instincts warrant continued monitoring.

**Australia and Southeast Asia**

The RBA's rate hike on Wednesday positioned Australia at the leading edge of major central bank tightening in 2026, with economists and futures markets pricing a further increase amid services-driven inflation persistence. The divergence from eurozone disinflation appears supportive for AUD on widening rate differentials, though housing-sensitive consumption represents a meaningful offset. In Southeast Asia, Moody's cut Indonesia's outlook to negative on fiscal risk concerns; Jakarta's finance ministry pushed back in unusually pointed terms, and the government's public criticism of a Citigroup economist raised questions about market communication under the Prabowo administration. The Philippines posted its weakest non-pandemic growth in 15 years. India's RBI held rates while citing improved clarity from a prospective US trade framework; reporting late in the week suggested a surprise bilateral deal could materially reset US-India ties, with Indian IT and pharmaceutical exporters among the most directly exposed beneficiaries.

**Metals and Capital Flows**

Chinese retail flows meaningfully amplified the metals cycle's volatility. Shanghai copper futures recorded their highest-ever monthly volumes in January. China's top four gold ETFs saw nearly $1 billion in outflows on Tuesday. Silver fell as much as 17% intraday on Wednesday to roughly $74/oz, taking its five-day decline to nearly 40%; trader Bian Ximing's large net-short silver position on the Shanghai Futures Exchange reportedly generated roughly $300 million in paper gains. The episode reinforced Beijing's challenge: with property and equity returns subdued, household savings continue to seek speculative outlets, testing official tolerance for retail risk-taking.
