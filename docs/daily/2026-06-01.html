US

  • ISM Manufacturing PMI and S&P Global manufacturing PMI due today; payrolls report on Friday expected to show +89,000 jobs with unemployment steady at 4.3%.

Inflation and Monetary Policy

The inflation picture remains the dominant domestic macro debate. Headline PCE has converged with CPI near 4% YoY in April — well above the Fed's 3% upper band — driven substantially by energy costs tied to the Iran conflict. New Fed Chair Kevin Warsh has indicated he follows trimmed-mean PCE, which is actually declining, potentially preserving optionality for easing later in 2026 if Middle East conditions improve. Compounding the complexity, one- and two-year inflation swaps have retraced roughly half their war-driven spike in the past two weeks, suggesting some moderation in near-term price expectations. Asset allocators nonetheless expect core PCE to rise further over the next year, a view that is difficult to reconcile with anything other than eventual rate hikes. The tension between a resilient real economy and persistent inflation will likely define Fed policy through year-end.

Equity Markets and Valuations

US equities are approaching another record despite a strategic stalemate in the Gulf. The AI investment boom — particularly in semiconductors — has been the primary driver, with Berkshire Hathaway's $6.8 billion acquisition of homebuilder Taylor Morrison adding a note of confidence in the broader economy. The Shiller CAPE ratio has exceeded its September 2000 peak, and sales multiples are the highest on record. A majority of global allocators now expect multiples to expand further — the first such consensus since the pandemic — a view that implicitly depends on profit forecasts materializing and the Iran disruption remaining contained. ---

China: Energy Drag and Deflationary Pressure

China's May activity data presented a deteriorating picture. The China Beige Book found broad weakness in revenues and margins, with decelerating output, soft retail sales, and fresh disappointment in housing. Services offered a partial exception. Despite producer prices rising at the fastest pace since the pandemic, consumer inflation remains subdued — reflecting demand constraints that limit firms' pricing power. Capital Economics notes a drawdown in Chinese oil inventories, which eases near-term price pressure but suggests the economy's energy buffer is eroding. The yuan has strengthened to a three-year high, partly reflecting exporters repatriating foreign currency receipts, though financial institutions are trimming appreciation forecasts in response to apparent central bank signaling. Export orders remain robust for now, supported by US tariff easing, but the durability of this channel depends heavily on Strait of Hormuz resolution.

Asia Semiconductor Boom

South Korea and Taiwan are the clearest beneficiaries of the AI capital expenditure cycle. Korean semiconductor exports at +169.4% YoY represent a record print; Taiwan has revised its full-year growth forecast upward, projecting export performance at a five-decade high. Nomura frames AI hardware positioning as a structural fourth variable in Asia's growth model, alongside labor, capital, and China integration. HSBC's Frederic Neumann cautions that the export boom remains narrow, and any pullback in AI hardware demand would expose underlying fragilities — a risk that remains difficult to quantify but worth monitoring as valuations extend. ---

ECB Policy and Inflation

The ECB faces a shifting calculus. Executive Board member Isabel Schnabel's statement that the bank can no longer look through war-driven inflationary pressures represents a meaningful rhetorical shift, suggesting the dovish case for further easing has weakened. Euro-area CPI due Tuesday will be the next data point to watch. Separately, the EU is considering a temporary freeze on its Russian oil price cap — currently $60 — ahead of a July review, with the ceiling likely to rise to at least $65 given prevailing market prices. This would limit Russia's windfall while acknowledging that the cap's original calibration is no longer consistent with current market realities.

UK Political Economy

UK political risk is rising. Andy Burnham, widely seen as a potential successor to Prime Minister Keir Starmer, has left open the possibility of an early general election — a statement that raises constitutional and market uncertainty. Meanwhile, the Starmer government faces pressure over leaked diplomatic exchanges involving former US ambassador Peter Mandelson. UK house prices fell at their fastest pace in nearly a year, compounding the political headwinds. EasyJet's board rebuffed a takeover approach from Castlelake as "highly opportunistic," though the airline's share weakness reflects genuine fundamental stress from elevated fuel costs. ---

Colombia: Political Realignment

The Colombian election result is the region's most consequential political development of the period. De la Espriella — a celebrity attorney running on a pro-Trump, law-and-order platform — demolished the traditional Uribista conservative movement while positioning himself against the left's Iván Cepeda. Investors consider Cepeda more radical than outgoing President Gustavo Petro, meaning a de la Espriella victory would likely be market-positive at the margin. The result extends a regional rightward pattern seen previously in Brazil, Argentina, and Chile. Immediate post-election rhetoric was inflammatory on both sides, underscoring the political fragility. A de la Espriella win on June 21 would strengthen US hemispheric alignment and may ease some of the investor concern around the Andean left's policy trajectory.

Mexico-US Tensions

President Sheinbaum's hardening stance toward Washington introduces a new variable for Mexican asset markets. Her framing of US intelligence activities as destabilization — since the April CIA deaths — carries political risk for near-shoring investment sentiment, even if the immediate macro impact is limited. The World Cup in Mexico City provides a brief moment of economic stimulus context, but the underlying bilateral relationship appears to be deteriorating at the margins. ---

Strait of Hormuz and Oil Markets

Three months into the US-Iran conflict, the strategic picture has not improved. Iran's forces retain the ability to constrain Strait of Hormuz traffic despite the destruction of its formal military assets, and this weekend's skirmish near the waterway confirms the ceasefire remains unstable. An interim deal — likely involving a roughly two-month extension with Iranian reopening of the strait in exchange for US lifting its port blockade — has been discussed, but exchanges over the weekend indicated no meaningful progress. Qatar has signaled openness to a temporary transit toll as a negotiating mechanism. The EU's consideration of a Russian oil price cap freeze is a direct consequence of elevated oil prices, and reflects how the Middle East conflict is reshaping European energy policy. Capital Economics suggests China's inventory drawdown may delay global oil price pressure until Q3, providing a temporary buffer.

AI and the Global Capital Cycle

The AI investment boom is functioning as a near-complete offset to war-related macro headwinds in financial markets. The semiconductor complex — Micron up more than 3x, SK Hynix +260%, Samsung +165% year-to-date — now includes three companies each with market capitalizations above $1 trillion. SpaceX is preparing to launch a $75 billion IPO at a $1.8 trillion valuation, large enough that index providers are rewriting benchmark entry rules in anticipation. HSBC's Neumann warns that the AI-driven export boom in Asia is narrow and concentration risk is rising. Separately, a looming LNG supply wave from 2026–2030, partly delayed by Hormuz closures, is expected to result in a prolonged glut — adding a deflationary counterforce to near-term energy inflation.
