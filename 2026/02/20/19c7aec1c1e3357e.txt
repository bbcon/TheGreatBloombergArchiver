Almost-Doom Loop?

One of the darkest scenarios for government borrowing features investors 
beginning to demand higher returns for the money they fork over to take on 
public debt. The government then has to borrow even more in order to pay for 
the higher interest costs on the bonds it’s just sold. That spurs investors to 
press foreven higher yields 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-13/long-dated-bonds-selloff-why-governments-face-higher-borrowing-costs?cmpid=BBD022026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260220>
. And so on. It’s known as the doom loop.

The latest benchmark projections 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/cbo-boosts-us-deficit-call-by-1-4-trillion-on-trump-policies?cmpid=BBD022026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260220>
 for US borrowing, assembled by the nonpartisan Congressional Budget Office, 
bore some element of this worrying storyline. Net interest payments by the US 
Treasury are seen climbing from $1 trillion this year to $2.1 trillion in 2036 
— or some 4.6% of GDP by then.

That ratio on its own is notably higher than the 3% total deficit-to-GDP 
target that Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has set for the end of the Trump 
administration, in January 2029. And a 4.6% ratio would make up the majority of 
the 6.7% overall deficit the CBO is penciling in for a decade from now.

But the agency isn’t anticipating the other part of the doom-loop scenario: 
Raucous demands from the so-called bond vigilantes to shell out more to sell 
the ever-increasing amount of debt. Ten-year Treasury yields are seen going 
from 4.1% this year to just4.4% in 2036 
<https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2026-02/61882-Outlook-2026.pdf> — hardly an 
earth-shattering change.

“It’s really a pretty benign outlook for Treasury yields,” CBO Director 
Phillip Swagel said last week. He pointed to demographics as one reason — an 
aging population is associated with helping damp interest rates.

Morgan Stanley strategists cited another: The liquidity and safety that US 
Treasuries offer investors the world over, and the relativelack of alternatives 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-10-17/termites-erode-dollar-s-foundation-but-alternatives-are-lacking?cmpid=BBD022026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260220>
. The bank’s macro strategy team, led by Matthew Hornbach, also wrote in a note 
last week that the income investors receive from Treasuries helps support the 
broader economy.

Even so, the “sheer size of the outstanding debt stock,” and its continuing 
increase, may end up constraining fiscal policy in future US administrations, 
Jim Reid, Deutsche Bank’s global head of macro research and thematic strategy, 
wrote in a note Thursday.

Eventually, Washington could look at policy levers to lower 10-year yields 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-02-07/trump-presidency-bonds-not-stocks-emerge-as-key-metric-for-economy-team?cmpid=BBD022026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260220>
, such as rejigging bank regulations, Reid’s colleagues including Matthew 
Raskin wrote separately.

It all adds up to not quite doom, but not cheery sunshine either.

Need-to-Know Research

Fiscal worries in Poland, a European Union member on the front-lines of the 
West’s tensions with Russia, have recently become so serious that they 
contributed to a decision by the nation’s government torein in a campaign 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/poland-s-bulging-debt-pushes-tusk-to-shelve-central-banker-probe?cmpid=BBD022026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260220>
 against the current central bank chief.

The thinking: Tensions over the central bank could hurt bond-investor 
confidence. Nevertheless, August Gudmundsson at Gavekal argues the nation’s 
economy is poised to see a transition toward an expansion of its supply side, 
putting Poland on track for a strengthening in nominal growth that should leave 
it well positioned for fiscal tightening later in the decade.

Poland is set to be the biggest beneficiary of the EU’s “Security Action for 
Europe” defense buildupprogram 
<https://defence-industry-space.ec.europa.eu/eu-defence-industry/safe-security-action-europe_en>
, and Warsaw’s own “East Shield”project <https://tarczawschod.wp.mil.pl/en/> 
will spur “substantial domestic construction and logistics” activity, 
Gudmundsson wrote in a note Thursday. Along with EU-funded capital expenditure, 
this means Poland’s growth is “becoming more investment-led and supply 
augmenting.” That should damp inflation pressures and create room for monetary 
easing, he said.
