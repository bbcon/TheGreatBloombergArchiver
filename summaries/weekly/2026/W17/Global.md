**Strait of Hormuz: Diplomacy and Market Implications**
The Hormuz impasse remained the week's defining global risk, and its evolution was characteristically volatile. Iranian Foreign Minister declared the strait "completely open" on Friday of the prior week, only for Tehran to reverse course within 24 hours after the US refused to lift its naval blockade. The US then boarded an Iranian-flagged cargo ship in the Gulf of Oman — the first such seizure since the blockade began — and separately intercepted two Iranian oil supertankers attempting to evade its blockade later in the week. President Trump's announcement of an indefinite ceasefire extension on Wednesday — delivered within 30 minutes of news that Vice President Vance's Islamabad trip had been cancelled — encapsulated the dynamic markets have priced: escalation optionality consistently resolved toward the off-ramp. The subsequent Friday sighting of the US-sanctioned supertanker Yuri carrying 2 million barrels of Iranian crude toward the strait suggested that enforcement resolve will continue to be tested. Iran's UN envoy indicated Wednesday that Tehran had received "some sign" Washington was ready to lift the blockade, suggesting backchannel communication persists even as formal talks remain stalled. Polymarket assigned only approximately a one-in-five probability to tanker traffic normalizing by end of April.

**Oil, Commodities, and Supply Chains**
The commodity dimensions of the conflict extend well beyond crude oil. The Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index has risen meaningfully since hostilities began, with wheat and rice moving higher; Bank of America flags corn as carrying the largest upside risk given mounting nitrogen fertilizer market pressure from Gulf supply disruption. Industrial metals have also rallied sharply, with copper and aluminum each up more than 11% since mid-March on a combination of supply chain disruption and structural demand from AI data center buildout and grid electrification. The copper-to-gold ratio recently touched four-decade lows, a level historically associated with commodity supercycle onset. Approximately 800 vessels remain stranded in the Persian Gulf, tankers face 3.5-day delays at the Panama Canal as diverted traffic surges, and DHL's CEO warned the global economy risks a "tipping point" if the supply shortfall persists. US shale producers, despite oil above $90 per barrel for nearly a month, have largely declined to increase drilling — citing policy uncertainty and price volatility linked to administration social media posts — with Continental Resources the only major producer to confirm expanded drilling plans.

**Energy Transition Acceleration**
The crisis appears to be functioning as an accelerant for structural energy transition. At the Petersberg Climate Dialogue in Berlin — the opening event on the road to COP31 in Turkey — delegates from nearly 40 countries framed the Hormuz disruption as a catalyst for reducing fossil fuel dependence. Battery installations are expected to jump roughly a third this year; China's solar cell exports rose 80% year-on-year in March, and EV exports surged 53%. China holds approximately 72% of cumulative long-duration energy storage installed capacity, though its dominance in that sub-segment appears less entrenched than in conventional lithium-ion, offering competitive openings for US and European developers. Brazil's program to electrify isolated Amazon communities via solar and battery storage illustrates how elevated diesel prices — a direct consequence of the Hormuz conflict — have improved the relative economics of clean alternatives, with the government projecting $171 million in subsidy savings and 800,000 metric tons of avoided greenhouse gas emissions by 2036.

**Geopolitics: Defense, Trade, and Dollar Dynamics**
Japan lifted most restrictions on lethal weapons exports for the first time since World War II, a development that drew formal warnings from Beijing about revived militarism. Chinese defense contractors made their largest-ever showing at the Defence Services Asia exhibition in Kuala Lumpur, with 192 firms and drone technologies prominently featured — a direct competitive response to the Western-aligned defense industry. The US unveiled measures targeting AI model distillation by Chinese developers, and Beijing moved to exclude Taiwan's president from African airspace by pressuring island nations to revoke overflight permits. On trade, the approximately $300 billion in tariff circumvention via Southeast Asian and Mexican transshipment is sharpening USMCA renegotiation tensions, with USTR Greer signaling potential enforcement action against Canada. Persian Gulf and Asian sovereigns seeking dollar swap lines with the US Treasury reflect the dollar's reinforced role in global trade under stress — a dynamic the IMF flagged as partly reflecting a relative deterioration in advanced-economy risk perception, with US Treasuries losing some of their traditional safe-haven premium amid fiscal trajectory concerns and questions about Federal Reserve institutional independence.
