US equity futures climbed Wednesday, with S&P 500 futures up roughly 0.9% and Nasdaq 100 futures gaining 1.1%, as diplomatic signals around a potential US-Iran ceasefire allowed markets to partially unwind recent risk-off positioning. The rally followed news that the Trump administration has drafted a 15-point peace plan, delivered to Tehran via Pakistan, though Iran's armed forces publicly dismissed the overture and continued missile and drone attacks on Gulf states and Israel. The market's relief trade remains conditional and fragile.

The Federal Reserve remains caught in a genuinely difficult position. Fed funds futures have briefly reflected a non-trivial probability of a rate hike at the April meeting — the first time since 2023 that a hike was priced as more likely than a cut at the next meeting. However, most economists close to the Fed, including Citigroup's Veronica Clark, characterize a near-term hike as highly unlikely. Chair Powell has made clear that the Fed will not even fully engage the question of whether to "look through" the energy inflation shock until the tariff impact has been separately assessed. The dual headwinds of an oil-driven inflation surge and a prospective growth slowdown leave the committee in a holding pattern. Fed Governor Michael Barr signaled rates may need to remain steady "for some time."

Adding a further layer of complexity, the anticipated arrival of Kevin Warsh as the next Fed chair is beginning to influence market pricing. New Fed chairs are typically tested for hawkish resolve, which arguably tempers the "debasement" narrative that had underpinned gold's January surge. Retail investor fatigue is also becoming apparent: Vanda Research data show Monday marked the first net selling day for individual stock buyers since 2023, with Citadel Securities' gauge of retail risk appetite declining sharply from February highs — a notable development given retail's stabilizing role through this bull market cycle.
