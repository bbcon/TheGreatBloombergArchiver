Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
EMEA Metals and Mining Team Leader Mark Burton looks at the serious, prolonged 
supply deficit that’s hit the aluminum market.

Hopes are rising that an end to the Iran war is in sight, but even an 
immediate resolution would come too late to avoid a major supply shock in the 
aluminum market.

Since the start of the conflict, the aluminum industry has been warning that 
Middle Eastern smelters would be forced to cut production if raw materials 
flowing into the region through the Strait of Hormuz were choked off for more 
than a few weeks.

Some curtailments have now materialized, but supply losses have also escalated 
dramatically in the wake of Iranianstrikes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/top-gulf-aluminum-producer-ega-halted-smelter-after-iran-strike?cmpid=BBBXT041626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 directly targeting two key smelters in Abu Dhabi and Bahrain at the end of 
last month.

The attacks helped push prices in London to four-year highs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-13/copper-falls-aluminum-spreads-spike-as-us-plans-hormuz-blockade?cmpid=BBBXT041626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 above $3,600 a ton and inflicted lasting production losses. That’saltered the 
calculus 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-29/iran-war-comes-for-aluminum-pitching-key-metal-into-crisis?cmpid=BBBXT041626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 for traders and analysts who’d previously been questioning whether short-term 
supply disruptions arising from the Hormuz blockage would be offset by a demand 
downdraft as the war ravaged the global economy.

Throughout the conflict, analysts at JPMorgan Chase & Co. have been warning 
that the industry was heading toward a black hole, or a “metaphorical point of 
no return” where the “global aluminum market will face a serious and prolonged 
supply outage” even if flows through Hormuz resume.

This week, it told clients the market has now entered that void, and forecast 
prices could top $4,000 a ton as the industry encounters the biggest supply 
deficit in more than 25 years. Aluminum’s all-time high of $4,073.50 a ton was 
set in 2022, when the Ukraine war sparked a similarly profound supply shock.
Aluminum billets at the Kato Light Metal Industry Co. factory in Kanie, Japan.
Photographer: Louise Delmotte/Bloomberg
It’s a stark turnaround for an industry that has been dogged by oversupply. 
Mined supplies are abundant and aluminum smelters are relatively cheap to build 
— but turning them off can be hugely costly and technically challenging.

In the past, that’s meant even in the face of low prices, smelters have been 
loath to cut output, fueling yawning mismatches between supply and demand. The 
flipside is that once smelters do power down, they can be expensive and 
difficult to restart, and supply is often lost for good.

So even in the face of surging prices — and with hopes for an end to the 
conflict rising — it’s likely to take at leasta year 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-03/ega-says-may-take-a-year-to-restore-abu-dhabi-aluminum-output?cmpid=BBBXT041626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260416>
 for regional supply to fully recover, and perhaps even longer.

The supply shock will hit western manufacturers particularly hard, given the 
key alternative suppliers are China and Russia, which have been effectively 
shut out of US and European markets by sanctions and trade tariffs.

Factories are already paying much higher prices to find other options, but for 
some buyers of the downstream products that Middle Eastern smelters specialize 
in, the shortfalls may prove impossible to resolve.

—Mark Burton, Bloomberg News

A glut is likely to build up in the global polyethene market as China halves 
the volumes it imports by 2030, aBloombergNEF analysis 
<bbg://news/stories/TDKLW5KGZAJU> shows. Mainland China is the biggest importer 
of the material used in goods from plastic bags to pipes and auto parts. The 
nation is building its own PE capacity, reducing the need for imports. Supply 
also is growing in the Middle East, US and Russia. Europe needs more PE as it 
shuts domestic capacity, but demand elsewhere can’t absorb all the new supply.
