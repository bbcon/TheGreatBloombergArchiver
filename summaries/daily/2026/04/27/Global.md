The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively impassable, with Polymarket odds placing the probability of a full reopening by end-April at zero and end-of-May odds also unfavorable — implying the world's most critical oil artery could remain closed for a third consecutive month. Iran's Foreign Minister Araghchi traveled to Moscow on Monday to coordinate with President Putin after the weekend's second round of US-Iran peace talks failed to materialize despite Pakistani mediation efforts. Tehran's latest proposal — conveyed through Islamabad — calls for reopening the strait in exchange for a US lifting of its port blockade, with nuclear negotiations separated into a distinct later track. Washington's response remains to be seen.

Oil markets reflect the unresolved standoff. Brent crude traded near $108 per barrel Monday, up roughly 2.5%, while WTI rose above $96. Goldman Sachs raised its fourth-quarter Brent forecast to $90 per barrel — up nearly $30 from pre-conflict projections — though spot prices remain elevated well above that level. December crude futures have settled near or above $80, suggesting markets are pricing a protracted disruption. The aviation sector is among the most acutely exposed: Delta Air Lines' fuel costs have doubled in a month, adding an estimated $2.5 billion to quarterly expenses. Lufthansa's 20,000-flight reduction is an early industry response; broader route cancellations appear likely if conditions persist into the peak summer travel season.

The US escalated its campaign to isolate Iranian oil revenues by sanctioning Hengli Petrochemical's Dalian refinery, the first time Washington has targeted a major Chinese private refiner. The move is designed to raise the cost to Beijing of continued Iranian crude purchases, though China's likely response involves calibrated ambiguity rather than compliance. Separately, the US and EU announced an agreement to coordinate on critical minerals supply chains and reduce dependence on Chinese rare earths — a strategically significant step even if implementation details remain to be defined. The first international conference on transitioning away from fossil fuels convened in Santa Marta, Colombia, drawing more than 50 countries; the Iran war's disruption to energy markets has lent the gathering added urgency, though translating ambition into binding commitments remains the central challenge.
