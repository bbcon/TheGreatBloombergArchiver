Weekly Brief

Feb 16 – Feb 22, 2026

US

  • Supreme Court strikes down "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs in a 6-3 ruling Friday, potentially cutting the effective US tariff rate by more than half.
  • FOMC minutes revealed surprising hawkishness, with several members flagging the possibility of rate increases if inflation remains elevated — markets had priced a more neutral posture.
  • AI-driven capital rotation intensified: Palo Alto Networks fell 7.5% on weak guidance while Nvidia rose on a Meta deployment deal; software names broadly underperformed hardware infrastructure.
  • Treasury markets posted their best week in months, yet a growing minority of managers question the consensus case for two or more Fed cuts, with "no landing" now seen as the modal scenario by some survey respondents.
  • January industrial production rose 0.7%, the largest monthly gain in nearly a year, though the data predate the full effect of subsequent tariff escalation.

Monetary Policy and Inflation

The week's most significant US macro development arrived Wednesday when FOMC minutes signaled that several members last month considered the possibility of rate increases rather than cuts, should inflation prove sticky. The hawkish tilt stood in contrast to market expectations of a more neutral posture and prompted a paring of easing bets. The dollar posted its best week in four months as a result. The underlying inflation picture nonetheless remained constructive heading into Friday's PCE release — the trimmed mean and median CPI sit at their lowest levels since 2021, Atlanta Fed sticky-price CPI fell to 3%, and Bloomberg Economics' city-level analysis showed the January national print averaged 2.16% across the twelve BLS-tracked metros, with the West South Central region below 1% and Dallas recording its first deflationary reading since the pandemic. One caveat merited attention: new car prices posted their largest January jump in years, masked by falling used-car prices, suggesting early tariff pass-through to consumers may only now be materializing. Friday's PCE — delayed since last November's government shutdown — carried unusual weight given the index had been tracking above core CPI near 3%.

Tariff Ruling and Trade Architecture

The Supreme Court's Friday ruling invalidating the emergency-powers statutory basis for reciprocal tariffs was the most consequential domestic policy development of the Trump presidency to date. The 6-3 decision struck the "Liberation Day" duties imposed on goods from dozens of countries, with Bloomberg Economics estimating potential importer refunds of up to $170 billion — a fight now remanded to lower courts. The White House indicated alternative statutory authorities remain available, and markets were not caught entirely off-guard given legal challenges had been widely anticipated. The ruling nonetheless materially alters the near-term trade architecture. The US-Indonesia bilateral deal — in which Jakarta committed to facilitate roughly $33 billion in US goods purchases in exchange for lower tariffs — was finalized this week, suggesting the administration will pursue country-by-country arrangements to reconstruct leverage.

Equity Markets and AI Capital Cycle

The AI capital expenditure cycle produced its starkest internal contradiction this week. A BofA global fund manager survey showed for the first time in a generation that a majority of money managers fear corporate overinvestment rather than underinvestment — a direct consequence of the $600 billion-plus in hyperscaler capex projected for 2026, which is visibly transforming cash-rich tech balance sheets into more leveraged ones. Single-name CDS on Meta and Alphabet became actively traded contracts, a structural shift worth monitoring. Goldman's software pair-trade basket — long infrastructure names including Cloudflare, CrowdStrike, and Oracle against shorts in Salesforce, Monday.com, and DocuSign — encapsulates the bifurcation. Apple's 40-day correlation with the Nasdaq 100 fell to roughly 0.2, its lowest since 2006, effectively functioning as a tech-sector haven. DRAM prices rose 75% from December to January, contributing to Cisco's worst share performance in nearly four years after weak guidance.

Fiscal Dynamics

New CBO projections showed net interest payments rising from $1 trillion this year to $2.1 trillion by 2036, reaching 4.6% of GDP — a ratio that alone exceeds Treasury Secretary Bessent's 3% total deficit target for 2029. The figures did not trigger an immediate bond market response, with the CBO projecting the 10-year yield moving from 4.1% to only 4.4% over the decade, though analysts noted the sheer scale of outstanding debt may constrain future administrations' fiscal options. Separately, Kevin Hassett's public criticism of a New York Fed study on tariff incidence — calling it "an embarrassment" and suggesting its authors should be "disciplined" — drew scrutiny as an unusual attack on independent economic research. Private equity also featured: with roughly $7 trillion in AUM, holding periods at a 20-year high of 4.4 years, and LP pressure mounting over distribution shortfalls, a meaningful clearing event appears unlikely to arrive quickly despite a modest pickup in IPO and strategic deal activity. ---

Asia

  • Former South Korean President Yoon Suk Yeol was sentenced Wednesday to life in prison for insurrection following his late-2024 martial law declaration; prosecutors had sought the death penalty.
  • Japan's Prime Minister Takaichi formally took office following a re-election vote and announced a $36 billion investment commitment in US oil, gas, and critical minerals — the first concrete tranche of the $550 billion bilateral framework with the Trump administration.
  • Indonesia's institutional credibility deteriorated sharply: MSCI placed its emerging-market classification under review, FTSE Russell followed, Moody's cut the credit outlook to negative, and the rupiah weakened to levels last seen nearly three decades ago.
  • India's AI Impact Summit in New Delhi concluded with AI infrastructure pledges rising to at least $270 billion by week's end, up from $140 billion at the outset, anchored by commitments from Reliance, Adani, and international partners.
  • DRAM prices rose 75% from December to January, with Samsung and SK Hynix among the principal beneficiaries as AI accelerator demand crowds out conventional buyers.

China: Policy Signals and Market Bifurcation

Xi Jinping's call early in the week for domestic demand to serve as China's "main driver" of economic growth represents a rhetorical reinforcement of a long-stated rebalancing objective — though concrete fiscal follow-through remains the operative test, as the IMF's Article IV assessment noted that household consumption has hovered near 40% of GDP essentially unchanged since 2005. Chinese AI capital formation showed no sign of slowing: Moonshot targeted a $10 billion valuation in a new funding round, more than doubling the $4.3 billion mark set weeks earlier in a round backed by Alibaba and Tencent. As Hong Kong markets reopened after the Lunar New Year break on Thursday, generative AI startups Zhipu and MiniMax Group surged as investors rotated into pure AI plays; robotics firms attracted buying interest following their Spring Festival gala showcase. The broader Chinese market nonetheless drifted lower as earnings season approached with subdued expectations, and the Pentagon's brief — and quickly reversed — decision to add Alibaba, BYD, and Baidu to its list of companies deemed to be aiding the Chinese military served as a reminder of how abrupt and unpredictable US-China tech friction can be, with Alibaba falling more than 3% in Hong Kong on the news before the designation was withdrawn.

Japan: Fiscal Ambition and BOJ Watch

Japan's economic news flow carried two competing signals. Fourth-quarter real GDP expanded just 0.2% annualized, far below the 1.6% consensus, with consumer spending rising only 0.1% — data that reinforces the case for Prime Minister Takaichi's expansionary fiscal posture and complicates the BOJ's path toward further normalization. Against that, manufacturing PMI remained in expansionary territory for a third consecutive month, and Takaichi's $36 billion US investment commitment — anchored by a large natural gas facility in Ohio — serves multiple strategic functions: visible progress on a trade framework, energy security diversification, and a potential buffer against tariff escalation. BOJ board nominations for the seats vacated by dovish member Asahi Noguchi and consensus voter Junko Nakagawa are expected as soon as February 25, and given Takaichi's historical association with reflation, the appointments will be parsed closely by JGB and yen markets. Mizuho Financial fell roughly 5% Monday and 3.8% Tuesday following disclosure of a regulatory investigation into suspected insider trading at its brokerage unit.

South Korea, Southeast Asia, and India

South Korea's Yoon verdict formally closes the most severe constitutional crisis the country has faced in decades, though an appeal is expected and political recalibration continues. In Indonesia, the compounding nature of governance erosion became legible in market terms this week: the replacement of technocrats with loyalists, a family member appointed as Bank Indonesia deputy governor, and a flagship school lunch program costing an estimated $28 billion annually combine to produce a currency under severe pressure, credit outlook deterioration, and index review — all simultaneously. Bank Indonesia held rates at 4.75% as expected but faces a familiar central bank dilemma: rate cuts are warranted by inflation but precluded by currency weakness. India presented a sharply contrasting picture. The AI Impact Summit demonstrated genuine convening power, HSBC's flash survey showed activity accelerating in February, the country formally joined a US-led critical minerals initiative, and Prime Minister Modi used the occasion to press an AI sovereignty agenda resting on domestic language models, shared compute, and semiconductor ambitions. Malaysia's exports rose at their fastest pace in over three years in January. Australia's BHP delivered earnings at the upper end of expectations, with copper contributing more than half of group profit for the first time; the Reserve Bank's February minutes confirmed its rate hike reflected a downgraded view of potential output, making it the first major DM central bank to tighten in 2026. ---

Europe

  • European equities set fresh records mid-week, with the Stoxx 600 reaching 627-628; strategists caution that eight consecutive months of gains and five-year-high valuations leave limited room for further multiple expansion.
  • Christine Lagarde is reportedly considering an early ECB departure before her October 2027 mandate expires, accelerating a succession debate with significant policy and political dimensions.
  • UK unemployment rose to its highest level since the pandemic while wage growth eased, prompting traders to fully price half a point of Bank of England cuts by year-end.
  • EU carbon allowances fell to their lowest since July as Slovakia, Germany, and France openly clashed over ETS reform at the Brussels summit; the Commission pledged a reform proposal for Q3.
  • Airbus fell 6.7% Thursday after disclosing Pratt & Whitney engine delivery shortfalls that will force reduced aircraft output.

ECB Succession and Monetary Policy

The week's most consequential European institutional development was the acceleration of ECB succession planning. Reports emerged that Christine Lagarde may step down before her mandate's formal October 2027 conclusion, calibrated around the French presidential election in April 2027 — a timing designed to preserve outgoing President Macron's ability to influence the appointment before a potential hard-right outcome. An ECB spokesperson confirmed only that Lagarde "has not taken any decision regarding the end of her term." Hawkish board member Isabel Schnabel stated she intends to serve her full term; Spain is actively campaigning for its own candidate; Dutch governor Klaas Knot appears the most plausible consensus choice. The succession process threatens to expose familiar fault lines between hawks and doves, and between national and supranational interests, at a delicate moment. The euro area flash PMI for February surprised to the upside, with manufacturers recording their best performance since 2022 driven by an unexpected improvement in Germany, and Q4 negotiated wages rose 3% — a data point the ECB will monitor carefully as it balances disinflation progress against residual services-sector price pressures.

UK: Labour Market and Fiscal Crosscurrents

The UK data week delivered a clear dovish impulse. Unemployment rose to its highest level since the pandemic, youth unemployment climbed above the EU average for the first time, and wage growth eased further — together prompting traders to fully price 50 basis points of Bank of England cuts by year-end, with some analysts citing March as an increasingly plausible timing for the first move. Against that, the UK recorded its largest budget surplus on record during the period, boosted by surging capital gains tax receipts and lower debt servicing costs, offering a brief fiscal respite for a government navigating difficult spending decisions. Prime Minister Starmer reversed plans to cancel some May local elections following internal backlash — the latest in a series of U-turns — while also outlining regulation requiring AI chatbots to comply with child online safety laws. The arrest of Prince Andrew on suspicion of misconduct in public office added institutional pressure at an already sensitive moment for the monarchy.

Corporate Developments

The week produced a notable dispersion of corporate outcomes. Airbus's disclosure that Pratt & Whitney has failed to deliver engines in sufficient volume to meet production targets is a material setback, shifting accountability toward the supply chain and complicating an already complex order backlog management challenge. Bayer agreed to pay more than $7 billion to resolve Roundup cancer litigation, potentially closing a years-long legal overhang. Nestlé gained 4.5% in Zurich after new CEO Philipp Navratil signaled a turnaround on track, guiding for 3-4% organic revenue growth in 2026. Moncler rallied 13% on results described as significantly ahead of estimates, lifting LVMH and Hermès. Hapag-Lloyd agreed to acquire ZIM Integrated Shipping at roughly $4.2 billion, sending ZIM shares up 35%. Norsk Hydro fell 4.4% Monday on Goldman and RBC downgrades, Pinewood Technologies tumbled 32% after Apax abandoned its bid citing market conditions, and Novo Nordisk's approximately 5,000 job cuts — among the largest layoffs in Danish history — began to weigh on Danish consumer sentiment. In Denmark, Prime Minister Frederiksen is reportedly weighing a snap election to capitalize on recovered standing following the Greenland dispute.

Carbon Markets and Geopolitical Repositioning

The EU carbon market faces its sternmost political test in years. Allowances fell to their lowest since July as leaders openly clashed at the Brussels summit, with Slovakia's Fico calling for suspension, Germany's Merz initially floating revision before softening, and Macron defending the program. JPMorgan analysts flagged that the resulting uncertainty is already impairing long-term green capex planning regardless of where reforms ultimately land. The broader European geopolitical repositioning continued in parallel: eurozone finance ministers pushed for a larger global role for the euro, the BofA fund manager survey showed the largest-ever overweight on the euro versus the dollar, and Sweden tentatively reopened the euro adoption debate — a shift framed through geopolitical rather than monetary lenses, reflecting the recalibration of the transatlantic relationship following US Secretary of State Rubio's weekend comment that the US-Europe alliance "has to change." ---

Global

  • Brent crude surged 4.3% on Wednesday to re-cross $70 per barrel as US-Iran tensions escalated, with prediction markets assigning roughly 70% probability to a US military strike by week's end.
  • The IEA Paris ministerial summit ended without a joint communiqué as the US threatened to exit unless the agency abandons its net zero framing — a significant fracture given Washington co-founded the institution in the 1970s.
  • The Supreme Court's tariff ruling removed — at least temporarily — the single largest source of global trade policy uncertainty, though the White House signaled it will seek alternative statutory authorities.
  • India's AI Impact Summit saw infrastructure pledges rise from $140 billion at the outset to $270 billion by conclusion, with AI sovereignty emerging as the defining strategic theme for non-US, non-Chinese economies.
  • OpenAI is close to finalizing a funding round exceeding $100 billion, valuing the company above $850 billion — among the largest private financings on record.

US-Iran: Two-Track Strategy and Oil Markets

The US-Iran dynamic dominated global market attention across the week. Indirect nuclear talks opened Monday in Geneva, with US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi holding what both sides described as constructive initial sessions, with Iran expected to return detailed proposals within two weeks. The diplomatic opening coincided with Iranian naval exercises that briefly closed portions of the Strait of Hormuz Monday — a calibrated reminder of Tehran's leverage. The tone shifted materially by Thursday: President Trump stated Iran has ten to fifteen days to reach an agreement and that the US is "ready, willing, and able to rapidly fulfill its mission, with speed and violence, if necessary." Iran responded it would "defend itself and respond like never before." The Wall Street Journal reported the administration is evaluating a limited early strike to pressure rather than preempt. Complicating military planning, the UK reportedly withheld access to Diego Garcia — a critical Indian Ocean base — for potential operations. Vice President Vance acknowledged talks had gone "well in some ways" while noting Iranian negotiators had not accepted core US red lines. WTI, which had opened the week near $63, moved toward $66 by Friday. The trajectory of negotiations over the coming two weeks is the single most consequential near-term variable for global energy markets and risk assets.

Energy Markets and Tanker Dynamics

Beyond the geopolitical risk premium, energy markets reflected structural repositioning. South Korea's Sinokor group has accumulated roughly 120 VLCCs over the past two months, helping push benchmark VLCC earnings above $120,000 per day — a more than fourfold increase over the month. Port of Los Angeles export volumes fell 8% year-on-year in January, with soybean shipments to China down 80% as Argentine and Brazilian suppliers locked in multi-month contracts that effectively displace US exporters for at least another agricultural cycle. Japan's $36 billion US energy investment commitment — anchored in the bilateral trade framework — represents a tangible win for the administration's bilateral deal architecture. The IEA fracture, meanwhile, appears genuine: the Paris ministerial ended without the customary joint communiqué, with only a Netherlands-authored summary published after US Energy Secretary Chris Wright assailed the agency's net zero framing. Members found limited common ground on critical minerals and energy security, but the institutional fault lines will likely persist.

AI Sovereignty as Strategic Competition

The week's other defining global theme was the emergence of AI sovereignty as an explicit policy objective across non-US, non-Chinese economies. India's AI Impact Summit — the first such gathering held in the Global South — crystallized a common structural concern: the most consequential AI systems are controlled by a small number of US and Chinese entities, and access to them can in principle be interrupted. The 2024 UniSuper incident, in which a Google cloud misconfiguration cut off Australian pension fund members, served as a reference point for technology dependency risk. India's approach combines domestic small language models, shared compute facilities, semiconductor design ambitions, and active capital attraction — a state-directed variant of the sovereign resilience logic articulated by others. OpenAI's near-finalized $100 billion-plus funding round, anchored by Amazon, SoftBank, Nvidia, and Microsoft, with sovereign wealth funds to follow, underscores that AI infrastructure deployment has become a geopolitical as well as commercial competition. Whether nations outside the two dominant blocs can meaningfully insulate themselves from dependency remains an open question that no summit this week resolved.

Climate Policy Divergence

The gap between US federal climate policy and the trajectory of the rest of the world widened measurably. The EPA moved to roll back the Biden administration's Mercury and Air Toxics Standards for coal-fired power plants, the administration advanced a proposed 9.2-gigawatt gas plant in Ohio with an estimated annual emissions footprint of roughly 19.4 million metric tons of CO₂, and EPA's own cost-benefit analysis of its endangerment finding rollback suggested costs could reach $1.4 trillion under higher-end scenarios — complicating the administration's $1.3 trillion savings claim. Meanwhile, former US officials were in Mumbai urging developing nations to accelerate green transitions without waiting for Washington. The tension within India itself — coal accounting for nearly three-quarters of power generation alongside a 2070 net zero target and accelerating renewables investment — reflects a broader pattern in which climate ambition and energy security imperatives coexist uneasily. Ethiopia's rapid EV adoption, driven by a fossil fuel import ban and tariff cuts rather than climate conviction, illustrates how fiscal necessity may prove a more durable driver of energy transition in fuel-importing emerging economies than any summit declaration.
