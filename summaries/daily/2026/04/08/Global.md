The dominant development of April 8 is the agreement between the US and Iran on a two-week ceasefire, brokered with Pakistan serving as an intermediary. Under the terms as understood, the American-Israeli military campaign is to halt in exchange for Iran reopening the Strait of Hormuz to commercial shipping. The oil market's response was immediate: Brent crude fell as much as 16% to approximately $91.70 a barrel, and WTI tumbled nearly 19% — among the largest single-day declines in crude oil history, comparable only to the Covid demand collapse in 2020 and the allied entry into Kuwait in January 1991. The historical parallel to the Kuwait crisis is instructive: oil more than doubled during that conflict before returning to pre-crisis levels within twelve months. The current shock has traced a broadly similar arc, though it appears to be resolving more rapidly.

Important caveats remain. Traffic through the Strait of Hormuz showed little immediate change on Wednesday, with more than 800 vessels still trapped in the Persian Gulf. Shipowners, insurers, and industry bodies including Bimco are awaiting technical clarity on transit procedures before committing vessels to re-enter. Iran's intent to levy fees on passing ships — a form of de facto toll on the waterway — introduces a novel geopolitical dynamic that Bloomberg Opinion's Javier Blas characterizes as transforming what was a free international waterway into a controlled one. Iran and the US are each characterizing the ceasefire as a victory, while analysts describe the arrangement as a fragile starting framework. Several Arab Gulf states reported continued Iranian missile attacks in the immediate aftermath of Trump's announcement, and both Kuwait and the UAE subsequently reported fresh strikes.

Pakistan's emergence as the key mediating channel — leveraging ties with Saudi Arabia, Iran, the US, and China — reflects a meaningful realignment of diplomatic influence in the region. The broader geopolitical fallout for US alliances appears significant: not a single NATO member or Gulf ally directly joined the US-Israeli campaign, and French President Macron publicly called on medium powers to assert greater independence from Washington. The IMF is poised to cut its global growth forecasts as a result of the conflict, while the World Bank cautioned governments against worsening fiscal positions through poorly targeted stimulus, urging that any measures be transparent, temporary, and targeted.
