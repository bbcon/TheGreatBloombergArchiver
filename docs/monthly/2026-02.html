Monthly Brief

February 2026

US

  • The Supreme Court struck down "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs in a 6-3 Friday ruling, potentially halving the effective US tariff rate and marking the most consequential domestic policy development of the Trump presidency to date.
  • Kevin Warsh's nomination as Fed Chair crystallized a durable tension: an incoming chair with a hawkish institutional record nominated by an administration that explicitly demands lower rates, with FOMC minutes later in the month revealing several members had considered rate increases.
  • AI capital expenditure concerns dominated equity markets, with the S&P 500 ranking 69th among 92 global benchmarks and trailing world ex-US equities by approximately 7.7 percentage points year-to-date by late February.
  • January payrolls of 130,000 — the strongest print in over a year — pushed the expected first Fed cut from June to July, while trimmed-mean and median CPI fell to their lowest since 2021, creating a genuinely mixed inflation picture.
  • New CBO projections showed net interest payments rising from $1 trillion currently to $2.1 trillion by 2036, reaching 4.6% of GDP — a figure that alone exceeds Treasury Secretary Bessent's stated 3% total deficit target for 2029.

Federal Reserve Transition

February's defining US macro theme was the transition at the Federal Reserve. President Trump's nomination of Kevin Warsh to succeed Jerome Powell in May generated immediate interpretive tension: Warsh's institutional record is characterized by an orthodox, inflation-conscious disposition that Wall Street broadly reads as hawkish, yet Trump indicated Monday of the final week that he would not have nominated Warsh had he signaled a preference for rate increases. The resolution of that contradiction remains genuinely open. Warsh would inherit a committee whose median view he cannot unilaterally redirect, and the confirmation path — while navigable through the Republican Senate — faces at least one hold pending resolution of a criminal investigation involving Chair Powell. Mid-month FOMC minutes amplified the uncertainty: several members flagged the possibility of rate increases rather than cuts if inflation remains elevated, a posture markets had not fully priced. The dollar posted its best week in four months following the minutes' release. Governor Lisa Cook reiterated the importance of central bank credibility, while Judy Shelton resurfaced in policy circles in conversations with Treasury Secretary Bessent about redeploying $2.9 trillion in bank reserves parked at the Fed. The MOVE index continued declining from its April Liberation Day peak, suggesting bond markets are treating the institutional noise as manageable — an equanimity that may face its first real test once Warsh arrives in May and faces a live inflation decision.

Equity Markets and the AI Rotation

Beneath headline index stability, February produced a significant factor rotation. The equal-weighted S&P 500 and small-cap indices hit all-time highs while the Magnificent Seven remained below recent peaks; the Russell 1000 Value extended a meaningful lead over Growth; and the Dow Jones Transportation Average outpaced the S&P 500 by 13 percentage points over six weeks. The catalyst appears to have been a sequence of AI capability releases from Anthropic, Google, and others that investors interpreted not as demand drivers but as displacement threats to incumbent software, insurance-broker, logistics, and financial-data business models. The S&P 1500 Software index fell more than 17% year-to-date by mid-month, with Thomson Reuters falling as much as 20% on concerns that Anthropic's legal-workflow tool threatens its core franchise, AMD extending losses to 17% post-earnings, and Alphabet shares falling despite a revenue beat after guiding 2026 capex to as much as $185 billion versus consensus near $120 billion. Amazon fell approximately 8% in pre-market trading after guiding to $200 billion in AI-related capital expenditure for 2026. A BofA global fund manager survey showed for the first time in a generation that a majority of money managers fear corporate overinvestment rather than underinvestment — a direct consequence of the $600 billion-plus in hyperscaler capex projected for the year. Single-name CDS on Meta and Alphabet became actively traded contracts. Apple's 40-day correlation with the Nasdaq 100 fell to roughly 0.2, its lowest since 2006, effectively functioning as a tech-sector haven. Q4 blended earnings growth reached roughly 14.5%, a four-year high, providing a fundamental floor that limited downside without arresting the rotation.

Trade Architecture and the Tariff Ruling

The Supreme Court's 6-3 Friday ruling invalidating the emergency-powers statutory basis for reciprocal tariffs altered the near-term trade architecture more sharply than any executive action this year. Bloomberg Economics estimated potential importer refunds of up to $170 billion, remanded to lower courts. The White House signaled alternative statutory authorities remain available, and the administration moved concurrently to construct bilateral arrangements: a US-Indonesia deal committed Jakarta to facilitate roughly $33 billion in US goods purchases in exchange for lower tariffs, and a formal trade pact with Taiwan featured $44 billion in LNG and crude purchases and $15 billion in civil aircraft orders. The Republican House's vote to terminate Canadian tariffs — passed with six GOP crossovers — exposed meaningful fissures within the party heading into midterm primaries, with swing-district members increasingly sensitive to affordability pressures. French customs data provided concrete evidence of the tariff regime's bilateral costs: Q4 2025 spirits exports to the US fell 47%, wine 39%, and cosmetics 25%. Trump's economic approval ratings softened as Democrats outperformed in recent special elections.

Macro Backdrop and Structural Constraints

The macro underpinning remained firmer in aggregate than the equity tape implied. Q3 GDP was revised to 4.4% annualized — the strongest in two years — and Bank of America flagged potential for front-loaded 2026 growth, citing roughly $135–$140 billion in fiscal stimulus and a normalization boost from the partial government shutdown's resolution. January industrial production rose 0.7%, the largest monthly gain in nearly a year, though the data predate the full effect of tariff escalation. Against these constructive aggregates, distributional stress is mounting: consumer confidence registered its weakest reading in more than a decade, the bottom income quintile now spends 55% of income on rent, and combined down payment plus first-year mortgage costs in major metros have risen to 160–200% of income from 90–120% in 2000. Goldman Sachs economists projected data centers will account for 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation near 6% through 2027 — a figure that would complicate the Fed's disinflation narrative and sharpen the political stakes around November's midterms. The EPA's rescission of the 2009 endangerment finding sets up litigation likely to reach the Supreme Court and constrains future climate rulemaking regardless of political control. Molina Healthcare's guidance — below half of consensus — triggered a sharp managed-care de-rating. Stellantis's $26 billion in EV-related charges carried direct implications for North American manufacturing given its Jeep, Ram, and Chrysler exposure. Private equity, with roughly $7 trillion in AUM and holding periods at a 20-year high of 4.4 years, faces LP pressure over distribution shortfalls that a modest pickup in IPO activity has not meaningfully relieved. ---

Asia

  • The MSCI Asia Pacific Index rose to a record, posting its strongest start relative to the S&P 500 this century — up roughly 13% year-to-date against 1.4% for US equities by late February, with January EM portfolio inflows approaching $100 billion, the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades.
  • Japan's Prime Minister Takaichi secured a two-thirds lower-house supermajority — the LDP's largest postwar single-party mandate — sending the Nikkei 225 up nearly 4% to a record high on the following Monday and reshaping Japan's fiscal-monetary configuration.
  • China's 2026 growth target appears set to be cut to 4.5%–5% at the March NPC, the first downward revision in three years, while regulators moved to end the auto-sector price war and the January factory-gate deflation reading moderated.
  • Indonesia's institutional credibility deteriorated sharply through February: MSCI placed its emerging-market classification under review, FTSE Russell followed, Moody's cut the credit outlook to negative, and the rupiah weakened to levels last seen nearly three decades ago.
  • India's AI Impact Summit concluded with infrastructure pledges rising from $140 billion to at least $270 billion over the course of the week, establishing AI sovereignty as the country's defining strategic economic theme.

Japan: Mandate, Fiscal Ambition, and Monetary Tension

Prime Minister Takaichi's snap-election landslide — delivering a result that prediction markets had assigned roughly even odds just a fortnight prior — was the month's single most consequential political event in the region. The Nikkei 225 surged nearly 4% to a record on the Monday following the election result and added 2.8% on Tuesday, while JGB yields, which had spiked sharply in January, edged lower and the yen steadied; by mid-week of that period, the yen had strengthened to ¥153 from near ¥160 in January, and 30-year JGB yields had fallen roughly 40 basis points from their January highs. Finance Minister Katayama's assurance that a proposed two-year food consumption tax cut would not require additional bond issuance provided an important initial anchor. The structural tension, however, remains unresolved. Planned fiscal expansion sits against a debt-to-GDP ratio of approximately 230%, and Takaichi's well-documented skepticism toward BOJ rate hikes creates an awkward configuration at a time when domestic inflation has not fully receded. Evercore ISI characterized the downside as a "fat right tail" — a Japanese analogue to the UK's 2022 Truss episode — should political authority erode fiscal restraint. Fourth-quarter real GDP expanded just 0.2% annualized, far below the 1.6% consensus, with consumer spending rising only 0.1%; the weak data reinforces the case for fiscal support while simultaneously complicating the BOJ's normalization path. BOJ board nominations for the seats vacated by dovish member Asahi Noguchi and consensus voter Junko Nakagawa are expected as soon as February 25, and given Takaichi's historical association with reflation, the appointments will be parsed closely by JGB and yen markets. Takaichi's $36 billion US investment commitment — anchored by a large natural gas facility in Ohio — serves multiple strategic functions: visible progress on the $550 billion bilateral trade framework with Washington, energy security diversification, and a potential buffer against tariff escalation. The China relationship adds a further complication: Takaichi's comments on a potential Taiwan military response have cooled Beijing's posture, with measurable consequences in tourism flows as bookings to Sapporo were projected to fall 62% in January-February. On the corporate side, Mizuho Financial fell roughly 5% on Monday and 3.8% on Tuesday following disclosure of a regulatory investigation into suspected insider trading at its brokerage unit. The Topix rally more broadly appeared underpinned by genuine governance reform — rising dividends, accelerating buybacks, and a re-rating of long-discounted sectors including non-ferrous metals, with Sumitomo Electric having tripled since the US election.

China: Policy Signals, Market Bifurcation, and Geopolitical Friction

China's macro picture through February was defined by a tension between modest activity improvement and accumulating structural headwinds. January services PMI picked up, and January factory-gate deflation moderated more than expected — though Morgan Stanley's Robin Xing cautioned there is "no decisive reflation yet," with improvement leaning heavily on the global metals rally. More than a dozen provinces lowered their 2026 growth targets through early February, and consensus coalesced around a national target of 4.5%–5% for the March NPC — the first downward revision in three years. Citi revised its stimulus baseline downward, treating its previously expected package — roughly one trillion yuan in fiscal support, a 20-basis-point rate cut, and a 50-basis-point RRR reduction — as a ceiling rather than a floor. The PBOC injected $86.4 billion via 14-day repos ahead of the Lunar New Year holiday, with Industrial Securities forecasting over $500 billion in total liquidity provision for the holiday period. Foreign positioning nonetheless improved materially: Goldman Sachs and BNP Paribas surveys pointed to accelerating allocations, Qube's China long-only fund grew more than tenfold year-on-year to $2 billion, and Eight Roads reversed plans to sell approximately $400 million in Chinese tech stakes — a reversal reflecting improved valuations and a lower temperature in US-China relations following a constructive phone call between Presidents Trump and Xi, though substantive trade details remained opaque. Xi's call for domestic demand to serve as China's "main driver" of economic growth represents rhetorical reinforcement of a long-stated objective; the IMF's Article IV assessment noted household consumption has hovered near 40% of GDP essentially unchanged since 2005, underscoring the implementation gap. On geopolitics, Beijing's response to a Panamanian court decision voiding a CK Hutchison port contract escalated materially mid-month: Chinese authorities instructed state-linked firms to halt new project discussions in Panama and intensified customs inspections on Panamanian goods, illustrating how port and logistics infrastructure has become a primary arena for US-China strategic competition. The Pentagon's brief — and quickly reversed — decision to add Alibaba, BYD, and Baidu to its list of companies deemed to be aiding the Chinese military, which sent Alibaba more than 3% lower in Hong Kong before the designation was withdrawn, served as a reminder of how abrupt and unpredictable US-China tech friction can be. Moonshot targeted a $10 billion valuation in a new funding round, more than doubling the $4.3 billion mark set weeks earlier; robotics firms attracted buying interest following their Spring Festival gala showcase. The abrupt closure of all Shanghai outlets of the premium restaurant chain Xiaonanguo days before Lunar New Year underscored ongoing consumption fragility.

South Korea, Indonesia, India, and the Broader Region

South Korea's KOSPI gained approximately 30% year-to-date by late February, supported by AI hardware exposure, shareholder-return reforms, and relatively attractive valuations. Former President Yoon Suk Yeol was sentenced to life in prison for insurrection following his late-2024 martial law declaration — formally closing the most severe constitutional crisis the country has faced in decades, though an appeal is expected. Indonesia presented a sharply contrasting picture: the replacement of technocrats with loyalists, a family member appointed as Bank Indonesia deputy governor, and a flagship school lunch program costing an estimated $28 billion annually combined to produce a currency under severe pressure, with the rupiah weakening to levels last seen nearly three decades ago. MSCI placed Indonesia's emerging-market classification under review, FTSE Russell followed, and Moody's cut the credit outlook to negative — compounding deterioration that Jakarta's finance ministry pushed back against in unusually pointed terms. Bank Indonesia held rates at 4.75% as expected but faces a familiar central bank dilemma: rate cuts are warranted by domestic inflation dynamics but precluded by currency weakness. India presented the most constructive regional picture outside Japan: the AI Impact Summit demonstrated genuine convening power with pledges rising from $140 billion to $270 billion over the course of the week, the country formally joined a US-led critical minerals initiative, HSBC's flash survey showed activity accelerating in February, and GQG Partners continued adding to a $24 billion India position. Malaysia's exports rose at their fastest pace in over three years in January. Australia's BHP delivered earnings at the upper end of expectations, with copper contributing more than half of group profit for the first time; the RBA's February minutes confirmed its rate hike reflected a downgraded view of potential output, making it the first major developed-market central bank to tighten in 2026, with markets pricing a further increase amid services inflation persistence. DRAM prices rose 75% from December to January, with Samsung and SK Hynix among the principal beneficiaries as AI accelerator demand crowds out conventional buyers. Indonesia ordered Weda Bay Nickel — the world's largest nickel mine — to cut permitted output to 12 million tons from 42 million, an aggressive supply-side intervention with immediate implications for stainless steel and battery material markets. ---

Europe

  • European equities set fresh records mid-week in the final week of February, with the Stoxx 600 reaching 627-628 — though strategists cautioned that eight consecutive months of gains and five-year-high valuations leave limited room for further multiple expansion.
  • Prime Minister Keir Starmer faced the most acute political crisis of his premiership through February, with his chief of staff and communications director resigning, Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar publicly calling for him to stand down, and prediction markets pricing his departure by end-June.
  • Christine Lagarde is reportedly considering an early ECB departure before her October 2027 mandate expires, accelerating a succession debate with significant policy and political dimensions at a delicate moment for eurozone monetary policy.
  • Stellantis announced €22 billion in EV-related charges — joining Ford, GM, and Porsche in a broad European auto industry retreat from electrification timelines — while Novo Nordisk fell as much as 20% in a single session on weak sales guidance.
  • The Bank of England held in a notably close 5-4 vote, and Governor Bailey subsequently endorsed market pricing of roughly 50% probability of a March cut; UK unemployment rose to its highest since the pandemic by month's end.

ECB Policy and Succession

February's most consequential European institutional development arrived late in the month when reports emerged that ECB President Christine Lagarde may step down before her mandate's formal October 2027 conclusion — calibrated, according to reporting, around the French presidential election in April 2027 and designed to preserve outgoing President Macron's ability to influence the appointment before a potential hard-right outcome. An ECB spokesperson confirmed only that Lagarde "has not taken any decision regarding the end of her term." Hawkish board member Isabel Schnabel stated she intends to serve her full term; Spain is actively campaigning for its own candidate; Dutch governor Klaas Knot appears the most plausible consensus choice. The succession process threatens to expose familiar fault lines between hawks and doves and between national and supranational interests. The policy backdrop is itself unsettled: euro-area HICP sank further below the 2% target through the month, sharpening the Governing Council's debate about how its reaction function handles sustained undershoots versus the overshoots of 2022–23. The February flash PMI surprised to the upside, with manufacturers recording their best performance since 2022 driven by an unexpected improvement in Germany. Q4 negotiated wages rose 3%, a figure the ECB will monitor carefully as it balances disinflation progress against residual services-sector price pressures. ECB Executive Board member Schnabel called for accelerated structural reform throughout the month, underscoring official concern that monetary policy alone cannot close the growth gap with the US. The BofA fund manager survey showed the largest-ever overweight on the euro versus the dollar, reflecting a broad institutional reappraisal of US asset concentration risk.

UK: Political Risk and Monetary Easing

Prime Minister Starmer's political position deteriorated materially through the month. The fallout centered on Peter Mandelson's appointment as US ambassador — complicated by disclosure that Mandelson had shared market-sensitive policy decisions with Jeffrey Epstein while serving as deputy prime minister — prompting the resignation of Starmer's chief of staff and communications director within 48 hours of the most intense coverage. Scottish Labour leader Anas Sarwar's public call for Starmer to stand down represented the most significant internal rupture to date; parliament voted to compel disclosure of related documents. Starmer secured a temporary reprieve by winning public backing from his full cabinet, though allies characterized the relief as tactical rather than strategic. Prediction markets by mid-week of the final week priced departure by end-June. Gilt yields reflected a renewed political risk premium, and hedge funds extended short positions in sterling against the euro, citing the combination of political turmoil and Bank of England rate-cut expectations. The succession question may prove the more durable concern for fixed income: the most plausible candidates sit to Starmer's left and are perceived as more fiscally expansive, adding a medium-term risk premium that gilt markets remain especially sensitive to following the 2022 Truss episode. On the monetary side, the BOE held in a notably close 5-4 vote earlier in the month, and Governor Bailey's subsequent endorsement of roughly 50% probability of a March cut shifted the interpretation toward resumed easing. By month's end, UK unemployment rose to its highest since the pandemic, youth unemployment climbed above the EU average for the first time, and wage growth eased, prompting traders to fully price 50 basis points of cuts by year-end. Against that, the UK recorded its largest budget surplus on record during the period, boosted by surging capital gains tax receipts and lower debt servicing costs — a brief fiscal respite for a government navigating difficult spending decisions. Q4 GDP barely expanded, with services stagnating. Chancellor Merz meanwhile pursued Middle Eastern energy suppliers to reduce German reliance on US LNG, and German factory orders rose at their fastest pace in two years, offering a constructive data point for an economy that closed 2025 with stronger-than-expected growth.

Corporate Developments and Auto Sector Restructuring

February produced a notable dispersion of corporate outcomes across Europe. Novo Nordisk's fall of as much as 20% in Copenhagen — one of the largest single-day declines in its history — on sharply weaker sales guidance reset consensus assumptions on GLP-1 competitive dynamics and carried index-level implications for Denmark; the company subsequently announced approximately 5,000 job cuts among the largest layoffs in Danish history. Stellantis's €22 billion in EV-related charges — exceeding analyst expectations and covering model cancellations and supplier compensation — extended the auto sector's retreat from electrification timelines, with implications for European supply chains and further questions about the continent's path toward 2035 combustion-engine phase-out targets. Airbus's disclosure that Pratt & Whitney has failed to deliver engines in sufficient volume to meet production targets is a material setback, complicating an already complex order backlog and shifting accountability toward the supply chain, contributing to a 6.7% fall on Thursday. Shell reported adjusted net income of $3.26 billion in Q4, down 11% year-on-year, on softer crude and weak trading, but maintained its $3.5 billion quarterly buyback. BP, by contrast, suspended its $750 million quarterly buyback and withdrew capital-return guidance, with shares falling as much as 7.7% across the final week — a divergence from Chevron, Exxon, Shell, and TotalEnergies that will weigh on its relative valuation until incoming CEO Meg O'Neill communicates a credible deleveraging pathway. Equinor cut its 2026 buyback to as much as $1.5 billion from $5 billion in 2025. UBS announced a $3 billion buyback alongside an earnings beat. Nuveen agreed to acquire Schroders for $13.5 billion at a roughly 29% premium, ending more than two centuries of independence for the UK's largest standalone asset manager. The proposed Rio Tinto–Glencore tie-up collapsed within 24 hours in the third week over price disagreements; Glencore shares fell roughly 11%. Bayer agreed to pay more than $7 billion to resolve Roundup litigation, potentially closing a years-long legal overhang. Adyen fell 20% in Amsterdam and Dassault Systèmes dropped around 20% in Paris, extending the AI-displacement selloff across European software and fintech. EU carbon allowances fell to their lowest since July as Slovakia, Germany, and France openly clashed over ETS reform at the Brussels summit, with JPMorgan analysts flagging that the resulting uncertainty is already impairing long-term green capex planning. French customs data provided concrete evidence of US tariff pass-through: Q4 2025 spirits exports fell 47%, wine 39%, and perfumes and cosmetics 25% — meaningful declines for export-sensitive French consumer categories. ---

Global

  • Brent crude oscillated between approximately $63 and $70 through February, caught between escalating US-Iran tensions — including an Iranian drone intercept, a US ultimatum, and confirmation of Muscat and Geneva nuclear talks — and a building global supply glut that kept the structural downward pressure intact.
  • Gold held above $5,000/oz through most of the month, peaked mid-month on safe-haven and geopolitical demand before a sharp Friday reversal, then stabilized; Bitcoin fell to roughly half its October peak, reinforcing bullion rather than crypto as the dominant store-of-value trade.
  • January non-resident EM portfolio inflows approached $100 billion — the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades — as the dollar softened and investors rotated deliberately away from concentrated US exposure.
  • The IEA's annual power outlook projects global electricity demand growing roughly 50% faster over the next five years than the prior decade, with consumption set to outpace GDP — a structural break with significant implications for inflation, capex, and the energy transition.
  • The IEA Paris ministerial ended without a joint communiqué as the US threatened to exit unless the agency abandoned its net zero framing, marking a significant fracture in the institution Washington co-founded in the 1970s.

Oil Markets and Iran Diplomacy

The US-Iran dynamic was February's most consequential global risk variable. The month opened with the US Navy shooting down an Iranian drone heading toward an American aircraft carrier in the Arabian Sea on the first Monday, sending Brent to approximately $68. Iran subsequently confirmed nuclear talks scheduled in Muscat, and oil declined for the first time in three days as diplomacy appeared to temper the risk premium — though the American military build-up in the region continued. A second diplomatic track opened in Geneva in the final week, with US Special Envoy Steve Witkoff and Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi holding sessions both sides described as constructive, with Iran expected to return detailed proposals within two weeks. The tone shifted materially when President Trump stated Iran has ten to fifteen days to reach an agreement and that the US is "ready, willing, and able to rapidly fulfill its mission, with speed and violence, if necessary." Iran responded it would "defend itself and respond like never before," the Wall Street Journal reported the administration was evaluating a limited early strike to pressure rather than preempt, and the UK reportedly withheld access to Diego Garcia for potential operations. WTI moved from near $63 at the month's open toward $66 by the final Friday. The trajectory of negotiations — and whether Iran's expected response accepts core US red lines covering ballistic missiles and proxy financing, or whether the diplomatic track narrows — is the single most consequential near-term variable for global energy markets. Bloomberg Opinion's John Authers argued through the month that markets are under-pricing a major oil shock, a view that sits uncomfortably with current term-structure pricing and implies asymmetric risk should hostilities escalate toward the Strait of Hormuz. Iranian and Russian sanctioned crude accumulated unsold through the period, driven less by enforcement than by ample availability of legitimate alternatives at competitive prices — a development with meaningful implications for the marginal price of oil and for discount structures available to remaining buyers, principally China. South Korea's Sinokor group accumulated roughly 120 VLCCs over two months, helping push benchmark VLCC earnings above $120,000 per day — a more than fourfold increase over the month.

Monetary Policy Divergence

February produced some of the sharpest cross-country monetary policy divergence observed this cycle. The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered the first rate hike among major central banks in 2026, while the ECB, Bank of England, and Bank Indonesia all held. Norway's January CPI surprise drove 10-year sovereign yields to levels not seen since early 2009, shifting OIS pricing toward a March Norges Bank hike — treated by markets as idiosyncratic given the energy-heavy economy's inflation dynamics rather than a regional signal, as eurozone yields fell on the same session. A senior BOJ hawk signaled that conditions for Japan's next rate hike could align by spring. Russia's central bank delivered an unexpected rate cut; South Africa's Reserve Bank is now expected to cut three further times; Peru held steady. India's RBI held while citing improved clarity from a prospective US trade framework. In the US, the January payrolls beat pushed the expected first Fed cut from June to July, with the FOMC minutes subsequently revealing members had considered the possibility of rate increases. The MOVE index continued its steady decline from its April Liberation Day peak, suggesting bond markets are treating the institutional uncertainty around the Fed succession as manageable — though that complacency may face its first real test once Warsh arrives in May. The dollar's trajectory through the month was itself contested: dollar weakness accelerated in portions of the final week on tariff ruling and rotation dynamics, before the hawkish FOMC minutes triggered the dollar's best week in four months. Gold's persistence above $5,000/oz while Bitcoin fell to roughly half its October peak reinforces that bullion — not crypto — is absorbing safe-haven and dollar-diversification flows tied to US fiscal and policy concerns.

AI Sovereignty and the Capital Expenditure Cycle

February established AI sovereignty as a defining strategic theme for non-US, non-Chinese economies. India's AI Impact Summit, the first such gathering held in the Global South, crystallized a common structural concern across middle-power nations: the most consequential AI systems are controlled by a small number of US and Chinese entities, and access can in principle be interrupted. The 2024 UniSuper incident, in which a Google cloud misconfiguration cut off Australian pension fund members, served as a reference point for technology dependency risk. India's approach combines domestic small language models, shared compute facilities, semiconductor design ambitions, and active capital attraction. OpenAI's near-finalized funding round exceeding $100 billion — valuing the company above $850 billion, with Amazon, SoftBank, Nvidia, Microsoft, and sovereign wealth funds among anchors — underscores that AI infrastructure deployment has become a geopolitical as well as commercial competition. The BofA global fund manager survey showed for the first time in a generation that a majority of money managers fear corporate overinvestment rather than underinvestment, a direct consequence of $600 billion-plus in hyperscaler capex projected for 2026. Goldman Sachs economists projected data centers will account for 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, with electricity-price inflation near 6% annually through 2027. China's ability to add generating capacity at a pace materially exceeding the US build-out — 543 GW of power capacity additions in 2025 — positions it on a more favorable power-cost curve for AI infrastructure, even as US export controls constrain its chip position. China's CO2 emissions fell 0.3% in 2025 — the first non-pandemic annual decline, as renewables absorbed all incremental power demand — while the US moved in the opposite direction, rescinding the 2009 endangerment finding and advancing a proposed 9.2-gigawatt gas plant in Ohio. The IEA fracture appeared genuine: the Paris ministerial ended without a joint communiqué, with only a Netherlands-authored summary published after Energy Secretary Chris Wright assailed the agency's net zero framing. The policy divergence between the two largest emitters appears increasingly structural and will shape investment flows, trade relations, and geopolitical competition through the decade.

Capital Flows and Trade Corridor Realignment

The global capital-flow picture reflected a deliberate reappraisal of US asset concentration risk. Non-resident portfolio inflows into emerging markets approached $100 billion in January — the second-strongest monthly reading in two decades per IIF data — with allocations broad-based across debt, equity, China, and ex-China. Hong Kong single-family offices, 25% more numerous at end-2025 than a year prior, reported among the highest reallocation intentions surveyed, with 19% planning reduced US exposure; China's official Treasury holdings fell to $683 billion in November, their lowest level in years. Against the backdrop of US tariff escalation reshaping traditional trade corridors, Gulf Cooperation Council economies emerged as a notable beneficiary: GCC trade expanded at 15% annually from 2021–24 versus a 9% global rate, and Asia-Middle East flows are projected to reach $1.5 trillion by 2030 from $905 billion in 2024. Britain signaled a GCC free-trade deal was imminent. The UAE imported approximately 560,000 Chinese passenger vehicles in 2025, reflecting its role as a transshipment hub for Chinese manufactured goods navigating Western tariff exposure. Port of Los Angeles export volumes fell 8% year-on-year in January, with soybean shipments to China down 80% as Argentine and Brazilian suppliers locked in multi-month contracts, effectively displacing US exporters for at least another agricultural cycle. The durability of these corridor shifts ultimately rests on whether the geopolitical and tariff environment that has accelerated them proves stable — a question that, as with much of February's macro landscape, remains genuinely unresolved.
