The Federal Reserve's policy decision dominated market attention on March 18, with the FOMC widely expected to hold rates steady for a second consecutive meeting. Chair Powell's press conference carried unusual weight given the conflicting signals the central bank must now navigate: energy prices rising sharply on the back of the Iran conflict, a softening consumer backdrop, and an administration publicly at war with itself over the economic response. President Trump called for an emergency rate cut — a move associated historically with acute crises — while National Economic Council Director Kevin Hassett struck a notably more sanguine tone on CNBC, saying a prolonged war's impact on consumers was "really the last of our concerns right now." A survey of 28 former Fed policymakers and staff, conducted ahead of Wednesday's meeting, projected PCE inflation of 3% for 2026 and year-end unemployment of 4.6%, with a majority expecting rates to remain on hold throughout the year.

The consumer outlook continues to deteriorate at the margin. Average gasoline prices have risen roughly 27% since hostilities began on February 28, reaching levels last seen in 2023, while diesel surpassed $5 per gallon for the first time since 2022. The mechanism differs from the post-Ukraine shock: households no longer hold pandemic-era savings buffers, and January consumer spending was essentially flat even before the energy spike hit. SMBC Nikko Securities estimates the current gasoline price increase could reduce the savings rate by 60 basis points to approximately 3.9%. University of Michigan consumer sentiment fell to a three-month low in the March preliminary reading.

On corporate news, Lululemon fell approximately 2% in premarket trading after forecasting a second consecutive year of profit declines, while DocuSign rose after beating earnings expectations. General Mills was set to report before the open, with Micron Technology scheduled after the close — the latter carrying particular macro relevance given its memory chip exposure and a market capitalization that has risen from roughly $100 billion at end-2024 to approximately $520 billion. Pending home sales rose 1.8% in the latest reading, partially reversing a prior decline, though Oxford Economics cautioned that higher energy costs threaten to suppress household spending decisions in the months ahead.
