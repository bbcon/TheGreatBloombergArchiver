**Hormuz and Oil Markets**
The interim US-Iran agreement has initiated a gradual reopening of the Strait of Hormuz, but the path to normalization is proving non-linear. Traffic through the strait picked up following Wednesday's deal, then thinned as peace talks were delayed and Israel-Hezbollah clashes in southern Lebanon — killing four Israeli soldiers — raised questions about Iranian compliance with ceasefire conditions. The commercial consequences are significant: Asian buyers, who represent the primary destination for Gulf crude, face a potential supply surge that their storage and refining infrastructure may struggle to absorb quickly. Japan, China, and South Korea have capacity to rebuild strategic reserves; India may need to cancel or resell cargoes to avoid demurrage penalties. Gulf producers, having been unable to sell new cargoes for months, face strong incentives to accelerate loadings, adding to the supply overhang.

**Dollar and De-dollarization**
President Trump's public rationale for returning frozen Iranian assets — that withholding them would deter future dollar investment globally — offers an unusually candid window into US thinking on dollar dominance. The interim deal also eliminates, for the 60-day negotiating window, the yuan-denominated Hormuz transit tolls Iran had imposed during the conflict, dampening near-term petroyuan momentum. The Fed's hawkish posture simultaneously reinforces dollar demand through interest rate differentials. These forces may temporarily arrest de-dollarization dynamics, though the structural incentives for reserve diversification remain intact.

**Technology and Chip Controls**
The ASML situation represents a meaningful escalation in US semiconductor export enforcement. Commerce Secretary Lutnick's direct engagement with ASML's senior leadership over a potential EUV machine in China — the most advanced lithography equipment in production — signals Washington intends to tighten compliance standards regardless of allied corporate interests. ASML denies any such shipment occurred, noting that EUV machines require constant vendor maintenance. The episode adds uncertainty to the European semiconductor supply chain and risks further straining US-EU technology policy coordination.
