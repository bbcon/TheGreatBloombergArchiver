US Diesel Squeeze Threatens to Ripple Through Economy

time since 2022.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Nathan Risser looks at the rally in diesel, the lifeblood of trucking, 
construction and industry.

The rally in oil prices triggered by the war in Iran is reverberating across 
the energy complex, affecting everything from gasoline to jet fuel. But for the 
broader economy,diesel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/us-diesel-tops-5-a-gallon-as-war-disrupts-fuel-supply-chains?cmpid=BBBXT031826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 is where the pressure may be the most acute.

The average price for a gallon of the fuel in the US surpassed $5 this week, a 
level last seen in the immediate aftermath of Russia’s invasion of Ukraine. The 
surge since Iran was attacked has easily outpaced gasoline.

As in 2022, when sanctions curtailed heavy Russian oil exports, the world is 
once again short of the type of crude needed by complex refineries toproduce 
distillates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/goldman-says-oil-s-biggest-shock-to-hurt-refined-products-most?cmpid=BBBXT031826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260318>
 such as diesel.

Not all oils are equal. Despite the US being the largest producer, the light 
varieties pumped out of shale basins such as the Permian — totaling about 13 
million barrels a day — are good for making gasoline.

But it’s the heavier grades from the Persian Gulf — most of which, in more 
normal times, travel through the Strait of Hormuz — as well as Venezuela and 
Canada that are best suited to making diesel.

The US imported about 500,000 barrels a day of Middle Eastern crude last year. 
With that flow more or less halted for now, American refiners are paying up to 
fill the gap.

Phillips 66 said yesterday <bbg://news/stories/TC22NSPR5MGX> that the discount 
on heavy crude has narrowed again, having widened earlier this year as more 
Venezuelan oil flowed to North America after the capture of ex-president 
Nicolás Maduro.

Diesel markets were tight even before the US and Israel attacked Iran. That 
was partly due to the lingering effects of the Russia-Ukraine war. US 
inventories started 2026 well below the 10-year average and the government 
projects theywill decline <https://www.eia.gov/outlooks/steo/pdf/compare.pdf> 
this year and next.

Diesel demand is also growing in the US, in stark contrast to the slow decline 
in gasoline use. And unlike in Europe, American diesel users are almost 
exclusively commercial. Surging prices are going straight to the bottom line 
for countless businesses.

Unless tanker traffic picks up soon in the Strait of Hormuz, the grumblings 
are likely to grow ever louder.

—Nathan Risser, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Oil and gas majors cut investment in low-carbon energy by 33% to just over $25 
billion in 2025, according toBloombergNEF <bbg://news/stories/TC35L5KIP3KQ> 
data. It was the first annual decline in eight years and the lowest reading 
since 2020. It coincided with retrenchment across the industry, with companies 
sharpening their focus on capital discipline and costs in key segments like 
upstream and refining.
