Trump Signals Long Slog for Companies Filing for Tariff Refunds
Known for speedy policy-making, Trump is signaling companies seeking tariff 
refunds face a long slog

Donald Trump’s administration rushed out tariffs and trade negotiations last 
year to address what it saw as the national emergency of unfair trade 
imbalances.Ninety deals 
<https://www.cfr.org/articles/tracking-trumps-trade-deals> in 90 days was the 
soundbite from White House adviser Peter Navarro, the author of a book about 
working in a political fast lane he dubbed “Trump time.”

But when it comes to sending out refunds on those now-illegal import taxes 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-02-23/ceo-who-challenged-trump-s-tariffs-on-scotus-decision-video?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
, the administration’s need for speed has faded away.

The initial reaction 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/trump-imposes-10-global-tariff-in-bid-to-salvage-trade-plans?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 on refunds from the president after losing the Supreme Court case on Friday 
was, “we’ll end up being in court for the next five years.” Treasury Secretary 
Scott Bessent spun the return ofthe money 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/bessent-says-tariff-revenue-to-be-virtually-unchanged-in-2026?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 to American importers as “corporate welfare.” Republicans in Congress sound 
aligned: Asked by a CNN reporter whether Trump should refund tariff revenue, 
House Speaker Mike Johnsonoffered no urgency 
<https://x.com/mkraju/statuses/2026022448353665202>.

But there are a few reasons why slow-walking or even preventing refunds — 
leaving in limbo about about $170 billion, by Bloomberg Economics’ estimate — 
may backfire.

Lengthy ‘Wrangling’

The first involves legal obstacles, according to Bloomberg’s Zoe Tillman. 
Justice Department attorneys hadwritten in court filings 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/trump-faces-tough-legal-landscape-to-oppose-tariff-refunds?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 last summer that plaintiffs whose cases went to the Supreme Court “will 
assuredly receive payment on their refund with interest” if they won. Trade 
lawyers said judges are likely to hold the administration to that pledge.

“We can expect protracted legal wrangling over any refunds,” Nicole 
Gorton-Caratelli, Chris Kennedy and Maeva Cousin of Bloomberg Economics wrote 
in aresearch note <> on Monday.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-19/us-trade-deficit-widens-capping-one-of-biggest-gaps-since-1960?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>

The second is political pressure. Prominent Democrats are seizing on Trump’s 
courtroom loss to wage anelection-year push 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/democrats-wage-populist-fight-to-refund-tariffs-to-us-households?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 to refund the overturned import duties to the public, according to Bloomberg’s 
Steven T. Dennis.

While importers are already readying for battle in the courts to win back the 
billions they paid, odds for action in Congress seem slim. “I don’t see a 
pathway in Congress that there’s going to be refunds,” House Ways and Means 
Chair Jason Smith toldBloomberg Television 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-02-23/open-interest-2-23-2026-video?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 on Monday.

E-Refund System

Addressing the refunds is up to the US Court of International Trade, which 
would determine whether refunds will be available, how they will be doled out, 
and to whom.

As a practical matter, the speed of returns hinges largely on the 
bureaucracy’s ability to process them. Presumably Customs and Border 
Protection, with Treasury’s help, would write the checks, which should be 
easier to distribute given that CBP started issuing all refunds on its 
electronic platform onFeb. 6 
<https://www.cbp.gov/trade/automated/ace-portal-and-ach-refunds-faqs>.

One key question, according to Isabel Gottlieb with Bloomberg Industry: Will 
companies that paid the tariffs automatically get a refund, or will importers 
have to sue at the trade court?

QuickTake: With Trump’s Tariffs Overturned, Who Gets Refunds? 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/what-to-know-about-trump-s-tariff-refunds-after-the-supreme-court-decision?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>

More than 1,500 tariff refund lawsuits are pending so far, Tillman’s reporting 
shows 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-07/trump-tariffs-face-supreme-court-with-1-000-firms-seeking-refunds?cmpid=BBD022426_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
. That’s a fraction of the total number of companies that could demand refunds. 
More than 300,000 importers had paid the challenged tariffs by the end of 2025, 
according to the government.

FedEx Sues

Among the latest companies to sue for refunds 
<https://assets.bwbx.io/documents/users/iqjWHBFdfxIU/rEcQez9ciHws/v0> is a 
prominent one — parcel delivery giant FedEx, which didn’t specify how much the 
company had paid in levies but said it seeks “a full refund from Defendants of 
all IEEPA duties Plaintiffs have paid to the United States.”

With the Supreme Court silent on remedies and refunds, importers now face one 
of the largest customs refund operations in modern history — a complex endeavor 
that carries enormous fiscal consequences, Ashley Akers at Holland & Knight 
wrote in aBloomberg Law Insight <>.
