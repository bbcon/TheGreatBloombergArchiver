# Global Daily Briefing — May 18, 2026

**Iran stalemate drives the global macro picture.** Brent crude rose above $111 a barrel and WTI traded near $107 as the US-Iran impasse over the Strait of Hormuz entered its third month with no diplomatic breakthrough in sight. President Trump warned Tehran the "clock is ticking" hours after drones targeted a UAE nuclear power plant, while Iran's Mehr agency characterized US terms — including transfer of uranium stocks, no reparations, and release of less than a quarter of frozen assets — as offering "no tangible concessions." Commercial shipping through the strait remains largely frozen. The UAE is accelerating construction of a pipeline to Fujairah to double bypass capacity by next year, though such workarounds will not substitute for restored Gulf flows at scale.

**Bond markets are doing the repricing the Fed has not.** A global sovereign selloff deepened, with Japan's 30-year yield surging as much as 20bp to its highest since the tenor's 1999 debut, and US 30-year yields approaching 5%. Traders have flipped from pricing US cuts this year to entertaining the prospect of a hike — a striking reversal that reflects how energy-driven inflation is overwhelming the disinflationary forces central banks had been counting on. Equity futures slipped but the AI-led rally has so far proven resilient; Morgan Stanley's Wilson warned a sustained yield grind higher could trigger the first meaningful correction since March.

**Policy divergence is widening under a common shock.** Japan's PM Takaichi reversed course and instructed the finance ministry to compile a supplementary budget; Thailand is readying a roughly $6.1 billion cost-of-living package. China's April data — retail sales up just 0.2% y/y, industrial production at 4.1%, fixed-asset investment contracting 1.6% YTD — undercut Beijing's reluctance to add stimulus and complicate the afterglow of last week's Trump-Xi summit, which yielded $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchases through 2028 but little operational detail. G-7 finance ministers convening in Paris confront these imbalances against a backdrop French Finance Minister Lescure called "correcting, but not collapsing."

**Tensions to watch:** Trump's July 24 tariff deadline could collide with elevated US pump prices and a still-closed Hormuz, testing the durability of the Beijing détente. Putin arrives in China this week with more leverage on Power of Siberia 2 than he has had in years, though Xi retains the structural upper hand given diversified supply options.
