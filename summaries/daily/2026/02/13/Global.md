# Global Macro Daily — February 13, 2026

**Oil markets caught between glut narrative and geopolitical tail risks.** Brent traded around $70 per barrel and headed for back-to-back weekly declines on broader risk-off sentiment, concerns over a global crude surplus, and the prospect of drawn-out US-Iran nuclear talks. At this week's London oil trading summit, sentiment was conflicted: bears point to current price levels as evidence of ample supply despite ongoing tensions, while bulls highlight the accumulation of sanctioned Russian and Iranian crude on tankers, arguing that unsanctioned barrels show little sign of the much-discussed surplus. Pimco's Greg Sharenow captured the prevailing mood: "The uncertainty band is wider than we would have thought six or 10 weeks ago." Cross-currents include US military activity near Venezuela and Iran, Kazakh supply disruptions, and a US-India trade arrangement that appears to be stranding Russian crude at sea. Separately, Reliance Industries received a US general license to purchase Venezuelan oil directly, a notable carve-out within the broader sanctions architecture.

**Monetary policy divergence broadens.** Russia's central bank delivered an unexpected rate cut, while Peru held steady; economists now anticipate three further cuts from the South African Reserve Bank. A senior BOJ hawk signaled that conditions for the next rate hike could be in place by spring, while the PBOC's growing focus on the overnight rate is fueling speculation of a formal shift in its policy target. In the US, markets await January CPI for cues on the Fed's resumption of cuts, though Citi and JPMorgan strategists separately warn of complacency on the inflation trajectory.

**Climate-energy split-screen.** Initial CREA analysis suggests Chinese carbon emissions fell 0.3% in 2025—the first non-pandemic decline—as EV sales and clean generation absorbed incremental power demand. The US moved in the opposite direction, with the EPA rescinding the 2009 endangerment finding that underpins federal greenhouse gas authority, alongside the repeal of Biden-era vehicle emissions standards. The policy divergence between the two largest emitters appears increasingly structural.

**Treasury stewardship.** China's holdings fell to $683 billion in November, the lowest since 2008, with Beijing reportedly advising banks to trim US sovereign exposure on concentration grounds. European and EM official holders have also pared positions, though Treasury volatility sits at a five-year low—suggesting reallocation remains gradual rather than disorderly.
