**Strait of Hormuz: Escalation and Tentative Signals**

The defining development across global markets this week was the continued closure of the Strait of Hormuz, now entering its fourth week, combined with a significant escalation mid-week and the first tentative signals of possible recalibration by week's end. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field on Thursday — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities in the conflict — and Iran responded by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City, causing "extensive damage" and sparking fires; two affected LNG trains are expected to remain offline for up to five years. Additional Iranian strikes hit a gas facility in Abu Dhabi, set ablaze two Kuwaiti oil refineries, and caused damage near a Saudi refinery. Tehran explicitly designated energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE as "legitimate targets." By Friday, however, Prime Minister Netanyahu stated Israel would no longer target energy infrastructure and said the war would end faster than people expect, while Iran's foreign minister signaled that recent restraint was deliberate. Whether this constitutes a durable de-escalation or tactical repositioning remains genuinely uncertain.

**Oil and Energy Markets**

Brent crude surged above $118 per barrel on Thursday — a gain of approximately 63% since hostilities began on February 28 — before pulling back to approximately $108–110 on Friday following Netanyahu's remarks. The IEA has characterized the disruption as the largest oil supply shock in recorded history by volume. Longer-dated Brent futures remain below $70, suggesting markets still price the closure as temporary, though that view has become harder to sustain with each passing week. Notably, Goldman Sachs analysts observed that refined products have rallied far more than crude, with jet kerosene in Singapore up more than 60%, reflecting the disruption to medium-heavy Gulf crude that feeds diesel, jet fuel, and fuel oil production. The gap between WTI near $96, Brent at $108–118, and Middle Eastern grades above $150 reflects a fragmented supply landscape in which the shortage is most acute for Asian importers. Saudi Arabia has reportedly been able to reroute some exports to above half of normal levels via contingency infrastructure. Iraq's agreement to resume pipeline exports via Turkey offered one partial workaround and contributed to Friday's price pullback. Insurance premiums for Hormuz transit have reached approximately 5% of hull value, roughly five times the conflict's outset level.

**Central Banks and Macro Consequences**

The week's central bank calendar was historically dense, covering approximately two-thirds of the global economy across 18 policy meetings. The Reserve Bank of Australia became the first to move, hiking rates on Tuesday in response to the inflationary impulse from energy — a signal that at least some inflation-targeting frameworks are treating the oil shock as a demand-side tightening event rather than a supply disruption to be looked through. The Fed held on Wednesday, the ECB and Bank of England held on Thursday, and the Bank of Japan held on Thursday. All shifted their tone materially toward caution, and the cumulative repricing across global rate markets — from an easing trajectory to a tightening one within the space of three weeks — represents one of the more significant collective monetary policy pivots of the current cycle. Bloomberg Economics estimates March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections. BofA's global fund manager survey found institutional investors rotating overweight commodities — the biggest such shift since the 2022 Ukraine invasion.

**Alliance Fractures and Geopolitical Realignment**

Trump's appeals for allied support have produced little tangible commitment. The UK, Japan, Germany, and South Korea have each declined to participate militarily; Europe's largest naval powers stated that reopening the Strait during active hostilities would require a deeply complex multinational capability not currently feasible. Trump publicly abandoned the request for allied warships after being rebuffed and criticized allies, exposing meaningful fissures in Western alliance architecture. China's state media have been openly dismissive of US appeals. The Trump-Xi summit postponement to mid-May adds to a picture of constrained US diplomatic leverage. Trump threatened to delay the summit further if Beijing does not assist, while simultaneously the Pentagon is reported to have requested an additional $200 billion from Congress to fund ongoing operations. Gold fell approximately 7% on the week — its largest weekly decline since March 2020 and on pace for its worst monthly performance since 2008 — as the global monetary policy repricing eroded the appeal of non-yielding assets, with ETF outflows exceeding 60 tons over three weeks. Tungsten prices have more than doubled this year amid Chinese export controls and rising military demand, illustrating how the conflict is amplifying pre-existing commodity tensions.
