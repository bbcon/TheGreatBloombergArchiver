**Energy Markets and Chokepoint Risk**
The oil market is now pricing two simultaneous supply disruption risks rather than one. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with tanker transits near zero, while Houthi preparations to blockade the Bab el-Mandeb — assessed at a 19% probability by end-August on prediction markets, rising to 33% by year-end — introduce a second vector of disruption that would affect both energy and container flows between Asia and Europe. US strategic petroleum reserves stand at their lowest level since 1983, limiting the buffer available to absorb further supply shocks. BCA Research's thesis of a "kinetic equilibrium" keeping Brent between $70 and $90 is now being tested, with prices above the upper bound. The analytical framework — that approaching price ceilings incentivize de-escalation from both Washington and Tehran — appears sound in theory, but red lines have already been crossed this week with US strikes on bridges and Iranian attacks on desalination plants.

**LNG and European Winter Risk**
Asia's scramble for LNG is a direct consequence of Hormuz disruptions to Qatari exports, which supplied more than a quarter of the region's LNG in 2025. European LNG deliveries are down approximately 35% even as the continent experiences its own heat wave. As European buyers begin building winter gas inventories, competition with Asia for spot cargoes appears likely to intensify materially, with speculative net-long positioning in European gas rising at its fastest pace since the conflict began. The convergence of Middle East supply risk, seasonal demand, and structurally constrained liquefaction capacity creates a tightening dynamic that may persist into Q4.
