# European Markets Briefing — May 20, 2026

**EU finalizes US trade deal.** After months of negotiation, EU lawmakers and member states reached agreement on the text of the long-delayed trade pact with Washington, clearing a major hurdle to ratification ahead of President Trump's July 4 deadline for higher tariffs. The framework removes EU tariffs on American industrial goods in exchange for a 15% ceiling on the bloc's exports — critically averting a threatened increase to 25% on European automobiles. The outcome offers near-term relief for European exporters, though it formalizes a meaningfully less favorable trading arrangement than the status quo ante. Eurozone export data underscored the stakes: EU shipments to the US fell 37% year-on-year in March, the sharpest decline in records dating to 2003, following last year's pre-tariff frontloading.

**ECB and Banque de France leadership.** Belgian Governor Pierre Wunsch indicated the ECB will likely hike rates in June, with the Iran-driven oil shock reshaping the inflation trajectory. Emmanuel Moulin, appearing before French lawmakers voting on his nomination to lead the Banque de France, reinforced that the ECB must be "ready to act." The shift toward a tightening bias marks a notable repricing from earlier in the cycle and aligns with broader developed-market dynamics, where global long-bond yields have reached their highest levels in nearly two decades.

**UK inflation and politics.** UK CPI fell to its lowest reading in more than a year, prompting traders to scale back expectations for further BoE hikes — a divergence from euro-area pricing worth monitoring. Separately, Reform UK deputy leader Richard Tice warned investors against participating in the AR7 offshore wind auction, pledging the party would unwind Labour-era renewables contracts. Given Reform's recent local election performance, the policy risk premium attached to UK green infrastructure assets warrants reassessment, though the legal basis for retroactive contract intervention remains untested.

**Corporate.** Marks & Spencer guided full-year profit above pre-cyberattack levels, gaining 4.7% in London. Mercedes-Benz unveiled an electric AMG GT positioned against rivals navigating the EV transition.
