**US Daily Briefing — January 30, 2026**

**Trump Nominates Warsh to Chair the Fed.** President Trump named Kevin Warsh as his nominee to succeed Jerome Powell when Powell's term concludes in May. Warsh, a former Fed governor passed over for the chairmanship in 2017, has publicly aligned himself with the president's views on monetary policy, though his policy record suggests a more orthodox stance than the dovish posture Trump has demanded. Initial Wall Street reaction characterized the pick as relatively hawkish — a notable tension given the administration's persistent pressure for lower rates. Confirmation by the Republican-controlled Senate appears likely but is not assured. The practical implications for the rate path remain uncertain: Warsh would inherit a committee whose median view he cannot unilaterally dictate, and his historical concerns about asset-price distortions and inflation credibility could constrain any rapid pivot toward easier policy. As Jonathan Levin observed in Bloomberg Opinion, Trump "demanded a dove [and] picked a hawk" — a characterization that, if it holds, complicates the narrative of imminent Fed capitulation to political pressure.

**Markets React to Fed News and Hotter PPI.** US equities declined following the Warsh announcement, with the move compounded by a hotter-than-expected PPI print that reinforces concerns about sticky upstream inflation. The combination — a perceived hawkish nominee alongside firmer producer prices — pressured rate-sensitive sectors and contributed to a sharp reversal in precious metals, with gold and silver both falling sharply after recent record-setting moves. The metals decline marks a notable unwind of the safe-haven trade that had built through January amid geopolitical strains, including renewed friction over Trump's Greenland posture that had earlier revived "Sell America" flows.

**Positioning Backdrop.** Wealth advisers surveyed by Bloomberg generally counsel against elevated cash allocations despite the risk-off tone, with several rotating toward value equities and selective international exposure, including Japan. The advice reflects a view that current volatility, while elevated, does not warrant defensive capitulation.
