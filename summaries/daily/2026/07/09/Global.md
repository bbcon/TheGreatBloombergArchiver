• Strait of Hormuz traffic fell to a near standstill Thursday, with observable crossings among larger vessels at the lowest since before the June interim agreement.
• FOMC minutes and rising energy prices are jointly shifting global rate expectations toward further tightening, complicating monetary policy across both developed and emerging markets.

**Hormuz: Renewed Escalation**
US forces struck approximately 90 Iranian military targets over two consecutive days, targeting Tehran's capacity to threaten commercial shipping. Iran retaliated with missiles and drones against US bases in Kuwait and Bahrain. The binary framework traders have applied since March remains operative: the risk of a ground conflict with permanent damage to production infrastructure appears contained — Trump explicitly called such escalation a "waste of time" — but the Strait's near-closure reintroduces meaningful supply uncertainty. Creative workarounds have emerged, including dark shuttle transits developed by Abu Dhabi producers and convoy crossings using Iranian-approved northern routes, but these are partial mitigants rather than structural solutions.

**Global Inflation and Monetary Policy Divergence**
The energy re-escalation arrives at a moment when inflation risks had appeared to be moderating. Bond market breakevens, while still below pre-war levels, are moving higher again. The combination of sticky refined product crack spreads, Ukrainian drone strikes turning Russia into a net diesel importer, and an intensifying Hormuz crisis creates a genuinely adverse inflation backdrop. A former Bank of Japan official suggested the BoJ may accelerate rate hikes later in 2026 and push its benchmark above 2%. Thailand's Constitutional Court cleared a $12 billion emergency borrowing decree, removing a major obstacle to fiscal stimulus. A new Super El Niño phase has begun, described as potentially among the strongest on record, adding climate risk to an already complex global macro environment.
