In one of the clearest signs that the US-Iran war is in the rearview mirror, 
Brent oil has returned to levelslast seen before 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-june-25?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5923>
 the conflict began in late February.

The global benchmark pushed lower for a fourth session, dropping below $72.48 
a barrel — its pre-war closing price. Instead of a shortage, key parts of the 
market are suddenly awash with supply. Buyers areinundated by offers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/hormuz-reopening-is-quickly-flooding-oil-markets-with-supply?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5923>
 from the Middle East as well as Africa, adramatic reversal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/asia-s-demand-for-middle-eastern-oil-slows-after-buying-spree?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5923>
 that’s led to widespread price weakness.

While peace talks between the US and Iran still face hurdles, optimism about a 
lasting agreement has led to more tankers openly crossing the Strait of Hormuz 
with their satellite signals switched on. The speed of the decline in the oil 
price has taken some observers by surprise.

“It’s quite amazing the price and narrative turnaround from even where the 
market was less than two weeks ago,” said Carolyn Kissane, associate dean of 
New York University’s Center for Global Affairs. “The idea that we are pivoting 
to seeing more supply with lower demand has really driven the downward price 
shift.”

That’s good news for central banks struggling to contain inflationary 
pressures.
