Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Rajesh Kumar Singh takes a look at developing nations’ growing 
enthusiasm for nuclear power.

Much of the world soured on atomic energy following Japan’s Fukushima disaster 
in 2011, but over the last few years the technology has undergone something of a
renaissance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-24/nuclear-power-makes-comeback-driven-by-climate-change-ai-energy-demands?cmpid=BBBXT071326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6465>
.

In richer countries, the growing interest in nuclear is tied to the upsurge in 
electricity demand for artificial intelligence. Developing nations are also 
keen, albeit for different reasons.

Of the roughly 80 nuclear reactors being built around the world, most are in 
emerging economies.

For these countries, nuclear helps to decarbonize their grids while avoiding 
an over-reliance on intermittent power sources such as solar and wind. It’s 
also a way of cutting dependence on imports of fossil fuels that are vulnerable 
to external shocks — notably Russia’s invasion of Ukraine and the Iran war.

India and Pakistan have already deployed nuclear, and the next cab off the 
rank is Bangladesh.A Russian-built atomic plant 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-07-12/bangladesh-s-nuclear-debut-tests-developing-world-s-atomic-ambitions?cmpid=BBBXT071326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6465>
 will supply 15% of the country’s electricity when it becomes fully operational 
in 2028.

One of the main drawbacks of nuclear is the massive upfront expense and 
lengthy construction times, which can often blow out costs if there are delays.

Bangladesh has experienced both.

Rooppur was supposed to start commissioning its first reactor in 2023, but 
that’s been pushed back to August this year. The plant’s $12.65 billion price 
tag has increased by almost a quarter in local-currency terms since the project 
was approved a decade ago, due to a sharp weakening of the Bangladeshi taka 
against the dollar.

The facility’s operator still expects the cost of energy to be cheaper than 
alternatives in the long-run given the lifespan of the plant and its lower 
operational expenses. However, the country’s energy minister has said the 
nation won’t go for large-scale nuclear again.

Instead, it is exploring the use of small modular reactors 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-11-26/can-small-nuclear-reactors-really-help-the-climate-quicktake?cmpid=BBBXT071326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260713&utm_content=6465>
, a newer technology that has yet to be deployed commercially in significant 
numbers around the world.

Whether Bangladesh can move past these setbacks and begin reaping the rewards 
of cheap, clean electricity will be of great interest to the next wave of 
potential nuclear adopters.

They will be watching closely next month when Rooppur’s first reactor is set 
to be connected to the grid.

—Rajesh Kumar Singh, Bloomberg News
