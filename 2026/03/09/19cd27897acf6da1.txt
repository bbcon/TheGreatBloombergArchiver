Trump’s War in Iran Roils a Vital Hub of Globalization
Trump’s war in Iran is disrupting global trade more widely than energy markets

It’s still not clear what President Donald Trump’s end goal is for the 
now-spreading US-Israeli war against Iran. Or, crucially for theglobal economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-03/iran-war-oil-price-surge-put-global-economic-recovery-at-risk?cmpid=BBD030926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
, how long the conflict will last. But there is one lesson surfacing quickly: 
America First can’t escape an interconnected world.

Since the initial attacks on Tehran nine days ago, much of the attention has 
been focused on energy markets and crude prices that have seen their biggest 
spike since 2022’s Russian invasion of Ukraine.

With a de-facto closure 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-08/hormuz-tracker-iran-linked-ships-are-the-only-ones-to-transit?cmpid=BBD030926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
 of the Strait of Hormuz continuing and Gulf producers starting to curtail 
their output, it’s hard to foresee anyshort-term relief 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/-100-oil-shock-set-to-strain-asia-s-cash-strapped-governments?cmpid=BBD030926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
. Which is why Brent crude crossed through $100 a barrel on Monday and is now 
up almost 70% from where it began the year.

But did Trump think that decapitating the Iranian regime would lead to the 
interruption of as much as a fifth of global air freight, hit container traffic 
between Asia and Europe, result in a scramble for aluminum around the world, 
threaten the availability of fertilizer as far away as Australia and the UK, 
and interrupt shipments of medical aid to Africa?

That’s not going to be the end of the disturbances, of course. 

Dubai, the United Arab Emirates, and Gulf states writ large long ago 
established themselves as not just petro states or entrepôt economies but 
financial centers and luxury tourism destinations. The ambition now is to take 
that a step further and become artificial intelligence hubs with vast new data 
centers built with imported labor and leading-edge chips.

Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE are significant exporters of capital. 
Together they last year pledged $3.2 trillion in purchases of US planes, chips 
and investment in US projects. that’s about a third of the $9.9 trillion in 
pledged investment that Trump’s White Househails on its website 
<https://www.whitehouse.gov/investments/>.

Even if the fighting is over in a matter of days, it’s hard to imagine a world 
in which everything returns to a pre-war normal in a hurry, or at all. How soon 
will it be before European honeymooners again book trips to Dubai?

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/us-launches-20-billion-reinsurance-plan-to-ease-gulf-oil-trade?cmpid=BBD030926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>

That’s because the US-Israeli attack on longtime enemy Iran has quickly 
embroiled one of the hubs of the version of globalization that has survived a 
decade of America First. This is no longer a war about an Islamic regime. It’s 
yet another conflict that serves as a reminder of all the surprising ways in 
which the world’s economies are connected.

Tariff Turmoil

The theme for trade in 2025 was resilience. Both the US and global economies 
were able to survive Trump’s construction of the largest tariff wall around the 
US seen since the 1930s. Largely, it should be said, because Trump chopped and 
changed his plans, delayed many, and quickly set about excluding large 
categories of products including the imported chips and processing units going 
into the data centers going up as part of an AI-fueled boom.

But 2026 already looks different. We’ve moved beyond economic warfare to the 
real deal.

Read More: Three Ways the Iran War Could End 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-08/iran-war-three-ways-the-conflict-could-end?cmpid=BBD030926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>

If you add what people already are saying could turn into the biggest oil 
shock since the 1970s to the mix, that seems bad. Add on all the additional 
ways in which Gulf states now interact with the global economy than they did 50 
years ago and the comparison actually seems quaint.

You don’t have to dig deep to find someone arguing that the potential for 
economic catastrophe is what will in the end restrain Trump, Israel and Iran’s 
regime and bring a quick end to this war. The missile launches and drone 
strikes could indeed be over a week from now.

It already seems clear, though: Even if that happens economic scars will be 
left behind. Is anyone really arguing this war will lead tofaster growth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/china-consumer-prices-rebound-driven-by-record-holiday-spending?cmpid=BBD030926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260309>
 for the US in even the medium term? Or the global economy?
