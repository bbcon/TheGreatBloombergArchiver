The $112 Billion Gap Making It Hard to Gauge US-China Decoupling
A $112 billion gap in reported trade between the US and China makes it hard to 
gauge decoupling

Last year, there was a record $112 billion gap between what China reported 
exporting to the US and what the US Customs and Border Protection agency said 
arrived.

That discrepancy, according to reporting this week 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-24/china-s-112-billion-cargo-gap-shows-record-us-tariff-evasion?cmpid=BBD022526_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260225>
 from Bloomberg’s Laura Curtis and James Mayger, suggests that as much as a 
quarter of what Asia’s top economy shipped to American shores may have slipped 
under the tariff radar.

Among the reasons explored in the story: fraud.

US authorities are aware of suspected tariff evasion tactics, but are 
hamstrung by their reach and jurisdiction. Enforcement efforts are failing to 
keep pace, for instance, with shell companies that offer deep shipping 
discounts, undervalue the arriving cargo on customs declarations and then 
disappear overnight.

“Tariff cheating is much, much worse than tariffs for us,” said Michael 
Kersey, president of the American Lawn Mower Company. His company began 
outsourcing production to China two decades ago and paid duties of as much as 
45% to bring those goods into the US over the past year.

 * Here’s how the suspected fraud works: 
For US-China watchers, customs malfeasance is among the reasons it’s hard to 
get an accurate read on commerce flowing across the Pacific — which is key 
right now given the fragile state of trade relations between Beijing and 
Washington.

“It has been clear for some time that through a combination of fraud and until 
recently the de minimis exception, US imports from China were undercounted,” 
Brad Setser, a senior fellow at the Council on Foreign Relations, wrote in a
post on X <https://x.com/Brad_Setser/status/2026564180837277871>. “Which is one 
of many reasons why I am not a fan of the articles using the US import data to 
say the US and China have decoupled.”

Michael Pettis, a senior fellow at the Carnegie Endowment, also took note of 
Bloomberg’s reporting on the issue of undercounting trade between the world’s 
largest economies.

“It also suggests why a hodgepodge of sectoral and bilateral tariffs are less 
efficient than a simple tariff on all imports,” he wrote inan X post 
<https://x.com/michaelxpettis/status/2026541965089845302>. “While the former 
mostly shift trade around, the latter is equivalent to a currency devaluation 
for a country that doesn’t intervene in its currency.”

Charted Territory

Quantifying the gap | The scale of the tariff-evasion problem is staggering. 
Trade data released last week showed a record $112 billion gap between what 
China reported exporting to the US and what US Customs said actually arrived 
last year. ADecember report <> from Bloomberg Economics estimated that if the 
trade gap was caused by evasion, around $3.5 billion in tariff revenue had gone 
uncollected since February.
