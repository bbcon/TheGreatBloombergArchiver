A Yen for Intervention

Japan’s economic Great Normalization <https://bloom.bg/3QxI9Up> is hitting 
some perilous frontiers. That almost certainly means a rate hike from the Bank 
of Japan next week, and possibly another attempt at currency intervention by 
the Ministry of Finance — but it’s not clear that either would help the country 
return to normal.

Monday saw the 10-year Japanese government bond yield, deliberately held at 
zero for many years under the BOJ’s former policy ofyield curve control 
<https://bloom.bg/4vzbahj>, go back above 2.7%. The yen held above 160 per 
dollar, a level that has previously prompted the government to intervene:

These are copper-bottomed reasons for the BOJ to hike next week, which is now 
priced as a near-inevitability. But the bank is dragging its feet. At the 
year’s outset, traders expected a hike by now, and the projection for two hikes 
by year-end is unchanged:

A hike would make sense because the rise in short-term rates hasn’t come close 
to catching up either with other countries or with Japan’s own inflation. This 
is how target rates and 10-year inflation breakevens compare with the US:

In such circumstances, a hike won’t do much to shore up the yen. Mansoor 
Mohi-Uddin of Bank of Singapore argues that a sustained yen rebound “will 
require the BOJ to lift interest rates above inflation” to ensure positive 
returns for yen-buyers.

This is unlikely. Inflation appears to have returned, but the question is 
clouded by the recent price spike for rice, Japan’s staple food. That has died 
down after core inflation as usually expressed in Japan — excluding fuel and 
fresh food — rose above 4%. When all food was excluded, however, a more 
conventional version of core inflation never reached 3% and is now close to 
dropping back through 1%. It’s still not clear that Japan’s deflationary slump 
has been beaten:

Further, long yields are rising and failing to strengthen the currency — a 
combination familiar from emerging markets crises when a government’s ability 
to pay its debts comes into question. Japan has a huge overhang of debt, more 
than double gross domestic product, and the expansive policies of Prime 
Minister Sanae Takaichi could increase it. The BOJ’s continuing intervention in 
the bond market limits how far yields are rising.

“What would be a bond market crisis without the BOJ has morphed into a 
currency crisis,” argues Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution. “One way or 
another, Japan’s debt overhang is making itself felt.”

The signal Japan needs to send is that it recognizes debt is a problem. It can 
do that with a wave of privatizations and sales of domestic assets. In my view, 
markets would reward that with a stronger yen and lower yields, i.e. exactly 
what Japan needs. FX interventions signal exactly the opposite.

That’s unlikely, in part because a weak yen suits many people, even if it 
stokes social concerns by making it easier for foreigners (particularly 
Chinese) to gobble up Japanese assets. Jesper Koll, who publishes the Japan 
Optimist newsletter, thinks the yen is heading for 200 and points out that 
inflation is the “lazy policymakers’ best shot of getting down debt-to-GDP,” 
(so aggressive hikes look very unlikely).

Further, Koll argues that a weak yen boosts companies’ earnings — for every 10 
yen of depreciation, Japan Inc. gets a windfall of around 8% to listed 
companies’ earnings, he says, with approximately 65% of their earnings coming 
from either exporting or offshore production.

The equity market has calmed down since the burst of excitement around 
Takaichi’s landslide <https://bloom.bg/4v2gSZk> election victory in February. 
That’s in great part because of the Iran war’s effect on such a large energy 
importer, but it does suggest that a big rally for the yen at this stage could 
hurt stocks:

How the government deals with pressure on the yen will cut to the heart of the 
greatest issues with Takaichi <https://bloom.bg/3QcOEvN>. She’s prepared to 
force through reforms to revive the corporate sector and spend money on 
boosting the economy. But as a strong supporter of the late Premier Shinzo Abe, 
she’s also perceived to be tied to his policies of a weak yen and intervention 
to keep bond yields down.

Provided you can handle the currency risk, Japanese equities at the moment 
look very attractive — they’re nowhere near as expensive as the US even though, 
in local currency terms, their earnings have been growing faster over the last 
decade:

This is because the corporate governance reforms started by Abe are at last 
having a serious impact, which should have a major effect on both earnings and 
valuations. This chart is from the GMO fund management group:

GMO’s Rick Friedman and John Thorndike <https://bloom.bg/4e6AYu5> published a 
rallying call to stay invested:

Japan is not in the late innings of a trade. It is still in the middle innings 
of a long game, one that increasingly rewards selectivity, engagement, and 
active ownership rather than broad market exposure... The influence of reforms 
has gained momentum at the company level and does not depend on a benign macro 
or geopolitical environment. 

There is much to be said for this, and the quieting of interest in the 
Takaichi Trade <https://bloom.bg/4ofDlzr> in the last few months has created a 
new opening to get involved. But bargain-hunters may need to hedge the yen 
before they do so.

Keeping It Private

Private equity has a record-high pile of dry powder — cash waiting to be put 
to work. That might justify optimism about the global industry's outlook. But 
the metric, a useful gauge of investor appetite, tells only part of the story. 
Look at actual buyout activity, and artificial intelligence — the same force 
behind arecord proliferation of unicorns <https://bloom.bg/43rMzPB> — appears 
to be dampening any attempt to light it. Bain & Co. says technology is largely 
responsible for a 70% plunge in global deal value in the first quarter, with 
fewer large software deals completed:

Growing doubts about software valuations in the face of AI disruption might 
explain this. It’s certainly affected them in the public market. But there’s 
more to it, as these problems long predate the AI boom. 

Even before ChatGPT, firms were finding it harder to sell companies at a 
profit and return capital to investors. Then they were derailed by last year’s 
tariff brinkmanship under President Donald Trump, which weighed on raw 
materials costs, narrowed portfolio margins, and complicated exit strategies. 
Now, the Iran war, software disruption, and private credit’s problems are a 
trio of headwinds, according to Bain’s Rebecca Burack:

Amid the new bout of uncertainty, bid-ask spreads have widened, investment 
committees have pulled back, and exit momentum has stalled out. Select 
transactions continue to clear — and at high prices — but mostly those 
involving A-plus assets.

There’s little improvement in other measures of private-equity health. Holding 
periods for portfolio companies have stretched to six or seven years, up from 
three to four during the industry’s heyday, according to Burack. Those longer 
periods raise the bar for returns. PE firms once needed roughly 5% annual 
earnings growth over the life of an investment to generate a 2.5-times return. 
Now, that figure has climbed to about 12%.

The picture looks different from the perspective of deal volume. According to 
With Intelligence,AI-related deal activity <https://bloom.bg/4uHYAM9> nearly 
doubled between 2023 and 2025:

The plunge in deal values underscores skepticism about valuations in 
AI-dominated sectors. Investors fear overpaying for businesses whose models 
could soon be disrupted. Enthusiasm for AI as an investment opportunity is 
increasingly matched by a desire to mitigate its risks.

Ropes & Gray note that capital is increasingly gravitating 
<https://bloom.bg/43jHpFl> toward industries with more visible cash flows and 
lower disruption risk. Sectors benefiting from this rotation include 
professional services, financials and construction. Sponsors have also steadily 
raised their investment in outsourcing, compliance and infrastructure, which 
offer more predictable demand than sectors grappling with the software reset:

Geopolitical uncertainty is another headwind. KPMG’s Carole Streicher says 
tariffs, inflation and other sources of uncertainty are forcing a return to 
cautious underwriting. Dry powder is going toward high-conviction, top-tier 
transactions:

To capture these capital-intensive opportunities, firms are increasingly 
forming consortiums with sovereign wealth and institutional investors. These 
collaborative investments are highly thematic, targeting scarce, “must-own” 
assets where strategic importance matches financial returns.

Managers must also contend with pressure from investors who want liquidity, a 
challenge that is becoming harder to ignore. Last week,Partners Group Holding AG
 <https://bloom.bg/4vwJKss> reported a pickup in redemption requests across its 
evergreen funds from private-wealth clients. It capped withdrawals in one 
vehicle at 5% of net asset value per quarter after redemption requests surged 
to an estimated 9.8% in the second quarter.

This growing tension between long-term investments and demands for return of 
cash suggests that the turnaround has much further to go.

—Richard Abbey
