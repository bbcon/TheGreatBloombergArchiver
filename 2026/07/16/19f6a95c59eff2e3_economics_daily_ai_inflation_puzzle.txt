AI Puzzle

Federal Reserve Chairman Kevin Warsh’s testimony before Congress showcased 
lawmaker angst over artificial intelligence, including that it’s fueling 
inflation. Warshplayed down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/warsh-says-price-pressures-from-ai-not-necessarily-inflationary?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
 such concerns and hailed the coming boost to productivity.

Bloomberg Economics’s Andrew Sacher summed up Warsh’s message this week as 
follows: AI is inflationary before it will become disinflationary.

“Everything technology touches ultimately gets cheaper,” Warsh said, while 
acknowledging the economy hasn’t reached that stage just yet.

It’s an issue the Fed chair’s peers must also consider, and nowhere more so 
than at the epicenter of the AI boom right now: South Korea. As the biggest 
producer of the memory chips that make the technology possible, policymakers in 
Seoul are having to grapple with a massive yet unevenbonanza 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-07-03/how-the-ai-boom-is-carving-a-k-shape-into-asian-economies?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
, as this newsletter hasexplored 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-01/how-the-ai-boom-is-rewiring-asia-s-growth-model?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
.

The Bank of Korea Thursday looked to take some steam out of price pressures 
with its firstinterest-rate hike 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-16/bok-delivers-first-rate-hike-since-early-2023-amid-chip-led-boom?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
 since 2023. Governor Shin Hyun Song, in his press conference, cited the AI 
boom as a major driver of stronger growth — a textbook case of demand spilling 
over to broader prices. He also said he expects the weak won will continue 
feeding into inflation for some time, addressing a currency issue that has been 
a little harder to fathom.

In a note this week, Wei Yao and Michelle Lam at Societe Generale examined the 
puzzle of why South Korea and Taiwan’s historicexport surge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-29/taiwan-lifts-2026-growth-outlook-to-more-than-9-on-ai-hunger?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
 this year hasn’t boosted their exchange rates as you might normally expect.

In Korea’s case, much of the export earnings are getting recycled abroad via 
equity outflows as global investors sell, in part to avoid portfolio 
concentration in SK Hynix and Samsung Electronics. A similar dynamic is at work 
in Taiwan, where it’s insurers, banks and corporates that are buying foreign 
assets.

The SocGen economists also argue the “narrow transmission,” where 
capital-intensive sectors are generating lots of output but few jobs, helps 
explains why the AI boom hasn’t spurred an even faster uptick in consumer 
prices. For sustained long-term growth accompanied by stronger currencies, two 
conditions must be met, they wrote:

 * Proactive redistribution that spreads the windfall through wages, household 
transfers, public services and investment in non-tech areas
 * A shift in capital allocation that keeps more semiconductor wealth at home 
through domestic capex, pension allocation and deeper capital markets.
In South Korea’s case, there’s clearly an effort to meet both those 
conditions, as shown by recent plans to use windfall tax receipts to launch an
investment fund 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-05/south-korea-eyes-growth-fund-from-chip-tax-windfall-yonhap-says?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
 to fuel long-term growth and a massiveinvestment program 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-30/lee-s-880-billion-ai-bet-ties-legacy-to-south-korea-chip-boom?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
 to transform the nation’s less developed southwest into a global chip hub.

A key question for BOK’s Shin — as for Warsh — is just how inflationary all 
that investment will prove to be, and how quickly the productivity payoff will 
arrive.

Need-to-Know Research

Ahead of the US Treasury’s overdue semiannual report on the foreign-exchange 
practices of American trading partners, a former veteran of the department 
argues that there could be grounds for tagging China as amanipulator of its 
currency 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/us-calls-on-china-to-strengthen-substantially-undervalued-yuan?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
.

Not only does China run outsize trade and current-account surpluses, but 
activity by the nation’s state-owned banks over the past 12 months “points to 
significant intervention to curb renminbi appreciation,” Mark Sobel, chief 
economist at the Official Monetary and Financial Institutions Forum,wrote in a 
note 
<https://www.omfif.org/2026/07/us-would-be-unwise-to-designate-china-currency-manipulators/>
 Wednesday. That said, a manipulator designation would be “unwise.”

For one thing, markets largely shrugged off past Treasury manipulator labels, 
he said. Moving ahead now could complicate an upcomingUS-China summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/xi-gives-trump-an-easy-win-smoothing-path-for-next-summit?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
, Sobel added. A pragmatic option is for the US to “work with Europe and the G7 
to press for faster renminbi appreciation,” he said, echoing a recent call by
two US senators 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/bessent-urged-by-us-senators-to-push-china-on-undervalued-yuan?cmpid=BBD071626_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260716&utm_content=6561>
. What’s unclear is whether other nations are willing to trust “locking arms” 
with Washington, and whether Beijing will even care.
