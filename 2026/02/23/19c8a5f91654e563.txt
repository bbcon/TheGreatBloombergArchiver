Trump’s New Tariffs Face Their Own Legal Questions
After a Supreme Court loss, Trump launches new tariffs that legal experts are 
already questioning

<https://sli.bloomberg.com/click?s=1149074&li=17008973&m=11b724c487f61753eb1a7a4a5f3ef261&p=02232026&lctg=6689f0ec-2ca1-4687-9972-bdcb0c2dfbdf&stpe=default>
It took just hours after the Supreme Court on Friday invalidated most of 
President Donald Trump’s tariffs for him to sign an executive proclamation 
replacing them with new 10% global duties for 150 days. And it took just hours 
after that for an angry Trump to turn to social media to threaten to increase 
that to 15% over the weekend.

All was back to Trump normal in other words. Which certainly is what his 
administration has been working hard toportray 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/us-bilateral-trade-deals-are-still-on-track-greer-says?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
. Whether it comes to raising revenues or the state of the tentative or fragile 
trade deals struck with other countries, Trump’s team insists all is well 
despite the court’s ruling and evidence otherwise emerging from Europe andIndia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/india-postpones-us-trip-to-finalize-trade-pact-on-tariff-ruling?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
.

Read More: EU Set to Halt US Trade Deal Approval Over Trump Tariff Risk 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/eu-set-to-freeze-us-trade-deal-approval-over-trump-tariff-risk?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>

The Trump administration has also been eliding over just what the statute 
Trump is invoking to justify his replacement duties means. Which really ought 
to be getting more attention and, as wewrote 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/trump-pegs-new-tariffs-to-a-payments-crisis-experts-doubt-exists?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 Sunday, is prompting questions about whether these new temporary tariffs might 
also one day be declared illegal as well.

Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 allows US presidents to impose duties for 
up to 150 days “in situations of fundamental international payments problems.” 
Those include “large and serious United States balance-of-payments deficits” 
and an “imminent and significant depreciation of the dollar.”

The thing is that many if not most economists would argue that while the US 
may be facing “large” trade and current-account deficits it’s not clear that 
they are “serious” given the world’s willingness to finance them. And that 
therefore the US is facing the “fundamental international payments problem” 
that Trump just declared to justify his tariffs.

There’s a clue in how financial markets have responded 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-22/dollar-trades-within-range-amid-tariff-uncertainty-markets-wrap?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>
 to Trump’s use of the law.

As Jay Shambaugh, who occupied the top international job at the US Treasury in 
the Biden administration, told us, if the US was facing a balance of payments 
crisis the dollar would be collapsing. Foreign investors would be selling off 
American assets aggressively for fear that the US could not meet its 
obligations.

But that’s not happening. Which implies markets don’t believe the US is facing 
the crisis Trump says it is.

IMF Monitoring

The international arbiter of these things is supposed to be the International 
Monetary Fund, which fortuitously will be weighing in Thursday when officials 
hold their annual press conference previewing their report on the state of the 
American economy.

A preview of that came on Sunday when Gita Gopinath, the IMF’s former first 
deputy managing director and chief economist,wrote 
<https://x.com/GitaGopinath/status/2025605225248108776?s=20> in a social media 
post that: “Wearing my (former) IMF hat I will say that the US does not have a 
fundamental international payments problem.”

Read More: Bitcoin Falls Below $65,000 in Latest Bout of Tariff Uncertainty 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/bitcoin-btc-slides-to-65-000-in-latest-bout-of-tariff-uncertainty?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>

Some trade experts argue 
<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/dawnmshackleford_the-imf-may-want-to-sharpen-their-pencils-activity-7431006658732093440-4NIy?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_desktop&rcm=ACoAAAEgwXkBRpDq-S7mFK3V4SYp3uJxOEpKIxI>
 that Trump’s very invocation of a balance-of-payments crisis to impose tariffs 
could lead the US or other countries to report the measures to the World Trade 
Organization. That, in turn, could lead to the IMF getting involved and being 
asked by the WTO to adjudicate whether the US faces a crisis that justifies the 
use of tariffs.

There are economists who argue that the Trump administration is partially 
justified in invoking the Section 122 provision.

Brad Setser, a former US Treasury and trade official now at the Council on 
Foreign Relations, said the US’s current-account deficit now around 3%-4% of 
gross domestic product was significant enough to merit the “large and serious” 
definition.

Legal Questions

But whether the US faced “‘fundamental international payments problems’ is a 
harder question,” he wrote in aseries 
<https://x.com/Brad_Setser/status/2025645191084798460?s=20> of social media 
posts Sunday. “The deficit is big,” Setser said. But portfolio inflows into the 
US in 2025 remained strong enough to fund it, he said, “and the dollar is 
currently quite strong.”

That may end up being more than just an issue for economists to debate. 
There’s already rumblings of a legal challenge to these new tariffs. Though 
Jennifer Hillman, a former top US government trade lawyer and judge now at 
Georgetown University’s law school, said such a case would be less clear-cut 
than the challenge Trump lost on Friday.

Read More: Supreme Court Draws a Line With Trump by Curbing Tariff Powers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/supreme-court-tariff-ruling-shows-willingness-to-push-back-on-trump?cmpid=BBD022326_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260223>

Then again Neal Katyal, the prominent lawyer who presented the argument 
against Trump’s global tariffs before the Supreme Court,pointed out 
<https://x.com/neal_katyal/status/2025251260840976418?s=20> over the weekend 
that Trump’s own lawyers argued in that case that Section 122 wasn’t 
appropriate.

In a legal filing last year they wrote that Section 122 didn’t “have any 
obvious application here, where the concerns the president identified in 
declaring an emergency arise from trade deficits, which are conceptually 
distinct from balance-of-payments deficits.”

Of course the reality is that we live in a different norms-busting world now. 
One in which it doesn’t seem to matter if the man in charge of the world’s 
largest economy declares that it is facing what in any other context would 
treated as a monumental crisis by global investors.

The truth, as Setser said Sunday, is that courts will probably move too slowly 
to restrain Trump. These new tariffs may be gone in 150 days and by then courts 
will likely only just be considering the issues they raise.
