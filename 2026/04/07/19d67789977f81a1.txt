‘Coiled spring’

Donald Trump’s latest deadline for Iran to agree to a deal is just hours away, 
and investors are once againstruggling to see the path forward for markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/traders-position-for-another-trump-deadline-as-frustration-grows?cmpid=040726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
.

It’s a scene that’s been replayed many times in recent weeks as Trump 
repeatedly issued ultimatums before granting Iran more time, sparking market 
swings that have left traders scrambling to keep up. Gauges of volatility in 
equities and currencies have hit multi-month highs as the clock ticks down to 
his latest deadline of 8 p.m. New York time.

“We just hope the next 24 hours see sense prevail,” said Gary Dugan at Global 
CIO Office. He’s lightening up on equity bets and buying oil through 
exchange-traded funds.

For now, investors overall are leaning slightly toward optimism that an 
escalation in the war will be avoided.An MSCI index of Asian stocks gained 
almost 1% overnight, oil is little changed and the dollar — the main haven 
since the war broke out — is ticking lower.

“The asymmetry is to the upside,” said Thomas Hayes at Great Hill Capital in 
New York, speaking of US stocks. “If we get a resolution or cease fire, this 
market is a coiled spring with about 10% aggressive upside.” —Ruth Carson 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AS35-mFs7Fw/ruth-carson>, Abhishek Vishnoi 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASxY3iVjhAY/abhishek-vishnoi>, and Masaki

Europe losing its edge

It took years to lure investors back to European stocks. Making them stay is 
starting to be a challenge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/european-stocks-are-losing-an-edge-that-took-them-years-to-gain?cmpid=040726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260407>
.

The European market is still beating the US this year, but the tide is turning 
as the Iran war hits stocks harder than their US peers.

Part of the problem for Europe is the outlook for earnings. Estimates had been 
trending higher, spurred by faith that fiscal spending and lower interest rates 
were on the way. Now, though, earnings momentum has tilted in favor of the US, 
with a gauge of estimate revisions in positive territory, in contrast to 
Europe, where analysts are marking down their predictions.

Bottom line: The surge in oil prices is weighing on the region’s economy, and 
the European Central Bank’s next move is likely be an increase in interest 
rates, potentially as early as this month. That’s a recipe for underperformance 
in the stock market. —Michael Msika 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AP7hp2DanV8/michael-msika>
