US equity markets are sustaining record highs even as the underlying economic picture grows increasingly bifurcated. The S&P 500 remains near all-time highs with volatility subdued, supported by a resurgence in the AI trade following strong earnings from Alphabet and other large-cap technology companies. That resilience is proving a strategic asset in the standoff over the Strait of Hormuz: financial conditions remain accommodative, undermining Iran's central weapon of economic coercion. However, the calm at the index level obscures genuine stress at the household level. Gas prices have exceeded $4 per gallon, and Pantheon Macro estimates the energy shock could translate into roughly a 1% annualized decline in real after-tax income in Q2. With the savings rate already at 3.9%, the buffer for lower-income households is thin. Bank of America estimates the full-year "gas tax" amounts to roughly 0.5% of GDP — approximately equal to the projected boost from either the One Big Beautiful Bill or AI capital expenditure.

The K-shaped character of US consumption is becoming more pronounced. New York Fed data show retail spending concentrated among households earning above $125,000 annually, while consumer confidence has fallen to fresh lows. Spirit Airlines ceased operations, a pointed illustration of the energy shock's asymmetric impact on businesses serving lower-income consumers. The temporary income boost from tax refunds is now fading, leaving that cohort increasingly exposed.

On the corporate front, GameStop has proposed a $56 billion all-cash-and-stock acquisition of eBay, offering a roughly 20% premium to Friday's close — a striking bid from a company with a $12 billion market capitalization. Separately, data-center startup Fermi ousted its CEO after failing to sign a single client despite a $19 billion IPO valuation, serving as a cautionary reminder that AI capital formation enthusiasm does not guarantee commercial execution. The April nonfarm payrolls report, due Friday, is expected to show approximately 62,000–65,000 jobs added, with the labor market yet to register the energy shock's full impact.
