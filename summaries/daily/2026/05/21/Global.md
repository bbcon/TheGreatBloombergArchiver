# Global Daily Briefing — May 21, 2026

**Oil, Hormuz, and the Diplomatic Track.** Crude prices remained the central macro variable, with Brent trading near $106 after a 5.6% decline Wednesday on hints of progress in US-Iran talks. President Trump told reporters Washington was in the "final stages" with Tehran, and Iran's Foreign Ministry confirmed it was reviewing the US response to its 14-point proposal, which envisions a short-term arrangement reopening the Strait of Hormuz in exchange for lifting the blockade on Iranian ports, followed by deeper nuclear negotiations. The diplomatic signals, however, sit alongside continued physical disruption: Goldman Sachs estimates visible global crude and product inventories are drawing down at a record 8.7 million barrels per day in May, nearly double the conflict's running average. Bloomberg Intelligence's latest survey shows market participants increasingly converging on oil capped near $100 over the next year, implying demand destruction rather than supply restoration does the rebalancing work. A resolution remains far from certain, and reports that Iran's supreme leader directed retention of near-weapons-grade uranium underscore the gap still to bridge.

**Monetary Policy Divergence Widens.** The Fed minutes revealed a markedly more hawkish FOMC than previously understood, with "almost all" participants expecting persistent inflation pressure from elevated energy prices and "many" preferring to drop the easing bias entirely. Futures now imply fed funds above 4% next summer, versus 2.8% pricing pre-conflict. Kevin Warsh, sworn in Friday as Fed Chair, inherits a committee whose consensus has shifted beneath him; the realistic ceiling on his dovishness appears to be holding rates rather than cutting. Bank Indonesia, by contrast, was forced into a surprise 50bp hike as the rupiah traded near record lows, compounded by President Prabowo's announcement that palm oil, coal, and nickel exports will be routed through a state entity under sovereign wealth fund Danantara — a move that blindsided even senior officials and sent the Jakarta Composite down 26% year-to-date. The Reserve Bank of India is reportedly weighing similar measures, including a rate hike, after the rupee touched nearly 97 per dollar.

**Asia's AI Cushion.** Korean and Japanese trade data suggest the semiconductor complex is partially offsetting the energy shock for net importers, with Korean chip exports up 202% year-on-year in the first 20 days of May and Japan's trade balance holding in surplus despite a 64% collapse in crude import volumes. Oxford Economics frames the resulting external surpluses as a narrower echo of the 2000s Asian savings glut, with capital recycling indirectly supporting US asset prices even as Treasury yields press higher.
