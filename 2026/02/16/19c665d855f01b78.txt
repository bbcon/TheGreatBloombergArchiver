Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
senior climate and energy reporter Ewa Krukowska examines what’s next for 
Europe’s flagship climate tool — its market for trading carbon emissions.

The European Union’s carbon market is facing its biggest political test in 
years, as some governments openly debate weakening the system and industry 
pushes for relief.

Carbon permits fell to the lowest since July 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-12/eu-leaders-split-on-carbon-price-in-push-to-bolster-industry?cmpid=BBBXT021626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260216>
 last week on concern that leaders may push to curb costs for heavy industry — 
a prospect that has rattled clean-energy investors who rely on rising emissions 
prices to justify long-term spending.

The issue has reached the highest political echelons. At a summit last week, 
EU leaders clashed over how far to go in revising the Emissions Trading System. 
European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen pledged to come up with a 
reform proposal, currently planned for the third quarter.

Governments are divided on how deep the reforms should be. 

While Slovak Prime Minister Robert Fico has called for a suspension 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-20/slovakia-s-fico-calls-to-suspend-europe-s-flagship-carbon-market?cmpid=BBBXT021626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260216>
 of the ETS, French President Emmanuel Macron defended the program as an 
important tool to drive investment in innovative technologies.

German Chancellor Friedrich Merz said the EU should be open to a revision or 
delay 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/merz-says-eu-should-be-open-to-revising-carbon-market?cmpid=BBBXT021626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260216>
 of the market, before softening his tone to clarify he supported the program.

The differing positions of member states have strengthened expectations that 
price volatility will remain elevated in the months to come.

“Ultimately, this current lack of visibility negatively impacts companies’ 
ability to plan long-term green capital expenditures,” analysts at JPMorgan 
Chase & Co. wrote in a research note last week.

When it was started two decades ago, the EU ETS imposed gradually shrinking 
quotas of tradable emissions permits on companies, rewarding those that cut 
pollution faster. Modeled after a US effort to control acid rain, it helped the 
EU overcome a decade-long stalemate on a carbon tax.

At the same time, it left price discovery in the hands of the market, with 
policymakers given limited tools to intervene.

But as rising energy and carbon costs play an increasingly important role in 
electoral campaigns, governments are becoming wary of backlash from voters and 
more willing to introduce fresh price controls when emission rules are revised. 
This time it is set to be no different.

—Ewa Krukowska, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Lithium-ion battery exports from five Southeast Asian countries fell by 14% to 
31 gigawatt-hours in the first 11 months of 2025 compared with the same period 
in 2024, but still vastly exceeded those in any previous year, according to
BloombergNEF <bbg://news/stories/TAJPWLT96OSI>. Indonesia, Malaysia and 
Thailand are emerging as manufacturing hubs for such batteries as their 
production capacity expands. Vietnam was particularly affected by US import 
tariffs, with its exports falling 49% year-on-year through November.
