China's annual National People's Congress dominated regional attention this week, with Premier Li Qiang delivering a government work report that set the growth target at its lowest level since 1991. While the headline figure signals diminished ambition, Chinese officials framed the target as more credible and achievable than prior years, reflecting a deliberate pivot toward "quality" growth and deflation elimination — officials made their strongest commitment yet to ending deflationary conditions in 2026. Defense spending rose 7%, the slowest pace since 2022. The work report's characterization of US-China trade relations was notably upbeat, suggesting Beijing's leadership enters the upcoming Trump visit in Beijing with confidence in their negotiating position. Preparatory meetings between He Lifeng, Scott Bessent, and Jamieson Greer are expected in Paris next week, with potential deliverables including Boeing aircraft purchases, soybean commitments, and the fentanyl tariff framework.

The Iran conflict's regional spillover is generating significant stress in Asian markets. Global funds sold a net $11 billion of emerging Asian equities excluding China this week — the largest outflow since March 2022 — with Taiwan absorbing $7.9 billion in withdrawals, South Korea $1.6 billion, and India approximately $1.3 billion. The US Treasury's temporary waiver allowing Indian refiners to resume Russian oil purchases provided some relief to New Delhi, though it simultaneously highlighted India's strategic exposure: a US submarine sank the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka after it had participated in an Indian Navy-hosted exercise, placing Modi in a diplomatically untenable position between Washington and Tehran.

On China-Taiwan dynamics, Beijing has not dispatched military aircraft near Taiwan for seven consecutive days — the longest such absence since Taiwan began regular disclosure of PLA activity — a development whose significance remains unclear. Meanwhile, three Filipino nationals were detained for allegedly leaking sensitive South China Sea operational data to Beijing, heightening regional security concerns. The Bank of Japan remains on a rate-hiking path, with a former official placing April move odds at 50/50, even as the Iran conflict injects additional uncertainty into the global outlook.
