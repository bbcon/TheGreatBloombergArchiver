**Iran and Strait of Hormuz**

The unresolved question of Iran's political trajectory following the US-Iran conflict is the dominant geopolitical variable for global energy markets this week. Bloomberg's framing — whether the post-conflict phase produces a strongman successor, a coup dynamic, or state fragmentation — captures the genuine uncertainty in the scenario distribution, each branch carrying materially different implications for Strait of Hormuz transit security. A consolidated successor regime would likely allow risk premia embedded in crude prices and tanker insurance rates to compress further, reinforcing the disinflationary tailwind that currently underpins DM rate-cut pricing. A fragmented or contested transition, by contrast, would sustain elevated shipping costs, keep Brent term structure in backwardation, and complicate the ECB's and Fed's ability to declare the energy price channel closed. Markets appear to be pricing a muddle-through outcome, but positioning remains light — a configuration that historically amplifies gap risk on Sunday futures opens and at the start of Asian sessions. The transmission mechanism from Hormuz disruption to global inflation is well understood and would operate with relatively short lags, making this the clearest single tail risk to the soft-landing narrative currently embedded in consensus forecasts.

**China Surplus and Global Liquidity**

The $1.2 trillion in Chinese trade surplus proceeds flowing into offshore assets rather than returning onshore represents one of the more consequential but least visible structural forces in global markets. As discussed in the Asia section, the dynamic reflects persistent negative CNY expectations and PBoC tolerance for gradual depreciation, but the global asset price implications are equally significant. Offshore Chinese capital directed into gold, DM sovereign bonds, and selected risk assets has contributed at the margin to the compression of term premia that has allowed equity multiples to remain elevated despite tighter real rates. A reversal of this flow — triggered by a shift in CNY expectations, tighter capital account management, or a meaningful improvement in onshore return prospects — would represent a non-trivial negative impulse for DM risk assets that does not appear to be priced. Attribution at the $1.2 trillion scale is inherently imprecise, and the flow composition across asset classes is opaque, but the directional implication warrants a place in global portfolio risk assessment.

**Central Bank Divergence and US Fed Risk**

The monetary policy divergence theme that entered 2026 as a central organizing narrative for global rates and FX markets was not materially advanced by this week's thin flow, but neither was it resolved. The Fed's 2026 path remains the anchor uncertainty: the absence of fresh FOMC communication, combined with recurring references to "turbulent forces reshaping the Fed," leaves the market reliant on December projections and incoming data. The ECB's easing trajectory, the BOJ's normalization pace, and the BoE's February decision all hinge in part on how US rates volatility is transmitted into European and Japanese duration markets — a transmission that runs through both fundamental channels and positioning dynamics. Any upward surprise in US inflation data, or any escalation in Hormuz-driven energy costs, would simultaneously complicate all three central banks' trajectories in ways that current consensus projections do not fully reflect. The week ends with this core uncertainty intact.
