Just weeks before a set of stopgap tariffs expire, the Trump administration is 
close to finalizing a new set of duties it says are intended to stamp out goods 
made with forced labor from the global economy.

This week, a USTR panel has been hearing testimony from dozens of foreign 
government officials, business owners and industry groups about the 
administration’s plan to impose 10% or 12.5% duties on imported goods from 60 
trading partners under Section 301 of the Trade Act of 1974.

The proposal is based on a report that found the targeted countries were 
either ineffective at enforcing bans on goods made with forced labor from 
entering their economies, or that they had no rules in place to prevent it.

But in a letter to US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer, Senator Ron Wyden 
of Oregon called the proposal “ill-designed to accomplish a legitimate 
objective,” and said the forced labor action “is merely an effort to 
reconstruct President Trump’s global tariff regime after it was invalidated by 
the Supreme Court.”

Wyden, the top Democrat on the Senate Finance Committee, called it a pretext 
for imposing broad tariffs that would raise costs for Americans and urged the 
administration to instead focus on better enforcement and investment in 
programs to combat forced labor where it happens.

US businesses and consumers have paid $317 billion in extra tariffs on US 
imports since March 2025, according to We Pay the Tariffs. That figure includes 
the emergency tariffs the administration is now in the process of refunding.

Of the $22 billion collected by the government in May, an estimated $9.2 
billion came from the temporary Section 122 tariffs, another $7.4 billion from 
Section 232 tariffs, and $2.4 billion was from Section 301 tariffs, according 
to data released by the coalition of small businesses on Wednesday.

“Rather than a targeted approach, USTR has proposed that some of the worst 
offenders for force labor receive the same rate under this proposed action as 
countries that have long been cooperative partners for the United States,” 
Wyden wrote in the letter, which was seen by Bloomberg News before its release.

Wyden added that President Donald Trump’s funding cuts have hobbled the Labor 
Department’s Bureau of International Labor Affairs and other agencies’ efforts 
to combat forced labor around the world, level the playing field for American 
workers, while ceding influence to China at the International Labor 
Organization.

More coming

Greer said the agency can move quickly to finalize forced labor tariffs, given 
the report has been released and requisite hearings are underway. “That’s 
something that I think we can come to a conclusion on fairly quickly,” he said 
in an interview with Bloomberg News last week.

The forced labor tariffs are only one of several investigations in the works, 
and Greer said he’s expecting the results of a separate inquiry into the 
structural excess capacity of over a dozen major US trading partners to come 
“in a matter of weeks, not months.”

“There’s a big fat draft sitting right there,” he said, pointing to his desk. 
“That report’s in draft. It’s quite lengthy, as you can see.”
