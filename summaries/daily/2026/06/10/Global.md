**Oil Markets and the Hormuz Risk Premium**
The overnight exchange of strikes between US forces and Iran represents a material escalation from recent weeks. While Centcom described the operation as a "proportional response" suggesting Washington is not seeking full-scale resumption of hostilities, Iran's retaliatory strikes on US regional targets indicate the conflict retains escalatory momentum. Crude prices are elevated but have not surged in a disorderly fashion, partly because China is drawing on commercial reserves rather than competing aggressively for spot cargoes — a stabilizing factor the IEA has acknowledged. The risk to global supply remains asymmetric: any disruption to Hormuz transit, through which approximately 20% of global oil flows, would constitute a supply shock of a different order of magnitude.

**Climate and Macro Tail Risks**
El Niño's emergence introduces a material cross-regional risk to agricultural output, energy demand, and inflation trajectories at a moment when central banks in multiple economies are already navigating supply-side price pressures. The 1997 episode caused an estimated $100 billion in damages and 30,000 deaths; the Japan Meteorological Agency's assessment that this event could be among the strongest on record warrants close monitoring. Separately, solar surpassed coal in US power generation for the first time in a calendar month in May — a structurally significant milestone, though the trend's durability depends on grid investment and regulatory continuity.
