Asian markets were broadly weaker Friday, with the MSCI Asia benchmark off 0.4% and the Hang Seng declining 0.9%, as investors awaited clarity on the war's trajectory over the weekend. The most consequential regional development came from China, where the deflationary outlook is shifting materially. With Brent crude above $100 per barrel, economists at Citigroup, Goldman Sachs, and Macquarie now project that Chinese producer prices could turn positive as soon as March, potentially ending a deflationary streak that began in 2022 — one of the longest experienced by any major economy since World War II. The key uncertainty is whether the resulting inflation reflects genuine demand recovery or purely cost-push dynamics; the latter would offer companies little pricing power and may compress margins further rather than restore them.

Alibaba and Tencent combined lost approximately $66 billion in market value over roughly 24 hours after results from both companies disappointed investors seeking a clear path to AI monetization. Alibaba's quarterly net income fell 67%, its worst performance since early 2024, partly reflecting heavy promotional spending. Xiaomi fell 8.6% in Hong Kong on concerns about EV profitability. Meanwhile, Nvidia CEO Jensen Huang confirmed the company is ramping production of H200 AI accelerators for Chinese customers following the Trump administration's reversal on tech export controls — a meaningful shift that reflects the broader improvement in US-China relations.

The Trump-Xi summit, originally planned for later this month, has been postponed to approximately mid-May. Beijing appears quietly relieved, given concerns that hosting Trump while the Iran war continues could be construed as implicit endorsement of US military action against a Chinese partner. The trade truce remains intact, and a Paris meeting between senior officials last weekend appeared constructive. However, a pending $14 billion US arms package for Taiwan — potentially one of the largest ever — represents a significant latent irritant.

India's rupee weakened past 93 per dollar to a record low, reflecting vulnerability to elevated crude prices given the country imports approximately 90% of its oil. Nomura projects the currency could reach 96 per dollar by end-June. The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a split-decision rate hike, citing the inflation risks from the energy shock.
