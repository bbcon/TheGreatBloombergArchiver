Financial markets entered the week's final session under pressure from a confluence of geopolitical and monetary policy concerns. S&P 500 futures traded 0.44% lower as of early Friday, with WTI crude at $84.46 — up over 4% on the day — deepening concerns about the inflationary trajectory. The setup is politically awkward for the Trump administration: prior to the Iran conflict, the president appeared to be achieving his stated market preferences of lower oil, lower yields, and a weaker dollar. All three have now reversed. The dollar has reclaimed safe-haven status, Treasury yields edged higher to 4.163% on the 10-year, and gasoline pump prices reached their highest level under Trump's presidency at $3.32 per gallon.

The Federal Reserve faces a familiar dilemma. With Jerome Powell at the helm, the institution's institutional instinct appears to be patience — the same posture it adopted during the tariff-driven inflation episode last year. Bloomberg Economics' Anna Wong argues the oil shock will create an "asymmetric easing bias": near-term headline inflation rises, but consumer spending on non-energy goods contracts, ultimately pushing unemployment higher. For now, hawkish voices appear to be prevailing within the FOMC. The monthly US jobs report released Friday provided an additional data point for policymakers to assess, with a stronger reading potentially further deferring rate cut expectations.

On the fiscal side, the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs continues to reverberate. The Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget estimates the ruling reduces government revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036. The administration's replacement 10% levy would recoup approximately $925 billion, leaving a meaningful gap even if escalated to 15%. Federal debt accumulation continues on any modeled trajectory. On the corporate side, Marvell Technology surged 12% in premarket trading on accelerating data center demand, while Gap fell 6% on a disappointing Old Navy performance. Trump also dismissed Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem, naming Oklahoma Senator Markwayne Mullin as her replacement, raising questions about FEMA's leadership continuity ahead of the spring disaster season.
