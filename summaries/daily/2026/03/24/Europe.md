Eurozone economic data released Tuesday provided the first systematic evidence of the Iran war's impact on European activity. The S&P Global Composite PMI fell to 50.5 in March from 51.9 in February — the slowest expansion since May 2025 — as energy-driven cost pressures and supply chain disruption weighed on both manufacturing and services. Input prices rose at the fastest pace since February 2023. Germany's composite reading declined more than expected, while France recorded a third consecutive month of contraction in the PMI services component. The data also showed the largest deterioration in future output expectations since Russia's invasion of Ukraine four years ago — a signal that corporate confidence is weakening ahead of any potential resolution.

The United Kingdom fared similarly. British businesses recorded near-stagnation in March, with factory input costs rising at the fastest pace in more than three decades as the conflict disrupted energy and supply chains. The Bank of England faces a familiar dilemma: deteriorating growth alongside a renewed inflation impulse driven by external commodity shocks.

ECB Governing Council member Boris Vujcic — set to become ECB vice president in June — struck a notably cautious tone, warning that the risk of stagflation is increasing and that the Bank must remain "very agile and vigilant." He declined to provide definitive guidance on rates, acknowledging that "how far we'll get in that direction is very difficult to predict."

On the political front, Italian Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni suffered a referendum defeat on judicial reform — a self-inflicted setback that has damaged her aura of invincibility ahead of a general election roughly a year away. Simultaneously, Denmark held a general election shaped heavily by concerns over Trump's Greenland threats, with polling pointing toward a vote for stability. The EU and Australia concluded a long-pending free trade agreement, with European Commission President von der Leyen taking a pointed implicit swipe at US tariff policy and Chinese critical minerals restrictions in her remarks.
