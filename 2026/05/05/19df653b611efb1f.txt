Fire in the Strait

The normal cycles of investing, finance and the economy seem almost 
inoperative. They’re overwhelmed by two huge shocks: the money pouring into 
making artificial intelligence a reality, and thehit to global oil supply 
<https://bloom.bg/4nfgxzy> from war in Iran. The former is somehow still 
outweighing the latter to leave US stocks near all-time highs.

That doesn’t make them impervious to the latest news from the Strait of 
Hormuz. President Donald Trump is offering safe passage to “neutral” ships as 
he tries toloosen Iran’s hold over the waterway <https://bloom.bg/4n6CujU>. 
That led to an exchange of fire and an Iranian missile attack on the United 
Arab Emirates.

Analysts tended to dismiss Iran’s retaliation as perfunctory, but the incident 
makes clear that the month-old ceasefire is fraying. As Tina Fordham of Fordham 
Global Foresight put it:

By launching renewed missile attacks today, Iran is signalling that they still 
have the capacity to inflict pain and won’t be forced into capitulation. The US 
increasingly faces a choice between a long war it doesn’t want to fight, or a 
bad, embarrassing deal.

This feeds into the oil price. Futures now put Brent crude for the end of this 
year above $90. That’s its highest since the conflict started, taking out the 
previous peak set when Iran attacked aliquefied natural gas facility in Qatar 
<https://bloom.bg/4cQxJYn>:

Any escalation that permanently removed regional oil infrastructure would be 
deadly for the market. Ditto any sign that the impasse will continue. Not 
coincidentally, prediction markets now give less than 50-50 odds that traffic 
will be back to normal by the end of June. Two weeks ago, this was a 90% shot:

This oil shock looks ever more like its 2022 predecessor, driven by Russia’s 
invasion of Ukraine. As both conflicts erupted at the end of February, 
comparisons are easy. This is how December crude prices for 2022 and 2026 
moved, starting from November of the previous year:

That Ukraine shock overlapped with a major stock selloff, but fell during the 
major resurgence ofinflation after the pandemic <https://bloom.bg/4wbYE8F>, so 
its use as a comparison is limited. That said, the current standoff has forced 
inflation expectations up, with two-year inflation swaps now above the Federal 
Reserve’s upper target of 3%, and at their highest since 2022:

With midterm elections due, that puts pressure on the US to make a deal. The 
logic points to a resolution like the nuclear agreement done by President 
Barack Obama (known as the JCPOA) from which Trump pointedly withdrew in 2018. 
He’s understandably reluctant to accept such an outcome. Viktor Shvets of 
Macquarie argues:

Our base case: The ultimate outcome of the war will involve returning to 
something similar to the ’15 JCPOA agreement, with Iran containing its nuclear 
program in exchange for opening flow of energy. While not a win for either 
side, it is not clear whether there are any other even modestly rational 
answers.

If there is anything positive, it’s that December oil prices couldn’t be so 
high unless markets were confident that the world economy would survive intact, 
and keep demanding oil. Marko Papic of BCA Research argues:

The alarmist view has been proven incorrect. We can muddle through. There’s no 
recession around the corner. And that unfortunately means there’s no pressure 
on either side to come to a deal.

Cash Dethroned

The euphoria <https://bloom.bg/4f1ohTq> is right back to the first excitement 
over ChatGPT in 2022 — except that this time, earnings are doing the heavy 
lifting rather than promise. Last week indicated that investors are indeed 
willing to tolerate the immense costs of AI data-center buildouts, provided 
there’s reason to believe that it can lead to profitability.

The fact that this latest rally coincides with the Iran war and a deepening 
energy shock makes the resilience all the more striking. Société Générale’s 
Andrew Lapthorne points out that were it not for the war in Iran and the very 
real prospect of major supply chain disruptions, investors would be celebrating 
booming profits. The Magnificent Seven tech platform groups, with only Nvidia 
Corp. left to report, havedelivered robust earnings <https://bloom.bg/3QOVsQa> 
that are propping up the broader markets.

If the capex boom is responsible for recent earnings strength, then it is 
worth asking what that performance is actually costing and whether it’s 
sustainable. Peter Berezin of BCA Research argues that earnings are benefiting 
from accounting rules. Thelack of computing capacity <https://bloom.bg/4tz41wl> 
that’s bidding up the prices of GPUs and memory chips is naturally bolstering 
the profits of companies that sell these items, as well as power generation 
companies:

For the most part, however, the companies that buy this equipment are not 
incurring larger losses. That is because most of these purchases are treated as 
capex. The net result is that aggregate profits go up, even though aggregate 
cash flows stay the same.

The “tell” is that companies aren’t choosing to pay out cash to shareholders. 
With share buybacks receding to about 4% per annum, Lapthorne notes that the 
surge in capital spending is coming at the expense of cash for shareholders. A 
surge in operating cash flow for US non-financial companies that is running at 
around 20% per annum is financing all the capex:

That’s forced shareholders to put up with historically low levels of cash paid 
to them. As Lapthorne shows, both dividend and buyback yields have dropped to 
levels previously seen only at the height of the dot-com bubble (when companies 
were producing far less cash to distribute):

The low dividend yields are all the more remarkable given that long-term bond 
yields are no longer offering support to equity valuations. Taking the classic 
metric for theequity risk premium <https://bloom.bg/4dmRRSa>, by subtracting 
the 10-year Treasury bond yield from the S&P 500 earnings yield (the inverse of 
the price/earnings multiple), reveals that investors are actually being 
penalized by 88 basis points for taking the extra risk of buying stocks. This 
gives no margin for error, and suggests extreme enthusiasm for stocks:

While this is all very alarming, there’s no cause for concern, providing AI 
proves as profitable as hoped. Lapthorne notes:

If this investment delivers higher post-capex profits, shareholder returns 
should recover; if it merely reflects the cost of staying relevant, given low 
dividend and buyback yields the implications are more troubling, but this will 
only be a concern when profit growth slows.

For now, earnings forecasts are so rosy that even the extreme rise in 
semiconductor stocks no longer looks alarming. They are trading at a multiple 
of expected earnings that’s almost exactly in line with the broader market — so 
if the forecasts are OK, so is the share price:

The insatiable appetite for computing capacity suggests that generous 
distributions of cash remain a way off. Wall Street projects hyperscalers’ free 
cash flow to fall by over 70% from its peak by the end of 2026, even as 
earnings keep climbing:


For now, investors can put up with this. As Points of Return noted here 
<https://bloom.bg/4tLIUri>, for all their apprehension toward signs of 
unrestrained capex that threaten margins, investors appear to be acting with 
greater nuance. In the most recent Bank of America Fund Manager Survey on what 
companies should do with cash, more than a third favored an increase in capex, 
and as the investment is not largely funded by debt, concerns over balance 
sheets are contained. The share who wanted to prioritize returning cash to 
shareholders slightly dipped from the prior month:


The AI-driven outperformance is also reverberating across emerging markets, 
where the war has slowed momentum without derailing it. Before the conflict, EM 
assets traded near record highs. More than two months later, the group has 
collectively weathered the storm — thanks in part to AI, which still relies on 
imports from Taiwan or South Korea.

To gauge AI’s contribution, BCA Research decomposed year-over-year growth in 
US goods imports into AI- and non-AI-related buckets. After moving together for 
30-plus years, spending on semiconductors, computing, and telecommunications 
hardware began decoupling from spending on all other imported goods in 2024. It 
moved sharply in the opposite direction in 2025, and now exports of anything 
not related to AI are tanking:

Ultimately, the current wave of building will taper off. What follows will 
determine whether the AI trade evolves or unravels. It’s possible that 
proliferating new uses will keep capex ticking along, if not at the current 
levels. Berezin argues the risk is not that AI proves to be a dud, but rather a 
transition to an AI-empowered economy that no longer requires trillions in 
ongoing spending. He draws a parallel with the internet, where the volume of 
data transmission is ever-growing but capex remains relatively subdued. The 
question is how quickly that transition happens.
—Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



"Ball game over,” as John Sterling used to say. The “Voice of the Yankees,” 
who at one point commentated on a streak of more than 5,000 consecutive games,
has passed away at 87 <https://bloom.bg/4ua2qOF>. It’s a beautiful evening here 
in New York as I type and a colleague sends this photo of the scoreboard uptown 
at the stadium:

Those of us who root for the Yankees’ rivals can dwell on late-career flubs 
<https://bloom.bg/48Etg8J>. But there’s something wonderful about the 
relationship you build with radio commentators <https://bloom.bg/4tR433h> on 
baseball (and cricket); they spend hours with you, and can describe all the 
action (not practical for less discontinuous sports). Sterling was one of the 
last greats when radio was still the main way most of us consumed baseball.

While Sterling could be annoying, self-parodying, and unapologetically in the 
camp with the home team <https://bloom.bg/4u2B4tm>, he was sincere, genuine and 
often truly funny <https://bloom.bg/48IjFhc>. Here is his call for back-to-back 
homers by Gio Urshela <https://bloom.bg/48FMlYf> — “a most happy fella” — and 
Mike Ford, two of the least heralded Yankees of his time, and another for
Giancarlo Stanton and Aaron Judge <https://bloom.bg/4tPuxSJ>. My personal 
favorite wasBern Baby Bern <https://bloom.bg/4nagtAY>. Although I never liked 
hearing that the-e-e-e Yankees had won, I will miss him. Thanks John — and 
nominations of other great radio voices happily received.
