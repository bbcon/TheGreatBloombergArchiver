**US-Iran Strikes: The Week's Defining Event**
Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities represent the most consequential geopolitical development for global energy markets in several years, arriving after a week in which diplomatic and military signals were simultaneously escalating. US-Iran nuclear talks resumed in Geneva on Thursday, with special envoy Steve Witkoff and Jared Kushner meeting Iranian Foreign Minister Araghchi, and both sides agreed to reconvene as soon as next week. Satellite imagery released during the week showed Iranian engineers actively reinforcing and concealing nuclear sites from last June's strikes, hardening tunnels and burying facilities — a reminder that military action can destroy physical infrastructure but cannot eliminate scientific knowledge, stockpiled material, or political will. A NATO AWACS aircraft in Turkey reportedly shifted its operational focus from Russia to Iran. Trump's overnight video address acknowledged the possibility of US casualties — a significant admission given the open-ended nature of the commitment.

**Oil Markets and Strait of Hormuz Risk**
Brent crude entered the week already up approximately 18–19% year-to-date, trading above $70 per barrel, reflecting a geopolitical risk premium that Saturday's events are likely to extend materially. Bloomberg Economics estimates a full Strait of Hormuz closure — through which approximately 20% of global oil flows — could push oil to $108 per barrel. Supertanker earnings had already surged to levels not seen since early 2020 during the week, with VLCC day-rates on the Middle East-to-China route exceeding $200,000, a rise of more than 600% year-to-date, driven by elevated volumes at sea and a concentrated market position being assembled by South Korean shipowner Sinokor Group. OPEC+ was set to meet Sunday with delegates signaling readiness to resume previously suspended output increases — a partial offset, though the group's ability to compensate for Hormuz disruption is constrained by logistics rather than production capacity.

**Global Monetary Policy: Inflation Risk Reintroduced**
The energy price shock materially complicates the easing cycles underway or anticipated across multiple central banks. The ECB, which had guided the eurozone toward its 2% inflation target, now faces the possibility of another energy-driven cost-push episode. French and Spanish inflation both surprised to the upside in February data released Friday, reinforcing caution ahead of forthcoming ECB meetings. In Asia, Japan, South Korea, and India — all major energy importers — face terms-of-trade deterioration if oil sustains elevated levels. The Federal Reserve's path is similarly complicated: core inflation already sits at 3%, a full percentage point above target, and sustained energy price elevation provides little room to accelerate easing even as AI-driven labor market disruption raises novel questions about the transmission of conventional monetary tools.

**Global Debt and Private Credit Stress**
Global debt reached $348 trillion at end-2025 per the Institute of International Finance, a fresh record driven by a $29 trillion annual increase — the fastest accumulation since the pandemic — underscoring fiscal vulnerability in a persistently elevated rate environment. Blue Owl Capital's decision to gate one of its private credit funds deepened scrutiny of the $1.8 trillion sector, with UBS raising its worst-case private credit default scenario to 15% on Friday, up from 13% less than a month earlier, citing rapid AI disruption as a clearer catalyst than previously modeled. Private equity returned distributions equivalent to just 14% of net asset value in 2025 — the second-lowest since 2008 — with $3.8 trillion in unsold assets weighing on the industry. The collapse of London-based Market Financial Solutions added a further data point to the stress narrative in non-bank lending.

**Reserve Currency Dynamics and Dollar Entrenchment**
Despite sustained "sell America" rhetoric, the dollar's reserve currency status appears further entrenched by the Iran shock, at least in the near term. Global investors continue to accumulate dollar-denominated assets, and the structural barriers facing alternatives remain unresolved. The ECB unveiled a repo facility extending euro liquidity to most foreign central banks — its most concrete step yet toward promoting the euro's international role — but German Bund annual trading volumes of roughly €6.74 trillion amount to less than two weeks of US Treasury turnover, illustrating the fragmentation problem. China's yuan ambitions face analogous constraints: foreign ownership of Chinese government bonds has declined to just under $400 billion, compared with $9.3 trillion of foreign-held Treasuries, reflecting persistent impediments from capital controls, limited market liquidity, and a banking sector that holds rather than trades fixed income. Reserve currency transitions, history suggests, occur over decades rather than geopolitical episodes.
