Tipping Point

Investors over the past two weeks made quite clear how they read the Iran war 
implications 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/iran-war-trauma-of-middle-east-conflict-will-reshape-gulf-arab-states?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 for most key central banks in the developed world: Tighter monetary policy, 
net-net.

Two-year yields on government securities — which are closely linked to central 
banks’ benchmark interest rates — have climbed in nations including the US, UK, 
Germany, Canada and Australia by more than a quarter percentage point since the 
conflict began.

Longer-term yields are also higher 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/treasuries-fear-gauge-surges-to-nine-month-high-on-war-risks?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, speaking to how traders are interpreting the surge in oil prices as 
inflationary. Economists, too, have reacted from a perspective of the crisis
imposing price pressures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/podcast-why-us-gas-prices-will-remain-high-long-after-the-war?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 that central bankers will have to take account of. Goldman Sachs this week 
pushed back its forecast for the Fed to resume rate cuts to September, from 
June.

That’s not to say that things could change, however. As James Rossiter, head 
of global macro strategy at TD Securities put it: “There is still a point for 
central banks at which demand destruction outweighs‘transitory’ inflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/tame-cpi-still-spells-trouble-for-fed-s-favored-inflation-gauge?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, justifying rate cuts instead of hikes in response to a supply shock.”

What is that point? Rossiter and his colleagues gauge that an oil price around 
$150 a barrel “is where demand destruction begins to outweigh inflation fears 
for central banks,” he wrote in a note Thursday, when Brent crude levels were 
fluctuating around $100.

One element of that demand destruction would come, of course, with the wallop 
to household purchasing power for all other items that a surge in energy costs 
would generate. Bloomberg Economics noted earlier this week how that dynamic 
already threatens to blunt the increase inconsumption capacity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-10/iran-war-trump-tax-cut-risks-getting-getting-canceled-out-by-energy-price-surge?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 Americans will get from tax cuts.

An even more important element is “financial conditions,” and arguably their 
most potent component: The stock market, which presumably would be spooked by 
the revenue and profit damage that $150 oil would imply.

TD Securities offered two scenarios for Fed rate cuts. In one, the Iran 
conflict proves protracted, butinfrastructure 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/strait-of-hormuz-how-is-iran-war-disrupting-oil-trade-can-naval-escorts-help?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
 — think oil and gas terminals, refineries, oilfield structures — largely 
remains intact. As financial conditions react sharply in response to the surge 
in oil, the Fed eases by more than a percentage point, to 2.5% to address 
rising recession risks.

If Middle East energy infrastructure is damaged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/iran-war-why-desalination-plants-are-key-vulnerability-for-gulf-states?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>
, oil gets to an eye-watering $200 a barrel and markets and sentiment get 
hammered so hard the US is staring at a GDP contraction of 1%, then the Fed 
would be seen slashing rates below 2%, the TD Securities team wrote.

The good thing is past conflicts have shown effects on financial conditions 
have usually been limited, Goldman economists Manuel Abecasis and David Mericle 
wrote in their latest assessment Thursday. There are exceptions, including the 
Gulf War and the Sept. 11, 2001 attacks and their aftermath, which saw equities 
sell off and hurt confidence, they noted.

Goldman did raise its US recession risk estimate for the next 12 months by 5 
percentage points, to 25%. “If the labor market softens enough to make a case 
for cutting earlier, we do not think that concern about the impact of higher 
oil prices on inflation and inflation expectations would hold the FOMC back.”

 from fighting in the gulf as closure of theStrait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/strait-of-hormuz-how-is-iran-war-disrupting-oil-trade-can-naval-escorts-help?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>

With the discount on Urals crude to Brent almost disappearing, Russia could be 
about to reapanother 2022-style windfall 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/germany-concerned-russian-oil-sales-will-help-fund-putin-s-war?cmpid=BBD031326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260313>

tankers in the Strait of Hormuz, pressure to lift Russia sanctions will build
