Any discussion of power in today’s world inevitably turns to the efficacy of 
economic tools. A good navy helps. Real geopolitical dominance, though, is 
about currencies and the control of frontier technologies, chokepoints, supply 
chains and capital, the refrain goes.

But what about a sublime midfielder?

The football World Cup that concluded Sunday offered familiar moments of 
national disappointment and angst (hello England). It also, though, offered a 
reminder of both how soft power remains a valuable commodity and how fragile it 
can be. And of how the vulnerabilities and imperfections of even the greatest 
powers can be exposed.

There was a moment early in this year’s World Cup when the host nations led by 
the US seemed likely to be the biggest winners. Yes, people in Canada, Mexico 
and the US bathed in the hope their teams provided early on. But the real 
victory was going to be in charming the world.

Just over three decades since the last time the US hosted the tournament in 
1994 — the same year Naftaentered into force 
<https://www.congress.gov/crs-product/R42965> with bipartisan support — this 
was going to be the summer in which the world was reminded how much it loved 
America and its quirks.

Scottish fans didn’t only drink Boston dry, they discovered baseball. Norway’s 
Erling Haaland, who was born in England, did his ownDe Toqueville tour 
<https://www.youtube.com/shorts/ya0A7kRSVqQ>, finding the New York deli 
sandwich and cowboy boots. A charmingly innocent German fan ventured into and 
celebrated small-town America before hundreds of thousands onsocial media 
<https://www.tennessean.com/story/entertainment/music/2026/07/17/world-cup-fan-freddy-nashville-opry/90953932007/>
.

Halftime Show

By the end of the tournament, though, it was hard to see the event as a 
victory for at least American soft power. Even if Trump told reporters after 
the final that the five-week-long event had caused the world to see the US in 
“a different light.”

Complaints about hydration breaks interrupting the flow of matches turned into 
ones about a Super Bowl-style halftime show. In Trump’sintervention 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-17/trump-praises-infantino-over-red-card-reversal-jabs-at-co-hosts?cmpid=BBD072026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6678>
 to get a red card suspended, fans around the world saw disrespect for the 
international rule of law. When the US team was outclassed by Belgium, its 
players celebrated by imitating Trump dancing.

 “Spain is a wasted cause” in the geopolitical sense and threatened to cut off

Spain won not just the World Cup but a soft power victory in a final that was 
expected to be seen by almost one in four people on earth. New Spanish heroes 
will be celebrated this week on playgrounds far beyond the Iberian peninsula.

That also took place in a world in which the view of the US is changing. A Pew 
Research Center poll of people in 36 countries foundthose surveyed 
<https://www.pewresearch.org/global/2026/07/15/people-in-many-countries-now-view-china-more-positively-than-the-u-s/>
 in a majority of those countries now have a more favorable view of China than 
the US.

The lessons relevant to geopolitics and economics at this World Cup weren’t 
just about American power, though.

Falkland Banner

England’s semi-final loss to Argentina after being just minutes away from 
victory reminded us that tactical mistakes have lasting strategic consequences, 
and that seemingly dormantterritorial disputes

 like the Falkland Islands are easily revived.

Cape Verde taught us tiny nations can be thorns in the sides 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-06-26/cape-verde-s-football-journey-from-artificial-pitches-to-world-cup?cmpid=BBD072026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6678>
 of even the biggest powers.

The Democratic Republic of Congo and its Patrice Lumumba super fan, who held 
the pose of a famous statue celebrating the late African nationalist through 
their matches, reminded us that political legacy is a powerful motivator.

The limited runs of earnest middle powers like Canada and Australia exposed 
the potential limits of Mark Carney’s vision of the future and the reality that 
gallant defeats still sting.

Even in an age of AI, the overarching lesson of this World Cup, however, was 
that power still depends on human capital. The best teams were built on 
immigrants and players with talents refined in football’s global market.

The US’s best striker (and the subject of Trump’s red card intervention) was 
Folarin Balogun, who learned to play in London and whose US citizenship came 
because he was born during a brief stop in New York, a birthright Trumpwants to 
end 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/trump-to-seek-supreme-court-rehearing-on-birthright-citizenship?cmpid=BBD072026_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260720&utm_content=6678>
.

Lamine Yamal, Spain’s superstar 19-year-old winger, is the Muslim son of 
African migrants.

We shouldn’t take this all too far. But in a world rife with geoeconomic 
battles, thinking for a moment about soft power is refreshing. Plenty of people 
today are certainly wondering how their team can find a game-defining 
midfielder before the next World Cup rolls around. More, probably, than are 
thinking about US tariffs, though Trump may unleashmore of those

 in coming days.
