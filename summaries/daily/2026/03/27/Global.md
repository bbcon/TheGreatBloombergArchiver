The Iran war remained the organizing fact of global markets this week. Trump extended his pause on strikes against Iranian energy infrastructure by ten days, to April 6, citing progress in negotiations — though the talks appear to lack clarity on interlocutors, given the deaths of multiple senior Iranian government and military officials. Iran and Israel exchanged missile fire, and Tehran targeted several Gulf states, underscoring that the conflict retains significant escalation potential despite the diplomatic pause. Brent crude traded at $110 per barrel, and Macquarie warned of a path to $200 if the Strait of Hormuz remains closed through June — a scenario that would represent a supply shock with no modern precedent.

The dollar's behavior has confounded pre-war consensus. The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on track for its largest monthly gain since July, an approximately 2% advance that reflects haven demand and the repricing of Fed easing expectations. The move complicates the global inflation picture: a stronger dollar raises the local-currency cost of oil and food for importing nations, compounding the energy price shock. JPMorgan strategists have turned bullish on the dollar for the first time in a year; speculative positioning has flipped from the most net-short in five years to net-long.

Gold markets face a structural challenge. Turkey has sold approximately $8 billion — around 60 tons — of gold reserves this month to defend its currency and meet dollar demands, a pace of selling larger than concurrent ETF outflows. Should other energy-importing central banks follow suit, the narrative of central banks as consistent net buyers of gold, which has underpinned the metal's more-than-doubling since 2022, would face meaningful revision. Gold has already fallen roughly 18% from its peak above $5,000 per ounce.

Reports that Iran is permitting Hormuz passage for vessels paying oil in yuan has prompted Deutsche Bank to flag the conflict as a potential "catalyst for erosion in petrodollar dominance." The geopolitical and monetary ramifications of that scenario, while uncertain in magnitude, are significant and warrant continued monitoring.
