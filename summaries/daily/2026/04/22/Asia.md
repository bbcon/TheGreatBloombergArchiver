Japanese equity markets drew renewed interest from US investors, according to Goldman Sachs, as confidence recovers following the initial shock of the Middle East conflict. The observation aligns with a broader MSCI global gauge that has risen approximately 7% since the ceasefire was announced two weeks ago, with AI-focused benchmarks in South Korea and Taiwan rallying more than 13%. Memory chipmakers remain central to that move: Samsung shares have tripled and SK Hynix's have quadrupled since late August as AI-driven demand pushed chip prices sharply higher. The debate among investors is whether this represents a structural supercycle — with data center buildout creating a sustained demand floor — or a cyclical upturn that will eventually revert. Samsung and SK Hynix still trade at under 10x earnings, a significant discount to semiconductor peers, reflecting persistent market skepticism about the durability of memory cycles.

In China, the People's Bank of China injected additional liquidity into the banking system as data revealed an unexpected deterioration in the labor market, particularly among early-career workers, where unemployment has risen as AI adoption accelerates. Separately, China's "national team" — led by Central Huijin Investment — appears to have stepped back from dominant ETF positions that had stabilized markets during the tech-led rally earlier this year, with first-quarter filings showing holdings falling below the 20% disclosure threshold. The retreat suggests authorities may be satisfied with current market levels, or are managing the risk of an overheated rally, though their current exposure remains opaque.

Trump's cryptic CNBC remarks about a vessel carrying a "gift from China" — implying potential lethal aid to Iran — introduce a significant geopolitical variable. If substantiated, such transfers would test a declared US red line and could materially complicate the bilateral relationship at an already sensitive moment. Beijing has not publicly responded. Japan's March export data showed faster-than-expected growth on rebounding Chinese demand, offering a modestly constructive data point even as the Bank of Japan's expected rate hike next week faces diminished probability given war-related uncertainty.
