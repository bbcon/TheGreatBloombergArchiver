**Ceasefire Relief and Its Limits**

Asian markets traced a volatile arc across the week. Tuesday saw tentative gains of close to 1% on the MSCI Asia index as investors assigned some probability to a diplomatic outcome. Wednesday's ceasefire announcement produced a sharper relief rally: the Topix recovered to its highest level since February 28 and China's CSI 300 advanced 3.5%. By Thursday, however, markets had partially reversed — the CSI 300 fell 0.6% and WTI futures rebounded toward $98–99 a barrel — as ceasefire optimism gave way to renewed uncertainty about whether the Strait of Hormuz would reopen in practice. The week's moves reflect an unwinding of war-premium positioning rather than any fundamental improvement in regional economic conditions.

**China: Policy Reorientation and Export Momentum**

China's policy direction is undergoing a structural shift with global implications. Rather than deploying demand-side stimulus in response to the energy shock, Beijing is firmly committed to a supply-side reorientation articulated in its latest Five-Year Plan, directing state support toward technology and what it terms "new productive forces." Urban private-sector wage growth fell below 1% by end-2025, against a pre-pandemic rate of approximately 8%, and the absence of specific numerical consumption targets reinforces the assessment that domestic demand will remain structurally subdued — implying China's already-record trade surplus is likely to persist or widen. Separately, factory-gate prices exited deflation in March, with PPI rising 0.5% year-on-year after a 0.9% decline in February, an inflection partly attributable to the global energy surge, though Capital Economics cautioned the turn may not persist. EV and hybrid exports surged 140% year-on-year in March to a record 349,000 units, while domestic auto sales remained under pressure — BYD's home sales fell more than 40% — illustrating the bifurcation between China's export competitiveness and subdued internal demand. Beijing also granted independent teapot refiners additional crude import quotas to maintain fuel production, a practical measure revealing the extent to which energy security was stretched by the Gulf disruption.

**Cross-Strait Signaling and US-China Dynamics**

Friday's meeting between Xi Jinping and KMT Chairwoman Cheng Li-wun — the first by a sitting KMT leader in a decade — carried explicit geopolitical signaling. Xi called for "cooperation and peace" while reaffirming sovereignty claims over Taiwan, a combination that underscored the conditional nature of Beijing's diplomatic warmth ahead of the anticipated Trump-Xi summit in mid-May. The KMT has stalled a $40 billion Taiwanese defense expansion, and a $14 billion US arms deal has reportedly not received Trump's approval, suggesting the summit is already shaping security decisions. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi separately began a visit to North Korea — his first in over six years — reflecting Beijing's broader effort to consolidate relationships amid shifting geopolitical alignments.

**India: Outflows, Currency Pressure, and Rate Hold**

India faced acute pressure throughout the week. Foreign investors sold $17.8 billion in equities over 23 consecutive sessions, a record outflow pace, as elevated crude prices fanned inflation fears. The Reserve Bank of India held rates unchanged Wednesday, with Governor Malhotra acknowledging the ceasefire while cautioning that persistent supply chain disruptions continue to pose risks. The rupee strengthened 0.5% following his remarks, partially reversing a roughly 7% decline over the prior year. India's RBI also imposed a trading ban on its $149 billion offshore rupee market — a measure that may deter the foreign investors it has sought to attract, a tension that remains unresolved. State Bank of India separately disclosed approximately $5 billion in positions against the rupee caught by the central bank's crackdown on currency speculators, with estimated losses of around $32 million from forced unwinds.

**Japan and Korea**

In Japan, real wages rose at the fastest pace since 2021, supporting the case for continued Bank of Japan normalization. Consumer confidence, however, fell in March by the most since the early stages of the Covid pandemic, compounding concerns about a security vacuum in Asia as US military assets remain concentrated in the Persian Gulf. Seven & i Holdings delayed the planned US listing of its convenience store operations, with shares falling 4.6%. In Korea, Samsung Electronics reported an eightfold increase in quarterly profit driven by AI memory chip demand, with shares rising as much as 4.9% on Tuesday — a signal that AI-related semiconductor demand has become a stabilizing force for the Korean technology complex even amid broader market deterioration.
