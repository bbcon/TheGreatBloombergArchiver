• China's Q2 GDP grew 4.3% YoY — the weakest reading since at least 1992 outside the pandemic, missing consensus and prompting Morgan Stanley, Goldman Sachs, and ANZ to cut full-year forecasts; fixed-asset investment contracted 5.7% and property investment fell 18% in H1.
• SK Hynix's US-listed ADRs trade at a 51% premium to Seoul shares; the Kospi has fallen 28% from its June high, with volatility measures reaching levels unseen since February 1998, and regulators halted new listings of leveraged single-stock ETFs tied to Samsung and SK Hynix.
• TSMC reported a 77% YoY jump in quarterly profit Thursday, beat consensus, and raised full-year capex guidance to as much as $64 billion — yet its ADRs fell approximately 3.8% in premarket as semiconductor valuation concerns outweighed the fundamental beat.
• The Bank of Korea raised interest rates for the first time since 2023 Thursday, explicitly citing AI-driven demand as a structural inflationary force and flagging persistent won weakness.
• China's GDP deflator turned positive for the first time in three years, but economists characterize the shift as "bad inflation" — reflecting oil-shock cost pressures rather than restored consumer demand.

**China Growth and Policy**
The 4.3% Q2 GDP print crystallizes China's structural imbalance. Exports remain the primary engine — semiconductor shipments rose at triple-digit rates for a second consecutive month — while domestic demand is uniformly weak, with consumer spending barely expanding and fixed-asset investment recording a historic contraction. The positive deflator reading is technically a milestone after three years of economy-wide deflation, but it widens margins between upstream commodity producers and downstream manufacturers in a way that suppresses rather than stimulates consumption. Goldman Sachs estimates roughly 1.8 trillion yuan in undeployed government bond issuance quota as a fiscal buffer, though Beijing's willingness to deploy it aggressively remains uncertain. Bloomberg Economics argues the Iran conflict represents only a "ripple" for China given its lower import dependency, leaving deflation as a domestic problem requiring a domestic demand cure.

**Korea and Semiconductor Markets**
The Kospi's 28% peak-to-trough decline has been amplified well beyond what fundamentals alone justify. Smart money outflows preceded the retail-driven collapse — tech funds recorded $9.3 billion in weekly outflows through late June and BlackRock's iShares MSCI South Korea ETF suffered a record $2.7 billion monthly outflow in May — while leveraged single-stock ETFs tracking Samsung and SK Hynix mechanically magnified daily moves. The Bank of Korea's rate hike adds a further headwind for leveraged domestic investors. The 51% ADR premium for SK Hynix reflects structural impediments to arbitrage rather than fundamental divergence, and regulators' decision to halt new leveraged ETF listings signals recognition that the product design itself contributed to the volatility spiral.

**AI Geopolitics and Regional Markets**
Xi Jinping's personal appearance at the World AI Conference in Shanghai Thursday — an elevation from prior years — and the launch of a China-led AI Cooperation Organization with 29 member nations including Brazil and Indonesia formalized Beijing's bid to shape global AI governance. Chinese startup Moonshot's Kimi K3 model, described as rivaling top OpenAI and Anthropic offerings, contributed to the regional equity selloff by prompting investors to question US AI capex return assumptions. Taiwan's Taiex shed 6.5% Thursday — its largest single-day decline since April 2025. The Bank of Korea's identification of AI semiconductor demand as a structural inflationary force illustrates that the boom's macro transmission remains narrow: generating output and profits without yet producing the wage and consumption gains needed for sustained domestic reflation.
