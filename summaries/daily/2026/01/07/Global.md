**Global Daily Briefing — January 7, 2026**

**Venezuela Re-Enters the Global Energy Map.** The most consequential cross-regional development today centers on Venezuela's abrupt return to investable status following Nicolás Maduro's ouster and his not-guilty plea this week to US narco-terrorism charges. Venezuelan sovereign bondholders captured roughly $4 billion in single-day mark-to-market gains, with the rally underpinned by expectations of a sovereign restructuring and the prospect of meaningful US capital deployment into the country's degraded oil infrastructure. President Trump's pledge that Washington would spend billions to rehabilitate Venezuelan production has drawn private capital and energy majors into preliminary assessments of a market that has been effectively off-limits for the better part of a decade.

The macro implications extend well beyond Caracas. Venezuela holds the world's largest proved crude reserves, though current output remains a fraction of its early-2000s peak. A credible, US-backed reinvestment program would, over a multi-year horizon, add incremental heavy-sour barrels to global supply — relevant for US Gulf refiners structurally configured for that grade, and potentially a marginal offset to OPEC+ supply discipline. The near-term price impact appears limited given the lead times involved in restoring damaged fields and infrastructure, but the directional signal is bearish for the medium-term oil curve if execution proceeds. Sanctions architecture, licensing frameworks for Chevron and other operators, and the still-unresolved question of political transition in Caracas all remain material uncertainties. It remains to be seen whether the restructuring path can be negotiated cleanly given the layered claims from defaulted sovereign and PDVSA debt, expropriation arbitration awards, and Chinese and Russian creditor positions.
