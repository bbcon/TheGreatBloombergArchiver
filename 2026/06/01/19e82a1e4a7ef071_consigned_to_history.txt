The unexpected win of a right-wing outsider 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-31/de-la-espriella-takes-surprise-early-lead-in-colombia-vote?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
 in the first round of a Colombian presidential election yesterday thrust the 
nation headlong into the polarized politics seizing Latin America and the world.

Abelardo de la Espriella, a colorful celebrity attorney 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-29/investors-warm-to-pro-trump-colombian-outsider-ahead-of-election?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
, goes into the June 21 runoff as the favorite to vanquish the legacy of 
outgoing President Gustavo Petro, Colombia’s first leftist leader, whose torch 
is being carried by Iván Cepeda.

De la Espriella has already claimed his first victim: the conservative 
movement led by ex-President Álvaro Uribe that has dominated Colombian politics 
for much of the last quarter century.

Colombians will now choose between a supporter of US President Donald Trump 
pledging 10 new mega-prisons modeled off Nayib Bukele’s in El Salvador — which 
takes deportees from the US — and the son of a slain communist who many 
investorsconsider even more radical 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/son-of-slain-communist-rattles-investors-ahead-of-colombian-vote?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
 than Petro.
De la Espriella at a rally in Barranquilla, Colombia, yesterday.Photographer: 
Carlos Parra Rios/Bloomberg
Traditional conservative supporters had aligned behind Paloma Valencia, a 
senator and Uribe protege whopromised to revive 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-09/security-hardliner-could-become-colombia-s-first-woman-president?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
 his mix of pro-market policies and hardline security approach.

De la Espriella adopted much of that policy backbone but with brasher ideas 
and a more charismatic face. In doing so, he demolished the established forces 
just as similar figures have done in Brazil, Argentina and Chile.

The same tensions were immediately on display.

Petro and Cepeda questioned the vote and railed against “fascism.” De la 
Espriella, donning the yellow national football shirt he’s adopted as a 
political symbol, branded them “miserable delinquents” and warned of a 
potential coup.

Similar scenes are playing out in Peru 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/stark-class-divides-are-deepening-peru-s-2026-presidential-election?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
 ahead of its own June 7 runoff, andagain in Brazil 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-13/dish-soap-and-flip-flops-turn-political-in-polarized-brazil?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
, which votes in October.

A De la Espriella victory would continue the region’s rightward shift, helping 
advance Trump’s aim of dominating the Western Hemisphere.

Even before that, it’s clear that Colombia can be added to the tally of 
countries riven by bitter political division.— Oscar Medina 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AQ4ddfHKMuU/oscar-medina?cmpid=BBD060126_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260601&utm_content=5046>
