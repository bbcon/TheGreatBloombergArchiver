Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

When the Chinese government is desperate to boost marriage and birth rates 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-13/china-s-struggle-to-boost-child-births-shows-limits-of-government-influence?cmpid=BBD030626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
, joking about family life can be risky for comedians.

Paziliyaer Paerhati, a Uyghur better known by her stage name Xiao Pa, learned 
that the hard way when her social media account was suspended due to her 
musings about marriage. The ban sparked an outcry among Internet users 
increasingly frustrated withChina’s online censorship 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-03/shanghai-censors-social-media-posts-talking-down-housing-market?cmpid=BBD030626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
.

In a post on Weibo last month, Xiao Pa suggested that being single can be a 
blessing in disguise sometimes.

“Spent the last two days in bed with a fever,” she wrote. “It hit me that if I 
had a husband and kids, I’d probably have to prop myself up against the wall 
right now just to cook for them.”

Many users found the comment funny but some men accused her of stoking “gender 
opposition.”

Later, Weibo said it banned Xiao Pa from posting as part of a government 
campaign to clean up the Internet duringthe Lunar New Year holiday 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-20/chinese-new-year-2026-what-lunar-holiday-tells-us-about-china-s-dining-habits?cmpid=BBD030626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
. The platform accused her of “stirring up gender conflicts and creating 
anxiety about marriage and childbirth.”

That move triggered an online backlash, with many people accusing censors of 
overreacting and voicing support for the comedian.

“What did she write? Let me see. Oh, it’s just the truth,” read one comment 
with more than 40,000 likes.
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
Xiao Pa started doing stand-up in 2020 and rose to wider fame last year on the 
showRock and Roast. Her routines often poke fun at her turbulent family life, 
including her father’s six marriages. In one interview, she said her dad’s only 
motivation for marriage was to find a woman who could cook, clean and bear 
children.

Her skeptical view of marriage resonates with many young, urban Chinese women, 
who are increasingly delaying or rejecting marriage due to economic pressure, 
persistent gender inequality, and concern about sacrificing their career and 
personal growth.

That trend worries the Chinese government, which has urged women to embrace 
more traditional domestic roles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2025-03-09/china-s-communist-party-wants-women-to-stay-in-their-place?cmpid=BBD030626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260306>
 and adopt “correct views” on marriage, childbirth and family as part of its 
push to raise birth rates — with mixed results.
