Weekly Brief

Feb 23 – Mar 01, 2026

US

  • The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariffs and the administration's rapid pivot to a replacement 15% global levy under Section 122 defined the week's policy landscape, with legal durability remaining in question throughout.
  • Nvidia's earnings beat failed to sustain a rally, AI disruption fears weighed on software names, and the S&P 500 closed February with its largest monthly decline since April — strikingly underperforming European and emerging-market peers.
  • A major winter storm paralyzed the Northeast early in the week, and US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities Saturday introduced a new geopolitical and energy price risk into the domestic outlook.
  • The Treasury market delivered its best monthly performance in roughly a year, with long-dated bonds gaining 4%, confirming their haven role even amid fiscal sustainability debates.
  • Kevin Warsh's anticipated succession to the Fed chair drew increasing attention, with analysts mapping a potential shift in the dot-plot framework and a markedly more dovish tilt than his earlier public record suggested.

Tariff Regime in Transition

The week opened with the legal and practical fallout from the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariff architecture. By Tuesday, a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974 had entered into force, subsequently raised to 15% by executive action. The legal footing is widely questioned: Section 122 applies specifically to "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath stated plainly the US does not meet, a view echoed by former Treasury official Jay Shambaugh, who noted that a genuine balance-of-payments crisis would be reflected in dollar weakness and capital flight — neither of which is occurring. The administration signaled it intends to slow-walk refunds on overturned levies, framing repayments as "corporate welfare," even as more than 1,500 refund lawsuits have been filed representing an estimated $170 billion in contested duties. Legal challenges appear likely, though courts may move too slowly to constrain tariffs that expire in 150 days under the statute.

Equity Markets and the AI Disruption Narrative

The week's dominant equity theme was a rotation away from US software and services names perceived as vulnerable to AI displacement. IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years after Anthropic's Claude Code tool was shown to modernize COBOL, threatening IBM's mainframe franchise. C3.ai tumbled 24%, Trade Desk fell 17% in pre-market, Salesforce slid roughly 4%, Workday dropped 9.2%, and Duolingo fell 24% on below-consensus guidance. Against this, hardware and infrastructure names held up: Dell gained 9.8% on AI server demand, and Block surged as much as 27% after announcing it would cut nearly half its workforce as an AI productivity bet. Nvidia's earnings — with revenue guidance of approximately $78 billion beating consensus — produced a muted after-hours response, encapsulating the increasingly demanding valuation bar AI-adjacent equities now face. The S&P 500 options market reflected the broader caution, with the put-call skew rising to a two-year high and normalized two-month skew approaching the upper end of its five-year range.

Monetary Policy and the Fed Transition

The National Association for Business Economics conference devoted its agenda almost entirely to AI's labor market implications, with Fed Governor Lisa Cook suggesting the economy may be approaching the most significant reorganization of work in generations and warning that conventional rate tools cannot offset AI-driven structural unemployment. Richmond Fed President Tom Barkin echoed the caution. The more politically consequential monetary story, however, surrounds Kevin Warsh: Bloomberg Economics analysis confirmed a marked shift from his hawkish 2009–2011 positioning toward dovish messaging since 2025, and Deutsche Bank analysts sketched potential reforms under his expected tenure including revisions to the dot plot and a possible shift toward rolling 12- and 24-month rate horizons.

The Iran Shock and Domestic Political Risk

Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities arrived as the week's most consequential late development. For Trump, the political arithmetic is uncomfortable: having campaigned on an America-first, anti-interventionist platform, he has now presided over strikes in Venezuela, Iran, Nigeria, and now a direct assault on Iran's nuclear infrastructure. Polling already suggests Republicans face a difficult path to retaining both congressional chambers. The immediate domestic economic concern is energy: oil has risen nearly 20% year-to-date, and any further spike feeds directly into gasoline prices at a moment of elevated household financial stress. Trump acknowledged in an overnight video address that US casualties are possible, an admission that will intensify scrutiny if losses materialize. On the corporate side, Berkshire Hathaway's PacifiCorp faces approximately $55 billion in wildfire-related claims, and Greg Abel's first annual letter as Buffett's successor inherits a materially complex liability picture as climate-driven risk intensifies across the American West. ---

Asia

  • Asian hardware and semiconductor names dominated inflows, with Taiwan Semiconductor up 30% year-to-date, Samsung up 70%, and SK Hynix up 57% as investors rotated away from US software toward AI infrastructure beneficiaries.
  • The People's Bank of China moved to temper yuan appreciation by removing the 20% reserve requirement on foreign-currency forward contracts, effective March 2, signaling concern that excessive currency strength could aggravate deflationary pressures.
  • Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iran introduced immediate energy import risk for Asia's largest crude importers — China, Japan, South Korea, and India — all of which depend heavily on Middle Eastern supply transiting the Strait of Hormuz.
  • Japan's monetary policy outlook grew more contested, with Prime Minister Takaichi nominating reflationist academics to BOJ board vacancies and reports of her skepticism toward further rate hikes, sending the yen lower and pressuring JGBs.
  • South Korea's equity market capitalization surpassed France to rank ninth globally, while its National Pension Service posted an 18.82% return in 2025 — its strongest ever — though foreign funds sold a record net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks on Friday after the benchmark's near-50% year-to-date run.

China: Trade, Currency, and Geopolitical Complexity

China emerged from the Supreme Court's IEEPA ruling as one of the clearer near-term beneficiaries, with Morgan Stanley estimating average US levies on Chinese goods fell to 24% from 32%. Hong Kong's Hang Seng China Enterprises index rallied 2.65% on Monday. The improved tariff arithmetic arrives ahead of President Xi Jinping's scheduled hosting of Trump in Beijing on March 31, materially altering the negotiating dynamic: Xi enters the summit with export volumes at full capacity, reduced average levies, and a US counterpart operating under legally constrained trade authority. Whether Beijing capitalizes on this position or maintains a measured posture to avoid fresh friction remains to be seen. China simultaneously warned against further tariff increases and issued new blacklistings of 20 Japanese entities including Mitsubishi Heavy affiliates — the first addition of Japanese firms to the list since its inception. A record $112 billion discrepancy between Chinese reported export data and US Customs arrivals for 2025 suggests that as much as a quarter of China-origin shipments may have bypassed US tariff controls, complicating revenue collection and any analytical assessment of the bilateral trade relationship. The PBoC's move to temper yuan appreciation by easing forward contract reserve requirements reflected concerns that China's record $1.2 trillion trade surplus continues to generate currency appreciation pressure at odds with an economy still grappling with deflationary dynamics. Lunar New Year data offered a mixed signal: 596 million tourist trips — 95 million more than a year prior — and record total tourism spending, but per-trip expenditure of 1,348 yuan fell marginally below the prior year, consistent with a consumer that is mobile but cautious.

Japan: Policy Tensions Mount

Japan's monetary policy outlook became more contested across the week. Reports that Prime Minister Takaichi had expressed apprehension over further BOJ rate increases in a meeting with Governor Ueda — taking a tougher stance than at their November meeting — sent the yen down approximately 1% to around 156 per dollar on Tuesday, reviving concerns that political resistance could constrain normalization. Later in the week, Takaichi nominated two reflationist academics to forthcoming BOJ board vacancies, reinforcing those concerns and sending the yen and JGBs lower. Notably, former BOJ Governor Haruhiko Kuroda — himself the architect of Japan's long-running easing experiment — called publicly for rate hikes and warned the yen may be "somewhat too weak," a striking inversion of his own legacy. On corporate governance, Toyota is preparing to unwind approximately ¥3 trillion ($19 billion) in cross-shareholdings with banks and financial institutions, a meaningful step in Japan's multi-year capital efficiency effort.

South Korea and the Broader Regional Rotation

South Korea's equity market continued to attract attention as a structural beneficiary of corporate governance reform and semiconductor cycle strength. The Kospi advanced 1.91% to 6,084 on Wednesday, with total market capitalization rising to $3.76 trillion, surpassing France to rank ninth globally. Overseas investors purchased a net $2.77 billion of Taiwan equities on Tuesday — the largest single-day foreign inflow since December 2005. The Bank of Korea held its benchmark rate at 2.5%, upgrading both growth and inflation forecasts and publishing new dot-plot projections. South Korea's fertility rate rose for a second consecutive year — a rare positive demographic signal — though foreign funds sold a record net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks on Friday after the benchmark's near-50% year-to-date advance, suggesting some profit-taking in one of the world's best-performing markets. Saturday's Iran strikes introduced a new downside risk variable: as a major crude importer with limited domestic energy production, South Korea is acutely exposed to any sustained disruption in Strait of Hormuz flows. ---

Europe

  • European equities outperformed their US counterparts for much of the week, with the Stoxx Europe 600 on course to close February with its eighth consecutive monthly gain — the longest winning streak since 2013 — driven by a rotation away from AI-disruption-exposed US names.
  • HSBC reported full-year pretax profit of $29.9 billion, beating expectations, with shares gaining 5.4% in London; by contrast, Diageo fell 5.8% after cutting its full-year organic net sales guidance for the second time this fiscal year.
  • The Green Party won the Gorton and Denton by-election in Manchester with 40.7% of the vote, with Reform UK second and Labour third — a result that illustrates the two-front political pressure on Keir Starmer's government.
  • Saturday's Iran strikes and the resulting Gulf airspace closures — including Emirates suspending all Dubai operations — introduced a new energy price and inflation risk for the eurozone, complicating the ECB's rate path.
  • The collapse of London-based Market Financial Solutions created acute exposure for Barclays (approximately £600 million), Atlas (£400 million), and Jefferies (£100 million), adding to broader stress narratives across non-bank lending.

European Equity Outperformance

The defining market theme of the week was European equities' relative strength against a US backdrop weighed by AI disruption fears. The Stoxx Europe 600 closed February up 3.7%, extending an eight-month winning streak not seen since 2013, with European companies announcing €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date — the highest ever for a January-February period, according to Barclays data. Goldman Sachs promoted what it labeled a HALO basket — Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence, spanning utilities, miners, industrials, and luxury goods — arguing these capital-intensive sectors are structurally insulated from AI displacement. The basket has outperformed capital-light peers by 35% since early 2025. This rotation reflects both the relative absence of high-multiple software names in European indices and a cyclical recovery that may be underappreciated by consensus. Germany provided some of the firmest datapoints: fourth-quarter GDP showed recovery taking hold, factory orders jumped in December by the most in two years, and the Ifo business confidence survey rose more than expected, suggesting early benefits from Merz's defense and infrastructure investment program are beginning to materialize, though the trajectory remains uncertain.

Corporate Earnings and Stress

HSBC's full-year results were the standout European corporate event, with pretax profit of $29.9 billion exceeding analyst expectations and the bonus pool rising 10% to $3.93 billion — the highest in at least a decade — as CEO Elhedery's pivot toward a more performance-differentiated compensation structure takes shape. The wealth division was the principal earnings driver, consistent with broader private banking flows into Asia. Diageo's second guidance cut of the fiscal year — projecting a 2–3% organic net sales decline — underscored how simultaneous consumer demand weakness in the US and China creates compounding risk for globally diversified consumer staples companies. Aston Martin announced plans to reduce its workforce by approximately 20%, citing Trump's tariffs among the complicating factors. The London Market Financial Solutions collapse, exposing Barclays, Atlas, and Jefferies, added to a broader narrative of stress in private credit and non-bank lending that spans both sides of the Atlantic this week.

Politics and Trade Policy

Labour's by-election loss in Gorton and Denton to the Green Party, with Reform UK second, illustrates the breadth of dissatisfaction with the Starmer government from both flanks. With local elections approaching and Starmer registering as the most unpopular UK prime minister by some modern polling measures, the political backdrop has become materially uncomfortable. Chancellor Reeves is expected to deliver a low-key economic update, with gilt issuance projected to be reduced — likely a constructive signal for markets given recent UK fiscal sustainability concerns. At the EU level, the European Parliament's trade chief moved to freeze ratification of the EU-US trade agreement pending clarification on Washington's revised framework, while an EU assessment confirmed Trump's 15% global tariff would push cumulative levies on certain EU exports above the EU-US agreement ceiling. Italy's industry minister escalated pressure on the Emissions Trading System, calling it "ineffective and harmful," following an 8% decline in European carbon prices after Merz indicated openness to revising or delaying the program — a signal with meaningful implications for climate policy credibility across the bloc. ---

Global

  • Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities represent the week's most consequential geopolitical development, triggering Iranian missile responses against US bases in Qatar, Bahrain, and the UAE, Gulf airspace closures, and a sharp reassessment of Strait of Hormuz risk across energy and financial markets.
  • Brent crude had already risen approximately 18–20% year-to-date on US-Iran tensions before the strikes, with Bloomberg Economics estimating a full Hormuz closure could push oil to $108 per barrel.
  • Global debt reached $348 trillion at end-2025, a fresh record driven by a $29 trillion annual increase — the fastest accumulation since the pandemic — underscoring sovereign fiscal vulnerability in a persistently elevated rate environment.
  • The dollar's reserve currency status appears further entrenched near-term, with structural barriers facing both the euro and yuan as alternatives remaining unresolved despite concrete initiatives from both the ECB and Beijing.
  • US renewable power generation hit a record 1,162 terawatt-hours in 2025, representing 26% of total US electricity output, even as the net-zero political commitment has effectively collapsed across rich-nation energy communiqués.

The Iran Shock and Energy Market Implications

Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities, and Tehran's rapid missile responses against US military installations in Qatar, Bahrain, and the UAE, represent the most significant disruption to Middle Eastern geopolitical stability in recent years. Iran produces approximately 5% of global oil supply, and roughly 20% of global oil flows transit the Strait of Hormuz. Brent crude had already risen approximately 18–20% year-to-date as US diplomatic warnings intensified, and Bloomberg Economics now estimates a full Strait closure could push prices to $108 per barrel. OPEC+ was reportedly considering a larger-than-expected supply increase at its Sunday meeting, though the group's logistical capacity to compensate for a Hormuz disruption is constrained regardless of production decisions. Supertanker earnings had already surged to their highest levels since early 2020 in the days before the strikes, with very large crude carrier day-rates on the Middle East-to-China route exceeding $200,000 — a rise of more than 600% year-to-date — driven by elevated volumes of oil at sea and a concentrated accumulation of tonnage by South Korean shipowner Sinokor Group. The combination of a tight tanker market and a potential supply shock represents a compounding risk for Asian crude importers in particular.

Global Monetary Policy and the Inflation Complication

Prior to the Iran strikes, the dominant cross-regional monetary theme was a divergence between central banks making progress on inflation and the ECB and several Asian central banks beginning or contemplating easing cycles. The energy price shock introduced by the strikes complicates that calculus materially. For the ECB, having spent two years managing an inflation shock partly attributable to energy price volatility following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, the prospect of another cost-push episode is unwelcome. French and Spanish February inflation data surprised to the upside even before the strikes, reinforcing the case for caution. The Bank of Korea held at 2.5% while upgrading growth forecasts, but South Korea's acute energy import dependence places it among the most exposed economies to a sustained oil price spike. The broad implication is that easing cycles anticipated across multiple central banks may be deferred or compressed if Hormuz disruption proves durable — a scenario financial markets have begun, but not fully, priced.

Reserve Currency Dynamics

The week produced substantive developments on the long-running debate over dollar alternatives. The ECB unveiled a repo facility extending euro liquidity to most foreign central banks — the institution's most concrete step toward promoting the euro's international role — with notable support from Italian central bank governor Panetta and Bundesbank President Nagel, who indicated openness to a European sovereign bond creating a liquid pan-European benchmark asset. China's President Xi reiterated ambitions for a broader global role for the renminbi. Yet the structural barriers facing both currencies remain formidable: foreign ownership of Chinese government bonds stands at just under $400 billion, compared with $9.3 trillion for Treasuries, reflecting capital controls, limited market liquidity, and a banking sector that holds rather than trades fixed income. German Bund annual trading volumes represent less than two weeks of equivalent US Treasury turnover. As events unfolded over the weekend, dollar-denominated assets continued to attract global safe-haven flows, further entrenching reserve currency dynamics that periodic "sell America" rhetoric has done little to structurally alter.

China's Belt and Road and the Trade Architecture

China's Belt and Road Initiative appears to be entering a new expansionary phase after a period of retrenchment attributed partly to Covid-era constraints. Total BRI-related engagements reached an all-time high of $214 billion in 2025, with both average deal size and geographic reach at record levels, concentrated in central Asia, energy, metals, and technology. The "small-but-beautiful" era declared in 2021 appears to have been a transitional posture rather than a structural recalibration. Meanwhile, the broader global trade architecture remains unsettled following the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA duties and the contested legal legitimacy of their Section 122 replacement. Indonesia, which signed a bilateral trade deal with the US just days before the ruling, found its arrangement's status uncertain within days. The British Chambers of Commerce estimated UK exporters face up to £3 billion in additional annual costs affecting some 40,000 companies as a result of losing their prior preferential arrangements — a concrete illustration of the distributional consequences of tariff regime instability.
