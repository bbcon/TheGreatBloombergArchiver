Day six of the Iran war and the conflict is going global. A US submarine sunk 
an Iranian warship off Sri Lanka’s coast just after the ship took part in a
Indian naval exercise <https://bloom.bg/40MbonU>. 

Iran vowed to retaliate, with Foreign Minister Abbas Araghchi calling the 
attack an “atrocity” that the US “will come to bitterly regret.” US Defense 
Secretary Pete Hegseth said the attack, which left more than 100 sailors 
missing or dead, was the first by an American submarine on a surface vessel 
since World War II. The ship was in India Feb. 15 to 25 to participate in the 
International Fleet Review, alongside vessels from 40 other countries — 
including the US.

“By sinking a vessel returning from an Indian-hosted multilateral exercise, 
Washington effectively turned India’s maritime neighborhood into a war zone, 
raising uncomfortable questions about India’s authority in its own backyard,” 
Brahma Chellaney, professor emeritus of strategic studies at the New 
Delhi-based Centre for Policy Research, said in a post on X.

So far, about a dozen nations have become embroiled in the war, with Tehran 
striking US bases and embassies across the Middle East, and Israel launching an 
air and ground offensive against Iran-backed Hezbollah in Lebanon. NATO said 
its air defenses downed a ballistic missile headed for Turkey.

Arab states across the Persian Gulf reported interceptions of Iranian missiles 
and drones overnight and into Thursday as the Islamic Republic said its 
retaliation against American strikes would escalate. Israel is carrying out 
waves of airstrikes on Tehran. The White House said American forces were moving
toward total control <https://bloom.bg/3Piw703> of Iranian airspace, with 
President Donald Trump saying the US was “doing very well on the war front.”

While financial markets have steadied, especially after the sharp selloff in 
Asian equities on Wednesday, oil continues to rise.Brent crude climbed 
<https://bloom.bg/4rQWDMu> toward $85 a barrel, adding to a 12% jump over the 
first three days of the week. The endgame remains elusive, with Hegsethtelling 
reporters <https://bloom.bg/4ri4fXt> “it could be six, it could be eight, it 
could be three” weeks before military operations are wrapped up.—Richard Frost

 What You Need to Know Today 



In further signs of widening disruptions from the war, China has told the 
country’s largest oil refiners tosuspend exports <https://bloom.bg/40JEWCz> of 
diesel and gasoline.

Officials from the National Development and Reform Commission met executives 
and verbally called for a temporary suspension of refined product shipments to 
begin immediately, according to people familiar with the matter. The refiners 
were asked to stop signing new contracts and to negotiate the cancellation of 
already-agreed shipments.

Beijing’s precautionary curbs reflect efforts across Asia, which is dependent 
on energy imports, to prioritize domestic needs. Japanese oil refiners are said 
to be asking the governmentto release crude <https://bloom.bg/40GOgaq> from the 
nation’s strategic petroleum reserves. Japan gets more than 90% of its oil from 
the Middle East.

Separately, a bulk carrier signaled it was Chinese-owned 
<https://bloom.bg/47eXv5j> as it transited the Strait of Hormuz this week, 
highlighting how vessels are trying to ensure safe passage through the 
waterway. The Iron Maiden changed its destination signal to “CHINA OWNER” from 
“For Orders” early Thursday as it made a break through the passage while 
hugging the Omani coastline, according to ship-tracking data from Bloomberg and 
Kpler.




China set its most modest growth target since 1991, a tacit acknowledgment 
that the model powering the country’s economic riseis showing strains 
<https://bloom.bg/40MeJTW>.

The goal — a range of 4.5% to 5% — is the first formal downgrade since 2023. 
Widely anticipated by economists, it signals Beijing’s tolerance of a slower 
pace of expansion while it searches for new sustainable sources of growth.

“The task of transitioning to new growth drivers is formidable,” Premier Li 
Qiang said. “The imbalance between strong supply and weak demand is acute, 
market expectations are weak, and there are many risks and hidden dangers in 
key areas.”

China also signaled it’s modestly dialing down <https://bloom.bg/47cD0pS> 
fiscal stimulus this year, a rollback after a trade war with the US that still 
allows for further support in the face of geopolitical uncertainty abroad and 
the risks facing the economy at home.
Xi Jinping, left, and Li Qiang in Beijing on March 5. Photographer: Qilai 
Shen/Bloomberg 



South Korea’s President Lee Jae Myung highlighted the nation’s 100 trillion 
won ($68.2 billion) market stabilization program on Thursday as Seoul grappled 
withhistoric volatility <https://bloom.bg/4rLXs9r> tied to the escalating 
conflict in Iran.

The benchmark Kospi climbed 10% on Thursday after tumbling 18% the previous 
two days. For Lee, thedanger of a reversal <https://bloom.bg/40cLyJz> in the 
market isn’t just a complication for his aims to rebalance savings away from a 
reliance on property speculation, but could also spell the end of his honeymoon 
as the nation’s leader. Local elections are scheduled for June.




Goldman Sachs CEO David Solomon said he’s watching the private credit market 
for “frothiness,” though in contrast with some of his peers he hasn’t seen 
significant cause for concern <https://bloom.bg/40Mbr34>. 

“We’re watching very closely to see if there’s been a little bit too much 
aggression, frothiness,” Solomon said in a Bloomberg Television interview in 
Sydney. “While there have been a bunch of idiosyncratic events where there have 
been problems, the broad portfolios are performing reasonably well.”

Concerns have been bubbling up over the $1.8 trillion private credit market in 
recent weeks, with investors spooked in part by the risk of AI on some 
borrowers and worries about valuations.
David Solomon, CEO of Goldman Sachs. Photographer: Al Drago/Bloomberg 



Mainland Chinese investors sold the most Hong Kong stocks in a single session 
on record, underscoringwaning appetite <https://bloom.bg/47cD2hu> for the 
city’s shares seen as more vulnerable to geopolitical uncertainty.

Onshore traders offloaded HK$27.7 billion ($3.5 billion) worth of shares 
through the trading link between the mainland and Hong Kong. The selling 
weighed on the city’s equity benchmarks, with the Hang Seng Tech Index erasing 
a gain of 1.8% to fall 0.7%. Mainland Chinese investors have been a crucial 
source of support for Hong Kong’s equity market, snapping up a record amount 
last year.




The Bank of England is planning to war-game the potential economic and 
financial impact of a full-blown AI shock amid fears that the technology will 
cost vast numbers of jobs <https://bloom.bg/47cD2y0> and cut through a swathe 
of businesses.

The UK central bank will also consider whether to incorporate the assessments 
into its broader banking stress tests to capture a possible surge in household 
and company loan defaults and the damage inflicted on lenders, a person 
familiar with the matter said.

Concerns about the economic threat posed by AI have rocketed after Anthropic 
CEO Dario Amodei warned that the technology “could displace half of all 
entry-level white collar jobs in the next one to five years.”
Dario Amodei, CEO of Anthropic. Photographer: Ruhani Kaur/Bloomberg 



More than 100 Labour Party members of parliament have urged Prime Minister 
Keir Starmer’s government to rethink changes to the UK’s immigration system, 
underscoring thescale of opposition <https://bloom.bg/46I4gfN> confronting him 
from within his own ranks.

They sent the private letter, seen by Bloomberg, to Home Secretary Shabana 
Mahmood on Wednesday afternoon. That came before a speech she plans to deliver 
Thursday intended to convince her colleagues of the reforms, which will make it 
harder for many low earners to gain residency and citizenship but will ease the 
path for those with high-paying jobs.

 For Your Commute 



As the war in Iran grounds flights worldwide, hundreds of thousands of 
travelers scrambling to get home are discovering a harsh reality: their travel 
insurance won’t cover replacement flights or extended hotel stays.

Insurers, including industry giants Allianz and Zurich Insurance, don’t cover 
claims tied to the conflict under standard travel policies, according to 
advisories. Fighting has shuttered Gulf airports, including the main one in 
Dubai, severing a critical transit corridor for long-haul travel. At least 
23,000 flights to Middle East hubs have been canceled, leaving passengers to 
rely on airlines for rebooking or pay thousands of dollars out of pocket.

The crisis is exposing a gap in the estimated $31 billion annual travel 
insurance market: Nearly all policies exclude war-related claims. Consumer 
advocates have long warned the product offers limited value, citing high 
commissions, low payouts and sweeping conflict exclusions.
