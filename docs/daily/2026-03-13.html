Daily Brief

Friday, March 13, 2026

US

US equity futures were modestly higher on Friday, with S&P 500 futures up 0.13% to 6,686, though the index has declined roughly 3% in March as the Iran conflict has reframed the macro outlook. The Magnificent Seven gauge is nearing correction territory, down nearly 10% from its peak, with the group trading mixed. The session's most notable corporate story was the departure of Adobe CEO Shantanu Narayen after 18 years, with shares falling approximately 8.5% in pre-market trading following a lackluster revenue outlook and deepening investor concern about Adobe's competitive positioning in the AI era. The macro backdrop for the Federal Reserve has grown meaningfully more complex. Economists have pushed back expectations for the next rate cut to June, and Goldman Sachs revised its forecast further to September. With oil above $100 per barrel, the inflation-growth tension has sharpened: elevated energy prices are simultaneously stoking price pressures and eroding household purchasing power, limiting the policy space typically available in a slowdown. The ICE BofA MOVE Index, a measure of Treasury market volatility, climbed to its highest level since June, as traders dialed back 2026 rate-cut expectations materially. Goldman also raised its 12-month US recession probability by 5 percentage points, to 25%. TD Securities quantified the threshold at which demand destruction might outweigh inflation concerns for the Fed at roughly $150 per barrel of Brent, a scenario under which it expects the Fed to ease by more than a percentage point to address recession risk. A more severe scenario involving material damage to Middle East energy infrastructure, with oil reaching $200, could see GDP contract and rates fall below 2%. On the fiscal front, American officials disclosed that the first six days of the Iran war cost over $11.3 billion, with the Financial Times reporting the US has burned through "years" of critical munitions. The financial and strategic costs of the campaign are accumulating without a discernible endgame, a dynamic that is beginning to register in market pricing and economic forecasts.

Asia

Asian markets declined on Friday, with the Hang Seng falling 0.98% as risk appetite remained subdued ahead of a potentially volatile weekend in the Middle East. Regional currency markets were notably stressed: the yen weakened to its softest level since 2024 at approximately 159.5 against the dollar, South Korea's won traded near its lowest since 2009, and India's rupee and the Philippine peso reached historic lows. The Thai baht dropped as much as 1.8% intraday. A gauge of dollar strength has risen 2.2% in March, reversing four months of depreciation, as hedge funds accumulate short-dated dollar call options to hedge weekend Iran-related risk. Southeast Asian governments moved to impose emergency energy conservation measures. Thailand ordered most civil servants to work from home full-time; the Philippines shifted to a four-day government work week; Malaysia is expected to announce similar arrangements next week. Philippine President Marcos framed the situation plainly: "We are victims of a war that we didn't choose nor want." Geopolitically, the Trump-Xi summit framework is taking shape. US Treasury Secretary Bessent, Trade Representative Greer, and Chinese Vice Premier He Lifeng will convene in Paris over the weekend to map deliverables for the March 31–April 2 summit in Beijing. Key agenda items include tariffs, fentanyl, Taiwan, potential Chinese investment in the US, and semiconductor export policy. Beijing has condemned the Iran strikes but shows no indication of withdrawing from the summit, prioritizing near-term stabilization of the bilateral relationship. Domestically, China's consumer spending may have recorded its worst start to any year outside the pandemic, according to economists surveying ahead of Monday's official release. The divergence between China's export strength — a $1.2 trillion trade surplus last year, with exports surging in the first two months of 2026 — and deteriorating domestic demand and wages for low-skilled workers in provinces like Guangdong remains a structural challenge for Beijing's rebalancing agenda. Worker protest activity rose 44% last year, with Guangdong the primary hotspot.

Europe

European equities declined modestly, with the Stoxx Europe 600 off 0.22% as concerns about energy costs and growth weighed on sentiment. The euro fell to its lowest level since August against the dollar, reflecting the continent's acute exposure to elevated oil prices through both import costs and supply chain disruption. The European Central Bank is now expected to hold rates unchanged through 2027, a marked shift from earlier dovish expectations, as inflation risks resurface from energy. The UK delivered a downside surprise on growth, with GDP flat in January against expectations for a 0.2% gain — weaker than every forecast in the analyst survey. The data predate any meaningful impact from the Middle East conflict, suggesting the UK economy was already losing momentum before the current energy shock. The figures add pressure on the government, which faces a deteriorating fiscal backdrop and limited room to respond. Germany's chemical industry association VCI warned that supply chain disruption and surging energy costs are beginning to constrain output across Europe's largest economy. The VCI's director general described "a spiral spinning in the wrong direction," with raw material shortages compounding the direct effect of higher energy prices. This dynamic raises the prospect of a fresh industrial slowdown in Germany, which was only beginning to stabilize after a prolonged contraction. In French politics, Marine Le Pen's effort to broaden National Rally's appeal to corporate and establishment elites is generating internal friction, with some party officials describing a new circle of elite advisers as alienating the working-class base that drove the party's rise. The tensions bear monitoring given Le Pen's proximity to the French presidency in current polling.

Global

The defining development of the period remains the Iran war, now approaching its third week, with no credible path to de-escalation visible. Brent crude held above $100 per barrel, with Iran's new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei explicitly vowing to keep the Strait of Hormuz effectively closed and threatening to open additional fronts if US-Israeli strikes continue. Approximately a fifth of global oil and natural gas flows through the strait; Saudi Arabia, Iraq, Kuwait, and the UAE have all been forced to curtail production. Trump responded with a stark social media threat, language that markets interpreted as signaling no near-term easing of hostilities. The strategic picture is deteriorating in ways beyond the energy market. Russia is providing Iran with satellite imagery and drone-targeting intelligence, according to multiple sources, complicating the US campaign while simultaneously benefiting from the Strait's closure. With the discount on Russian Urals crude nearly eliminated relative to Brent, Russia stands to capture a windfall comparable to its 2022 energy revenues. The US has responded by issuing a second authorization to ease Russian oil sanctions, allowing buyers to take cargoes already at sea — a decision that has drawn particular resentment among European allies. The traditional 60/40 portfolio hedge is breaking down as bonds and equities move in tandem, driven by stagflation concerns. Government bond yields have risen across the US, UK, Germany, Canada, and Australia by more than 25 basis points since the conflict began, eroding bonds' conventional cushioning role. Chinese assets, gold, the dollar, and buffer ETFs are emerging as the practical havens of the moment. US shale producers have not yet publicly committed to expanding output despite WTI touching its highest level since August 2022, though early signals — rising rig counts and government production forecasts revised upward for 2027 — suggest the industry's capital discipline may be tested. The White House has limited tools to suppress prices, and Trump, who campaigned on low energy costs, may move to pressure producers directly if prices remain elevated.
