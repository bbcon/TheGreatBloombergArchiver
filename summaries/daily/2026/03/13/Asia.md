Asian markets declined on Friday, with the Hang Seng falling 0.98% as risk appetite remained subdued ahead of a potentially volatile weekend in the Middle East. Regional currency markets were notably stressed: the yen weakened to its softest level since 2024 at approximately 159.5 against the dollar, South Korea's won traded near its lowest since 2009, and India's rupee and the Philippine peso reached historic lows. The Thai baht dropped as much as 1.8% intraday. A gauge of dollar strength has risen 2.2% in March, reversing four months of depreciation, as hedge funds accumulate short-dated dollar call options to hedge weekend Iran-related risk.

Southeast Asian governments moved to impose emergency energy conservation measures. Thailand ordered most civil servants to work from home full-time; the Philippines shifted to a four-day government work week; Malaysia is expected to announce similar arrangements next week. Philippine President Marcos framed the situation plainly: "We are victims of a war that we didn't choose nor want."

Geopolitically, the Trump-Xi summit framework is taking shape. US Treasury Secretary Bessent, Trade Representative Greer, and Chinese Vice Premier He Lifeng will convene in Paris over the weekend to map deliverables for the March 31–April 2 summit in Beijing. Key agenda items include tariffs, fentanyl, Taiwan, potential Chinese investment in the US, and semiconductor export policy. Beijing has condemned the Iran strikes but shows no indication of withdrawing from the summit, prioritizing near-term stabilization of the bilateral relationship.

Domestically, China's consumer spending may have recorded its worst start to any year outside the pandemic, according to economists surveying ahead of Monday's official release. The divergence between China's export strength — a $1.2 trillion trade surplus last year, with exports surging in the first two months of 2026 — and deteriorating domestic demand and wages for low-skilled workers in provinces like Guangdong remains a structural challenge for Beijing's rebalancing agenda. Worker protest activity rose 44% last year, with Guangdong the primary hotspot.
