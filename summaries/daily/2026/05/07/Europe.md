UK local elections took place Thursday, with control of the Scottish and Welsh parliaments and more than half of England's councils at stake. The vote functions as a verdict on Prime Minister Keir Starmer's tenure of under two years, during which a combination of policy missteps, tax increases, and questions over judgment — notably the appointment of Peter Mandelson as ambassador to Washington — have eroded Labour's standing significantly. The political arithmetic is complicated: Labour holds a large parliamentary majority and faces no general election until 2029, but market attention has focused on whether leadership pressure could push successor candidates toward looser fiscal commitments, which gilt markets would view unfavorably.

That gilt market backdrop is already under strain. The spread of UK gilts over US Treasuries has widened beyond levels seen at the peak of the Liz Truss episode in 2022, reflecting a combination of the UK's negative net international investment position, twin current account and fiscal deficits, and the perception that the Bank of England faces a more constrained path than the Federal Reserve. Overnight index swaps imply significantly more BoE tightening than was priced before the Iran conflict, a divergence that has widened materially from the Fed's expected trajectory. Bank of England officials this week joined private forecasters in questioning the reliability of recent UK economic data, adding further complexity to rate-setting.

On the European corporate front, Shell reported stronger-than-expected first-quarter earnings as elevated energy prices and increased trading volatility — both direct consequences of the Hormuz closure — more than offset production declines. Campari sank 11% in Milan after disappointing results. Looking further ahead, China's newly issued five-year plan, with its emphasis on upgrading traditional industries and cultivating frontier technologies including robotics and biomedicine, is generating significant anxiety in European industrial circles, particularly in Germany. Machinery association VDMA describes the level of corporate concern as unprecedented, as Beijing's shift from scale toward quality and sustainability directly threatens sectors that have historically underpinned European export competitiveness.
