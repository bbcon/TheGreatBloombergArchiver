**Equity Markets and the AI Momentum Trade**

US equities extended their extraordinary run through the week, with the S&P 500 reaching successive record closes driven by a narrow but powerful semiconductor complex. Micron Technology surged 19.3% on Tuesday, joining a ten-member club of US companies valued above $1 trillion and reaching that threshold in 48 trading days — the fastest doubling on record — underpinned by a projected earnings increase exceeding 1,200% through fiscal 2027. The concentration of returns is striking: the SOX index contributed roughly 369 points to the S&P 500's 672-point year-to-date gain, against a more modest 79 points from the remaining Magnificent Six. MSCI momentum stocks are on track for their strongest two-month outperformance versus the broad market on record. Goldman Sachs lifted its year-end S&P 500 target to 8,000 on Tuesday, citing AI tailwinds and earnings momentum, with Ed Yardeni running the highest tracked estimate at 8,250. The AI infrastructure theme continued to produce uneven outcomes across the software stack: Snowflake surged approximately 36% Wednesday after a $6 billion AWS agreement and raised guidance, lifting MongoDB (+10%) and Datadog (+5.5%), while Salesforce fell roughly 3% on a disappointing revenue forecast — illustrating the tension between monetizing AI features and the risk that AI reduces the complexity of workflows enterprise vendors serve. Dell's Thursday premarket surge of 40% simultaneously lifted Hewlett Packard Enterprise (+20%) and Super Micro Computer (+12%). The week closed with household equity exposure now above even 2000 dot-com peak levels, a dynamic Bank of America's Michael Hartnett characterized as "bubbly," noting retail mania and compressed volatility.

**Monetary Policy**

Kevin Warsh's tenure as Fed Chair began with markets wasting little time pricing its implications. The 5s30s Treasury spread compressed to its tightest level in a year, reflecting a higher-for-longer repricing at the front end, as traders assigned high probability to the first rate hike arriving by March 2027. The inaugural Bessent-Warsh breakfast produced a carefully calibrated signal: the Treasury secretary framed his expectations around Warsh doing "the right thing for inflation and growth" — invoking the statutory dual mandate rather than political objectives — and did not request rate cuts. Minneapolis Fed President Kashkari, speaking in Seoul on Thursday, reinforced the cautious stance, warning that elevated energy prices and unanchored inflation expectations preclude declaring victory. Thursday's PCE print, with consensus near 4% driven primarily by energy costs, represented the week's pivotal data point; any significant upside surprise would likely accelerate the timetable for tightening. The tension between a potential cyclical oil-price disinflationary impulse — if the Hormuz deal holds — and persistently elevated real yields leaves the Fed's near-term path genuinely uncertain.

**Consumer, Political, and Corporate Developments**

The record equity prints stand in sharp contrast to a University of Michigan consumer sentiment reading that, by Bloomberg's account, is the weakest since the survey's inception in 1978. That divergence — financial asset inflation alongside deteriorating household confidence, partly reflecting elevated energy costs from the unresolved Strait of Hormuz situation — represents a tension the market has thus far chosen to set aside. In corporate news, Gap fell 15% after cutting its sales outlook, underscoring uneven consumer spending beyond the AI complex, while IBM gained 6% on a $10 billion quantum computing commitment. The Trump administration's effective freeze on onshore wind permitting is creating measurable economic consequences, with trade groups estimating $50 billion in stranded investment risk across approximately 150,000 jobs — a policy divergence that sits uncomfortably with the strategic case for domestic energy diversification at a moment of Middle East supply disruption. The Texas Railroad Commission primary result, in which energy majors including Occidental, ExxonMobil, Chevron, and ConocoPhillips failed to protect their preferred candidate, introduces genuine regulatory uncertainty into the US's most prolific producing basin.
