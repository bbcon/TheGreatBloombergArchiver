It’s Still About Earnings

The president of the US virtually announced the end of NATO 
<https://bloom.bg/3N7mTmY>; the war in the Gulf intensified with the killing of 
Iran's security chief <https://bloom.bg/4bU41QL>; oil prices continued to rise 
as Iran made clear that it retains totalcontrol over the Strait of Hormuz  
<https://bloom.bg/4bxzY0K>waterway.

And stocks went up, more or less everywhere.

This is not an all-clear signal from the market, and trading was desultory as 
financiers get ready for a raft of central bank decisions. But oil has been 
leading share prices on a tight leash, and that does now appear to be loosening:

Stock markets have plainly raised risk premia in response to the conflict, as 
is only sensible, but not in any way that’s excessive or presents big 
opportunities for contrarians. Breaking down how the mainfactors 
<https://bloom.bg/4lDwoHl> have performed in global equities since the war’s 
outbreak on Feb. 28, nothing has made money, but the pain has been least for 
conservative strategies such as low-volatility and high dividend-yield:

The attack came as a surprise three weeks ago. At that time, most in the 
markets would have expected hostilities to be wrapped up by now. Certainly, few 
would have predicted that by mid-month President Donald Trump would beg for 
military help from NATO and berebuffed <https://bloom.bg/4sbV3VL>. They would 
have regarded this as very negative.

So why the calm? The single biggest reason equities are holding up is that the 
earnings forecasts <https://bloom.bg/4sbNqyw> that underpin their prices are 
also stable. Even if energy companies (obviously helped by a big oil price 
increase) are excluded, the war has had no effect so far on rising earnings 
expectations for this year:

Earnings momentum as strong as this will limit the downside for a stock 
market. It’s likely that brokers are holding off changing their estimates until 
there is more clarity on the duration of the conflict. If it goes on a few more 
weeks, it’s possible the market will have to deal with a raft of downgrades. 
But currently, bullish forecasts limit the downside, and the damage.

We now have the first investor sentiment surveys carried out in their entirety 
since the conflict started, and they confirm a picture of calm. The war is 
perceived to require a rise in the risk premium for stocks, and not a 
particularly big one. This comes out in the latestBank of America survey 
<https://bloom.bg/4ssJoSo> of global fund managers, who say they’re now taking 
less risk than usual — but remain far less cautious than they were in 2022, or 
after the Liberation Day tariffs a year ago:

The American Association of Individual Investors’ weekly survey of whether its 
members consider themselves bulls or bears, a great contrarian indicator over 
the years, suggests that retail investors are in much the same place. They’ve 
switched to their most net bearish since November, but are nowhere near as 
downbeat as after Liberation Day or for most of 2022 and 2023. They appear to 
be retrenching prudently rather than panicking in a way that would present a 
buying opportunity:

At the level of asset allocation, BofA found fund managers — wholly rationally 
— deciding to go overweight commodities. This is not surprising after history’s 
biggest ever shock to oil supply:

That is confirmed by the price action. Bloomberg’s commodities index 
(including industrial and precious metals and agricultural crops as well as 
fuel) is beating stocks and bonds (represented by the Bloomberg 60:40 index) in 
the most impressive way since the 2022 invasion of Ukraine:

All of this is consistent with a significant but less than catastrophic hit to 
materials prices by the time the dust settles. The fund managers quizzed by 
BofA expect crude to be at about $76 by the end of the year:

That’s not far out of line with the futures market, which is pricing Brent 
crude <https://bloom.bg/4dyTdcS> at just over $80 nine months hence. That means 
inflation up and growth down a little, but far from a 1970s-level stagflation:

Stagflation is now clearly back as the greatest risk on the horizon. But the 
BofA survey has shown large numbers of asset managers worried about this 
scenario constantly for the last four years. Even if stagflation risk has just 
increased, many regarded themselves as adequately positioned to deal with such 
an outcome:

If there is a sign of complacency, it’s in the volatility indexes, which 
monitor how far investors will go to hedge against future disruption in the 
derivatives market. Both the MOVE index (covering bonds) and the VIX (the 
implied volatility of the S&P 500) have risen this month, but both treat this 
situation as less dangerous than Liberation Day, or even the carry trade unwind 
of August 2024. For bonds, this remains far less dangerous than the banking 
crisis that followed the fall of Silicon Valley Bank in 2023:

This does smack of complacency, as the worst outcomes are far more plausible 
than they were three weeks ago, and could yet inflict a greater and more 
prolonged oil spike than even the Ukraine conflict did in 2022. For the time 
being, markets are adequately priced for a serious but not disastrous shock to 
supply that should be resolved within weeks. Clear evidence that crude prices 
will be significantly above $80 throughout the rest of the year would change 
things, but we don’t have that evidence yet.

While the fog of war continues, investors now want to know whether central 
banks <https://bloom.bg/4btHkCy> feel similarly calm. The Reserve Bank of 
Australia was the first of 18 meeting this week, and thefirst to blink with a 
rate hike <https://bloom.bg/4sdyrEd>. Few others will follow suit, but some 
carefully chosen hawkish words from the Fed and the others could yet have a 
bigger impact than Tuesday’s momentous events in the Gulf.

Can Consumers Keep Buying?

Consumers aren’t catching a break any time soon if Trump’s shifting timelines 
on the war are any indication.Pain at the pump <https://bloom.bg/4buUIpR> is 
getting worse, with the average gasoline price tracked by the American 
Automobile Association at its highest since 2023. It’s still well below the 
peaks seen after Russia invaded Ukraine, but the speed of the increase is fully 
comparable:

The key difference is the consumer backdrop. In 2022, households were emerging 
from thepandemic flush with stimulus cash <https://bloom.bg/4lFYHF6>. They no 
longer have that cushion. Consumers are instead grappling with elevated 
borrowing costs, thinner savings, and little margin for another sustained 
increase in energy prices.Consumer spending barely rose in January 
<https://bloom.bg/4cPtNaM>, and economic growth was weaker than previously 
reported at the end of last year, suggesting a momentum loss even before the 
war started.

The average price of diesel has now risen above $5 per gallon, pushing up 
supply chain costs and setting the stage for broader inflation. The spike is 
hitting farmers, truckers and construction firms. BloombergNEF analysis shows 
that with $5 diesel, these industries must this week spend around $6.1 billion 
on fuel, compared to just $4.5 billion ahead of the war, a 35% increase.

This is the very antithesis to affordability politics. The steady decline in 
gas prices since 2022 brought real gains. Spending on energy and services fell 
to 3.4% of disposable income in January. Outside the pandemic period, that’s 
arguably the lowest this century:

Troy Ludtka of SMBC Nikko Securities warns that a sustained rise in energy 
costs could reverse this trend:

We estimate that the current increase in gasoline prices could lead to a 
60-basis-point decline in the savings rate, bringing the overall reading to 
3.9%, in line with other periods of instability, such as in 1999-2001 and 
2005-2008. This recessionary kindling is especially worrisome because the labor 
market is fragile and the unemployment rate essentially has one way to go from 
here (up).

Beyond energy costs, pending home sales <https://bloom.bg/4cPtPPW> are another 
affordability measure whose rebound is now under threat. The National 
Association of Realtors index, which is recorded when contracts are signed and 
typically leads existing home sales by one to two months, rose by 1.8%, erasing 
the prior month’s decline:

Oxford Economics’ Grace Zwemmer argues that the crimp in real disposable 
incomes caused by oil prices could prompt households to postpone some spending 
decisions, setting back the American Dream. The picture isn’t much different in 
Canada, where mortgage rates are likely to climb in the coming weeks. But the 
country is a net oil exporter and gains from oil price shocks, which should 
cushion the economy and reduce the need for rate hikes.

Still, Stephen Brown of Capital Economics points out that money markets are 
pricing a tightening, pushing up bond yields. That, in turn, could press upward 
on mortgage rates, which remain frustratingly high for a government preoccupied 
by affordability. Bankrate.com’s average 30-year fixed mortgage rate is 
stubbornly above 6%, a level it didn’t reach for 13 years after the Global 
Financial Crisis:

The slide in US consumer sentiment <https://bloom.bg/4lBkRYY>, at a 
three-month low in March’s preliminary reading, may be an early warning of 
deeper strain. Respondents to the University of Michigan survey see little 
letup in gasoline prices, which tend to move inversely with sentiment:

With affordability a key issue <https://bloom.bg/4lxph2U> ahead of the 
November midterms, a swift resolution to the war is necessary for the 
administration, but it isn’t sufficient to ensure victory. Washington will 
require a meaningful reversal in these cost pressures. Otherwise, it’s hard to 
see voters looking past that issue that decided their vote in the last election.

—Richard Abbey
