Not Quite Golden

Donald Trump promised a “golden age <>” for America in his second term. And 
the president may well aim to sell a message of economic success in his
state-of-the-union speech 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/trump-reverts-to-first-term-tumult-as-republican-fears-mount?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 Tuesdaybefore Congress 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/state-of-the-union-2026-tariffs-immigration-other-topics-to-watch?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
. He facesa tough task 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/trump-says-economy-is-his-now-faces-a-tough-job-selling-it?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
.

Based on the latest economic data, it would be difficult to claim 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-opinion-trump-promises-data?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 the age is yet golden. Friday’sGDP report 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-20/us-gdp-rose-at-slower-than-forecast-pace-of-1-4-last-quarter?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 showed growth in 2025, at 2.2%, was the slowest since the post-Covid expansion 
began in 2020. And a core gauge of inflation ended the year running at 3%, a 
whole percentage point above the Federal Reserve’s goal.

Still, the recession that some prominent economists  
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-08/summers-warns-us-likely-headed-to-recession-2-million-jobless?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
had warned about when Trump unveiled his “Liberation Day” tariffs last April 
didn’t eventuate — in part because he took the levies down in seeking deals 
with trading partners. And even if inflation is above target, the pass-through 
to prices from import duties has proved less alarming than many had expected.

The labor market has recently shown signs of stabilizing after a slowdown last 
year. Employers added130,000 jobs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/us-payrolls-rise-130-000-unemployment-rate-unexpectedly-falls?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 in January, compared with an average of just 15,000 a month in 2025 — and the 
unemployment rate edged down to 4.3%. Wages are also rising faster than 
consumer prices, allowing workers to post real income gains.
Donald TrumpPhotographer: Scott Morgan/Bloomberg
As for housing, a key target for the administration, home sales and new 
construction remain sluggish. Starts on new homes saw a fourth straight annual 
decline last year. And while 30-year fixed mortgage rates have come down to 
around 6% from 7% at the start of Trump’s second term — the lowest in more than 
three years — borrowing costs are still elevated compared with what Americans 
got used to in the decade before 2022.

As for consumption, retail sales remained relatively resilient for much of 
2025, posting steady gains until momentum slowed in the latter half of the year 
andunexpectedly stalled 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-10/us-retail-sales-unexpectedly-stagnated-to-close-holiday-season?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 in December. While wealthier households have continued to power spending, 
lower-income Americans have become more cautious, perhaps because they’ve seen 
slower wage growth lately.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent regularly says that 2025 was about “laying 
the table” — putting policies in place — while this year will be when the 
“banquet” begins. But for now, Americans aren’t feeling like they’re about to 
enjoy a feast. A recent Washington Post-ABC News-Ipsos poll showed that a 
majoritydisapproved <> of Trump’s handling of the economy, inflation and 
tariffs.

“The actual economy is fairly solid, but voters are probably not going to see 
it that way,” said Stephanie Roth, chief economist at Wolfe Research. “People 
feel things are unaffordable, and the administration cannot fix that.”

Need-to-Know Research

With the dates announced for Trump’s visit to China, outlooks for the first 
American presidential visit to the US geostrategic rival since 2017 are 
starting to roll out. Longtime China economist Arthur Kroeber says there may be 
good vibes, but no “grand bargain.”

Kroeber, head of research at Gavekal, notes President Xi Jinping’s ambitions 
for the March 31-April 2 visit are likely grander than Trump’s — ideally 
including a loosening of Washington’s backing for Taiwan, which the Communist 
leadership regards as part of China. But Taiwan’s role in the American AI boom 
that Trump has been championing is among the hurdles for such a deal, Kroeber 
said in a note Monday.

And as for a big Chinese investment push into the US, which this newsletter 
examined 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-18/could-china-s-manufacturers-shift-output-to-the-us-japan-did-it?cmpid=BBD022426_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260224>
 last week, Kroeber says there’s a mismatch problem. “Trump’s own industrial 
priorities (a 1950s mix of coal, steel and shipbuilding) are not ones where 
Chinese help is wanted or likely to be provided,” he wrote. And the areas where 
Chinese companies have strong interest are low on the administration’s list. 
Further, items such as electronic vehicles “would be subject to intense 
national security fears.”
