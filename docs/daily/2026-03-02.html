Daily Brief

Monday, March 02, 2026

US

US equity markets opened under significant pressure on Monday, with S&P 500 futures declining approximately 1.0% as the US-Israeli strikes on Iran upended investor sentiment that, until the weekend, had sat at its most optimistic since the pandemic. Defense names proved a clear exception: Lockheed Martin rose 7.1%, RTX gained 6.5%, and Northrop Grumman advanced 5.2% in premarket trading. Airlines fell sharply — United Airlines down 5.7%, Delta off 5.1% — on the combination of higher fuel costs and regional travel disruption. Oil majors advanced, with Exxon Mobil up roughly 5% and Conoco gaining 6%. The Treasury market behaved unexpectedly for a risk-off session, with the 10-year yield rising around 3 basis points to approximately 3.97%, rather than declining as a traditional haven bid would imply. The most plausible explanation is the inflationary signal embedded in an oil spike: rising energy prices reduce the probability of near-term Federal Reserve rate cuts, and money markets scaled back easing wagers accordingly. Société Générale's historical analysis of five oil supply shocks over the past 50 years found the 10-year Treasury consistently weaker at one-week, three-month, and six-month horizons. Gold, rising to $5,385 per ounce, appears to be the haven of preference in the current environment. Politically, the conflict raises a distinct domestic variable. The Trump administration's decision to target Iran's supreme leader represents a departure from decades of US foreign policy convention, and the president's stated objectives have remained notably fluid — ranging from a deal with new Iranian leadership to a campaign lasting four to five weeks. With midterm elections in November, the inflationary consequences of a sustained oil price elevation carry political weight, as consumer affordability concerns remain elevated. The Federal Reserve transition, with a new chair assuming the role in roughly two months, adds a further layer of institutional uncertainty at a sensitive moment. Berkshire Hathaway's Class B shares slipped 1.7% after the conglomerate reported a near-30% decline in operating profit in what was Warren Buffett's final quarter as CEO, with analysts flagging insurance business weakness.

Asia

Asian markets registered broad declines on Monday, with the MSCI Asia benchmark retreating 1.6% as the implications of the US-Israeli strikes on Iran reverberated across regional financial centers. The session's most acute stress emerged in Pakistan, where the KSE-30 Index plunged 9.6% in early trade to trigger an hour-long trading halt — a reflection of Pakistan's geographic proximity to the conflict and domestic instability, with at least ten killed in clashes linked to pro-Iran protests and a concurrent declaration of "open war" against Afghanistan following cross-border strikes. Dubai's status as the pre-eminent financial hub for high-net-worth individuals fleeing earlier geopolitical episodes made the Gulf city a focal point of risk. Goldman Sachs, JPMorgan, Citigroup, and Nomura instructed regional employees to work from home. The Fairmont The Palm Hotel in Dubai sustained damage in an explosion. Emirates halted all operations until Tuesday afternoon with disruption warnings extending through Thursday; Etihad and Qatar Airways followed suit. Private jet charters to Europe were reportedly reaching $350,000 as affluent travelers sought overland routes to Riyadh. China's position warrants particular attention. Beijing called for an immediate ceasefire and is assessed to be among the most economically exposed nations given that it purchases an estimated 99% of Iranian crude exports. A complete Iranian supply outage could lift oil prices by around 20%, while a Strait of Hormuz closure poses broader LNG supply risks. Asian buyers were already contacting non-Middle Eastern suppliers for early LNG deliveries to cover potential March gaps. China's annual National People's Congress is due to convene later this week, where the economic consequences of the conflict are likely to feature prominently in policy discussions. In Japan, the Toyota group reached a landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over the privatization of Toyota Industries, with the group offering ¥20,600 per share — a 9.6% increase from the prior bid — in a deal valued at approximately ¥6.7 trillion ($42.8 billion). A Bank of Japan deputy governor declined to signal any imminent rate increase. Separately, Canadian Prime Minister Mark Carney's visit to India produced a C$2.6 billion uranium supply agreement with India and agreement on terms of reference for a bilateral trade deal, reflecting both nations' effort to reduce US dependency.

Europe

European equity markets declined on Monday, with the Stoxx Europe 600 falling 1.45%, though the regional picture was uneven. The UK's FTSE 100 outperformed materially, down less than 1%, benefiting from its structural tilt toward energy, commodities, aerospace and defense, and defensive sectors — a composition Citigroup described as an effective geopolitical hedge, upgrading UK equities to overweight from underweight. UK bases, including Diego Garcia and RAF Fairford, were granted to the US for destroying Iranian missile depots following discussions with Prime Minister Keir Starmer, though Britain did not join the strikes directly. A suspected drone attack on the UK's Cyprus base occurred hours after Starmer's announcement, underlining the costs of British association with the campaign. The UK's fiscal backdrop shifted notably ahead of Chancellor Rachel Reeves' Spring Statement scheduled for Tuesday. Tax receipts came in approximately £22 billion ahead of Office for Budget Responsibility forecasts, driven by stronger-than-expected self-assessed income tax and VAT revenues. The outperformance raises the prospect of reduced gilt issuance and relieves some pressure on UK yields, which remain the highest in the G7. Reeves' fiscal headroom against the rule requiring current spending to be covered by taxation by decade's end will be closely scrutinized, though the statement was broadly anticipated to be a measured affair before this weekend's events added an external complication. European natural gas futures surged as much as 52% on Monday — the largest intraday gain since the 2022 energy crisis — as Qatar shut down the world's largest LNG export facility following an Iranian drone attack. Goldman Sachs estimates a month-long Hormuz shipping halt could cause European gas prices to more than double. Italian fiscal credibility received a modest setback after data showed it failed to meet the EU's 3% deficit ceiling last year. German Chancellor Friedrich Merz is due in Washington on Tuesday, where clarity on tariff policy for EU goods will be a central agenda item, alongside the broader geopolitical situation.

Global

The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike marks the most consequential geopolitical rupture since Russia's invasion of Ukraine, and arguably the most direct US targeting of a foreign head of state in the modern era. Oil markets reacted with Brent crude surging approximately 12.5% before partially retracing, trading above $80 per barrel. The Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately 20% of global oil exports transit — has effectively closed, with tanker traffic all but halted and more than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs announcing withdrawal of war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels starting Thursday. Saudi Arabia suspended operations at its 550,000 barrel-per-day Ras Tanura refinery following a drone strike, and QatarEnergy shut LNG production at the world's largest export plant. ICE gasoil futures surged more than 20% intraday, the largest gain since March 2022. The range of oil price scenarios remains wide. A brief conflict and rapid regime transition might return Brent toward $60 per barrel; a full Hormuz disruption could push prices to $125 or beyond; a middle scenario of constrained but continuing flows might see an $80 ceiling. The binding near-term constraint appears to be the insurance market rather than an explicit military blockade, as commercial shipping has self-suspended pending war-risk coverage restoration. OPEC+ announced a modest April production increase on Sunday, but additional volumes are of limited utility if tankers cannot safely transit the strait. Trump's objectives have remained notably fluid — variously described as taking four to five weeks or ending within days, seeking regime change on a "Venezuela model" or opening negotiations with new Iranian leadership. Iran's own signals are contradictory: President Pezeshkian vowed retaliation while security chief Larijani publicly rejected US talks even as the Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries. The Assembly of Experts must now choose Khamenei's successor, with the choice between a continuity hardliner and a more pragmatic figure carrying profound implications for the conflict's duration and the region's stability. The Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, whose influence expanded substantially under Khamenei, represents a significant institutional actor unlikely to relinquish leverage without resistance. Markets, for their part, are trading probabilities rather than resolved outcomes — a posture that may persist for some time.
