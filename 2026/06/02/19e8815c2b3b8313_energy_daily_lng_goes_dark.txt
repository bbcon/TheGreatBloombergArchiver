Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia Energy Team Leader Stephen Stapczynski looks at efforts to unlock gas 
shipments trapped in the Middle East.

Major liquefied natural gas 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-27/adnoc-exports-another-lng-shipment-through-hormuz-to-india?cmpid=BBBXT060226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5108>
 exporters in the Persian Gulf are preparing for a potential future where 
disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz persist for longer.

In a bid to move their LNG through the critical waterway, QatarEnergy and Abu 
Dhabi National Oil Co. have begun “going dark 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-25/qatar-quietly-exports-lng-through-hormuz-destined-for-key-buyers?cmpid=BBBXT060226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5108>
,” adopting tactics typically employed by Russia’s so-calledshadow fleet 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2023-russian-oil-flows-after-sanctions/?cmpid=BBBXT060226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5108>
.

That’s got some shipments — though limited — flowing again, and could be seen 
as a test case for more.

It’s a significant shift 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/iran-war-pushes-gas-trade-to-rip-up-rulebook-as-tankers-sail-dark-through-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT060226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5108>
 for the LNG industry, where until recently almost every cargo could be tracked 
in real time and traded between well-known buyers and sellers.

Now, with the Iran war entering its fourth month, QatarEnergy and Adnoc — 
which together made up a fifth of global LNG exports last year — are turning to 
alternative practices to ship gas quietly out.

That involves switching off vessel-tracking signals. It’s a strategy that’s 
been used byRussian tankers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-02-18/russia-s-shadow-oil-tanker-fleet-becomes-everyone-else-s-problem?cmpid=BBBXT060226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5108>
 to evade detection, but the ships carrying Qatari and UAE gas are not 
sanctioned, and the companies say the measures are intended to protect tankers 
operating in a conflict zone.
Liquefied natural gas storage tanks.Photographer: Buddhika 
Weerasinghe/Bloomberg
The adjustments extend to hiring, with ships forced to replace captains and 
crew members who are reluctant to navigate the strait.

In one case, a Qatari vessel hired crew through an Indonesian recruiter that 
had previously supplied personnel for ships transporting sanctioned Russian LNG.

Following several successful Hormuz transits, empty LNG carriers are now 
returning to the region to load additional cargoes. Four vessels linked to 
QatarEnergy and Adnoc have entered the Gulf of Oman in the last few days, near 
the eastern entrance to Hormuz.

One stopped transmitting its location two days ago, suggesting it may already 
be making the passage through the strait.

As the war drags on, the global energy industry is watching closely for any 
diplomatic breakthrough 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/trump-tries-to-stop-israel-s-lebanon-push-derailing-an-iran-deal?cmpid=BBBXT060226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260602&utm_content=5108>
 that may allow normal traffic to resume. But one thing is becoming clear: The 
region’s two biggest LNG exporters are no longer prepared to wait for that 
outcome.

—Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News
