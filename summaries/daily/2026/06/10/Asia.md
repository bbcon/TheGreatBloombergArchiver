**Indonesia: Monetary Credibility Under Stress**
Bank Indonesia's emergency hike is a tacit acknowledgment that last month's surprise increase failed to stabilize investor sentiment. The rupiah's decline had become increasingly disorderly, and foreign-exchange reserves fell for a fifth consecutive month in May — the longest losing streak since 2018. Capital Economics describes the hike as "merely acting as a sticking plaster," and the fundamental issue remains Prabowo Subianto's interventionist economic agenda rather than external shocks alone. Restoring confidence likely requires a policy shift from the government, which has not yet materialized.

**China: Diverging Price Dynamics**
China's CPI stagnation alongside surging PPI captures the economy's central tension: the Iran war has reignited upstream cost pressures, but deficient domestic demand prevents transmission to consumer prices. Beijing is drawing on commercial crude reserves to cushion the supply shock, a strategy that is also helping moderate global oil prices at the margin. At the Bloomberg Invest Hong Kong conference, investors from BlackRock and Apollo highlighted China's $25 trillion in household savings as a potential catalyst — contingent on a housing recovery and improved consumer confidence.
