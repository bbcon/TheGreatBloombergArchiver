A subsidy skirmish is heating up again, only this time the focus isn’t China’s 
pervasive state support across the global economy drawing the concerns.

US Trade Representative Jamieson Greer, asked Tuesday on CNBC about new 
European Investment Bankfinancing 
<https://www.eib.org/en/press/all/2026-228-eib-commits-eur3-billion-to-airbus-to-boost-europe-s-industrial-base-and-technological-edge-in-aerospace-innovation?utm_source=socialmedia&utm_medium=Social&utm_campaign=na&utm_content=na&utm_term=na_na&utm_id=2026-06-29_01_en>
 for Airbus announced in late June, said he reached out to his counterparts in 
Brussels about the deal and is speaking with Boeing, too.

The EIB, the lending arm of the European Union, said the money will be used to 
help “underpin enterprising innovation” across countries including France, 
Germany and Spain and that the amount was the largest corporate loan the EIB 
has ever authorized.

Read More: Airbus Says It Can Double Profit With Boost to Aircraft Output 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-21/airbus-targets-earnings-boost-plans-buyback-to-help-lift-stock?cmpid=BBD072226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6695>

“We’re looking at it very closely,” Greer said 
<https://www.cnbc.com/2026/07/21/trump-trade-tariffs-greer-section-301.html> of 
the €3 billion ($3.42 billion) package. “We want to make sure that our 
companies can compete on a level playing field, and if the European Union is 
giving unfair loans, then we need to take care of it and address it.”

Speaking at an event yesterday, Airbus CFO Thomas Toepfer defended the EIB 
arrangement and explained how it’s is among several financing options at the 
company’s disposal. He said its terms are set “completely at market rates.”

Flexible Financing

“We’ve only drawn a very minor portion,” he said. “It’s part of our policy how 
we want to finance the company, with a buffer when this flexibility might be 
needed.”

Toepfer and much of the aviation world are meeting at the Farnborough airshow 
near London this week, with Toulouse, France-based Airbus and Arlington, 
Virginia-based Boeingpulling in orders <bbg://news/stories/TIIXGGKK3NY8> from 
airlines and leasing companies.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/china-s-trade-surplus-with-eu-hits-record-as-tensions-intensify?cmpid=BBD072226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6695>

As longtime trade observers might recall, preceding President Donald Trump’s 
tariff onslaught were the subsidy wars. Two of the main combatants were Airbus 
and Boeing, which squared off at the World Trade Organization accusing each 
other of benefitting from state aid dating back to the 1980s.

In 2011 the WTO ruled 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2011-12-01/eu-says-it-has-complied-with-wto-ruling-on-airbus-subsidies?cmpid=BBD072226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6695>
 that European governments provided Airbus with billions of dollars of illegal 
subsidized financing that enabled Airbus to launch its widebody and short-haul 
planes. The EU opened a parallel case that successfully argued Boeing benefited 
from state subsidies as well as space and military contracts, which defrayed 
the cost of civilian aircraft development.

The cases wound their way 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2020-07-31/why-the-boeing-vs-airbus-fight-is-coming-to-a-head-quicktake?cmpid=BBD072226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6695>
 through the WTO dispute process until 2019, when the Geneva-based trade body 
authorized the US to retaliate with tariffs against $7.5 billion worth of EU 
exports annually. In October 2020 the EU won WTO permission to hit back with 
tariffs on $4 billion of American goods a year.

‘Can’t Accept’

Fast forward a few years and the US and the EU are now locked in separate 
struggles to preserve their industrial bases against intensifying competition 
from China. Airplane manufacturing ranks alongside autos, semiconductors, AI 
and pharmaceuticals at the heart of efforts to bolster national and economic 
security.

In June, EU member states agreed to extend the suspension of tariffs set to 
hit American products related to the Airbus-Boeing dispute, leaving it 
open-ended.

Read More: EU Extends Tariff Suspension in Boeing-Airbus Trade Dispute 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/eu-extends-tariff-suspension-in-boeing-airbus-trade-dispute?cmpid=BBD072226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6695>

In his CNBC interview, Greer noted that Washington and Brussels had settled 
the matter since the WTO’s rulings but indicated the latest EIB financing for 
the Boeing rival had raised eyebrows.

“We can’t accept a situation where companies don’t have to operate under 
market-based systems, where Boeing has to operate and compete against an Airbus 
that gets unfair loans or loans that are unfair with respect to everything that 
we’re trying to do in America,” he said.

Asked in late June whether the loan could hit the planemakers’ truce, EIB 
President Nadia Calviño told reporters that “this has nothing to do with any 
other program that may be undertaken by national governments or the European 
Union. This is a normal loan with normal market conditions.”

According to WTO documents 
<https://www.wto.org/english/tratop_e/dispu_e/cases_e/ds316_e.htm>, a panel 
found that all 12 challenged EIB loans to Airbus units between 1988 to 2002 
were subsidies. But none were specific and the panel “therefore dismissed the 
US claims in respect of the EIB loans from further consideration.”
