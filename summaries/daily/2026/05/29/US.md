**Equity Markets**
US equities held at all-time highs heading into the final session of May, with the S&P 500 up 4.9% for the month and the Nasdaq 100 up 10%, following a 16% gain in April. The rally is being led by mega-cap technology, with Dell's premarket surge of 40% — after its AI server revenue outlook far exceeded consensus — lifting Hewlett Packard Enterprise (+20%) and Super Micro Computer (+12%) simultaneously. The AI infrastructure theme continues to dominate earnings surprises, and strategists at Citadel Securities note the market conversation has shifted from hedging downside to identifying remaining sidelined cash as the next marginal buyer.

**Monetary Policy**
The first Bessent-Warsh breakfast yielded a carefully calibrated public signal: the Treasury secretary did not request rate cuts, framing his expectations around Warsh doing "the right thing for inflation and growth" — language that pointedly invokes the Fed's statutory dual mandate rather than political objectives. Minneapolis Fed President Kashkari, speaking in Seoul, reinforced the cautious stance, warning that elevated energy prices and the risk of unanchored inflation expectations preclude declaring victory. Markets have incrementally repriced toward a Fed hike by early 2027, providing modest support to the dollar, though the Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index's monthly gain of 0.7% is attracting skepticism about follow-through.

**Corporate and Political**
Beyond technology, Gap fell 15% after cutting its sales outlook, underscoring uneven consumer spending. IBM gained 6% on a $10 billion quantum computing commitment. In Texas, a little-known hard-right candidate defeated an oil-industry-backed incumbent in the Railroad Commission primary, unsettling Permian Basin operators who had mobilized backing from Occidental, ExxonMobil, Chevron, and ConocoPhillips — a result that introduces genuine regulatory uncertainty into the US's most prolific producing basin.
