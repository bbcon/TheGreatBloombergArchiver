**China Capital Flows and Currency Dynamics**

The most substantive Asia-Pacific development to emerge from this week's coverage concerns the quiet recycling of China's external surplus into global markets. Bloomberg reporting highlights an estimated $1.2 trillion in cumulative trade proceeds that appear to have been redirected into offshore deposits, gold, and foreign securities rather than converted onshore through the formal FX settlement system. The phenomenon warrants attention on several dimensions. The persistent gap between recorded export proceeds and onshore FX conversion suggests Chinese exporters and corporates continue to harbor negative expectations for the renminbi, a dynamic the PBoC appears to be tolerating rather than resisting — consistent with a managed, gradual currency adjustment rather than an aggressive defense. The offshore retention of these flows dampens the domestic liquidity impulse that would otherwise accompany a trade surplus of this magnitude, reinforcing the case for additional onshore monetary easing or fiscal stimulus to sustain activity. At the global level, the redirection of Chinese surpluses into DM sovereign bonds and risk assets has plausibly contributed at the margin to compressed term premia and resilient equity valuations. The durability of this channel depends on whether CNY expectations stabilize or deteriorate further, and whether Beijing moves to tighten capital account management — both of which remain open questions.
