Daily Brief

Tuesday, March 03, 2026

US

US financial markets entered a period of significant stress on Tuesday as the fourth day of the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran concentrated investor attention on the intersection of geopolitical risk and domestic inflation dynamics. S&P 500 futures fell 1.88%, extending Monday's session in which the main indexes had held roughly flat — a relative calm now appearing fragile. The more durable market signal came from the bond market, where the 10-year Treasury yield rose to 4.104%, climbing six basis points on the session, and briefly recording its largest single-day decline in price since the tariff-driven turmoil of last April. The catalyst for renewed rate anxiety predates the conflict but is now amplified by it. The ISM manufacturing survey for February showed the prices-paid component surging to its highest level since 2022, a reading that surprised to the upside by the widest margin on record. Respondents were pointed in their frustration, with one manufacturer noting that domestic steel and aluminum tariffs were simultaneously raising input costs and compressing demand — precisely the opposite of their intended effect. Former Treasury Secretary Janet Yellen, speaking at an industry conference, said the Iran conflict leaves the Federal Reserve "even more on hold," a view that aligns with market pricing, which has continued to pare rate-cut expectations. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon captured the prevailing mood among institutional leaders, cautioning that market exuberance does not match what he described as an economy that is merely "fine," and flagging higher inflation as a potential spoiler. The Trump administration indicated it has no immediate plans to tap the Strategic Petroleum Reserve in response to surging crude prices, despite WTI rising more than 8% to nearly $77 a barrel. On the corporate side, MongoDB shares sank 27% following a weaker-than-expected revenue forecast, dragging down enterprise software names broadly. Blackstone's flagship private credit fund faced a record redemption request equivalent to 7.9% of shares, approximately $3.8 billion, the latest indicator of mounting stress in that asset class.

Asia

Asian equity markets bore the sharpest losses of any region on Tuesday, with MSCI's Asia-Pacific benchmark declining approximately 3% as the Iran conflict entered its fourth day with no visible path to de-escalation. The standout move was in South Korea, where the Kospi fell 7.2% as markets reopened following a long weekend — a severe repricing that nevertheless leaves the index up 37% year-to-date, a performance driven largely by semiconductor exporters Samsung and SK Hynix benefiting from AI-driven demand. The magnitude of Tuesday's decline may partly reflect catch-up selling, but it underscores the vulnerability of tech-heavy markets to a sharp reversal in risk appetite. China's response to the conflict has been notably calibrated. Beijing issued its most direct call to date for all parties to ensure safe passage through the Strait of Hormuz, a waterway through which nearly half of China's crude imports transited as recently as December. Chinese solar companies — beneficiaries of renewed energy-security sentiment — were among the few gainers in the region, with some inverter manufacturers rising more than 10% on the week as investors reconsidered the strategic value of domestically produced clean energy. On the diplomatic front, US and Chinese trade negotiators are reportedly scheduled to meet in Paris in mid-March, with Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, USTR Jamieson Greer, and Vice Premier He Lifeng expected to discuss the framework for a potential Trump-Xi summit. The meeting's continuation despite the Iran crisis appears to signal that both sides view the bilateral economic agenda as distinct from the geopolitical environment. Separately, Australia's Reserve Bank Governor Michele Bullock signaled that the central bank's patient approach to inflation has limits and that a rate hike remains possible this month — a reminder that the Iran-driven energy shock compounds pre-existing inflation management challenges across the region.

Europe

European markets absorbed some of the heaviest losses outside of Asia, with the Stoxx Europe 600 falling 3.16% and the Stoxx 50 declining 2.5% the previous session. Banks and insurers led the selloff, with major names including Société Générale, UniCredit, Santander, and BNP Paribas each declining more than 4%, pushing both sectors into negative territory for the year. The selloff reflected a combination of higher sovereign yields, deteriorating growth expectations, and uncertainty over the conflict's duration. UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves delivered her spring fiscal statement against an unusually turbulent backdrop. The statement was designed to be deliberately low-key, consistent with the government's pledge to confine major tax-and-spend decisions to a single annual event and avoid the market disruption that characterized recent fiscal communications. The conflict overshadowed the occasion, and Prime Minister Keir Starmer sharpened his distancing from US military action, stating that the UK does not support "regime change from the skies" and questioning the legal basis for the strikes. President Trump publicly criticized Starmer for refusing a US request to use British military bases for operations against Iran, adding bilateral friction to an already complicated alliance dynamic. On the economic outlook, analysts have framed the conflict's consequences for Europe in binary terms: if hostilities conclude within roughly a month, the energy price shock may amount to a manageable speed bump for a continent that had been showing signs of modest recovery, supported by increased German fiscal spending. A prolonged disruption would constitute a materially more serious challenge, given Europe's dependence on Middle Eastern fuel — approximately 30% of EU diesel and kerosene imports originate from the region, with roughly two-thirds of those volumes transiting the Strait of Hormuz.

Global

The defining market dynamic of March 3 was the collision between a genuine geopolitical shock and an already-complicated global inflation and monetary policy outlook. With the Strait of Hormuz effectively halted for tanker traffic, Brent crude rose nearly 5% on Tuesday to top $80 a barrel, and has now reached levels last seen in July 2024. European natural gas prices surged more than 20% for a second consecutive session following Iranian drone attacks on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility — the world's largest, accounting for approximately one-fifth of global LNG supply. QatarEnergy has provided no timeline for restart, and the uncertainty is acute: if the outage extends for weeks, Europe and Asia will compete for a finite pool of alternative supply, pushing prices materially higher. The 2022 energy crisis offers a reference point, though current price levels remain far below that episode's peak. The monetary policy implications are increasingly difficult for central banks to navigate. A sustained $20-per-barrel oil shock could add up to one percentage point to global inflation, according to Société Générale, at a moment when most major economies have not yet returned inflation to target. Traders are unwinding rate-cut expectations across the UK, Turkey, Hungary, and potentially the US, producing the counterintuitive result that sovereign bonds — normally a haven — are selling off alongside equities. Gold wavered after earlier gains, while the US dollar extended its advance, with the DXY index rising almost 1% on Monday and extending gains Tuesday, breaking above its 200-day moving average. Emerging markets face a differentiated but broadly negative picture. The FTSE EM index fell 2% Monday, its worst session in eleven months, with EM currencies under pressure as the dollar strengthened. Oil-exporting EMs such as Brazil and Colombia stand to benefit from elevated prices, while net importers including Mexico, Chile, and much of Asia face headwinds. The container shipping industry, gathering for its major annual conference in Long Beach, California, effectively abandoned its pre-conflict agenda of managing overcapacity, with leading voices warning that the logistics disruption could define 2026 as completely as prior crises shaped recent years. Global supply chains, only recently recovering from successive shocks, appear to be entering another period of elevated uncertainty.
