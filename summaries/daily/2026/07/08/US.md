• Trump declared the Iran ceasefire "over," triggering a renewed wave of US military strikes on more than 80 sites; the 10-year Treasury yield rose 8.2 basis points to 4.55%.
• S&P 500 futures slid approximately 1% while energy stocks rallied sharply; the USMCA non-renewal adds a further layer of trade policy uncertainty.

**Trade Data**
The May trade deficit widened to its largest reading since March 2025, just before Liberation Day tariffs, as imports rose 3.3%—the strongest monthly gain in three months—while exports fell 3.2%, their first decline in five months. The export weakness was concentrated in nonmonetary gold, which fell approximately 52% month-on-month, likely reflecting the unwinding of earlier tariff-driven stockpiling flows from Switzerland. Stripping out gold, goods exports still declined 2.4%. AI-driven capital goods imports remained elevated. Bloomberg Economics flagged "downside risk" to Q2 GDP from the widening gap, while noting the longer-term backdrop reflects structural twin deficits amplified by a large fiscal shortfall.

**Markets and Iran Escalation**
Trump's declaration that the Iran ceasefire is "over," coming alongside fresh US strikes on more than 80 Iranian sites and revocation of Iran's oil-sales waiver, jolted markets late in the session. The 10-year Treasury yield rose 8.2 basis points to 4.55%, partially puncturing confidence that inflation had been contained. Energy stocks outperformed—Exxon gained 2.6%, Occidental 3.9%—while semiconductor and memory names extended recent declines, with Micron falling 6.1% in premarket trading. Amazon's $25 billion bond offering attracted demand of only 1.6 times deal size, well below the year's four-times average, suggesting appetite for hyperscaler credit is cooling.
