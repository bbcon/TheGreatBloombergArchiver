Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Power & Natural Disasters Americas Team Leader Brian Eckhouse explores how 
renewables are challenging coal for global electricity supremacy.

The Trump administration reiterated its commitment to coal power 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-16/us-rejects-centerpoint-energy-s-bid-to-close-indiana-coal-plant?cmpid=BBBXT042326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260423>
 this week when US Energy SecretaryChris Wright 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/5777543> testified before a 
congressional committee in Washington.

“Coal has been the largest source of global electricity since the data began,” 
hesaid Tuesday <https://bsky.app/profile/atrupar.com/post/3mjzccxzelc2t>. “I’m 
pretty confident coal will lead the world in global electricity production when 
I die.”

The comments from Wright, 61, would seem to imply that the former energy 
executive sees a couple of decades at least where King Coal holds on to the 
throne.

Yet there are clear signs that its reign is in doubt.

The International Energy Agency, for one, says coal only narrowly beat 
renewables 
<https://www.iea.org/reports/global-energy-review-2026/electricity-supply> 
globally last year in terms of electricity generation. Ember, a think tank 
focused on the energy transition, said this week that it estimates renewables 
actuallysurpassed coal 
<https://ember-energy.org/latest-insights/global-electricity-review-2026/?utm_source=newsletter&utm_medium=email&utm_campaign=newsletter_axiosgenerate&stream=top>
 in 2025.
Turbines from the Roth Rock wind farm spin next to the Mettiki Coal processing 
plant in Oakland, Maryland.Photographer: Chip Somodevilla/Getty Images North 
America
To be clear, the polluting fuel is likely to be a key source of power for many 
years to come, even if the transition to cleaner alternatives accelerates. The 
black stuff is readily available and a relatively easy way to keep the lights 
on, especially in times ofdisruption to energy supplies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-29/iran-war-s-gas-supply-shock-pushes-top-consumers-back-to-coal?cmpid=BBBXT042326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260423>
.

But it’s also true that renewables broadly continue to experience strong 
growth. While global coal-power generation registered a small decline in 2025, 
electricity from renewables jumped 8.5%, according to the IEA.

Even in the US, where President Donald Trump 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/1252249> is pushing back hard against 
renewables, solar is still making dramatic gains. A government report earlier 
this year found that of all the utility-scale capacity additions planned for 
2026, a little more than half —43.4 gigawatts 
<https://www.eia.gov/todayinenergy/detail.php?id=67205> — is solar.

In fact, that same report found that renewables and battery storage account 
for about 93% of planned utility-scale electricity additions this year. Natural 
gas, another fossil fuel favored by Trump’s administration, is pegged at just 
7%.

Coal, once again 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/us-plans-first-new-coal-fired-power-plant-since-2013-in-alaska?cmpid=BBBXT042326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260423>
, won’t see any new US capacity.

—Brian Eckhouse, Bloomberg News

Chart of the day

France’s forecasting office flagged suspected tampering 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-23/france-probes-weather-data-glitch-after-surge-in-polymarket-bets?cmpid=BBBXT042326_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260423>
 with weather sensors at the country’s largest airport and referred the case to 
police, after detecting unusual readings alongside heavy betting on a popular 
prediction market. Automated temperature readings taken at the Charles de 
Gaulle hub spiked unexpectedly on April 6 and April 15. The data is important 
for the safe operation of the airport, but is also used to settle contracts for 
daily high temperatures on Polymarket, where weather betting has boomed.

BNEF today

The UK’s moves to reform energy markets 
<https://www.gov.uk/government/news/decisive-action-to-break-influence-of-gas-on-electricity-prices>
 won’t achieve the government’s stated aim of delinking electricity and gas 
prices, according to BloombergNEF. A more effective way to meet that goal would 
be to press ahead with — and even accelerate — plans to improve the power grid 
and increase system flexibility, hastening the shift away from gas-power toward 
cheaper and cleaner technologies, BNEF said.
