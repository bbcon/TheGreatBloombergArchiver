US War Aims? Take Your Pick

President Donald Trump is far more considerate than people give him credit 
for. Since world markets closed Friday, he spent the weekend posting startling 
pronouncements on his next steps in the war with Iran. It was nice of him to do 
so while markets were closed. During trading hours he would have caused mayhem. 
As it was, everyone watched the blinding switchbacks with no new money at stake 
— and a cautious open to Asian trading suggests nobody feels much wiser.

Shortly after markets closed last week, he posted at length on Truth Social, 
saying that he was planning to wind down the war:

We are getting very close to meeting our objectives as we consider winding 
down our great Military efforts in the Middle East with respect to the 
Terrorist Regime of Iran.

After giving a list of objectives that he said had been met, or were close to 
being achieved, he added that keeping open the Strait of Hormuz was not one of 
them, and that the US could disengage even while Iran effectively closed it to 
traffic:

The Hormuz Strait will have to be guarded and policed, as necessary, by other 
Nations who use it — The United States does not! If asked, we will help these 
Countries in their Hormuz efforts, but it shouldn’t be necessary once Iran’s 
threat is eradicated. Importantly, it will be an easy Military Operation for 
them.

In other words, the TACO (Trump Always Chickens Out) moment had arrived. Many 
(Points of Return included) thought it would be impossible for him to declare 
victory and walk away until the Strait had reopened. This showed him rehearsing 
an argument that he could do just that — and lumber the rest of the world with 
an almighty problem.

Then, at 7:45 p.m. on Saturday, he followed up with  this 
<https://bloom.bg/4sZhhdv>: 

If Iran doesn’t FULLY OPEN, WITHOUT THREAT, the Strait of Hormuz, within 48 
HOURS from this exact point in time, the United States of America will hit and 
obliterate their various POWER PLANTS, STARTING WITH THE BIGGEST ONE FIRST!

If anyone wants to argue that the second posting is consistent with the one 24 
hours earlier, they are welcome to try. It isn’t. The risk implied by official 
US policy moved in 24 hours from a very messy end to hostilities to an extreme 
escalation.

What should markets make of this? It’s best to assume that the second 
pronouncement came after the president was informed that the US did in fact 
need the Strait to stay open. Walking away with Iran in command of it isn't 
going to work. We can also assume that the administration has now decided that 
the politics decisively favor a swift resolution.

That means escalation in the short term. If the US can neutralize Iran’s 
ability to close the Strait that will be market positive. If it can’t, that 
will be reason to sell. A lot now rests on how effective the US military’s next 
move is. Markets can pick up where they left off Friday, and keep watching.

When Is a Correction Correct?

One of the more irritating and arbitrary market definitions is that any 10% 
fall from peak to trough can be called a “correction.” Beyond implying that the 
price is now “correct,” the definition takes no account of how expensive the 
security was in the first place. If it was already cheap, a 10% decline isn’t 
so much a correction as a body blow.

That said, the steady drip of bad news out of the Gulf had by the end of the 
conflict’s third week brought many of the world’s most important equity indexes 
10% lower than their peak. Japan, Europe, and emerging markets have all now 
corrected as have the Magnificent Seven platform stocks that dominate the 
financial world. The average US stock remains conspicuously incorrect:

What’s strange is that if we take “correction” to mean an adjustment to a 
company’s valuation, the US has corrected far more in a process that continued 
uninterrupted the last three weeks. The US premium — the extra multiple of 
prospective earnings it commands compared to the rest of the world — peaked 
amid the post-election excitement at the end of 2024. It reduced sharply around 
Liberation Day in April, and it is repeating that now.

Price makes it look as though the US is uncorrected. Based on relative 
valuation, a correction is underway:

US stock prices are holding up because markets think that it will be less 
affected than the rest of the world by the interruption to oil supplies. We can 
proxy that with the gap between the Brent and West Texas Intermediate crude 
benchmarks, reflecting the oil price in the US and globally. It has widened 
dramatically, with futures for the next month now showing an even wider gap 
than they did during the worst of the Ukraine invasion. More significantly, 
over nine months it is even wider than it was then:

This feeds into profits outlook. Since war broke out, Bloomberg finds that 
first-quarter forecasts for the rest of the world have edged down a bit. In the 
US they are up, while for the second quarter they have risen dramatically. That 
likely reflects the positive prognosis for US oil companies:

The risk is that if the conflict lengthens or takes unexpected directions, 
earnings forecasts will come down. For now, with the war as confused as ever, 
it’s hard to say that market prices are correct — but neither are they 
obviously incorrect in any way that creates opportunities for contrarians.


Trump’s Cake

President Trump's agitation over the Strait of Hormuz betrays his commitment 
to his signature policy, “drill, baby, drill.” His executive order seeking to
unleash America's untapped energy potential <https://bloom.bg/3TDnE6U> was 
premised on the notion that more supply is the cure for volatility. Reality 
tells a different story.

A year after the directive, subdued oil prices have discouraged drillers from 
aggressive expansion. Now war has pushed prices higher and, in theory, made new 
production economically viable. If the Friday presidential posting that the US 
is happy for the Strait to stay closed is taken at face value, then Trump is 
very directly forcing domestic producers to amp up their supply.

But as we’ve seen, US policy isn’t clear. And the same price surge that 
improves the calculus for drillers hits differently in the broader economy, 
raising costs for consumers and businesses when many think the president’s job 
is to reduce them:

The 40% surge in Brent crude futures compares favorably with the over 30% 
surge in the State Street exchange-traded fund of exploration and production 
companies, the cohort favored by this administration. But the White House is 
fast realizing that the advance for Big Oil isn't all-around good news for 
Washington’s agenda.

Rising gas prices make it harder for the Federal Reserve, still reeling from 
its failure to curb growing inflation in 2021, to deliver on Trump’s clamor for 
lower rates — which is ironic, as the impact of tariffs appears to be fading 
and would otherwise have made cuts easier. Futures now price a 40% chance of a 
Fed hike this year — and it’s the only major central bank for which hikes are 
not fully priced:

Meanwhile, clean energy’s survival suggests that pragmatism is trumping 
ideology. In the global artificial intelligence race, the US faces a severe 
power constraint. Unlike China, the power supply pipeline is dry, making the 
ideological antagonism toward alternative energy sources that much harder to 
explain. For the hyperscalers building data centers, that distinction in energy 
source is meaningless. This helps explain how clean energy ETFs move in 
lockstep with data-center builders:

Even for Big Oil, which benefits from higher energy prices, there is growing 
disquiet. Iran’s missiles have already dealt severe damage to facilities that 
cost a fortune and years to bring on stream. TD Cowen estimates that Exxon and 
France’s TotalEnergies have 10% of their Middle East cash flow exposed to 
curtailed production, mainly in Qatar.

Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex, in which Exxon is a joint venture partner, has 
suffered damage that couldtake up to five years to repair. 
<https://bloom.bg/4sZvNSq> Shell's gas-to-liquids plant in the same complex was 
also impacted. These disruptions fundamentally shift the risk profile of global 
energy and digital infrastructure, with only a limited boost to US energy 
policy. Columbia University’s Ira Joseph points out that the conflict catalyzes 
two divergent trends: A rush for new LNG export projects and a strategic 
reshoring of data-center infrastructure. Both would mean higher US gas demand.

The question remains whether such “unintended” benefits justify the price 
consumers are paying at the pump. Even if the Strait is soon reopened, via 
diplomacy or a successful military operation, a return to normalcy will not be 
immediate. Infrastructure has been compromised, and investors are unlikely to 
commit to rebuilding without credible assurances against renewed disruption. If 
affordability fails to improve, the responsibility will be difficult to deflect 
— even if oil companies do, indeed, “drill, baby, drill.”

—Richard Abbey
