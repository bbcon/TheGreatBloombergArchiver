Thursday, June 11, 2026

US

  • US CPI rose to 4.0%+ YoY in May — a three-year high — though core inflation (ex-food and energy) came in below consensus, limiting the immediate policy implications.
  • NFIB small business optimism slipped below expectations in May, giving up all post-election gains; the NFIB prices index reached its highest reading since 1981.
  • The Fed is widely expected to hold rates at next week's FOMC, Kevin Warsh's first meeting as chair; market-based inflation expectations continued to decline, signaling perceived transience.
  • Oracle shares fell approximately 10% in after-hours trading after reporting higher-than-expected data center capex; the Nasdaq 100 remains down roughly 7% from last week's peak but is still up 12% year-to-date.

Inflation and Monetary Policy

May CPI topped 4% year-over-year for the first time in approximately three years, driven heavily by energy. However, core inflation — excluding food and energy — came in below consensus, and markets adjudged the print insufficiently alarming to force a rate hike at next week's FOMC. That conclusion appears well-founded in the near term but may be premature on a longer horizon. The Fed's preferred "supercore" measure (services ex-housing) is rising sharply, the Atlanta Fed's sticky-price index has moved above 3%, and both trimmed-mean and median CPI exceeded headline core. CPI excluding shelter now exceeds 4%, making it difficult to attribute current inflation primarily to housing's well-understood lag dynamics.

Small Business and Corporate Divergence

The NFIB May optimism index declined below expectations, erasing post-election gains entirely. More than 70% of small business owners cited supply-chain disruptions; capital expenditure fell to its lowest since 2009; and hiring plans deteriorated. The NFIB prices survey — which has historically led inflation inflection points by nine months — currently signals reacceleration. By contrast, larger firms demonstrate greater resilience, aided by scale, capital access, and AI deployment capacity. SpaceX's IPO, set for Friday with demand exceeding four times available supply, exemplifies continued investor enthusiasm at the large-cap end, even as PIMCO warned that a "credit loss cycle" may be underway among more leveraged borrowers. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's financial regulator fined Coupang a record 624.7 billion won ($409 million) for a data breach affecting approximately 34 million accounts.
  • Chinese regulators publicly rebuked Alibaba and JD.com over allegedly misleading promotional claims during the "618" shopping festival; shares of both companies declined.
  • China deployed maritime agency vessels to question foreign merchant ships near Taiwan — the first such action — in what analysts describe as rehearsal for a potential maritime quarantine.

Japan and Inflation Dynamics

Japan's May corporate goods price data reinforced the picture of sustained imported cost inflation, with the monthly increase of 0.9% coming alongside an upward revision to April. The Iran conflict's continued pressure on energy costs is the primary transmission mechanism. These figures strengthen the case for the Bank of Japan to maintain its gradual policy normalization trajectory, though the BOJ has not yet signaled any acceleration in the tightening path.

China: Regulatory and Geopolitical Pressures

The domestic regulatory environment tightened further as state television CCTV reported that China's market regulator criticized leading e-commerce platforms for misleading subsidy promotions, reflecting Beijing's ongoing effort to curtail destructive price competition eroding corporate margins. Separately, China's assertion of jurisdictional claims around Taiwan through maritime interrogations of foreign vessels marks a tactical escalation. Analysts at Tamkang University described the maneuver as rehearsing a broader quarantine capability. The combination of tighter domestic regulation, AI-related labor concerns flagged by the Workers' Daily, and Taiwan Strait tensions presents a complex backdrop for investors assessing Chinese risk assets. ---

Europe

  • The ECB is set to raise its deposit rate by 25 basis points to 2.25% — its first hike since 2023 — in direct response to war-driven inflation exceeding prior tolerance thresholds.
  • Stoxx Europe 600 rose 0.8% in early trading; ASML, ASMI, and BE Semiconductor each gained 3% or more on Oracle's elevated capex figures.
  • Hugo Boss shares rose 8.3% in Frankfurt after Frasers Group offered approximately €2 billion ($2.3 billion) to acquire the remainder of the German fashion label.
  • UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves announced plans to ease double-taxation burdens on wealthy US investors relocating to Britain.

ECB Policy Pivot

The ECB's anticipated 25-basis-point hike represents a meaningful shift after more than two years on hold. Policymakers concluded that persistent oil-price elevation — now exceeding three months — combined with evidence that price pressures are broadening beyond energy, made further inaction untenable. Market expectations are building for additional tightening this year, reflecting the view that a single move will be insufficient if the Iran conflict and Hormuz disruption continue. This puts the ECB in an awkward position: tightening into a growth environment already pressured by elevated energy costs risks amplifying the economic drag from the war.

UK and Corporate Developments

Chancellor Reeves' move to address double-taxation on US LLC members signals a deliberate strategy to attract capital from the United States amid bilateral trade and investment repositioning. The UK housing market showed tentative signs of stabilization per agent surveys, though the macro backdrop of elevated rates and persistently high services inflation remains challenging. On the corporate side, Frasers Group's Hugo Boss bid continues Mike Ashley's acquisition strategy in European fashion; at current terms the offer implies a modest premium that markets have received constructively. ---

Peru: Institutional Stability Amid Political Flux

The Peruvian central bank's expected rate hold, under Governor Julio Velarde's nearly two-decade tenure, illustrates an institution that has maintained credibility through persistent political instability — Peru is on its ninth president in a decade. The presidential count remains tight, with the outcome potentially determined by overseas votes. The investment implications appear limited in either direction: Sánchez lacks legislative support for market-concerning structural reforms, while Fujimori's Fuerza Popular controls the largest congressional bloc, providing greater governing capacity. Velarde's continuity arguably matters more for investor confidence than the electoral outcome itself.

Regional Trade and Sovereign Credit

Argentina's S&P upgrade reflects tangible progress in fiscal consolidation and debt management under the Milei administration, though significant execution risk persists. Brazil's outreach to Mexico on energy cooperation and the impending tariff discussions with the US Trade Representative indicate Lula is pursuing diversified trade relationships while managing bilateral tensions with Washington. Trump's statement that he will not reauthorize USMCA sets up a protracted renegotiation with direct implications for Mexico's manufacturing-export model — a significant medium-term risk for Mexican assets that markets are beginning to price. ---

  • Brent crude traded near $92/barrel, with December forward contracts reflecting continued upward pressure but not a disorderly spike; oil has remained below $100/barrel for more than 100 days of conflict.
  • Fertilizer prices, which peaked above $900/ton in April following Hormuz near-closure, have since fallen roughly 50%, suggesting some supply-chain normalization.

Iran Conflict and Energy Markets

The second consecutive day of US strikes against Iran marks a material deterioration from the April ceasefire, which has, in the assessment of multiple analysts, effectively collapsed. Yet commodity markets have absorbed the renewed escalation with notable composure: Brent reversed early gains and traded lower, and forward curves reflect elevated but not catastrophic price expectations. The explanation appears to lie in three factors — rising Hormuz traffic signals, drawdown of strategic inventories, and market confidence that the conflict will not escalate to full-scale regional war. That confidence remains an assumption, not an established fact, and represents the principal tail risk to the global macro outlook.

Monetary Policy Divergence and AI Capital Flows

The ECB's anticipated hike, the Fed's hold, and Turkey's unchanged rates collectively illustrate growing divergence among major central banks, each calibrating against different combinations of war-induced inflation, growth risk, and domestic demand dynamics. Bond market participants at KKR and elsewhere are warning that long-duration government securities no longer offer reliable shock-absorbing properties in an environment where inflation risks are structurally higher. Meanwhile, Goldman Sachs estimates hyperscaler capex could reach $1.1 trillion in 2027 — implying 45% growth versus the 22% consensus — a figure that, if realized, would have significant implications for electricity demand, nuclear investment timelines, and global capital allocation.
