 Yield surge 



Three weeks into the war, the bond market is being turned upside down. Central 
bankers have dashed the once-widespread expectations for easier monetary policy.

Short-term bonds plunged <https://bloom.bg/40IkUIW> yesterday and the selling 
is continuing today. The UK, in particular, is turning into an epicenter of the 
turmoil, with 10-year yields hitting the highest level since 2008.

The drama kicked off on Thursday after the Bank of England said it “stands 
ready to act” <https://bloom.bg/4uGB432> against a surge in inflation, leading 
traders to ramp up bets on an interest-rate hike as soon as next month.

It’s the same trend in Europe too, with Governing Council member Joachim Nagel 
saying the European Central Bank will need to consider hiking interest rates as 
soon as next month if price pressures build further. Money markets are betting 
ontwo quarter-point hikes <https://bloom.bg/4sXwT1k> from the ECB this year.

In the US, traders are no longer pricing <https://bloom.bg/3NQGgki> in any 
chance the Fed will cut rates this year.

With no sign the war is ending, investors are reacting as if inflation is here 
to stay for a while.According to Goldman Sachs’s trading desk, clients who 
previously expected a quick resolution to the Iran war are starting to have 
doubts. While some remain bullish, other clients now either anticipate a 
stock-market correction or a grind lower similar to that of 2022, the bank said.

“There was a consensus view that this was going to be over relatively 
quickly,” said Brij Khurana at Wellington Management. “The fear is now finally 
in the market that this could last a lot longer.” —Greg Ritchie 
<https://bloom.bg/4jcnAFR> and Michael MacKenzie <https://bloom.bg/422rgV4>

 Gold plunges 



Along with bonds, gold is getting hammered by the global reset in 
interest-rate expectations. It’s a massive unwind of what was one of hottest 
trades of last year.

Bullion, at about $4,699 an ounce, is down 7% on the week 
<https://bloom.bg/4t0GDIb> and more than 11% so far in March. If that move 
holds through the end of the month, it would be the biggest drop since 2008.

It’s a move is driven as much by technicals as by fundamentals. Spot prices 
broke through their 50-day moving average this week and briefly tested the 
100-day level, which has consistently acted as support since 2023.

Once-hot gold-mining stocks are tumbling <https://bloom.bg/4rKLD2v>, and 
investors are pulling cash from bullion-backed ETFs. The funds are set for a 
third week of outflows, with holdings falling more than 60 tons, data compiled 
by Bloomberg show. Higher interest rates hurt gold as it doesn’t pay interest.

“Do not buy-the-dip — there’s way too much volatility,” said Robert Gottlieb, 
a former precious-metals trader at JPMorgan. —Tatiana Darie 
<https://bloom.bg/477C4SL> and Yihui Xie <https://bloom.bg/4ip8jAN>
