China: Consumer Stagnation Deepens

China's May retail sales data delivered a sobering signal: a 0.6% year-on-year contraction, the first outside pandemic-era disruptions since 1990. While the expiration of government-supported trade-in programs for durable goods provides a partial statistical explanation, the broader trend is difficult to dismiss. Sales growth averaged 8% annually in 2019; over the past three years, the pace has run at roughly 4% — barely matching that of the vastly wealthier United States. For an economy at China's per-capita income level, stagnant consumption represents a meaningful structural failure rather than a cyclical soft patch.

The Japanification Debate

Analysts at Macquarie highlight notable parallels between China today and 1990s Japan: a burst asset bubble, subdued consumption, persistent disinflationary pressure, and an aging population. Home prices have fallen approximately 30% from their 2021 peak. However, the comparison has clear limits. China's manufacturing sector continues to gain global market share, supported by a deliberately weak yuan — in contrast to the sharp yen appreciation that hollowed out Japanese industry in the 1990s. State-directed credit flows and industrial subsidies further distinguish Beijing's policy toolkit. What is emerging may be better described as a structural policy preference for industrial accumulation over household consumption — a dynamic without a clean historical precedent. ---

China's Structural Demand Problem as a Global Risk

The most consequential cross-regional theme this week is China's deepening consumer stagnation and what it implies for the global growth outlook. An economy representing roughly 18% of global GDP that is running consumer spending growth on par with the United States — despite a per-capita income roughly one-sixth the size — represents a significant drag on global aggregate demand. For commodity exporters across Latin America, Africa, and Southeast Asia, the implications are material: Chinese import demand for consumer goods and construction-linked materials faces a structurally weaker trajectory absent a decisive policy pivot toward household income support. Beijing has shown no indication of pursuing such a pivot, with fiscal policy reportedly tightening even as domestic demand softens.

Energy Market Disruption

Russia is reportedly mulling a diesel export ban in response to Ukrainian strikes on energy infrastructure, a development that — if implemented — would tighten European and emerging market diesel supply at a moment when energy markets remain sensitive to geopolitical disruption. The situation warrants monitoring, though no formal policy announcement has been confirmed.
