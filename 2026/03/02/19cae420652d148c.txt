 Five things you need to know 



 * Stocks tumbled as  the US attack on Iran rattled global markets 
<https://bloom.bg/3Nc5JnZ>. Gold and the dollar rose as investors piled into 
havens. 
 * Crude oil  surged the most in four years <https://bloom.bg/4rMf4C9> as 
tanker traffic through the Strait of Hormuz all but halted and Saudi Arabia 
shut a big refinery. 
 * Donald Trump said the bombing campaign against Iran  could last for weeks 
<https://bloom.bg/4rUjXZR> and called on the nation’s leaders to capitulate. 
The Islamic Republic’s security chief ruled out negotiations. 
 * Money markets  scaled back wagers <https://bloom.bg/46Cbn9D> on 
interest-rate cuts in the US, UK and euro area as rising energy prices fanned 
inflation fears. 
 *  Travel was disrupted in the Middle East <https://bloom.bg/46vYUUT> and 
beyond, sending airline stocks sharply lower. Carriers across the Persian Gulf 
extended blanket flight suspensions, causing major disruptions at some of the 
world’s busiest airports. 


 ‘Fog of war’ 



For investors, only the Iran war matters for now — looking beyond the human 
toll, they’re trying to price in how long it lasts and how much it curbs the 
global economy.

Most financial markets have behaved as might be expected after the dramatic 
weekend events:Airline stocks slumped <https://bloom.bg/4rJJdSq> on the 
prospect ofhigher fuel costs <https://bloom.bg/4rUk8Ev> and travel disruption, 
defense stocks rallied and havens such as gold jumped. US Treasuries were a 
surprise (see below).

Of note: 

 * Morgan Stanley’s Mike Wilson says equities are unlikely to experience a 
sustained selloff.After similar events in the past, the S&P 500 recorded gains 
in subsequent months, his strategy team wrote. The bear case is that a 
persistent rise in oil derails the strengthening business cycle, they added. 
 * The UK stock market may hold up relatively well given its exposure to oil 
companies and military contractors, as well as defensive sectors, according to 
Citigroup. The bank  raised the UK to overweight from underweight 
<https://bloom.bg/40HQN40>. Indeed, the FTSE 100 is down less than 1%. 
 * Credit markets, already vulnerable to risks from AI and private lending, 
are wobbling. Bond spreads widened <https://bloom.bg/4rPORCE> and credit 
default swaps blew out. Some issuers are delaying bond sales 
<https://bloom.bg/40DbDl7>.
“The endgame remains highly uncertain, ranging from a relatively swift 
political exit to a broader regional spillover,” said Mathieu Racheter at 
Julius Baer. “In such a fog of war, markets tend to trade probabilities rather 
than shifting facts.” —Phil Serafino <https://bloom.bg/42HUmtw>

 Not a haven today 



Rather than rising, as a haven asset normally would, US Treasuries are under 
pressure <https://bloom.bg/4rLqPJ6>, with the yield on the 10-year note rising 
3 basis points to about 3.97%.

There are several possible explanations. The top contender would be the 
inflationary impact of higher oil prices.Sticky price pressures would make it 
harder for central banks to cut interest rates any time soon.

So perhaps the rise in yields shouldn’t have been a surprise. Société 
Générale’s Manish Kabra found that, on average, the 10-year Treasury note was 
weaker after one week, three months and six months in five oil supply shocks 
going back 50 years.

In any case, a lot was already priced in for bonds, with the 10-year yield 
last week reaching its lowest levels since the aftermath of President Trump’s 
tariff announcements in April.

So it’s possible traders were simply using Treasuries as a “cash machine” to 
free up some money to cover exposures elsewhere.

Ultimately, if the conflict lasts for an extended period, at some point the 
drag on the global economy will increase demand for Treasuries. Meanwhilegold’s 
surge <https://bloom.bg/4u44DLI> suggests that for today, at least, alternative 
assets are offering a better haven. —Paul Dobson <https://bloom.bg/3DbGBZA>
