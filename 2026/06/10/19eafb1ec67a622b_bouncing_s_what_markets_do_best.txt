Boing!

Just like Tiggers <https://bloom.bg/4vxGlcS>, or Rowan Atkinson 
<https://bloom.bg/4em0eNk>, markets like bouncing. Since Friday’s dramatic 
selloff for chip stocks, the US market has staged two impressive rebounds, at 
the open Monday and then on Tuesday afternoon. The upshot is still that the 
Nasdaq 100 is down 4.25% for the month so far, and volatility is sharply 
higher. But the market isn’t giving up its massive recent gains calmly:

We’ve established that the BTFD (“Buy The — Dip”) <https://bloom.bg/3Sh6mih> 
mentality remains strong, and this is averting a major selloff. We’ve also long 
established two broad parameters. First, the market by any trustworthy 
long-term metric looks historically expensive. But second, that’s largely 
because of phenomenally rising earning forecasts. This is most dramatic in 
semiconductors, buoyed both by massive AI-driven demand and tight supply:

Decomposing equity returns, as done here by Oxford Economics, confirms that 
the rise is primarily spurred by earnings, rather than expanding valuations:

Alternatively, Bank of America Corp.’s Savita Subramanian offers this analysis 
of the S&P 500, showing that performance is entirely driven by sectors where 
earnings forecasts have risen the most:

But there’s a snag with the “earnings forecasts are higher so it’s OK” thesis: 
Rising earnings growth expectations tend to correlate with lower returns, as 
Subramanian shows here. The intuition is clear — if you’re braced for the best, 
you’ve set yourself up for disappointment:

There’s also the problem that profits have to come from somewhere and may not 
be a sustainable basis for investing. Vincent Deluard of StoneX Capital points 
out that Alphabet Inc., Amazon.com Inc. and Nvidia Corp. reported abnormal 
income of $36 billion, 17$ billion, and $16 billion respectively in the past 
quarter, primarily from unrealized gains on their stakes inAnthropic 
<https://bloom.bg/4gb7huz>, OpenAI, and SpaceX <https://bloom.bg/43mRgdB>, all 
of which are due for IPOs this year. “These extraordinary gains accounted for 
more than two-thirds of the combined free cash flow generated by the 
Magnificent Seven stocks,” Deluard says. They are unlikely to be repeatable:

Deluard also warns that profit growth owes much to the tax cuts in last year’s 
One Big Beautiful Bill <https://bloom.bg/4vJaUwy> Act, which are not repeatable:

Corporate profits are boosted from a deluge of refund checks: OBBA-related 
income tax refunds returned $330 billion to households, $70 billion more than 
last year. Accelerated depreciation caused corporate income tax payments to 
plummet by $49 billion so far this year, despite record profits.

Beyond earnings quality, there’s the issue of just how concentrated those 
earnings are. As this chart from Societe Generale SA’s Andrew Lapthorne 
demonstrates, the profits of the MSCI All-Country (ACWI) index, including 
developed and emerging markets, are dominated by 20 mega caps:

There are 2,513 stocks in the ACWI, the most broadly diversified index in 
common use by investors. The difference between the top 20 and the “other 
2,493” is staggering. To quote Lapthorne:

Bursts of profit growth this strong typically follow a preceding slump, i.e. a 
lower base effect. That is not the case this time. Upgrades have been both 
large and highly concentrated. 2027 earnings have been revised up by roughly 
$580 billion this year, with 75% of that coming from the top 1% of stocks in 
MSCI AC World, who have been upgraded 33% this year. The remaining 99% of 
stocks have been upgraded by just 3%.

It’s the nature of capitalism that at any one time a few winners will be 
winning big, but polarization this extreme is unusual, and should be taken as a 
warning sign.

Further, the impact on returns has also been differentiated. Over time, the 
equal-weighted version of the S&P 500, in which each constituent accounts for 
0.2% of the index, outperforms the version weighted by market cap; generally, 
the most highly valued companies get that way because all the good news has 
been priced in. That’s not true at the moment. Indeed, the equal-weighted 
index, which can also be viewed as the “average stock,” is lagging in a way not 
seen since the dot-com bubble:

That gap has been driven by valuations, not earnings. The price/earnings 
ratios of the two versions of the S&P tend to be very closely aligned, but not 
now. Since late 2022 (when ChatGPT launched), the cap-weighted version has 
moved to a big premium:

Further, as this chart from Subramanian demonstrates, this market is seeing 
the most expensive stocks (judged by forward p/e multiples) outperforming 
cheaper companies. That’s unusual and tends to happen before selloffs or bear 
markets:

The dispersion in returns is also extreme, and unhealthy. The gap between the 
best and worst S&P performers is at levels not seen outside major incidents:

Within technology, gains by some (led by chips) have been at the expense of 
others (primarily software groups) leading to a spread within the sector that 
rivals even the top of the dot-com era:

Again as in 2000, this is primarily driven by outsized gains by a few 
companies. The market’s acceptance that such gains must be to an extent at the 
expense of competitors is so far only limited:

The extreme imbalances don’t necessarily mean getting out of the market 
altogether, but do argue for caution. “We see opportunity in S&P 500 stocks, 
but not the overall cap-weighted index,” said Subramanian. “Our S&P 500 
year-end target of 7100 suggests 6% downside from here.” The fundamentals of 
the critical tech sector are enough to cause some alarm:

Tech fundamentals today are healthier on many metrics (leverage, valuation, 
capital intensity) but since [November] most have worsened — cash flow 
conversion has flat-lined, investment-grade and equity supply has increased, 
buybacks as a percent of market cap have slowed and capex as a percent of 
operating cash flow for hyperscalers is forecast to reach nearly 100% by 
year-end, up from 40% in 2023.

Investing in the index involves doubling down on exactly these issues. The 
leading companies have an undeniably exciting story to tell, but diversifying 
away from them makes evident sense. The market’s latest bounces reflect this 
concern — and the US inflation numbers for May, due Wednesday, offer the next 
potential excuse for investors to get out their pogo-sticks.

Elephant in the Room
Customers at a currency exchange office in Jakarta. Photographer: Dimas 
Ardian/Bloomberg 
For any central bank, an emergency policy meeting <https://bloom.bg/4e8M7Mt> 
that results in a hike barely three weeks after ajumbo rate 
<https://bloom.bg/4vCJhoD> increase underscores that the bankers think they 
have to reassure investors as a matter of urgency. Tuesday’s Bank Indonesia 
decision is a tacit admission that Governor Perry Warjiyo’s surprise hike last 
month failed to calm investors unnerved by the country’s trajectory under 
President Prabowo Subianto.

Instead, policymakers felt compelled to reassert their credibility and halt 
the rupiah’sincreasingly disorderly decline <https://bloom.bg/4v0IVIr>. Whether 
Warjiyo’s second bite at the apple will succeed in the long run remains to be 
seen, but the currency’s lag relative to other emerging markets since Prabowo 
took office in 2024 shows why the central bank wanted to put a floor under the 
decline:

There’s no shortage of reasons for Indonesia’s underperformance. The AI trade 
and the Iran war have siphoned capital from emerging markets, especially those 
most exposed to disruptions in the Strait of Hormuz. But investors appear more 
rattled by Prabowo’sincreasingly interventionist economic 
<https://bloom.bg/4vFD3o1> agenda. As this chart shows, markets can tolerate 
external shocks but are far less forgiving of policy uncertainty:

It’s too soon to dismiss Bank Indonesia’s latest intervention. The rupiah 
recovered about 1.1%, while the MSCI Indonesia Index rallied more than 7%. 
Before the hike, the Jakarta Composite Index had tumbled 38% tobecome the worst 
performer <https://bloom.bg/4vEp4i7> this year among more than 90 global gauges 
tracked by Bloomberg. But by moving ahead of next week’s scheduled meeting, 
policymakers are clearly keeping it a priority to defend the currency. This is 
beginning to drain foreign-exchange reserves, which fell for a fifth month in 
May, the longest losing streak since 2018. To some extent, the second off-cycle 
hike in since Warjiyo took over BI eight years ago also signals that
policymakers won’t hesitate <https://bloom.bg/4e0qmhN> to deploy additional 
measures if necessary.

Is the market response a dead cat bounce or an inflection point? To quote 
Capital Economics’ Jason Tuvey, BI’s hike is “merely acting as a sticking 
plaster.” For now, Warjiyo is carrying Prabowo’s burden. Investors may admire 
his determination, but there’s little ambiguity about where the source of the 
problem, and its solution, ultimately resides:

What’s needed is a clear shift in direction by the government towards more 
investor-friendly policymaking. So far at least, this doesn’t seem to be in the 
works.

If Prabowo doesn’t rein in his interventionism, investors’ protests will 
likely intensify. Monetary policy can only do so much to narrow the widening 
risk premia on Indonesian assets and the deterioration in the economy’s 
long-term prospects:

Given the position the archipelago finds itself in, it’s increasingly clear 
that stabilizing markets and restoring investor confidence cannot be left only 
to the central bank. BI tried to get across that it wasn’t acting alone:

The already strong fiscal-monetary coordination will be continuously 
strengthened and sustained over time to remain mutually supportive and aligned.

However, the bank’s argument that the hike is a follow-up measure to stabilize 
the exchange rate amid Iran war volatility hardly acknowledges the deficit in 
fiscal policy credibility. The cost of propping up the currency could be seen in
rising bond yields <https://bloom.bg/4v4HKIa>:

Prabowo’s reluctance to reconsider his flagship policies could prove costly. 
Indonesia risks burning through foreign-exchange reserves and, more worryingly, 
eroding the credibility of BI itself. That’s a price few emerging markets can 
bear and one Prabowo can ill-afford to pay.

—Richard Abbey
