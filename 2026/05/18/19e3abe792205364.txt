Good vibes matter in geopolitics and economics. They help avoid wars and 
recessions. Then again, if all you have to go on is good vibes that probably 
spells trouble.

If there was one big takeaway from last week’s Beijing summit between US 
President Donald Trump and China’s Xi Jinping, it was the calm atmospherics. 
From the moment he set off for China to his flight home Trump seemed determined 
to be a gracious guest. And Xi was an effusive host.

But what we’ve learned since is that there may not have been much more to come 
out of thetwo days of meetings 
<https://www.whitehouse.gov/fact-sheets/2026/05/fact-sheet-president-donald-j-trump-secures-historic-deals-with-china-delivering-for-american-workers-farmers-and-industry/>
 than the vibes. That’s potentially ominous. Because there are big tests 
between now and the two leaders’ next planned meeting in September.

Both sides agreed to pursue commercial deals, map out a plan for agricultural 
purchases, and establish new boards to manage trade and investments.

Related: US Fuels Hopes for Wider China Crop Trade, Lifting Grain Prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/grains-jump-after-us-says-china-to-buy-billions-in-farm-goods?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>

The agreements themselves were vague, however. Boeing shares dropped 6% last 
week in a sign of how disappointed investors were that China’s mooted plane 
purchases weren’t detailed.

It’s also still not clear how the boards will actually work and that’s still 
up for negotiations. The scope of the trade board alone seems likely to be 
limited. Jamieson Greer, the US trade representative, said its remit would 
cover about $30 billion in trade, orhalf the value 
<https://www.census.gov/foreign-trade/balance/c5700.html> of Chinese goods 
imported by the US in the first three months of this year. That’s hardly a game 
changer.

‘Place Holder’ Summit

Stephen Roach, the former Morgan Stanley chief economist and China watcher who 
is now working on a new book at Yale,over the weekend 
<https://stephenroach.substack.com/p/a-place-holder-summit> judged the Beijing 
meetings a “place holder summit.”

“This is not a bad result but is hardly worthy of the post-summit celebratory 
spin coming out of Beijing and Washington,” Roach wrote. That’s because, he 
concluded, “going through the motions is hardly enough to shift the trajectory 
of the Sino-American relationship from conflict escalation to resolution. Maybe 
no harm for now — but still room for plenty of regret.”

There are two big, relatively immediate, and related tests of the relationship 
looming this summer: Trump’s own tariffs and the persisting closure of the 
Strait of Hormuz.

Trump is trying to reassemble 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-20/how-trump-will-change-us-trade-policy-after-supreme-court-ruling?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>
 the tariff wall that the Supreme Court declared illegal in February. The 
temporary 10% global duties he imposed immediately after the ruling expire July 
24. To replace those the plan is to use twin investigations intoindustrial 
overcapacity 
<https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/march/ustr-initiates-section-301-investigations-relating-structural-excess-capacity-and-production>
 andforced labor 
<https://ustr.gov/about/policy-offices/press-office/press-releases/2026/march/ustr-initiates-60-section-301-investigations-relating-failures-take-action-forced-labor>
 to impose new tariffs and restore the import tax wall to its pre-February 
height.

Related: US to Mull Action If China Overcapacity Found, Trade Envoy Says 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/us-to-mull-action-if-china-overcapacity-found-trade-envoy-says?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>

That sets up a sensitive moment in the Trump-Xi relationship. If all goes to 
plan Trump will be announcing new tariffs this summer that Xi is likely to 
object to and may retaliate against.

Asked about the forthcoming duties Greer in an interview 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-15/greer-us-china-willing-to-continue-trade-truce-video?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>
 with Bloomberg’s Annmarie Hordern on Friday made clear he was anticipating 
friction.

“I haven’t yet met a country where we’ve imposed tariffs and they say, ‘Thank 
you. We love these tariffs,’” he said. “What the Chinese know and we’ve agreed 
is there is going to be a certain level of tariffs.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-05-15/greer-us-china-willing-to-continue-trade-truce-video>
USTR Jamieson Greer on Bloomberg TV
Beijing, he said, would be judging what comes next based on the terms of prior 
deals and the ongoing trade truce between the two sides. “We’ll just have to 
try to manage that.”

Trump’s tariff decision won’t just depend on the China relationship. He also 
faces domestic political considerations. His tariffs are unpopular and have 
been tied to theaffordability concerns 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/iran-war-will-be-eclipsed-by-trump-s-tariffs-come-midterms?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>
 expressed by voters ahead of November’s mid-term elections.

Energy Shock

Which is where the path of tariffs relates to the other potential summer 
irritant in the US-China relationship. The biggest US consequence of Trump’s 
Iran war has been the most significantenergy shock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/how-gas-prices-and-the-iran-war-hurt-trump-and-shape-us-politics?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>
 in at least a generation and a surge in politically-sensitive gasoline prices 
that are fueling a resurgent inflation and consumer discontent.

What will Trump’s political appetite be for new tariffs if Americans are 
paying $5 or more at the pump in July? (Up from around $4.50 currently.) And 
how will Xi be feeling if new tariffs add to the shock Trump’s war has 
delivered to the global economy and not only China’s economy but those of its 
export markets?

Latest: US Tells Iran ‘Clock Is Ticking’ as UAE Nuclear Plant Targeted 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/us-tells-iran-clock-is-ticking-as-uae-nuclear-plant-targeted?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>

China has so far managed the impact of the energy shock by leveraging vast 
domestic stockpiles. But that can’t last forever.

Xi and Trump reportedly agreed in Beijing that the Strait of Hormuz should be 
open. They didn’t publicly offer a joint plan for how to do that, though. And 
there’s certainly a chance that the strait could still be closed when they next 
meet in September given thelack of progress 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-16/trump-returns-from-china-with-little-progress-to-reopen-hormuz?cmpid=BBD051826_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260518&utm_content=4549>
 in US talks with Iran.

So that’s where we are. We got pageantry and good vibes. And a gnawing fear 
that’s probably not enough as big tests loom. Because good vibes only last so 
long.
