# Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — February 4, 2026

**Australia leads global tightening cycle.** The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered the first interest-rate increase among major central banks this year, positioning the country at the leading edge of a "splintering global policy outlook." Economists and markets are already pricing in a further hike, suggesting underlying inflation pressures — likely services-driven — have proven more persistent than the RBA had anticipated. The divergence is notable against a backdrop of disinflation in the euro area, where headline prices have sunk further below the ECB's 2% target, and points to potential support for AUD on widening rate differentials, though the implications for housing-sensitive consumption bear watching.

**China — services firm, but tensions accumulate.** Services activity picked up in January, and Beijing reiterated its commitment to building a "unified national market" to unlock domestic consumption — a recurring structural priority that has yet to translate into a durable demand-side rebound. Separately, a Panama court decision voiding CK Hutchison's contract for two ports drew a sharp response from Beijing, which warned of a "heavy price" to be paid. The episode underscores how port and logistics infrastructure has become a contested arena in US-China strategic competition. Domestically, a speculative gold rush — amplified by bullion's move back above $5,000/oz after a sharp recent correction — is testing official tolerance for retail risk-taking, with authorities mindful of any threat to social cohesion.

**Selective decoupling, not full retreat.** New NBER research from Alfaro and Chor finds China's share of US imports has fallen to roughly 9%, back to pre-WTO accession levels, with Vietnam, Mexico and Taiwan the principal beneficiaries. The reallocation has largely been a reshuffling among existing top-20 partners rather than a broader deglobalization — a meaningful nuance for Southeast Asian exporters positioning around tariff arbitrage.

**Australia critical minerals.** Canberra is weighing a price floor for rare earths and other critical minerals to counter Chinese dominance and attract investment — a policy lever whose effectiveness will depend on coordinated demand-side commitments from allied buyers.
