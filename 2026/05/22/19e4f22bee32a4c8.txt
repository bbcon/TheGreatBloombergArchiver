Barely halfway through his first year in office, Bolivian President Rodrigo 
Paz is staring down a familiar predicament: road blockades erected by angry 
protesters.

Those are just the most visible obstacles to his progress.

Paz inherited one of the worst economic crises 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-08-14/how-bolivia-s-economic-crisis-is-shaping-the-2025-presidential-election?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>
 in Bolivia’s history when he took office after two decades of socialist rule. 
Six months in, he’s already running out of time to enact the tough measures 
needed to pull the Andean country out of its hole.
Paz during his inauguration at the National Legislative Assembly in La Paz on 
Nov. 8.Photographer: Luis Gandarillas/Pool/Bloomberg
Acute shortages of food, dollars and motor fuels, plus double-digit inflation, 
help explain the paralyzing blockades now in their third week.

In the political capital, La Paz, prices are shooting up while gasoline and 
medical supplies run low. The city has been battered by looting, 
dynamite-hurling protesters and increasingly vocalcalls for Paz’s resignation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/bolivia-protestors-angry-over-inflation-food-shortages-call-on-paz-to-resign?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>
.

Industrial production has been disrupted, worsening food shortages. On Monday, 
supporters of former President Evo Morales were among those to clash with 
security forces.

Bolivia’s volatile politics aren’t new, but Paz’s presidency has proved 
especially unruly.

Part of a wave of new Latin American leaders allied with the Trump 
administration, Paz was elected with the support of a chunk of voters from the 
socialist MAS party of Morales and his successor, Luis Arce.

But conservative policies aimed at stabilizing the economy, like partially 
ending fuel subsidies, have come at a political cost. Luring foreign investors 
or pursuing a debt deal with the IMF nowlook an even harder sell 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-05-01/tourism-in-bolivia-is-seen-as-the-key-to-boosting-its-crumbling-economy?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>
.
A demonstrator fires a projectile during a protest in La Paz on Monday.
Photographer: Jorge Bernal/AFP/Getty Images
Paz’s honeymoon faded when low-quality gasoline damaged engines.

Now, many just want him out.

It’s a particularly acute example of electorates’ diminishing patience 
worldwide.

On Wednesday, Secretary of State Marco Rubio assured Paz of US support amid 
efforts to “overthrow” him. Like-minded governments from Argentina to Chile
jointly condemned attempts 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-17/latin-american-nations-amp-up-their-concerns-over-bolivia-unrest?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>
 at “destabilizing the democratic order.”

Paz is unlikely to voluntarily leave 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-20/bolivia-to-overhaul-cabinet-as-highway-blockades-strangle-la-paz?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>
 office early.

Yet given Bolivia’s short-fuse politics, the landscape is being scrambled in 
unpredictable ways.— Sergio Mendoza 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVCB0DdwjSE/sergio-mendoza?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>

And Finally

For two months, Thai fisherman Bunyut Chaosamut kept his boat in port. The 
Iran war had driven up fuel costs, making it too expensive to go out. Now, with 
a crew to feed plus docking fees, it’s become too expensive not to. Across 
Southeast Asia,fishermen are struggling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-05-21/soaring-fuel-costs-push-fishermen-in-southeast-asia-closer-to-the-brink?cmpid=BBD052226_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260522&utm_content=4734>
 as a protracted blockade in the Strait of Hormuz leaves the region desperately 
short of fuel and pushes up costs for everything from transport to 
refrigeration. It’s another blow for an already beleaguered industry.
