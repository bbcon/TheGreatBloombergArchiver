Tuesday, July 07, 2026

US

  • Samsung's earnings miss on sentiment (beat by ~6% but shares fell 10%) ripples into US chipmakers, with Sandisk, Western Digital, Micron, and Seagate down 4–6% in premarket trading.
  • S&P 500 futures fell 0.1%, Nasdaq 100 futures declined 0.9% as semiconductor weakness pressured the AI trade.
  • Vertex Pharmaceuticals agreed to acquire Crinetics Pharmaceuticals for $10 billion in cash, its largest deal ever, to expand into endocrinology.

AI and Semiconductor Sentiment

The core development in US equity markets Tuesday was not a domestic data release but a sentiment inflection in the AI trade. Samsung's 19-fold surge in quarterly operating income — beating consensus by roughly 6% — nonetheless failed to satisfy investors conditioned to exceptional upside from AI-adjacent chipmakers. The selloff in Seoul cascaded into US premarket trading, with Micron down 5.1%, Seagate off 4.4%, and Western Digital declining 5.6%. The episode illustrates a growing tension in the AI trade: order backlogs for data-center infrastructure reportedly extend nearly three years, yet market participants are beginning to rotate out of semiconductors and reassess whether capital expenditure intensity at hyperscalers — Microsoft, Amazon, Meta — leaves adequate room for equity multiple expansion. Morgan Stanley's Michael Wilson argued the divergence between chipmakers and hyperscalers is unsustainable and that a semiconductor correction represents a healthy rebalancing rather than a structural break.

Tariff Policy

The US Trade Representative opened a three-day public hearing Tuesday on forced-labor tariff investigations covering 60 countries, with a 10% or 12.5% additional duty already flagged. White House advisers have signaled intent to use these proceedings to restore tariff levels toward the emergency tariff regime. The existing 10% global baseline tariff expires later this month, adding near-term policy uncertainty for importers. ---

Korea: When a Beat Isn't Enough

Samsung's preliminary results — operating income of 89.4 trillion won, revenue more than doubling to 171 trillion won — represented the company's strongest quarter on record, exceeding the combined earnings of 2024 and 2025. Yet the market response was unambiguous: shares fell as much as 10%, SK Hynix slid 6%, and an 8% intraday decline in the Kospi triggered a circuit-breaker pause. The episode appears to signal that Korean equities, after more than doubling this year on AI-driven enthusiasm, are now priced for sustained outperformance rather than merely record results. The risk is that elevated valuations leave little margin for any moderation in AI capital expenditure cycles.

China and Regional Developments

The PBoC's expansion of Hong Kong's RMB Business Facility — more than doubling its size to 500 billion yuan — represents a meaningful step in Beijing's push to internationalize the yuan, granting Hong Kong banks greater access to renminbi liquidity. Separately, Xi Jinping's agreement to release imprisoned Christian pastor Ezra Jin Mingri at President Trump's request was noted as a gesture of bilateral goodwill ahead of a scheduled September summit, though structural tensions in trade and investment policy remain intact. Australia publicly criticized China's submarine-launched ICBM test in the Pacific as destabilizing. ---

France: Fiscal and Political Pressure Mount

The French government's "public finances alert committee" convened Tuesday against a backdrop of deteriorating budget indicators. Budget Minister David Amiel described the situation in unusually stark terms, warning that all fiscal indicators are "flashing red." The central government balance through May has worsened year-on-year, with risks compounding from local governments and social security. OAT spreads over Bunds remain elevated relative to pre-2024 levels, reflecting persistent political risk premium that has built steadily since Macron's snap election decision. A budget must be presented to parliament in October, in what is likely to be a contentious process given the three-way parliamentary impasse.

Le Pen Ruling and 2027 Elections

The appeals court decision on Le Pen's five-year ban from office carries significant market implications, though perhaps less than commonly assumed: polling consistently shows the Rassemblement National leading France's first-round presidential race regardless of whether Le Pen or Jordan Bardella leads the ticket. Markets appear to have been gradually pricing in elevated political risk rather than awaiting a discrete event, which may limit the immediate market reaction to the ruling. The 2027 election nonetheless represents a genuine policy discontinuity risk given the fiscal trajectory. ---

Brazil Trade Exposure

Brazil faces a distinctive bilateral trade risk. The USTR investigation, now in its second day of public hearings, spans a broad set of grievances — electronic payments infrastructure, ethanol market access, IP enforcement, and deforestation compliance — suggesting Washington is treating Brazil as a multi-issue trade partner requiring structural concessions rather than a single-issue negotiation. Flávio Bolsonaro appeared at the hearing to defend Brazilian commercial interests, underscoring the domestic political salience of the proceedings. The outcome remains uncertain but carries meaningful implications for agricultural and digital-sector trade flows.

Regional Investment Climate

Mexico's April investment data illustrates the challenge facing the Sheinbaum administration: while the headline rebound is a positive, its dependence on government outlays rather than private capital formation suggests limited durability. Sheinbaum has pursued state-led energy development strategies while simultaneously lobbying business leaders for capacity-expanding investment — a tension that remains unresolved. Cuba's complete electricity grid collapse, driven by crumbling infrastructure and a de facto US oil embargo, represents an extreme case of the region's energy vulnerability but carries limited direct macro contagion for larger economies. ---

  • WTI crude climbed 1.2% to $69.39 and Brent approached $73 per barrel following the attack, which also drew a reported Iranian missile strike on at least two additional commercial vessels.

Strait of Hormuz and LNG Supply

The attack on the Al Rekayyat — a Nakilat-owned vessel carrying Qatari cargo and attempting to conceal its position via Omani waters — arrives at a particularly sensitive moment. QatarEnergy had been offering spot LNG cargoes for late-August delivery to Asian buyers as recently as Monday, satellite thermal data indicated production restarts at Ras Laffan, and the Qatari prime minister had suggested exports could resume within weeks. That entire restart calculus now appears at risk. One LNG carrier already appears to have reversed course toward Pakistan, forcing Islamabad to seek replacement supply. Qatar accounted for roughly one-fifth of global LNG supply in 2025, and every cargo bound outside the Middle East must transit Hormuz — there is no alternative routing. With LNG prices already approximately 70% above pre-war levels, any renewed Qatari caution will intensify the competition between European and Asian buyers replenishing inventories ahead of winter.

AI Productivity: Structural Optimism, Near-Term Caution

Tuesday's semiconductor selloff — triggered by Samsung's technically strong but sentiment-disappointing results — reflects a broader reassessment of the AI trade's second phase. The core tension is straightforward: hyperscalers are now allocating virtually all free cash flow to capital expenditure, compressing a key valuation advantage the technology sector historically enjoyed over industrials. Meanwhile, token expenditure data suggests pricing power may be softening, with users potentially migrating toward lower-cost, including Chinese, model alternatives. The medium-term productivity case remains intact — BCG analysis of 100-plus public technology companies finds evidence that AI-enabled workflows are already generating measurable output advantages — but Citadel's Frank Flight argues that the path will be one of uneven diffusion rather than vertical ascent, with asset prices periodically forced to reconcile ambition with physical and competitive constraints.
