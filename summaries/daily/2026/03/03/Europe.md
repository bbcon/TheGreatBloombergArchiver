European markets absorbed some of the heaviest losses outside of Asia, with the Stoxx Europe 600 falling 3.16% and the Stoxx 50 declining 2.5% the previous session. Banks and insurers led the selloff, with major names including Société Générale, UniCredit, Santander, and BNP Paribas each declining more than 4%, pushing both sectors into negative territory for the year. The selloff reflected a combination of higher sovereign yields, deteriorating growth expectations, and uncertainty over the conflict's duration.

UK Chancellor Rachel Reeves delivered her spring fiscal statement against an unusually turbulent backdrop. The statement was designed to be deliberately low-key, consistent with the government's pledge to confine major tax-and-spend decisions to a single annual event and avoid the market disruption that characterized recent fiscal communications. The conflict overshadowed the occasion, and Prime Minister Keir Starmer sharpened his distancing from US military action, stating that the UK does not support "regime change from the skies" and questioning the legal basis for the strikes. President Trump publicly criticized Starmer for refusing a US request to use British military bases for operations against Iran, adding bilateral friction to an already complicated alliance dynamic.

On the economic outlook, analysts have framed the conflict's consequences for Europe in binary terms: if hostilities conclude within roughly a month, the energy price shock may amount to a manageable speed bump for a continent that had been showing signs of modest recovery, supported by increased German fiscal spending. A prolonged disruption would constitute a materially more serious challenge, given Europe's dependence on Middle Eastern fuel — approximately 30% of EU diesel and kerosene imports originate from the region, with roughly two-thirds of those volumes transiting the Strait of Hormuz.
