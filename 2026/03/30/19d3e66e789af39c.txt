Need-to-Know Research

As this newsletter highlighted week before last, thereтАЩs likely to be some 
tipping point 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-13/here-s-what-could-tip-global-central-banks-toward-aiding-growth?cmpid=BBD033026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260330>
 at which the demand-destruction effects of the Iran war will outweigh the 
immediate, inflationary impact. Now, economists at Morgan Stanley offer a 
fresh, somewhat wonky, gauge to keep an eye on.

The bond market offers ways of deriving expectations for inflation over the 
coming year, as well as expectations for year-ahead inflation at a point 12 
months from now. In other words, effectively, what inflation will be over March 
2026-27, and what it will be over March 2027-28. If itтАЩs high in the first year 
but down in the second, that suggests demand destruction will in time dominate.

So far, both one-year and one-year one-year forward (1y1y) inflation gauges 
have moved up, тАЬsuggesting that financial markets see the rise in oil prices as 
more about inflation than growth,тАЭ Morgan Stanley economists led by Michael 
Gapen wrote Friday. At some point, if oil prices keep rising, тАЬfinancial 
markets would likely take a different view,тАЭ causing lower 1y1y inflation bets,
