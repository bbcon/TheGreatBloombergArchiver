Viktor Orbán's 16-year tenure as Hungarian Prime Minister ended Sunday when opposition leader Péter Magyar's Tisza party secured a supermajority in parliament, representing the most significant shift in Hungarian politics in over a generation. Magyar's victory carries immediate European implications: the incoming prime minister has pledged to dismantle Orbán's "illiberal" governance structures, reorient Hungary toward the EU and NATO, and unlock approximately €90 billion in EU funding that Orbán had blocked, including funds critical to Ukraine's defense. Leaders across Europe, including Starmer and Macron, offered congratulations. The forint surged to a four-year high following the result. The transition is not without complication — Magyar inherits real economic challenges that contributed to Orbán's political vulnerability — but the geopolitical dividend for Kyiv, Brussels, and the broader EU project appears substantial. The result is simultaneously a setback for both Trump, who had endorsed Orbán, and Putin, who had relied on Budapest to fracture EU sanctions consensus.

Elsewhere in Europe, the macro environment is deteriorating under energy price pressure. UK household energy costs are tracking roughly £500 higher for a typical household, and the RICS homebuyer appetite gauge fell to its lowest since August 2023. Germany's coalition agreed on €1.6 billion in fuel-price relief measures for consumers; the EU is separately preparing a recommendation to lower energy taxes across member states. Ireland announced a fuel price relief package following public protests. Moody's warned that French elections risk stalling budget consolidation. Lufthansa shares fell 4.4% after pilots announced a two-day strike over pensions. LVMH reports quarterly sales after the Paris close Monday, serving as the first read on European luxury demand amid deteriorating consumer confidence.
