Enter Central Banks,  Pursued By a Bear  <https://bloom.bg/47EyVuT>

After two weeks of war in the Gulf <https://bloom.bg/4s8iGOW>, we all know the 
stakes. It’s the biggest shock tooil supply <https://bloom.bg/4rwKoDN> in 
history. Whether it becomes a similarly big economic shock depends now on the 
financial policy response. So attention turns to central banks, 18 of which 
meet this week, including the Federal Reserve and European Central Bank. Can 
they look through this war? And if not, what will they do about it?

There are layers of complexity. An oil shock pushes up prices, but acts like a 
tax hike on the overall economy, bringing down activity and employment. That 
creates a difference between those with a mandate to deal with both (led by the 
Fed) and those charged only withlimiting inflation (led by the ECB) 
<https://bloom.bg/4sUla3z>.

Further, central bankers know that they have no power over fuel prices. This 
is why they tend to look at “core” inflation, excluding energy and food. Thus, 
they generally attempt to look through oil shocks until they’re sustained 
enough to affect prices throughout the economy.

Judging by US bonds, this is an unusual oil spike that’s had almost no impact 
on inflation expectations. The Fed sets great store by the five-year/five-year 
inflation breakeven, which is the average price rise the market expects in the 
period from five to 10 years hence. In theory, this should be impervious to 
current oil price moves. In practice, they tend to rise and fall together. But 
not in the last two weeks:

Real interest rates on inflation-linked bonds have barely budged. Indeed, 
two-year real rates are falling. The market is not tightening US financial 
conditions, despite the rising oil price:

This could reflect admirable calm on the bond markets, or complacency. Take 
your pick. Now we need to know what central bankers think — and market 
confidence that this won’t have a medium- or long-term effect on inflation 
hasn’t stopped a shift in expectations for the Fed. Until the attacks started 
on Feb. 28, there was total confidence in the fed funds futures markets that 
there would be at least two 25-basis-point cuts this year. Now, that’s down to 
one:

This isn’t just because inflation forecasts are relatively controlled. The 
Fed’s dual mandate <https://bloom.bg/4bMvPY9> should make it more reluctant to 
raise rates; it wants full employment. The latest jobs report, for February, 
surprisingly showed a fall in payrolls, giving an incentive to cut. Further, 
there will almost certainly be a new chairman in May. Nominee Kevin Warsh has 
made clear that he wants to cut rates. President Donald Trump would make life 
very difficult for him if he reneged.

Also mitigating the risk of US hikes is relative energy independence. The 
country doesn’t depend on oil imports. That grows clearer if we comparehow 
expectations have moved <https://bloom.bg/4uvkyCY> for four other major central 
banks — the ECB, the Bank of England, the Bank of Canada (which also benefits 
from plentiful energy supplies at home), and the Swiss National Bank. This 
chart, based on the Bloomberg World Interest Rate Probabilities function, shows 
how expectations for cuts or hikes by the end of 2026 have moved this year:

This is a startling shift in market expectations, making it all the more 
surprising that the dollar has strengthened over this period. With expectations 
suddenly so fluid, this week’s meetings are guaranteed to move the needle 
further — quite possibly in different directions in different countries. How 
the bankers choose to express the change the war has wrought to their views of 
the balance of risks on inflation and growth will matter almost as much to 
markets as the news from the battlefield.

A Changed Environment

A further issue for the central banks is that they face different realities. 
The Hormuz supply shock hits differently. Dubai crude contracts, for oil whose 
loading point is behind the Strait, have leapt more than 80% this month, while 
the price of jet kerosene quoted in Singapore is up more than 60%. Natural gas 
in Europe, which already went through the roof after the Ukraine invasion, has 
also increased more than 60%.

US gasoline futures have risen about 46%, and the price at the pump, according 
to the American Automobile Association, is up about 25%. The futures market 
plainly signals that that could get worse:

The issue for the Fed, which has already been thrust into political battle by 
attacks on its independence, is that the president will want it to help out on 
affordability and cut rates to ease the price of mortgages. Further, new 
chairmen almost inevitably get tested by the market, which will mean seeing if 
Warsh is willing to hike rates. And that’s going to be difficult, because the 
war so far is seen to have sharply improved the chances that Democrats can take 
control of both houses ofCongress in November <https://bloom.bg/4sy40sR>:

Jerome Powell will chair only one more meeting of the Federal Open Market 
Committee meeting after this week, luckily for him. But for the committee as a 
whole, and those trying to predict how members will react to emerging economic 
shocks, life is getting ever more complicated.


No Betrayal

In the span of five years, China’s two main strategic partners, Russia and 
Iran, have embroiled themselves in wars. That increasingly adversarial 
geopolitical landscape raises the stakes for Beijing.

Trump’s overtures to President Xi Jinping to pressure Tehran 
<https://bloom.bg/4rw3utP> into reopening the Strait of Hormuz amount to more 
than a diplomatic request; he’s inviting Beijing to align itself with a war 
effort against a regime that it regards as a friend. His request is largely 
premised on the assumption that China is economically reliant on the key 
shipping route, perhaps more than anyone else. He may be right about that, but 
much now hangs on whether Beijing accepts his invitation.

So far, Chinese markets have weathered the turbulence spilling out of the 
Middle East, even as global economies stare down the prospect of major 
inflationary shock.Chinese equities <https://bloom.bg/4rLbiZ2>, still below 
their highs before the Communist Party’s 2021 clampdown on the private sector, 
have regained some footing. The leadership’s pivot to a “whatever it takes” 
posture nearly 18 months ago helped restore some steady momentum:

Still, as Points of Return noted here <https://bloom.bg/4rzlun0>, China’s 
post-Covid-Zero recovery remains fragile, particularly if the conflict plunges 
the global economy into recession. This isn’t the base case, but the 
uncertainty surrounding Trump’s endgame makes it too plausible to dismiss in a 
hurry. For Beijing, the economic data released since theNational People’s 
Congress <https://bloom.bg/4lDlod2> offers some reassurance. Domestic demand, 
which policymakers have long resisted aggressively stimulating, rebounded in 
January and February. Fixed investment, contracting a year ago, also turned 
higher, as Capital Economics shows:
Sources: CEIC, Capital Economics 
But it’s still early days, and Beijing needs these indicators to trend in the 
same direction for some time before drawing much comfort. The situation in Iran 
puts this at risk. The 15th Five-Year Plan’s 2026growth target of around 5% 
<https://bloom.bg/4lDlod2> has more to do with exports to countries other than 
the US than with domestic demand, as Gavekal Research illustrates:
Source: Gavekal Research 
An oil-driven global recession <https://bloom.bg/4rAAnFW> would mess up this 
outlook. Gavekal’s Wei He argues that rising commodity prices will translate 
into higher costs and lower margins for companies that cannot pass on the 
increases to consumers. That could lead them to pull back further on investment 
in coming months, forcing policymakers to ramp up supportive measures.

This goes beyond the geopolitics of deciding a stance over Iran. The aversion 
to stimulating domestic consumption, Seafarer Capital’s Nicholas Borst 
explains, is ideological. It is not China’s preferred growth model:

They don’t want to emulate the US and have a very private-consumption-oriented 
economy. When you look at the statements coming from Chinese leaders, it’s much 
more focused on, how can we have this enormous high tech manufacturing hub that 
generates profits and high-paying jobs? And that’s ultimately what we want to 
drive our economy.

No matter how insulated China may be, it’s obviously concerned about the 
Middle East. Reports that Trump has delayed a planned meeting with Xi to court 
China’s help over the war may not carry much weight. Washington’s tariff 
leverage has weakened — partly thanks to theUS Supreme Court 
<https://bloom.bg/414RZPg>, and in part due to China’s important hedge 
<https://bloom.bg/4189LRH> from its dominance in the supply of rare earth 
minerals. Even if China were to become more directly involved, it would likely 
do so on its own terms and at a pace of its choosing, rather than yielding to 
what Beijing would see as Trump’s brand of coercive diplomacy.

—Richard Abbey



 Survival Tips 



It’s a political axiom that you can survive looking bad in a scandal, but 
ridicule is fatal. Look stupid and your career is over. Trump, lampooned for 
decades, is the exception that proves the rule. His defense secretary, Pete 
Hegseth, may not be so lucky;Colin Jost’s lampoon <https://bloom.bg/47A3Kkk> of 
him onSaturday Night Live seems unerringly accurate, and very damaging. Other 
figures defined by SNL to their significant political detriment —Sarah Palin 
<https://bloom.bg/4rByjxh> (by Tina Fey <https://bloom.bg/4rB6J3g>); Gerald Ford
 <https://bloom.bg/4rDmfvE> (Chevy Chase <https://bloom.bg/4lwWATL>); and 
George HW Bush <https://bloom.bg/4rU9gG5> (Dana Carvey 
<https://bloom.bg/4bcPSPo>). All are Republicans, and Democrats have tended to 
get off more lightly — but there’s a strong argument that the knife-edge 2000 
election would have been different withoutDarrell Hammond’s Al Gore 
<https://bloom.bg/40AMGXD>. Will Ferrell <https://bloom.bg/3NmdAQ8> made George 
W. Bush look like a halfwit <https://bloom.bg/3Ntc7rd>; but Hammond unerringly 
hit on all the things aboutGore <https://bloom.bg/4rtGqMd> that people liked 
even less. Other examples welcomed.
