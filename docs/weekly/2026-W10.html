Weekly Brief

Mar 02 – Mar 08, 2026

US

Geopolitical Shock and Market Resilience

US equity markets endured a turbulent week as the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran, which commenced over the preceding weekend, rapidly became the dominant variable in financial markets. The S&P 500 opened Monday under pressure with futures declining approximately 1.0%, but the benchmark displayed notable resilience relative to global peers, ending the week down less than 1% in aggregate — a divergence Goldman Sachs chairman David Solomon described as "benign" and somewhat surprising. The structural explanation is straightforward: the US is a net energy exporter, insulating it from the Strait of Hormuz disruption that proved acutely damaging to Asian and European markets. Defense names were the clearest equity beneficiaries, with Lockheed Martin rising 7.1%, RTX gaining 6.5%, and Northrop Grumman advancing 5.2% in Monday premarket trading, while airlines fell sharply — United Airlines down 5.7%, Delta off 5.1% — on higher fuel costs and regional travel disruption.

Bond Market and Monetary Policy Complications

The Treasury market behaved counter-intuitively for a risk-off environment throughout the week. Rather than attracting haven demand, the 10-year yield rose from approximately 3.97% on Monday to 4.163% by Friday, as investors priced out Federal Reserve rate cuts in response to the oil-driven inflation signal. The ISM manufacturing survey for February, released Tuesday, showed the prices-paid component surging to its highest level since 2022, surprising to the upside by the widest margin on record. Gasoline prices rose 25 cents to $3.32 per gallon over the week — the highest level under Trump's presidency — while diesel surged nearly 30%. Former Treasury Secretary Yellen stated the Iran conflict leaves the Fed "even more on hold," and money-market funds swelled to a record $8.27 trillion as investors sought shelter. The transition to a new Fed chair in roughly two months — with Kevin Warsh reportedly the frontrunner — adds institutional uncertainty at a particularly sensitive juncture.

Political and Fiscal Crosscurrents

The domestic political picture grew more complicated across the week. The Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs, according to the Committee for a Responsible Federal Budget, reduces government revenue by $1.7 trillion through 2036, with the administration's replacement 10% levy recouping only approximately $925 billion. The president dismissed Homeland Security Secretary Kristi Noem on Friday, naming Oklahoma Senator Markwayne Mullin as her replacement — raising questions about FEMA leadership continuity ahead of the spring disaster season. The Trump administration also ruled out tapping the Strategic Petroleum Reserve despite WTI crude rising above $84 per barrel by Friday.

Corporate Developments

Corporate news reflected the week's stress. MongoDB shares sank 27% following a weaker-than-expected revenue forecast. Blackstone's flagship private credit fund faced a record redemption request equivalent to 7.9% of shares, approximately $3.8 billion, with more than 25 senior executives collectively contributing approximately $150 million to support payments. Fitch reported a trailing 12-month private credit default rate of 5.8% in January — the highest since tracking began in 2024 — and short interest against Blue Owl Capital reached an all-time high. Marvell Technology surged 12% in premarket Friday on data center demand, while Gap fell 6% on Old Navy underperformance. Berkshire Hathaway's Class B shares slipped 1.7% after reporting a near-30% decline in operating profit in Warren Buffett's final quarter as CEO. ---

Asia

Regional Markets Under Severe Pressure

Asia-Pacific markets bore the heaviest losses of any region during the week, with the MSCI Asia-Pacific benchmark declining approximately 3% by Tuesday and individual markets suffering sharper dislocations. The most dramatic sequence occurred in South Korea: the Kospi fell 7.2% Tuesday as markets reopened following a long weekend — partly catch-up selling after a roughly 50% year-to-date rally driven by AI-exposed chipmakers Samsung and SK Hynix — then collapsed a further 12% Wednesday in the index's largest single-day decline in its history. President Lee Jae Myung, abroad in the Philippines, convened an emergency cabinet session and invoked the country's 100 trillion won market stabilization program, which helped the Kospi rebound 10% Thursday, illustrating the severity of the volatility. The political consequences for Lee appear significant: his identification with the rally has transformed the rout into a direct credibility test. Global funds sold a net $11 billion of emerging Asian equities excluding China in the week — the largest outflow since March 2022 — with Taiwan absorbing $7.9 billion, South Korea $1.6 billion, and India approximately $1.3 billion.

China's National People's Congress

China's annual legislative session dominated the region's policy agenda. Premier Li Qiang set the GDP growth target at 4.5% to 5% — the lowest since 1991 — in an explicit acknowledgment of structural headwinds, characterizing the transition to new growth drivers as "formidable" and citing imbalanced supply and demand. Officials made their strongest commitment yet to ending deflationary conditions in 2026. The accompanying 15th Five-Year Plan emphasized AI, brain-computer interfaces, and pharmaceutical innovation, signaling that Beijing's technology competition with Washington will intensify. Defense spending rose 7%, the slowest pace since 2022. China simultaneously instructed its largest oil refiners to suspend diesel and gasoline exports immediately, a precautionary measure reflecting the vulnerability of Gulf crude flows. Mainland Chinese investors sold a record HK$27.7 billion of Hong Kong shares in a single session Thursday — the largest net selling on record. Separately, US-China trade negotiators are expected to meet in Paris in mid-March, with Treasury Secretary Bessent, USTR Greer, and Vice Premier He Lifeng preparing the ground for a potential Trump-Xi summit.

India and the Strait Incident

India's position became diplomatically fraught mid-week after a US submarine sank the Iranian warship IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka — the first US submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II — following the ship's participation in an Indian Navy-hosted multilateral fleet review. The Indian rupee fell to a record low against the dollar, and Indian equity benchmarks erased all gains in dollar terms since last April's Liberation Day turmoil. Prime Minister Modi called for "dialog and diplomacy" while facing pointed questions about sovereignty in India's own maritime neighborhood. The US Treasury's temporary waiver allowing Indian refiners to resume Russian oil purchases provided some relief, though it simultaneously illustrated India's acute energy exposure.

Japan and Australia

In Japan, the Toyota group reached a landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over the privatization of Toyota Industries, offering ¥20,600 per share — a 9.6% increase from the prior bid — in a deal valued at approximately ¥6.7 trillion ($42.8 billion). A Bank of Japan deputy governor declined to signal imminent rate increases, though a former official placed the odds of an April move at 50/50. Australia's Reserve Bank Governor Michele Bullock signaled the central bank's patient approach to inflation has limits, noting a rate hike remains possible — a reminder that the Iran-driven energy shock compounds pre-existing inflation management challenges across the region. ---

Europe

Equity Markets and Energy Shock

European equity markets experienced one of their most volatile weeks of the year. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 1.45% Monday, declined a further 3.16% Tuesday as banks and insurers led the selloff, then partially recovered Wednesday — rising 1.3% on reports of indirect Iran-US ceasefire contact — before ending the week under mild pressure, down 0.28% Friday. The underlying driver was energy: European natural gas futures surged as much as 52% in a single session Monday — the largest intraday gain since the 2022 crisis — following Iranian drone attacks on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility, the world's largest export plant. By week's end, European gas prices had risen more than 60% from pre-conflict levels, breaching €50 per megawatt-hour. Bloomberg Economics estimated sustained high energy prices could push Europe to the brink of recession, while Goldman Sachs modeled a month-long Hormuz closure as potentially more than doubling European gas prices.

UK Politics and Fiscal Policy

The UK navigated a particularly complex week. Chancellor Rachel Reeves delivered her spring fiscal statement against an unusually turbulent backdrop; tax receipts came in approximately £22 billion ahead of Office for Budget Responsibility forecasts, driven by stronger-than-expected income tax and VAT revenues, reducing gilt issuance pressure. However, the statement was overshadowed by geopolitical friction. Prime Minister Starmer sharpened his distancing from US military action, stating Britain does not support "regime change from the skies" and questioning the legal basis for the strikes. President Trump publicly criticized Starmer for refusing a US request to use British military bases for Iran operations, adding bilateral friction to an already complicated alliance. Despite the political difficulty, Citigroup upgraded UK equities to overweight from underweight — citing its structural tilt toward energy, commodities, aerospace and defense, and defensive sectors as an effective geopolitical hedge — and the FTSE 100 outperformed materially on Monday, falling less than 1% versus broader European declines. Separately, the OBR indicated meeting Labour's commitment to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035 would require approximately £40 billion in additional outlays.

ECB Policy Dilemma and Individual Economies

The European Central Bank faces an increasingly uncomfortable stagflationary tension. Bundesbank President Nagel stated Thursday that the inflation impact of the Iran war currently outweighs the growth drag — a meaningful signal given the ECB's recent easing posture — and traders moved to fully price in a 2026 rate hike. Euro-area fourth-quarter GDP was simultaneously revised lower, with trade weighing on output. The euro declined roughly 2% against the dollar over the week. Spain emerged as a diplomatic flashpoint after Prime Minister Sánchez denied Washington access to military bases; Trump threatened to cut off all trade with Spain and to terminate LNG exports, with Spanish equities falling to their lowest in nearly three months. Italian fiscal credibility received a modest setback after data showed it failed to meet the EU's 3% deficit ceiling last year. Germany provided a rare constructive datapoint: strong solar generation fed over 40 gigawatts into the grid for five consecutive days in March, helping cap domestic electricity prices and reinforcing policy arguments for accelerated energy transition investment. In corporate news, Adidas fell 7.6% in Frankfurt after its profit forecast missed expectations on US tariffs and currency headwinds, while Novo Nordisk rose 3.5% after US regulators moved against copycat GLP-1 drugs. ---

Global

The US-Iran Conflict: Scope and Uncertainty

The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike — the most direct US targeting of a foreign head of state in the modern era — defined the week's macro backdrop across every asset class and region. By week's end, the conflict had expanded geographically: NATO air defenses downed a ballistic missile over Turkey, Iranian missiles and drones struck Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, and Bahrain, Israeli airstrikes continued on Tehran, and a US submarine conducted the first American submarine attack on a surface warship since the Second World War, sinking the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka. US Defense Secretary Hegseth estimated operations could last three to eight weeks. Iran's signals remained contradictory throughout — President Pezeshkian vowed retaliation, security chief Larijani rejected US talks publicly, yet the Wall Street Journal reported renewed nuclear negotiation overtures through Omani intermediaries, and on Wednesday the New York Times reported indirect Iranian contact with Washington exploring a ceasefire, prompting an equity rebound. The endgame remains genuinely opaque.

Energy Markets

Brent crude surged approximately 12.5% at Monday's open before partially retracing, then continued higher through the week to settle above $82 per barrel by Thursday, representing an approximately 18% weekly gain — levels last seen in July 2024. US diesel futures were on track for a weekly gain exceeding 40%. The Strait of Hormuz effectively ceased functioning as an oil transit corridor; only two commercial vessel transits were recorded in a 24-hour period by Friday and no oil shipments passed through at all on certain days. More than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs announced withdrawal of war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels. Saudi Arabia suspended operations at its 550,000 barrel-per-day Ras Tanura refinery following a drone strike. Goldman Sachs flagged the plausibility of Brent crossing $100 per barrel if low flows persist for another five weeks; Bloomberg Economics modeled a severe scenario in which prolonged closure lifts Brent to approximately $108. Iran's military commander stated Tehran "does not believe in closing the Strait of Hormuz at all" — a signal worth monitoring carefully, as an explicit blockade would represent the most severe escalation scenario. OPEC+'s modest April production increase, announced Sunday, is of limited utility if tankers cannot safely transit the strait.

Monetary Policy and Safe-Haven Dynamics

Central banks globally face a structurally uncomfortable supply-side inflation shock — the fourth major such event in six years following Covid, the Russia-Ukraine war, and mercantilist trade disruptions. The IMF's Georgieva warned a sustained 10% energy price increase would push global inflation up 40 basis points and shave 0.1-0.2% from growth. The monetary policy implications are particularly acute for central banks still managing above-target inflation. Traditional safe-haven assets behaved anomalously throughout the week: gold wavered and was trading at a steep discount in Dubai, while Treasuries sold off rather than rallying, leaving the US dollar as the primary haven vehicle — the DXY broke above its 200-day moving average early in the week and extended gains, on track for its largest weekly rise in over a year. Emerging market currencies came under broad pressure, with the FTSE EM index falling 2% Monday — its worst session in eleven months.

Geopolitical Realignments

Russia has emerged as the clearest early beneficiary of the conflict: elevated energy prices replenish Kremlin revenues at a critical juncture when sanctions had been beginning to bite, and India's return to Russian crude purchasing — facilitated by the US Treasury waiver — reinforces the trend. The minerals competition between the US and China adds a further dimension to the week's geopolitical texture: China conducted roughly $200 billion in annual trade with sub-Saharan Africa in 2024, approximately four times the US figure, and controls an estimated 80% of Congo's copper output. The Trump administration's push to secure Congolese copper and cobalt stakes represents strategic intent, though closing that gap without decades of accumulated relationship capital appears a formidable task. A new study published in Geophysical Research Letters provided the first statistically significant evidence of an acceleration in global warming — at approximately 0.35°C per decade since 2015, nearly double the 1970-2015 average rate — adding long-term structural urgency to energy transition debates even as immediate policy attention is consumed by the conflict.
