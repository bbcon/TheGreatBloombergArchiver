Gauging Global Imbalances

A new report from the research arm of consultants McKinsey & Co. has taken 
stock of the global balance sheet — from household real estate holdings and 
government debt loads to the stock of equity issued by companies round the 
world.

Among the takeaways are some metrics that seem stretched — serving as 
potential flashpoints to keep an eye on. Perhaps unsurprisingly, a big one is 
US equities. While there are several countries that have high ratios of stock 
to GDP, the US stands out when comparing its equity with corporate net assets.

In 2025, that ratio hit 2.4 times — versus around 1.0 in most countries, 
according to McKinsey, whose team looked at 23 countries representing more than 
70% of global GDP. Stock prices in the US have soared so much that the US 
accounts for nearly half of equities — including non-listed firms — originated 
globally. It’s the highest such ratio since at least the early 1990s.

“Fundamentally, high US equity valuations depend on corporate earnings 
continuing to outgrow GDP in the long run,” McKinsey said in its report, 
released Thursday.

And that’s not just an American issue. “The impact of US equity markets 
extends beyond American households. More than one-third of US equity 
liabilities are owed to foreign entities,” the McKinsey analysts found.

Another notable takeaway: China’s outsized corporate debt. Measured against 
corporate real assets — things including land, machinery and intellectual 
property — the typical range across countries is 40% to 50%, McKinsey says. 
China’s ratio weighs in at 80%.

One area looking less extreme nowadays is real estate, the largest single 
asset on the global balance sheet and in most nations the biggest component of 
household wealth.

“In most economies, household real estate relative to GDP peaked during the 
pandemic. Since then, values have recalibrated toward long-term averages,” the 
McKinsey team wrote. “Across major economies, real estate values are still 
higher than what was typical relative to income in the last decades of the 20th 
century.”

For Jan Mischke, a partner at the McKinsey Global Institute in Zurich, the 
central message is that a global balance sheet getting distorted by valuation 
gains rings alarm bells.

“We have a balance sheet that is by all means out of proportion with the 
global economy, and that situation has a history of reverting sometimes in not 
so favorable ways,” Mischke says.

Need-to-Know Research

Wednesday saw Japanese two-year government bond yields hit their highest 
levels since 1995, at just below 1.5%. It was the latest in a string of 
historical highs for rich-nation debt yields, from a near-two-decade high forUS 
long bonds

 to the most elevated 30-year UK ratessince 1998 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-05/gilts-selloff-pushes-30-year-yield-to-the-highest-since-1998?cmpid=BBD072326_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260723&utm_content=6839>
.

It’s all one component of what Moody’s Ratings says is a “new macro regime” 
across global financial markets. The post-2008 world of low growth, subdued 
inflation and “suppressed” inflation-adjusted interest rates is in the 
rear-view mirror. Stronger investment demand and higher expected inflation 
means governments have to pay more for their debt, especially at the long end, 
Moody’s credit strategists including Madhavi Bokil wrote in a recent note.

“Structurally higher yields narrow governments’ fiscal space,” the team noted. 
And that’s something to monitor in this new macro regime: “Evidence that 
policymakers lack the fiscal room — not just the will — to contain a large 
shock, given debt and yields well above 2020 levels. A disorderly sell-off at 
the long end of a major sovereign curve — yields rising even as growth or risk 
sentiment weakens — would be an early signal that markets are repricing this 
constraint.”
