High Hurdle

Federal Reserve Chair Jerome Powell said Wednesday his handover of the 
leadership baton 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/fed-chair-pick-kevin-warsh-wins-key-senate-committee-vote?cmpid=BBD043026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3984>
 of the US central bank to Kevin Warsh — expected within the next few weeks — 
“will be a very normal, standard kind of transition process.”

Not everyone may agree on that.

First off, Warsh will be taking the helm after the most divided policy vote by 
the Federal Open Market Committeesince 1992 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/fed-holds-rates-three-officials-dissent-against-easing-bias?cmpid=BBD043026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3984>
.

Three reserve bank presidents wanted a shift in forward guidance to something 
that pointed away from any potential resumption of interest-rate cuts. The 
policy statement said they “did not support inclusion of an easing bias.” That 
came as a bit of a head-scratcher, because neither Powell nor many Fed watchers 
had been using the term “easing bias” to describe the current language.

As Katherine Judge, an economist at CIBC Capital Markets put it, Beth Hammack, 
Neel Kashkari and Lorie Logan dissented “even though the text they were 
objecting to was not present in the statement.”

So why do it? Some speculation focused on sending a message to Warsh that the 
policy-setting panel isn’t about to roll over and back a resumption of rate 
cuts when Trump’s nominee takes charge. (The remaining dissent was from Trump’s 
former chief economist, Stephen Miran, who will leave the board when Warsh 
arrives.)
Jerome PowellPhotographer: Daniel Heuer/Bloomberg
Then there’s the historically unusual decision by Powell to stay on the Fed 
board for a time after Warsh joins. The outgoing chair said he would only leave 
once the Trump administration’s Justice Department’scontroversial investigation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-24/doj-s-partial-reversal-on-powell-probe-keeps-fed-drama-alive?cmpid=BBD043026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3984>
 into a Fed building renovation project was concluded.

It will be the first time a chair sticks on the board since 1948 
<https://www.federalreservehistory.org/people/marriner-s-eccles#:~:text=Eccles%20was%20appointed%20to%20lead,member%20until%20July%2014%2C%201951.>
. Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent made clear his take: “This is aviolation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-29/bessent-says-powell-staying-on-is-violation-of-all-fed-norms?cmpid=BBD043026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3984>
 of all Federal Reserve norms.”

Powell said he planned to keep a “low profile” and “my intention is not to 
interfere.” He described his idea of the role of a governor being one of trying 
to “support the chair when you can.”

But he also pointed to the challenges of suddenly swinging the bulk of the Fed 
board and reserve bank presidents behind a new tack. The context, of course, is 
Trump’s desire for rate cuts and Warsh’s backing of them last year while the 
Fed was standing pat.

“Monetary policy is going to get made by nineteen people — there is a lot of 
stability there,” he said. The chair has to “get consensus with 19 
strong-minded people,” he said. Every Fed chief must pull people together, he 
said. As for Warsh, “he has the capabilities, skills to be very good at that, I 
would think,” Powell said.

He doubtless will need them 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-04/warsh-will-need-his-vaunted-people-skills-to-smooth-return-to-fed-he-s-scorned?cmpid=BBD043026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3984>

Need-to-Know Research

For economists mapping the outlook for the rest of the year, the weather is 
emerging as a key variable in Latin America. That’s because awhopper of an El 
Niño 
<https://blinks.bloomberg.com/news/stories/TDJELQKIJHAH?cmpid=BBD043026_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260430&utm_content=3984>
 is looming for the second half.

“Few forces shape Latin America’s economic destiny as powerfully — or as 
unpredictably — as the El Niño–Southern Oscillation,” or ENSO, JPMorgan Chase 
economists including Diego Pereira wrote in a note Wednesday. “The region’s 
vulnerability is structural—its economies are deeply tied to climate-sensitive 
sectors, and chronic infrastructure gaps magnify the devastation wrought by 
floods and droughts.”

Different nations have different effects, the team wrote. Peru is the “highest 
risk economy,” with major El Niños historically costing 1% to 5% of GDP and 
pushing up inflation. Colombia, which relies on hydropower for about 70% of its 
electricity, will get hit by reduced precipitation, but Paraguay — virtually 
100% hydro — gets more rain, boosting its generation. For Brazil, El Niño is 
normally inflationary, with aggregate GDP effects typically “slightly negative.”
