Exit Interview

“Jarring and fast-paced.” That’s how Pierre Yared, who stepped down Monday as 
the White House’s actingchief economist 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/white-house-economist-calls-citrini-ai-report-science-fiction?cmpid=BBD070726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6281>
, describes economic policymaking in the Trump administration.

Yared, who’s returning to a teaching post at Columbia University, argues that 
“shock-and-awe” is what’s needed to rejuvenate not just the US economy, but 
also to provide the stimulus for other nations to escape their own stagnation.

“The US can lead by example,” Yared said in an exit interview days before his 
departure. He cites the administration’s focus on reducing business and
financial regulations 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-22/bank-regulation-under-trump-how-rules-are-easing-for-big-lenders?cmpid=BBD070726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6281>
, boosting productivity and leading the artificial intelligence build-out.

Another is reconstructing a strong industrial base and domestic supply chain, 
something Europe is now tilting towards thanks — Yared says — to the American 
example.

Critics might point out that some Trump policies, such as attempting to 
browbeat 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-02/trump-says-fed-s-warsh-faces-board-that-s-a-little-bit-hostile?cmpid=BBD070726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6281>
 the central bank into slashing interest rates, haven’t been widely followed.
Pierre YaredPhotographer: Matt McClain/Bloomberg
The now-former member of the Council of Economic Advisers — a sort of White 
House think tank and forecasting/research group — may prove a name to watch 
after Donald Trump lavished praise on him last week in a social mediapost 
<https://truthsocial.com/@realDonaldTrump/posts/116841344198975517>.

Trump referred to the 47-year-old economist as a “STAR” and member of his 
“team of ‘Brainiacs.’” That’s the kind of applause from a president that keeps 
a respected economist on potential lists of candidates for future policymaking 
jobs, such as at the Treasury Department or Federal Reserve. A public thank you 
from the commander-in-chief of the day is a chit for the future.

Yared remains mum about any such prospect. But he indicates that the world 
should remain braced for more rapid policymaking.

“Going slow at a time when the country really needs to change 
<https://home.treasury.gov/news/press-releases/sb0539> is not an option — we’ve 
been going slow for a really long time.”

Meantime, Christopher Phelan, a Federal Reserve Bank of Minneapolis economist, 
is awaiting Senate confirmation to take the lead at the CEA. His knowledge of 
the Fed may come in handy as the Trump administration continues its campaign to

Need-to-Know Research

Economists and policymakers the world over have been anguishing for years over 
the looming damage to GDP and national budgets from falling birth rates. Anew 
paper 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/economies-thrive-with-older-smaller-population-new-study-finds?cmpid=BBD070726_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260707&utm_content=6281>
 released by the National Bureau of Economic Research questions that very 
premise.

In fact, aging and shrinking populations have historically raised an economy’s 
output per worker and had no damping effect on overall GDP, the four authors, 
including Nobel in economics laureate Daron Acemoglu, found. Workers and 
companies turn to technology to augment a reduced labor force, raising the 
productivity of each worker, they said.
Sources: Daron Acemoglu, David Autor, Keelan Beirne and Andrew Scott, and 
United Nations Department of Economic and Social Affairs
“In cross-country data, declining birth rates lead to higher total factor 
productivity, larger capital stocks, a shift toward exports in high-tech 
industries, and more labor-saving patenting,” the authorswrote 
<https://www.nber.org/papers/w35401?utm_campaign=ntwh&utm_medium=email&utm_source=ntwg8>
. This technological response produces a “positive relationship between baby 
busts and subsequent growth booms,” they said.
