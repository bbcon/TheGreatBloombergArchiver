When’s the Retest?

The news that Vice President JD Vance wouldn’t be flying back to Pakistan for 
renewed peace talks with Iran, shortly before the ceasefire was due to expire, 
broke only minutes before the end of Tuesday’s trading on Wall Street. Thestock 
market <https://bloom.bg/48hDw6G>’s response: “I don’t care.” 
<https://bloom.bg/4mRoYRm> Or maybe, “I’m so bad baby, I don’t care.” 
<https://bloom.bg/4eHaPUu>

Crude oil prices surged, with Brent topping $100 for the first time in seven 
trading days. But the inverse relationship between oil prices and US stocks 
ended two weeks ago when the pause in hostilities was announced, and nothing — 
not even the continued blockage of the Strait of Hormuz 
<https://bloom.bg/48dPVsh> or failure to advance the peace talks — could bring 
it back. A sharp rise in crude was balanced by only a muted fall for stocks:

If there’s a good basis for traders’ relaxed approach, it lies in a reading of 
the balance of risks and how they appear to President Donald Trump. The 
argument seems to go as follows.

First, a messy compromise in which Iran extracts tolls from ships passing 
through the Strait and life otherwise carries on would not be great, nor a 
worthwhile return on Washington’s decision to go to war, but capital markets 
could live with it — much as they have lived with the conflict 
<https://bloom.bg/3Ql38JO> in Ukraine. The world is de-globalizing 
<https://bloom.bg/48LspTC> anyway, and this just throws yet more sand into the 
wheels of trade.

Second, what markets really couldn’t tolerate would be an escalation involving 
a US ground operation and Iranian attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure. That 
would be a disaster. It can only happen if Trump decides, and he’s threatened 
it on several occasions. But the ceasefire has shown that he’s more concerned 
to find an off-ramp. Convinced that this meant that there would be no 
escalation, stocks have had space to surge (even without reopening the Strait). 
In the argot of the moment, the TACO trade <https://bloom.bg/41J4alj> is on; 
Trump is being relied upon on to “chicken out” and save markets from the worst.

As if to confirm this reasoning, the bad news that Vance’s trip had been 
called off was followed within 30 minutes by Trump announcing that the 
ceasefire would continue indefinitely <https://bloom.bg/4u08Q2b> until Iran 
offers a new proposal. This is how that drama played out in markets:

It was an instant victory for the  TACO traders <https://bloom.bg/4tYZu6J>, 
and anyone who took part in the brief selloff at the end of the afternoon must 
be kicking themselves.

But there may be more to the market shrug, because it fits into a new pattern 
of behavior that has become the norm in this decade. These days, when the 
market sells off and then starts a rally, it just keeps rallying. ’Twasn’t ever 
thus.

To be clear, there isn’t a big sample of S&P 500 selloffs of more than 10%, 
but there’s nothing we can do about that. Yet over four decades leading up to 
the pandemic, the pattern was clear. London’s Longview Economics published this 
analysis back in 2020:

In almost all pullbacks, equity indices retest their lows from the initial 
“wave” of selling… the history of stock market pullbacks has a compelling 
message: Since 1978, there have been 15 S&P 500 corrections in bull markets in 
which the initial pullback was 10% or greater. In 13 of those corrections, the 
index retested its low (i.e. 87% of the time).

The two exceptions were both relatively minor selloffs that didn’t involve a 
macroeconomic shock, so a retest after a major correction and rebound seemed 
close to a given, part of the mechanics of markets and the crowd psychology 
that underlies it. Sentiment moves in waves, and technical analysts (sometimes 
known as chartists) have grown rich navigating such predictable moves.

However, since that passage was published, there have been five such 
selloff-and-rebounds (including the current one), and none has been retested. 
The pandemic somehow moved the retest rate from 87% to 0%. The small sample 
includes some substantial alarms — the pandemic itself, the Silicon Valley Bank 
crisis, the carry trade unwind in 2024, Liberation Day, and now the Iran war. 
And yet never once has the market questioned a judgment once traders have 
decided that risk is back on. How?
Vance is grounded, markets aren’t. Photographer: Jacquelyn Martin/Getty 
“You used to have retests and you used to have a sense of capitulation at the 
low,” says Longview Economics’ Chris Watling. Not any more. Instead, he finds 
hedge funds are putting tighter restrictions on their managers, while the wave 
of new retail investors who entered during the pandemic are committed to the 
notion of “buying on the dip.” All of this creates a herd, which tends to move 
in one direction.

Under Trump, that has morphed into blind obeisance of TACO. Peter Atwater of 
Financial Insyghts points out that the notion has become ubiquitous: “What 
started as an event a year ago became a pattern, became a trend, and is now a 
certain trend that can be extrapolated.” Confidence in the TACO trade is now so 
strong that “ChatGPT can and will tell you how you can maximize your financial 
return from it.”

To prove this, he asked for advice on dealing with Trump. The bot raised no 
concern about retesting lows. Instead, after a long exegesis, it concluded:

The big insight
This isn’t random — it creates a tradable rotation pattern:
• “Fear trades” → energy, defense
• “Relief trades” → tech, consumer, growth
That’s why some traders actually anticipate these swings rather than just 
react.

It’s possible that the automated buy-the-dip behavior can be attributed to a 
newfound reliance on AI. It’s certainly true that the tendency to buy and never 
rethink the dip has become almost an automated reflex, when for generations the 
conditioned reactions were different.

Spoils of War

Commodity prices have taken less of a hit from the Middle East conflict than 
in the early stages of the Ukraine war four years ago. It’s reassuring that 
prices have generally stayed below 2022 levels, but that doesn’t mean there 
isn’t a cause for concern. Oil has naturally dominated the headlines, but the 
Strait of Hormuz is crucial to more than oil.

As Points of Return noted here <https://bloom.bg/3QRUsKW> and here 
<https://bloom.bg/4u7GfrX>, reducing the disruption from crude potentially 
risks overlooking an equally important threat that’s already playing out, food 
security. Fertilizer from the Gulf region, crucial to grain production, has 
barely trickled out, forcing farmers to rely on costly alternatives or cut back 
on overall application. The effects should be visible within several months, if 
not sooner. There’s no telling how high prices go, although the Ukraine 
aftermath offers a grim possibility. For context, this is the performance of 
the Bloomberg Agriculture sub-index:

This is concerning on several levels. The index tracks food staples, meaning 
the burden of price increases is likely to be widespread. Wheat and rice have 
jumped since the war started:

The muted surge relative to 2022 suggests that they have yet to price in the 
war’s impact fully, or that there’s a sense this conflict will be short-lived. 
The renewed ceasefire might help this, even if the Strait’s ongoing closure 
argues otherwise. If the markets are proven wrong and supply delays continue, 
there’s a risk of a rally that makes it costly to put food on the table around 
the world.

Trading also provides further evidence of the inextricable link between 
agriculture and energy, as oil and natural gas prices push up production costs. 
Bank of America’s strategist Daryna Kovalska argues that if the war extends 
into the second half of the year:

The biggest upside risk is for corn, as mounting pressures in the nitrogen 
fertilizer market threaten to cut corn production across major exporters and, 
potentially, worldwide. This could trigger a new bull cycle, possibly 
resembling the rallies following the 2022 Russia-Ukraine war and the 2012 US 
drought.

This wouldn’t do the US administration any favors ahead of November’s midterm 
elections. Affordability politics were instrumental in bringing Trump back to 
the White House, and any sense that living costs are rising even further — 
especially if driven by his own policies — will not go down well with voters.

Industrial metals prices are flourishing in the growing disruption for 
manufacturing. Bloomberg’s measure of industrial metals is up more than 10% 
since mid-March, with additional room to run if the supply chain troubles don’t 
let up:

The strong performance of aluminum and copper, both more than 11% higher, has 
driven the sub-index’s run. The heightened geopolitical volatility creates 
uncertainty for firms, making it harder to forecast sales, prices paid, and 
cost of capital. Oxford Economics’ Bernard Yaros points out that investors tend 
to delay committing to capital spending projects amid such murkiness. That 
suppresses investment.

Copper and aluminum are also aided by strong structural demand. Long driven by 
China’s need for copper as it grew its housing stock, appetite for the metal 
has broadened. Longview’s Watling suggests that the buildout of data centers 
gives the trend much more room to run:

The switch from precious to industrial metals is playing out and will continue 
to play out. Copper is an everything story. It’s AI, and it’s electrification. 
Aluminum is a substitute for copper. It’s a classic industrial play now — it’s 
no longer a Chinese housing story. Electricity grids around the West need to be 
fixed.

China’s growth after admission to the World Trade Organization in 2001 drove 
the last major commodity supercycle, which peaked and crashed during the Global 
Financial Crisis. The ratio of copper to gold, much watched in markets, has 
recently plumbed four-decade lows, suggesting a new cycle is due:

The risk to a long rise for industrial metals is that protracted high energy 
prices could choke off that growing demand. Oxford Economics has dialed back 
its forecast for growth in industrial production over the balance of 2026, but 
still doesn’t anticipate any outright quarter-on-quarter declines. ISM 
manufacturing surveys, which suggested after a month of war that the conflict 
hadn’t yet dented confidence, give that view some support:

But if the future really ends up in a low-grade conflict and ongoing 
disruptions to traffic through the Strait, that does involve higher prices for 
agricultural and industrial commodities. Stock traders might want to bear that 
in mind.

—Richard Abbey
