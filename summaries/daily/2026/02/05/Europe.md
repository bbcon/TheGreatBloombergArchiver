# European Markets Briefing — February 5, 2026

**Central banks hold the line.** The European Central Bank is expected to leave policy rates unchanged today, with the Bank of England following suit. The BOE decision came down to a 5-4 vote in favor of holding, signaling a more divided committee than the consensus framing implies and leaving open the possibility that the easing cycle could resume in coming meetings. Only one of 32 economists surveyed had expected a cut. The ECB's hold reflects continued patience as policymakers assess whether disinflation is durable enough to justify further accommodation.

**UK political risk reasserts itself.** Prime Minister Keir Starmer's position has deteriorated materially over his ties to Peter Mandelson, with Labour MPs describing a "febrile, mutinous mood" within the parliamentary party. Parliament voted to compel disclosure of documents related to Mandelson's appointment as US ambassador, given his longstanding association with Jeffrey Epstein. The political strain has begun to spill into markets, with sterling under pressure and gilt yields reflecting renewed political risk premium, though rivals have so far refrained from a formal leadership challenge. How long Starmer can persist absent a credible challenger remains an open question.

**Macro backdrop remains constructive.** German factory orders unexpectedly rose at their fastest pace in two years, reinforcing tentative signs of a manufacturing recovery in the euro area's largest economy, which closed 2025 with stronger-than-expected growth. The UK government separately indicated that a free-trade agreement with the Gulf Cooperation Council is "imminent." Beneath the political noise, there are early signs of a long-anticipated UK productivity resurgence, though this remains to be confirmed in the data.

**Corporate.** Shell missed fourth-quarter expectations with adjusted net income of $3.26 billion, down 11% year-on-year, weighed by softer crude prices, weak oil trading and chemicals losses; the $3.5 billion quarterly buyback was maintained. HSBC is preparing minimal or zero bonuses for underperformers across investment banking and wealth as it adopts a more Wall Street-style compensation model. BNP Paribas's CFO described the bank as "strongly delivering" on growth targets. European software names were caught in the broader AI-driven tech rotation, with Thomson Reuters down as much as 20% earlier in the week on concerns Anthropic's new tool threatens its legal workflow franchise.
