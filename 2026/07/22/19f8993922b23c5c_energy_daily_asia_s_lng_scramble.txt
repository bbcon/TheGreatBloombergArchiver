Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
Asia energy team leader Stephen Stapczynski looks at the continent’s rush for 
LNG shipments in light of the war in the Middle East.

The ongoing crisis in the Middle East, combined with scorching summer 
temperatures, is sending Asian buyers rushing to secure liquefied natural gas. 
Europe should be paying attention.

Relentless heat across South Asia is forcing countries to buy some of their 
most expensive LNG 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-21/iran-war-forces-cash-strapped-asian-nations-to-buy-expensive-lng?cmpid=BBBXT072226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6828>
 shipments in years.

Normally, these price-sensitive buyers would step back from the market when 
rates are this high. But the effective closure of the Strait of Hormuz has 
choked supplies from Qatar — the region’s top supplier — leaving them with 
little choice to avoid a crippling energy shortage.

The strain is particularly acute in Pakistan, where weaker solar generation 
has increased the need for gas-fired power plants. The country has already been 
grappling with rolling blackouts for months, and now one of its pipeline 
operators has declared force majeure on supplies to some customers, including a 
fertilizer producer and a compressed natural gas distributor.

Bangladesh is also paying about twice what it would under its long-term 
contracts with Qatar to replace canceled shipments and keep electricity flowing 
to homes and businesses.

The crunch is playing out across Northeast Asia, too. Abnormally high 
temperatures and pricey gas have pushedJapan’s spot power prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-22/japan-power-price-highest-over-3-years-triple-blow?cmpid=BBBXT072226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6828>
 to the highest in over three years.
An LNG tanker near Himeji, Japan.Photographer: Buddhika Weerasinghe/Bloomberg
LNG imports to China — the world’s top buyer — have also rebounded since the 
early days of the conflict, rising 12% so far this month from a year earlier. 
Domestic gas production underperformed earlier in 2026, while utilities are 
preparing for an El Niño that isforecast 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-17/china-warns-of-severe-flood-risks-heat-waves-through-mid-august?cmpid=BBBXT072226_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260722&utm_content=6828>
 to bring intense heat to densely populated areas along the east coast and 
parts of central China.

The shift is visible in ship-tracking data. The 30-day moving average of LNG 
deliveries to Asia is up 2% from a year ago, suggesting buyers have not only 
replaced lost shipments from Qatar and the UAE — which together supplied more 
than a quarter of the region’s LNG last year — but have increased imports 
overall.

Europe, meanwhile, is moving in the opposite direction. LNG deliveries are 
down about 35% even as the continent deals with its own heat wave.

In effect, Asia is pulling LNG away from Europe. But as European buyers 
prepare to replenish gas inventories ahead of winter, they may be forced to 
compete with Asian rivals for an increasingly limited supply.

—Stephen Stapczynski, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

Diverging policies in Japan and South Korea are set to narrow the gap between 
the world’s second- and third-largest LNG markets through 2035, according to
BloombergNEF <bbg://news/stories/TIGR7RT96OSG>. While both governments back 
nuclear and renewable expansion, Japan’s stronger support for nuclear restarts 
and renewables is expected to weigh more heavily on LNG, whereas South Korea’s 
ambitious coal-to-gas switching provides greater support for gas consumption.
