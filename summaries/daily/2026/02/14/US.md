**US Daily Briefing — February 14, 2026**

**Power Costs Emerge as Midterm Liability for GOP.** A Goldman Sachs study released this week by economists Manuel Abecasis and Hongcen Wei projects that data centers will account for roughly 40% of new US power demand over the next five years, with electricity-price inflation holding near 6% through 2027. The analysis points to a politically sensitive dynamic heading into November's midterms: cost of living, and electricity bills specifically, are shaping up as a defining voter concern. The administration has responded with an initiative pressing hyperscalers to fund the generation capacity their facilities require — a structure the Goldman authors flag as material, warning that if non-AI ratepayers absorb a disproportionate share of capex, headline electricity inflation could run higher still. The IEA's latest outlook reinforces the demand picture, projecting global power consumption growth roughly 50% faster over the next five years than the prior decade, decoupling from GDP in a manner that marks a structural break.

**AI Competitive Position vs. China.** The power constraint is increasingly framed as the binding variable in US-China AI competition. While US firms retain leadership across semiconductors, cloud, models, and applications, Chinese generation capacity additions are outpacing the US by a wide margin — a concern voiced publicly by Elon Musk and consistent with Jensen Huang's Davos framing of electricity as the foundational layer of the AI stack. The implication for investors: utility capex, grid interconnection timelines, and behind-the-meter generation deals warrant closer attention as gating factors for hyperscaler buildouts.

**Used EV Market Diverges from New.** Cox Automotive data show used EV sales rose 13.5% year-over-year in Q4 2025 to nearly 89,000 units, with inventory turning in roughly 50 days. New EV sales fell 36% over the same period following the expiration of federal purchase incentives — a bifurcation relevant to OEM residual value assumptions and dealer floor-plan economics.
