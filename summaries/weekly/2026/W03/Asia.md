**China Capital Flows and the Surplus Recycling Question**

The most consequential Asia-Pacific development reported during the week was analysis of China's accumulating external surplus and its offshore recycling dynamics. Bloomberg flagged an estimated $1.2 trillion in Chinese capital flowing into global markets, reflecting the cumulative trade surplus generated over recent quarters. The gap between recorded export proceeds and onshore FX settlement suggests Chinese exporters and corporates continue to park dollar receipts offshore — a pattern consistent with persistently soft renminbi expectations and apparent PBoC tolerance for a managed, gradual currency adjustment. The offshore redirection of these flows into dollar deposits, gold, and foreign securities has plausibly contributed to compressed term premia in developed-market sovereigns and to the resilience of risk assets despite elevated real rates. The domestic policy implication is equally significant: as long as surpluses remain unconverted, the liquidity impulse from external trade is attenuated onshore, reinforcing the case for additional fiscal or monetary support. Whether this dynamic persists or reverses — as would occur if CNY expectations shift materially or capital account controls tighten — remains a key watching item for global allocators.

**US Interventionism and the Asian Corporate Exposure**

A recurring theme across the week's coverage was the Trump administration's interventionist posture toward corporate America, which carries direct implications for Asian exporters, multinationals, and policymakers. Japanese and Korean industrial groups with significant US production or export exposure, Taiwanese semiconductor manufacturers, and Chinese firms operating through third-country structures all face an environment in which tariff threats, bilateral concessions extracted from individual CEOs, and public pressure campaigns have become routine policy instruments. Reporting midweek indicated that the playbook of direct presidential engagement and symbolic accommodation — developed in the US domestic context — appears to be migrating into the strategies of Asian executives, though the efficacy of these approaches across different cultural and institutional contexts remains to be tested. For regional policymakers in Tokyo, Seoul, Canberra, and across ASEAN, the implication is that bilateral commercial diplomacy may increasingly run through informal channels, complicating efforts to anchor expectations.
