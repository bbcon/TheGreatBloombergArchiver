The End of the Descent

History is replete with episodes of incoming Federal Reserve chairs inheriting 
an economy they hadn’t expected. Alan Greenspan gotBlack Monday 
<https://bloom.bg/3QhWCUu>. Ben Bernanke got a housing bubble 
<https://bloom.bg/49UY2Li>. Jay Powell took over a decade-long experiment 
<https://bloom.bg/4erQLnC> in quantitative easing, and soon found he couldn’t 
reverse it. Kevin Warsh’sinitiation <https://bloom.bg/4vaMs75> is likely a 
renewed inflation scare driven by war, energy prices and an economy that 
refuses to slow. Until President Donald Trump’stariff brinkmanship 
<https://bloom.bg/4fMHJ6T> last year, <https://bloom.bg/4fMHJ6T> the 
post-pandemic price surge was all but extinguished, butthe tariff shock 
<https://bloom.bg/49ZaI3E>, followed by war in Iran, changed that. 

Powell’s legacy <https://bloom.bg/4fQKV1v> will always be marked by his 
initial mischaracterization of inflation as transitory, but his more enduring 
achievement may be guiding inflation back toward target without triggering a 
recession. In economic policy as in mountaineering, most accidents happen on 
the descent from great heights. If Powell were the leader ofa team of climbers 
descending <https://bloom.bg/4glCdIB> from a treacherous summit — an analogy we 
have regularly used since 2023 <https://bloom.bg/4ejE8uz> to chart coming down 
from high interest rates — the fact that the group made it most of the way 
without incident would be judged a success.

Points of Return named 2024 the Year of Descending Dangerously 
<https://bloom.bg/4glCdIB> and charted the steady descent 
<https://bloom.bg/4fKSdDV> from what had been an almost universal 
<https://bloom.bg/4fQKVi1> inflation shock. It was made without a major 
accident, but we can now see that it was never completed before fresh shocks 
brought an end to the cycle. A new ascent is underway. This will be our last 
return to the project we started when central banks were at the peak.

As leadership now passes to Warsh, whose first meeting in charge of the 
Federal Open Market Committee is this week, hopes for a smooth continuation are 
understandable. But under Trump 2.0, it’s become less a measured climb than a
bus swerving around dangerous hairpins <https://bloom.bg/4osJZ5m> with no clear 
view ahead. The path has become considerably more perilous, obscured by renewed 
inflation risks, geopolitical turmoil, and rising rate expectations.
Don’t look down. Above: Guoliang wall road in Henan, China. Photographer: 
TPG/Getty 
Inflation Is Rising Again

Following the unusual calm of 2010 to 2019, a decade when inflation and rates 
were way below historical norms throughout the developed world, the last few 
years have been a major shock. This chart, created using z-scores [1]  <>, 
which gauge how far from the norm inflation rates are for respective markets, 
shows the developed world suffered a massive shock, with inflation at rare 
extremes by the standards to which countries had grown accustomed during the 
quiet decade:

For emerging markets, the shock was never so severe, and they contained 
inflation much more quickly (with some important outliers like Argentina and 
Turkey). The lessons aren’t lost on the developed world, where central bankers 
seem keen to respond to the latest rising prices far more proactively than they 
did during the surge in 2021. With those lessons still fresh, policymakers seem 
determined to stay ahead of the curve and protect the hard-won gains.

Rates Hit a Plateau

As a result, to use another mountainous analogy, it looks like rates have 
already hit a plateau, at a much higher altitude than in the decade after the 
Global Financial Crisis. It’s also clear from the methodology that the shock 
was far greater to the developed world than to emerging markets, which had 
never worked on the assumption that inflation had been vanquished:

The backdrop to Warsh’s appointment was Trump’s preference for lower interest 
rates <https://bloom.bg/4ebfT38>. For the president, Powell’s success in 
reining in inflation and eventually beginning a rate-cutting cycle was never 
quite enough. That doesn’t mean that Warsh will be a yes-man, but markets 
plainly expect that under similar macroeconomic conditions, the new Fed chair 
would show less hesitation in cutting.

The problem is that the conditions that allowed Powell to begin easing are 
fading. The line between prudence and monetary-policy recklessness has become 
increasingly pronounced since Trump ordered strikes on Iran more than three 
months ago, triggering an energy shock that has pushed inflation expectations 
higher around the world and forced central banks to recalibrate their policy 
decisions:

The latest edition of our diffusion index, in which rate hikes and cuts by 
each of 52 central banks are weighted equally, shows that monetary tightening 
is once again becoming the norm, with the European Central Bank last week
becoming the first developed central bank <https://bloom.bg/4v6xrDr> to respond 
to mounting inflationary pressures with a hike. This doesn’t mean a similar 
move from the Fed is imminent, as its rates are higher and more restrictive, 
but it reinforces a broader message: Inflation pressures are manifesting at a 
pace that’s too close for anyone’s comfort:

The ECB and the FOMC

Lessons from the ECB’s experience are not reassuring. The bank recently 
updated its macroeconomic projections and now expects both higher inflation and 
weaker growth, with the upward revisions to inflation proving more pronounced.

For now, the US does not face the same growth challenge. Yet, absent a swift 
resolution to the war in Iran, the ECB’s assessment carries an important 
warning. The bank expects higher energy prices to feed into core inflation and 
sees both headline and underlying inflation remaining above target next year. 
Oxford Economics’Angel Talavera <https://bloom.bg/4fLFkcK> indicates that the 
latest rate increase is unlikely to be a “one-and-done.”

This suggests the ECB is trying to stay ahead of the curve and considers that 
a policy recalibration is needed to ensure that a worsening of the conflict 
does not have a longer-lasting impact on the medium-term inflation outlook.

The stagflationary risks confronting the euro area leave policymakers with 
little room to maneuver, as hikes could impede growth as well as inflation. ECB 
President Christine Lagarde has stressed that there is no pre-committed path, 
offering markets little forward guidance. But the implicit policy rates 
discounted on the overnight index swaps market as of Feb. 26 and again on June 
11, as calculated by Bloomberg’s World Interest Rates Probabilities function, 
shows just how much the war has changed expectations:

If the FOMC wants clues about the appropriate path for interest rates, it need 
look no further than the Treasury market. Yields on policy-sensitive two-year 
notes are at their highest level in more than a year, signaling that investors 
believe ratesmay need to move higher <https://bloom.bg/4gfsDac>. At roughly 
4.15%, the two-year yield sits well above the Fed’s current policy range of 
3.5% to 3.75%, a gap that first emerged in March. Even after arelatively benign 
inflation reading last week <https://bloom.bg/4oya9Ea>, markets are 
increasingly skeptical that current policy settings will be sufficient to 
contain inflationary pressures — and indeed, they still expect Warsh to have to 
hike at least once this year:

The Fed is already leaning hawkish, as shown by the minutes of the last 
pre-Warsh FOMC meeting. The US oil industry, and the boost it sustains from 
spending on AI, make its macroeconomic backdrop less challenging than Europe’s. 
But after five years of struggling to bring inflation sustainably back to 
target, policymakers don’t want to repeat their post-pandemic misjudgment that 
inflation would prove fleeting. In this sense, Capital Economics’ Stephen Brown 
argues a rate hike is almost a base case for the Fed even if oil prices ease 
from their current levels:

Before the oil price spike, the main argument in favor of looser policy was 
that the risks to the labor market lay to the downside. That argument is 
looking far less convincing after yet another upside surprise to non-farm 
payrolls in May.

As things stand, Warsh, leading a divided FOMC while navigating Washington’s 
preference for lower rates, may well try to avoid an overtly hawkish tone from 
the outset. Instead, he is likely to seek cover in the data. After all, as
Oxford Economics’ Michael Pearce <https://bloom.bg/4fMp1MQ> argues, the bar for 
a rate hike remains high. If anything, Warsh may be more inclined to emphasize 
the possibility of an improving inflation outlook to justify rate cuts later 
this year.

Pearce argues that the Fed won’t overreact to the strong jobs reports of the 
last three months, and that Warsh “will push the committee to balance that 
against the stability of services inflation and the disinflationary effects of 
strengthening productivity growth.”

Emerging Markets Emerged

Other central banks, particularly in emerging markets, see a need to look out 
for themselves, rather than wait for a lead from the Fed. Straying too far from 
the US carries risks, but it doesn’t stop them from addressing the 
idiosyncratic challenges that higher oil prices pose for their own economies.

Emerging-market central banks’ aggressive response to the post-pandemic 
inflation surge proved a masterstroke, allowing them to get ahead of 
developed-market peers. They have not descended so far and — unlike the 
developed central banks — still have positive real rates, above inflation. That 
might give them a little more flexibility:

Across the world, central bankers roped together on Rates Mountain need the 
fog from the war in the Middle East to lift. Warsh’s first FOMC may, on the 
latest reports, come two days before Iran and the US are tosign a peace deal 
<https://bloom.bg/43wGCB0>. Until there is clarity on this, then the descent 
from high rates is over, and it could be safer to seek refuge on higher ground.
