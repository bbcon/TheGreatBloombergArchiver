The defining cross-regional theme this week is the compound effect of the US-Iran conflict on energy markets, trade flows, and the architecture of dollar-denominated global finance. The joint American-Israeli operation against Iran has produced consequences that extend well beyond the immediate theater: retaliatory attacks on Persian Gulf infrastructure, paralysis of Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic, and sharp increases in oil and fertilizer prices. Even if hostilities were to cease in the near term, the supply chain and inflationary aftereffects appear likely to persist for months, with meaningful implications for global growth trajectories and central bank sequencing across multiple jurisdictions.

The Hormuz disruption is simultaneously accelerating certain structural transitions. Chinese commercial truck operators are reportedly accelerating electrification in response to surging diesel costs — a concrete illustration of how energy price shocks can compress the adoption timeline for alternatives. More broadly, the Iran conflict has elevated the perceived security premium of domestic clean energy capacity, reinforcing investment rationales for renewables and EVs that had faced headwinds from US policy reversals and subsidy reductions.

De-dollarization dynamics, long discussed but slow-moving, appear to be gaining modest momentum through specific, observable channels: Iran collecting Hormuz tolls in Bitcoin, China defying secondary sanctions, Gulf sovereign wealth funds reassessing their US asset allocations as American security guarantees prove less reliable, and central banks across emerging markets increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves. The long-run dollar story remains contested — stablecoin growth creates new Treasury demand, and many smaller economies have no practical payments alternative — but the accumulation of stress points is becoming harder to dismiss as purely episodic. The more salient risk, as several Milken conference participants noted, is not displacement by a rival currency but erosion through sustained US policy inconsistency.
