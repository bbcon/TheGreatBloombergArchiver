Equity and credit markets opened the week under pressure as Trump's weekend social media posts delivered conflicting signals on war strategy and the Strait of Hormuz. S&P 500 futures declined 0.9%, though markets partially recovered after Trump postponed military strikes on Iranian energy infrastructure, extending his 48-hour ultimatum by five days following what he described as productive talks with Tehran. The whipsaw in presidential messaging — moving within 24 hours from signaling disengagement to threatening to "obliterate" Iran's power plants, then stepping back — has left investors trying to price an inherently unpredictable decision-making process.

Bond markets are absorbing a significant inflation repricing. US 10-year Treasury yields rose to 4.44%, with two-year yields climbing 17 basis points last week to 3.89% — the third consecutive week of losses. Futures now assign a 40% probability to a Fed rate hike this year, a striking development given that cuts were the consensus expectation entering 2026. The dynamic reflects a genuine policy bind: tariff-related inflation was fading and might have opened the door to easing, but the roughly 40% surge in Brent crude has revived stagflation concerns that constrain the Fed's room to maneuver.

On energy policy, the administration's "drill, baby, drill" directive is running into practical contradictions. Higher oil prices improve the economics for domestic producers, but US E&P stocks have risen only around 30% against a 40% rise in Brent — and the broader consumer impact, with average US diesel prices surpassing $5 per gallon and retail gasoline up roughly a third from a month ago, complicates the political calculus. Exxon and ConocoPhillips edged higher on the session, while the quarterly earnings outlook for US energy has risen sharply relative to the rest of the world.

Elsewhere, Fannie Mae and Freddie Mac have begun placing sizable orders for mortgage-backed securities following a Trump directive to purchase $200 billion of the bonds, an attempt to reduce borrowing costs that sits in some tension with the broader rate environment.
