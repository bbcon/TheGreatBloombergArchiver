European equities continue to outperform their US counterparts in what has become the dominant cross-regional market theme of early 2026. The Stoxx Europe 600 rose 0.18% Friday and is set to close February with an 8th consecutive monthly gain — the longest winning streak since 2013 — ending the month up 3.7%. The region's relative insulation from AI disruption risk, alongside a cyclical recovery that some argue is underappreciated, appears to be drawing capital from US equity allocations. European companies in the Stoxx 600 have announced €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date through February, the highest ever for the January-February period, according to Barclays data.

The collapse of London-based Market Financial Solutions has created an acute problem for several Wall Street institutions. Barclays has approximately £600 million in exposure, Atlas roughly £400 million, and Jefferies around £100 million. The situation adds to a broader narrative of stress in parts of the private credit and non-bank lending ecosystem, consistent with the Apollo Global Management fund that separately cut its dividend and marked down asset values this week.

On the UK political front, the Green Party won the Gorton and Denton by-election in Manchester with 40.7% of the vote; Reform UK placed second with 28.7%, and Labour trailed third with 25.4%. The result illustrates the two-front pressure on Keir Starmer's government from both the progressive left and the nationalist right. Chancellor Rachel Reeves is expected to deliver a deliberately low-key economic update next week, with gilt issuance projected to be reduced — an outcome markets are likely to receive constructively given recent concerns about UK fiscal sustainability.

Inflation data from the eurozone's second and fourth largest economies surprised to the upside. French inflation quickened more than anticipated in February, and Spanish inflation accelerated unexpectedly, complicating the ECB's path toward further easing and reinforcing the case for caution at forthcoming policy meetings.
