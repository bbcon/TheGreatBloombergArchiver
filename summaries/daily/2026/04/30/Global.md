Brent crude briefly surpassed $126 per barrel on Thursday, its highest level since the aftermath of Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine, after Axios reported that President Trump is scheduled to receive a briefing on new military options against Iran. The US Central Command chief is preparing fresh strike plans, suggesting the conflict may be approaching an inflection point rather than winding down. Trump separately confirmed to Axios that he will not lift the naval blockade of Iran's ports without a nuclear agreement, removing any near-term ambiguity about the conditions for Hormuz reopening. Polymarket assigns only 52% odds to the strait reopening by end-June, making a protracted blockade the probabilistic base case. The Pentagon's publicly stated $25 billion war cost estimate has drawn skepticism from analysts who argue it omits munitions replenishment, facility repair, and rising fuel costs for military operations themselves.

The UAE's withdrawal from OPEC+ adds a structural dimension to energy market dynamics that extends beyond near-term price moves. The departure reflects a broader regional realignment: Abu Dhabi's posture has increasingly diverged from Riyadh's across multiple dimensions — its alliance with Israel, its commercial ambitions in Africa, and now its willingness to challenge the cartel's production architecture. Reports that the UAE is reconsidering its participation in the Arab League, the Organization of Islamic Cooperation, and potentially the Gulf Cooperation Council suggest a durable reorientation rather than a tactical maneuver. A Turkey-Saudi axis may emerge as a counterweight, with implications for the region's post-conflict order.

The Hormuz closure is producing second-order logistics adaptations. Container throughput at Khor Fakkan — a UAE port outside the strait — has surged, with truck volumes rising from roughly 100 per day pre-conflict to approximately 6,000 currently. Jebel Ali's container vessel arrivals have fallen from 12 per day to approximately one. US crude exports meanwhile reached a record above 6 million barrels per day as importing nations seek alternatives to Middle Eastern supply. China's top oil producer has pledged an all-out domestic supply effort, leaning on stockpiles, coal, and electrification, but cracks are visible in refinery runs and industrial power costs. Gold has fallen roughly 13% since the conflict began, as markets price the inflation consequences of elevated energy costs into rate expectations — a reminder that the war's financial transmission channels remain complex and, in some respects, counterintuitive.
