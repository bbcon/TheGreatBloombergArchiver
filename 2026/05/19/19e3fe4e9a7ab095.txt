A Yippy Welcome

A selloff in US Treasuries 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-19/us-yields-flirting-with-2007-highs-entice-and-divide-investors?cmpid=BBD051926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4611>
 that’s taken some yields to their highest in years may have little direct 
connection with the impending change in Federal Reserve leaders. But that 
hasn’t stopped a budding narrative that markets are “testing” the incoming 
chair, Kevin Warsh.

Benchmark 10-year yields hit 4.63% Monday, the highest since last February and 
up more than 2/3 of a percentage point since the end of February. As with bond 
markets overseas, Treasuries have been walloped by inflation concerns tied to 
the energy crisis caused by the closure of the Strait of Hormuz. Thirty-year 
bond yields were a few basis points away from the highest since 2007.

Markets are arguably the most “yippy” (to use Trump’s term) since April 2025, 
when threats of steep US tariffs undermined demand for American assets.

The danger for Warsh, whose swearing-in ceremony 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/trump-to-swear-in-warsh-as-fed-chair-on-friday-white-house-says?cmpid=BBD051926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4611>
 is set for Friday, is that any move he makes to attempt to engineer the 
interest-rate cuts that Trump wants will add fuel to the fire of the selloff. 
The latest US inflation reading showed headline consumer prices surging 3.8% in 
the 12 months to April, the most since 2023, highlighting the risk of a dovish 
shift by the Fed.

Market sensitivity to any perceived policy mistakes was on display Monday in 
Japan, where an announcement of a supplementary fiscal package by Prime 
Minister Sanae Takaichi helped sparkfresh record highs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/japan-s-takaichi-set-to-announce-extra-budget-amid-mideast-shock?cmpid=BBD051926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4611>
 in super-long bond yields. Later in the day, Finance Minister Satsuki Katayama 
cited financial markets as “one of the risks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/katayama-signals-care-for-bond-market-in-compiling-extra-budget?cmpid=BBD051926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4611>
” for policymakers to keep in mind.

Similarly, Britain’s bond market last week telegraphed concerns about fiscal 
policy under any replacement to the embattled current Prime Minister, Keir 
Starmer. Gilts extended gains Monday when Andy Burnham, the current favorite to 
replace Starmer, ruled out changing the government’s self-imposedlimits on 
borrowing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-18/burnham-to-reject-any-change-to-uk-fiscal-rules-if-he-wins-power?cmpid=BBD051926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4611>
.

“We suspect a Chair Warsh would prefer lower rates to higher, but inflation is 
currently high enough that doing so would raise questions over 
inflation-fighting credibility,” Jonathan Pingle, chief US economist at UBS, 
wrote in a note to clients Friday.

What Warsh might instead do is argue against rate hikes that impair the boom 
in investment in artificial intelligence, Pingle said. “We expect him to argue 
that the last thing good policy would do is work to restrain the investment 
today that might represent tomorrow’s disinflation.”

Need-to-Know Research

The energy crunch caused by the Iran war risks casting a shadow over the 
artificial intelligenceinvestment boom 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/is-an-ai-bubble-set-to-burst-navigating-the-artificial-intelligence-boom?cmpid=BBD051926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260519&utm_content=4611>
, should the increase in costs be sustained over the medium term, according to 
Madhur Jha, head of thematic research at Standard Chartered.

“The main concerns revolve around the likelihood of a drop in investment 
spending,” Jha wrote in a note Monday. “Historically, oil shocks have lowered 
the rate of technology adoption by firms and led to a drop in productivity 
growth, as firms – facing higher operating costs – cut back on tech-related 
investment.” Supply-driven oil shocks triggered more severe impacts, she said.

Jha cited OECD analysis showing even a mild energy shock — a 5% price rise — 
cut businesses’ productivity by 0.4% after a year. “For more severe energy 
shocks, productivity fell even on a more medium term basis, with firms not in a 
position to improve investment.” She added that AI investment plans may pivot 
further toward energy-rich areas — as already happened after the 2022 
Russia-Ukraine shock — and ones away from potential conflict zones.
