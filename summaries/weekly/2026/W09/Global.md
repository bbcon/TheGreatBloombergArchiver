**The Iran Shock and Energy Market Implications**
Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities, and Tehran's rapid missile responses against US military installations in Qatar, Bahrain, and the UAE, represent the most significant disruption to Middle Eastern geopolitical stability in recent years. Iran produces approximately 5% of global oil supply, and roughly 20% of global oil flows transit the Strait of Hormuz. Brent crude had already risen approximately 18–20% year-to-date as US diplomatic warnings intensified, and Bloomberg Economics now estimates a full Strait closure could push prices to $108 per barrel. OPEC+ was reportedly considering a larger-than-expected supply increase at its Sunday meeting, though the group's logistical capacity to compensate for a Hormuz disruption is constrained regardless of production decisions. Supertanker earnings had already surged to their highest levels since early 2020 in the days before the strikes, with very large crude carrier day-rates on the Middle East-to-China route exceeding $200,000 — a rise of more than 600% year-to-date — driven by elevated volumes of oil at sea and a concentrated accumulation of tonnage by South Korean shipowner Sinokor Group. The combination of a tight tanker market and a potential supply shock represents a compounding risk for Asian crude importers in particular.

**Global Monetary Policy and the Inflation Complication**
Prior to the Iran strikes, the dominant cross-regional monetary theme was a divergence between central banks making progress on inflation and the ECB and several Asian central banks beginning or contemplating easing cycles. The energy price shock introduced by the strikes complicates that calculus materially. For the ECB, having spent two years managing an inflation shock partly attributable to energy price volatility following Russia's invasion of Ukraine, the prospect of another cost-push episode is unwelcome. French and Spanish February inflation data surprised to the upside even before the strikes, reinforcing the case for caution. The Bank of Korea held at 2.5% while upgrading growth forecasts, but South Korea's acute energy import dependence places it among the most exposed economies to a sustained oil price spike. The broad implication is that easing cycles anticipated across multiple central banks may be deferred or compressed if Hormuz disruption proves durable — a scenario financial markets have begun, but not fully, priced.

**Reserve Currency Dynamics**
The week produced substantive developments on the long-running debate over dollar alternatives. The ECB unveiled a repo facility extending euro liquidity to most foreign central banks — the institution's most concrete step toward promoting the euro's international role — with notable support from Italian central bank governor Panetta and Bundesbank President Nagel, who indicated openness to a European sovereign bond creating a liquid pan-European benchmark asset. China's President Xi reiterated ambitions for a broader global role for the renminbi. Yet the structural barriers facing both currencies remain formidable: foreign ownership of Chinese government bonds stands at just under $400 billion, compared with $9.3 trillion for Treasuries, reflecting capital controls, limited market liquidity, and a banking sector that holds rather than trades fixed income. German Bund annual trading volumes represent less than two weeks of equivalent US Treasury turnover. As events unfolded over the weekend, dollar-denominated assets continued to attract global safe-haven flows, further entrenching reserve currency dynamics that periodic "sell America" rhetoric has done little to structurally alter.

**China's Belt and Road and the Trade Architecture**
China's Belt and Road Initiative appears to be entering a new expansionary phase after a period of retrenchment attributed partly to Covid-era constraints. Total BRI-related engagements reached an all-time high of $214 billion in 2025, with both average deal size and geographic reach at record levels, concentrated in central Asia, energy, metals, and technology. The "small-but-beautiful" era declared in 2021 appears to have been a transitional posture rather than a structural recalibration. Meanwhile, the broader global trade architecture remains unsettled following the Supreme Court's invalidation of Trump's IEEPA duties and the contested legal legitimacy of their Section 122 replacement. Indonesia, which signed a bilateral trade deal with the US just days before the ruling, found its arrangement's status uncertain within days. The British Chambers of Commerce estimated UK exporters face up to £3 billion in additional annual costs affecting some 40,000 companies as a result of losing their prior preferential arrangements — a concrete illustration of the distributional consequences of tariff regime instability.
