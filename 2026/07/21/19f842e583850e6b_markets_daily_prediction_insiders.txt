A new kind of insider

Like other types of markets, prediction markets reward traders who get 
information first. HereтАЩsone way to do that 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-polymarket-traders-who-knew-the-future/?cmpid=072126_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6780>
:

In February, Caden Booth flew to San Francisco and headed for the stadium 
where Super Bowl LX was taking place. He waited 12 hours, until he heard 
rehearsals for the national anthem begin. He timed how long each version took. 
Then he bet more than $50,000 on Polymarket that the anthemтАЩs length would be 
under 117 seconds тАФ and scored big when it turned out to be 104.

Booth didnтАЩt appear to have broken any rules тАФ he had been standing on a 
public sidewalk. Still, the episode underscores how prediction markets have 
blurred the line between enterprising and potentially unlawful approaches to 
getting information. So far, regulators attempting to crack down have focused 
on cases involving nonpublic information that was allegedly stolen or shared.

There are signs that more bets are being placed by traders with an information 
edge. A Bloomberg Businessweek analysis found that trades with characteristics 
often associated with insider activity totaled roughly $200 million on 
Polymarket in the first six months of the year. A company spokesperson said it 
тАЬsurveils and monitors for insider trading and other illegal activity.тАЭ

Over time, prediction markets could settle into an equilibrium similar to that 
of stock markets, where extreme, clearly illegal tipping schemes are 
occasionally investigated and prosecuted, but informal tipping goes largely 
unpursued. For now, traders might think twice about who exactly theyтАЩre betting

Fed uncertainty

WhatтАЩs the Federal ReserveтАЩs next move?

The lack of clarity on that question is prompting money market funds to shift 
toward ultra short-term holdings 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-20/money-funds-keep-cash-closer-as-fed-leaves-markets-guessing?cmpid=072126_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260721&utm_content=6780>
 and away from assets with even modest interest-rate risk. The weighted average 
maturity of fund holdings has fallen to 40 days from 45 days in mid-May, 
according to Crane Data LLC.

A surge in oil prices and a more hawkish tone from the Fed under Chairman 
Kevin Warsh had traders pricing in a rate increase as soon as this month. But a 
pair of benign inflation reports last week clouded the policy outlook, 
prompting markets to reassess those expectations. Against that backdrop, money 
funds are prioritizing flexibility.
