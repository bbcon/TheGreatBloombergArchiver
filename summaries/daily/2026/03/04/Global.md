The dominant global development remains the US-Iran conflict, now entering its fifth day with no material sign of military de-escalation. Brent crude extended its gains to roughly 13% since hostilities began, settling above $82 per barrel after briefly reaching toward $84. European natural gas prices are up more than 50% on the week. The Strait of Hormuz — through which approximately one-fifth of global oil supply and a comparable share of LNG transits — remains effectively closed to commercial shipping, with Iraq already cutting output as onshore storage fills and Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility offline following drone strikes. Aluminum prices rose to their highest since 2022 after Aluminium Bahrain declared force majeure on some deliveries. Airlines have now canceled over 12,300 flights, stranding crews and equipment across the region.

President Trump announced the US would provide naval escorts and insurance guarantees for tankers transiting the strait. The shipping industry regards this as a partial solution at best: ING analysts warned US Navy escorts risk becoming targets, Iran's Revolutionary Guards claim "complete control" of the waterway, and ultimately it is individual ship captains — not desk-bound executives — who determine whether to sail. Saudi Aramco is exploring rerouting cargoes through Yanbu on the Red Sea.

A New York Times report that Iranian intelligence officials had made indirect contact with Washington to explore an end to the conflict prompted an equity rebound and modestly pared oil gains, though the situation remains unresolved. Bloomberg Economics has modeled a severe scenario in which prolonged closure lifts Brent to approximately $108 per barrel, generating a material inflation shock across all major economies. For the Federal Reserve and other central banks, the dynamic is particularly uncomfortable: an oil-price spike pushes inflation higher while simultaneously depressing growth, complicating any easing cycle. Russia's government, meanwhile, is reported to see potential benefit in Gulf states diverting air-defense missile procurement away from Ukraine.
