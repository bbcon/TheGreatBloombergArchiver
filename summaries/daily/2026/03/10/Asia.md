Asian equity markets suffered significant losses when oil briefly surged toward $120 per barrel during Monday's session — the region's exchanges were open at the worst of the spike — before recovering Tuesday as Brent retreated toward $90. The Nikkei 225 rebounded 2.88% on Tuesday, partially recouping Monday's damage, while broader Asian indices bounced in sympathy with easing crude prices and Trump's signals on a possible near-term resolution to the Iran conflict.

China's trade data, released Tuesday, provided a striking counterpoint to the broader anxiety: exports surged 21.9% year-on-year in the first two months of 2026, well above the 7.2% median estimate, generating a record trade surplus of $214 billion for the period. Imports rose nearly 20%. The strength was broad-based geographically, with shipments to the EU and UK holding up despite trade friction concerns, though exports to the US fell 11% reflecting tariff effects. The juxtaposition between China's export machine and domestic labor conditions is notable: workers in Guangdong province report falling wages and disappearing jobs, with automation and AI increasingly substituting for human labor. Citigroup economists project nearly a third of Chinese jobs could eventually be affected by AI-driven displacement.

India presents a more nuanced energy picture. New Delhi had been reducing Russian oil purchases under US pressure since December and pivoting toward Saudi and Iraqi barrels — precisely the supply chains most disrupted by the Hormuz closure. Washington's announcement last Thursday of a one-month sanctions waiver allowing India to resume Russian crude purchases provided material relief, with Indian refiners immediately absorbing available Urals spot cargoes. Tankers carrying over 20 million barrels of Russian crude were already within a week's sailing time of Indian ports. Retail fuel prices have been held steady, and India appears unlikely to need to draw on strategic reserves in the near term, though LPG and LNG supply stress persists.

Across the broader region, Asian airlines are raising fares — Indian carriers hiked long-haul ticket prices 15% — while Thailand has mandated government work-from-home arrangements to curb energy demand. Apple's continued production diversification into India, where iPhone output rose 53% to approximately 55 million units in 2025, illustrates the ongoing supply-chain reconfiguration away from China, independent of the current crisis.
