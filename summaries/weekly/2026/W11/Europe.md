**ECB and Monetary Policy Repricing**
European interest rate markets underwent a substantial reassessment across the week. By Monday, swaps implied two full 25 basis point ECB rate hikes in 2026, compared with one priced the prior Friday, with the first fully priced by June. ECB Governing Council member Peter Kazimir stated explicitly by Wednesday that the war risks forcing the central bank to raise rates sooner than anticipated, while François Villeroy de Galhau struck a marginally more measured tone, noting that next week's meeting is not the moment for action but that the institution will not permit inflation to become entrenched. ECB President Christine Lagarde separately pledged that the bank would act to prevent the conflict inflicting the same inflationary damage as Russia's 2022 invasion of Ukraine. For the Bank of England, money markets shifted from pricing cuts to a 70% probability of a hike by year-end, with ING and RSM UK estimates suggesting UK inflation could exceed double the 2% target if current energy price levels persist. The ECB and BOE face a genuinely uncomfortable configuration: easing into an energy-driven inflation shock risks credibility loss, while tightening risks amplifying a growth slowdown that was already evident in incoming data.

**Growth Data Deteriorate**
The macro backdrop deteriorated concretely across several economies. German industrial production and factory orders both declined at the start of the year, tempering expectations for a cyclical recovery in Europe's largest economy. UK GDP was flat in January against expectations for a 0.2% gain — weaker than every analyst forecast — data that predate any meaningful Middle East impact and suggest the economy was already losing momentum. UK consumer sentiment fell to a four-month low in March according to Barclays data. German bund futures slid to an almost 15-year low on Monday, and the EU warned that eurozone inflation could exceed 3% this year if oil remains near $100 per barrel and gas prices stay elevated. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 1.13% on Wednesday and declined a further 0.22% on Friday, with Tuesday's 2.21% bounce proving short-lived.

**Industrial and Corporate Stress**
Germany's chemical industry faces layered pressure: higher energy costs predating the Iran conflict have already forced plant closures, but the war has materially intensified the strain. European chemical producers are estimated to pay approximately €3 billion more annually in natural gas costs if the post-war price surge persists. The VCI described "a spiral spinning in the wrong direction" on Friday, warning that raw material shortages are compounding direct energy cost effects and raising the prospect of a fresh industrial slowdown. In credit markets, the iTraxx Europe investment-grade index reached its highest level since May, and the Crossover index crossed 300 basis points for the first time since June. Goldman Sachs analysis identified nearly 150 European companies that have already ceded control to direct-lending funds after failing to service debts. Rheinmetall shares declined 4.5% on Wednesday after guiding below analyst sales expectations for 2026.

**Politics and Policy**
German Chancellor Friedrich Merz suffered a notable setback as the CDU lost the Baden-Württemberg regional election to the ruling Greens on Sunday — a state the CDU had been projected to reclaim after 15 years of Green governance. EU Commission President von der Leyen called nuclear energy abandonment a "strategic mistake," a pointed reference to Germany's reactor closures as energy security anxiety intensified. In France, Marine Le Pen's National Rally is contesting municipal elections in Marseille, with emerging internal friction between her new circle of establishment advisers and the working-class base that drove the party's rise. The Swiss franc continued appreciating as a safe-haven destination, placing the SNB in a difficult position between intervention risks and exporter pressure.
