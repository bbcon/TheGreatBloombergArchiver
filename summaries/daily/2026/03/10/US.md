US equity markets staged a partial recovery on Tuesday, with S&P 500 futures adding 0.36% and the Nasdaq turning positive for the month, as President Trump signaled the Iran conflict could end "very soon" and suggested he was weighing sanctions waivers and US Navy escorts for tankers through the Strait of Hormuz. The late-day recovery on Monday extended into overnight trading, though the broader macro picture remains materially complicated.

The most consequential domestic economic development may be the interaction between the Iran shock and Trump's fiscal agenda. Bloomberg Economics estimates that average US households stand to receive approximately $800 in additional tax refunds from the "One Big Beautiful Bill" this year — but crude oil settling around $80 per barrel would impose an estimated $650 energy cost increase, effectively neutralizing most of the benefit. At $83 per barrel the OBBB gain is entirely erased, and at $110 it represents a net household loss of roughly $1,960. The distributional concern compounds this: energy costs are regressive, disproportionately affecting lower-income households, and the top 20% of earners already account for over 59% of personal consumption. Evercore ISI's Krishna Guha flagged the risk of amplified K-shaped dynamics as the conflict prolongs.

Credit markets registered additional stress, with the Markit CDX North American Investment Grade Index posting its largest spread widening since last May, partly catalyzed by a weak February payrolls report. The Bloomberg US Corporate Bond Index fell 0.95% last week, pushing investment-grade yield-to-worst to 4.9% and leaving year-to-date returns barely above zero. Private credit faces compounding pressure, with Blue Owl Capital's redemption difficulties still fresh and alternative asset managers navigating competing obligations to investors seeking exits.

The Federal Reserve is in its pre-meeting quiet period ahead of next week's FOMC, but the committee's reaction function has become the pivotal near-term variable. Whether the Fed frames the oil shock primarily as inflationary or as a growth headwind will carry immediate market implications. Deutsche Bank's framework suggests a sustained bear market requires a large and lasting price spike, a hawkish policy pivot, and sufficient macro damage to tip an already-softening economy into recession — none of which is yet confirmed, but none of which can be dismissed.
