# US Weekly Briefing: May 18–May 24, 2026

## Macro Backdrop: Inflation Anxiety Eclipses Easing Narrative

The dominant US theme this week was the bond market's accelerating repricing of the inflation outlook, as the closure of the Strait of Hormuz approached its three-month mark and the Iran war showed no clear path to resolution. Thirty-year Treasury yields rose to 5.19% on Tuesday, the highest level since June 2007, while 10-year yields touched 4.63% on Monday before easing modestly later in the week. The selloff reflected a convergence of factors: persistent energy-driven inflation concerns, expanding fiscal deficits, and a substantive reassessment of the Federal Reserve's policy path. Fed funds futures, which in late February implied a rate of approximately 2.8% by mid-2027, now price in a rate above 4%, with traders assigning roughly a 70% probability to a quarter-point hike by December.

## Federal Reserve Transition Amid Hawkish Repricing

Minutes from the April FOMC meeting, released Wednesday, revealed a more hawkish committee than markets had previously assumed. Almost all participants noted that energy prices could remain elevated longer than expected even after the conflict ends, and a majority indicated some policy firming would likely become appropriate if inflation continued to run persistently above 2%. Many preferred removing the easing-bias language from the post-meeting statement altogether. Kevin Warsh is scheduled to be sworn in as Fed Chair on Friday, inheriting a constrained environment in which engineering the rate cuts President Trump has sought would risk exacerbating the bond selloff. Headline CPI rose 3.8% year-over-year in April, the highest reading since 2023.

## Equity Markets: AI Narrative Sustains a Narrowing Rally

US equities showed remarkable resilience despite the bond market pressures, with the S&P 500 on track for its eighth consecutive weekly gain — the longest such streak since 2023. However, the rally remains exceptionally narrow: an equal-weighted S&P 500 is unchanged since the war began in late February, with ten technology names accounting for essentially all index gains. The S&P 500 declined more than 1% on Friday of the prior week before stabilizing, and futures touched 7,489 on Friday. Bank of America's fund manager survey showed equity allocations rising at the fastest pace on record, a positioning extreme that strategist Michael Hartnett characterized as approaching "bull capitulation."

Nvidia reported Wednesday after the close, posting 85% revenue growth and 62% gross margins, alongside an additional $80 billion buyback authorization. The stock dipped modestly in extended trading as the sales forecast drew a lukewarm response, though the results catalyzed a substantial rally in Asian AI-linked names Thursday. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index remains up roughly 60% year-to-date.

## SpaceX Files for Historic IPO

SpaceX filed publicly Thursday for what would be the largest IPO in history, targeting up to $75 billion at a valuation exceeding $2 trillion. The filing disclosed 2025 revenue of $18.7 billion and a swing to a $4.94 billion loss from a $791 million profit in 2024, with Starlink accounting for roughly two-thirds of first-quarter sales. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley lead the underwriting syndicate. OpenAI is reportedly preparing its own IPO filing within weeks, with Anthropic potentially following in October.

## Corporate and Sector Developments

NextEra Energy announced a $66–67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy, the largest US utility deal on record, reflecting consolidation pressures from AI-driven power demand. Google and Blackstone agreed to form an AI cloud joint venture anchored by a $5 billion equity commitment from Blackstone, with total leveraged value of approximately $25 billion. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon indicated the firm would hire more AI specialists and fewer traditional bankers, while warning that interest rates "could be much higher than they are today." Meta began notifying approximately 8,000 employees of layoffs as it reassigns 7,000 workers to AI-related roles.
