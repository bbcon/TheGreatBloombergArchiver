OPEC+ Looks Willing to Defy Oil Bears’ Warnings Again
OPEC+ delegates say the group will likely agree to resume output hikes at a 
meeting this weekend.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
OPEC reporter Grant Smith discusses what’s in store at the group’s policy 
meeting this weekend.

Almost a year ago, OPEC+ brushed aside warnings of a global supply surplus and 
chose to increase oil production. History seems about to repeat.

Industry heavyweights ranging from the International Energy Agency to top 
traderTrafigura Group 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-09/oil-market-faces-super-glut-with-supply-hikes-trafigura-says?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 have predicted amassive oil glut 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-global-oil-supply-prices/?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 in 2026. JPMorgan Chase & Co. even said OPEC+ will need to slash output to 
prevent prices plunging into the $40s.

Nonetheless, delegates expect that when the Organization of the Petroleum 
Exporting Countries and its allies gather this weekend, they’ll likelyagree to 
resume 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/opec-delegates-expect-group-to-resume-modest-supply-hikes?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 the output increases they suspended during the first quarter. No decision has 
yet been made.
The Nahr Bin Omar oil field near Iraq’s southern port city of Basra.
Photographer: HUSSEIN FALEH/AFP via Getty Images
That Saudi Arabia and its partners can consider this option is — at least in 
part — a result of luck.

While world oil supplies are outpacing demand, the excess has been tempered by 
an array of disruptions — from winter storms in North America to outages in 
Kazakhstan and sanctions on Russia. The size of the overhang is also being 
obscured as China continues to scoop up barrels for itsstrategic reserves 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-11/china-oil-stockpiling-masks-outlook-for-slowing-demand-growth?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

Furthermore, oil prices 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-21/oil-traders-rush-to-hedge-iran-risk-after-wild-start-to-year?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 have rallied 19% this year as US President Donald Trump threatens military 
action against OPEC member Iran. Brent futures flickered around $70 a barrel 
this week as the two sidesnegotiated in Geneva 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-26/us-iran-to-hold-talks-at-technical-level-next-week-oman-says?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 to avert a conflict.

If Trump does strike Iran, Saudi Arabia may need to temporarily ramp up output 
— as it did during the US bombardment of Iranian nuclear facilities last June. 
Riyadh and other exporters have already started toaccelerate shipments 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/saudi-arabia-iran-drive-up-oil-exports-as-mideast-tensions-rise?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
.

In short, OPEC+ has gained room to maneuver thanks to factors beyond its 
ability to control, or even foresee. But there’s also the possibility that 
other forecasters have simply overestimated the scale of the supply excess.

Chief executives of Diamondback Energy Inc. 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/shale-giant-diamondback-says-threat-of-oil-glut-is-receding?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 and EOG Resources Inc. said this week that the surplus will arrive later than 
projected, whileBaker Hughes Co. <bbg://news/stories/TAYZH8KJH6V5> said it’s 
likely to dissipate entirely.

Last year, OPEC+ defied its doubters by restarting a substantial layer of 
halted supply — though that helped trigger an 18% price slump. Whether a repeat 
strategy will pay off remains to be seen.

—Grant Smith, Bloomberg News

Germany’s government agreed to abolish a controversial ban 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-24/german-government-waters-down-controversial-heating-law?cmpid=BBBXT022726_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260227>
 on new gas and oil heating systems earlier this week. The move severely 
weakens one of Europe’s most consequential demand-side electrification

law could have led to a 15% reduction in heating emissions from 2026 to 2029, 
according to BNEF estimates, while the new proposal targets a 10% reduction 
starting in 2029 — materially slowing the sector’s decarbonization.
