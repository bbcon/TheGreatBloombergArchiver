Goldman Sachs kicks off the first-quarter US reporting season on Monday against a backdrop of deteriorating macro conditions. Analysts still project approximately 12% earnings growth for S&P 500 companies, though that estimate faces meaningful headwinds: March CPI printed at its highest level in more than two years, briefly pushing the headline rate back above the Fed's effective upper-range target of 3%. Core CPI and the trimmed mean remained below that threshold, and goods prices have not yet reflected the tariff shock as severely as many anticipated. However, the Fed's "supercore" services inflation ex-shelter ticked higher, and all four main CPI components are now rising — a configuration that appears to preclude near-term rate cuts.

The Federal Reserve, which is due to install a new chairman next month pending Senate confirmation, appears firmly on hold. Bond markets have absorbed the inflation data poorly, with yields drifting higher and mortgage rates remaining elevated. Mortgage applications fell more than 10% through March, and existing home sales appear set to hold near 4.1 million annualized — roughly flat with last year. The pent-up demand that might have driven a housing recovery remains sidelined by financing costs.

Consumer sentiment warrants attention in its own right. The University of Michigan's preliminary April index fell to 47.6, the weakest reading in the survey's history dating to 1978 — surpassing troughs reached during the 1980 hostage crisis, the 1990 Gulf War, and the Covid pandemic. Year-ahead inflation expectations rose a full percentage point to 4.8%. The political polarization embedded in sentiment data provides some analytical comfort, but even Republican respondents now report conditions worse than at any point during the Biden years. For an administration sensitive to the political costs of energy-driven inflation, the imperative to resolve the Hormuz situation quickly is substantial. S&P 500 futures were down roughly 0.6-0.7% as markets reopened Monday.
