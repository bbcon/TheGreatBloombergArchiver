China's yuan ended its longest winning streak since 2010 after the People's Bank of China moved to temper the currency's appreciation by removing the 20% reserve requirement on foreign-currency forward contracts, effective March 2. The adjustment lowers the cost of bearish derivative positions, signaling that authorities view excessive yuan strength as a risk to exporters and a potential aggravant of already-entrenched deflationary pressures. Currency-option markets nonetheless continue to price in further yuan gains, with traders positioning for a move toward 6.50 per dollar by year-end — suggesting the PBoC's signal has not fully dislodged bullish conviction.

Domestic consumption data from the Lunar New Year holiday offered a mixed signal. China recorded 596 million tourist trips over the nine-day break, 95 million more than a year earlier, and total domestic tourism spending reached a record. However, per-trip expenditure of 1,348 yuan was marginally below the prior year. The divergence between volume and per-capita spending is consistent with a consumer that is mobile but cautious — and with the deflationary environment reflected in unusually cheap airfares and hotel rates even in popular destinations like Harbin. It would be premature to interpret the holiday data as a consumption inflection point.

Japan's political economy is drawing increasing scrutiny. Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi this week nominated two reflationist academics to forthcoming Bank of Japan board openings, reinforcing concerns that she intends to constrain the pace of rate normalization. The yen and JGBs fell on the announcement. Notably, former BOJ Governor Haruhiko Kuroda — himself the architect of Japan's decades-long easing experiment — has called publicly for rate hikes and warned that the yen may be "somewhat too weak." The tension between Takaichi's fiscal and monetary preferences and the BOJ's tightening trajectory represents a developing risk for Japanese markets. Separately, South Korea's National Pension Service posted an 18.82% return in 2025, its strongest ever, driven by semiconductor and AI-related equity gains, though foreign funds sold a record net $4.7 billion of Kospi stocks on Friday after the benchmark's near-50% year-to-date run.
