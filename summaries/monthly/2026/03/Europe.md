• European equities outperformed US counterparts decisively in the first half of the month, with the Stoxx Europe 600 closing February with its eighth consecutive monthly gain — the longest winning streak since 2013 — before conflict-driven energy inflation reversed the narrative sharply in the second half.
• European natural gas futures surged more than 50% in a single week following Iranian drone strikes on Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG facility, with benchmark prices ultimately more than doubling from pre-war levels; Goldman Sachs estimated a month-long Hormuz halt could cause European gas prices to more than double from already elevated starting points.
• The ECB held at 2.0% in March but dropped language describing policy as being in a "good place," with traders fully pricing two 2026 rate hikes by month's end — a dramatic reversal from the easing trajectory that prevailed entering the month.
• UK 10-year gilt yields briefly breached 5% mid-month — levels not seen since before the Global Financial Crisis — driven by Bank of England pricing pivoting from at least two cuts to at least two hikes, with ING and RSM UK estimating UK inflation could exceed double the 2% target if current energy price levels persist.
• UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank, Unilever confirmed it is evaluating a separation of its food assets, and HSBC announced it is weighing cuts of approximately 20,000 roles — corporate restructuring activity that would have led the news cycle absent the geopolitical backdrop.

**European Equity Outperformance and Subsequent Reversal**
The defining market theme of early March was European equities' relative strength against a US backdrop weighed by AI disruption fears. The Stoxx Europe 600 entered the month having gained 3.7% in February, with European companies announcing a record €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date for a January-February period, according to Barclays data. Goldman Sachs promoted what it labeled a HALO basket — Heavy Assets, Low Obsolescence, spanning utilities, miners, industrials, and luxury goods — which had outperformed capital-light peers by 35% since early 2025, arguing these capital-intensive sectors are structurally insulated from AI displacement. Germany provided some of the firmest early datapoints: factory orders jumped in December by the most in two years, and the Ifo business confidence survey rose more than expected, suggesting early benefits from Merz's defense and infrastructure investment program were beginning to materialize. That constructive narrative was substantially complicated as the month progressed. The Stoxx Europe 600 fell 2.0% on the Thursday following Ras Laffan strikes; France's CAC ended March down 7.6% and Switzerland's SMI off 8.1%. The euro declined roughly 2% against the dollar across the conflict period, consistent with the terms-of-trade deterioration that characterized the 2022 energy crisis. FTSE 100 proved a notable exception, with Citigroup upgrading UK equities to overweight, citing the index's structural tilt toward energy, commodities, defence, and defensives as an effective geopolitical hedge.

**ECB and Bank of England: Policy Dilemma**
The ECB's policy trajectory shifted materially across the month. The March meeting held at 2.0% in line with consensus, but the removal of language describing policy as being in a "good place" was significant. By month's end, traders were fully pricing two 25-basis-point hikes in 2026 — a configuration that was essentially unthinkable entering March. Bundesbank President Nagel was explicit early in the month that the inflation impact of the Iran war outweighs the growth drag; Governing Council member Kazimir stated the war risks forcing a rate increase sooner than anticipated; and Governing Council member Vujcic described stagflation risk as increasing and the bank as needing to remain "very agile." ECB President Lagarde, addressing the ECB Watchers Conference, signaled the bank will not act until it has "sufficient information on the size and persistence" of the shock, while pledging not to allow inflation to become entrenched. Goldman Sachs Chief Economist Hatzius estimated that in a severely adverse oil scenario, euro-area headline inflation could rise nearly 3 percentage points over the next year — the largest projected impact among major economies. The Bank of England similarly held, but Governor Bailey's explicit statement that "the prior expectation of scope to cut has changed" drove sharp gilt repricing, with UK 10-year yields rising to their highest level since 2008. February borrowing data exceeded forecasts due to a surge in debt-interest costs. Tax receipts came in approximately £22 billion ahead of OBR forecasts in Chancellor Reeves's spring fiscal statement — a modest relief on issuance pressure, though insufficient to alter the broader sovereign stress narrative. The UK's Office for Budget Responsibility indicated that Labour's commitment to raise defence spending to 3.5% of GDP by 2035 would require approximately £40 billion in additional outlays.

**Growth Deterioration**
The macro backdrop deteriorated concretely across multiple economies through the month. The S&P Global Composite PMI for the eurozone fell to 50.5 in March from 51.9 in February — the slowest expansion since May 2025 — as energy-driven cost pressures and supply chain disruption weighed on activity. Input prices rose at the fastest pace since February 2023, and future output expectations posted their largest deterioration since Russia's 2022 invasion. Germany's composite reading declined more than expected; France recorded a third consecutive month of services contraction. UK businesses reported near-stagnation, with factory input costs rising at the fastest pace in more than three decades. Euro-area fourth-quarter GDP was simultaneously revised lower, with trade weighing on output. Italy is now preparing to cut its 2026 GDP forecast to as low as 0.5%, and the OECD assessed the UK as facing the largest output hit among G7 nations this year. Germany's chemical industry faces compounding pressure: energy costs predating the conflict had already forced plant closures, and the war materially intensified the strain, with European chemical producers estimated to pay approximately €3 billion more annually if the post-war gas price surge persists. The iTraxx Europe investment-grade index reached its highest level since May, and the Crossover index crossed 300 basis points for the first time since June.

**Politics, Geopolitics, and Corporate News**
The political landscape shifted notably across several countries. UK Prime Minister Starmer publicly distanced Britain from US "regime change from the skies," with Trump criticizing Britain for declining base access and a suspected drone attack on the UK's Cyprus base occurring hours after the announcement. Over 100 Labour MPs wrote to the Home Secretary urging an immigration policy rethink, and prediction markets assigned a 68% probability that Starmer exits office before year-end. The Green Party's win in the Gorton and Denton by-election — Labour third — illustrated the two-front pressure on the government. German Chancellor Merz traveled to Washington on trade and geopolitics, while his CDU dislodged the Social Democrats from power in Rhineland-Palatinate after 35 years, though it lost Baden-Württemberg to the incumbent Greens, leaving his political standing mixed. Trump threatened to cut off trade with Spain after Prime Minister Sánchez denied base access; Spanish equities fell to their lowest in nearly three months. In France, Socialist Emmanuel Grégoire won the Paris mayoral race, extending the left's hold on the capital. On the corporate side, HSBC's full-year pretax profit of $29.9 billion exceeded expectations; UniCredit launched a $40 billion bid for Commerzbank; Unilever confirmed it is evaluating food asset separation with a Barclays valuation of approximately €28–31 billion; Pernod Ricard entered preliminary discussions to acquire Brown-Forman; and HSBC announced evaluation of approximately 20,000 role cuts in an AI-driven restructuring. Adidas fell 7.6% after its profit forecast missed on tariff and currency headwinds.
