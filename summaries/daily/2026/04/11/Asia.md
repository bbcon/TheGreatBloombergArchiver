China's economic policy framework is undergoing a structural shift that carries significant implications for global trade and capital allocation. Rather than deploying another round of demand-side stimulus in response to the energy-cost surge triggered by the Iran conflict, Beijing appears to be firmly committed to a supply-side reorientation articulated in its latest Five-Year Plan. The message, as Dong Tao of Springs Capital characterizes it, is unambiguous: fiscal and monetary policy will no longer serve as the primary levers of growth management. State support is being directed toward technology firms and the advancement of what Beijing terms "new productive forces," rather than infrastructure or consumer transfer payments.

The consequences for household incomes are notable. Urban private-sector wage growth fell below 1% by end-2025, according to Natixis economist Alicia Garcia-Herrero, against a pre-pandemic rate of approximately 8%. The absence of specific numerical targets for consumption growth in the Five-Year Plan reinforces the assessment that domestic demand will remain structurally subdued. That, in turn, implies China's already-record trade surplus is likely to persist or widen — an outcome that sits in direct tension with calls from Washington, Paris, and others for external rebalancing.

What is perhaps most consequential is the tone shift. Andrew Polk of Trivium China notes that at last month's China Development Forum, Premier Li Qiang rejected criticism of China's surpluses while signaling no meaningful course correction. China's plan, Polk argues, is to double down on industrial upgrading, which by definition requires export markets to absorb excess production. Separately, China ended more than three years of factory-gate deflation this week, a development that may signal early upstream price recovery, though the durability of that turn remains to be seen. India, meanwhile, roiled its $149 billion offshore rupee market by imposing a trading ban, adding a layer of regional financial uncertainty.
