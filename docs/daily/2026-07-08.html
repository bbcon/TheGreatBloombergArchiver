Daily Brief

Wednesday, July 08, 2026

US

  • US trade deficit widened to its largest since March 2025, with imports rising 3.3% in May and exports falling 3.2%, missing expectations and raising downside risk to Q2 GDP.
  • Trump declared the Iran ceasefire "over," triggering a renewed wave of US military strikes on more than 80 sites; the 10-year Treasury yield rose 8.2 basis points to 4.55%.
  • S&P 500 futures slid approximately 1% while energy stocks rallied sharply; the USMCA non-renewal adds a further layer of trade policy uncertainty.

Trade Data

The May trade deficit widened to its largest reading since March 2025, just before Liberation Day tariffs, as imports rose 3.3%—the strongest monthly gain in three months—while exports fell 3.2%, their first decline in five months. The export weakness was concentrated in nonmonetary gold, which fell approximately 52% month-on-month, likely reflecting the unwinding of earlier tariff-driven stockpiling flows from Switzerland. Stripping out gold, goods exports still declined 2.4%. AI-driven capital goods imports remained elevated. Bloomberg Economics flagged "downside risk" to Q2 GDP from the widening gap, while noting the longer-term backdrop reflects structural twin deficits amplified by a large fiscal shortfall.

Markets and Iran Escalation

Trump's declaration that the Iran ceasefire is "over," coming alongside fresh US strikes on more than 80 Iranian sites and revocation of Iran's oil-sales waiver, jolted markets late in the session. The 10-year Treasury yield rose 8.2 basis points to 4.55%, partially puncturing confidence that inflation had been contained. Energy stocks outperformed—Exxon gained 2.6%, Occidental 3.9%—while semiconductor and memory names extended recent declines, with Micron falling 6.1% in premarket trading. Amazon's $25 billion bond offering attracted demand of only 1.6 times deal size, well below the year's four-times average, suggesting appetite for hyperscaler credit is cooling. ---

Asia

  • South Korea's Kospi fell 5.3% on the session, extending its decline from last month's peak to 20% and entering a technical bear market, while the Hang Seng surged 3.0% as capital rotated toward Chinese internet names.
  • New Zealand's Reserve Bank raised its Official Cash Rate 25 basis points to 2.5%—the first hike in three years—and signaled further tightening may be required.
  • Indonesia's equities declined after S&P Dow Jones Indices warned the country could lose emerging-market status if liquidity and transparency concerns persist.

Equity Rotation

A sharp intra-regional divergence is the dominant market story in Asia. The Kospi's 20% drawdown from June highs reflects investor rotation away from Korean and Taiwanese chipmakers following Samsung's 19-fold surge in quarterly profit—a print that paradoxically triggered a sell-the-news response, compressing Samsung's P/E to its largest discount to MSCI World on record. Capital has migrated toward Chinese internet names: Alibaba surged 13% after a pre-earnings analyst briefing, and the MSCI China Index, which had touched its lowest level relative to global equities since the aftermath of September 11, 2001, has gained 7% in July. Reports that DeepSeek and Zhipu are developing proprietary AI chips provided additional catalyst for Chinese tech.

New Zealand Policy and Regional Risks

The Reserve Bank of New Zealand's 25-basis-point hike to 2.5%—its first tightening in three years—reflected above-target inflation and expectations of strengthening economic activity. The NZD and government bond yields both advanced on the decision. Separately, the Philippines' impeachment trial of Vice President Sara Duterte continues to weigh on investor sentiment, with the case threatening to distract policymakers from addressing one of Southeast Asia's slowest-growing economies at a moment when the US-Iran oil shock has already dented consumer confidence. ---

Europe

  • UK 10-year gilt yields rose 10 basis points to 4.95%—the highest level in nearly a month—as renewed US-Iran hostilities revived rate-hike expectations across European bond markets.
  • Marine Le Pen confirmed she will contest France's 2027 presidential election despite an upheld conviction for misusing public funds, maintaining her status as frontrunner given National Rally's polling lead.
  • The UK, France, and Germany launched a $50 billion NATO initiative to develop long-range strike systems exceeding 2,000 kilometers in range without US involvement.

Rates and Macro Backdrop

The dominant market development in Europe was the bond selloff triggered by Trump's Iran escalation. Gilt yields jumped 10 basis points to 4.95%, while traders added to wagers on central bank rate hikes across the region. This occurred against a modestly improving UK labor market backdrop: a survey tracked by Bank of England rate-setters showed employment optimism had recovered following the June US-Iran truce, though that truce now appears fragile. The renewed energy price shock—Brent rising over 6% during London trading—creates a stagflationary headwind that complicates the BOE's path.

Politics

Le Pen's confirmation that she will run for president in 2027, electronic monitoring bracelet notwithstanding, keeps France's political risk premium elevated heading into next year. Her appeal to the country's highest court will delay enforcement of her sentence, allowing her to campaign unencumbered. In the UK, Nigel Farage resigned his parliamentary seat to force a by-election in Clacton amid corruption allegations, framing the contest as a populist versus establishment referendum—though major parties declined to field candidates, limiting its national significance. Lufthansa fell 6.4% after a Citi downgrade to sell. ---

USMCA and Trade Uncertainty

The Trump administration's decision not to renew the USMCA introduces sustained uncertainty rather than immediate rupture. By declining to formally select the 16-year extension option, Washington retains implicit leverage to pursue full termination if its demands—including raising regional vehicle content thresholds from 75% to 82% and requiring 50% US-sourced value—are not met. The auto sector, representing nearly 18% of US trade with its neighbors, faces the sharpest near-term exposure. Toyota's announced shift of some truck production from Mexico to Texas was cited by the administration as validating its approach. Oxford Economics cautions the outcome may suppress cross-border investment irrespective of which party formally bears the political cost.

Colombia Transition Risk

The mutual suspension of transition meetings between Colombia's incoming and outgoing governments represents an unusual breakdown in institutional process. The handover, already expected to be the most contested in decades, now carries elevated policy continuity risk across fiscal, energy, and security portfolios. ---

Global

  • Brent crude rose more than 6% in London trading after Trump declared the Iran ceasefire "over" and the US struck more than 80 sites in Iran while revoking the oil-sales waiver; WTI futures rose approximately 5.7%.
  • Approximately 63 million barrels of Iranian crude are stranded on tankers with no clear buyer following the waiver revocation, while Strait of Hormuz transit volumes remain well below pre-conflict norms.
  • Hedge funds returned 7.2% on average in the first half of 2026, the strongest H1 performance since 2021, with tech-focused funds averaging 27%.

Oil and the Strait of Hormuz

The escalation represents the most direct challenge yet to the interim ceasefire signed June 17. Trump's declaration that the agreement is "over" came alongside US Central Command strikes targeting more than 80 sites in retaliation for Iranian attacks on commercial shipping. Roughly 63 million barrels of Iranian crude are now stranded across Persian Gulf and Asian waters, with most vessels showing no declared destination. Strait traffic, while not fully closed, remains materially below pre-conflict levels. Iran's parliament speaker issued a pointed warning—"the era of bullying and extortion is over"—while Tehran's display of regime continuity around the Khamenei funeral proceedings signals no near-term willingness to capitulate. Market-implied US one-year inflation swaps, at 2.23%, remain below the 3.67% consumers expect, a divergence that may narrow if energy prices remain elevated.

Gold and Momentum Reversal

Gold has benefited structurally from geopolitical fragmentation, with central bank accumulation accelerating and new clearing infrastructure being established in Hong Kong and Singapore. Simultaneously, global equity momentum strategies are suffering a significant reversal: quant funds are caught between collapsing semiconductor winners and a sharp recovery in prior losers across the US, Japan, and Europe. The trigger is less clear-cut than prior momentum shocks, suggesting the unwind may have further to run before stabilizing.
