# Global Macro Cycle: State of Play and Commodity Implications
*June 5, 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

The global macro cycle entered 2026 in a late-cycle extension, supported by AI-driven capital expenditure and residual fiscal stimulus. That baseline has been violently disrupted by the US-Israel strikes on Iranian nuclear facilities in late February and the subsequent closure of the Strait of Hormuz — the most significant energy supply shock since the 1970s and 2022 combined, per the IEA's own characterization. The world is now operating in a stagflationary configuration that few central banks, governments, or investors had adequately provisioned for. The net effect on commodities is sharply bifurcated: energy prices elevated and volatile; metals complex caught between a supply shock (aluminum smelters struck, urea/ammonia disrupted) and a demand headwind from a structurally weak Chinese consumer; agricultural inputs under pressure.

---

## United States

The US entered 2026 with consensus forecasting a goldilocks late-cycle: AI and fiscal spending extending growth while inflation gradually normalized toward the Fed's 2% target. That script held through January. The key early complication was the Warsh nomination — an incoming Fed chair with an inflation-conscious record, nominated by an administration demanding lower rates, inheriting a committee that had already begun debating *rate hikes*. The January PPI print surprised hot. January payrolls at 130,000 (strongest in a year) pushed the expected first cut from June to July.

The macro picture shifted decisively in February–March. The Supreme Court's 6-3 invalidation of Trump's IEEPA tariff architecture removed approximately $1.7 trillion in projected revenue and forced a pivot to a 15% global levy under Section 122 of the Trade Act, whose legal durability is itself contested. The tariff environment became a source of persistent uncertainty rather than a settled constraint.

The Iran conflict defined the remainder of the period. US average gasoline prices rose approximately 27% from the onset of hostilities in late February, reaching $3.70/gallon by end-March and above $4.50 by late May. Diesel surpassed $5/gallon. April CPI printed 3.8% YoY — highest since 2023, above 3.5% consensus — with core inflation ex-energy at 2.8% and supercore services rising sharply, indicating the oil shock is generating second-order wage and price effects. Consumer prices outpaced average hourly earnings in April for the first time since 2022. PPI rose 6% YoY with electronics components inflating 27%.

The Fed held at 3.5–3.75% through the entire period, but the internal consensus shifted dramatically. Powell's final meeting produced four dissents — the most divided since October 1992. FOMC minutes showed "almost all" participants expected persistent energy-driven inflation. Futures markets moved from pricing two cuts (January) to zero cuts (late April) to a 70–75% probability of a *hike* by early 2027 (by end of May). Warsh was confirmed by the narrowest margin on record and sworn in in late May.

Equity markets produced a strikingly bifurcated outcome: the S&P 500 closed May up 4.9% YTD despite the worst consumer sentiment since the survey's 1978 inception, driven almost entirely by the AI semiconductor complex. The SOX index contributed roughly 369 of the S&P 500's 672-point YTD advance. Outside AI semis, consumer stress — particularly in the bottom two income quintiles — was measurable. Goldman Sachs raised its 12-month US recession probability to 30% in March.

**Commodity implication**: Sustained energy cost inflation is compressing household discretionary spending and weighing on US industrial demand for commodity inputs. The distribution is regressive — lower-income cohorts face a structural squeeze that is demand-destructive for bulk commodities. Against this, AI infrastructure buildout is a genuine incremental demand driver for copper, steel, and specialized materials. Goldman projects data centers will account for 40% of incremental US power demand through 2030, which is structurally bullish for natural gas, uranium, and grid infrastructure.

## China

China's macro trajectory is the key demand-side variable for most commodity markets. The headline is deceptive: Q1 2026 GDP expanded 5.0% YoY, beating consensus. The composition is what matters. Industrial output rose 5.7% in March; retail sales rose only 1.7%. By April, the picture deteriorated materially across every indicator. Retail sales +0.2% YoY (weakest since December 2022), auto sales -15%, jewelry -21%. Industrial production 4.1% (weakest in nearly three years). Fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% YTD — a result no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated.

The structural demand shortfall is persistent. Urban private-sector wage growth had fallen below 1% by end-2025 versus approximately 8% pre-pandemic. Household consumption has hovered near 40% of GDP essentially unchanged since 2005 (IMF). The 15th Five-Year Plan contains *no specific numerical consumption targets*, reinforcing the assessment that domestic demand will remain subordinated to industrial and export objectives.

The offsetting factor is China's export competitiveness. EV and hybrid exports surged 140% YoY in March (record 349,000 units). Lithium battery shipments +34%, solar cells +80%. BYD raised its 2026 export target to 1.5 million units even as domestic BYD sales fell more than 40% — the bifurcation of external competitiveness and domestic weakness in a single company. The PBoC has been accommodative at the margin but stimulus has been characterized by Citi as having a "ceiling, not floor."

Independent analysts estimate true NPL ratios at approximately 10% of total credit (versus 1.5% official), implying roughly $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt. Domestic 10-year bond yields are below 2% — now trading *below* JGB yields, a striking inversion reflecting the dichotomy between external competitiveness and subdued domestic financial conditions.

The Hormuz disruption exposed meaningful Chinese energy vulnerabilities. Iran is a critical crude supplier; Saudi shipments to Chinese customers were set to halve in May. Teapot refiners held approximately 38 million barrels in floating stockpiles, providing roughly two months of buffer. Beijing released strategic reserves, granted additional import quotas, and pivoted to US ethane imports (record 800,000 tons in April). Yuan-denominated crude transactions hit a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS.

**Commodity implication**: China's demand outlook is structurally bifurcated. For old-economy commodities — iron ore, thermal coal, crude oil into conventional refining — the domestic demand trajectory is uninspiring. The property sector is in managed contraction. For new-economy commodities — lithium, cobalt, rare earths, copper into EVs and grid — China is simultaneously the world's dominant manufacturer and a rapidly growing internal deployer. The net signal for most industrial metals is that Chinese demand growth will be structurally lower than the 2002–2012 supercycle implied, but with pockets of strong demand in the energy transition supply chain that China effectively controls.

## Rest of World

**Europe**: Entered the period in soft cyclical recovery before the Iran shock reversed it. Composite PMI fell to 47.5 in May — sharpest contraction in 2.5 years. France contracted in Q1. Germany halved its 2026 growth forecast to 0.5%. The ECB, having signaled ongoing easing in January, has pivoted to pricing three hikes in 2026. Bundesbank's Nagel called hikes "increasingly likely"; market base case is a June increase. The ECB faces a genuine Trichet 2.0 risk — tightening into a weakening economy. Hungary's change of government (Orbán removed, Magyar's supermajority) was the most constructive political development of the period, removing a persistent EU cohesion obstruction.

**Japan**: Attempting monetary normalization (BOJ at 0.75%, most divided vote under Ueda at 6-3 in April/May) while facing fiscal pressure from PM Takaichi. Currency intervention deployed (~$30B) as yen approached ¥160. Three consecutive years of ~5% Shunto wage growth signal genuine deflationary exit. 30-year JGB yields at post-1997 highs. Japan's structural energy import dependence is acutely exposed by Hormuz.

**Emerging markets**: Sharply bifurcated. Korea (+73% Kospi YTD) and Taiwan (GDP revised to 9.6%) are extraordinary AI-driven beneficiaries. Indonesia faces institutional deterioration, rupiah near 3-decade lows, and policy uncertainty. India facing record equity outflows and rupee near 97/dollar. LatAm: Brazil in pre-election fiscal expansion, Mexico sovereign downgraded to one notch above junk.

**Commodity implication**: The global macro cycle is caught between a supply-driven inflationary shock (energy) and genuine demand deterioration in the world's largest commodity consumer (China) and across most developed-market consumers facing energy cost squeeze. The commodity-positive case rests on: (1) supply disruption duration greater than the forward curve implies; (2) energy transition acceleration structurally constructive for non-oil commodities; (3) the copper/gold ratio at 4-decade lows historically signals onset of commodity supercycles. The commodity-negative case rests on: (1) Chinese domestic demand structurally subdued; (2) demand destruction as the rebalancing mechanism if Hormuz remains closed; (3) rising real rates globally compressing commodity valuations.
