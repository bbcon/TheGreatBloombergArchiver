The Supreme Court delivered the most consequential domestic policy ruling of the Trump presidency on Friday, striking down in a 6-3 decision the administration's "Liberation Day" reciprocal tariffs imposed under emergency powers. The ruling invalidates duties on goods from dozens of countries and could cut the US average effective tariff rate by more than half. The court left unresolved whether importers are entitled to refunds, which Bloomberg Economics estimates could total as much as $170 billion — a messy legal fight now remanded to lower courts. The White House indicated tariffs remain available under alternative legal authorities, suggesting the administration will seek to reconstruct its trade architecture on different statutory grounds. Market reaction, while notable, was not a complete surprise given legal challenges had been widely anticipated.

The ruling lands against a backdrop of a broader market debate over Federal Reserve policy. A growing minority of money managers are skeptical of consensus expectations for two or more rate cuts in 2026. Invesco's Rob Waldner identifies one cut as his base case, with "no cuts" an increasingly plausible scenario given robust employment data and the surge of AI-driven capital expenditure. Carmignac's Guillaume Rigeade holds a short US Treasury position and projects the 10-year yield drifting toward 4.5% from roughly 4.07% currently. The dollar is on track for its best week in four months as rate-cut expectations are pared. Complicating the outlook further: Fed Chair Jerome Powell's term expires in May, adding institutional uncertainty to an already complex policy calculus.

On fiscal dynamics, new CBO projections show net interest payments rising from $1 trillion this year to $2.1 trillion by 2036, reaching 4.6% of GDP — a ratio that alone exceeds Treasury Secretary Bessent's 3% total deficit target for 2029. The CBO does not anticipate a disorderly bond market response, projecting 10-year yields moving from 4.1% to only 4.4% over the decade, though analysts note the sheer scale of outstanding debt may constrain future administrations' fiscal flexibility. S&P 500 futures were little changed heading into the session.
