Daily Brief

Monday, June 29, 2026

US

  • May core PCE inflation rose further above the 3% Fed target — the most menacing reading in roughly 20 years outside the post-pandemic surge — beating consensus concerns and predating the Iran conflict.
  • The US merchandise trade deficit widened 27.4% month-on-month in May to $105.8 billion, the largest in more than a year, as goods exports fell 5.4% while imports rose 3.6%.
  • S&P 500 futures gained 0.7% and Nasdaq 100 futures rose 1.1% in early trading, recovering from the prior week's rotation out of top-performing equities.
  • Amazon Prime Day generated $26.4 billion in online spending, narrowly exceeding Adobe's $26.3 billion forecast.

Inflation and Monetary Policy

May core PCE, the Federal Reserve's preferred gauge, rose again to a level well above the 3% target — the sharpest non-pandemic rise in two decades — reinforcing the case for a hawkish Fed posture under new Chair Kevin Warsh. Richmond Fed President Tom Barkin said Sunday that inflation "is too high," welcoming lower gasoline prices but warning that AI infrastructure build-out represents an independent inflationary force. One-year inflation swaps have meanwhile fallen sharply, suggesting markets are pricing in a swift pass-through from lower oil prices to consumer prices — a confidence that appears premature given US gasoline prices remain roughly 20% above year-ago levels. The divergence between market pricing and underlying data creates meaningful risk heading into the second half.

Markets

US equity futures recovered modestly from last week's tech-led selloff, with chip-equipment names — Applied Materials, KLA, and Lam Research — gaining 3–5% in premarket on South Korea's announced AI investment program. Healthcare stocks continue to outperform, beating the S&P 500 on 85% of down-days exceeding 1% this year, reflecting both defensive rotation and growing investor conviction that AI will drive material productivity gains in the sector. The Magnificent Seven have now returned approximately in line with the broader S&P 500 over the past twelve months. Bitcoin ETFs are on pace for their worst month of net outflows — exceeding $4.1 billion — since launching in January 2024. ---

Asia

  • South Korea announced a coordinated investment program of at least 1,350 trillion won ($880 billion) in chips, AI data centers, and robotics, led by Samsung and SK Hynix, framed by President Lee as a matter of national survival.
  • The People's Bank of China set the rate on its new overnight liquidity facility at a level below market expectations, in what several economists characterize as a de facto easing.
  • The three largest foreign banks in Indonesia — Citigroup, Standard Chartered, and HSBC — have remitted approximately $640 million from the country since 2024, slightly exceeding their combined local profits over the same period.

Korea's AI Investment Commitment

The scale of South Korea's announced technology investment — $880 billion across memory chips, data centers, and physical AI — is striking in both its ambition and its political framing. President Lee Jae Myung described Samsung and SK Hynix executives as "national heroes," pledging state support to build out an AI ecosystem rapidly. The announcement reflects intensifying competitive pressure from the US and China in next-generation semiconductor infrastructure. Apple and Microsoft have already cited memory chip shortages in justifying consumer price increases, and Micron's CEO indicated supply constraints may persist until 2028, suggesting the investment rationale is grounded in durable demand dynamics rather than cyclical positioning.

China Monetary Easing and Indonesia Capital Flight

The PBoC's below-consensus overnight rate setting signals a continued bias toward accommodation, as advisers flag a widening divergence between high-tech sector outperformance and sluggish domestic consumption. In Indonesia, the exit of major foreign bank capital reflects investor unease with President Prabowo's increasingly state-directed economic policies — a trend worth monitoring for broader emerging-market capital flow implications. ---

Europe

  • Spain's flash CPI for June came in faster than expected, complicating the ECB's rate deliberations ahead of its July decision and prompting hawkish commentary from Executive Board member Isabel Schnabel.
  • France recorded approximately 1,000 excess deaths last week from an "exceptional" heat wave currently sweeping east through Germany, Italy, Hungary, and the Balkans.
  • Andy Burnham, set to succeed Keir Starmer as UK prime minister, pledged the largest devolution of power to English local authorities in modern times.

ECB Sintra Forum

ECB President Christine Lagarde opened the annual Sintra forum — this year themed "Shaping Europe's future: innovation, growth and stability" — with remarks scrutinized for signals on whether a July rate hike remains possible. Kevin Warsh makes his international debut as Fed Chair at the same event, alongside Bank of England Governor Andrew Bailey and Bank of Canada Governor Tiff Macklem. The BIS's annual report, released Sunday, identified AI investment disappointment and a credit shock as top systemic risks. Spanish CPI surprising to the upside reinforces the hawkish contingent's concerns despite some relief from lower energy prices; the tension between feeble growth and persistent services inflation remains the central ECB dilemma.

Heat Wave Economic Impact

ING's global macro head Carsten Brzeski notes the ECB has estimated heat waves could add 0.4–0.9 percentage points to food inflation, an effect that may double over 30 years. The current event — arriving ahead of the typical July peak — has disrupted transport, damaged crops, and shuttered schools and cultural institutions across the continent. ING characterizes extreme heat as having "graduated from weather event to macro variable," a framing increasingly shared by European policymakers. ---

LatAm

  • Venezuela's acting President Delcy Rodríguez is managing a humanitarian crisis following twin earthquakes that killed more than 1,400 people, with tens of thousands unaccounted for — drawing an unusually broad coalition of international aid.
  • Argentine President Javier Milei's cabinet chief resigned Saturday following months of corruption allegations, marking the most significant departure from his administration since taking office.
  • Brazil and India appear in analysts' "sweet spot" of low inflation and positive growth momentum in Variant Perception's leading indicator framework, a relative bright spot versus developed markets.

Venezuela's Earthquake Response

The disaster has created an unusual diplomatic opening for Rodríguez, who has departed from her predecessors' anti-US posture by welcoming aid from approximately two dozen countries — including Trump-aligned governments in Argentina, El Salvador, and Ecuador alongside traditional partners Cuba, China, and Iran. The US pledged aid, search-and-rescue teams, and temporary sanctions relief for earthquake-related transactions through October 23. Whether Rodríguez can convert this moment of relative international engagement into durable political legitimacy remains uncertain; the risks of mismanaged relief and opposition pressure from exiled leader María Corina Machado are real constraints.

Argentina Political Risk

The resignation of Milei's cabinet chief adds to near-term political uncertainty in Argentina, though the libertarian administration's core economic program — fiscal consolidation and peso stabilization — has not publicly been called into question. Mexico's central bank reiterated that any rate pause carries no predetermined duration, signaling continued data-dependency as the region navigates divergent growth and inflation dynamics. ---

Global

  • The US and Iran agreed to halt attacks on each other ahead of peace talks in Doha, but commercial traffic through the Strait of Hormuz remains well below normal after Iranian forces struck two vessels over the weekend.
  • Transpacific shipping costs jumped nearly 12% over the past week to their highest level in a year, as Hormuz uncertainty ties up capacity and container carriers add peak-season surcharges.
  • The EU–China trade meeting in Brussels Monday features EU Commissioner Sefcovic pressing Beijing on a widening trade imbalance, with Chinese state media warning Beijing can withstand a further deterioration in ties.

Hormuz: Fragile Ceasefire, Durable Uncertainty

Financial markets have moved aggressively to price in a normalized Hormuz — oil prices suggest conditions approximating pre-conflict levels — yet the underlying situation remains materially unsettled. The IRGC declared on Sunday that "traffic control in the Strait of Hormuz is with Iran," a stance directly at odds with US terms. The IMO Secretary-General indicated approximately 80 mines may remain in the main traffic separation scheme. Oman has signaled to European officials that a return to the pre-war status quo is unlikely and that transit fees may be imposed. The divergence between market pricing and physical reality in shipping flows represents a meaningful risk; as Standard Chartered's Eric Robertsen notes, oil volatility may be giving way to rates and currency volatility in H2.

Trade Policy Fragmentation

The EU begins imposing a €3 flat duty on sub-€150 imports Wednesday, targeting Chinese e-commerce platforms. Trump simultaneously threatened 100% tariffs on countries imposing digital services taxes on US technology companies — a threat that arrived one day after the EU-US trade accord received final sign-off, illustrating the instability of the current trade framework. USMCA review talks are scheduled for Wednesday, with Washington seeking tighter rules-of-origin and labor provisions. Chinese cars surpassed 10% of new European purchases for the first time last month. The cumulative picture is one of accelerating trade fragmentation across multiple bilateral axes simultaneously.
