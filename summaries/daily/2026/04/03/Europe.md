European cities and policymakers are navigating the fifth week of elevated energy costs with a mixture of demand-side interventions and fiscal support. EU officials in Brussels are urging residents to work from home and reduce driving, while France has rolled out an aid package that includes fuel subsidies for the transport sector. Melbourne's temporary suspension of public transit fares — though an Australian rather than European measure — illustrates the range of policy responses being deployed across developed economies facing similar pressure. In the European automotive market, the oil price shock is producing a meaningful structural tailwind for Chinese EV manufacturers. Chinese brands' share of fully electric car sales has risen two percentage points to approximately 8% of the total European EV market, nearly double their share from a year prior. Bernstein analysts note that BYD stands to benefit most from an acceleration in EV adoption driven by elevated fuel costs. The trend is strategically significant: it compounds competitive pressure on European legacy automakers already grappling with the EV transition and tightening emissions standards. In the UK, sales of Chinese-made goods grew more than three times as fast in the first two months of 2026 as in the comparable 2025 period, a dynamic that is providing an effective disinflationary offset to energy-driven price pressures. The net effect on UK inflation — a key variable for Bank of England policy — depends on the balance between these two forces, which pulls in opposite directions and may complicate the rate path.
