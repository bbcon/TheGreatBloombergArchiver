Triumphs of the Optimists <https://bloom.bg/40IOUEg> 

When a shock hits the geopolitical order, generally the optimists emerge 
triumphant. As much research has shown in the last few days, most shocks are 
brief and offer an opportunity to buy cheap. That is a reassuring thought after 
investors used the second full day of trading after the outbreak of hostilities 
in Iran toexit risk assets <https://bloom.bg/4cZTV2H> in an increasingly 
panicked way.

The selloff of stocks accelerated, particularly outside the US. Investors even 
took profits in gold. Oil spiked for a second day, making this now clearly the 
biggest shock to the market since the Ukraine invasion four years ago, while 
the dollar continued to surge. Overall, this is the biggest wave of “risk-off” 
selling sinceLiberation Day <https://bloom.bg/3OXstbQ> last April:

This might create an opportunity to buy, if not now then very soon. The 
problem is that every so often the pessimists win. If a country is drawn into a 
world war that it loses, or succumbs to a communist revolution in which the 
stock market is closed down altogether, then that will mean terrible losses. 
Most geopolitical shocks are transitory — but on occasion, there’s aSarajevo 
assassination <https://bloom.bg/4cJHTKX> at which point it would have been best 
to sell Austrian stocks in their entirety.

Historical Context

This week’s publication of the latest Global Investment Returns Yearbook, a 
massive work of financial history updated annually and currently sponsored by 
UBS, demonstrates the issues. Guided by the British academics Elroy Dimson, 
Paul Marsh and Mike Staunton for three decades, their database shows that 
markets over the last century and a quarter have witnessed theTriumph of the 
Optimists <https://bloom.bg/40IOUEg>. Usually, the worst hasn’t happened, and 
those who had the faith to buy stocks when they were available at a discount to 
account for risk could enjoy massive returns from compounding the risk premium 
each year.

Geopolitics do matter at the extreme. When Dimson, Marsh and Staunton ranked 
market returns since 1900 for bills, bonds and stocks, the pattern is obvious: 
The countries that lost world wars or sustained particularly terrible damage in 
them did worst, while the victors, and those that avoided the conflicts, did 
best. The list of laggards, on the left in the chart below, includes Austria, 
Italy, Belgium, France, Germany and Japan:
Source: Global Investment Returns Yearbook 
However, to profit from this, it was necessary to work out in advance that 
outright conflict was at hand and to identify the winners. Further, while the 
20th century’s two world wars both inflicted financial losses (and nobody is 
suggesting that these were remotely as grievous as the human costs), neither of 
them were as severe as any of the four greatest peacetime bear markets since 
1900, as illustrated here:
Source: Global Investment Returns Yearbook 
The bear market that followed the Yom Kippur War is a partial exception, in 
which a relatively small war in human terms interrupted oil supply at a time 
when inflation was already rising. The bear markets starting in 1929, 2000 and 
2007 all began in times of peace and economic expansion, and helped to cause 
recessions.

There is also potential for total disaster when communist revolutionaries take 
over and close the stock market. Russia and China don’t have a continuous 
history — but with hindsight, it would have been a good idea to sell before Mao 
began his Long March, or Lenin began to rally the Bolsheviks in St. Petersburg.

Another Yom Kippur?

The odds are that the optimists will triumph this time. But the reasoning 
behind this is changing. AsIraq is already slowing <https://bloom.bg/3MTe3Jl> 
production thanks to the bottleneck in the Gulf, and air traffic shut has down 
over much of the Middle East, the risks to supply look more significant than 
many thought a few days ago. Optimism now focuses on the notion that this 
cannot continue for long.

On the US side, a drawn-out war would be terrible politics, and the 
administration has done nothing to prepare people for it. After a matter of 
weeks, there is a good chance that President Donald Trump would look for an 
excuse to desist and declare victory (whether it had been truly achieved or 
not).

Iran’s ability to extend the war depends on attrition. Its store of weaponry 
<https://bloom.bg/4047wyt> is limited and hard for China and Russia to 
resupply. The cost of missile defenses for the Gulf states is far higher than 
the cost to Iran of launching the projectiles, so its arsenal is very effective 
at inflicting economic as well as human pain. But it is finite.

Thus the odds are that the war burns out after a matter of weeks, falling far 
short of the damage inflicted by the world wars or by the Yom Kippur War.

Further, the setting is more propitious. In 1973, and also in 2022 when Russia 
invaded Ukraine, the global economy was fragile, inflation was high, and 
central banks needed to hike rates. These factors made markets more vulnerable 
to an oil price shock than they are now.

But even though this selloff looks like a buying opportunity, it’s not free 
money. If it’s an opportunity, it’s only because the risk is real. Those buying 
must hope to be compensated for that risk. Their chances are good but less than 
100%.

China

Since China’s widely flourished late-2024 pivot toward pro-growth policies, it 
has largely disappointed the expectations that it would follow through with a 
meaningful fiscal push to shore up consumption. Beijing’s complex political and 
bureaucratic machinery has shown little urgency in jump-starting domestic 
demand, even as Trump’s tariffs made it ever more important to diversify away 
from exports.

International investors have noticed. After leaping in late 2024, Chinese 
stocks have steadily lagged further behind the rest of the world amid both the 
trade war and continuing disappointment over stimulus:

Doubts that China really can continue to grow at 5% per year permeate 
investors’ thinking. The most recent Absolute Strategy Research survey of 
global asset allocators found that a majority thought the target could not be 
reached. The official data have continued to reach 5% despite these persistent 
doubts, but a Chinese slowdown remains one of the biggest risks to prevailing 
optimism:

On Thursday, the National People’s Congress <https://bloom.bg/4r7ruDu> 
convenes once more, and policymakers will face the question of whether it has 
enough firepower to sustain the growth. Beyond announcing this year’s target, 
they should also provide clarity on the macro picture and offer meaningful 
insights into the 15thFive-Year Plan <https://bloom.bg/40MUeGF>. Even as 
investors increasingly accept that a dramatic surge in fiscal stimulus is 
unlikely, the issue will continue to dominate discussions.

With fixed‑asset investment contracting in 2025 — the first time in more than 
three decades — while household consumption softened as earlier subsidies 
faded, Helen Qiao of Bank of America argues that the need for stronger policy 
support is becoming increasingly difficult to ignore.

Bloomberg Economics’ Eric Zhu and Chang Shu note that there’s no universally 
accepted benchmark for household consumption as a share of gross domestic 
product, but contend that China’s 41% lags peers considerably. That suggests 
potential for further upside. How much it can rise will depend on the scale and 
effectiveness of policies to address structural drags onconsumer spending 
<https://bloom.bg/40LGQCE>. In their most optimistic scenario, Zhu and Shu 
forecast that the household consumption will be about 50% of GDP by 2030:

In the upside scenario, forceful measures would revive sentiment and spending 
quickly, ramping up consumption to Japan’s current level. Even then, growth 
would not return to pre-pandemic trends. The base case is more restrained — 
moderately faster fiscal spending and gradually strengthening policy measures 
would lift consumption to about 46% of GDP. China Beige Book shows that 
domestic demand is sprouting, though not impactful enough to inspire hope of a 
turnaround.

Gavekal Research’s Ernan Cui attributes some of the gains to policy rather 
than organic consumer demand. For example, the rise in long-haul trips stems 
from unusually long holidays. Officials extended the statutory break to nine 
days in a bid to boost retail spending, from eight in 2025 and seven in 
previous years:

The government support is helpful, but the primary constraint on consumption 
growth remains: Chinese households are unusually cautious. This is reflected in 
the elevated household savings rate implied by quarterly household surveys, 
which rose in 2025.

Meanwhile, BofA’s Qiao expects overall growth for 2026 to be set slightly 
lower, most plausibly as a “4.5-5.0%” range, compared with last year’s “around 
5.0%”:

A marginally lower national target would acknowledge the current reality: A 
still‑soft domestic demand backdrop and lingering structural headwinds from 
property, demographics, and local‑government finances. But it would not imply a 
withdrawal of policy support.

Beyond consumption, artificial intelligence should feature prominently. 
Rebecca Arcesati of theMercator Institute for China Studies 
<https://bloom.bg/4u2V4wJ> points out that if the “AI Plus” action plan 
released in August is any guide, then AI devices, agents, and applications 
should reach 70 percent penetration by 2027, 90 percent by 2030, and ubiquitous 
deployment by 2035:

That these are aspirational goals rather than actionable KPIs is clear... 
These numbers function as signals rather than actual targets: They tell the 
Chinese Communist Party cadres and firms where the central government expects 
them to channel their efforts.

China’s ambitions for AI might seem a stretch but what they may lack in access 
to advanced AI chips, they can offset elsewhere. Vast electricity-generation 
capacity and dominance of rare earths offer structural advantages. The 
Five-Year Plan, to be adopted after the leadership convenes, should provide a 
clear blueprint of how the party intends to press that advantage.

—Richard Abbey
