May 18 – May 24, 2026

US

Macro Backdrop: Inflation Anxiety Eclipses Easing Narrative

The dominant US theme this week was the bond market's accelerating repricing of the inflation outlook, as the closure of the Strait of Hormuz approached its three-month mark and the Iran war showed no clear path to resolution. Thirty-year Treasury yields rose to 5.19% on Tuesday, the highest level since June 2007, while 10-year yields touched 4.63% on Monday before easing modestly later in the week. The selloff reflected a convergence of factors: persistent energy-driven inflation concerns, expanding fiscal deficits, and a substantive reassessment of the Federal Reserve's policy path. Fed funds futures, which in late February implied a rate of approximately 2.8% by mid-2027, now price in a rate above 4%, with traders assigning roughly a 70% probability to a quarter-point hike by December.

Federal Reserve Transition Amid Hawkish Repricing

Minutes from the April FOMC meeting, released Wednesday, revealed a more hawkish committee than markets had previously assumed. Almost all participants noted that energy prices could remain elevated longer than expected even after the conflict ends, and a majority indicated some policy firming would likely become appropriate if inflation continued to run persistently above 2%. Many preferred removing the easing-bias language from the post-meeting statement altogether. Kevin Warsh is scheduled to be sworn in as Fed Chair on Friday, inheriting a constrained environment in which engineering the rate cuts President Trump has sought would risk exacerbating the bond selloff. Headline CPI rose 3.8% year-over-year in April, the highest reading since 2023.

Equity Markets: AI Narrative Sustains a Narrowing Rally

US equities showed remarkable resilience despite the bond market pressures, with the S&P 500 on track for its eighth consecutive weekly gain — the longest such streak since 2023. However, the rally remains exceptionally narrow: an equal-weighted S&P 500 is unchanged since the war began in late February, with ten technology names accounting for essentially all index gains. The S&P 500 declined more than 1% on Friday of the prior week before stabilizing, and futures touched 7,489 on Friday. Bank of America's fund manager survey showed equity allocations rising at the fastest pace on record, a positioning extreme that strategist Michael Hartnett characterized as approaching "bull capitulation." Nvidia reported Wednesday after the close, posting 85% revenue growth and 62% gross margins, alongside an additional $80 billion buyback authorization. The stock dipped modestly in extended trading as the sales forecast drew a lukewarm response, though the results catalyzed a substantial rally in Asian AI-linked names Thursday. The Philadelphia Semiconductor Index remains up roughly 60% year-to-date.

SpaceX Files for Historic IPO

SpaceX filed publicly Thursday for what would be the largest IPO in history, targeting up to $75 billion at a valuation exceeding $2 trillion. The filing disclosed 2025 revenue of $18.7 billion and a swing to a $4.94 billion loss from a $791 million profit in 2024, with Starlink accounting for roughly two-thirds of first-quarter sales. Goldman Sachs and Morgan Stanley lead the underwriting syndicate. OpenAI is reportedly preparing its own IPO filing within weeks, with Anthropic potentially following in October.

Corporate and Sector Developments

NextEra Energy announced a $66–67 billion acquisition of Dominion Energy, the largest US utility deal on record, reflecting consolidation pressures from AI-driven power demand. Google and Blackstone agreed to form an AI cloud joint venture anchored by a $5 billion equity commitment from Blackstone, with total leveraged value of approximately $25 billion. JPMorgan CEO Jamie Dimon indicated the firm would hire more AI specialists and fewer traditional bankers, while warning that interest rates "could be much higher than they are today." Meta began notifying approximately 8,000 employees of layoffs as it reassigns 7,000 workers to AI-related roles.

Asia

The week was defined by the collision of two macro forces shaping Asia: the ongoing Iran war and the energy shock emanating from a closed Strait of Hormuz, and the increasingly concentrated AI-driven rally in regional equity markets. The two narratives pulled in opposite directions, leaving policymakers and investors to navigate sharply divergent outcomes across the region. China's economy showed broad-based weakness in April data released Monday, with retail sales rising just 0.2% year-on-year — the weakest reading since December 2022 — while industrial production expanded 4.1% and fixed-asset investment unexpectedly contracted 1.6% year-to-date. Car sales fell 15% and gold and jewelry sales declined 21%. The figures undershot every economist estimate surveyed by Bloomberg and prompted renewed calls for stimulus, though Beijing has thus far appeared reluctant to act. Separately, the previous week's Trump-Xi summit was reassessed by analysts as a "placeholder" exchange: China committed to $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchases through 2028 and to establishing trade and investment boards, but specifics remained vague, and the Boeing-related disappointment weighed on aircraft-linked names. Putin's visit to Beijing on Tuesday-Wednesday delivered atmospherics but no announced breakthrough on the Power of Siberia 2 pipeline. Xi publicly urged against any resumption of US strikes on Iran, while Russia's leverage in the gas negotiations appeared diminished — China retains alternative supply via Central Asia and robust domestic coal and renewables, and Beijing has folded the pipeline into its five-year plan on its own terms. The energy shock continued to ripple through the region. Japan's PM Takaichi reversed her earlier stance and instructed the finance ministry to compile an extra budget of roughly $19 billion, including revived energy subsidies; the 30-year JGB yield surged as much as 20 basis points Monday to its highest level since the tenor's 1999 debut. Japan's Q1 GDP, reported Tuesday, surprised to the upside at 2.1% annualized. Friday's CPI print showed core inflation slowing to a four-year low, complicating the BOJ's path. Thailand announced a 200 billion baht ($6.1 billion) cost-of-living package. India's rupee approached 97 per dollar, prompting the RBI to weigh rate hikes, FX swaps, and overseas dollar-raising measures reminiscent of its 2013 taper-tantrum playbook. Foreign outflows from Indian equities accelerated, and Reliance's Jio IPO hit roadblocks tied to war-driven volatility. Indonesia delivered the week's largest policy surprise. Bank Indonesia hiked the policy rate 50 basis points Wednesday to 5.25% — double consensus — to defend the rupiah. Hours earlier, President Prabowo announced that exports of palm oil, thermal coal, and ferro-alloys (totaling over $65 billion annually) would be routed through a new state-controlled entity under sovereign wealth fund Danantara. The Jakarta Composite fell 3.5% Tuesday and is down roughly 26% year-to-date, the world's worst major benchmark. Moody's flagged risks to confidence, and even senior Danantara officials were reportedly blindsided. The contrast with North Asian equities was stark. South Korea's Kospi, up 73% year-to-date and the world's best performer, fell more than 3% Tuesday before surging 8.4% Thursday after Samsung reached an eleventh-hour deal with its union — averting a strike — and Nvidia's earnings catalyzed a regional tech rally. Samsung chip employees stand to receive average bonuses of approximately $340,000. Taiwan's Taiex rose 3.4% Thursday; SK Hynix gained nearly 8%, pushing its market value toward $910 billion. Korean and Japanese export data for the first 20 days of May showed chip shipments up 202% and 44% respectively year-on-year, evidence that the AI capex cycle continues to insulate North Asian trade from the energy shock — though concentration risk is acute, with Samsung and SK Hynix accounting for two-thirds of the Kospi's 2026 gain. Europe

Eurozone activity contracts as energy shock bites

The clearest signal of the week came from Thursday's flash purchasing managers' indices, which showed euro-area composite activity falling to 47.5 in May from 48.8 in April — the second consecutive month below the 50 contraction threshold and the weakest reading in two and a half years. France deteriorated sharply to its lowest level since 2020, while Germany's composite reading was broadly stable. The European Commission concurrently projected the bloc would experience its fastest inflation since 2023. Against this backdrop, Belgian ECB Governor Pierre Wunsch indicated a June rate hike is likely, and Christine Lagarde, questioned in Paris during the G-7 finance ministers' meeting on Monday, acknowledged the bond-market gyrations were "always my job" to monitor. The contrast with prior easing expectations is stark: the energy shock from the closure of the Strait of Hormuz, now approaching its third month, is forcing a broad reassessment across European central banks, with markets pricing further tightening rather than the cuts anticipated earlier this year.

Bond market under sustained pressure

European sovereign yields participated in the global selloff that took 30-year US Treasury yields to 5.19% on Tuesday, the highest since 2007. The Iran war's inflationary impulse, combined with fiscal expansion concerns, drove the move. UK gilts proved a partial exception, gaining Monday after Andy Burnham — currently the leading contender to replace Keir Starmer — ruled out altering the government's self-imposed borrowing limits. UK inflation data released Wednesday showed price growth at a more than one-year low, prompting traders to scale back Bank of England hike expectations. Separately, Emmanuel Macron's nominee Emmanuel Moulin was confirmed by parliament as Bank of France governor, removing a source of political uncertainty.

EU finalizes US trade deal text

On Wednesday, EU lawmakers and member states reached agreement on legislation implementing the bloc's trade pact with the US, clearing a major hurdle ahead of Trump's July 4 deadline. The deal removes EU tariffs on American industrial goods in exchange for a 15% ceiling on European exports. Failure to ratify risked a 25% levy on European autos — a particularly acute threat given the industrial sector's existing strain from the energy shock. Separately, EU officials began exploring new defensive measures against Chinese import flows, with an orientation debate on China planned ahead of a June leaders' summit. Beijing warned of countermeasures if Brussels proceeds with restrictions.

UK political turbulence and policy response

Labour's leadership contest continued to color sterling markets, with hedge funds maintaining bearish positioning. The contest has also reopened the question of EU relations, with both Wes Streeting and Burnham suggesting a case for rejoining the bloc — comments that, as Lionel Laurent observed, will shape European capitals' calculus on how far any UK reset can plausibly extend. Chancellor Rachel Reeves on Thursday unveiled cost-of-living measures including free August bus travel for children, potential food-tariff cuts, and closure of an oil-and-gas tax loophole projected to yield approximately £300 million annually. UK retail sales fell at the fastest pace in nearly a year, and the Institute for Fiscal Studies reported only 50.6% of 16-to-24-year-olds are in payrolled work, down from 54.9% at end-2022.

Corporate developments

UK M&A activity continued its surge, with year-to-date target value reaching roughly $150 billion — a more than 250% increase and the strongest since 2015. Standard Chartered named Manus Costello CFO Monday and announced plans Tuesday to eliminate close to 8,000 support roles by 2030 through AI adoption; CEO Bill Winters subsequently apologized for characterizing the change as replacing "lower-value human capital." The planned Estée Lauder–Puig acquisition of Charlotte Tilbury collapsed on Thursday over compensation terms. Germany launched the privatization of Uniper, potentially Europe's largest utility transaction this year. The Council of Experts on Climate Change warned Germany is likely to miss its 2030 emissions target by up to 100 million tons of CO2, with the Merz coalition having abandoned the renewable heating mandate. ##

Global

The Hormuz Premium Hardens Into a Macro Regime

What began nearly three months ago as an acute supply disruption has, by the close of week 21, settled into something resembling a structural condition. The Strait of Hormuz remained largely closed throughout the week, with commercial shipping reduced to limited Iranian-linked movements. Iran said on Friday that Washington's latest proposal had partly bridged differences, but Supreme Leader statements on retaining near-weapons-grade uranium and a reported Iran-Oman plan for a permanent toll regime in the strait undermined hopes of imminent resolution. President Trump called off a planned strike on Tuesday at the request of Gulf allies seeking more time for diplomacy, then by week's end threatened renewed action. NATO began discussions on potential intervention to reopen the waterway if it remained closed into early July. Oil prices reflected the oscillation between escalation and de-escalation rather than any directional conviction. Brent traded above $111 on Monday before falling 5.6% on Wednesday on diplomatic optimism, then climbing back toward $105 by Friday. WTI ended the week near $98. Goldman Sachs estimated global visible inventories were drawing down at 8.7 million barrels per day in May — nearly double the average pace since the conflict began — and US crude stocks including the SPR fell by a record 17.8 million barrels in the latest weekly print. Rapidan Energy warned that sustained closure through August could push Brent toward $130. A consensus is forming among market participants around $100 as the likely cap over the next year, with demand destruction expected to do the rebalancing work that supply cannot.

The Bond Market Reprices Inflation Risk

The most consequential development of the week occurred in sovereign debt. The US 30-year Treasury yield reached 5.19% on Tuesday, the highest since June 2007, while the 10-year touched 4.63%. The selloff was genuinely global: Japanese 30-year yields surged as much as 20 basis points on Monday to their highest level since the tenor's 1999 debut, prompting Prime Minister Takaichi to reverse course and order an extra budget of roughly $19 billion. UK gilts, German bunds, and Indonesian and Indian sovereigns all came under pressure. Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields reached its highest since July 2008. The G-7 finance ministers' meeting in Paris on Monday and Tuesday was overshadowed by the move. Traders have shifted decisively: fed funds futures now imply roughly a 70% probability of a Fed rate hike by December, having priced cuts as recently as late February. Minutes from the April FOMC meeting, released Wednesday, showed a majority of participants prepared to consider firming if inflation remained persistently above target, and many wanted to drop the easing bias from the post-meeting statement. Kevin Warsh is scheduled to be sworn in as Fed chair Friday into a backdrop that constrains rather than enables the dovish pivot the administration had sought.

Divergence Sharpens Across Central Banks

Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50-basis-point hike on Wednesday to 5.25%, double consensus expectations, as the rupiah approached record lows near 16,500 per dollar. The Reserve Bank of India is reportedly considering its 2013 taper-tantrum playbook — rate hikes, swap lines, overseas dollar issuance — with the rupee near 97. The ECB is widely expected to hike in June, with Belgian Governor Wunsch among those signaling readiness. Japan's core inflation slowed to a four-year low, complicating but not eliminating the BOJ's tightening path. The pattern is one of central banks responding to a common supply shock with differentiated urgency depending on currency vulnerability and energy import exposure.
