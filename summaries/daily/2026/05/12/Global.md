The Iran conflict remains the most consequential cross-cutting variable in global markets. Trump's characterization of the US-Iran ceasefire as on "massive life support" — after he rejected Tehran's latest peace offer as "a piece of garbage" — drove Brent crude above $107 a barrel, with WTI near $98.93. The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively closed, with one commodity shipping major already planning for the scenario where it stays shut through year-end. BloombergNEF estimates disruption across 27 Middle Eastern oil fields has reached 9 million barrels per day. A supertanker that exited the Persian Gulf over the weekend appeared to reverse course toward the Gulf of Oman, illustrating the operational paralysis affecting physical oil flows. Adding to supply-chain stress, Qatar has directed vessels near the Ras Laffan LNG export facility to disable their AIS transponders, with at least nine tankers going dark from May 11 — ostensibly a safety measure, though the opacity itself adds uncertainty.

The short-term buffer preventing a more severe oil price spike rests on two factors that analysts at Morgan Stanley and Mercuria both characterize as unsustainable: record US crude and product exports, running above 14 million barrels per day with emergency stockpile drawdowns at record rates, and a sharp reduction in Chinese crude purchases as state refiners draw on commercial inventories. Morgan Stanley estimates neither dynamic can persist, and that a prolonged Hormuz closure into late June or July would push prices materially higher.

The supply-chain stress from the energy crisis is registering in indicators that previously flashed red during the pandemic period. The Iran war has squeezed global jet fuel supplies ahead of peak driving season. Separately, the race for critical minerals — particularly rare earths and antimony — continues, with Europe struggling to mobilize capital for projects such as a Cold War-era antimony mine in Slovakia, while concern grows in Brussels that any US-China investment accommodation reached at this week's summit could prioritize American companies at European expense.
