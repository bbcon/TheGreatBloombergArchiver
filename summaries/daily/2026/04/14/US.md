US equities continued their recovery on Monday, with the S&P 500 rising more than 1% to erase all losses recorded since the outbreak of the US-Iran conflict on February 28 — a milestone that has surprised many market observers given the scale of economic disruption since. The recovery appears driven less by geopolitical optimism than by structural momentum: momentum-driven strategies that were performing well ahead of the conflict never suffered a serious reversal, and investor appetite to resume the prior bull run has proven resilient. As SocGen's Andrew Lapthorne noted, the market does not appear to be pricing elevated tail risk so much as reflecting an investor base "conditioned to buy the dip" after three years and $35 trillion in MSCI All-World gains.

Still, the macro backdrop has deteriorated materially since February 28. The 10-year Treasury yield has climbed from 3.94% to 4.28%, Brent crude has risen from roughly $72 to near $99 per barrel, and the IMF is expected to downgrade its global growth projections in its World Economic Outlook release. First-quarter earnings season began with disappointments from Goldman Sachs and industrial bellwether Fastenal, though bank earnings from JPMorgan, Citigroup, and Wells Fargo were due before Tuesday's open.

The White House used the IMF-World Bank Spring Meetings in Washington to publish its annual Economic Report of the President, projecting 3.5% growth this year and an average of 3% through 2036 — figures well above Fed and IMF estimates. The administration attributed its bullish outlook to deregulation and immigration controls, and projected 10-year Treasury yields falling to 3.7% this year, against a current rate of approximately 4.28% and a CBO forecast of 4.4% for the early 2030s. The gap between White House projections and independent forecasts remains substantial.

Soaring electricity costs are emerging as a notable political risk ahead of November midterm elections, with rapid AI data center buildout, tariff-related cost pressures, and grid upgrade spending pushing utility bills higher at rates not seen in decades. The Iran conflict's impact on gasoline prices has compounded the affordability strain.
