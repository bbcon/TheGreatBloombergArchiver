UK political risk moved sharply to the foreground on Friday after Greater Manchester Mayor Andy Burnham secured a credible path to challenge Keir Starmer for the Labour leadership, following the decision by the MP for Makerfield to stand aside in his favour. Burnham, characterised in domestic media as a figure with genuine popular appeal, is now considered the frontrunner to unseat the prime minister. Markets responded promptly: the pound fell, gilt yields surged — with the 10-year reaching 5.14%, up 15 basis points on the day — and UK assets underperformed materially relative to already-weak European peers. The political anxiety is compounded by investor concern that a leadership transition could produce a more expansive fiscal posture at a time when the gilt market is already absorbing significant supply pressure from oil-driven inflation.

The broader context is one of European political fragility. Romania's government has fallen, Latvia's prime minister resigned over a drone-related controversy, and Germany's chancellor faced trade union protests. The analysis that "few places appear governable" may be rhetorical in framing but captures something real about the compression of political time horizons across the continent. Italy under Giorgia Meloni stands as a notable exception to this pattern of rapid leadership attrition.

On green finance, the UK has halved its pledge to the Green Climate Fund — reducing its 2024-2027 contribution from £1.6 billion to £815 million — a further withdrawal from multilateral climate commitments that follows the US cancellation of a $4 billion pledge last year. Separately, an analysis of London commercial property suggests that as much as 78% of Westminster offices and 71% of City of London offices will fail to meet Minimum Energy Efficiency Standards expected to take effect in the early 2030s, raising material stranded-asset risk across the sector. In eastern European bond markets, Hungary's borrowing costs have fallen below Poland's for the first time since 2020, reflecting investor optimism surrounding new Prime Minister Peter Magyar's push to align Budapest with EU mainstream policy and revive the country's euro accession path.
