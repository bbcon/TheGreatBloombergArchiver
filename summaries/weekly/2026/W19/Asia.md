**Semiconductor Rally and the AI Super-Cycle**
Asian equity markets delivered one of their more striking weekly performances. South Korea's Kospi surged more than 14% on the week, with Samsung Electronics rallying 14–16% on Wednesday to cross a $1 trillion market capitalization — only the second Asian company to achieve that threshold. SK Hynix rose 30% over the week. Goldman Sachs raised its Kospi target for the fourth time this year, projecting an additional 20% gain and citing forecast earnings growth of 300% for 2026 — characterizing it as the strongest such figure for any Asian market since the 1999 post-crisis recovery. Taiwan's benchmark gained approximately 7% and Japan's Nikkei 225 rose 5.8% to a record high, recovering sharply from Monday's modest pullback as Strait of Hormuz diplomacy shifted sentiment. Morgan Stanley's chief Asia economist Chetan Ahya framed the broader dynamic as the beginning of a multi-year industrial super-cycle driven by AI infrastructure, energy transition, and defense spending.

**China: Sanctions Defiance and Financial Maneuvering**
Beijing's posture on US secondary sanctions evolved in nuanced and consequential ways across the week. The week opened with Beijing ordering private companies to disregard US secondary sanctions — a deliberate departure from its historically cautious approach of quiet compliance — following Washington's sanctioning of Hengli Petrochemical over Iranian crude purchases. By Thursday, however, China's largest banks had been quietly directed to halt new lending to five recently sanctioned refiners, leaving existing loans intact. The juxtaposition reveals a calculated approach: projecting defiance on sovereignty grounds while shielding state-owned financial institutions from dollar-access risks. JPMorgan and Citigroup are simultaneously being sued in Chinese courts after freezing payments tied to a subsequently sanctioned firm, illustrating the narrowing corridor available to global banks caught between competing legal jurisdictions.

**Japan: Intervention, Deployment, and Diplomatic Positioning**
Japan's Ministry of Finance deployed approximately $30 billion in estimated interventions across the week to support the yen near ¥160 per dollar, producing short-term moves of 1–2% on multiple days but achieving limited durable effect. Analysts broadly characterize the operations as increasingly ineffective level-targeting absent a meaningful shift in Bank of Japan policy, given elevated energy import costs and still-accommodative monetary settings. On the geopolitical front, Japan's deployment of 1,400 combat troops to the Philippines for joint Balikatan drills — including a ship-sinking exercise in the South China Sea on Wednesday — marks the first such deployment since World War II. Prime Minister Takaichi's overseas visits to Vietnam and Australia, alongside a ruling-party delegation to Taiwan, underscore a deepening regional security posture whose contrast with limited China engagement was conspicuous.

**Regional Differentiation: Oil-Exposed vs. AI-Leveraged**
The bifurcation between AI-leveraged and oil-exposed economies is becoming a defining feature of the regional growth landscape. Indonesia's first-quarter GDP expanded 5.61% year-on-year — a three-year high — even as the rupiah set a record low, authorities tightened foreign currency purchase limits to $25,000, and the Philippines registered its worst consumer spending data since the pandemic. The Reserve Bank of Australia delivered a third consecutive rate hike in an 8-1 vote on Tuesday, unwinding all of last year's easing and leading developed-market peers in the hawkish response to the energy shock. Toyota issued a notably cautious earnings outlook on Friday, projecting ¥3 trillion in operating income against a consensus of ¥4.6 trillion, citing raw material cost pressures from the Iran conflict — the stock fell as much as 3.5%.
