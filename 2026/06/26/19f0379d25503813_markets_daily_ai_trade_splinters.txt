AI trade splinters

If there’s one takeaway from this week’s head-snapping moves in global stocks, 
it’s this:the days of everything AI going up may be over 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/the-ai-trade-still-works-but-it-s-getting-harder-taking-stock?cmpid=062626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5970>
.

Tuesday delivered a major scare, when a 10% drop in South Korea spilled into a 
near 8% slide in major chipmakers. After a brief respite courtesy of Micron’s 
blowout earnings report, sellers returned in force on Friday.

The reason has to do with Apple’s decision to hike prices. It’s sent the 
loudest signal yet 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/apple-price-hikes-spark-asia-tech-selloff-on-memory-cost-concern?cmpid=062626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5970>
 of the strains coming from rising component costs. With chipmakers flexing 
their pricing power, other companies are being forced to pass that onto 
consumers.

“Markets are no longer treating memory strength as an automatic positive for 
the whole AI trade,” said Charu Chanana of Saxo Markets. “It validates AI 
infrastructure demand, but it also raises the cost of building and consuming 
AI.”

Zooming out, it’s clear that the AI rally has stopped being a single trade. 
Memory names have risen more than 200% this year, while data center and 
electrical-equipment baskets are up more than 100%. AI semiconductors have 
rallied around 85% and are still trending higher.

Compare that to the Magnificent Seven bundle that includes Alphabet, Amazon, 
Meta and Apple — that group is down for 2026. The software cohort most exposed 
to AI disruption has shed a third of its value.

While there hasn’t been panic selling, investors are more discerning. Merely 
owning “AI” no longer tells you what you own. —Jan-Patrick Barnert 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AR_gUvLK3Qk/janpatrick-barnert?cmpid=062626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5970>

Dollar ‘activated’

A year ago, de-dollarization, the debasement trade 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/the-debasement-trade-is-unraveling-and-kevin-warsh-is-one-big-reason?cmpid=062626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5970>
 and the so-called hedge America strategy were popular themes on Wall Street. 
Kevin Warsh may haveput an end to all that 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/wall-street-embraces-the-dollar-as-warsh-s-fed-activates-bulls?cmpid=062626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5970>
.

Strategists at JPMorgan, Bank of America, Goldman Sachs and other banks have 
expressed have renewed faith in the greenback after the new Fed chair vowed to 
restore price stability, spurring interest rate-hike bets.

The Fed has “activated” the dollar bullish outlook, said Meera Chandan of 
JPMorgan. “It doesn’t look like other central banks will catch up and the rate 
gap will shrink for the dollar.”

It’s not just a hawkish Fed emboldening dollar bulls. The US economy remains 
strong and AI is driving massive corporate spending and inflows into the stock 
market, as investors bet on productivity gains that will further lift the 
currency.

To see a more prominent dollar rise, the Fed needs to deliver more than the 
roughly one to two quarter-point hikes the market has priced in through early 
next year, according to Jayati Bharadwaj of TD Securities. —Anya Andrianova 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ARyaBUHvMI8/anya-andrianova?cmpid=062626_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5970>
