Weekly Brief

Mar 09 – Mar 15, 2026

US

  • S&P 500 declined roughly 3% over the week, with the Magnificent Seven nearing correction territory, as the Iran conflict and stagflationary pressures overshadowed a broadly benign February CPI print.
  • February payrolls recorded their first private-sector decline in five years, yet swap markets repriced toward Fed tightening — a textbook stagflation tension that will define the central bank's posture heading into next week's FOMC.
  • Amazon raised $37 billion in the fourth-largest US corporate bond sale on record; private credit stress deepened, with Morgan Stanley and Cliffwater capping fund redemptions and JPMorgan restricting lending to certain private credit vehicles.
  • The USTR filed sweeping Section 301 investigations against more than a dozen economies — including China, the EU, Mexico, India, and Taiwan — marking the first formal step to reconstitute the tariff architecture following last month's Supreme Court ruling.
  • Goldman Sachs raised its 12-month US recession probability by 5 percentage points to 25%, citing the oil shock, softening labor data, and narrowing Fed policy space.

Labor Market and the Stagflation Bind

The week's most consequential domestic data point arrived before it began: Friday's February payrolls report showed the first private-sector decline in five years, alongside a rising unemployment rate. Under ordinary circumstances, that combination would reinforce expectations for Federal Reserve easing and satisfy the administration's persistent pressure on the central bank to cut rates. Instead, implied fed funds rates in overnight index swap markets moved higher across the week, as oil-driven inflation expectations overwhelmed the labor market signal. Oxford Economics maintained its baseline of Fed inaction until June, while Goldman Sachs revised its first cut expectation further to September. The ICE BofA MOVE Index climbed to its highest level since June as traders dialed back rate-cut expectations materially. The February CPI print — released Wednesday — showed core inflation at 2.5% year-on-year, the lowest since the 2021 breakout, with shelter costs rising just 0.1% month-on-month. The figure would ordinarily comfort policymakers, but it is being overshadowed by a divergence: core PCE appears on track to print near 3.1% for January, the widest gap between the two gauges in decades, with Barclays, Morgan Stanley, and Bank of America projecting core PCE goods prices advancing at least 0.8% in February alone. With Kevin Warsh still unable to secure a clear Senate confirmation path for the Fed chair role, the central bank's leadership question remains unresolved at precisely the moment its reaction function is most consequential.

Fiscal and Energy Cost Interaction

Bloomberg Economics quantified the household stakes with uncomfortable precision: the "One Big Beautiful Bill" is estimated to deliver approximately $800 in additional tax refunds this year, while crude settling near $80 per barrel would impose roughly $650 in incremental energy costs — effectively neutralizing most of the gain. At $83 per barrel the benefit is entirely erased; at $110 the net household position worsens by approximately $1,960. The distributional concern compounds the aggregate: energy costs are regressive, and the top 20% of earners already account for over 59% of personal consumption, raising the prospect of amplified K-shaped dynamics as the conflict prolongs. Pre-conflict consumer data offered a partial counterpoint — Bank of America card spending rose 3.3% year-on-year in February, the strongest in over three years — but that strength predates gasoline reaching its highest level in more than 18 months. The 30-year Treasury yield approached 4.90% mid-week as investors priced in war financing costs and potential household energy subsidies, even as the February budget deficit narrowed at a slower pace than the prior month.

Corporate and Credit Developments

Amazon's $37 billion bond issuance — with a further $11.6 billion sought in European markets — underscored that investment-grade issuers with genuine AI infrastructure narratives retain market access. Oracle shares surged approximately 10% in extended trading Thursday after reporting strong AI cloud demand, reinforcing hyperscaler capital expenditure momentum. The contrast with private credit was stark: Morgan Stanley and Cliffwater capped redemptions from multi-billion-dollar funds as withdrawal requests exceeded permitted limits, JPMorgan moved to restrict lending to certain private credit vehicles after marking down software company loan valuations, and the Bloomberg US Corporate Bond Index's year-to-date returns fell barely above zero with investment-grade yield-to-worst reaching 4.9%. The Markit CDX North American Investment Grade Index posted its largest spread widening since last May. Adobe fell approximately 8.5% in pre-market trading Friday following CEO Shantanu Narayen's departure after 18 years and a lackluster revenue outlook, reflecting deepening investor concern about the company's competitive positioning in the AI era. Hims & Hers Health surged 49% Monday after Novo Nordisk agreed to distribute its weight-loss drugs on the telehealth platform, while airline stocks — American, Delta, and United — fell more than 3% on fuel cost exposure early in the week. The USTR's Section 301 filings against more than a dozen economies, with a public hearing set for May 5th, introduce a further layer of uncertainty into corporate supply chain planning and complicate the planned Trump-Xi summit. ---

Asia

  • China's first two months of 2026 trade surplus reached a record $214 billion on export growth of 21.9% year-on-year, even as domestic consumption may have recorded its weakest non-pandemic start to a year.
  • Asian currencies came under significant pressure by Friday: the yen weakened to approximately 159.5 against the dollar — its softest since 2024 — South Korea's won neared its lowest since 2009, and the Indian rupee and Philippine peso reached historic lows.
  • China's strategic petroleum reserves at a record 851 million barrels in onshore commercial inventories provided a meaningful buffer against the Hormuz shock, even as Chinese refiners began canceling agreed refined-fuel export cargoes to manage domestic supply.
  • India secured a US sanctions waiver allowing resumed Russian crude purchases, providing near-term relief to refiners pivoting away from Gulf supply chains.
  • The Trump-Xi summit framework — preparatory talks in Paris over the weekend involving Bessent, Greer, and He Lifeng — is taking shape for a March 31–April 2 meeting in Beijing, with tariffs, Taiwan, fentanyl, and semiconductors on the agenda.

Equity and Currency Markets

Asian markets experienced the sharpest regional equity losses of any zone early in the week. The MSCI Asia-Pacific index fell 3.7% on Monday, extending its weekly decline to nearly 10%, before partially recovering Tuesday as Brent retreated from its intraday high and President Trump signaled a possible near-term conflict resolution. The Nikkei 225 dropped 5.2% Monday before rebounding 2.88% Tuesday; South Korea's Kospi fell 6% at the worst of the selloff. By Friday, sentiment had soured again, with the Hang Seng declining 0.98% and regional currencies under acute pressure. The Thai baht fell as much as 1.8% intraday Friday. The oil shock interacts with Japan's structurally weak yen to amplify import cost burdens, raising the risk of stagflation in an economy where activity was already sluggish; more than a third of surveyed economists now expect the Bank of Japan to raise rates in April. South Korea's won, Indian rupee, and Philippine peso all reached multi-year or historic lows against the dollar as the week progressed.

China: Export Strength, Domestic Fragility, and Strategic Positioning

China's trade data released Tuesday provided a striking counterpoint to broader regional anxiety: exports surged 21.9% year-on-year in the first two months of 2026, well above the 7.2% median estimate, with a record $214 billion trade surplus for the period, even as exports to the US fell 11%, reflecting tariff effects. Yet the divergence between China's external performance and domestic conditions is widening. Workers in Guangdong province report falling wages and disappearing jobs, with automation and AI increasingly substituting for human labor; worker protest activity rose 44% last year, with Guangdong the primary hotspot. China's consumer spending may have recorded its worst start to any year outside the pandemic, according to economists surveying ahead of the official data release. The National People's Congress concluded its annual budget deliberations with the 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030) prioritizing advanced manufacturing, semiconductors, and AI while maintaining a 4% of GDP fiscal deficit — a continuity document that disappointed investors hoping for meaningful household consumption stimulus. Chinese authorities also moved to restrict state enterprises from deploying OpenClaw AI applications on office computers, causing Minimax to fall 6.5% in Hong Kong and Zhipu to drop 6.1% on cybersecurity concerns. Beijing's record oil stockpiles, renewable investment, and diplomatic positioning — Foreign Minister Wang Yi describing 2026 as a potentially defining year for US-China relations — reflect a calculated effort to emerge from the crisis with strategic advantages intact, though Bloomberg Economics cautions that a sustained oil price above $108 per barrel could meaningfully drag on Chinese export demand via secondary global demand effects.

India, Southeast Asia, and Regional Stress

India's position evolved notably across the week. New Delhi had been reducing Russian oil purchases under US pressure since December, pivoting toward Gulf suppliers — precisely the supply chains most disrupted by the Hormuz closure. Washington's announcement of a one-month sanctions waiver allowing resumed Russian crude purchases provided material relief, with Indian refiners immediately absorbing available Urals spot cargoes and tankers carrying over 20 million barrels within a week's sailing time of Indian ports. Nevertheless, the rupee remains under pressure with capital outflows accelerating, and Indian equities have underperformed EM peers significantly. India is simultaneously conducting direct negotiations with Iran to secure safe passage for more than twenty tankers — a transactional engagement with Tehran that illustrates New Delhi's multi-vector foreign policy calculus. Across Southeast Asia, emergency energy conservation measures proliferated: Thailand ordered civil servants to work from home full-time, the Philippines shifted to a four-day government work week, Vietnam faces potential airfare increases of up to 70% as jet fuel costs tripled, and Philippine President Marcos framed his country's position plainly as "victims of a war we didn't choose nor want." Indian carriers hiked long-haul ticket prices 15%, and Fitch Ratings identified India and Pakistan as particularly exposed to deteriorating credit metrics from sustained high oil prices. ---

Europe

  • ECB rate expectations shifted materially across the week, with swaps moving from pricing one 25 basis point hike in 2026 to two by week's end, and the first fully priced by June; the Bank of England shifted from pricing cuts to a 70% probability of a hike by year-end.
  • UK GDP was flat in January against consensus expectations of 0.2% growth — weaker than every analyst forecast — a pre-conflict data point that suggests the economy was already losing momentum before the current energy shock.
  • German bund futures slid to an almost 15-year low as overlapping pressures — rising yields, elevated energy costs, weak industrial data, and war financing concerns — weighed on Europe's benchmark sovereign market.
  • CDU lost the Baden-Württemberg regional election to the Greens, a state it had been projected to reclaim after 15 years of Green governance, compounding Chancellor Merz's domestic political difficulties.
  • European credit markets registered significant stress, with the iTraxx Crossover index crossing 300 basis points for the first time since June and nearly 150 European companies already having ceded control to direct-lending funds.

Monetary Policy Repricing

The ECB entered the week facing a genuinely uncomfortable policy configuration: an energy-driven inflation shock arriving atop a growth backdrop that was already fragile. By Monday, interest rate markets implied two full 25 basis point hikes in 2026, compared with one priced the prior Friday, with the first fully priced by June — a remarkable shift in a matter of days. ECB Governing Council member Peter Kazimir stated explicitly that the war risks forcing the bank to raise rates sooner than anticipated; colleague François Villeroy de Galhau acknowledged next week's meeting was not the moment for action but signaled the ECB would not permit inflation to become entrenched. ECB President Lagarde separately pledged the bank would act to prevent the conflict from inflicting the same inflationary pain as Russia's 2022 invasion — language that closes off accommodation without committing to tightening, a delicate balance. By week's end the ECB was expected to hold rates unchanged through 2027 in some analyst frameworks, reflecting the growth headwind balancing the inflation risk. For the Bank of England, money markets shifted from pricing cuts to a 70% probability of a hike by year-end, with ING and RSM UK estimating UK inflation could rise to more than double the 2% target if current energy price levels persist. The BoE and ECB face a symmetric dilemma: easing into an energy shock risks credibility loss, while tightening risks amplifying a slowdown.

Growth and Industrial Backdrop

UK GDP data released Friday delivered a downside surprise, with January output flat against a 0.2% consensus expectation — weaker than every forecast in the analyst survey. The figures predate meaningful impact from the Middle East conflict, suggesting the UK economy was losing momentum independently. Germany's industrial data at the start of the week compounded the difficult backdrop, with both industrial production and factory orders declining at the start of the year, tempering recovery expectations. Germany's chemical industry faces a structural crisis that predates the conflict — higher post-Ukraine energy costs, rising carbon prices, new Chinese capacity, and weakening demand have already forced plant closures — but elevated energy prices intensify the pressure materially. European chemical producers face an estimated €3 billion annually in additional natural gas costs if the post-war price surge persists. The Leuna chemical park in eastern Germany has become a visible test case of this dynamic. The VCI chemical industry association described "a spiral spinning in the wrong direction," with raw material shortages compounding direct energy cost effects. German bund futures slid to an almost 15-year low across the week, and Scope Ratings identified Italian fiscal credibility as at risk from a prolonged conflict.

Politics and Credit Markets

Chancellor Merz suffered a notable political setback as CDU lost the Baden-Württemberg regional election to the ruling Greens — a state CDU had been projected to reclaim after 15 years — reflecting broader voter unease with the governing coalition's performance. The iTraxx Europe investment-grade index reached its highest level since May, while the Crossover index crossed 300 basis points for the first time since June. Goldman Sachs analysis showed nearly 150 European companies have already ceded control to direct-lending funds, a measure of how private credit stress is materializing. Primary bond issuance paused as borrowers stood down from planned debt sales during the most acute volatility. The euro fell to its lowest level since August against the dollar by Friday, reflecting the continent's acute energy import exposure. EU Commission President von der Leyen described nuclear energy abandonment as "a strategic mistake," a pointed reference to Germany's reactor closures as energy security anxiety intensifies. The EU separately warned that inflation could exceed 3% this year if oil remains near $100 and gas stays elevated. In France, Marine Le Pen's effort to broaden National Rally's appeal to corporate and establishment elites is generating internal friction with the party's working-class base — a tension bearing monitoring given her proximity to the presidency in current polling. UK consumer sentiment fell to a four-month low in March per Barclays data, while Rheinmetall declined 4.5% after guiding below analyst sales expectations for 2026. The Swiss franc continued appreciating as a safe-haven destination, placing the SNB in a difficult position where analysts suggest negative interest rates may be revisited. ---

Global

  • Brent crude surged intraday to $119.50 on Monday — the largest intraday percentage move since April 2020 — before partially retracing; oil held above $100 per barrel for much of the week after further Iranian escalation on Thursday.
  • The IEA announced an emergency release of 400 million barrels from member-country stockpiles — the largest in the agency's history — providing temporary relief but covering an estimated 25 days of disrupted supply at the high end of disruption estimates.
  • The traditional 60/40 portfolio hedge broke down, with government bond yields rising more than 25 basis points across the US, UK, Germany, Canada, and Australia since hostilities began as bonds and equities moved in tandem on stagflation concerns.
  • Russia is providing Iran with satellite imagery and drone-targeting intelligence according to multiple sources, while simultaneously benefiting from the near-elimination of the Urals discount to Brent — a fiscal windfall comparable to 2022 energy revenues.
  • The global aviation network absorbed one of its largest shocks since the pandemic, with over 46,000 flights canceled and economy-class Sydney-to-London round-trip fares rising more than 80%.

The Oil Shock: Scale and Mechanism

The Strait of Hormuz remains effectively at a near-standstill, with the IEA estimating the closure has cut the corridor's usual 20 million barrels per day of throughput by approximately 90%, forcing Gulf producers to shut in around 10 million barrels per day of output. Saudi Arabia, Iraq, the UAE, and Kuwait have each curtailed production as closed storage fills; QatarEnergy delayed a major LNG expansion to at least 2027 following drone attacks on the Ras Laffan facility, which produces roughly 20% of global LNG supply, driving European and Asian gas futures to their highest levels since early 2023. Citigroup estimates the effective supply removal at approximately 16 million barrels per day of crude and refined products — roughly 16% of world supply — a disruption that materially exceeds the 2022 Russian shock. The IEA's record 400-million-barrel coordinated release, along with Japan's separate unilateral release of approximately 80 million barrels beginning as early as March 16, provided temporary relief but the pace and logistics of deployment remain uncertain. Six-month Brent futures were pricing approximately $80 per barrel at week's end, embedding a meaningful probability of resolution that the military and diplomatic evidence does not yet fully support. Beyond crude, European natural gas futures jumped as much as 30% on Monday alone, aluminum briefly hit a near four-year high, and coal prices reached their highest level in over a year as gas substitution effects propagated through power markets.

Geopolitical Architecture

Iran's new supreme leader Mojtaba Khamenei — appointed Monday by the Assembly of Experts, with deep IRGC ties — explicitly vowed to keep Hormuz effectively closed and threatened additional fronts if US-Israeli strikes continue. Iran's ceasefire conditions — a permanent pledge from both the US and Israel never to strike again — appear incompatible with either government's stated positions, leaving the diplomatic path narrow. Prediction markets moved across the week from a 48% to a 52% probability that the conflict extends beyond April 30. Russia's posture adds strategic complexity: Putin has maintained a communication channel with Trump while reportedly providing Iran with satellite imagery and drone-targeting intelligence, and Moscow is considering ending most gas sales to the EU. The Urals discount to Brent has nearly disappeared, delivering a fiscal windfall that reduces pressure on Russia's war budget while US political attention is diverted from Ukraine. UK naval forces reported three vessels struck in the Strait of Hormuz and the Persian Gulf on Wednesday; Dubai International Airport briefly halted operations following drone strikes that injured four people; Iraq halted terminal operations and Oman evacuated ships from one of the last viable regional export ports on Thursday. BCA Research raises the uncomfortable possibility that even a ceasefire may not reopen Hormuz, as factions beyond Tehran's control could sustain drone harassment of shipping indefinitely.

Secondary Effects: Food, Supply Chains, and the GCC

The war's secondary consequences are accumulating across multiple systems. Iran accounts for approximately 5% and 11% of global urea and ammonia trade respectively; urea prices rose 25% since hostilities began, reaching levels last seen during Russia's Ukraine invasion, with crop prices rising in parallel and a global food inflation impulse expected to materialize with a lag. The conflict is disrupting approximately one-fifth of global air freight and compressing Asia-Europe container traffic. GCC economies face what may prove a structural setback to their diversification ambitions: Iranian strikes have targeted AWS facilities in the UAE and Bahrain, exposing physical infrastructure assumptions that underpinned Gulf-US technology partnerships, and the energy revenue curtailment from Hormuz interdiction reduces the available pool for outward investment commitments made to the Trump administration. Environmental consequences are also emerging: Israeli drone strikes on refineries near Tehran on March 8 triggered fires releasing toxic particulates that mixed with rainfall to produce contaminated black rain over a metropolitan area of approximately 18.5 million people, with analysts identifying over 300 incidents carrying environmental risk from the conflict to date. The IMF has urged policymakers to prepare for a prolonged conflict scenario. Societe Generale and Deutsche Bank identify the shock's persistence and central bank reaction functions — particularly the Fed's — as the two decisive variables for the global macro trajectory, a question unlikely to be resolved before next week's FOMC meeting.
