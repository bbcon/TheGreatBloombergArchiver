Tuesday, June 30, 2026

US

  • Supreme Court ruled 5-4 that the president cannot fire Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook without due process, reinforcing Fed independence; separately ruled presidents can fire officials at other independent agencies at will.
  • S&P 500 set to record its best quarterly gain in six years; futures steady at 7,507 as of early trading.
  • Strategy Inc. unveiled a new financing framework allowing Bitcoin sales of up to $1.25 billion, signaling a shift away from unconditional BTC accumulation.
  • Global deal value on track to exceed $4 trillion in 2026, the most since 2021, supported by SpaceX's record $86 billion IPO and AI-related transactions.

Fed Independence

The Supreme Court's 5-4 ruling that President Trump cannot remove Federal Reserve Governor Lisa Cook without due process represents a significant, if largely anticipated, affirmation of central bank independence. Markets barely reacted, for several reasons: the outcome had been telegraphed in oral arguments, inflation and Iran war fallout have rendered near-term rate cuts implausible regardless of FOMC composition, and the window to install a pro-administration majority on the rate-setting committee has effectively closed. The more consequential element of the ruling may be the companion decision granting the president broad authority to dismiss officials at the SEC, CFTC, and other independent agencies — a development with direct implications for crypto regulation and financial oversight.

Markets and Deals

US equities are closing out the strongest quarter in six years, with S&P 500 futures at 7,507. Deal activity has proven more resilient than feared amid the Iran conflict: PwC estimates full-year global deal value will exceed $4 trillion, with AI transactions and mega-IPOs — including SpaceX's record $86 billion offering — providing much of the impetus. IPO volumes remain well below the euphoric 2021 peak of 250 deals, which Goldman's Ben Snider argues limits bubble comparisons, though valuations remain elevated by historical standards. Strategy Inc.'s decision to allow Bitcoin sales introduces uncertainty into crypto markets by removing a key source of structural demand.

Asia

  • China's official manufacturing PMI rose to 50.3 in June, beating the Bloomberg consensus estimate, marking four consecutive months of expansion — the longest such streak since early 2022.
  • The yen breached 161.95 per dollar, its weakest level since Christmas Eve 1986, despite a record ¥11.73 trillion intervention between April and May that failed to reverse the trajectory.
  • South Korea unveiled an $880 billion investment plan in chip manufacturing and AI infrastructure; Japan is pursuing a $2.3 trillion 14-year blueprint targeting AI, semiconductors, and defense.

China Manufacturing

China's June PMI beat underscores the resilience of its export-oriented industrial base through the Iran war period. Booming exports have provided a sustained buffer, and the recent decline in oil prices — as Strait of Hormuz traffic partially resumes — should reduce input costs for manufacturers in coming months. However, a separate report noted that Chinese equities are trailing global peers by the widest margin since 2001, as domestic investors remain underexposed to the global AI boom. Tencent has responded by ramping up share buybacks after losing approximately $309 billion in market value since October.

Yen and Intervention

The yen's slide to four-decade lows reflects persistent interest-rate differentials: even after the Bank of Japan's most recent hike — its highest benchmark rate in 31 years — rates remain deeply negative in real terms relative to US and European peers. Japan's Finance Minister offered measured verbal warnings but stopped short of "bold action" language, suggesting intervention may not be imminent. The combination of rising Japanese government bond yields and a weakening currency is unusual and may signal deteriorating confidence in Japan's fiscal trajectory. HSBC warns the dollar rally could turn "explosive" in H2 if the Fed signals additional tightening.

Europe

  • Spain's June CPI came in at 3.6%, France at 2.0%, and Italy at 3.1% — the latter below consensus — ahead of the eurozone-wide flash estimate of 3.0% expected Wednesday.
  • The ECB raised rates this month, leading G7 peers in responding to the post-Iran energy price shock; ECB President Lagarde opened the Sintra symposium signaling Europe's economy can withstand further tightening if needed.
  • The UK's defense investment plan details £5 billion in drone spending as outgoing Prime Minister Starmer prepares Britain's military for potential conflict by 2030.

ECB Policy

The contrast with the ECB's 2022 Sintra moment is notable. Four years ago, with rates still negative and Spanish inflation approaching 10%, policymakers were plainly behind the curve. Today, having led G7 central banks with a rate hike following the Iran war's energy shock, the ECB convenes at Sintra from a position of relative confidence. Inflation, while above target, is smaller in magnitude than the 2022 shock, and the economy appears able to absorb tightening. Chief Economist Philip Lane declined to pre-commit to further moves, emphasizing a meeting-by-meeting approach as policymakers assess how four months of elevated energy costs are still feeding through to prices. Belgian governor Wunsch — typically hawkish — acknowledged another hike is possible but less urgent than markets had previously priced.

Spain Solar Oversupply

Spain's solar sector illustrates the investment risk inherent in rapid renewable buildout. Overcapacity has driven wholesale prices deeply negative during peak hours, with 2026 already surpassing the annual record for negative-price hours. Asset values are falling sharply, several projects are for sale at steep discounts, and short sellers have opened positions in listed solar names. For consumers, the oversupply has delivered some of Europe's lowest power prices — roughly half German levels — but the episode highlights grid management challenges that are also causing the UK to likely miss its 2030 clean electricity target by five years.

LatAm

  • Venezuela's earthquake death toll surpassed 1,700 following twin tremors last week, with a subsequent 5.1-magnitude aftershock complicating search-and-rescue operations.
  • Keiko Fujimori narrowly won Peru's presidential election, becoming the country's first female president; her platform combined iron-fist security policies with market-friendly economic plans.
  • Latin America's battery-storage market is entering a new phase, with annual additions forecast to exceed 5 GWh by 2028 from 817 MWh in 2024; Chile leads, with Brazil and Mexico expanding.

Peru Political Transition

Fujimori's victory in her fourth presidential bid adds Peru to a regional pattern of electorates gravitating toward security-focused, economically orthodox candidates. For copper markets — Peru is among the world's largest producers — her market-friendly orientation is broadly constructive, though her track record in office and ongoing legal challenges merit monitoring. The result follows similar right-of-center swings in other Latin American elections and reflects voter prioritization of security over redistributive economics.

Venezuela Crisis

The humanitarian situation in Venezuela has deteriorated sharply following last week's earthquakes. With a death toll exceeding 1,700 and ongoing aftershocks hampering relief efforts, the disaster compounds pre-existing economic and political fragility. The broader regional macro picture remains shaped by commodity dynamics: Latin America's energy storage buildout is accelerating, driven by Chile's solar boom, with Brazil and Mexico increasingly contributing to capacity expansion — a structural shift with longer-term implications for the region's power economics.

Global

  • Strait of Hormuz traffic resumed partially for the first time since Iran's recent ship attacks, with approximately 24 commodity vessels transiting Monday; Iran simultaneously reiterated its intent to control maritime passage, raising stakes ahead of Qatar peace talks.
  • Morgan Stanley cut oil price forecasts for the second time in two weeks as Hormuz flows recover; WTI near $70.97, on course for its largest quarterly decline since the pandemic.
  • The EU and China set an October deadline to resolve trade disagreements, as subsidized Chinese goods continue to flood European markets and the bilateral trade deficit widens.

Oil and the Strait of Hormuz

Oil markets are pricing a meaningful easing of supply disruption risk as tanker transits through the Strait of Hormuz gradually resume. WTI at $70.97 reflects a combination of recovering Gulf flows, strong US supply, and weak Chinese demand — a configuration that Morgan Stanley warns could produce a supply glut. However, Iran's simultaneous assertion of sovereign control over the waterway introduces considerable uncertainty: the ceasefire remains fragile, peace talks in Qatar have yet to produce a formal settlement, and any re-escalation could rapidly reverse the recent easing in energy prices. The ECB has explicitly flagged residual pipeline inflation from four months of elevated energy costs.

Global Monetary Policy and Trade

Global monetary policy divergence is narrowing at the margin. The ECB has hiked; the Fed under Chair Kevin Warsh has adopted a hawkish stance; and the Bank of Japan has raised rates to a 31-year high — though not fast enough to arrest yen weakness. The USMCA faces its first renewal deadline on July 1, likely to be missed, with President Trump's stated preference to exit the agreement representing a tail risk to North American supply chains. Meanwhile, a US Commerce Department report on copper tariffs was due Tuesday, with a potential 15% levy on refined copper in January 2027 creating fresh uncertainty for industrial metals markets.
