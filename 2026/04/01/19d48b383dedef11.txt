Asian Factories Show Mixed Activity Amid Strains From Fuel Price Shock

The first full month of Asian factory data since the Iran war erupted showed a 
mixed picture of the economic fallout.

Manufacturing across much of Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/asia-manufacturing-holds-up-but-cracks-show-as-war-lifts-prices?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 expanded in March, with activity improving in South Korea, Malaysia and 
Thailand, according to S&P Global data published Wednesday. The conflict 
appeared to weigh on activity in Vietnam, Indonesia and semiconductor hub 
Taiwan.

The March reports reflected the first full month of manufacturing PMI figures 
since the war on Iran began on Feb. 28 and subsequently escalated, disrupting 
shipping through the Strait of Hormuz, a key oil transit route.

US President Donald Trump said Tuesday he foresaw ending the conflict 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/trump-talks-iran-war-end-even-as-attacks-continue-across-region?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 within two to three weeks.

The supply shock unleashed over the past month pushed oil prices above $100 a 
barrel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/brent-oil-falls-below-100-on-optimism-iran-war-may-end?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
, raising the risk of renewed inflationary pressures. That’s especially the 
case across Asia-Pacific, where many economies are highly dependent on Mideast 
oil, gasoline, diesel, jet fuel, chemicals and fertilizers.

Mixed Results

In China, factory activity slowed last month for export-oriented firms as 
their costs surged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/china-factory-activity-gauge-slows-as-iran-war-threatens-exports?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
, according to a private survey, contrasting with an official gauge that showed 
manufacturing improving despite the war.

Australia’s manufacturing sector posted its first contraction 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-31/australia-manufacturing-slips-to-contraction-on-war-disruptions?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 in five months as demand weakened and cost pressures surged.

But South Korea’s exports continued to surge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/s-korea-exports-continue-to-surge-despite-escalating-iran-risks?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
, as record semiconductor shipments driven by the artificial intelligence boom 
and demand from China helped cushion the economy even as external risks 
intensify.

Japan’s large manufacturers showed greater confidence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/japan-s-manufacturer-mood-improves-supporting-boj-s-rate-stance?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 in the state of business conditions for a fourth straight quarter even as the 
Middle East crisis erupted, the BOJ’s Tankan business survey showed Wednesday.

Meanwhile in Europe, UK factories are enduring the most intense strain 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-01/uk-factories-face-worst-supply-chain-stress-since-2022-pmi-says?cmpid=BBD040126_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260401>
 on supply chains and steepest price pressures since the aftermath of Russia’s 
invasion of Ukraine, according to a closely watched survey.
