Distortions are becoming more extreme in financial markets. That’s resulting 
in rising efforts to guard actions that could worsen such contortions — in Asia 
at least.

Take South Korea, where the $5 trillion market has been trading with almost 
the volatility of a penny stock. Global banks are said to belimiting hedge 
funds’ 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-12/banks-curb-hedge-fund-bets-on-sk-hynix-samsung-after-wild-moves?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 leveraged bets on chipmakers such as SK Hynix and Samsung due to concern over 
what happens if there is a sustained pullback. The two stocks,winners 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-02/s-korea-surpasses-india-as-world-s-sixth-largest-stock-market?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 of the AI boom, are worth a combined $2.25 trillion. That is almost six times 
their value of just 12 months ago.

Or look at China, where the weak economy has stifled demand for loans, 
resulting in a liquidity glut that dragged down borrowing costs. Officials are 
said to have told state-owned banksto reduce lending 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-12/china-asks-big-banks-to-curb-interbank-lending-to-ease-cash-glut?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 to other banks, essentially to drive the interbank rate up. Loose financial 
conditions risk fanning bubbles in assets such as government bonds, especially 
at a time whenprice wars 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-11/alibaba-jd-com-slide-after-beijing-slams-price-cut-promotions?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 are undercutting corporate profits and the government iscracking down 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/china-targets-billions-in-offshore-stock-trading-in-biggest-crackdown-in-decades?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 on capital outflows.

Lets not forget about Indonesia, which has seen its stock market and currency 
take a hammering this year. The central bank is said to havetightened control 
of banks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-12/indonesia-tightens-wall-street-banks-fx-trading-to-boost-rupiah?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
’ foreign-exchange dealings to ensure trading is commercial related, rather 
than speculative. Additionally, two funds that hold Indonesian bonds are 
delaying redemptions as market liquidity deteriorates. Bank Indonesia this week 
conducted a surpriseinterest-rate increase 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/indonesia-may-hike-rate-again-to-shield-currency-analysts-say?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 to shore up the currency, the worst performer in Asia this year.

Of course, such actions can only do so much while the conditions that create 
such distortions prevail. With the AI boom in particular warping markets, 
expect more unusual behavior by both investors and authorities.

India is preparing for a wider-than-expected budget deficit this year, putting 
it on course to miss its targetfor the first time 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-12/india-willing-to-let-fiscal-gap-widen-to-as-much-as-4-8-of-gdp?cmpid=eveapac&utm_campaign=eveapac&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260612&utm_content=5474>
 since the pandemic as the Iran war drives up energy subsidy costs and 
pressures government finances.

Authorities are willing to let the deficit widen by as much as half a 
percentage point to 4.8% of gross domestic product compared with the 4.3% goal 
set in February, said an official familiar with the matter. India imports more 
than 80% of its crude and higher energy prices have boosted the government’s 
fuel and fertilizer subsidy bills.
