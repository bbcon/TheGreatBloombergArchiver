Asian equity markets closed March under significant pressure. The region's benchmark indices fell roughly 13% over the month — the steepest decline since October 2008 — as surging oil prices, supply-chain disruptions, and deteriorating risk appetite weighed broadly. South Korea, which had been the world's best-performing market entering the year on AI optimism, is now among the hardest hit, having approached bear market territory. Seoul announced a supplementary budget to cushion households and businesses from the oil shock, while the incoming Bank of Korea governor nominee played down concerns over currency weakness.

China offered a more nuanced picture. Factory activity returned to expansion in March for the first time this year, supported by ramped-up fiscal spending and continued demand linked to artificial intelligence infrastructure. However, input costs surged sharply, reflecting the upstream pass-through of higher energy prices — a tangible sign that the war's economic spillover is reaching Chinese manufacturing. Meanwhile, Chinese state oil and gas majors are reportedly moderating aggressive expansion plans amid market turbulence, balancing energy security objectives against near-term uncertainty. Separately, Chinese officials are intensifying scrutiny of offshore trusts holding shares in Hong Kong-listed companies, targeting wealth structures used by the country's ultra-rich to invest overseas.

In Southeast Asia, Vietnam is emerging as a structural beneficiary of trade diversion away from China. Bloomberg's shipment-level customs data shows the country surpassed China as the leading US supplier of laptops and gaming consoles last year for the first time — though Vietnam's exposure to the very tariff policies driving that shift represents a meaningful vulnerability. In Australia, the online safety regulator launched investigations into Meta, Snap, TikTok, and YouTube over alleged failures to enforce the country's under-16 social media ban, with fines of up to A$49.5 million possible.

India faces mounting pressure from soaring crude import costs, with the current account deficit widening in ways that complicate the Reserve Bank of India's efforts to stabilize the rupee.
