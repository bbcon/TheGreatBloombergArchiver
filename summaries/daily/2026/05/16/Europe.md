The EU's industrial policy ambitions are coming into direct conflict with Beijing's regulatory counter-offensive. China's new supply chain coercion framework appears calibrated in part as a warning to Brussels, which has been advancing the Industrial Accelerator Act — the so-called "Made in Europe" proposal — that would mandate preference for domestic suppliers in public procurement and target 20% of GDP from manufacturing by 2035. Separately, Chinese authorities warned the EU this week that countermeasures would follow if Brussels proceeds with its probe into Nuctech, the Chinese security-devices manufacturer whose state subsidies the European Commission is investigating for providing an unfair competitive advantage over local firms. The pairing of these moves suggests Beijing is prepared to use both regulatory and diplomatic levers to resist European supply chain reorientation.

The broader structural concern for Europe — particularly for manufacturing-intensive economies such as Germany — is that China's industrial dominance is no longer merely a competitive challenge but an actively managed strategic position. Rhodium Group's analysis highlights specific vulnerabilities for German automotive communities, Italian machinery districts, and Japanese electronics cities, where prolonged margin compression and revenue erosion may prove difficult to reverse without coordinated policy responses across advanced economies. Whether the EU can maintain the political cohesion required for such coordination, given member-state divergence on China exposure, remains an open question.

Turkey's decision to scrap its inflation target in the face of an energy-price shock points to additional stress within emerging European economies, though details remain limited from available coverage.
