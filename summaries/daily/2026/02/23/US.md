The dominant US macro development of the day is the aftermath of the Supreme Court's ruling invalidating President Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs, and the administration's rapid pivot to replacement levies. Trump signed an executive proclamation imposing a 10% global tariff under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974, subsequently raising it to 15% over the weekend. The legal foundation appears fragile: Section 122 permits duties only in response to "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold most economists do not believe the US currently meets. The IMF's former chief economist Gita Gopinath stated plainly that the US does not face such a problem, and former Treasury official Jay Shambaugh noted that a genuine balance-of-payments crisis would be reflected in dollar weakness and capital flight — neither of which is occurring. Legal challenges appear likely, though courts may move too slowly to constrain tariffs that expire in 150 days.

S&P 500 futures fell approximately 0.3% on the day, with market participants pricing in continued policy uncertainty rather than relief from the court ruling. Treasury Secretary Bessent characterized the IEEPA tool as "instantaneous" leverage now lost, while flagging what he termed a "draconian alternative" — the option of complete trade embargoes. Analyst commentary emphasizes that Trump retains an array of coercive instruments beyond formal tariff authority, including sector-specific duties, regulatory threats, and financial access.

A major winter storm named Hernando has paralyzed the US Northeast, with 41 million people facing blizzard conditions. More than 10,000 flights were canceled through Tuesday, with Delta suspending operations at LaGuardia, JFK, and Boston Logan. US natural gas futures jumped nearly 7% as heating demand surged. The storm is among the most severe on record for the New York area and will likely weigh on near-term regional economic activity. Kevin Warsh, Trump's expected nominee for Federal Reserve chair, continues to attract attention; Bloomberg Economics analysis of his public statements confirms a marked shift from hawkish positioning in 2009–2011 toward dovish messaging since 2025.
