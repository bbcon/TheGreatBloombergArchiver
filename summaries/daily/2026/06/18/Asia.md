• China May retail sales fell 0.6% year-over-year, well below already-subdued consensus forecasts, marking the first contraction in consumer spending since the pandemic and raising fresh doubts about Beijing's ability to achieve its domestic demand targets.
• Bank Indonesia raised its benchmark BI-Rate 25 basis points to 5.75% — its third hike in approximately one month — while the Bangko Sentral ng Pilipinas raised its reverse repurchase rate 25 basis points to 4.75%; Taiwan's central bank held for a ninth consecutive quarter.
• The MSCI China index fell 1.5% and is now approximately 20% below its October peak, approaching bear market territory, as weak consumer data and disappointing earnings from Alibaba and Tencent weigh on sentiment.

**China: Consumer Deterioration**
The May retail sales print of -0.6% year-over-year laid bare the depth of China's consumption challenge. Sub-components were more alarming: home appliance and automobile purchases each declined by double digits, reflecting the exhaustion of government trade-in subsidies that were reduced to 250 billion yuan in 2026 from 300 billion yuan in 2025. Even catering expenditure — historically more resilient — rose at its slowest pace since late 2022. Bloomberg Economics' Gavekal Dragonomics economist Wei He cautioned that "weak labor-market conditions will continue to weigh on household income growth, and the slow-burn property price decline will sap both the balance sheets and confidence of anxious consumers." Separately, global holdings of Chinese interbank bonds rose $13.3 billion in May — the first monthly increase in over a year — suggesting Chinese fixed income retained safe-haven appeal amid the Iran war's energy shock.

**Southeast Asia and Regional Policy**
Indonesia's three consecutive rate hikes reflect the urgency with which Bank Indonesia is defending the rupiah amid global risk-off sentiment and dollar strength amplified by the hawkish Fed pivot. India's monsoon deficit — running approximately 40% below normal as of mid-June according to the India Meteorological Department — introduces an additional risk to agricultural output and rural incomes in the coming months. An impending wave of roughly 62 million barrels of crude oil trapped in the Persian Gulf is set to reach Asian markets following the Hormuz reopening, likely producing near-term disinflationary pressure for energy-importing economies across the region.
