Investment Blueprint?

As the pivotal summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-11/china-confirms-x-trump-summit-that-was-delayed-by-iran-war?cmpid=BBD051226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4365>
 looms between Trump and his Chinese counterpart Xi Jinping, the Biden 
administration’s point-person on Asia, Kurt Campbell, highlighted the unusual 
aspects of this tête-à-tête.

“Not since Richard Nixon’s historic meeting with Mao Zedong in 1972 has each 
country’s leader had so much personal authority in deciding the future of the 
relationship,” Campbellwrote 
<https://www.foreignaffairs.com/united-states/stakes-trump-vs-xi-kurt-campbell?s=ESPZZ006Y1&utm_medium=promo_email&utm_source=post&utm_campaign=PROM_MJ26%20Post%20Release%202_051126_Trump-Xi%20Faceoff&utm_content=20260511&utm_term=N&utm_id=A#>
 Monday inForeign Affairs. Campbell highlighted that “each man comes to the 
table with remarkably few institutional constraints, substantial personal 
latitude and manifest ambition” to shape US-China ties.

The consensus view, arguably, says little by way of true “deliverables” is 
likely this week beyond a potential lengthening of the 12-monthtrade-war 
ceasefire 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-30/trump-xi-truce-buys-time-as-both-seek-leverage-in-broader-fight?cmpid=BBD051226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4365>
 that was declared at the last meeting in South Korea in October. If there were 
a surprise on the economic agenda, however, it might come from some 
accommodation on bilateral investment.

Behind that wild card: Trump’s remarks in Detroit in January saying of 
hypothetical Chinese auto plants in the US, “that’s great.I love that <>. Let 
China come in.” Just west of Detroit, there’s a potential blueprint for that, 
in the town of Marshall.

Ford Motor is poised to cut the ribbon on a new plant that had a big assist 
from China’s CATL, the world’s leader in battery tech. The venture isstructured 
to allay concerns 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/gilts-slump-with-uk-prime-minister-under-pressure-to-stand-down?cmpid=BBD051226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4365>
 about the threat Chinese competitors pose to the US auto industry.

The US carmaker owns the plant, land and equipment and hires the workers. 
CATL, which holds no equity, offers lithium iron phosphate battery chemistry 
under a licensing agreement, and dispatches Chinese engineers to train Ford 
employees.
Blue Oval Battery Park in Marshall, Michigan, on April 22.Photographer: Caria 
Taylor/Bloomberg
Both sides have reason to like 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-02-18/could-china-s-manufacturers-shift-output-to-the-us-japan-did-it?cmpid=BBD051226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4365>
 this setup. Use of CATL batteries by US automakers can help them become the 
industry standard, and boost market share.

Once operational, the Ford site would use CATL technology to produce batteries 
not only for EVs but also power storage for data centers — a huge sector that 
electricity strained America needs to expand to support the rapid growth of 
artificial intelligence.

While the Washington debate over Chinese investment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-08/us-acts-against-foreign-ownership-of-farmland-amid-china-tension?cmpid=BBD051226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4365>

the bigger risk is pretending American companies can go it alone, according to

With a relative lack of advance planning and choreography for this week’s

it caused 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-says-he-s-rescheduling-xi-meeting-as-iran-war-rages-on?cmpid=BBD051226_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260512&utm_content=4365>

leaders themselves.”

All the more reason to be on watch for any investment-accommodation surprise.

Need-to-Know Research

What impact did the extraordinary influx of immigrants to the US have on 
inflation? In sum, it was essentially a wash, according to analysis by six 
economists, mainly from the Federal Reserve Bank of Dallas, in a new National 
Bureau of Economics Researchpaper <http://www.nber.org/papers/w35168>.

Total net immigration over the 2021-24 period amounted to 10.5 million, the 
economists said, using Congressional Budget Officeestimates 
<https://www.cbo.gov/system/files/2025-09/61390-demographic-update-data.xlsx> 
from last September. Economic theory says that such an event generates two 
competing effects, they noted. “It increases the labor supply, which eases cost 
pressures and pushes down inflation, but it also increases consumption demand 
and spurs investment as firms race to equip new workers.”
Source: Economists Anton Cheremukhin, Sewon Hur, Ronald R. Mau, Karel Mertens, 
Alexander W. Richter and Xiaoqing Zhou
And indeed, the evidence suggests that these two dynamics canceled each other 
out, the economists found. The migrants who arrived in the US were “primarily 
low-skilled relative to the existing workforce,” which meant less need for new 
capital investment to make use of the new workers. In the counter-factual 
scenario of a surge of high-skilled immigrants, that “generates a much larger 
increase in inflation,” their analysis found.
