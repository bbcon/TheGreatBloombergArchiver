Big Oil Is Struggling to Find the Next Guyana
All the supermajors are hunting for the next Guyana.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
senior oil reporter Kevin Crowley explores why the supermajors are frantically 
hunting for the next Guyana.

Guyana recently became the world’s top oil producer per capita, pumping almost 
1 million barrels a day — or roughly one for every resident.

“Guyana is exceptional,” Exxon Mobil Corp. Upstream President Dan Ammann 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/7137005> said at a conference in the 
capital, Georgetown, this week. “I see energy projects on every continent. Many 
are important, very few are truly exceptional.”

The problem for Big Oil is that Guyana may turn out to be too exceptional — 
perhaps not to be repeated.

The South American country’s crude 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-15/guyana-s-oil-boom-gathers-pace-with-exports-set-for-fresh-record?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
 costs less than $35 a barrel to produce, making it among the most profitable 
supply outside OPEC and amajor prize 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-06-25/exxon-plans-new-guyana-oil-project-to-boost-output-into-2030s?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
 for Exxon and its joint-venture partners, Chevron Corp. and Cnooc Ltd.

With Wall Street beginning to value the world’s largest energy companies on the
longevity of their reserves 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2023-10-28/exxon-and-chevron-megadeals-reveal-why-days-of-easy-oil-are-over?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
, all the supermajors are now hunting for the next Guyana. It’s not easy.
Exxon’s Guyana offices in Georgetown.Photographer: Bloomberg
Iraq, Libya and Venezuela have huge resources — but their political problems 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-31/exxon-chevron-cautious-on-venezuela-as-trump-urges-oil-growth?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
 are significant.

Searches for big discoveries in Suriname, which neighbors Guyana, and Namibia 
have so far failed to match initial hopes.

Exxon, Chevron, Shell Plc and others are investing in the eastern Mediterranean
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-01-14/exxon-makes-natural-gas-discovery-in-mediterranean-sea-off-egypt?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
, but the reserves there are mostly natural gas, which is typically lower value 
than oil. Even the go-go days of US shale are fading as declining rock quality 
erodes the benefit of technology improvements.

Sure, there are bright spots, such as the Vaca Muerta 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-05/harold-hamm-s-continental-expands-push-into-vaca-muerta-shale?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
 in Argentina and low-cost field extensions in the Gulf of Mexico and West 
Africa.

But none can match Guyana. It helps explain why Exxon’s stock hit a record 
earlier this month as European peers struggled, and why Chevronpaid $60 billion 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-18/hess-wins-exxon-arbitration-case-paving-way-for-chevron-deal?cmpid=BBBXT021926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260219>
 for Hess Corp. and its 30% stake in Guyana’s Stabroek Block last year.

It also perhaps explains why Shell Chief Executive Officer Wael Sawan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/profile/person/17559980> sounded 
uncharacteristically regretful on a recent conference call. The London-based 
oil giant held a 50% interest in Stabroek but exited just months before Exxon 
made its discovery in 2015.

“If I were to look back, I would say I wish we hadn’t walked away from Guyana 
when we did,” he said. “That’s the honest truth.”

—Kevin Crowley, Bloomberg News

Carbon markets have proliferated as governments turn to the mechanism to slash 
emissions and achieve climate goals. There are now about 80 operational markets 
inBloombergNEF <>’s Carbon Policies Database. Yet most have yet to reach a high 
enough price or cover sufficient emissions to drive meaningful decarbonization.
