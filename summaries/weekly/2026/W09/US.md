**Tariff Regime in Transition**
The dominant US macro development of the week was the Supreme Court's invalidation of President Trump's IEEPA-based tariffs and the administration's rapid pivot to a replacement framework under Section 122 of the Trade Act of 1974. Trump signed an executive proclamation imposing a 10% global tariff effective Tuesday at 12:01 a.m., subsequently signaling an intention to raise the rate to 15% — though the timeline remained unfinalized through the week. The legal foundation appears fragile: Section 122 requires evidence of "fundamental international payments problems," a threshold most economists, including former IMF chief economist Gita Gopinath, argued the US does not meet. More than 1,500 refund lawsuits had already been filed on approximately $170 billion in contested duties by mid-week, and Treasury Secretary Bessent framed tariff repayments as "corporate welfare." Courts may move too slowly to constrain duties that expire in 150 days, preserving policy uncertainty in the interim.

**Equity Markets and AI Disruption**
US equity markets ended February under meaningful pressure, with the S&P 500 on course for its largest monthly decline since April, down roughly 0.4% — a striking underperformance relative to European and emerging-market benchmarks. AI disruption anxiety remained the dominant sector narrative. IBM suffered its steepest single-day decline in 25 years on Monday following Anthropic's announcement that its Claude Code tool can modernize COBOL, the language central to IBM's mainframe business. Software names broadly repriced: Salesforce fell roughly 4% on Wednesday on an underwhelming outlook, C3.ai tumbled 24% on a revenue guidance cut, and Trade Desk dropped 17% on a weak forecast. Nvidia's Thursday earnings — a revenue forecast of approximately $78 billion that beat consensus — produced a muted after-hours response, reflecting an increasingly demanding bar. Partially offsetting, Dell gained 9.8% on robust AI server demand, and Block surged as much as 27% in extended trading Friday after announcing the elimination of approximately 4,000 positions framed as an AI-driven productivity bet.

**Treasury Market and Federal Reserve**
US Treasuries delivered their best monthly performance in roughly a year, with long-dated securities gaining approximately 4% in February, as a flight-to-quality bid absorbed equity and geopolitical volatility. The performance underscores the market's continued treatment of US government bonds as the primary haven asset. Fed Governor Lisa Cook and Richmond Fed President Tom Barkin both spoke at the National Association for Business Economics conference, each cautioning that AI-driven labor market reorganization may be the most significant structural shift in generations — and one that conventional rate policy cannot adequately offset. Kevin Warsh, Trump's expected Fed chair nominee, continued to attract analytical attention, with Deutsche Bank sketching potential reforms to the dot-plot framework and Bloomberg Economics confirming a marked hawkish-to-dovish shift in his public statements since 2025.

**Macro Backdrop and Storm Disruption**
The macro context entering the week was notably mixed: 2025 GDP growth was confirmed at 2.2% — the slowest since the post-COVID expansion — with core inflation ending the year at 3%, a full percentage point above target. January payrolls of 130,000 represented a meaningful rebound from the approximately 15,000 monthly average through 2025, and unemployment edged to 4.3%. A major winter storm named Hernando paralyzed the Northeast through Tuesday, canceling more than 10,000 flights, suspending Delta operations at LaGuardia, JFK, and Boston Logan, and sending US natural gas futures up nearly 7% as heating demand surged.

**Geopolitical Risk and Fiscal Exposure**
Saturday's joint US-Israel strikes on Iran dominated the weekend close, introducing a new political risk dimension for President Trump ahead of November midterm elections. Having campaigned on anti-interventionism, Trump has now presided over actions against Venezuela, Nigeria, Iran, and now a direct assault on Iranian nuclear facilities — a cumulative departure from MAGA orthodoxy that polling suggests may complicate Republicans' path to retaining Congress. The energy price channel is the most immediate domestic economic concern: oil already up nearly 20% year-to-date means further upside will translate into gasoline price pressure on households already feeling financially strained. On the corporate side, Berkshire Hathaway's PacifiCorp faces approximately $55 billion in wildfire-related claims, with its credit rating sitting one notch above junk at S&P — a complex inheritance for Greg Abel as he penned his first annual letter as Warren Buffett's successor.
