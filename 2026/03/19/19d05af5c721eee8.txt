Hawkish sentiment

For bond traders, the prospect of even one interest-rate cut this year from 
the Fed isnow essentially a coin flip 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/us-bonds-pare-losses-as-fed-keeps-outlook-for-one-2026-rate-cut?cmpid=031926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 — with the war in the Middle East and spike in oil only adding to a fraught 
debate.

On paper, policymakers still project one cut this year. Yet Powell said there 
was “meaningful” movement toward fewer cuts, and some talk about the 
possibility of the next move being a rate hike.

The hawkish sentiment is spreading:

 * Traders are fully pricing two hikes from the European Central Bank 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/traders-bet-on-two-ecb-hikes-this-year-as-energy-prices-surge?cmpid=031926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 this year on fears of an inflation spike from the war. ECB policymakers are
likely to keep rates on hold today 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/ecb-to-hold-as-it-weighs-war-s-inflation-threat-decision-guide?cmpid=031926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
.
 * The Bank of England today is set to skip a rate cut 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/iran-war-puts-bank-of-england-in-holding-pattern-decision-guide?cmpid=031926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 that markets once saw as a near certainty. Markets now see a hike as the next 
most likely change — a complete reversal from before the war.
 * The Bank of Japan also kept its policy rate unchanged 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/boj-holds-rates-steady-as-iran-conflict-clouds-inflation-outlook?cmpid=031926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
. Bond yields rose as Governor Kazuo Ueda commented on subjects including price 
risks.
Away from the bond market, the prospect of still-higher borrowing costs around 
the world is adding to investors’ angst. The S&P 500 is down 3.7% since the war 
broke out, losses are even bigger in Europe and Asia and brokers are warning it
might get worse from here

US crude lags in uneven rally

The surge in benchmark oil prices is uneven across the globe, sparking some of 
the largest gaps between US crude and the rest of the world 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/as-global-oil-prices-skyrocket-us-crude-is-lagging-behind?cmpid=031926_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 in years.

While West Texas Intermediate in the US is around $96 a barrel, the global 
Brent benchmark is at about $115 and some grades in the Middle East have topped 
$150 as the hostilities have escalated.

That’s partly a reflection of diverging supply outlooks between regions as 
conflict rages on. Oil and gas sites in Iran and around the Persian Gulf were 
targeted overnight while US production continues unabated. The US also can 
source crude from Venezuela and elsewhere in Latin America.

Traders said plans to release emergency stockpiles would add supply mainly to 
Western markets, meaning it will take time to reach where the shortage is most 
acute: Asia, where buyers are heavily reliant on Middle Eastern barrels. —Mia 
Gindis <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AWWNvsm7f7g/mia-gindis>, Yongchang 
Chin <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUJztO3yV14/yongchang-chin>, and Alex 
Longley <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASWZ4CP5myQ/alex-longley>
