European equities declined modestly, with the Stoxx Europe 600 off 0.22% as concerns about energy costs and growth weighed on sentiment. The euro fell to its lowest level since August against the dollar, reflecting the continent's acute exposure to elevated oil prices through both import costs and supply chain disruption. The European Central Bank is now expected to hold rates unchanged through 2027, a marked shift from earlier dovish expectations, as inflation risks resurface from energy.

The UK delivered a downside surprise on growth, with GDP flat in January against expectations for a 0.2% gain — weaker than every forecast in the analyst survey. The data predate any meaningful impact from the Middle East conflict, suggesting the UK economy was already losing momentum before the current energy shock. The figures add pressure on the government, which faces a deteriorating fiscal backdrop and limited room to respond.

Germany's chemical industry association VCI warned that supply chain disruption and surging energy costs are beginning to constrain output across Europe's largest economy. The VCI's director general described "a spiral spinning in the wrong direction," with raw material shortages compounding the direct effect of higher energy prices. This dynamic raises the prospect of a fresh industrial slowdown in Germany, which was only beginning to stabilize after a prolonged contraction.

In French politics, Marine Le Pen's effort to broaden National Rally's appeal to corporate and establishment elites is generating internal friction, with some party officials describing a new circle of elite advisers as alienating the working-class base that drove the party's rise. The tensions bear monitoring given Le Pen's proximity to the French presidency in current polling.
