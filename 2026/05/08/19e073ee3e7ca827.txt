Energy Scramble

The energy shocks of the 1970s forced Asian countries to pivot away from the 
Middle East as an energy supplier, with Japan and South Korea building up 
nuclear power and switching to seaborne liquefied natural gas to diversify.

Governments were heavily involved, supporting nuclear plant construction and 
investment into LNG plants in Australia, Indonesia and elsewhere.

ItтАЩs too early to say if weтАЩre going to see a similar reaction push to 
diversify, this time with investments into renewables. In the meantime, 
governments are intervening into fossil fuel markets to try and smooth over the 
disruptions.
A liquefied natural gas tanker truck travels past an LNG storage tanks in 
Himeji, Japan.Photographer: Buddhika Weerasinghe/Bloomberg

export financing program tohelp importers buy fuel 
<https://minister.dcceew.gov.au/bowen/media-releases/joint-media-release-securing-australias-future-fuel-supply-and-critical-minerals-strategic-reserve>

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-09/australia-turns-to-asia-for-fuel-security-as-us-ally-distracted?cmpid=BBD050826_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260508&utm_content=4267>

Whether this oil and gas shock prompts a permanent change in how Asia powers

reacting,pivoting

 to electric vehicles and solar panels to cope with the slump in supply and 
spike in prices, while governments are releasing fossil fuels from their 
reserves and trying to buy as much as they can.
