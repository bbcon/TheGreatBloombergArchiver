US markets returned from the Presidents' Day holiday to a broadly quiet session, with the S&P 500 gaining 0.1% and the 10-year Treasury yield adding one basis point to sit near 4.07%. Beneath the calm surface, however, a meaningful structural rotation appears to be accelerating. Bank of America's latest global fund manager survey reveals that, for the first time in a generation, a majority of money managers are concerned about corporate overinvestment rather than underinvestment — a direct consequence of the AI capital expenditure cycle. The clearest market expression of this anxiety is the divergence between hardware and software: chipmakers have surged while software companies face growing fears that AI will commoditize their core products. Nvidia rose 1.2% in premarket trading after Meta agreed to deploy millions of its processors, while Palo Alto Networks fell 7.5% on a weaker-than-expected earnings outlook. Apple, conspicuously absent from the AI spending bonanza, saw its 40-day correlation with the Nasdaq 100 fall to around 0.2 — its lowest since 2006 — effectively functioning as a haven within the tech sector.

The macro backdrop carries an internal tension worth flagging explicitly. The economy appears firm, fiscal stimulus from last year's One Big Beautiful Bill is filtering through to consumers — average tax refunds are running roughly $200 above last year's pace — and capex spending is providing an additional impulse. Yet markets are simultaneously pricing two Fed rate cuts within a year as near-certainties, with roughly 50/50 odds on a third. Fund managers surveyed by BofA now believe a "no landing" scenario — in which the economy runs hot, inflation re-accelerates, and the Fed is eventually forced into a sharp response — is more likely than either a soft or hard landing. Bond yields are broadly expected to rise, and investors appear to be positioning accordingly: rotating toward non-US equities, value stocks, and away from the Magnificent Seven. US corporate bond daily trading volume hit a record $61 billion in January, reflecting the intensity of repositioning in credit markets as well.
