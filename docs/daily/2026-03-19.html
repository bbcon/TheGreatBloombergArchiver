Daily Brief

Thursday, March 19, 2026

US

The Federal Reserve's March meeting delivered no policy change — the FOMC voted 11-1 to hold the benchmark rate at 3.5%-3.75% — but the press conference that followed generated meaningful market volatility. Chair Jerome Powell made clear that rate cuts remain contingent on demonstrated progress on inflation, a bar that appears increasingly distant given both the persistence of tariff-driven price pressures and the rapidly escalating Middle East energy shock. The Fed's preferred core inflation gauge ran at an estimated 3% in February, a full percentage point above target. February producer price data reinforced the concern: core PPI rose to a three-year high, with goods prices continuing to climb since Liberation Day. As Carl Weinberg of High Frequency Economics noted, the pipeline was already accelerating before the Strait of Hormuz blockade. The signal from futures markets is stark. Expectations have shifted from three cuts over the next 18 months to one, and that single cut is now essentially a coin flip. Powell acknowledged "meaningful" internal movement toward fewer cuts, and explicitly noted that colleagues had raised the possibility that the next move could be a hike. Bond yields rose and equities sold off as he spoke, with the S&P 500 recording its largest Fed-day decline since 2024. Powell's remarks on his own tenure compounded market uncertainty. He confirmed that if Kevin Warsh is not confirmed before his term expires in May, he will remain as chair pro tempore. More significantly, he stated he will not vacate his board seat — which runs to January 2028 — until the Justice Department investigation into the Fed's building renovation project is "well and truly over." The succession is now a prolonged open question, with Senator Thom Tillis blocking Warsh's confirmation vote until the probe concludes and prosecutor Jeanine Pirro having signaled she will not abandon the inquiry. Markets appear increasingly uncomfortable with the prospect of a protracted, politically charged transition at the central bank.

Asia

Asian equity markets declined sharply on Thursday, with the Nikkei 225 falling 3.4% as the surge in oil prices and tightening global monetary policy expectations weighed on regional sentiment. The Bank of Japan kept its policy rate unchanged at its March meeting, with Governor Kazuo Ueda citing uncertainty surrounding the Middle East conflict. The BOJ maintained its conditional tightening bias, pledging to raise borrowing costs if its inflation outlook materializes, but the conflict has materially clouded the timing of that adjustment. The most acute near-term pressure in Asia is on energy-importing economies. Asian buyers are heavily reliant on Persian Gulf crude, and the near-complete closure of the Strait of Hormuz since late February has forced a significant reorientation of supply chains. Asian purchases of US crude have surged to approximately 60 million barrels for April loading — the highest single-month volume since April 2023 — as buyers scramble for alternatives. Emergency stockpile releases are expected to benefit Western markets first, meaning the supply shortage remains most acute in Asia. The Philippine peso fell past 60 per dollar as energy costs weighed on the country's outlook. Thailand's parliament reconfirmed Anutin Charnvirakul as prime minister, providing some political stability as the country navigates the economic fallout. China stands as a notable exception to the regional distress. Jefferies' Christopher Wood argued that Chinese mainland equities are the most defensible in the current environment, citing the country's relative energy insulation — comprising robust domestic renewable capacity, battery storage, and reserve buffers — as well as potential support from state funds and easing factory deflation. The AI dimension also skews positively for China: OpenClaw, an open-source agentic AI platform generating significant excitement, has integrated with Chinese platforms, with two recently listed Hong Kong companies surging 19% on its momentum. Beijing's Five-Year Plan emphasis on low-cost, open-source AI deployment aligns structurally with this development, though Bloomberg Intelligence notes that high computational costs associated with agentic functions may compress API margins at firms like Alibaba, SenseTime, and Baidu. Samsung separately announced a record ¥110 trillion capital expenditure plan targeting AI semiconductor leadership.

Europe

European markets bore significant pressure on Thursday, with the Stoxx Europe 600 falling 2.0% as the combination of surging energy costs and a hawkish repricing of central bank expectations weighed on equities. European natural gas futures jumped as much as 35%, pushing benchmark prices to more than double their pre-war levels following Iranian missile strikes on Qatar's Ras Laffan complex. The energy shock is feeding directly into inflation expectations across the region. The ECB held its key rate at 2.0% at its March meeting, in line with consensus expectations — the rate has been at that level since last June. The policy decision itself was largely mechanical given the circumstances, but the forward guidance has shifted materially. Traders are now fully pricing two ECB hikes this year, a dramatic reversal from the easing trajectory markets anticipated before the conflict began. The Bank of England also held rates, with markets having abandoned prior near-certain expectations of a cut and now pricing a hike as the most likely next move. Sweden's Riksbank similarly stood pat. The direction of travel across European central banks is now unmistakably toward tighter policy, a consequence of an energy shock whose inflationary pass-through the region has historically absorbed more acutely than the US. In the UK, the labour market showed some resilience ahead of the BOE decision, with unemployment steady and employment modestly higher. The UK government separately announced higher tariffs on steel imports and reduced import quotas, framing the measure as support for the domestic industry amid global competition — a decision with some political resonance given the broader trade environment. UK Prime Minister Keir Starmer faces mounting pressure from Labour's left flank ahead of anticipated losses in May's local elections, with Nigel Farage's Reform UK continuing to lead in national polls. HSBC's announcement that it is weighing cuts to approximately 20,000 roles as part of an AI-driven restructuring of its middle and back offices is among the first concrete signals of how the technology may reshape large financial institution workforces.

Global

The most consequential development across global markets on March 19 is the escalation of direct attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities — and Iran responded by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City, home to the world's largest LNG export facility, causing "extensive damage" and sparking fires. Additional strikes hit a gas facility in Abu Dhabi, set ablaze two Kuwaiti oil refineries, and caused damage near a Saudi refinery. Tehran explicitly designated energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE as "legitimate targets." Brent crude rose above $118 per barrel on the day, a gain of approximately 63% since the conflict began in late February. Three-month Brent futures have returned to $100, signaling the market is pricing a more prolonged disruption. President Trump sought to contain the escalation, stating the US was not involved in the South Pars strike and warning Iran that further attacks on Qatari LNG would prompt the US to "massively blow up" the entire South Pars field. He simultaneously waived the Jones Act for 60 days, allowing foreign-flagged vessels to transport commodities between US ports — a logistical measure to ease domestic energy distribution. The gap between WTI, trading near $96, and Brent at $115, and Middle Eastern grades above $150, reflects a fragmented supply landscape in which the shortage is most acute for Asian importers dependent on Gulf barrels. The LNG disruption carries particularly broad consequences. Ras Laffan supplied roughly a fifth of global LNG before the war; a restart now expected to take months rather than weeks is pressuring gas prices globally, with downstream effects on fertilizer production, power generation, and semiconductor manufacturing costs. Many African economies are reportedly running on weeks of refined fuel. The Strait of Hormuz remains near-completely closed, with approximately 40,000 seafarers stranded on either side — trapped between drone attacks and electronic warfare. Saudi Arabia has reportedly been able to reroute some oil exports to above half of normal levels, suggesting contingency infrastructure is partially functional, but the broader disruption to global energy flows remains severe and its duration uncertain.
