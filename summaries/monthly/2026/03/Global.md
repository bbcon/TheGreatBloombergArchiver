**The US-Iran Conflict: Scope and Trajectory**

The defining event of March 2026 — and the organizing variable for virtually every global asset class by month-end — was the US-Israeli military campaign against Iran's nuclear infrastructure, which commenced over the final weekend of February. The conflict escalated rapidly and non-linearly: within the first week, satellite imagery had confirmed active Iranian efforts to conceal and harden nuclear sites, a NATO AWACS aircraft in Turkey shifted operational focus from Russia to Iran, and Trump's overnight video address acknowledged the possibility of US casualties. The killing of Ayatollah Ali Khamenei in a US-Israeli strike in the second week — the most direct US targeting of a foreign head of state in the modern era — triggered a succession to his son Mojtaba Khamenei, with deep IRGC ties, who vowed to keep Hormuz effectively closed. By the third week, the conflict had expanded geographically: Iranian missiles and drones struck Saudi Arabia, Kuwait, Bahrain, and UAE infrastructure; Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field; a US submarine sank the Iranian frigate IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka in the first American submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II; and Dubai International Airport briefly halted operations following drone strikes. Trump extended a 48-hour ultimatum on energy infrastructure strikes by five days in the final week, citing diplomatic contact, then extended it a further ten days to April 6. Iran publicly rejected US overtures, while Wall Street Journal and New York Times reports suggested back-channel Omani intermediation — a contradictory signaling environment that reflects genuine uncertainty about Iran's internal decision-making following the deaths of multiple senior officials. Prediction markets placed a 52% probability on the conflict extending beyond April 30.

**The Strait of Hormuz and Global Oil Markets**

Brent crude entered March already up approximately 18–19% year-to-date above $70 per barrel. The conflict's progression drove a sequence of historic moves: Brent surged approximately 12.5% at Monday's open in the second week, settled above $82 by Thursday of that week — an approximately 18% weekly gain — then surged above $118 per barrel intraday in the third week before partially retracing, recording an intraday swing of approximately $36 that was the widest on record, surpassing moves at the Ukraine invasion's onset. By the final week, Brent held near $108–$113 and is on pace for its largest monthly gain since 1990. The Strait effectively ceased functioning as an oil transit corridor by the third week, with only two commercial vessel transits recorded in a 24-hour period and no oil shipments passing through on certain days. More than half the world's largest maritime insurance clubs withdrew war-risk coverage for Persian Gulf vessels; insurance premiums for Hormuz transit reached approximately 5% of hull value, roughly five times the conflict's outset level. Saudi Arabia suspended operations at its 550,000 barrel-per-day Ras Tanura refinery following a drone strike. The IEA announced a record emergency release of 400 million barrels in the third week — the largest in the agency's history — with Japan separately announcing a unilateral release of approximately 80 million barrels. The IEA characterized the cumulative supply disruption as equivalent to the combined effect of the two 1970s oil shocks and the 2022 European gas crisis. Goldman Sachs raised the plausibility of Brent crossing $100 per barrel if low flows persisted; BloombergNEF's base case for April averages $106.70, with a delayed reopening to mid-June pushing toward $154.40; and Macquarie warned of a path toward $200 in a prolonged closure scenario. Iran's military commander stated Tehran "does not believe in closing the Strait at all" — a signal worth monitoring carefully.

**Commodity Contagion and Supply Chain Disruption**

The conflict's commodity footprint broadened materially as March progressed. Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — producing roughly 20% of global LNG supply — sustained damage that analysts estimate could take years to repair, with two affected trains expected offline for up to five years. Iran stopped natural gas exports to Turkey following strikes on South Pars. European natural gas futures rose more than 60% from pre-conflict levels at the peak, breaching €50 per megawatt-hour. Urea prices rose 25% since hostilities began, reaching levels last seen during Russia's Ukraine invasion, with Iran accounting for approximately 5% and 11% of global urea and ammonia trade respectively. Aluminum briefly hit a near four-year high, coal prices reached their highest in over a year on gas substitution effects, and jet kerosene in Singapore surged more than 60%. Approximately one-fifth of global air freight was disrupted, over 46,000 flights were canceled in the third week alone, and economy-class Sydney-to-London fares increased more than 80%. Supertanker earnings reached levels not seen since early 2020, with VLCC day-rates on the Middle East-to-China route exceeding $200,000 — a gain of more than 600% year-to-date — with Sinokor Group assembling a concentrated market position. The fertilizer and food price impulses are expected to materialize with a lag, and the IMF urged policymakers to prepare for a prolonged conflict scenario.

**Global Monetary Policy: A Collective Pivot**

The conflict triggered one of the more significant collective monetary policy pivots of the current cycle. Central banks globally had entered March on easing trajectories or holding patterns; by month-end, nearly all had shifted materially toward caution or tightening. The Reserve Bank of Australia was the first to move, hiking rates in the third week in response to the inflationary impulse. The ECB, Bank of England, Federal Reserve, and Bank of Japan all held at their March meetings but pivoted their communications toward tightening bias. Bloomberg Economics estimated March inflation across advanced economies was running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections. IMF Managing Director Georgieva warned a sustained 10% energy price increase would push global inflation up 40 basis points and shave 0.1–0.2% from growth. The Dallas Fed modeled a Strait closure lasting through June as reducing global GDP growth by 2.9 percentage points next quarter, and the OECD raised its G20 average inflation projection to 4.0% from 2.8% in December. Pre-existing global debt vulnerabilities compound the challenge: global debt reached $348 trillion at end-2025, a fresh record driven by a $29 trillion annual increase — the fastest accumulation since the pandemic — and private credit stress deepened throughout the month, with Blue Owl's fund gating, UBS raising its worst-case default scenario to 15%, and private equity returning distributions equivalent to just 14% of net asset value in 2025.

**Dollar, Reserve Currencies, and Geopolitical Realignment**

The Bloomberg Dollar Spot Index is on pace for its largest monthly gain since July, approximately 2%, reflecting haven demand and the repricing of Fed easing. JPMorgan strategists turned bullish on the dollar for the first time in a year; speculative positioning flipped from the most net-short in five years to net-long. Gold's trajectory was counterintuitive: after peaking intraday above $5,000 per ounce earlier in 2026, the metal fell approximately 27% from that peak through the worst weeks of March — its worst multi-week stretch in over a decade — posting its worst weekly decline since 1983 in the final week as the global monetary policy repricing eroded the appeal of non-yielding assets, dollar strength intensified, and Turkey sold approximately $8 billion in gold reserves to defend its currency. Reports that Iran is permitting Hormuz passage for vessels paying in yuan prompted Deutsche Bank to flag the conflict as a potential catalyst for petrodollar erosion — uncertain in magnitude but significant in long-run ramifications. The structural barriers facing dollar alternatives remain formidable: German Bund annual trading volumes of roughly €6.74 trillion amount to less than two weeks of US Treasury turnover, and foreign holdings of Chinese government bonds stand at just under $400 billion against $9.3 trillion in foreign-held Treasuries.
