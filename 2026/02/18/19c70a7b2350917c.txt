Texas Oil Drillers See Salvation in Data Centers
There are hopes that toxic wastewater from oil drillers could be put to work 
at new data centers.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter David Wethe looks at potential uses for the huge quantities of 
wastewater that spew from oil wells in the largest US shale patch.

The Permian Basin has a growing problem 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-permian-basin-geyser/?cmpid=BBBXT021826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 with toxic wastewater.

A possible solution may come from a surprising place: the huge number of data 
centers planned for the largest US oil patch.

The fracking that transformed the Permian — a swath of land spanning Texas and 
New Mexico — involves the use of water to squeeze oil out of wells.

Once the crude is extracted, it leaves producers with salty, tainted water — 
largely from the enormous quantities that already lie alongside the oil — which 
they typically dispose of by re-injecting it underground.

But that practice appears to be nearing its limits. As Bloomberg reported 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-permian-basin-geyser/?cmpid=BBBXT021826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 last year, it has triggered earthquakes and geyser-like leaks at the surface.
Injection disposal wells inside an earthquake zone in Odessa, Texas.
Photographer: Bloomberg
Meanwhile, Texas’ cheap energy, plentiful land and relatively easy permitting 
are attracting the tech industry in its quest to build infrastructure for 
artificial intelligence. There are more than 400 data centers currently planned 
or under construction in the state.

Each data hub needs its own cooling system. So, what if the wastewater — 
cleaned up by the oil industry — was put to work at those locations?

For a 1-gigawatt project, an average of 5 million gallons (119,000 barrels) of 
water a day will be needed, Ken Nichols, director of water sustainability at 
Tetra Tech Inc., said at anevent in Houston <https://producedwatersociety.com/> 
last week.

All told, data centers could boost water demand by 10 million barrels a day by 
2030, according to estimates from Laura Capper, principal at EnergyMakers 
Advisory Group and one of the closest observers of water use in the oil patch.

It may not be a perfect match. By the most optimistic of estimates, the oil 
industry may be able to supply about 5.5 million barrels of cleaned-up water by 
then, she said. The deficit would only add to Texas’ water stress.

But the concept still offers hope for a sector that has struggled to figure 
out what to do with its wastewater. Other plans floated includeselling it to 
farmers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-08-21/shale-s-fracking-wastewater-woes-may-be-solved-by-parched-texas-ranches?cmpid=BBBXT021826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 and ranchers, or simply discharging cleaned-up water intorivers and streams 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-12-11/texas-mulls-allowing-oil-companies-to-pump-treated-fracking-water-into-rivers?cmpid=BBBXT021826_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
.

Compared to that, supplying the deep-pocketed tech industry is a tantalizing 
prospect.

—David Wethe, Bloomberg News
