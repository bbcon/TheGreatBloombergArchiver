Equity markets steadied early Tuesday, with S&P 500 futures rising 0.2% after a broad selloff the prior session driven by renewed anxiety over AI's disruptive potential. The catalyst was a weekend report from Citrini Research outlining AI-related risks across multiple sectors, which, combined with a warning from Nassim Taleb about escalating volatility and potential software-sector bankruptcies, sent IBM to its steepest single-day decline in 25 years — triggered specifically by Anthropic's announcement that its Claude Code tool can modernize COBOL, the dated programming language central to IBM's mainframe business. Indian IT exporters Infosys, TCS, and Wipro fell 3–4% in Mumbai as the selloff propagated globally. Elsewhere in corporate news, Paramount Skydance raised its offer for Warner Bros. Discovery, and PayPal was reported to be attracting acquisition interest after losing roughly half its market value.

Against this backdrop, Jamie Dimon offered a sobering assessment at an investor event, drawing parallels to the pre-2008 period when competitive pressure to generate net interest income led banks toward poor underwriting decisions. "I see a couple people doing some dumb things," he said, a remark notable for its directness even if the systemic parallels remain contested. Bitcoin extended its February decline to approximately 19%, consistent with its behavior as a risk asset rather than the safe-haven narrative some proponents advance.

On fiscal and trade policy, President Trump's 10% global tariff entered into force at 12:01 a.m. Washington time Tuesday — a replacement measure following the Supreme Court's invalidation of his original IEEPA-based duties. The administration is preparing a formal directive to raise the rate to 15%, though timing remains unfinalized. Separately, the administration signaled it intends to slow-walk refunds on the overturned tariffs, with Treasury Secretary Bessent framing repayments as "corporate welfare." Legal challenges are accumulating, with more than 1,500 refund lawsuits already filed. Bloomberg Economics estimates roughly $170 billion in contested duties.

The macro backdrop ahead of Trump's State of the Union address was notably mixed: 2025 GDP growth came in at 2.2% — the slowest since the post-COVID expansion began — while core inflation ended the year at 3%, a full percentage point above the Fed's target. January payrolls of 130,000 represented a significant rebound from the meager 15,000 monthly average through 2025, and unemployment edged down to 4.3%. Housing, however, remained sluggish, with new home starts posting a fourth consecutive annual decline.
