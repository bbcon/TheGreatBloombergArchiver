 Selling this year’s winners 



The war in the Middle East is barely registering in the US stock market: The 
S&P 500 is down less than 1% this week and futures point to a modest gain this 
morning.

“I look at the market reaction and I’m actually surprised,” Goldman Sachs CEO 
David Solomon saidat a conference in Australia <https://bloom.bg/4s7TOXP> 
overnight. “The market reaction has been benign.”

It’s another story in Asia, Latin America and Europe, where the losses have 
been much steeper(though European equities are ticking higher today). Of 
course, that’s in part to be expected because those regions have been 
outperforming the US in a big way for the past few months. Andthe dollar is 
surging as investors seek a haven <https://bloom.bg/4rSgScT>, making it less 
appealing for US investors to own international assets.

Take South Korea, the poster child for AI-related euphoria. Stocks there fell 
the most on record today <https://bloom.bg/3OMpyCS> as traders unwound 
momentum-fueled trades <https://bloom.bg/4rSgFX9>.

There’s another factor at work too: The biggest economic risk is that the war 
leads toan extended closure of the Strait of Hormuz <https://bloom.bg/4rVA49I>, 
a critical route for oil exports from the Persian Gulf. That’s much less of an 
issue for the US, which is a powerhouse oil and gas producer, while Asian 
economies depend on imported oil, notes Ajay Rajadhyaksha, global chairman of 
research at Barclays.

“Markets are differentiating here, and the fact of the matter is that the 
United States does not depend on its oil needs from the Middle East anymore,’’ 
he said onBloomberg TV <https://bloom.bg/4aTlqrW>. “The Straits of Hormuz are 
far more important for Europe but most importantly for the big Asian economies. 
Those are the markets that are giving it up, correctly, I think.’’ —Phil 
Serafino <https://bloom.bg/42HUmtw>, with Paul Dobson <https://bloom.bg/3DbGBZA>
 andHaslinda Amin <https://bloom.bg/4nAYpiz>

 Private markets, public losses 



Amid the worst start to the year for their stocks in more than a decade, 
leaders of Wall Street’s biggest private markets firms had a surprising message:
 Investors have reason to be concerned. <https://bloom.bg/4cK7gfF>

A chorus of executives at the Bloomberg Invest conference yesterday warned of 
more trouble to come.Soros Fund Management’s Dawn Fitzpatrick talked of “a 
painful 18 to 24 months” in both private credit and private equity.

Private credit funds are facing a wave of withdrawals and analysts are warning 
default rates could soar if AI disrupts corporate America. And in private 
equity, managers are struggling to offload assets and return cash to investors.

“People made choices: If you wanted a higher dividend, you could take more 
risk,” Apollo Global Management’s Marc Rowan said on stage. “That felt really 
good on the way up. That’s not going to feel so good on the way down.” —Laura 
Benitez <https://bloom.bg/4rMOkkK>, Allison McNeely <https://bloom.bg/3ESfaVe> 
andLayan Odeh <https://bloom.bg/4cwITlv>
