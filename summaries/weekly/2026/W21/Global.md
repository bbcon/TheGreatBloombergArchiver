# Global Weekly Briefing: May 18–24, 2026

## The Hormuz Premium Hardens Into a Macro Regime

What began nearly three months ago as an acute supply disruption has, by the close of week 21, settled into something resembling a structural condition. The Strait of Hormuz remained largely closed throughout the week, with commercial shipping reduced to limited Iranian-linked movements. Iran said on Friday that Washington's latest proposal had partly bridged differences, but Supreme Leader statements on retaining near-weapons-grade uranium and a reported Iran-Oman plan for a permanent toll regime in the strait undermined hopes of imminent resolution. President Trump called off a planned strike on Tuesday at the request of Gulf allies seeking more time for diplomacy, then by week's end threatened renewed action. NATO began discussions on potential intervention to reopen the waterway if it remained closed into early July.

Oil prices reflected the oscillation between escalation and de-escalation rather than any directional conviction. Brent traded above $111 on Monday before falling 5.6% on Wednesday on diplomatic optimism, then climbing back toward $105 by Friday. WTI ended the week near $98. Goldman Sachs estimated global visible inventories were drawing down at 8.7 million barrels per day in May — nearly double the average pace since the conflict began — and US crude stocks including the SPR fell by a record 17.8 million barrels in the latest weekly print. Rapidan Energy warned that sustained closure through August could push Brent toward $130. A consensus is forming among market participants around $100 as the likely cap over the next year, with demand destruction expected to do the rebalancing work that supply cannot.

## The Bond Market Reprices Inflation Risk

The most consequential development of the week occurred in sovereign debt. The US 30-year Treasury yield reached 5.19% on Tuesday, the highest since June 2007, while the 10-year touched 4.63%. The selloff was genuinely global: Japanese 30-year yields surged as much as 20 basis points on Monday to their highest level since the tenor's 1999 debut, prompting Prime Minister Takaichi to reverse course and order an extra budget of roughly $19 billion. UK gilts, German bunds, and Indonesian and Indian sovereigns all came under pressure. Bloomberg's gauge of average sovereign yields reached its highest since July 2008.

The G-7 finance ministers' meeting in Paris on Monday and Tuesday was overshadowed by the move. Traders have shifted decisively: fed funds futures now imply roughly a 70% probability of a Fed rate hike by December, having priced cuts as recently as late February. Minutes from the April FOMC meeting, released Wednesday, showed a majority of participants prepared to consider firming if inflation remained persistently above target, and many wanted to drop the easing bias from the post-meeting statement. Kevin Warsh is scheduled to be sworn in as Fed chair Friday into a backdrop that constrains rather than enables the dovish pivot the administration had sought.

## Divergence Sharpens Across Central Banks

Bank Indonesia delivered a surprise 50-basis-point hike on Wednesday to 5.25%, double consensus expectations, as the rupiah approached record lows near 16,500 per dollar. The Reserve Bank of India is reportedly considering its 2013 taper-tantrum playbook — rate hikes, swap lines, overseas dollar issuance — with the rupee near 97. The ECB is widely expected to hike in June, with Belgian Governor Wunsch among those signaling readiness. Japan's core inflation slowed to a four-year low, complicating but not eliminating the BOJ's tightening path. The pattern is one of central banks responding to a common supply shock with differentiated urgency depending on currency vulnerability and energy import exposure.
