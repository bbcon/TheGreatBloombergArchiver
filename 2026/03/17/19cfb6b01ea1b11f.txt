Stocks are anything but resilient

Global stocks are posting their worst month in three and a half years. Despite 
that fact,a misplaced perception of equity resilience is fueling broader 
investor complacency and will facilitate much greater declines, strategist Mark 
Cudmore writes on Bloomberg’s Markets Live blog.

The MSCI All-Country World Equity Index is down 4.3% in March, for its biggest 
monthly drop since September 2022. As of yesterday’s close, the annualized pace 
of decline so far this month was about 63%. That’s pretty bleak.

And yet there’s a lot of talk that equity investors don’t seem that bothered 
by the war 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/markets-may-be-underpricing-iran-risks-bank-of-america-warns?cmpid=031726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
.What’s with the cognitive dissonance between the poor returns and the 
perception of resilience? Simple: US stocks.

As of Monday’s close, the S&P 500 was down only 2.6% for the month, with the 
Nasdaq 100 only off a measly 1.2%. The latter in particular is feeding this 
false idea of markets strength.

Some observations:

 * US stocks were expected to be relative outperformers once the war started 
because the country is a net energy exporter.
 * The moves negate the prior broad consensus that US stocks were set for 
another year of underperformance.
 * That relates to the key role of de-risking. An index of S&P 500 software 
and services stocks is up 3.1% this month, precisely because it had dropped 26% 
over the prior four months on worries related to AI.
 * In other words, market participants are reducing positions, including 
closing shorts. This has helped the index at the margin but it’s not a 
risk-positive dynamic.
Stock markets are crashing, globally. Look what’s happened so far this month 
in Japan (-7.9%), South Korea (-9.7%), France (-7.6%), Switzerland (-8.1%) or 
Indonesia (-14%) — using benchmark indexes for all. And we’re only just over 
two weeks into a war with no clear sign of resolution. The negative wealth 
impact is large and growing, while the real supply disruptions are yet to hit 
with full force.

Diverging rate paths

Bond managers are doubling down on bets that central banks’ monetary policies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/bond-funds-boost-diverging-rate-bets-bucking-inflation-threat?cmpid=031726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
 will diverge, even as the inflation fear unleashed by the war in Iran boosts 
the case for higher interest rates.

Investors say it’s wrong to assume rates will rise across the board because of 
the war. Some are buying the bonds of nations where growth is weak — on the 
grounds that policymakers in places like the UK won’t pile on the economic pain 
by raising rates and may even continue cutting.

At the same time, others are sticking with bonds whose yields are relatively 
high because rates were already rising, such as those of Australia and Japan. 
Australia’s central bank todayraised its key rate for a second straight meeting 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/australia-delivers-first-back-to-back-rate-hikes-since-mid-2023?cmpid=031726_marketsdaily&utm_campaign=marketsdaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260317>
.

Investors will be paying close attention to guidance this week from central 
banks, with most expected to keep rates unchanged. Since the war in Iran 
started, markets have flipped to betting on rate hikes in the UK and Europe, 
while confidence in cuts in the US this year has faded.

“You’ve got three different directions within the G7 central banks: up, down 
and no change,” said David Roberts at Nedgroup. “People aren’t used to that.” —
Alice Atkins <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUJRzAw42ww/alice-atkins> and 
Naomi Tajitsu <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVtlmblMDyc/naomi-tajitsu>
