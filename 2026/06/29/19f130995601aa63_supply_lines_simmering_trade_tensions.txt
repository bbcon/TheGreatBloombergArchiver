Global trade uncertainty is poised to intensify this week with disagreements 
and disruptions flaring up on multiple fronts.

The first involves the European Union and China. EU Trade Commissioner Maros 
Sefcovic is scheduled to meet Commerce Minister Wang Wentao in Brussels on 
Monday as the 27-nation bloc presses Beijing to address a widening trade 
imbalance.

Chinese state media last week accused 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/china-s-state-media-say-eu-risks-freezing-of-ties-over-trade?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>
 EU officials of a “lack of sincerity” in preparatory talks ahead of the 
meeting. Over the weekend, a social-media account affiliated with China Central 
Television said the world’s No. 2 economycan endure 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-28/china-says-it-can-withstand-eu-trade-freeze-cctv-account-says?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>
 a further deterioration — or even a freeze — in economic and trade ties with 
the EU, which it called a “rule-breaker.”

cars

“My guess is Europe is going to shift more and more in our direction,” Stephen

Starting on Wednesday, the EU is imposing a €3 duty 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/europe-sees-6-billion-reasons-for-new-levy-on-online-shoppers?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>

low-priced Chinese merchandise sold by sites like Shein and Temu.

Digital Trade

Another trade-policy battleground involves the EU, but the antagonist is US 
President Donald Trump, whothreatened on Friday 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/trump-vows-100-tariff-on-countries-over-digital-services-taxes?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>
 to impose 100% tariffs on goods imported from European and other countries 
that impose digital services taxes on American tech giants.

Trump said the levy would be imposed “immediately” and would supersede any 
existing trade deals. His comments came a day after the EU gave itsfinal 
sign-off 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/eu-gives-us-trade-deal-final-approval-ahead-of-trump-deadline?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>
 to a trade agreement with Washington.

In response, a European Commission spokesperson said EU member states have the 
sovereign right to regulate economic activity and any taxes are 
non-discriminatory by design and apply equally to all large companies 
regardless of origin.

Deborah Elms of the Hinrich Foundation noted on LinkedIn 
<https://www.linkedin.com/posts/deborah-elms_section-301-digital-services-taxes-activity-7476492883944972288-ufvU/?utm_source=share&utm_medium=member_android&rcm=ACoAAAHb-j4BSK3SrVDpkrvWQcJOSsgVBA8ApBk>
 that “the first Trump administration already opened Section 301 investigations 
into thesame topic 
<https://ustr.gov/trade-topics/enforcement/section-301-investigations/section-301-digital-services-taxes>
 against a wide array of trade partners such as the EU, member states like 
France, and India.”

USMCA Talks

On Wednesday, the signers of the US-Mexico-Canada Agreement are expected to 
hold a virtual meeting to discuss next steps in re-negotiations for the North 
American trade deal that Trump revamped in his first term and marked July 1, 
2026, for a trilateral a review.

Trump and Trade Representative Jamieson Greer have indicated that rather than 
rubberstamping it, the accord needs some tightening up on issues like 
rules-of-origin requirements for goods other than autos, tougher labor 
provisions and addressing non-tariff barriers. Tensions between theWashington 
and Ottawa 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/howard-lutnick-delayed-canada-bridge-opening-to-seek-bigger-cut-of-toll-revenue?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>
 are already frayed.

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/carney-plans-faster-permits-labor-reforms-to-boost-canada-trade-flows?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>

According to analysts with Bloomberg Intelligence, the US won’t withdraw from 
USMCA and it will remain in force, but Washington will seeks to subject the 
accord governing $2 trillion in trade annually to regular reviews.

The American business community is pushing for continuity and clarity. The US 
Chamber of Commerce brought dozens of pro-USMCA businesses to the Congress last 
week to remind lawmakers of the trade deal’s importance to the agricultural and 
automotive industries.

“The ask was consistent: support maintaining the framework, press for full 
compliance from all three governments, and encourage an expeditious and orderly 
review that delivers certainty for businesses who want to invest in their 
communities and strengthen their supply chains,” the chamber said in ablog post 
<https://www.uschamber.com/international/u-s-chamber-emphasizes-to-congress-the-importance-of-a-strong-usmca-agreement>
 last week.

Widening the gap 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-26/us-goods-trade-deficit-widens-to-biggest-in-more-than-a-year?cmpid=BBD062926_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260629&utm_content=5935>
 | The US merchandise-trade deficit widened in May to the biggest in more than 
a year as exports pulled back from record levels and imports continued a steady 
march higher. The shortfall in goods trade grew 27.4% from the prior month to 
$105.8 billion, Commerce Department data showed. Exports of goods decreased 
5.4%, while imports rose 3.6% to the highest level since March 2025.
