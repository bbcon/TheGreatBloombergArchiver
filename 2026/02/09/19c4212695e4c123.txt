 ‘Buckle up’ 



There’s hardly any conviction <https://bloom.bg/4qtUu8a> that US stocks can 
keep rallying.

Friday’s sharp recovery — the Dow industrials topped 50,000 
<https://bloom.bg/4kq2960> for the first time — wiped out most of the week’s 
losses. However, investors are still wrestling with howAI will reshape the 
software industry. <https://bloom.bg/3O0kLgR>

Beneath the surface of Friday’s strength, there were hints that confidence in 
the move was thin:

 * A measure of expected volatility in the S&P 500 is above its average this 
year, suggesting a sense of unease. (Goldman Sachs traders warned stocks  face 
more selling this week <https://bloom.bg/4toGa3l> from trend-following 
algorithmic funds. “Buckle up,” they wrote.) 
 * Trading volume pointed to a lack of exuberance, clocking in around 13% 
below the five-day average. 
 * A roughly 9% rally in a Goldman Sachs basket of most-shorted names 
indicated some of the recovery came fromtraders exiting bets against riskier 
shares.
“The AI theme has changed from one where it will lift all boats to becoming 
more winner-take-all,” said Sameer Samana of the Wells Fargo Investment 
Institute. “Until the market is able to separate the winners from the losers, 
it will have difficulty figuring out leadership and making new highs.” — 
Alexandra Semenova <https://bloom.bg/4iPcfw5>

 China scaling back 



China is directing its banks <https://bloom.bg/4tqolAU> to pare back their 
holdings of US Treasuries, and yet investors barely blinked.

Yields on 10-year notes are up only two basis points to 4.23%. The reason for 
the muted reaction is that China already has been scaling back on US debt for 
years, says Bloomberg Markets Live strategist Skylar Montgomery Koning.

“China’s holdings of US Treasuries have been declining for more than a decade 
without causing a bear market in bonds or the dollar,’’ she writes. “The 
process reflects gradual diversification rather than all-out dumping, and 
reductions in Chinese holdings have been more than offset by increased demand 
from other foreign buyers.’’

As of November, China’s combined state and private holdings of US Treasuries 
stood at $683 billion, around 7% of foreign holdings and just 2% of total 
securities outstanding. Banks hold less than half of the total. — Phil Serafino 
<https://bloom.bg/42HUmtw>



 The week ahead 



Investors this week will get arguably the two most consequential US economic 
snapshots — the monthly jobs report Wednesday and consumer inflation Friday. 
The reports are unusually close together after the brief government shutdown 
delayed each by a few days. Normally, the jobs report lands on a Friday and CPI 
comes the following week. Click here for the full rundown on the week ahead 
<https://bloom.bg/4toBn1Z>.


Monday: Mexico CPI. Central bankers making public remarks include Fed’s Waller 
and Miran, the Atlanta Fed’s Bostic, BOE’s Mann and ECB’s Lagarde, Lane, Simkus 
and Nagel.

Tuesday: US retail sales, NFIB small business optimism, employment cost index. 
Cleveland Fed’s Hammack, Dallas Fed’s Logan speak. Barclays, Spotify, 
Coca-Cola, Ford earnings.

Wednesday: China PPI, CPI. Italy industrial production. US nonfarm payrolls, 
jobless rate, federal budget balance. Central bank speakers include Fed’s 
Bowman, Kansas City Fed’s Schmid, ECB’s Schnabel, Cipollone, Buch and Tuominen, 
and Riksbank’s Seim. OPEC monthly market report. Hilton, Cisco earnings.

Thursday: India CPI, Japan PPI, Netherlands CPI, Poland GDP. UK GDP, 
industrial production. US initial jobless claims, existing home sales. Central 
bank speakers: Dallas Fed’s Logan, Fed’s Miran, ECB’s Cipollone, Radev, 
Stournaras, Lane, Nagel and Machado, Bank of Canada’s Rogers. IEA monthly oil 
market report. Coinbase earnings.

Friday: China home prices, euro-area GDP, Spain CPI, US CPI. ECB’s de Guindos, 
BOE’s Pill, Riksbank’s Jansson, BOJ’s Tamura speak.
