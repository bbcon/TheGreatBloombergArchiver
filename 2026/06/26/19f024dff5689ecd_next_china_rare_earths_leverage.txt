Hi, this is Martin Ritchie in Shanghai, where this week I’ve been deep-diving 
into the rare earths trade.

I wanted to know how President Xi Jinping is using China’s minerals dominance 
to strike back at Japan in the Asian nations’ worst geopolitical dispute in 
years, and what that might mean economically.

What I found was a clear but calibrated squeeze 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/xi-pressures-takaichi-by-throttling-key-mineral-exports-to-japan?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 on some categories of critical minerals. It is far from a bazooka blast for 
Japanese factories but enough of an issue to cause targeted snarls and, 
perhaps, get the attention of officials in Tokyo.

Rare earths have gained massive attention since April last year, when China’s 
export controls on some of them rocked global manufacturing and brought US 
President Donald Trump to the trade negotiating table.

As a reminder of how rare earths remain a contentious issue between the 
superpowers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2026-us-china-heavy-rare-earth-magnets-defense/?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
, this week Beijing placed export controls on two American rare earth producers 
— companies that are part of US efforts to slash dependence on China.

Japan has much more experience with China using rare earths as a diplomatic 
tool. Back in 2010, it faced a sweeping embargo during a dispute over maritime 
issues.

This time round, the standoff involves the Communist Party’s sensitivities 
over Taiwan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-18/taiwan-president-appeals-to-world-s-media-to-defy-china-pressure?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
, the self-ruled island that the People’s Republic claims as its own despite 
never ruling it.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-23/china-targets-us-rare-earths-firms-in-signal-to-pentagon-video?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
WATCH: Beijing hits American companies with export controls.
In November, Japanese Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi rattled China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-11-24/china-uses-japan-spat-to-pressure-world-to-pick-sides-on-taiwan?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 with comments that seemed to imply Japan could get involved in any war over 
Taiwan. Beijing’s response included bans on seafood imports, travel curbs and 
stopping approvals for new films.

It has also involved expanded scrutiny on exports of controlled minerals. So 
some seven months after Takaichi’s comments, what’s happening?

Since January, two materials have seen the clearest changes in export flows. 
One is a rare earth called yttrium, vital for LED screens and semiconductor 
manufacturing gear. The other is tungsten, a tough mineral (but not a rare 
earth!) used in high-performance cutting tools and special alloys. Car 
factories need lots of it.

Exports of key forms of those two minerals have been at precisely zero since 
the start of 2026. Key types of tworare earths used in magnets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/how-xi-is-using-rare-earths-against-trump-in-the-us-china-trade-war?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 have also been at zero but they have been close to that level for more than a 
year.

The good news for Japan is that many other materials — including many that 
were affected in 2010 — are still flowing for now, albeit at lower volumes. 
Tungsten and rare earths in their more refined “metal” form are still 
available. So are the more common “light” rare earths and the magnets that were 
at the heart of last year’s supply shock.

So far, any alarm in Japan has been subdued, or at least well-hidden, but that 
may change in the event of a more prolonged or widening squeeze.

Masayoshi Matsumoto, the boss of major Japanese tungsten buyer Sumitomo 
Electric Industries, suggested during a visit to the Chinese capital this week 
that his company was doing okay for now but that Tokyo may eventually have to 
talk with Beijing about the matter. The company can’t even get tungsten from 
its own plant in China, he said.
A rare earth mine in central China.Source: Chinatopix/AP Photo
Japan has prepared for such situations for more than 15 years by stockpiling 
and readying alternative sources. Tungsten processors have developed 
significant recycling businesses, for example. Australia’s Lynas Rare Earths 
started shipping crucial heavy rare earths to Japan last year, which may 
provide modest relief.

Also this week, Japanese Chief Cabinet Secretary Minoru Kihara said two of his 
country’s nationals weredetained in China on suspicion of smuggling 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/china-detained-two-japanese-in-may-on-suspicion-of-smuggling?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 — cases that appear linked to the rare earths industry. Beijing has railed 
against illicit exports since launching curbs a year ago that sent global 
prices rocketing.

As if to drive its point home, this week China’s Ministry of Commerce unveiled 
a new hotline for whistleblowers to report supply-chain violations, including 
illicit exports.

It is clear the government is intent on implementing a mine-to-market regime 
in which every ton of key mineral imported, mined, processed and sold is 
monitored, and nothing leaks out without the government’s knowledge.

And Beijing will use this edge whenever it needs.

AI Battle

The US-China trade truce is masking a burgeoning battle between the 
superpowers over whose AI models the world will adopt.

Despite plans by Chinese startups such as Zhipu, as Beijing-based Knowledge 
Atlas Technology is popularly known, toraise billions of dollars in share sales 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/zhipu-considers-multibillion-dollar-share-sale-in-hong-kong-after-2-000-gain?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 to catch up with the US in the hotly contested race, they can’t match the 
financial firepower of their American rivals or their technology due to US 
export controls restricting access to cutting-edge chips.

That hasn’t stopped them from positioning themselves as an alternative by 
offering lower-priced, open-source models 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-27/why-china-s-deepseek-qwen-and-moonshot-are-a-worry-for-us-ai-rivals?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
. For leading US AI companies such as Anthropic and OpenAI, that represents a 
threat to a business model predicated on investing billions and getting users 
to pay top dollar for the most powerful, proprietary technology.

This week, Anthropic threw down the gauntlet, accusing Alibaba of waging a 
large-scale effort to“illicitly” access its Claude AI model 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/anthropic-accuses-alibaba-of-illicitly-accessing-its-ai-models?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 using thousands of fraudulent accounts that undermine the developer’s decision 
to keep its products out of China.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-06-25/anthropic-says-alibaba-illicitly-accessing-ai-model-video?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
WATCH: Anthropic says Alibaba “illicitly” accessing AI model.
Anthropic said that a campaign by operators linked to Alibaba’s Qwen AI lab 
targeted Claude’s most prized capabilities, including software engineering and 
agentic reasoning, according to a letter that it sent to several American 
senators and White House officials.

The accusations come after the US government this month ordered Anthropic to 
bar foreign nationals from accessing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-15/anthropic-block-marks-us-reversal-warning-to-silicon-valley?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 its most advanced AI platforms because it discovered users could “jailbreak”— 
or bypass the guardrails of — its latest Fable 5 model.

Alibaba, which is already suing the Pentagon to be removed from a blacklist 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/alibaba-sues-the-us-seeking-removal-from-pentagon-s-blacklist?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 that identifies the company as a supporter of the Chinese military, had no 
comment. The company’s shares tumbled, extending a slump from this year’s peak 
due to doubts over its AI progress and the nation’s sluggish consumption sector.

Tencent, whose shares have slid on perceptions it is also an AI laggard, has 
startedtesting a new AI assistant for WeChat 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/tencent-tests-deepseek-powered-ai-agent-for-wechat-corporate-app?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 as part of its efforts to catch up with peers. The losses for the two tech 
giants have contributed to a bear market for a key gauge of Chinese companies 
trading in Hong Kong.

US developers have accused Chinese tech companies of piggybacking on their 
work in the past. Anthropic and OpenAI previously warned that startups, 
including DeepSeek and Minimax, have employed distillation to develop their own 
models.

Distillation is when developers train systems using results from another AI 
model to create similar capabilities in a new one at a far lower cost. While 
tolerated for training smaller, less-advanced systems, distillation violates AI 
labs’ terms of use when it is employed to replicate a cutting-edge AI model 
without permission.

The stakes are high. What may once have been global competition that turned on 
price and competitiveness has now come under regulatory scrutiny, threatening 
to bar Chinese models from the US market.

“Chinese AI models face an elevated risk of a US ban,” wrote Robert Lea, an 
analyst at Bloomberg Intelligence. “The White House’s prohibition on foreign 
access to Anthropic’s flagship Fable 5 and Mythos 5 models signals a shift 
toward a more aggressive US regulatory posture on AI.”

For the Chinese AI startups, there is comfort that Beijing has their back, 
much like it did when the US government sought to kneecap Huawei by preventing 
the so-called national champion from accessing American tech. The Chinese 
government views open-weight models that can be embedded across industries as a 
national asset.

For instance, Chinese authorities recently introduced more measures to expand 
AI adoption, and deepen integration in e-commerce, logistics and retail. The 
nation is alsobuilding out an ambitious, expensive 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-09/china-prepares-295-billion-plan-to-fund-nationwide-ai-buildout?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
 data center network.

The government has also pledged to ease listing requirements, a show of 
support for unprofitable startups like Hong Kong-listed Zhipu, which also aims 
to issue shares in Shanghai.

Zhipu will need the money. Its cash-hungry business is likely to require 
continual fundraising over the next three years as rising agentic AI use causes 
operating losses to widen, according to Bloomberg Intelligence.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

A former tech executive’s inspirational battle with ALS and his artful use of 
artificial intelligence to raise awareness about the disease transfixed Chinese 
social media this week.

Cai Lei, who stepped down from JD.com in 2019 after being diagnosed with the 
neurological disorder 
<https://www.ninds.nih.gov/health-information/disorders/amyotrophic-lateral-sclerosis-als>
 that is also known asLou Gehrig’s disease 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-10-06/new-als-research-backs-trailblazer-s-focus-on-immune-system?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
, released a video on theGlobal Day for ALS 
<https://www.als-mnd.org/events-programs/global-day/> on Sunday that drew more 
than60 million views 
<https://s.weibo.com/weibo?q=%23%E8%94%A1%E7%A3%8A%E8%AF%B4%E5%B0%86%E4%BA%B2%E7%9C%BC%E8%A7%81%E8%AF%81%E6%B8%90%E5%86%BB%E7%97%87%E8%A2%AB%E7%BB%88%E7%BB%93%23>
 on Weibo.

In the 47-minute clip, Cai issued a public declaration of war against ALS, 
which has no known cure though a range of therapies can prolong lives. He has 
already beaten the odds, outliving most people in terms of time from diagnosis 
with amyotrophic lateral sclerosis.

Cai, 48, is paralyzed and without speech, though he has been able to use tech 
to recreate his voice. Before ALS damaged his vocal cords, Cai recorded many 
hours of himself speaking. With the use of AI, he can replay that to make it 
sound as if he is talking normally.

In the clip, Cai vowed to use more tech if his condition worsens further, 
pointing to brain-computer interface technology that could link his brain with 
things like computers or even robots.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed?cmpid=BBD062626_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260626&utm_content=5885>
Social media users praised his single-minded push to find a cure for ALS, 
which affects an estimated 100,000 to 200,000 people in China.

“No one can come out calmly after watching Cai Lei’s speech,” said one Weibo 
user with almost 5 million followers. “This short film really showed his 
extraordinary belief and will.”

Cai set up the ALS Patient Support Network to help expedite drug 
breakthroughs. His efforts also include starting a medical technology company, 
developing his own research team, inviting pharma companies to forums that he 
organized and building a big data platform to collect patient information for 
research purposes.

A Weibo user with some 63,000 followers said that campaign makes Cai 
“confident that he can witness the end of ALS with his own eyes.”
