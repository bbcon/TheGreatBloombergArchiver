Iran War Fallout Bursts South Korea’s Bubble
Middle East turmoil has ended a brief honeymoon period for South Korea’s 
president.

Past performance is no guarantee of future results.

The common refrain of financial advisers the world over is likely ringing in 
the ears of President Lee Jae Myung followinga shock 12% plunge 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/korean-stock-gauge-poised-for-correction-on-iran-war-selloff?cmpid=BBD030426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 in South Korea’s stock market today — its biggest selloff yet — on the back of 
the fallout from the Iran war.

Lee, a former day trader, invested much of his political capital 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-23/south-korea-president-s-day-trading-losses-fuel-kospi-stock-market-reform?cmpid=BBD030426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 into making equities a measure of his success. His pledge to push the 
benchmark KOSPI index past the 5,000 mark appeared to pay off when he achieved 
that in February, less than a year into his four-year term.

Turmoil in the Middle East now threatens to burst that bubble 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/trump-pledges-safe-oil-transit-from-mideast-as-iran-war-widens?cmpid=BBD030426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
. Even if equities bounce back, confidence in the resilience of Korea’s 
extraordinary stock surge is unlikely to fully return.

To be fair, Lee’s focus on the Kospi index ranged beyond the simple idea of 
what is good for tech giant Samsung is good for Korea, as he tried to wean the 
country off itslong-standing dependence 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-27/south-korea-faces-property-ticking-bomb-as-lee-backs-bok-hold?cmpid=BBD030426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 on real estate and into assets like stocks.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-03-04/asian-markets-in-free-fall-as-iran-saps-sentiment-video>
WATCH: Bloomberg MLIV Strategist Mark Cranfield discusses the latest.
The problem is that it went a bit too well, amplified by a global 
artificial-intelligence rally that boosted South Korea’s chipmakers.

After the grand fanfare of crossing the 5,000 milestone — rapidly followed by 
the 6,000 mark — some debutante investors may have arrived at the party at the 
wrong time.

Participation by amateur so-called retail investors has surged. Many are 
precipitously exposed to sharp losses.

As the panic set in, Bank of Korea Governor Rhee Chang Yong delayed his 
departure for a summit of financial chiefs in Bangkok to convene an emergency 
meeting on the fallout.

Lee, who was in the Philippines today as the crash unfolded, will hold an 
extraordinary cabinet meeting tomorrow to discuss the war’s impact.

The hit to Lee’s popularity remains to be seen. But it seems fair to say that 
his honeymoon 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-03/south-korea-s-lee-holds-clear-lead-in-election-exit-poll-shows?cmpid=BBD030426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 is over. — Heesu Lee <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AROCY-6lRy4/heesu-lee>

Chart of the Day

Trump’s attack on Iran threatens to deal a severe blow to a global economy 
still grappling with the impact of his tariff regime. Washington and Tehran may 
find an off-ramp, allowing oil to settle back at its pre-escalation average of 
$65 a barrel and the global economy to dodge a hit, but the latest signs 
suggest there may be worse to come. Bloomberg Economics hasmodeled scenarios 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-03/iran-war-oil-price-surge-put-global-economic-recovery-at-risk?cmpid=BBD030426_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260304>
 for what lies ahead, and what it means for oil prices, major economies, and 
the future of Iran.
