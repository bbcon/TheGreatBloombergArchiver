US

US Daily Briefing — May 23, 2026

Coverage relevant to US markets and policy is thin on this Saturday, with Bloomberg's weekend distribution focused primarily on global thematic analysis. Two storylines nonetheless carry implications for US investors.

Gulf capital recycling under strain

Bloomberg Economics highlights how the ongoing Iran conflict, now in its third month, is materially altering the petrodollar recycling mechanism that has historically supported US asset prices and Treasury demand. With the Strait of Hormuz largely closed, Gulf sovereigns are confronting simultaneously lower hydrocarbon revenues and higher domestic outlays — Saudi Arabia's Q1 budget reflects roughly $17 billion in war-related costs, equivalent to 1.5% of GDP. The Public Investment Fund had already cut its targeted foreign asset allocation to 18% from a prior ambition near 50%, and recent withdrawals from LIV Golf and a $200 million Metropolitan Opera deal suggest a continued pivot inward. For US markets, the implication is incremental rather than acute: a structurally smaller Gulf bid for Treasuries and US equities at the margin, and diminished credibility around the multi-trillion-dollar investment pledges made to the Trump administration in 2025. The magnitude and persistence of the effect remain to be seen, particularly given uncertainty around the conflict's duration.

Subnational climate policy diverges from federal

Berkeley, California has become the first US municipality to require fossil-fuel appliance replacement or equivalent green upgrades as a condition of single-family home sale, under its Building Emissions Saving Ordinance effective January. The move is notable less for its direct economic footprint than as a marker of the widening gap between municipal climate action and federal rollback under the Trump administration, which has canceled grant programs including a Groundswell-administered residential energy resiliency initiative in Alabama and Georgia. For homebuilders, appliance manufacturers and residential energy retrofitters, the patchwork of state and local mandates increasingly substitutes for the federal incentive architecture being dismantled. No major US macro, Fed, or corporate developments were reported in today's distribution.

Asia

Asia-Pacific Daily Briefing — May 23, 2026

Coverage for the region is thin in today's flow, with the bulk of newswire material focused on the ongoing Iran war's disruption of Gulf petrodollar flows and US domestic climate policy. We highlight the Asia-relevant threads below.

China growth momentum fades, reviving stimulus debate

Bloomberg Economics flagged that China's economy is slowing across the board, reigniting expectations for additional policy support. The signal is consistent with the softer prints seen through the spring, though specifics on the composition of any forthcoming package — fiscal, monetary, or property-targeted — remain unclear. Markets will look for clarity from Beijing in the weeks ahead, particularly given that external demand is being further pressured by the Hormuz disruption and elevated energy costs.

India digests a sharp equity drawdown tied to AI underexposure

Indian equities have seen a notable rout, with commentary framing it as the cost of "missing the AI wave." The narrative marks a meaningful shift from the bullish India-as-alternative-to-China positioning that dominated 2024–25 flows, and warrants scrutiny as to whether this reflects a durable rerating of sector composition or a tactical rotation. We would caution against extrapolating either way until earnings revisions confirm the trend.

Indirect Asia channel: shrinking Gulf capital pools

The most consequential cross-regional development remains the contraction of petrodollar recycling. Gulf sovereign wealth funds, historically large allocators to Asian tech, infrastructure, and EM debt, are turning inward to fund war-related spending and domestic projects such as Saudi Arabia's $35 billion Aramco privatization push. Pakistan, which has previously relied on Gulf bilateral support to avert default, sits among the more exposed sovereigns; Egypt-style rescues will be harder to replicate. Asian beneficiaries of GCC FDI — including Indian infrastructure platforms and Southeast Asian data-center projects — may face delayed or scaled-back commitments, though the magnitude remains to be seen and will depend on the war's duration. No material flow today on Japan, Korea, or Australia.

Europe

European Daily Briefing — Saturday, May 23, 2026

Weekend coverage of European markets and policy is light, with no major eurozone data releases or ECB communications crossing the wires. We focus on two structural themes with material implications for European assets and policy.

Gulf capital retrenchment poses a headwind for European assets

The ongoing Iran conflict, now in its third month, has largely shut the Strait of Hormuz and is reshaping the petrodollar recycling mechanism that has long supported European markets. Gulf sovereign wealth funds — collectively managing assets approaching multiple trillions of dollars — have historically deployed capital across European equities, real estate and prestige assets including English football clubs. With Saudi Arabia's first-quarter budget showing roughly $17 billion (1.5% of GDP) in war-related costs, and Qatar facing potential losses of $20 billion from damaged gas infrastructure that could take five years to restore, the pool of Gulf capital available for foreign investment is contracting. Saudi Arabia had already pivoted inward before the conflict, with PIF cutting its targeted international allocation to 18% from a previous goal approaching 50%, and is now pursuing a $35 billion Aramco privatization to court domestic capital. The implications for European luxury, sports franchises, prime real estate and sovereign debt markets warrant monitoring, though the magnitude of impact remains to be seen.

UK political shift complicates climate and fiscal agenda

Reform UK has overtaken the Conservatives as the principal party of the British Right, introducing meaningful uncertainty over the durability of UK climate policy. Against a backdrop of sluggish growth, local authorities — illustrated by Cardiff's combined cycle-lane and flood-mitigation projects — are increasingly designing infrastructure to qualify for multiple funding streams, a pragmatic response to fiscal constraints. Reform's stated opposition to net-zero policy, articulated by deputy leader Richard Tice, suggests a potential reordering of UK energy and infrastructure investment priorities should current polling translate into electoral outcomes. Global

Gulf capital flows reverse as Hormuz disruption enters third month

Nearly three months into the US-Iran conflict, the largely shuttered Strait of Hormuz is reshaping not only energy markets but the global capital cycle that has long depended on recycled petrodollars. Bloomberg Economics estimates Saudi Arabia's first month of fighting alone cost roughly $17 billion, or 1.5% of GDP, with defense, logistics and economic-support outlays climbing across the six GCC states. Qatar reports that one damaged gas facility could take up to five years to restore, with associated losses potentially reaching $20 billion. The UAE is accelerating construction of pipelines that bypass Hormuz. The combination of compressed export revenues and elevated domestic spending implies a meaningful contraction in the pool of Gulf capital available for deployment abroad — a flow that, over decades, helped absorb US Treasury supply and suppress global borrowing costs. The magnitude and persistence of that effect on US rates remains to be seen, but the directional implication is clear.

Pledges to Washington increasingly in question

The trillions in investment commitments made to the Trump administration last year look progressively less deliverable. Saudi Arabia's Public Investment Fund had already cut its targeted share of foreign assets to 18% from an earlier ambition near 50%, and the kingdom shifted from net lender to net borrower in 2024. The war is accelerating an inward turn: LIV Golf is preparing for possible bankruptcy after PIF withdrew funding, a $200 million Met Opera deal has been abandoned, and Riyadh is now focused on the $35 billion Aramco privatization to court capital domestically rather than export it. Recipients of Gulf largesse — Egypt, Pakistan, Jordan, and prospective reconstruction in Gaza, Lebanon and Syria — face a tighter funding environment.

Climate policy bifurcation

With the Trump administration unwinding federal climate action, sub-national actors are filling gaps: Berkeley's new ordinance mandating emissions-reducing upgrades at point of sale is the first US measure of its kind, joining disclosure regimes in Austin, Minneapolis and Portland.
