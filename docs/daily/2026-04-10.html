Daily Brief

Friday, April 10, 2026

US

US equity markets continued their recovery, with the S&P 500 recording its seventh consecutive day of gains — the longest winning streak since October — as the Iran ceasefire appeared to hold heading into weekend peace talks in Islamabad. US junk bond spreads have returned to pre-war levels, and the Bloomberg US Corporate High Yield Index closed at a record high, reflecting a meaningful easing of financial conditions. The market's durability through a turbulent geopolitical period has been notable, though investors remain alert to weekend headline risk. The day's macro focus shifted to the March CPI release, which analysts expect to show the fastest monthly headline increase in nearly four years, driven by the energy price surge following the US-Iran conflict. That inflationary impulse is complicating the Federal Reserve's path, with Bloomberg Economics now projecting only two cuts this year, down from four forecast in January. The outlook was further complicated by an unusual joint action from Fed Chair Jerome Powell and Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent, who summoned Wall Street bank CEOs to an urgent meeting over concerns that Anthropic's new AI model, Mythos — released to a limited set of firms — could enable a new generation of cyberattacks. Officials stressed that banks need to harden their defenses against AI-enabled threats, a signal that financial sector regulators are increasingly treating advanced AI as a systemic risk issue. In corporate developments, an index of SaaS stocks fell 4.8% on Thursday to its lowest level in over three years, extending a roughly 40% decline in 2026, as Anthropic's release of its Claude Managed Agents product and a new Meta model renewed fears that AI agents will erode the competitive moats of incumbent software vendors. Optical component maker Lumentum rose nearly 5% in premarket trading on accelerating demand from large technology companies, while Nike slipped after a downgrade citing athleisure market saturation. First-quarter S&P 500 earnings growth is now estimated at 12.5%, up from 10.9% prior to the war, with results from Goldman Sachs and JPMorgan Chase due next week.

Asia

The dominant story across the Asia-Pacific region Friday was Xi Jinping's meeting in Beijing with Cheng Li-wun, chairwoman of Taiwan's Kuomintang opposition party and the first KMT leader to visit mainland China in a decade. Xi called for "cooperation and peace," while Cheng echoed the message and expressed hope that the Taiwan Strait would not become a "chessboard for external interference" — language calibrated to appeal to Beijing's longstanding opposition to what it views as foreign meddling. Xi simultaneously reaffirmed China's sovereignty claim over Taiwan, making the diplomatic warmth conditional rather than unconditional. The meeting carries explicit geopolitical signaling: with President Trump due to visit Beijing for a summit with Xi in mid-May, Beijing appears to be demonstrating to Washington that Taipei contains significant constituencies favoring engagement over deterrence. The KMT has stalled a $40 billion Taiwanese defense expansion and a $14 billion US arms deal has not received Trump's approval, reportedly to avoid jeopardizing the planned summit. On the economic front, China's factory-gate prices exited deflation in March, with PPI rising 0.5% year-on-year after a 0.9% decline in February — an inflection partly attributable to the global energy price surge following the Iran war. Consumer prices, however, continued to cool as holiday-season demand faded. Capital Economics cautioned the PPI upturn remains modest and may not persist. In a separate positive development for Chinese industry, EV and hybrid exports surged 140% year-on-year in March to a record 349,000 units, with BYD, Geely, and Chery leading shipments. Domestic auto sales remain under pressure — BYD's home sales fell more than 40% — underscoring the continued bifurcation between China's export competitiveness and subdued internal demand. Alibaba disclosed ownership of the "Happy Horse" AI video model, which debuted atop global rankings and displaced ByteDance's Seedance 2.0. Alibaba's cloud unit also led a $293 million funding round for ShengShu Technology, further intensifying competition in AI video generation. In India, the central bank's aggressive currency support effort — the boldest in a decade — risks deterring the foreign investors it has sought to attract, a tension that remains unresolved.

Europe

European markets traded higher Friday, with the Stoxx 600 gaining 0.6%, following Asian equities upward as the Iran ceasefire appeared to hold ahead of weekend talks in Islamabad. The macro backdrop, however, carries growing tightening implications. Bloomberg Economics now sees the European Central Bank shifting to rate hikes rather than cuts, reflecting the persistence of energy-driven inflationary pressure from the Middle East conflict. UK gilt markets remain under particular strain: the Bank of England, previously expected to cut, is now seen holding, with one fixed-income specialist noting that "for about a year gilts have been looking standout cheap" but that the war has upended the prior consensus. Hungarian politics commanded significant attention as Prime Minister Viktor Orbán faces his most serious electoral test in Sunday's vote. The outcome may hinge on older voters who consume primarily state-controlled media — outlets Orbán has shaped into government mouthpieces over 16 years in power — and who remain largely unaware of corruption allegations or reports of Russian influence. Should Orbán fall, it would mark the end of a political era in Central Europe, though the result remains genuinely uncertain. In Italy, Prime Minister Giorgia Meloni ousted the CEO of state-backed aerospace and defense contractor Leonardo while retaining the heads of Eni and Enel, a recalibration of executive leadership at state firms that follows a referendum defeat last month. The opposition is energized: Silvia Salis, a 40-year-old former Olympic hammer thrower and progressive mayor of Genoa, is emerging as a potential challenger, with some commentators already labeling her the "anti-Meloni." In corporate news, Sodexo plunged 20% in Paris after issuing weaker-than-expected revenue and margin guidance, and UBS confirmed it will not hand over privileged documents to the Credit Suisse Nazi-linked accounts investigation, citing legal exposure risks following its court loss earlier in the week.

Global

The defining macro theme this week has been the recalibration of global monetary policy expectations in response to the energy shock produced by the US-Iran conflict. Bloomberg Economics' aggregate measure of advanced-economy interest rates now sits approximately 35 basis points higher at the end of 2026 compared with projections from three months ago — a meaningful upward shift reflecting widespread recognition that damage to Middle East energy infrastructure may prove durable. The Fed, ECB, and Bank of England are all navigating asymmetric pressures: growth headwinds on one side, energy-driven inflation on the other. Oil markets remain central to the picture. WTI held near $99 per barrel Friday, on track for its steepest weekly loss in nine months as ceasefire optimism tempered the conflict premium, though Saudi Arabia's disclosure that Iranian attacks have impaired its own production capacity provided a partial floor. Strait of Hormuz traffic remains severely disrupted — only a handful of vessels transited on April 8-9 versus a pre-war norm of roughly 135 per day — and reopening the waterway is the critical near-term variable. Trump demanded Iran reopen the strait ahead of weekend talks in Islamabad, where Vice President JD Vance will lead the US delegation. Pakistan, which brokered the ceasefire, has gained significant diplomatic capital from hosting the talks, though it faces risks as a Saudi treaty ally should hostilities resume. The geopolitical implications extend well beyond the Hormuz. China's role in persuading Iran to accept a ceasefire — credited by Iranian officials to a last-minute Beijing push — illustrates the growing influence Beijing derives from its deep financial footprint across the Gulf, where it has outcompeted the US as a regional financier. Xi's $270 billion in Middle East investments constrain how openly China can back Iran, creating a nuanced position: quietly facilitating de-escalation while avoiding overt alignment with either side. Separately, the US-China space competition took a concrete step forward with the successful Artemis II mission, which broke the distance record for human spaceflight and puts a crewed lunar landing as early as 2028 on the table — approximately two years ahead of China's current timeline. The IPCC's funding and data challenges following the US withdrawal represent a quieter but consequential long-term development, with China projected to surpass the US in publicly funded research within two years.
