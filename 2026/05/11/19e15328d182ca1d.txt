Chips Off the Old Block

Chokepoints matter. For the present, the stranglehold that chip manufacturers 
have <https://bloom.bg/4ttC3Sv> over the development of artificial intelligence 
is having a far greater impact on markets than even Iran’s control over the 
vital oil bottleneck of the Strait of Hormuz. That in turn has driven a boom in 
earnings that’s truly unprecedented.

Earnings seasons almost always see companies exceed their forecasts, because 
they have themselves had a hand in setting them. But the way the S&P 500 is 
blowing through its estimates for the first quarter is different:

This can be attributed mostly to the quite extraordinary boom for 
semiconductors. Demand is surging thanks to the artificial intelligence 
buildout, and supply is more limited than expected (in a way that might be 
exacerbated by the Hormuz shutdown). The need for less sophisticatedmemory chips
 <https://bloom.bg/42Xzb5p> — as well as the brilliantly engineered AI chips 
that made Nvidia Corp. the most valuable company in the world — is also proving 
greater than thought. Putting this all together now leads brokers to forecast 
that next year’s earnings for theSOX semiconductor index 
<https://bloom.bg/3RxV34M> will be almost double last year’s:

The boom in semiconductor share prices is based on very genuine revenues and 
profits, but it also reflects a growing realization that markets have misjudged 
the biggest winners from AI. Since the launch of ChatGPT ushered in the AI 
excitement, the Magnificent Seven — the tech platform groups that stand to 
dominate the provision of AI large language models, plusNvidia 
<https://bloom.bg/4d1xnhJ> — were seen as likely to benefit far more than the 
semiconductor manufacturers who make the “picks and shovels” for data centers. 
In the last six months, this has turned on its head, and the SOX index has now 
outperformed the Magnificents since ChatGPT launched:

(Nvidia is in both the numerator and denominator, but this is a useful 
comparison.)

One thing that has not changed, however, is that the benefits of the overall 
market’s latest run to all-time highs have grown ever more concentrated. The 
Magnificents continue to outperform the rest of the S&P 500, but what’s more 
remarkable is the way the cap-weighted version of the index is outstripping the 
equal-weighted version, in which each company is weighted at 0.2% regardless of 
size. Concentration has intensified throughout the AI era:

For now, the implicit bet is that the overall pie will grow, even if a 
disproportionate slice goes to a few winners. In the shorter term, it’s 
impossible to argue against earnings that are this strong — particularly as, 
despite everything, the macroeconomic environment continues to look benign.

NFP = No Financial Problems

The US jobs market is calm. Indeed it’s almost in Goldilocks, not too hot/not 
too cold territory. Non-farm payrolls for April, the second month of the war 
with Iran, showed a solidgain of 115,000 <https://bloom.bg/4tq9GV4>, while the 
unemployment rate was unchanged. Growth has slowed, but the jobs market is no 
longer showing the signs of incipient recession it exhibited through much of 
2024 and 2025:

For financial markets, the key implications are a) any recessionary impact 
from the war isn’t being felt in the US yet, and b) the case for further rate 
cuts has more or less evaporated. Next Tuesday’sinflation 
<https://bloom.bg/4tq9Hs6> data loom large, as higher oil prices are almost 
universally expected to have driven headline inflation toward the totally 
unacceptable level of 4%:

Despite the desires of the White House, significant rate cuts are impossible 
in this environment. Nancy Vanden Houten, US chief economist at Oxford 
Economics, pushed back her forecast for the next rate cut from June to 
December. She said the numbers would give Fed officials “assurance that the 
labor market is healthy enough to withstand an extended period of steady 
interest rates as the Fed focuses on the inflation side of its dual mandate.” 
The futures market suggests that even that is optimistic, and prices the Fed to 
stay on hold into the second half of next year:

Viktor Shvets of Macquarie agreed with outgoing Fed Chair Jay Powell 
<https://bloom.bg/4wgBMVv> that the jobs market was in an unstable equilibrium, 
with no hiring balanced by no firing:

AI investment and wealth impact will continue to boost GDP but at the expense 
of inequalities and affordability. Immigration policies reduced unemployment 
breakevens while whiplashes from trade and geopolitics cause uncertainties for 
non-AI investment, inflation and employment. Investors should be prepared for a 
greater than usual number of dissents at the Fed (already the highest since 
’92), with tightening as likely as easing, and switches from “dovish” to 
“hawkish” and back in weeks not years.

Macroeconomic conditions like this do not get in the way of a big equity 
rally, as we’ve seen. But they do reflect an awkward equilibrium. Starting with 
Tuesday’s CPI, we should brace for the risk of further macro-driven volatility.



The Road Ahead

Driverless cars are roaming US streets in greater numbers than ever. Waymo’s 
robotaxi fleet completes at least500,000 trips a week <https://bloom.bg/3PxMh6j>
. Their journey to this point offers a glimpse into the future of mobility as 
Tesla’s robotaxi business joins the fray.

There’s a long road ahead. As of 2024, Uber alone handled roughly a million 
mobility trips each hour, underscoring the sheer scale driverless vehicles must 
reach to become a dominant force in transportation. ButVitaliy Katsenelson, who 
writes The Intellectual Investor <https://bloom.bg/3R0beIc>, argues that 
ride-hailing services like Uber are gradually integrating them into their 
fleets. Partnerships with Waymo or Tesla might allow operating companies to 
improve at crucial metrics including safety, wait time, and price.

The scale of that task ahead reveals much about driverless vehicles’ long-term 
potential.Goldman Sachs forecasts <https://bloom.bg/4dBN9Qs> that the global 
market will reach $415 billion in 2035, with the US hitting $48 billion:

The US accounts for 40% of robotaxi deployment to date. China makes more than 
a fifth of such autonomous vehicles. The breakneck development of advanced 
artificial intelligence models, and the number in different stages of being 
piloted, suggests that it’s only a matter of time before driverless vehicles 
become a mainstay on our roads:

But optimism has dampened since the pandemic era. Beyond the obvious boost 
that lockdown restrictions gave to autonomous transport, deployments are 
running into stringent regulatory standards that have tightened following a 
series of high-profile accidents.

According to CR Legal Team <https://bloom.bg/4udKdQ3>, AV crashes reported to 
the US National Highway Traffic Safety Administration doubled in 2024, to 544 
from 288, although that did coincide with a big expansion of the fleet. Only 
one fatality was reported. Roughly 94% of all US crashes involve some form of 
human error, so AVs could still deliver substantial safety benefits, but the 
UBS Autonomous Vehicles index, covering 21 major AV manufacturers, is far off 
its Covid highs. Performance during the 2022 energy shock, and the rebound in 
the last few months, underscore how geopolitical disruptions hasten the 
evolution of transport:

Bloomberg’s Autonomous Vehicles index, which includes a broader range of 
companies that benefit from AVs (so it’s helped by the rally in chips), traded 
in line with the S&P 500 for five years but has surged in the last month:

Inevitably, the story would be incomplete without Elon Musk’s Tesla Inc. Its 
long-touted driver assistance mode was expected to blossom into full autonomy, 
but it’s behind schedule, allowing other manufacturers to leapfrog Tesla. 
George Noble of Noble Capital Advisors, a trenchant Tesla critic, argues that 
its self-driving mode falls short of requirements to be classified as 
autonomous:

It is a camera-only system running probabilistic inference. The car is making 
statistical guesses about what it sees thousands of times per second, with no 
redundancy when those guesses are wrong.

Musk has had to push back the timeline for fully autonomous vehicles for 
private use until later this year <https://bloom.bg/4udKdQ3>. As for robotaxis, 
the feature onlyrecently became available <https://bloom.bg/4cYDRhm>, but 
delays left Tesla unable to refine its semi-autonomous system in time to 
dominate demand for commercial fleets.

While troubles may have delayed Tesla, the robotaxi party is only starting. 
Goldman Sachs’ Mark Delaney forecast that a company that both builds and 
operates its own robotaxis could achieve gross margins of 30% to 50%, implying 
a global gross profit pool of about $150 billion 10 years from now. Over the 
next decade, he argues that robotaxis’ cumulative gross profits could reach 
$440 billion.

Are these projections plausible? Much depends on whether AVs can bring down 
the costs of fleet management as hoped. Goldman’s researchers estimate that the 
total cost of goods sold per mile could fall below $1 by 2035, thanks to 
falling insurance premiums and layoffs of remote operators. It’s not just 
drivers who will lose their jobs; the ratio of vehicles per human supervisor 
should improve from roughly 6-to-1 today to 26-to-1 by 2035.

The road ahead for autonomous vehicles no longer appears quite so futuristic. 
Ultimately, the breakneck pace of development in advanced AI models suggests an 
acceleration of the future of mobility and freight.

—Richard Abbey
