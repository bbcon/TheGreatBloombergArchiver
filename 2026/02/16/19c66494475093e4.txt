 Someone has to be wrong about AI 



There’s a contradiction at the center <https://bloom.bg/4aXyKfh> of how Wall 
Street has been talking about artificial intelligence.

On one side, there’s the view that AI will dramatically change the economy. 
(That’s been the driving force behind the punishing selloffs across a number of 
industries, like real estate services, wealth management, insurance and 
logistics.)

On the other side, there’s still a lot of hand-wringing that Big Tech is in an 
AI bubble. (Microsoft and Amazon shares have been hammered this year by 
investors worried about them spending billions on more AI development.)

“Those two things can’t be true at the same time,” said Julia Wang of Nomura 
International Wealth Management.

Even if the answer lies somewhere in the middle, that both of these scenarios 
have become major narratives shows how AI is still the only story that matters 
in the US stock market.

The same is true for credit. Microsoft, Amazon, Meta, and Alphabet alone are 
expected to spend more than $600 billion on capital expenditures in 2026.That 
means some of the richest tech companies in the world are rapidly turning into 
some of the most indebted.
 <https://bloom.bg/4crAsHW> 
The risk that AI bets don’t pay off is breathing new life into the market for 
credit derivatives <https://bloom.bg/3OhxGeq>, where banks, investors and 
others can protect themselves against borrowers adding on too much debt and 
becoming less able to pay their obligations.

Credit derivatives tied to single companies didn’t exist on many high-grade 
Big Tech issuers a year ago. Now they are some of the most actively traded US 
contracts in the market outside of the financial sector, according to 
Depository Trust & Clearing Corp.

While contracts on Oracle have been active for months 
<https://bloom.bg/4tEpl4D>, in recent weeks, trading on Meta and Alphabet has 
become much more active, the data shows. —Jeran Wittenstein 
<https://bloom.bg/4ai60gd>, Ryan Vlastelica <https://bloom.bg/3Xk5l8K>, Sujata 
Rao <https://bloom.bg/4ifkvUX> and Caleb Mutua <https://bloom.bg/3FG9HkS>

 AI-proof basket 



Goldman Sachs has rolled out a  new custom basket <https://bloom.bg/4bWzdjV> 
aimed at navigating the upheaval in software stocks, betting on companies 
perceived to be better insulated from AI disruption than others.

The firm introduced a software pair trade basket that goes long companies 
whose businesses are seen as difficult for AI to displace while shorting firms 
whose workflows AI could automate or replicate internally.

Making the cut: Goldman favors businesses that stand to benefit directly from 
rising AI adoption, including providers of compute, data infrastructure, 
observability, cybersecurity, hyperscale cloud and AI development platforms. 
That includes Cloudflare, CrowdStrike, Palo Alto Networks, Oracle and 
Microsoft, among others.

On the short side: Traders targeted software-tilted workflows that could be 
automated or rebuilt in-house. Stocks include Monday.com, Salesforce, DocuSign, 
Accenture and Duolingo. —Natalia Kniazhevich <https://bloom.bg/4h3iYAl>
