The defining macro development of this period is the Iran war's uneven transmission through global energy markets, and the growing evidence that the conflict is reshaping monetary policy divergence across major economies. Brent crude rose to $104 a barrel on Tuesday, with the more severe dislocations appearing downstream: Goldman Sachs analysts noted that refined products have rallied far more than crude, reflecting the disruption to medium-heavy crude supplies that feed diesel, jet fuel, and fuel oil production. Jet kerosene in Singapore is up more than 60%, and in parts of Asia fuel costs have approximately doubled.

Iran's intensified attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure — including setting the Shah natural gas field in the UAE ablaze and temporarily shutting the Fujairah port — represent the first confirmed damage to upstream oil and gas facilities in the UAE since the war began. Iran has explicitly rejected ceasefire overtures, and there is no clear pathway to resolution. Vessel-tracking data suggest Tehran is consolidating rather than losing control over Strait of Hormuz transit, with tankers navigating through only by hugging the Iranian coastline with apparent approval from Tehran. Insurance premiums for vessels transiting the strait have reached approximately 5% of hull value — roughly five times the level seen at the conflict's outset — and at least 20 security incidents have been recorded in and around the Persian Gulf since March 1.

Trump's appeals for allied support have produced little tangible commitment. The UK, Japan, Germany, and South Korea have each indicated they will not participate militarily, and China's state media have been openly dismissive. Finland's President Stubb has been among the more forthcoming NATO voices, urging allies to take Trump's warnings seriously. Global shipping disruption continues to mount, with more than 25,000 flights canceled by major Middle Eastern carriers and air freight rates on Asia-Europe routes up 45-80%. Russia, notably, has benefited: Moscow has accelerated crude loading to capture elevated prices while a US sanctions waiver window remains open, with revenues from its key export blend hitting a record for weekly income since the Ukraine invasion began.
