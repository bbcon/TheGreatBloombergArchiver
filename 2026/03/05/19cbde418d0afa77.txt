Productivity’s Dark Side

Even before the full impact of the diffusion of artificial intelligence makes 
itself felt,US productivity 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-08/us-productivity-picked-up-in-third-quarter-labor-costs-declined?cmpid=BBD030526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 has been accelerating. While Thursday’s latest reading is expected to come in 
a little slower, the pace of increase from the start of 2023 to the third 
quarter of last year was 2.6% — more than double the average for the decade 
through 2019.

This is a development policymakers have cheered in Washington. After all, a 
more productive workforce drives the kind of efficiency gains that can allow 
both corporations and their employees to earn more without triggering 
inflation. Historically, economies adapting well tobig tech breakthroughs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/wall-street-s-ai-stock-trade-is-about-the-next-blockbusters?cmpid=BBD030526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
 see the creation of new industries and professions. Living standards rise.

Calculating labor productivity is essentially a simple mathematical equation: 
Some measure of economic output divided by the total number of hours worked by 
employees. Note that denominator. If you slash the number of workers, and/or 
their hours, productivity surges.

This dark side of the matter is what’s been behind recent market wobbles 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-25/wall-street-doesn-t-know-what-to-think-about-ai-anymore-mm2iccu5?cmpid=BBD030526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
. The fear that AI does deliver the promised productivity leap, and then some, 
was captured in a research note by the little-known firm Citrini last month. It 
sent the S&P into a brief nosedive at the start of last week.

History provides some justification for concern. Past technology booms have 
seen household-name businesses fall by the wayside – like camera-maker Kodak 
and video-rental chain Blockbuster, left behind by the internet.
Blockbuster Video, once a high-street stalwart, has all but disappeared — 
driven out of business by the internet.
“Having some boom-bust in a sector is normal,” says Simon Johnson, the Nobel 
prizewinning economist at MIT. “Maybe even how it has to be.” But as companies 
go under, he says, it can create wider risks – especially if the failed 
businesses borrowed lots of money. “What you don’t want is to infect the 
credit, and you definitely don’t want to getinside the banking system 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-12-18/how-ai-borrowing-could-emerge-as-a-problem-for-the-federal-reserve?cmpid=BBD030526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
.”

Among the sectors seen at risk: Back-office services, content production, 
customer support, legal and financial analysis, and coding. Anton Korinek, an 
AI expert at the University of Virginia, says that “Eventually, the disruption 
will extend to any firm whose competitive advantage lies in human expertise that
AI can replicate 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-10-23/what-is-agi-why-openai-anthropic-are-targeting-artificial-general-intelligence?cmpid=BBD030526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
.”

“The transition period may involve stranded assets, debt overhang, and the 
potential for sharp market corrections,” Korinek warns.

Need-to-Know Research

It’s well understood by now that many governments are carrying historically 
high debt burdens, years after their gargantuan outlays for Covid. Partly 
because of that, investors lately have been demanding higher yields to buy the 
longest-dated securities. That’s in turn pushed a number of countries to tilt 
toward selling short-dated obligations.

The consequence: markets are more vulnerable to shocks 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/shock-vulnerability-of-sovereign-debt-is-mounting-oecd-says?cmpid=BBD030526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260305>
, according to the OECD, the Paris based club of developing nations with a 
formidable data analysis arm. While things may look stable on the surface, that 
“masks deeper structural developments that may be adding to risks which could 
crystallize suddenly,” the OECD said in a report Wednesday.

The OECD also noted a reduced share of government debt held by central banks. 
While “in many ways a normalization,” this means the market is “increasingly 
dependent on more price-sensitive investors, including hedge funds, households 
and certain foreign holders.” Issuers may be exposed to shocks and volatility 
that challenge the stability of the world’s $109 trillion of sovereign and 
corporate bond markets, the staff wrote.
