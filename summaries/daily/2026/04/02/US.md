US equity markets are extending their now-familiar pattern of late-week deterioration, with S&P 500 futures down roughly 1.1% as of Thursday morning. Since the Iran conflict began five weeks ago, the index has posted cumulative gains over the first three days of each week before cratering on Thursdays and Fridays — a net loss of approximately 9% on those two sessions alone. The dynamic reflects a straightforward calculus: with weekends offering ample time for geopolitical developments and President Trump historically inclined to announce major moves when markets are closed, investors are increasingly choosing to reduce risk rather than carry positions into a trading blackout. The upcoming long weekend amplifies that anxiety, with the monthly nonfarm payrolls report due Friday when equity markets are shut.

Trump's primetime address, in which he pledged to hit Iran "extremely hard" over the next two to three weeks while simultaneously claiming the war is "very close" to completion, generated precisely the mixed-signal reaction that market participants have come to expect. The strategic ambiguity — promising escalation while signaling an endgame — appears to satisfy neither bulls seeking resolution nor bears seeking clarity on duration. Treasury yields rose across the curve as oil surged, with traders rapidly paring expectations for Federal Reserve rate cuts. The Fed faces an increasingly uncomfortable configuration: energy-driven inflation that shows little sign of abating against a backdrop of slowing growth and elevated policy uncertainty.

On the trade front, the administration is preparing to announce a tiered system shifting steel and aluminum tariffs from metal content to full product value — a structural simplification that has been under discussion since February. Separately, 100% tariffs on pharmaceutical imports are reportedly imminent for companies that have not struck bilateral deals with the White House. Both announcements fall on the one-year anniversary of Trump's original "Liberation Day" tariff declaration. US manufacturing ISM data for March showed the sharpest expansion since 2022, though the prices-paid subindex climbed to 78.3 — its highest since mid-2022 — underscoring that input cost pressures remain acute. SpaceX has also filed confidentially for an IPO that would rank among the largest in history.
