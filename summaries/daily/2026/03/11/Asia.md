China's National People's Congress concluded its annual budget deliberations this week with the 15th Five-Year Plan (2026–2030) signaling policy continuity rather than transformation. The 2026 draft budget prioritizes advanced manufacturing, semiconductor development, and artificial intelligence — the so-called "new-productive forces" — while keeping the fiscal deficit at 4% of GDP. Analysts at Oxford Economics and Gavekal Research noted that the plan's twenty main indicators are broadly unchanged from those of the 14th Plan, and the absence of meaningful household consumption stimulus disappointed investors hoping for a demand-side rebalancing. Whether perpetuating the investment-heavy model will prove sustainable in a structurally weaker global trade environment remains an open question.

China's resilience amid the oil shock has drawn notice. Chinese equities have declined less than global peers since the conflict began, the yuan has held broadly steady against the dollar, and government bond yields have barely moved. Beijing's years of investment in renewables, electric vehicles, and strategic oil stockpiling — now at a record 851 million barrels in onshore commercial inventories — have provided a tangible buffer. However, Bloomberg Economics cautions that the primary risk for China is not domestic inflation but an export demand shock: a 5% decline in external demand would subtract 0.16 percentage points from GDP growth, while a 10% drop would widen the drag to 0.31 percentage points. With oil briefly clearing $100 per barrel during the week, the threshold at which secondary global demand effects meaningfully pressure Chinese exports — estimated by Bloomberg Economics at a sustained $108/bbl — appears less remote than it did two weeks ago.

Separately, Chinese authorities moved to restrict state enterprises and government agencies from deploying OpenClaw AI applications on office computers, citing cybersecurity concerns over the platform's unusually broad data access requirements. The move caused immediate market reaction: Minimax fell 6.5% in Hong Kong and Zhipu dropped 6.1%. India's situation deteriorated further amid the Gulf crisis, with the rupee under pressure, capital outflows accelerating, and the Reserve Bank of India intervening in currency markets. Indian equities have underperformed EM peers significantly since Modi's 2024 coalition government took office, even as real GDP expanded 7.8% last year and inflation remains within target.
