European markets opened with mixed futures on Thursday as ceasefire optimism gave way to renewed uncertainty over whether the Iran truce will hold. The Stoxx Europe 600 was down 0.7%, reflecting the broader retreat from Wednesday's risk-on surge.

The EU's senior economy official warned that Europe remains at risk of stagflation despite the ceasefire, a concern reinforced by data showing German industrial production declined even before the outbreak of hostilities — suggesting structural weakness predates the energy shock. UK mortgage rates rose above 5% in March, according to RICS data, with homebuyer demand falling to its lowest since August 2023, demonstrating that the Middle East conflict is transmitting through borrowing costs into domestic property markets. Labour has slipped to fourth place in UK national polling ahead of May local elections, a notable deterioration in Prime Minister Starmer's political standing that may complicate the government's fiscal and policy choices.

On geopolitics, the US has formally requested that European NATO allies submit concrete plans within days to help secure the Strait of Hormuz, marking a shift from rhetorical commitment to operational demand. Trump publicly berated the alliance again, raising the Greenland issue, after NATO members declined to participate in the Iran operation or offer basing rights. This friction is creating political space for alternative European alignments: former Italian Prime Minister Conte called for deeper engagement with China following repeated US tariff threats, while Hungary's Viktor Orbán faces the most serious electoral challenge of his sixteen-year tenure ahead of Sunday's vote — an outcome with ramifications extending from Brussels to Kyiv. Aldi and Lidl's expansion rivalry has become a minor illustration of continued UK consumer bifurcation, with both German discounters competing for a site in the affluent Norfolk town of Holt.
