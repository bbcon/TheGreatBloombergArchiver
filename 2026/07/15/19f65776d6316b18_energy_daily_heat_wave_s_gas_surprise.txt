Why US Gas Is Shrugging Off Record Heat

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
reporter Julian Hast explores why heat-related power demand in the US may not 
be a boon for natural gas this summer.

A series of US heat waves is revealing an unexpected weakness in natural gas, 
a market that typically strengthens when power demand climbs.

Benchmark futures have languished around $3 per million British thermal units, 
even at the beginning of the month when temperatures above 100F (38C)drove 
demand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-03/largest-us-grid-saw-demand-hit-record-high-thursday-on-heat?cmpid=BBBXT071526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260715&utm_content=6477>
 on the country’s biggest grid to a record.

With extreme heat straining a network already under pressure from the 
proliferation of data centers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-ai-data-centers-electricity-prices/?cmpid=BBBXT071526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260715&utm_content=6477>
, the sluggish response from gas is surprising.

One explanation could be US gas-storage levels, which are persistently above 
average, providing a healthy buffer of supply. New pipelines in West Texas are 
also carrying more fuel outside the oversupplied basin.

Then there’s the matter of the shifting energy mix. While demand for gas 
remains robust, solar power isdominating growth 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-10/solar-surpasses-coal-in-historic-shift-for-us-electricity-mix?cmpid=BBBXT071526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260715&utm_content=6477>
 in new generation capacity — even in theTrump era 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-15/trump-officials-vow-to-keep-all-us-coal-plants-running?cmpid=BBBXT071526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260715&utm_content=6477>
.

That may be trickling through to output. Gas supplied less than 40% of US 
power in recent weeks — 4 to 5 percentage points lower than a year ago, 
according to Timm Schneider, founder of Schneider Capital Group.

Meanwhile, solar and wind generation rose near all-time highs.

Solar is now the cheapest and fastest source 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-28/us-clean-power-growth-to-hit-record-this-year-despite-trump?cmpid=BBBXT071526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260715&utm_content=6477>
 of new capacity in much of the US. It’s also helped by the accelerating 
deployment of batteries that can store power from panels in the middle of the 
day and discharge it when demand peaks.

Solar and storage together made up 91% of new generation 
<https://seia.org/news/solar-and-storage-provide-over-90-of-new-power-in-q1/> 
installed in the first quarter, according to the Solar Energy Industries 
Association and Wood Mackenzie.

The gas market’s insipid reaction to the hot weather suggests a potential 
fading correlation between power and fossil fuels, even when air conditioners 
are cranked all the way up.

The commodity is “losing market share in the power stack,” Schneider wrote in 
a note. And that’s “even in record heat.”

—Julian Hast, Bloomberg News
