The most consequential development across global markets on March 19 is the escalation of direct attacks on Gulf energy infrastructure. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities — and Iran responded by striking Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City, home to the world's largest LNG export facility, causing "extensive damage" and sparking fires. Additional strikes hit a gas facility in Abu Dhabi, set ablaze two Kuwaiti oil refineries, and caused damage near a Saudi refinery. Tehran explicitly designated energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE as "legitimate targets." Brent crude rose above $118 per barrel on the day, a gain of approximately 63% since the conflict began in late February. Three-month Brent futures have returned to $100, signaling the market is pricing a more prolonged disruption.

President Trump sought to contain the escalation, stating the US was not involved in the South Pars strike and warning Iran that further attacks on Qatari LNG would prompt the US to "massively blow up" the entire South Pars field. He simultaneously waived the Jones Act for 60 days, allowing foreign-flagged vessels to transport commodities between US ports — a logistical measure to ease domestic energy distribution. The gap between WTI, trading near $96, and Brent at $115, and Middle Eastern grades above $150, reflects a fragmented supply landscape in which the shortage is most acute for Asian importers dependent on Gulf barrels.

The LNG disruption carries particularly broad consequences. Ras Laffan supplied roughly a fifth of global LNG before the war; a restart now expected to take months rather than weeks is pressuring gas prices globally, with downstream effects on fertilizer production, power generation, and semiconductor manufacturing costs. Many African economies are reportedly running on weeks of refined fuel. The Strait of Hormuz remains near-completely closed, with approximately 40,000 seafarers stranded on either side — trapped between drone attacks and electronic warfare. Saudi Arabia has reportedly been able to reroute some oil exports to above half of normal levels, suggesting contingency infrastructure is partially functional, but the broader disruption to global energy flows remains severe and its duration uncertain.
