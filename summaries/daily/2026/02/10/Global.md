# Global Daily Briefing — February 10, 2026

**Energy markets: Qatar's LNG calculus and North Sea decline.** Qatar's aggressive long-term contracting drive — including a 27-year supply deal with Japan and a notable first sale to Malaysia, traditionally an exporter — has locked in roughly 39 million tons since 2022. Yet these commitments cover only about 60% of planned capacity expansion, forcing QatarEnergy toward greater spot-market participation and potentially a slower production ramp. Given Qatar's structural weight in seaborne gas, any strategic shift would reverberate globally. Separately, Carlyle's Jeff Currie argued oil and metals remain underinvested, with glut fears overstated. The contrast with the UK North Sea is stark: no new exploratory well was drilled in Britain last year — the first such pause since the 1960s — as Aberdeen's industry, now roughly 1% of GDP, contracts under the combined weight of fiscal policy and the energy transition.

**Iran sanctions enforcement broadens.** Adani Enterprises disclosed that OFAC has requested information regarding alleged imports of Iranian LPG through its Mundra port, following a WSJ report from June. Separately, the Indian Coast Guard seized three tankers in what appears to be its first material enforcement action against the dark fleet — a potentially meaningful signal given India's role as a residual buyer of sanctioned barrels.

**Defense spending entrenches as a structural theme.** European aerospace and defense has returned 236% since the February 2022 invasion, with US peers also outperforming materially. The pipeline remains supportive: Trump is pushing US military outlays toward $1.5 trillion by 2027, while Takaichi's Japan and Carney's "middle powers" accelerate their own build-ups. Valuations have stretched, and de-escalation episodes — such as Trump's recent softening on Greenland — will likely produce tactical volatility, but the structural thesis appears intact.

**Monetary policy divergence and central bank independence.** Bank of France Governor Villeroy de Galhau announced an unexpected June departure, handing Macron the opportunity to make a six-year appointment ahead of presidential elections in which Le Pen or Bardella are polling competitively. The timing, alongside Trump's continued pressure on the Fed and his claim that Warsh could deliver 15% growth, underscores rising political pressure on G-7 central banks — though Villeroy denies any non-personal motive.
