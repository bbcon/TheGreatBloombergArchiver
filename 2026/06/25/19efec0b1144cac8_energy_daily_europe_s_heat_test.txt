Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
weather and climate reporter Joe Wertz looks at how Europe’s intense heat wave 
is disrupting energy systems.

The brutal 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-25/heat-wave-breaks-french-and-uk-records-as-temperatures-soar?cmpid=BBBXT062526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5925>
 heat wave blanketing western Europe is setting scorching temperature records 
and pressuring the region’s electricity market.

Western France saw extreme highs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-24/france-records-hottest-day-ever-as-european-heat-wave-deepens?cmpid=BBBXT062526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5925>
 of 43.8C (110.8F) yesterday, lifting power demand for air conditioning just as 
the weather curbs supply.

It’s only June, and Europe is already seeing disruptions, price spikes, a 
strained grid and supply constraints. Meteorologists and weather models point 
to months of heat on the horizon, suggesting this early-season stress test is 
only the beginning.

French power demand <bbg://securities/RTELREAL%20Index> stayed above 40 
gigawatts throughout the day, around 10 gigawatts higher than on recent days 
with milder weather. That level is comparable to demand typically seen in 
April, at the tail-end of the heating season.

Regions further north are also experiencing strains. The UK recorded its 
hottest June day yesterday, and grid operators were briefly concerned there
wasn’t enough power 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-23/uk-issues-energy-supply-warning-as-heat-wave-strains-power-grid?cmpid=BBBXT062526_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260625&utm_content=5925>
 in the network.
Pedestrians in London during this week’s heat wave.Photographer: Chris J. 
Ratcliffe/Bloomberg
The spike in demand has coincided with a period of low wind and other forms of 
generation, with evenings proving to be a particular crunch point as solar 
comes offline. The same high-pressure weather pattern fueling the heat dome is 
stilling wind turbines across parts of Europe, while hot rivers limit 
generation from French and Swiss nuclear reactors.

Average electricity prices in Germany and UK are trending to their highest 
levels since the 2022 crisis, and French energy costs are poised to reach the 
highest monthly average since 2023, EPEX Spot data show.

The hotter conditions and tighter power market are requiring costlier — and 
dirtier — fossil-fuel power plants to come online. France and Germany have been 
leaning more on natural gas to temper sharp price surges since the heat wave 
started.

On Wednesday, French gas generation rose to nearly 5 gigawatts, the highest 
level since April.

The disruption underscores the scale of the challenge faced by the energy 
system in the world’s fastest-warming continent. European utilities will have 
to make sizable infrastructure investments, not only to transition to 
low-carbon sources of power but also to make their networks more resilient to 
the effects of a more volatile climate.

—Joe Wertz, Bloomberg News
