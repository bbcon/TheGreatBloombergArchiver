Trump Delaying His Visit to Beijing Is a Big Relief for Xi
China’s leader is happy to avoid awkward moments with Trump as the war in Iran 
rages

Hi, this is Allen Wan in Shanghai.

President Donald Trump postponed his highly anticipated trip to China set for 
later this month. Far from being disappointed, Beijing is probablyat least a 
tad relieved 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/china-gains-an-edge-from-trump-s-war-with-iran-officials-say?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
.

Besides frustration at the perceived lack of preparation by the US for the big 
summit, China was apparently concerned that hosting Trump could be seen as
condoning the US leader’s actions in Iran 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-says-he-s-rescheduling-xi-meeting-as-iran-war-rages-on?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, especially if the fighting was ongoing.

China has said and done very little about the war that the US and Israel 
launched against Iran — one of Beijing’s friends — beyond calling for a 
ceasefire and sidestepping Trump’sinexplicable request to send ships to help 
reopen the Strait of Hormuz 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-s-call-for-chinese-warships-hits-on-sensitive-topic-for-xi?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
.

Instead, a meeting with President Xi Jinping will probably take place in 
“about five or six weeks,” said Trump. The Chinese side hasn’t confirmed a 
date, giving it wiggle room if the mercurial US leader changes his mind again.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-03-17/trump-says-he-s-resetting-a-meeting-with-china-s-xi-video>
WATCH: President Donald Trump says trip would happen in “about five or six 
weeks.”
There is little reason to believe that a short postponement will upend the 
improvement in US-China relations in recent months, given the good vibes coming 
out ofa meeting in Paris last weekend between top officials 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/us-china-mull-board-of-trade-to-manage-bilateral-economy-ties?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 from the nations.

But uncertainty is anathema to Xi. China’s economy is projected to slow even 
further this year after the government set its most modest growth target in 
three decades, hamstrung by a property collapse, slumping consumption and 
lingering deflation.

Those are all the reasons China needs to refrain from linking the war with the 
trade truce. Never mind that Iran wasa reliable supplier of cheap oil 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/china-set-to-tap-massive-oil-stockpiles-to-offset-iran-crisis?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, and a member of the Global South nations that Beijing wants to be seen as 
leading.

The trade truce has indeed paid off for Xi, clearing the way for his country 
to boost exports around the world. It has also seen the Trump administration 
change course on tech export controls, meaning Nvidia can sell H200 AI 
accelerators to the nation. This week, CEO Jensen Huang said Nvidia wasfiring 
up production of those chips for customers in China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/nvidia-ceo-says-company-is-firing-up-h200-production-for-china?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
.

America’s defense establishment, long hawkish on China, also seems to be 
playing along with Trump’s embrace of Beijing. This week a new US intelligence 
assessmentbacked away from earlier projections 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/us-intel-agencies-soften-outlook-on-china-s-plans-for-taiwan?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 that China wants to be ready to seize Taiwan by force as early as next year.

A while back, a US admiral suggested that China wanted to be ready by 2027 to 
invade the democracy. Since then, just mentioning “2027” was enough to cause 
much agita about possible tensions in the Taiwan Strait, even if Beijing 
repeatedly denied it had any timeline for an invasion.

The new assessment says Beijing doesn’t have a fixed timeline, would prefer a 
peaceful solution, and “Chinese officials recognize that an amphibious invasion 
of Taiwan would be extremely challenging and carry a high risk of failure, 
especially in the event of US intervention.”
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/videos/2026-03-18/why-china-isn-t-speaking-up-on-the-iran-war-video>
WATCH: Why China doesn’t have much to say about the US and Israel attacking 
Iran.
Yet despite the better China-US relations and apparent desire from both sides 
to play nice, the longer that Xi and Trump don’t meet, the greater the risk 
that some issue overturns the apple cart.

There are a lot of things that could do that. Complaints from the US about 
fentanyl 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-30/us-china-trade-war-why-fentanyl-is-at-the-center-of-tariffs-fight?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 or agricultural purchases. Beijing’s military activity in the South China Sea 
or displeasure about that lack of access to high-end US tech.

Then there is the mother issue of them all when it comes to Chinese politics: 
Taiwan and the US’s backing for it.

Washington appears to have a $14 billion arms deal for the self-run island of 
some 23 million people in the pipeline,one that has already 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/china-urges-us-to-stop-taiwan-arms-sales-after-new-deal-report?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 raised hackles in 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/china-urges-us-to-stop-taiwan-arms-sales-after-new-deal-report?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
Beijing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-13/china-urges-us-to-stop-taiwan-arms-sales-after-new-deal-report?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
. That sale would not only be one of the biggest ever but come hot on the heels 
ofan $11 billion package that the US approved 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-12-18/us-approves-11-billion-arms-sale-to-taiwan-risking-china-anger?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 in December last year.

Those weapons agreements irk Beijing to no end, and Xi has signaled that 
Taiwan is an issue on which he sees absolutely no room for negotiation. In a 
phone call just last month, he told Trump that Beijing would never allow the 
island to be separated from China.

The Xi and Trump talks are off for now and could very well happen in the near 
future. Yet the longer they wait to reschedule, the greater the chance of 
something going wrong.

The Oil Shock Upside

When China’s leadership finally put its foot down earlier this month with its 
strongest pledge yet to end deflation 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-05/china-commits-to-ending-deflation-in-2026-with-strongest-vow-yet?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, Washington had already started the heavy lifting.

Days earlier, US and Israeli missiles rained down on Iran, provoking a 
retaliation that’s stillrippling through oil markets 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-14/why-the-us-attack-on-iran-s-kharg-island-is-a-worry-for-oil-markets?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 and jacking up costs for consumers and businesses. Whilerelatively insulated 
from the worst of the price shock 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-11/oil-risk-highest-for-philippine-bonds-in-asia-china-insulated?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 now roiling Asia’s energy-hungry economies, China should feel enough of a jolt 
from oil above$100 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-15/how-to-hedge-against-100-oil-according-to-market-strategists?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 a barrel to snap out of its longest deflationary streak in decades.

For industries laboring under razor-thin margins, the higher cost of doing 
business would be the latest drain on finances.

But the news isn’t all bad 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-20/china-finds-dangerous-cure-for-record-deflation-in-war-oil-shock?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 for the world’s biggest importer of crude. With consumer inflation already 
hovering above zero for five months, producer prices might finally turn 
positive as soon as March, according to forecasts from economists at Citigroup, 
Goldman Sachs and Macquarie Group, endinga stretch of deflation that started in 
2022 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-china-deflation-cost/?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
.

Even before the latest upheaval in oil markets and global shipping, a rally in 
metals like copper has been slowly but surely pulling up China’s producer 
costs, with monthly readings at zero or above for seven straight months.

Whenever it comes, the final leap from negative territory means the GDP 
deflator — a broad economy-wide measure that encompasses both producer and 
consumer prices — could soon end its three sub-zero years. Except for Japan, no 
other major economy has experienced such prolonged price declines since the end 
of World War II.

Deflationary pressures may still lurk in corners of the economy, but an 
upswing in prices could be a balm for companiescaught up in grueling 
competition with rivals 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2025-06-07/how-china-s-woes-resurrected-the-economic-term-involution?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 and under pressure from a glut in an overcrowded domestic market.

If China finally breaks its deflationary spiral, it won’t be long before the 
strain could start to ease on profits and workers’ incomes. To a large extent, 
the outcome hinges on whether a temporary price shock resets the expectations 
of households and businesses about inflation in the future.
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/audio/2026-03-17/big-take-asia-war-sends-energy-shocks-through-region-podcast>
What is less clear is if China will end up with the wrong kind of inflation, a 
product not of buoyant consumer demand but of rising production costs or supply 
constraints.

Known as “cost-push inflation” in economist jargon, it would mean China is 
trading one malady for another, since factories may be left to absorb the 
higher costs instead of passing them on to consumers.

Yet a handful of upbeat voices is starting to make the case that a new 
inflationary era is indeed dawning. The Beijing-based think tank CF40 reckons 
“it won’t be ‘bad’ inflation for China if the oil price hikes are temporary.”

Soon enough, it said, China should be able to turn the page on deflation and 
“enter a positive feedback loop.”

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

Some Chinese people are having a field day with America’s allies largely 
giving Trump the cold shoulder 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-gets-chilly-response-from-allies-over-plea-for-hormuz-help?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
 over his request for them to send warships to the Strait of Hormuz.

As the war rages on, many on Chinese social media see Trump’s request for help 
to unblock the narrow waterway as evidence that he is struggling to deal with a 
problem of his own making.

Trump lashed out at NATO allies for their inaction, calling it a “very foolish 
mistake.” He then insisted thatthe US didn’t need anyone’s help 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-17/trump-belittles-allies-that-rejected-his-iran-war-appeals?cmpid=BBD032026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260320>
, describing his request as a loyalty test.

“Why is he acting like a spoiled, tantrum-throwing giant infant?” one person 
wrote on Weibo.
Iranians set fire to American and Israeli flags in Tehran on March 17.Source: 
Getty Images/Getty Images Europe
Others said Trump was behaving like a character in a novella by Lu Xun 
<https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_True_Story_of_Ah_Q> who consistently loses 
fights but consoles himself by reframing defeat as a moral triumph.

“The world doesn’t need America,” another person wrote. “Trump, on the other 
hand, just goes ahead and claims that America doesn’t need anyone at all.”

Internet users were also amused by the American president’s description of 
Iran as a “paper tiger” — a phrase that Chinese leader Mao Zedong famously used 
to describe the US during the Cold War.

“Trump must have read the Selected Works of Mao Zedong,” one person quipped.
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
Trump’s return to the White House — and the upheaval he has unleashed — is 
viewed by some people in China as good for Beijing.

On Chinese social media, the US president is frequently referred to as “Chuan 
Jianguo,” meaning “Comrade Nation Builder.” That’s a sarcastic suggestion that 
his policies are inadvertently strengthening China’s position on the world 
stage.

To those people, the war with Iran is the latest example of the ways that 
Trump makes China look good by comparison.

“I have long believed that the ‘rise of the East and the decline of the West’ 
was inevitable,” one person said online, echoing a phrase popularized by Xi. 
“This year’s US-Iran war has only accelerated that trend.”
