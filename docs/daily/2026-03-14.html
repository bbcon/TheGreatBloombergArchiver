Global

Two weeks into the US-Israel military campaign against Iran, the conflict's secondary consequences are becoming clearer — in economic dislocation, environmental damage, and geopolitical realignment — even as the primary military situation remains fluid. The Gulf Cooperation Council economies face what may prove to be a structural setback to their diversification ambitions. The implicit bargain underpinning the GCC's relationship with Washington — large investment pledges and increased oil output in exchange for regional stability — has effectively collapsed. Saudi Arabia, the UAE, Qatar, and Kuwait have each absorbed Iranian drone and missile strikes on oil and gas infrastructure, representing the first such attacks in their histories. Energy revenue has been curtailed by Tehran's ongoing interdiction of Strait of Hormuz shipping traffic, which Iran has applied with apparent effectiveness despite degraded military capacity. Bloomberg Economics analyst Ziad Daoud notes that the financial arithmetic is straightforward: less revenue means less available for the outward investment commitments made to the Trump administration, which now appears unlikely to reach the headline figures announced during Trump's May 2025 Gulf tour. The ambition to position the Gulf as a hub for global AI infrastructure appears particularly vulnerable. The UAE and Saudi Arabia had leveraged their cheap energy and permissive regulatory environment to attract large-scale data center investment, with G42's divestiture from Chinese assets positioning the region as a conduit for American technology's global expansion. Iranian strikes have now targeted AWS facilities in the UAE and Bahrain, exposing a physical security assumption that underwrote the entire partnership. As Bloomberg Economics technology analyst Michael Deng observes, the Gulf states satisfied Washington's chip security demands but left physical infrastructure exposed. Separately, the environmental toll of strikes on Iranian oil infrastructure is emerging as a significant humanitarian concern. Israeli drone strikes on refineries and fuel depots on Tehran's outskirts on March 8 triggered large-scale fires, releasing toxic particulates that mixed with rainfall to produce contaminated black rain over a metropolitan area of approximately 18.5 million people. Tehran's geography — situated at the base of the Alborz mountains, which create thermal inversions trapping pollutants — amplifies the risk of prolonged exposure. Environmental analysts identify over 300 incidents carrying environmental risk from the conflict to date, with the Tehran refinery strike the single largest. The health legacy, including heavy metals and hydrocarbons absorbed into soil, water, and human tissue, may extend for decades.
