Asian equity markets declined sharply on Thursday, with the Nikkei 225 falling 3.4% as the surge in oil prices and tightening global monetary policy expectations weighed on regional sentiment. The Bank of Japan kept its policy rate unchanged at its March meeting, with Governor Kazuo Ueda citing uncertainty surrounding the Middle East conflict. The BOJ maintained its conditional tightening bias, pledging to raise borrowing costs if its inflation outlook materializes, but the conflict has materially clouded the timing of that adjustment.

The most acute near-term pressure in Asia is on energy-importing economies. Asian buyers are heavily reliant on Persian Gulf crude, and the near-complete closure of the Strait of Hormuz since late February has forced a significant reorientation of supply chains. Asian purchases of US crude have surged to approximately 60 million barrels for April loading — the highest single-month volume since April 2023 — as buyers scramble for alternatives. Emergency stockpile releases are expected to benefit Western markets first, meaning the supply shortage remains most acute in Asia. The Philippine peso fell past 60 per dollar as energy costs weighed on the country's outlook. Thailand's parliament reconfirmed Anutin Charnvirakul as prime minister, providing some political stability as the country navigates the economic fallout.

China stands as a notable exception to the regional distress. Jefferies' Christopher Wood argued that Chinese mainland equities are the most defensible in the current environment, citing the country's relative energy insulation — comprising robust domestic renewable capacity, battery storage, and reserve buffers — as well as potential support from state funds and easing factory deflation. The AI dimension also skews positively for China: OpenClaw, an open-source agentic AI platform generating significant excitement, has integrated with Chinese platforms, with two recently listed Hong Kong companies surging 19% on its momentum. Beijing's Five-Year Plan emphasis on low-cost, open-source AI deployment aligns structurally with this development, though Bloomberg Intelligence notes that high computational costs associated with agentic functions may compress API margins at firms like Alibaba, SenseTime, and Baidu. Samsung separately announced a record ¥110 trillion capital expenditure plan targeting AI semiconductor leadership.
