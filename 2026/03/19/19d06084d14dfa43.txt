Attacks on Qatar Make LNG’s Worst Nightmare a Reality
Attacks on Qatar are turning an expected glut of LNG into a scramble for 
supplies.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, 
energy reporter Dan Murtaugh examines the high stakes for global gas markets 
after the world’s biggest LNG facility came under fire in the Middle East war.

Liquefied natural gas buyers who expected to feast on cheap fuel this year are 
adjusting to a harsh new reality after the latest attacks by Iran on the 
world’s biggest production plant.

The importance of the Ras Laffan facility in Qatar 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-19/iran-war-strikes-on-qatar-s-lng-crown-jewel-reshape-the-future-of-gas?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 cannot be overstated. Before the war, it supplied about a fifth of the world’s 
LNG and enjoyed a reputation for reliability that allowed it to negotiate 
premium prices. This helped turn the small Gulf nation into the Middle East’s 
wealthiest economy on a per capita basis.

That image was shattered on Thursday morning, when a second wave of Iranian 
missiles targeted the complex, causing “sizable fires and extensive further 
damage 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-18/qatar-reports-extensive-damage-at-site-of-ras-laffan-lng-plant?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
” to LNG facilities, according to QatarEnergy.

The plant had already halted production 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/iran-war-dents-qatar-s-reputation-as-lng-s-safest-bet?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 after a previous drone strike. The latest, more devastating attacks have 
sparked fears amongcustomers and traders 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/gas-traders-weigh-attacks-at-world-s-largest-lng-plant-in-qatar?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 that a restart that could’ve taken weeks will now take months — leaving Asian 
buyers scrambling to make up the millions of tons of lost fuel.
Ras Laffan supplied about a fifth of the world’s LNG before the war began.
Photographer: SeongJoon Cho/Bloomberg
It’s a stark turnaround for the market. A host of new LNG projects around the 
world — including expansions at Ras Laffan — were scheduled to come online in 
2026, pushing supplies into a glut, reducing energy bills and boosting emerging 
economies such as India and Pakistan — both key importers.

Instead, these countries suddenly face higher inflation and a shift back to 
more-polluting fuels like coal.

The effects of the war are already rippling into markets such as agriculture 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-14/fuel-shortages-from-war-begin-to-threaten-global-food-supply?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
 — where gas is needed to make fertilizers — and even tech, where more 
expensive power in major player Taiwan will raiseprices for semiconductors 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/iran-war-chokepoints-begin-to-cast-doubt-on-global-chip-supply?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
.

With gas prices surging in Europe and Asia 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-19/gas-traders-weigh-attacks-at-world-s-largest-lng-plant-in-qatar?cmpid=BBBXT031926_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260319>
, LNG exporters far from the Middle East — such as the US and Russia — are 
poised to gain.

But faced with a tighter market, some developing economies may simply stop 
buying, making a victim of the industry itself.

—Dan Murtaugh, Bloomberg News

BNEF today

This week, an intense heat dome will send temperatures across the western US 
far above seasonal norms, with 38C (100F) forecast in some parts. The heat 
arrives earlier than expected for this time of year and may start melting an 
already meager snowpack,BloombergNEF <> says. That would jeopardize hydropower 
assets later in the year and set up the conditions for wildfires come summer.
