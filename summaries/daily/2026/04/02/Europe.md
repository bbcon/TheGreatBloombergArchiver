Europe's energy stress is intensifying on multiple fronts simultaneously. Diesel futures for the benchmark ICE Gasoil contract broke above $200 per barrel on Thursday — the highest since 2022 — as Hormuz disruptions compress refined product flows globally. The dynamic is counterintuitive on the surface: tankers that loaded diesel in UK waters are diverting cargoes to destinations as distant as Australia rather than discharging locally, while vessels originally heading toward Europe across the Atlantic have turned toward Africa. The explanation is straightforward: Asia and other regions are outbidding European buyers. The market appears to be signaling that if Europe wishes to attract sufficient diesel supply, benchmark prices will need to rise further still.

Compounding the imported energy shock is a domestic supply problem. Norway — which under normal conditions functions as Europe's hydropower reserve, exporting roughly 15% of its annual production — is emerging from its driest winter in decades. Snow reserves have fallen to their lowest levels in two decades, leaving an estimated 25 terawatt-hour energy deficit, equivalent to nearly a fifth of Norway's total hydropower output last year. Electricity exports to the UK have fallen approximately 50% year-on-year, and to Germany by around 40%, while Nordic prices have surged to more than four times 2025 levels in parts of the region. The shortfall arrives precisely when European gas markets are already under pressure from Persian Gulf supply disruptions, leaving the continent navigating a dual squeeze.

UK businesses are planning larger-than-normal price increases in response to surging energy costs, according to a Bank of England survey. ECB Governing Council member Fabio Panetta noted that the war's economic impact will persist even if hostilities conclude relatively soon, while stopping short of indicating a clear direction for interest rates — a posture that reflects the genuine policy dilemma facing the institution as inflation risks from energy collide with downside growth risks. The broader question of European defense funding also remains live, with NATO allies skeptical that Trump will follow through on withdrawal threats but increasingly concerned that the rhetoric itself is degrading alliance cohesion at a vulnerable moment.
