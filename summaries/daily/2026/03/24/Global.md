The central tension shaping global markets remains unresolved: whether a diplomatic off-ramp to the US-Iran conflict exists, and if so, how quickly it can materialize. Trump's Monday Truth Social post claiming "productive conversations" briefly sent Brent crude down approximately $17 per barrel to as low as $96 before prices rebounded toward $100–$102 as Iran's denial of any negotiations, combined with continued overnight missile exchanges, reasserted themselves. The episode illustrates a structural vulnerability: markets are now dependent on presidential social media for price discovery in one of the world's most systemically important commodity markets, a dynamic that carries obvious risks of manipulation and error.

The Strait of Hormuz question remains unresolved in a way that extends beyond any ceasefire. Roughly a fifth of global oil and LNG transits the waterway; a CBS report on Monday indicated approximately a dozen Iranian mines are now present in the strait, raising questions about the timeline and logistics of any reopening even in a post-ceasefire scenario. Control of Hormuz gives Iran a structural lever over global energy prices that will persist regardless of near-term political outcomes. Saudi tanker freight rates from Yanbu — the Red Sea alternative routing — have eased somewhat as capacity arrives, but remain well above pre-conflict levels.

Saudi Arabia and the UAE have reportedly taken concrete steps toward joining the conflict on the US-Israeli side, including Riyadh granting US military access to King Fahd Air Base — a reversal of its earlier position. Pakistan's army chief is simultaneously engaged in mediation efforts. The convergence of these dynamics suggests the conflict is at a genuinely uncertain inflection point: both escalation and de-escalation pathways remain live. Iran stopped natural gas exports to Turkey following an Israeli strike on the South Pars gas field, removing another supply route from global markets.

The macroeconomic transmission mechanism is broadening. Oil-driven inflation is now arriving in consumer prices, airline costs, food logistics, and industrial inputs across geographies from Italy to India to South Korea. The IEA has reported record stockpile withdrawals; the US is considering further SPR releases. Central banks globally face a stagflationary configuration — the ECB's Vujcic encapsulated it directly — that limits their conventional toolkit precisely when fiscal positions are also under stress.
