The UK economy delivered its strongest quarterly growth in a year, with GDP rising 0.6% in Q1 2026. The IT sector contributed meaningfully, and activity held modestly positive even in the early weeks of the Iran conflict. However, virtually all forecasters expect the pace to prove unsustainable. Bloomberg Economics projects near-stagnant growth over the remainder of the year as higher energy costs, tighter borrowing conditions, and political uncertainty compound one another.

The political situation deteriorated further. Health Secretary Wes Streeting is expected to resign from Cabinet to mount a leadership challenge against Prime Minister Keir Starmer, while former deputy prime minister Angela Rayner signaled she is preparing her own bid following clearance from a tax investigation. Markets have long discounted a degree of Starmer instability — Polymarket has placed the odds of his departure before year-end above 50% for months — which limits the marginal selloff from fresh headlines. Nevertheless, UK 30-year gilt yields rose to levels last seen in 1998, and the political noise is beginning to concern overseas bond buyers. The spread between gilts and Treasuries has tracked the oil price since the outbreak of hostilities, suggesting the Iran war may be a more proximate driver of funding costs than domestic politics alone.

The ECB's policy path also became less certain. Governing Council member Martins Kazaks warned that the data are pointing toward stagflation risks, and stated that a rate hike would be warranted if oil price increases feed through to inflation expectations. Two weeks after ECB President Lagarde signalled a tightening bias, a June move is now described as "less obvious" rather than imminent — reflecting the tension between above-target inflation and softening growth, a tension that Oxford Economics estimates could add 0.6 to 0.7 percentage points of additional inflation pass-through given elevated public inflation awareness.
