Straitened Circumstances

The plight of the Middle East continues to darken. The Strait of Hormuz 
<https://bloom.bg/44E90ll>, the critical Persian Gulf bottleneck that has been 
the nexus of the conflict for months, is effectively closed once more as 
military action between the US and Iran intensifies. The number oftankers 
<https://bloom.bg/3R5Wp7l> making the perilous crossing is back virtually to 
zero, and topped out at 25 — compared to an average of closer to 80 transits 
each day before the war broke out.

At the start of July, prediction markets thought that traffic would probably 
return to normal by the end of the month. Now that chance is seen — surely 
correctly — as close to zero. Not only is the situation bad, but it’s 
deteriorating:

Beyond that, Iran is now trying to create double jeopardy by leaning on its 
Houthi allies <https://bloom.bg/4h9lt87> in Yemen to try closing off a similar 
chokepoint for the Red Sea, the Bab el-Mandeb, which would affect the flow of 
Saudi oil exports. That would also — unlike Hormuz — potentially throttle 
container traffic bound for Suez. A hastily organized Polymarket contract now 
puts the chance at 19% that this will happen by the end of August, and thereby 
force traffic between Europe and Asia to go around the Cape of Good Hope. By 
year-end, the odds rise to 33%.

That sounds terrifying. Meanwhile, tariff tit-for-tat is back. The Trump 
administration is now talking about a 100% tariff on generic drugs, to follow 
Monday’s announcement of 50% duties on a range of Canadian goods, under a 
rationale it believes will pass muster with the Supreme Court. Last year’s 
Liberation Day levies briefly prompted all-out market panic, but the effective 
US tariff rate had been declining for months. This also had scope to alarm 
markets:

It’s reasonable to counter that President Donald Trump’s credibility is so 
badly shot that investors feel they can safely ignore him. And also that the 
Strait and tariffs are no longer regarded as binary financial risks. Both can 
be viewed on a continuum in a world where investors are resigned to steady 
de-globalization and the increasing costs that accompany it — counterbalanced 
by the artificial intelligence investment boom.

If there’s a remaining binary geopolitical risk, with straightforward bullish 
or bearish consequences, it concerns a Middle East escalation that puts 
American troops on the ground. That would make the issue far more politically 
divisive in the US, and likely lead to destruction of oil production facilities 
that restricts supply longer term.

Viewed through this prism, stocks’ reactions to the Iran war begin to make 
sense. The chance of a ground invasion was perceived to surge during the month 
of March, and the S&P 500 sold off hard. At the end of the month, some 
conciliatory words from Iran, and announcements of a bombing pause by Trump, 
turned sentiment around swiftly. The US didn’t have the appetite for a ground 
war, and neither did Iran, so it wouldn’t happen. The chances of escalation 
dropped on prediction markets, while the S&P started a great rally:

There’s one problem with this. It implies that the stock market is wrong now 
to ignore a sudden and undeniable rise in the risk of a ground campaign. A lot 
rests on what are perceived to be strong physical constraints, which make it 
even harder to go to war now than four months ago.Inventories kept for a rainy 
day <https://bloom.bg/4wiXfwM>, in the US, China, and elsewhere, have been 
tapped in a successful attempt to keep prices under control. But that can’t go 
on much longer. Every gallon of oil drawn down adds to the incentive to 
negotiate whatever it takes to quickly start the flow again. The US had already 
drained much of itsstrategic reserve <https://bloom.bg/3RS85uB> to get through 
the first year of the Ukraine war, and stocks are now their lowest since 1983:

This supports what BCA Research calls a “kinetic equilibrium” with the oil 
price between $70 and $90. The US and Iran had increasingly been “coloring 
inside the lines” drawn during the first month of the war, and confining 
attacks to military facilities.

Brent crude however, is now above $90 again, while longer-term prices — 
arguably more important — suggest growing concern:

The red lines have been crossed this week, with US attacks on bridges and 
Iranian attacks on desalination plants. But BCA’s Felix Vezina-Poirier argues 
that the kinetic equilibrium is intact: “Iran’s negotiating team is under 
growing pressure, while the approachingmidterms <https://bloom.bg/45fyYeW> are 
increasing pressure on the US administration.”

He argues that the best way to navigate the situation is to recognize that 
Iran, the US and Israel are conditioned by oil prices. “As prices approach the 
upper end, escalation gives way to de-escalation.”

Prices are back at the upper end now. That theory is about to be tested.

The Beachball in the Pool

Hold a beachball under water, and it leaps vertically above the surface once 
you remove the pressure. The same basic physics are now having much the same 
effect on Japanese bonds, with much less entertainment value.

The rise in JGB yields <https://bloom.bg/4wQjTfK> has been close to vertical 
since the long period of yield curve control — buying bonds to keep longer 
yields down — finally ended two years ago:

In isolation this might be tolerable, but the historic rise in yields has 
co-existed with a run on the currency. Early trading Wednesday brought the yen 
below 163 per dollar for the first time since 1986. The dollarexchange rate 
<https://bloom.bg/45i4fxS> possibly understates its weakness. On a broad basis, 
and taking into account that inflation in Japan has been lower than anywhere 
else (which all else equal should strengthen the yen), it has lost about 
two-thirds of its buying power over the last three decades:

Investors have grown used to treating Japan as an exception to all rules. That 
might now be a mistake. Mike Howell of Global Liquidity Indexes argues that the 
many other countries that have intervened to keep yields down (though less 
spectacularly than Japan) face the same problem:

The investment risk is an abrupt adjustment: Japan’s experience shows that 
once suppressed yields break higher, the move can overshoot as investors demand 
an additional risk premium.

Howell argues that longer-term bond yields are out of kilter with the current 
rate of nominal growth in gross domestic product, in the US and Europe as well 
as Japan.

If US yields followed their historic correlation with GDP growth, they would 
hit 6%, not the current 4.62%, he contends. Japan suggests such a swift move 
could happen, and it’s not comfortable to contemplate.

Robin Brooks of the Brookings Institution argues that Japanese yields have far 
further to rise. He bases his arguments on levels ofgovernment indebtedness 
<https://bloom.bg/4wNypVE>, which also has a clear relationship with the 
borrowing costs that markets impose. Japan stands out as a screaming outlier:

This chart excludes Greece, Italy and Spain, all of which have benefited from 
de facto caps on yields in the euro zone. Reinstating them would still leave 
Japanese yields more than 100 basis points too low.

Brooks also cites an infamous gaffe <https://bloom.bg/4fsaHqV> by European 
Central Bank President Christine Lagarde as a “natural experiment” to show how 
euro zone yields had relied on intervention. In March 2020, as the early stages 
of the pandemic put pressure on peripheral countries’ yields, Lagard said it 
was “not our job to close spreads.”

As Brooks shows, this was followed by a bond market revolt and in due course 
by concerted ECB measures to keep spreads tight. The point is that markets only 
consent to leave spreads where they are, and accept a low risk premium, because 
it’s clear that authorities will intervene to keep yields under control:

One obvious solution would come from monetary policy. Central banks could hike 
rates. Investors generally assume that all the main central banks are now in a 
hiking cycle in part because yields are artificially low given the strength of 
the economy.

Yield suppression, Howell argues, could become self-defeating: “Cheaper energy 
and lower yields stimulate nominal growth, which then increases pressure for 
both oil prices and yields to rise.” Rate hikes might head off this risk, and 
also avert more beachball-like reactions from bond yields in other countries. 
And indeed the ECB, which meets Thursday, is expected to tighten this year — 
but not this week:

But the onus has shifted to fiscal policy. Japan’s best way forward, Brooks 
suggests, might be through selling government assets to pay down debt — a 
decision for politicians, not central bankers. The US Treasury is keeping rates 
low by shifting to make most of its issuance over short terms (which reduces 
supply of longer bonds and compresses their yields). All run the same risk that 
if they keep the debt beachball below the surface too long, the bounce when it 
comes will be too much to handle.



 Survival Tips 



Daniel Ortega could give us all some tips on survival, but they wouldn’t be 
inspiring. Nicaragua’s 80-year-old president announced this week that he was
doing away with elections <https://bloom.bg/4wQgIVm>. These days he presides 
over as nasty and venal a personalized regime as any Latin America has seen — 
barely any better than theSomoza family dictatorship <https://bloom.bg/4b5GPiM> 
that his Sandinistas displaced. And despite the US revival of the Monroe 
Doctrine and keenness to intervene in Venezuela and Cuba, nobody seems to care.

Half a century ago, Ortega was considered a bold and romantic revolutionary in 
the eyes of enthusiastic acolytes in the West (and as a dangerous adversary by 
the Reagan administration, which poured money into guerrillas opposing him). 
Students (now targeted by Ortega <https://bloom.bg/4bGepMr>) named buildings 
after him, according him accolades otherwise reserved forNelson Mandela 
<https://bloom.bg/4fersXN>. Like Mandela <https://bloom.bg/4vBmnxr>, he 
inspired music. The Clash even released a triple album calledSandinista! It 
wasn’t their greatest effort, but it did includeThe Magnificent Seven 
<https://bloom.bg/4b8BI1b> (which hits differently these days), Hitsville UK 
<https://bloom.bg/4whIcU8>, and The Call Up <https://bloom.bg/3T8KxC9>. The 
music made by a group of angry young men is still thrilling — but unlike 
Mandela, the Sandinistas were unworthy of it.
