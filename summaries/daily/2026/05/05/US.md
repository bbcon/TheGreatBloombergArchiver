US equity markets remain remarkably resilient in the face of a deteriorating geopolitical backdrop. S&P 500 futures edged higher by roughly 0.3%, with the index holding near record highs despite the renewed Iran-related tensions that sent bond yields climbing on fresh inflation concerns. The durability of that performance owes much to the ongoing AI capital expenditure cycle: first-quarter earnings from the Magnificent Seven — with Nvidia still to report — have been broadly robust, with AI-driven investment in information-processing equipment rising 64% in real terms and software spending up 46% in Q1. All other capex declined 17%, underscoring that the AI trade remains the dominant force in US business investment.

Corporate buybacks are providing a structural floor. S&P 500 companies announced $665 billion in authorized repurchases in the first four months of the year, the highest pace on record to start any year, with full-year authorized repurchases projected at $1.55 trillion — potentially eclipsing last year's record. The buyback surge partially offsets a notable compression in shareholder returns via dividends and free cash flow, as hyperscalers redirect cash toward data-center buildouts. Wall Street projects free cash flow for the largest cloud platforms to fall more than 70% from peak by end-2026, even as earnings continue to climb — a tension that reflects accounting treatment of capex rather than underlying deterioration.

On the corporate front, Amazon's launch of Amazon Supply Chain Services — packaging its freight, trucking, air, ocean, and last-mile capabilities into a single commercial offering — represents a potentially significant competitive incursion into freight markets. Shares in FedEx, UPS, and Expeditors declined on the announcement, though Bloomberg Intelligence analysts characterize the near-term threat as incremental rather than structural. Elsewhere, Palantir shares fell after disappointing US commercial revenue, Duolingo tumbled on a soft bookings outlook, and Pinterest surged after results beat expectations. The Fed left rates unchanged last week, with internal divisions over the inflation outlook deepening as two-year inflation swaps have risen above 3%.
