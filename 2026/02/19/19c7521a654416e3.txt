Bill Gates abruptly scrapped his keynote <https://bloom.bg/4qPOTt1> at a 
marquee AI summit in New Delhi, bowing out just hours before he was set to take 
the stage. The Gates Foundation said the decision was made “after careful 
consideration” to keep the spotlight on the event’s priorities. The move comes 
amid renewed scrutiny of Gates’s past relationship with convicted sex offender 
Jeffrey Epstein, a connection Gates has previously described as a“huge mistake” 
<https://bloom.bg/4qOcudj> and one that foundation CEO Mark Suzman recently 
acknowledged had “sullied” the organization’s reputation,according to the 
Financial Times <https://bloom.bg/4qPOXZN>.

The timing is awkward. India’s AI summit <https://bloom.bg/4rpvkIZ>, a 
centerpiece of Prime Minister Narendra Modi’s push to position the country as a 
global tech heavyweight, opened this week with ambitions running high. Gates’s 
absence shifts attention, at least briefly, fromAI investment 
<https://bloom.bg/4kFOo38> and innovation to reputational risk management. The 
foundation’s India and Africa head will step in instead, while Microsoft has 
reiterated its own $50 billion commitment to expanding AI across the global 
south, a reminder that money and momentum behind the AI race continue apace.
-Jui Chakravorty



What You Need to Know Today



Meanwhile, Mukesh Ambani is putting serious muscle behind India’s AI ambitions,
pledging up to 10 trillion rupees <https://bloom.bg/4kFOo38> ($110 billion) 
over the next seven years to build out infrastructure across Reliance 
Industries and Reliance Jio.

Announced at New Delhi’s AI Impact Summit, the commitment plants Reliance 
firmly in the middle of a high-stakes tech spending spree that’s sweeping 
corporate India. The move follows rival Gautam Adani’s$100 billion AI pledge 
<https://bloom.bg/4rtzsrA> and Tata Group’s plans to partner 
<https://bloom.bg/4qPOTt1> with OpenAI, as the country courts $200 billion in 
AI-driven investment, turning the summit into more of a capital deployment 
parade.




If India’s tycoons are writing eye-popping checks for AI, the companies 
building the brains areraising bigger money <https://bloom.bg/40ia8IV> for 
themselves. OpenAI is close to locking in the first phase of a funding round 
expected to top $100 billion, a record-setting haul that could push its 
valuation past $850 billion, people familiar with the matter said.

Strategic backers including Amazon, SoftBank, Nvidia and Microsoft are set to 
anchor the round, some of the people said, as the ChatGPT maker gears up for
trillions in long-term infrastructure spending <https://bloom.bg/40mW1SA>. A 
second wave of capital from venture firms and sovereign funds is slated to 
follow, underscoring the fact that the arms build-up is about who can marshal 
the deepest pools of capital, fastest.
Tata Sons chairman Natarajan Chandrasekaran and Sam Altman. Source: Tata 
Group/Tata Group 



Former South Korean President Yoon Suk Yeol was sentenced to life in prison 
<https://bloom.bg/3ZNkcJR> after being found guilty of leading an insurrection 
over his shock 2024declaration of martial law <https://bloom.bg/4qV0xmw>, a 
move that plunged the country into its deepest political crisis in decades.

The Seoul Central District Court delivered the verdict after prosecutors 
sought the death penalty, arguing Yoon had threatened the constitutional order. 
His brief suspension of civilian rule led to impeachment and marked the first 
time a sitting South Korean president was arrested and indicted. Yoon, who 
denies wrongdoing and can appeal, had defended the decree as a necessary step 
against what he called pro–North Korea forces.




Japan’s markets are watching Prime Minister Sanae Takaichi’s upcoming picks 
for two open seats <https://bloom.bg/4kRPetS> on the Bank of Japan’s board for 
clues on how hard she plans to lean on monetary policy. Replacements for dovish 
member Asahi Noguchi and consensus voter Junko Nakagawa are expected to be 
proposed as soon as Feb. 25, people familiar with the matter said.

Investors are parsing whether Takaichi, long seen as pro-stimulus, will stack 
the panel with reflationists or opt for a steadier hand to avoid rattling the 
yen and bond market. While a like-for-like dove swap may not dramatically shift 
the board’s balance, the tone of her choices will signal how aggressively she 
intends to shape rate policy as inflation runs above target and as she cements 
her post–landslide mandate.
Junko Nakagawa Photographer: Kiyoshi Ota/Bloomberg 



Bank Indonesia left its benchmark rate unchanged at 4.75%, opting for caution 
<https://bloom.bg/4aqN4Oi> as the rupiah hovers near record lows and market 
volatility lingers. The widely expected pause underscores the central bank’s 
balancing act: defending a currency battered by dollar demand and downgrade 
concerns while supporting growth in Southeast Asia’s largest economy.

Governor Perry Warjiyo signaled stepped-up stabilization efforts and again 
nudged banks to pass through more rate cuts, even as loan growth picks up and 
the government pushes for faster expansion. With new leadership appointments 
and the rupiah among Asia’s worst performers this year, investors are likely to 
keep a close eye on both policy independence and the currency’s next move.

 For Your Commute 



Murder (fictional, of course) is  having a moment <https://bloom.bg/4azk2w1> 
in the travel industry. From Brooklyn black-box theaters to Hudson Valley inns 
and private islands in the Florida Keys, travelers are shelling out for 
immersive whodunit weekends that blend improv, role-play and luxury escape, 
writes Lauren Mechling <https://bloom.bg/4kMgpX1>.

Luxury travel adviser Black Tomato says it organized several White Lotus
–inspired weekends at the request of private clients over the past year, with 
prices ranging from the low $100,000s up to $1 million, depending on 
complexity, scale and location. Think, say, St. Barts or a castle in Scotland, 
while historic hotels report sellout crowds for cozy crime capers. The appeal 
spans generations but skews younger, with Gen Z and millennials eager to swap 
screen time for scripted sleuthing: donning costumes, chasing clues and, for a 
weekend at least, making “who did it?” the only notification that matters.
