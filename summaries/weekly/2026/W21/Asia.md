# Asia-Pacific Weekly Briefing: May 18–24, 2026

The week was defined by the collision of two macro forces shaping Asia: the ongoing Iran war and the energy shock emanating from a closed Strait of Hormuz, and the increasingly concentrated AI-driven rally in regional equity markets. The two narratives pulled in opposite directions, leaving policymakers and investors to navigate sharply divergent outcomes across the region.

China's economy showed broad-based weakness in April data released Monday, with retail sales rising just 0.2% year-on-year — the weakest reading since December 2022 — while industrial production expanded 4.1% and fixed-asset investment unexpectedly contracted 1.6% year-to-date. Car sales fell 15% and gold and jewelry sales declined 21%. The figures undershot every economist estimate surveyed by Bloomberg and prompted renewed calls for stimulus, though Beijing has thus far appeared reluctant to act. Separately, the previous week's Trump-Xi summit was reassessed by analysts as a "placeholder" exchange: China committed to $17 billion in annual US agricultural purchases through 2028 and to establishing trade and investment boards, but specifics remained vague, and the Boeing-related disappointment weighed on aircraft-linked names.

Putin's visit to Beijing on Tuesday-Wednesday delivered atmospherics but no announced breakthrough on the Power of Siberia 2 pipeline. Xi publicly urged against any resumption of US strikes on Iran, while Russia's leverage in the gas negotiations appeared diminished — China retains alternative supply via Central Asia and robust domestic coal and renewables, and Beijing has folded the pipeline into its five-year plan on its own terms.

The energy shock continued to ripple through the region. Japan's PM Takaichi reversed her earlier stance and instructed the finance ministry to compile an extra budget of roughly $19 billion, including revived energy subsidies; the 30-year JGB yield surged as much as 20 basis points Monday to its highest level since the tenor's 1999 debut. Japan's Q1 GDP, reported Tuesday, surprised to the upside at 2.1% annualized. Friday's CPI print showed core inflation slowing to a four-year low, complicating the BOJ's path. Thailand announced a 200 billion baht ($6.1 billion) cost-of-living package. India's rupee approached 97 per dollar, prompting the RBI to weigh rate hikes, FX swaps, and overseas dollar-raising measures reminiscent of its 2013 taper-tantrum playbook. Foreign outflows from Indian equities accelerated, and Reliance's Jio IPO hit roadblocks tied to war-driven volatility.

Indonesia delivered the week's largest policy surprise. Bank Indonesia hiked the policy rate 50 basis points Wednesday to 5.25% — double consensus — to defend the rupiah. Hours earlier, President Prabowo announced that exports of palm oil, thermal coal, and ferro-alloys (totaling over $65 billion annually) would be routed through a new state-controlled entity under sovereign wealth fund Danantara. The Jakarta Composite fell 3.5% Tuesday and is down roughly 26% year-to-date, the world's worst major benchmark. Moody's flagged risks to confidence, and even senior Danantara officials were reportedly blindsided.

The contrast with North Asian equities was stark. South Korea's Kospi, up 73% year-to-date and the world's best performer, fell more than 3% Tuesday before surging 8.4% Thursday after Samsung reached an eleventh-hour deal with its union — averting a strike — and Nvidia's earnings catalyzed a regional tech rally. Samsung chip employees stand to receive average bonuses of approximately $340,000. Taiwan's Taiex rose 3.4% Thursday; SK Hynix gained nearly 8%, pushing its market value toward $910 billion. Korean and Japanese export data for the first 20 days of May showed chip shipments up 202% and 44% respectively year-on-year, evidence that the AI capex cycle continues to insulate North Asian trade from the energy shock — though concentration risk is acute, with Samsung and SK Hynix accounting for two-thirds of the Kospi's 2026 gain.
