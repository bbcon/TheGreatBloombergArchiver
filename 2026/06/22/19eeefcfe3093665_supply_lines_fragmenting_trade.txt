China is warning that supply chains may become disconnected as key trading 
partnerstake steps 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/china-warns-of-fragmented-supply-chains-as-trade-tensions-grow?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
 to fix growing trade imbalances and reduce rare earths dependencies.

“Unilateralism and protectionism are on the rise, the risk of global supply 
chain fragmentation is growing,” Chinese Vice Premier Ding Xuexiang said during 
a speech at the China International Supply Chain Expo’s opening ceremony in 
Beijing on Monday.

China’s growing trade surplus with key trading partners has come in focus in 
recent months. Among those wising up to the imminent economic threats is the 
European Union, which is contemplatingnew measures 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-03/left-with-few-choices-eu-braces-for-a-trade-fight-with-china?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
 to counter Asia’s export powerhouse.

Explainer: Why Rare Earths Are China’s Trump Card in Trade War 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/how-xi-is-using-rare-earths-against-trump-in-the-us-china-trade-war?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>

Last week the Group of Seven countries — including four European nations, the 
US, Japan and Canada —agreed 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-17/g7-aims-to-ensure-china-supplies-no-more-than-60-of-rare-earths?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
 that no single country should supply more than 60% of their imports of rare 
earths by 2030 in an effort to reduce their reliance on China.

In Washington, the Trump administration is reconfiguring a tariff regime that 
will replace the one the Supreme Court struck down in February.

The US president imposed temporary 10% duties that expire on July 24, so the 
next 30 days could see a flurry of tariff announcements using more legally 
tested authorities and reshuffling the list ofwinners and losers 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/trump-builds-a-new-us-tariff-wall-in-shakeup-of-winners-losers?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
.

Export Bans

In April, China announced it would set up a sweeping new mechanism 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-07/china-issues-new-supply-chain-rules-targeting-foreign-disruption?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
 to ensure the security of its supply chains. The rules give government 
agencies the authority to start security probes against foreign entities if 
they adopt discriminatory bans or carry out actions that harm the security of 
the country’s supply chains.

On the issue of rare earths and critical minerals, a statement Monday from 
China’s Commerce Ministry underscored the tensions and served as a reminder of 
Beijing’s leverage.

The statement said China is imposing export controls 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-22/china-places-two-us-rare-earths-producers-on-export-control-list?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
 on two US rare earth producers that are part of Washington’s effort to 
establish alternative supply chains for minerals critical to advanced 
manufacturing and defense.

MP Materials and USA Rare Earth have been added to China’s export control 
list, which bans Chinese exporters from shipping items to these companies that 
can be used for commercial and military purposes. China also placed eight other 
US companies, including drone makers and robotics and aerospace firms, on the 
export control list.

Recent data also showed China’s exports of some critical minerals to Japan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-20/china-s-key-minerals-exports-to-japan-stay-at-low-levels-in-may?cmpid=BBD062226_TRADE&utm_campaign=trade&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260622&utm_content=5748>
 remained at low levels, extending a downturn amid a political dispute between 
the two countries. Exports of some key types of tungsten, as well as 
rare-earths dysprosium and terbium, stayed at zero in May, according to Chinese 
customs data.
