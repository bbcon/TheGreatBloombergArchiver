The Conundrum Continues

Rest in Peace Alan Greenspan, who has passed away at 100 
<https://bloom.bg/3QgAdXJ>. One of the greatest and most contentious figures 
<https://bloom.bg/3QwHbrG> in modern economic history, his legacy will 
doubtless be contested for at least another century. As it stands, the two 
decades since he stood down after 18 years at the Federal Reserve saw first an 
extraordinary reversal of once nearly mythical standing, and in recent years 
signs that he is regaining favor.

His legacy is not purely a matter of history. Greenspan didn’t give press 
conferences or offer dot plots, and deliberately spoke in a convoluted way that 
committed him to nothing. Current Fed Chair Kevin Warsh wants to return to a 
similar communication policy.

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent has resurrected Greenspan’s decision to keep 
rates low as the bull market boomed in the late 1990s, with the aim of
bolstering the case for rate cuts <https://bloom.bg/4eXTF4L> now:

The Fed needs to have merely an open mind. The open-mind maestro, former Fed 
Chairman Alan Greenspan, resisted premature rate hikes during the technology 
boom of the 1990s — and history proved him right.

Has it? Here is a first take, in charts.

The Mandate

It’s indisputable that the Greenspan Fed fulfilled every part of its mandate 
of 1) low inflation, plus 2) low unemployment, with 3) low long-term interest 
rates. All three improved during his chairmanship:

The Economy

Greenspan’s own memoir was called The Age of Turbulence 
<https://bloom.bg/4g6cUud>, but US economic growth was anything but turbulent. 
Apart from a brief and shallow recession after Saddam Hussein’s invasion of 
Kuwait in 1990, the Greenspan Fed managed to avoid a single quarter of falling 
year-on-year GDP, even after the dot-com bust. Productivity improved, thanks to 
factors beyond monetary policy (globalization, the advent of the internet, and 
an influx of Mexican migrants), and the Fed did nothing to thwart it:

The Bubble

The turning point came a decade into his chairmanship. Until then, Greenspan 
was seen as a continuation of his predecessor, the inflation hawk Paul Volcker. 
He even provoked a bear market in bonds in 1994. In December 1996, he made a 
speech worrying about“irrational exuberance” <https://bloom.bg/4oME0J1> in 
stocks, and followed with a rate hike that drove a brief correction.

But then he mused publicly about whether there was a “new economy.” 
<https://bloom.bg/4fVoM1Y> In the fall of 1998, Russia’s debt default caused 
the meltdown of the Long-Term Capital Management hedge fund. Greenspan blinked, 
organized a bailout for LTCM (to Volcker’spublic disgust 
<https://bloom.bg/4w3h0I3>), and cut rates. A stock market that looked to have 
peaked instead melted upward. In late 1999, worried by the“Y2K bug,” 
<https://bloom.bg/4epPiPZ> the Fed pumped more liquidity, before finally 
cutting off the spigot and bursting the bubble. Then it drastically reduced 
rates again. Has history really proved Greenspan right about this, as Bessent 
suggests?

Another Bubble

Money is fungible. Funds to ease the economic impact of falling stock markets 
found their way into housing finance. As mortgage rates continued to fall, a 
historic bubble in house prices formed, which peaked on the eve of Greenspan’s 
departure in 2006. Everyone knows what happened next:

Moral Hazard

More damagingly, from LTCM onward a new phrase joined the market lexicon: the 
“Greenspan Put.” The idea was that 401(k)s and high asset prices in general had 
become so critical to the economy that the Fed would let the stock market drive 
rates. And indeed, circumstantial evidence points that way. The sense was that 
any fall in stocks would be greeted by lower rates, and that created what’s 
known as moral hazard — the recklessness that comes when actors believe they 
will be bailed out from the worst consequences of their actions:

The Conundrum

Greenspan’s last years were dominated by what he labelled a “conundrum” 
<https://bloom.bg/3SnK6U6>; the Fed hiked short-term rates repeatedly, but the 
10-year Treasury yield scarcely ever budged. That helped to exacerbate the 
housing finance bubble. That conundrum set in after China’s accession to the 
World Trade Organization in 2001. Its buildup of reserves, mostly in US 
Treasuries, didn’t help:

Greenspan was an extraordinary human being <https://bloom.bg/4eIsmKx>. His 100 
years on the planet cannot be reduced to a few charts. But they make clear his 
legacy’s relevance to the world we now inhabit, and can structure a vital 
debate as Warsh takes his chair.

In 2000, when the economy was booming, the doyen of Washington journalists, 
Bob Woodward, published a book with the self-explanatory title:Maestro: 
Greenspan’s Fed and the American Boom <https://bloom.bg/4eqZzLM>. He asserted 
<https://bloom.bg/4eB1U5t> that all the roads to explaining the great American 
economy of the 1990s led to Greenspan. He was right about that. But did those 
roads lead from that boom to the ensuing bust? That’s the enduring question 
about Greenspan’s tenure and we still need to answer it.

Keir We Go Again…

Keir Starmer <https://bloom.bg/43QM2ae> has resigned as Britain’s prime 
minister. Virtually all doubt over both his fate and the identity of his 
successor was extinguished first by Andy Burnham’s startlingly strong 
performance inlast week’s Makerfield byelection <https://bloom.bg/4b3ApAq>, and 
then Monday by the news that Wes Streeting, the other most plausible candidate, 
was not running. Both Streeting and Starmer yielded to what has swiftly become 
inevitable. Once he had cleared the considerable obstacles of finding a seat in 
Parliament (when the sitting Makerfield MP resigned in his favor), and beating 
the resurgent Reform party in the byelection (whichhe did with surprising ease 
<https://bloom.bg/3SfhkVI>), Burnham went from long shot to certainty:

Markets betrayed some nerves about this last week, as Burnham is positioning 
himself somewhat to Starmer’s left. Hischoice of chancellor 
<https://bloom.bg/4xZ4oUi> is important. But there’s no political uncertainty 
now, and both gilts and sterling suggest there is no crisis:

Burnham is a more natural communicator and politician than Starmer. But the 
terrifying example ofwhat the gilts market did to former Prime Minister Liz 
Truss <https://bloom.bg/4eYSJ00> in 2022 makes it hard for Burnham 
<https://bloom.bg/3Sfho7U> to engineer significant changes, for good or bad, 
and investors appear to recognize that. In short-run financial terms, this is 
probably not that big a deal.

In the longer term, Starmer is now the sixth prime minister to resign since 
the Brexit referendum, whose10th anniversary falls today 
<https://bloom.bg/4w54JTE>. Only one of them — Rishi Sunak in 2024 — was 
dismissed as the direct result of a general election.Brexit has failed to 
deliver <https://bloom.bg/4w54JTE> any improvements to date, but the defenders 
of leaving the European Union can still blame this on the hideous mess made by 
the political class. That shambles continues, and not just in the UK, as 
long-established parties of the center-left and right are in trouble across the 
western world. It can’t carry on indefinitely.


Only the Beginning

Emerging-market central banks know they can’t celebrate the breakthrough in 
Middle East peace talks too soon. Developments in recent days showed the 
ceasefire remains fragile, even as oil markets sent an encouraging message.

Brent crude is currently up only 7% since before the conflict began. As 
transit through theStrait of Hormuz ramps up <https://bloom.bg/4w8rFBt>, 
policymakers still need to know how much damage the price spike did, and how 
long its effects will linger. The fallout won’t simply disappear. The 
pandemic-era experience with supply chain disruptions shows that shocks can 
outlive the events that caused them. As things stand, though, the oil market 
should calm nerves:

Ripple effects have sent consumer prices rising across emerging markets 
<https://bloom.bg/4xzZmx8>. Inflation remains well below the post-pandemic 
extremes, but those scars have yet to heal fully:

Cheaper oil should ease pressure, particularly for oil-importers like India, 
but the balance of risks still points toward higher inflation. As William 
Jackson of Capital Economics points out, whether central banks respond to this 
depends on their credibility, the tightness of the current monetary stance, and 
the extent that inflation deviates above target. Simon MacAdam, also with 
Capital Economics, highlights the lagged effects and notes that record 
increases in jet fuel prices have yet to be fully passed on to consumers 
through airfares:

Regarding natural gas, MacAdam notes that there’s often a three-month lag 
before the spike shows up in the prices of gas piped to homes. In economies 
where natural gas is a key price-setter in power markets, it takes an average 
of four months to boost electricity prices. The full extent of the disruption 
is yet to be felt. A similar case can be made for food inflation, whichPoints 
of Return <https://bloom.bg/3QUOHwA> has noted is likely to rise. A fifth of 
global exports of ammonia-based fertilizer come from the Gulf:

In addition to the Strait’s closure having disrupted these supplies, higher 
natural gas prices have increased production costs for fertilizers elsewhere. 
In conjunction with higher energy costs, higher fertilizer prices have gone 
hand-in-hand with higher food commodity prices.

Broader impacts vary across countries and regions. Jackson points out that 
healthcare inflation has accelerated in many economies, typically reflecting 
regulatory decisions. Food prices are rising in Asia, potentially as a result 
of domestic harvests. EM central banks historically tend to respond swiftly to 
inflation. That has fresh impulse from the US: Last week’s Federal Open Market 
Committee unexpectedly tilted more hawkish, with half of the members expecting 
to hike at least once this year, whileWarsh emphasized 
<https://bloom.bg/4eIshqd> restoring price stability. 

Deutsche Bank’s Henry Allen suggests markets had a relatively muted response 
to the deal because investors had always priced in a temporary conflict:

Any upside from a resolution was always going to be limited. Notwithstanding 
the clarity that the truce brings, central bankers know that the hard part is 
only just beginning.

—Richard Abbey
