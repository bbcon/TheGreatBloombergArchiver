**Hormuz: Divergent Recovery**
The divergence between oil and LNG markets illustrates the uneven character of post-conflict normalization. Crude flows through the Strait of Hormuz recovered to above 10 million barrels per day under US military escort, with Citigroup now forecasting Brent could reach $60 per barrel by year-end as Goldman Sachs projects the global market swinging back into oversupply. LNG is a materially different story: Qatar extended force majeure clauses on shipments to Asia and Europe, a fire at Ras Laffan complicated plant restarts, and an estimated 8,000 seafarers remain stranded in the Persian Gulf. Shell warned sustained disruption could produce the first annual decline in global LNG exports in over a decade. The ceasefire remains fragile — Iran simultaneously reiterated sovereign control over the strait, and approximately 80 mines may remain in the main traffic separation scheme. Leading European powers and Gulf Arab officials are privately accepting that transit fees to Iran and Oman may become a structural feature of global energy trade, a consequential normalization if it consolidates.

**Trade Fragmentation**
Trade policy fragmentation accelerated across multiple bilateral axes simultaneously. The EU began imposing a €3 flat duty on sub-€150 imports on Wednesday, targeting Chinese e-commerce platforms; Chinese cars surpassed 10% of new European purchases for the first time. Trump threatened 100% tariffs on countries imposing digital services taxes on US technology companies, one day after the EU-US trade accord received final sign-off. The EU-China October deadline to resolve trade disagreements, set at Monday's Brussels meeting, provides a near-term focal point.
