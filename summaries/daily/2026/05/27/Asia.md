**Semiconductor Wealth Creation**

Asia's chipmakers are among the principal beneficiaries of the current AI investment cycle. SK Hynix's crossing of the $1 trillion threshold — following Samsung's earlier milestone — reflects the company's dominant position in high-bandwidth memory, a component for which demand is expected to outstrip supply through at least 2027. That pricing power translates directly into earnings revisions that analysts are still catching up to. Samsung's workforce, meanwhile, accepted a wage agreement providing an average bonus of approximately $340,000 per chip worker, averting a strike that would have threatened near-term production.

**Hong Kong's Wealth Management Resurgence**

Hong Kong's emergence as the world's leading offshore wealth hub is a notable reversal from the narrative of decline that dominated commentary on the city several years ago. The $2.9 trillion in cross-border assets managed there — up 11% year-on-year — reflects both mainland Chinese capital seeking diversification and the recovery in the city's IPO market, which saw the Hang Seng rise 28% last year. Boston Consulting Group projects the gap between Hong Kong and Switzerland will widen to $600 billion by 2030, underpinned by Asia's wealth accumulation trajectory and China's manufacturing base.

**China's AI Infrastructure Ambition**

ByteDance's reported discussions around $70 billion in capital expenditure for 2026 signal a more aggressive posture from Chinese technology companies in the global AI race. This comes alongside Huawei's announcement of a "Logic Folding" breakthrough aimed at producing competitive AI processors without access to leading-edge Western equipment. Whether these initiatives translate into genuine competitive parity with US semiconductor leaders remains to be seen, but the investment commitment is substantial and has been interpreted by markets as further validation of the AI infrastructure supercycle thesis.
