Hi, this is Philip Glamann in Beijing, where President Donald Trump and 
Chinese leader Xi Jinpingwrapped up their summit 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-15/trump-and-xi-begin-second-day-of-talks-in-beijing-s-power-center?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
. The question now is how successful these roughly 36 hours have been.

The optics were certainly good, with the event high on pageantry and 
showmanship 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-05-14/a-day-of-bonhomie-in-beijing-keeps-us-china-trade-truce-intact?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
. On Friday, Xi hosted Trump at the ruling Communist Party’s Zhongnanhai 
compound. Video of that encounter showed Xi pointing out trees, while Trump 
complimented the roses.

The gathering followed a day of spectacle that started with goose-stepping 
soldiers and a marching band, involved a trip to a grand temple, and then 
wrapped up with a lavish state dinner and warm toasts.

Oh, and the leaders of the world’s biggest economies sat down for talks to 
sort through the issues that have strained ties. The two sides also signed off 
on a few deals, pacts that seemed to fall short of some of the more optimistic 
predictions entering the week. At least until more details are disclosed.
President Donald Trump and Chinese leader Xi Jinping at Zhongnanhai on May 15.
Photographer: Evan Vucci/Pool/AFP/Getty Images
Beijing agreed to buy 200 Boeing planes, Trump said. That marked a rare win 
for the US planemaker in China but fell short of the 500 737 Max and additional 
widebody aircraft that airlines were expected to buy at the upper end. 
Highlighting how that was a disappointment to investors,shares of Boeing fell 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/boeing-wins-200-jet-plane-order-from-china-trump-says?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
 afterward.

And Xi did indicate that the two sides agreed to stabilize economic and trade 
relations — a sign that the one-year trade truce reached in South Korea last 
fall remains in place. The two sides will have a chance to bolster ties in 
September if Xi takes Trump up on his invite to visit the White House then.

But there’s also reason to think it wasn’t a 12 out of 10, as Trump described 
talks in Busan in October last year. Despite the public displays of 
conviviality and deals this time around, there’s still reason for concern about 
China-US ties.

For one thing, there was very little clear progress on hot-button issues like 
tensions in the Middle East andrare earths — the materials so crucial to the 
American economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/pentagon-aims-to-break-china-rare-earths-grip-with-critical-minerals-plan?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
.

Trump did say he talked to Xi about Iran, a nation that sells most of its oil 
exports to China. And Beijing did repeat its call for the fighting to end, 
adding that the Strait of Hormuz should reopen. But there’s no reason to 
believe the standoff that has caused the biggest energy crisis in decades is 
any closer to a resolution.

And, most notably, China sent a pointed message on Taiwan 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/xi-s-threat-to-trump-cements-taiwan-as-top-risk-to-us-china-ties?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
, the democracy of 23 million that it has vowed to bring under its control, by 
force if necessary.

If the Taiwan question is “mishandled,” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-14/trump-meets-xi-in-china-for-summit-as-tensions-over-iran-simmer?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
 Xi told Trump, “the two nations will experience collision or even clashes, 
pushing the entire China-US relationship into a highly dangerous situation.”

Whether that is just elevated rhetoric or a real warning that could badly 
upset the relationship is hard to know right now. But we’ll find out soon.

Before Trump’s trip, the US apparently held off on a $14 billion arms package 
for Taiwan to make sure everything went well — and few things irk Beijing like 
those weapons deals.

Lawmakers in the hub for high-end chips have already set aside the money for 
the arms, and a group of US senators recently prodded Trump to advance the 
deal, calling it “vital to our own national interests.”

If that deal goes down in the coming weeks and months, we’ll find out what Xi 
meant exactly with his warning to Trump, and if this summit was worth all the 
hoopla.

The $3 Trillion Risk

China’s exact level of bad loans — money that borrowers aren’t paying back to 
banks — has always been something of a mystery.

The official rate is 1.5%, though most economists say the ratio is much higher.

An analyst at Absolute Strategy Research in London estimates the figure at 
around 10%,meaning a whopping $3 trillion in loans 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-12/china-s-3-trillion-of-hidden-bad-debt-prolongs-economic-pain?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
 should be classified as past due but are not.

Others say it could be double that amount.

Take Tom Hu, who should be in default on a $730,000 bank loan for his plastics 
business in Zhejiang province, near Shanghai. He barely brings in enough 
revenue to pay expenses and can’t cover the debt costs, largelybecause of weak 
domestic demand 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-30/china-s-factory-activity-expands-despite-pressure-from-iran-war?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
 and volatile exports.

But his lender is letting him defer payments. That keeps him afloat and avoids 
another past-due loan on the bank’s books.

Similar scenarios are playing out across the world’s second-largest economy. 
This is largely the result of credit extended to companies whose earnings don’t 
cover interest payments. About 10% of listed non-financial firms have failed to 
cover those payments from their earnings before interest and tax for three 
straight years, according to Absolute Strategy Research.

The bad loan issue “could have led to a financial crisis if not for the 
forbearance policies and the government’s intervention,” said May Yan, head of 
Asia financials research at UBS Group. The fact that China is not in crisis 
speaks to the success of these measures, she said.

In other words, rather than cracking down on deadbeat borrowers, China’s banks 
are encouraged to cut them some slack. Regulators have for years urged the big 
banks to keep their reported bad loan ratio under 2%, according to people 
familiar with the guidance.
 <https://www.bloomberg.com/games/pointed>
With the forbearance policy — a legacy of Covid support programs that’s been 
extended to property developers and other firms — Beijing is signaling its 
desire tomaintain financial stability 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-06/china-s-new-push-against-rat-race-what-s-changed-versus-2025?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
. It wants to avoid a slew of bank failures that would follow a surge in 
reported bad credits and company defaults.

The leniency also extends to underwater mortgages, in which loans are worth 
more than the home itself.

The strategy carries a trade-off. Financial resources are trapped in 
unprofitable and even inactive firms, hindering banks’ ability to promote 
growth in healthy businesses. Overall loan growth is slowing significantly 
after fixed-asset investment experienced an unprecedented contraction last year.

For Victor Shih, an associate professor at the University of California San 
Diego who’s written a book on China finance, it’s a clear case of moral hazard, 
where entities take on excessive risk knowing there are no real consequences.

“The banks are not lending on the basis of commercial viability of the 
debtors’ profile,” he said. “Banks are lending because the government has told 
them to lend, and that will just generate a lot of bad assets.”

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

Chinese smartphone maker Oppo landed in hot water this week over a Mother’s 
Day ad that triggered public anger.

The marketing campaign was intended to celebrate modern mothers who live 
beyond traditional stereotypes. Instead, it backfired spectacularly, drawing 
accusations that it mocked family values and promoted inappropriate behavior.

“My mom has two husbands. One is my dad. The other she sees twice a year,” the 
ad went. The line was a cheeky reference to Chinese fan culture, where people 
often refer to celebrities as their “husbands.”

“She barely dresses up for dates with my dad but for the other one, she’d wear 
a wedding dress if she could,” the ad continued.

Many internet users thought the joke went too far. “Isn’t this openly 
promoting spiritual infidelity?” one Weibo user asked in a post representative 
of online sentiment.
One of Oppo’s smartphone offerings on a store shelf.Photographer: Dhiraj 
Singh/Bloomberg
The backlash was swift. Oppo pulled the ad, issued repeated apologies and 
demoted several executives, including a senior VP in charge of the China market.

“For a company of this size to exhibit such serious issues with its core 
values, the demotion of the person in charge is actually very lenient,” a Weibo 
user wrote in a comment that drew more than 3,000 likes.

Internet users also targeted an Oppo employee who reportedly led the ad 
campaign, prompting her alma mater, Wuhan University, to publicly distance 
itself from her.

Official bodies, including the China Advertising Association and China Women’s 
News (the official newspaper of the All-China Women’s Federation) also weighed 
in, warning advertisers not to cross ethical boundaries.

The controversy underscores how fraught discussions about gender have become 
in China 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2021-09-20/china-slams-effeminate-men-in-xi-s-mounting-push-for-conformity?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
, where the ruling Communist Party has increasingly promoteda traditional view 
of how women should live 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2022-11-03/china-tells-women-to-respect-family-values-in-revised-law?cmpid=BBD051526_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260515&utm_content=4426>
 and urged them to uphold what it calls “family values.”

Some users said the ad campaign failed badly in what the company said was an 
attempt to present “a more diverse and multi-dimensional portrayal of 
modern-day mothers.”

“Instead of empowering, it belittled mothers by portraying them as infatuated 
fangirls obsessed with a ‘virtual husband,’” a person wrote online. “It offends 
not only the multifaceted nature of motherhood but also all women with 
independent intellectual pursuits.”
