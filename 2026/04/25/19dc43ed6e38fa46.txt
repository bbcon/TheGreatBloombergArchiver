The narrative of the private citizen who becomes alarmed at how things look 
upon taking power is an old one — arguably going back to whenDamocles 
<https://www.livius.org/sources/content/cicero/tusculan-deputations/damocles/> 
sat himself down on a Greek throne. That’s not, however, been the experience of 
Guillermo Tolosa.

Before he took the helm of Uruguay’s central bank 
<bbg://news/stories/TDV2HTT9NJLY> last year, Tolosa, 50, worked for years at 
the International Monetary Fund mapping out risk scenarios across the global 
economy. But when he arrived at the central bank, “the reality on the ground I 
found to be much, much more rosy,” he says. Even amid the energy crisis brought 
on by the Iran war, economies in Latin America and many other emerging markets 
have been taking things in stride, he says.

Part of that is thanks to monetary policymakers having fought hard to build 
credibility as stewards of price stability. As this newsletter noted in 2023, a
watershed moment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2023-01-06/what-s-happening-in-the-world-economy-emerging-markets-turn-optimistic?cmpid=BBD042526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260425&utm_content=3799>
 came during the pandemic price surge, when Latin American central banks moved 
ahead of the Federal Reserve to hike interest rates. That helped the region 
withstand what became an historically steep US tightening cycle without a hint 
of the debt crises such moves used to cause.

In the old days, big Fed rate hikes or some other source of turmoil would 
trigger immediate capital flight from developing nations. Central banks would 
scramble to halt disorderly exchange-rate plunges with sudden rate hikes, only 
adding to the pain for their economies. “Our recessions could quickly escalate 
into afull-blown crisis 
<https://www.piie.com/commentary/testimonies/latin-american-economic-crisis>. 
This was the pattern in Latin America for a long time,” Tolosa says.“Now we can 
finally have normal recessions.”

And that opens up a whole new set of possibilities for policymakers and for 
foreign investors, as detailed below.

Being confronted with just an economic downturn, rather than an outright 
crisis, means “now we have the luxury — and this is the first time for Uruguay 
— that we can have counter-cyclical monetary policy,” Tolosa said in an April 
17 interview in Washington during the IMF/World Bank spring meetings. In other 
words, to help the economy by easing policy.

Tolosa and his fellow board members this week halted a series of interest-rate 
cuts, noting theuncertain landscape 
<https://www.bcu.gub.uy/Comunicaciones/Paginas/Detalle-Noticia.aspx?noticia=504&title=El-BCU-mantiene-la-Tasa-de-Pol%C3%ADtica-Monetaria-en-5,75%>
 in the wake of the Middle East war that’s sent global inflationary pressures 
higher. Even so, with the pace of Uruguayan price increases running below 3% — 
theslowest since 1956 
<https://www.riotimesonline.com/uruguay-inflation-hits-70-year-low-at-3-1/> — 
and the benchmark rate at 5.75%, that means a negative real rate.

While Uruguay’s peso did decline in the immediate aftermath of the US-Israel 
attack on Iran, it stabilized by the end of last month, and is on course to 
appreciate in April.

“You don’t see the type of violent depreciations that we have observed in the 
past” in emerging markets, Tolosa said, describing relative exchange-rate 
stability as “fantasyland” for developing-nation policymakers. “Now we can be 
actually helping to stabilize the economy like an advanced-country central 
banker would.”

Emerging-market nations’ currencies aren’t just more stable because of their 
work in areas including central bank credibility, he added. On the other side 
of the ledger, things are now “probably not that risk free in the advanced 
world” as previously perceived, he highlighted.

Tolosa didn’t spell out his thinking on that shift, but some have warned about 
the US and other wealthy countries running unsustainable fiscal deficits. The 
IMF warned this month that US Treasuries are losingsome of the premium 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-15/imf-says-treasuries-losing-premium-warns-us-on-debt-management?cmpid=BBD042526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260425&utm_content=3799>
 they once commanded, due to outsize borrowing. The Fed’s independence to fight 
inflation also has come under a cloud thanks to President Donald Trump’s 
regular attacks on the institution.

Without having a crisis to contain, what Tolosa is particularly keen on is 
developing his central bank’s soft power — ingraining in the broader public’s 
mind the idea that monetary policymakers will do what it takes to maintain 
price stability. He tells his staff they need to become “expectations 
engineers” and “narrative entrepreneurs” as they go out and tell the central 
bank’s story.
Notes: BCU is the Central Bank of Uruguay. IT is the inflation target
Among the critical stakeholders is Uruguay’s government, with Tolosa crediting 
President Yamandu Orsi, who tapped him for the job. Orsi’s administration has 
sought faster growth with low inflation, marking amajor shift 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-31/uruguay-s-new-central-bank-chief-vows-firm-hand-with-inflation?cmpid=BBD042526_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260425&utm_content=3799>
 compared with previous governments formed by his party that tolerated bigger 
price increases. Tolosa said it’s vital the central bank explains to the 
government why it’s making the decisions it does.

Uruguay also benefits from mutual reinforcement with regional neighbors, he 
said. Chile was the “first mover” to contain inflation. Brazil and Colombia are 
each also “a success story for us,” he says. As foreign companies come to 
realize that Latin American economies are “not as volatile as in the past,” 
that will unlock greater direct investment, he said. (Argentina, still 
struggling to achieve price stability, would of course be a notable exception.)

Stable inflation and interest rates also will give companies and households 
the confidence to borrow, propelling growth, said Tolosa, who described Uruguay 
as under-leveraged.

Tolosa’s not alone in being upbeat on the region. The IMF’s latest 
projections, released earlier this month, show South American annual economic 
growth averaging more than 2.5% from 2027 through 2031, after the region 
sustained in excess of 2% GDP gains each year going back to 2021. Such a decade 
worth of solid growth would mark anotable improvement 
<https://data.imf.org/en/Data-Explorer?datasetUrn=IMF.RES:WEO(9.0.0)> from past 
volatility.

Tolosa sums it up: “There’s never been a better time to be a central banker in 
Latin America.”—Chris Anstey
