China’s first long-range test 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-06/china-holds-rare-test-of-sub-launched-missile-in-pacific-ocean?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 of a submarine-launched intercontinental ballistic missile into the Pacific 
Ocean this week caused alarm for its neighbors. Japan’s top government 
spokesman expressed “grave concern,” and Australian Foreign Minister Penny Wong 
said it was “destabilizing to the region.”

Though the test adds to tensions 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/australia-slams-china-test-of-nuclear-capable-missile-in-pacific?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 among countries in East Asia as China develops and fields new weapons, the 
strongest signal was probably directed at the US, rather than its Pacific 
allies.

Countries like Japan and Australia were already at risk from China’s large 
arsenal of tried and tested medium- and long-range missiles.

With the latest trial, Beijing is showing that it has — or is at least closing 
in on — a new way to hit back at the US homeland with a nuclear weapon if it is 
attacked first.
A long-range ballistic missile rises out of the sea during a test from a 
Chinese nuclear-powered submarine in the South Pacific on Monday.Photographer: 
Li Xiangchao/Xinhua/AP Photo
Mystery still surrounds many details of the latest launch. China hasn’t said 
what type of missile was used or where it was fired from. What is known is that 
the weapon, which carried a dummy warhead, flew several thousand kilometers.

China’s latest model of submarine-launched ballistic missile, or SLBM, is 
known as the JL-3 and is estimated by the US to have a range of about 10,000 
kilometers. That model could be fired from a sub close to China’s coast and 
comfortably hit most of the US.

It is unclear if China tested a JL-3 or an older version but the launch was 
the clearest demonstration yet of its progress in developing what’s known as 
the nuclear triad: the ability to attack with nuclear-tipped missiles from 
land, air or sea.

The option of launching intercontinental-range ballistic missiles from 
submarines is considered the gold standard of nuclear deterrence since the 
vessels are widely regarded as harder to find and destroy than land- or 
air-based launch systems.

While US submarines are seen as stealthier and harder to detect than others, 
China is working on improving its designs.

China Flashes Its Military Muscle
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/audio/2026-07-07/big-take-china-flashes-its-military-muscle-podcast?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
Another notable aspect of the latest test is the advance warning that China 
gave to countries in the region.

Similarly, Beijing notified other nations ahead of its test of a land-based 
intercontinental missile in 2024 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-09-25/china-launches-icbm-with-dummy-warhead-into-pacific-ocean?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 that also flew into the Pacific Ocean, breaking from a pattern of not 
acknowledging its tests.

The openness provides an opportunity for countries to warn airplanes and 
ships, although security analysts noted that China’s alerts came after the 
internationally agreed cut-off of 24 hours before the launch, causing some 
countries to scramble.

There was also confusion from Beijing’s move to give two different flight 
paths for the test — one from the northern Bohai Sea and another from the South 
China Sea.

Still, the Asian nation appears to be moving closer to the transparent approach
 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2024-09-26/us-hails-china-s-advanced-notice-of-icbm-test-as-good-thing?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 of countries like the US that regularly test long-range missiles into the sea 
and provide advance notice.

If the trend continues for any future launches — and more are expected 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/china-s-icbm-tests-point-to-new-normal-rattling-the-pacific?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 — that will provide some relief for countries in the region.

The US conducted its most recent operational test of an unarmed Minuteman III 
ICBM in May in California, with a splashdown in the Pacific Ocean.

Few nations in the Pacific will welcome the prospect of more Chinese ballistic 
missile tests. Some analysts anticipate the country will at some point look to 
begin testing nuclear-capable missiles from its strategic bombers to further 
develop its triad capabilities.

More demonstrations of nuclear capabilities hardly seem to be stabilizing for 
the region, but in the logic of nuclear deterrence a strong sign of the ability 
to respond to a nuclear attack can help dissuade a rival from striking first.

That was very likely a big part of China’s calculus this week.

Old, Shabby and Small

In recent months, many people have wondered whether China’s property market 
has turned the corner some five years into its slump.

The latest answer is a definitive “nope.”

For a long while, analysts have been on the lookout for green shoots signaling 
a turnaround, and demand for small apartments is the latest source of hope. But 
even in this part of the market, the recovery is only occurring in the biggest 
cities.

As part of that trend, buyers in China’s major cities are putting their money 
into flats largelybuilt during a Soviet Union-inspired construction drive 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-08/china-demand-for-soviet-style-apartments-shows-limits-to-revival?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 in the 1970s and 1980s. They are known aslaopoxiao — meaning old, shabby and 
small — and they tend to suffer from poor insulation and outdated plumbing.
So-called laopoxiao apartments in Shanghai.Photographer: Qilai Shen/Bloomberg
But, as everyone knows, the single most important factor in real estate is 
location, location, location.

So, if these old, shabby and small apartments are near the best schools, 
public transport and the other nice things about big cities, they are in great 
demand. This is why the values of small homes have risen lately, at least in 
some locales.

In Shanghai, the average price of flats smaller than 70 square meters (753 
square feet) climbed 2.4% from a November low through the end of April. That 
increase may not sound like much but it defiesa nationwide decline in the wider 
market 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-16/china-home-prices-fall-at-faster-pace-in-setback-to-revival?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
, according to research firm Proptech Innovations.

“At first glance, it might feel like you’re living in a prison cell,” said 
Jason Lu, a landlord who owns a four-decade-old flat in a Shanghai housing 
estate that has iron bars on the windows.

His pitch to potential renters is the neighborhood. Within a short walk, they 
can enjoy riverside views, a tennis court and access to the Lujiazui business 
district, he said. A subway station is also planned for the area.

The appeal of the old apartments is unusual in China, which for years was 
obsessed with new projects. It means that cash-strapped developers, relying in 
part on demand returning for their glut of unsold properties, have little hope 
of benefiting from the partial recovery.

The trend is also a reminder that the efforts of local governments to revive 
real estate are having mixed results.

This matters given the property crisis is a running source of pain for the 
economy,discouraging a consumption rebound 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/china-consumer-inflation-surprises-by-slowing-more-than-forecast?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 because Chinese households tend to have most of their wealth tied up in their 
homes. Lower home prices than in years past leave them feeling poorer and less 
willing to spend.

This focus on small, older homes suggests a broader recovery in China’s 
property market is still a long way off.

Behind the Great Firewall

A weekly look at the big water cooler news in China.

This week, many people in China mourned the death of an economist known for 
his unusuallyblunt, honest takes on the economy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-07/gao-top-china-economist-who-questioned-official-gdp-data-dies?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
.

Gao Shanwen, who famously challenged the reliability of official growth data, 
died after an illness, according to local media outlets. Reports gave his age 
as 55.

A former advisor to government regulators and top officials, Gao publicly 
questioned Beijing’s official GDP figures at an event in Washington in 2024, 
saying the economy was probably expanding at around 2% rather than the official 
rate of about 5%.

The remarks reportedly went over poorly with Chinese authorities, who ordered 
Gao disciplined.

The economist also set off broad discussion in late 2024 when he said Chinese 
societyafter the pandemic 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-06-14/chinese-economy-stuck-in-slow-lane-as-consumption-heads-for-drop?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 was “full of vibrant old people, lifeless young people and despairing 
middle-aged people.” Those comments were scrubbed from some domestic social 
media platforms.

“I am deeply shocked to hear the sad news of Dr. Gao Shanwen’s passing,” Dan 
Bin, one of China’s best-known fund managers, wrote on Weibo. “What a 
remarkably talented individual. It seems heaven is always envious of great 
talent.”

Gao’s public frankness was exceedingly rare in China, where the government has 
cracked down on negative commentary about the economy as it struggles with a 
housing market collapse,deflationary pressure 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/graphics/2025-china-deflation-cost/?cmpid=BBD071026_CN&utm_campaign=china&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260710&utm_content=6370>
 and high youth unemployment.

His remarks did bother some people. Some nationalists accused Gao of 
defeatism, pointing to his 2018 comment that Beijing was “mentally unprepared” 
for escalating friction with Washington.

But his outspokenness mostly earned him respect, especially among people who 
value critical thinking and are tired of views that simply parrot the official 
line.

“Another person who spoke the truth is gone, while those full of lies continue 
to spout nonsense,” one person wrote in a fairly representative comment on 
Weibo. “Rest in peace, Dr. Gao!”
