Debut Breakfast

Weekly breakfasts between the US economy’s top stewards — the Treasury 
secretary and Federal Reserve chair — have been a custom for decades. Rarely 
are there revelations about what was discussed (the 2011 filmToo Big to Fail 
depicted 
<https://www.wsj.com/video/film-clip-too-big-to-fail-fed-to-the-rescue/980ABD41-C577-4853-AF16-A4A8C123A183>
 some of them, set during the global financial crisis.)

On Thursday, Scott Bessent indicated something that didn’t come up in his 
first meeting with newly installed Fed Chairman Kevin Warsh.

“I had my first breakfast with Chair Warsh this morning, and I believe that he 
will do the right thing to balance inflation and growth,” he said in a White 
Housepress briefing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/bessent-defends-proposal-for-trump-s-face-on-special-250-bill?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
. Asked whether he had requested that he lower interest rates, Bessent 
responded that “I never did that” in 41 breakfasts with Warsh’s predecessor, 
Jerome Powell.

It was the second time Bessent offered such a comment with regard to Warsh. 
Friday, just after Warsh’sswearing-in ceremony 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-22/warsh-to-be-sworn-in-as-fed-chief-during-white-house-ceremony?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
, he told reporters when asked the same question, “He’s going to do the right 
thing for inflation and growth.”
Scott BessentPhotographer: Stefani Reynolds/Bloomberg
The comment alludes to the Fed’s statutory mandate, to ensure stable prices 
and maximum employment. And it adds to an impression Bessent gave last month 
that he’s offering Warshsome cover 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-04-16/bessent-signals-potential-cover-for-warsh-to-stand-pat-on-rates?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
 to hold off on pushing strongly for the interest-rate cuts President Donald 
Trump has been calling for.

Trump himself said during last Friday’s ceremony that “I want Kevin to be 
totally independent.” But things can change. Bessent at the start of this 
administration repeatedly touted that Trumphadn’t asked 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-03-06/bessent-highlights-trump-s-fed-silence-rejects-inflation-worry?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
 for Fed easing. He said in March 2025, “You will notice that he has stopped 
calling for the Fed to cut rates.”

But that all changed as the year went on, with the president heaping criticism 
on Powell for not slashing the Fed’s benchmark. Bessent himself alsochanged his

<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-09/bessent-says-fed-should-recalibrate-after-revised-us-data?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
.

This time around, Warsh may have at least a couple of months of quiescence 
from the White House and Treasury.

Bessent said earlier this month there may be another “one, two more hot 
inflation numbers” due to the run-up inenergy costs 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-05-28/warsh-s-new-challenge-containing-fed-hawks-as-inflation-simmers?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
. If the Fed isn’t cutting after that, investors and economists will be on keen 
watch for another messaging shift from the administration.

Need-to-Know Research

The use of weight-loss drugs is changing the way 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-02/ozempic-glp-1-users-spend-more-at-restaurant-chains-like-olive-garden?cmpid=BBD052926_NEF&utm_campaign=nef&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260529&utm_content=4918>
 some consumers shop, with US spending on sugary foods down and Britons 
swapping fast food for vegetables. Macroeconomic data may start reflecting the 
shift, James Pomeroy at HSBC reckons.

Food away from home accounts for 5% of US consumer spending, and more in many 
other parts of the world, Pomeroy wrote in a note this week. The impact of 
reduced demand in this space could easily trim overall consumer spending by 
more than 1%, he says, as total food consumption drops back toward minimum 
calorie intake levels and as users consume less fast food.
A box of Ozempic inside a pharmacy in Los Angeles, Aug. 6, 2025.Photographer: 
Eric Thayer/Bloomberg
“Even if we assume these drugs are only taken by half 
<https://www.kff.org/public-opinion/poll-1-in-8-adults-say-they-are-currently-taking-a-glp-1-drug-for-weight-loss-diabetes-or-another-condition-even-as-half-say-the-drugs-are-difficult-to-afford/>
 of the people who are obese, that could lower food consumption by 2-9% 
depending on markets, pulling down overall consumer spending by 0.5-1.5%,” 
Pomeroy calculates. “In more extreme scenarios of a wider push towards 
lower-calorie intakes, we could see overall consumption fall by 6-7% if calorie 
intakes more universally fall by the 30-40% drops that are possible.”
