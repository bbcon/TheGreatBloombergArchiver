**The Hormuz Arc: Escalation, Diplomacy, and Partial De-escalation**
The Strait of Hormuz remained the week's dominant cross-cutting fault line, with the conflict entering its tenth week and the operational and diplomatic picture shifting materially across the period. The week opened with the breakdown of the month-old ceasefire: US warships escorted American-flagged vessels through the strait on Monday, prompting an Iranian missile and drone attack that caused a large fire at the UAE's Fujairah port. President Trump then suspended "Project Freedom" on Wednesday, citing a request from mediator Pakistan and what he described as "great progress" toward a deal. On Thursday, a one-page memorandum of understanding circulated publicly, proposing that Tehran reopen the strait in exchange for a lifting of the US port blockade and a uranium enrichment moratorium, with broader nuclear questions deferred. Brent crude fell sharply on the news before paring losses after Tehran indicated a deal was not imminent. By Friday, US forces struck Iranian missile and drone launch sites near the strait following attacks on three American warships, illustrating that the ceasefire's resilience remains deeply uncertain.

**Energy Markets: Price Action and Supply Adaptation**
Oil prices traversed a wide range during the week. WTI touched $112 per barrel in intraweek trading before falling nearly 10% on Wednesday as diplomacy advanced, then recovering on Friday's fresh clashes. Brent futures for end-2026 crossed $90 per barrel — their highest since the conflict began. Prediction markets assigned less than 50% odds to a return to normal Hormuz traffic by end of June, down from roughly 90% two weeks prior. US shale producers are responding: Diamondback Energy's CEO signaled an inflection point, with the Permian Basin expected to add as many as 30 rigs, while Exxon plans a 12% production increase. US crude exports have exceeded 250 million barrels over the past nine weeks. The New York Fed's global supply chain pressure index reached its highest reading since July 2022 in April.

**Geopolitical Architecture: Trump-Xi Summit and Caucasus Competition**
The Trump-Xi Beijing summit, scheduled for May 14–15, is the week's principal forward-looking geopolitical event. Senior executives from Blackstone, Citigroup, Nvidia, Apple, Exxon, and Boeing are expected to accompany Trump in what would be the first visit by a sitting US president to China since 2017. Trade, Taiwan, and Iran are all on the agenda, with expectations for substantive deliverables appropriately low given the breadth of bilateral divergence. China's Foreign Minister Wang Yi hosted Iran's Abbas Araghchi this week and publicly urged Tehran to reopen the strait — positioning Xi Jinping as a potential peacemaker while simultaneously reflecting Beijing's dependence on Middle Eastern oil for approximately two-fifths of its imports. In a separate theater, the US signed a 99-year agreement with Armenia for management of a transit corridor linking Azerbaijan to Nakhchivan, directly challenging Russian influence in a region Moscow has historically dominated.

**Dollar Architecture and De-Dollarization Dynamics**
De-dollarization dynamics, long discussed but slow-moving, accumulated observable momentum across the week. Iran's reported collection of Hormuz transit tolls in Bitcoin, China's instruction to private refiners to disregard US secondary sanctions, Gulf sovereign wealth funds reassessing US asset allocations, and central banks across emerging markets increasing gold holdings while reducing dollar reserves represent individually modest but collectively notable stress points on dollar-centric financial infrastructure. At the Milken Institute conference in Los Angeles, EQT's Per Franzén — overseeing roughly €270 billion — noted clients actively reassessing historical overweights to dollar-denominated assets. The more salient risk, as multiple participants framed it, is not displacement by a rival currency but erosion through sustained US policy inconsistency. Project mBridge, the BIS-coordinated cross-border settlement system developed alongside China, Hong Kong, the UAE, Saudi Arabia, and Thailand, remains nascent in volume but is developing under conditions that could accelerate adoption faster than baseline projections assume.
