The US-Iran conflict has emerged as the dominant macro variable across multiple dimensions this week. Oil markets are pricing in a meaningful risk premium as the conflict raises questions about potential disruption to the Strait of Hormuz, through which a substantial share of global crude flows. The energy policy implications are genuinely uncertain: the conflict could accelerate the clean-energy transition by reinforcing the strategic case for domestic renewable capacity, or it could prompt fossil-fuel-dependent economies to extend hydrocarbon infrastructure investment in the name of energy security. Both dynamics may operate simultaneously in different regions, creating divergent policy trajectories rather than a clean directional shift.

The US decision to go to war with Iran, while simultaneously pursuing Venezuela ties and sanctioning Rwanda's military, illustrates the breadth of the Trump administration's geopolitical activism and its underlying logic: securing physical access to critical resources — energy and minerals — as a strategic priority. The minerals competition with China is the clearest expression of this, with the US moving to acquire stakes in Congolese copper and cobalt assets while Beijing retains commanding market shares across sub-Saharan African commodity supply chains. China conducted roughly $200 billion in annual trade with sub-Saharan Africa in 2024 — approximately four times the US figure — and controls an estimated 80% of Congo's copper output. Closing that gap through deal-making alone, without the decades of relationship capital China has accumulated, appears a formidable task.

The energy transition's near-term trajectory faces genuine uncertainty. Surging gasoline prices may accelerate hybrid and EV adoption in consumer markets, but simultaneously strengthen the political economy arguments for fossil fuel expansion in producing nations. This tension is unlikely to resolve cleanly in the near term, and investors with exposure to energy transition assets should treat the conflict's impact as a source of volatility rather than a directional catalyst.
