European bank earnings delivered broadly constructive results. UBS reported first-quarter net income of $3.04 billion, beating all estimates, with revenue rising nearly 30% driven by equities and foreign exchange — shares jumped 4.3% in Zurich and management confirmed intentions to add to its $3 billion share buyback program. Deutsche Bank posted higher-than-expected profit and revenue, supported by trading income and stronger contributions from asset management and retail banking. Banco Santander's profit also beat expectations, with its US expansion — anchored by the recent Webster Financial acquisition — providing incremental support. Adidas gained 8.3% in Frankfurt on strong apparel momentum.

In the UK, a notable split-screen moment unfolded: King Charles III addressed the US Congress with an unusually direct appeal to reject isolationism, support NATO, and maintain the Ukraine commitment — remarkable in its pointed political content for a constitutional monarch. Simultaneously, Prime Minister Keir Starmer survived a parliamentary vote related to the Peter Mandelson appointment controversy, underscoring how managing the relationship with Washington continues to consume significant bandwidth in Downing Street. A UK research group separately warned that a deepening Middle East crisis could tip the UK into recession and force aggressive rate hikes, a tail risk that may be underpriced given the country's energy import exposure.

In the Netherlands, the government is advancing a plan to impose a 36% annual tax on unrealized gains from stocks, bonds, and cryptocurrencies, effective 2028. Critics warn of capital flight and reduced domestic investment appetite. The proposal adds the Netherlands to a growing list of Western governments pursuing wealth-tax measures, though the political and economic pushback appears more acute given the country's historical reputation for stable economic policymaking. Separately, the EU is considering adding conditionality to its €90 billion Ukraine loan package, linking some disbursements to an unpopular business tax reform.
