A Rally for the Memory Banks

Demand for silicon chips is sky-high, their supply is limited, and that means 
their share price has gone parabolic. That’s more or less all you need to know 
about why the S&P 500 has managed to finish at yet another record despite the 
worst consumer sentiment reading from the University of Michigan since its 
survey began in 1978.

Micron Technology Inc., a long-established chipmaker, provided the greatest 
excitement with a 19.3% gain that put it into the now 10-strong club of US 
companies valued at more than $1 trillion. Hours later, the South Korean 
semiconductor manufacturer SK Hynix Inc. also passed the landmark, reached a 
few weeks ago by Samsung Electronics Co. The biggest chipmakers now surpass or 
rival Meta Platforms Inc., one of the leading hyperscaler tech groups:

There appeared to be two catalysts for Micron’s rally Tuesday, both tied in 
with the critical drivers of the moment. First, there is Wall Street optimism. 
UBS announced that it was doubling its target for the company. If the bank is 
right, Micron will be worth about $1.8 trillion this time next year. And 
naturally, optimism like that from a major investment bank prompted many to buy.

Second, there is an impulse from China in its bid to win what both the world’s 
economic superpowers seem to regard as the AI War.Huawei proclaimed a technical 
breakthrough <https://bloom.bg/4f60GkE> in a process it calls Logic Folding, 
whichRobert Lea of Bloomberg Intelligence can explain <https://bloom.bg/4f60GBa>
. While this is aimed at helping China make competitive semi-processors without 
the most advanced equipment available to competitors, it was also taken up as a 
reason to pile in to Micron and other US chipmakers.

The market’s current surge owes nearly everything to the rally in the chips — 
but it’s worth stressing that this is in part a correction to the previous 
excitement over the hyperscaler tech platforms in the Magnificent Seven who 
until this year were expected to hog all the greatest returns from the 
revolution in artificial intelligence. Their advance reflects a belated but 
necessary grasp that the hyperscalers need to spend a lot of money, and that 
some of their projected profits will have to go to key suppliers.

It’s difficult to get your arms around this boom, but here are a few attempts. 
If Hindsight Capital, Points of Return’s imaginary hedge fund that makes 
perfect trades with the aid of hindsight, had started this year by shorting the 
Magnificent Seven(!) and putting the proceeds into the SOX index, the main 
benchmark for US semiconductors, it would have made 71% by now. If they’d put 
the trade on a little earlier, the money would have doubled:

Spoilsports might point out that there were better candidates for the short 
leg, as the Magnificent Seven are up for the year. A trade of long the SOX and 
short the S&P 500 Financials (down 5.5% year-to-date) would have gained 92.3%. 
But it remains flabbergasting that it’s possible to make so much money by 
betting against the Magnificents and trusting a large and well-established 
index. This is how they compare so far this year:

Another way to look at this is the contribution to the S&P 500’s 672-point 
rally for 2026. The Magnificent Seven account for 160 of those points, while 
seven big chipmakers in the SOX, listed in the graphic below, have added 289. 
But that understates the turn in favor of chipmakers as the biggest, Nvidia, is 
in both indexes. Move Nvidia, and the new Magnificent Six has added 79 points 
while the SOX 8 has contributed 369:

It’s important to stress that unlike most of history’s investment bubbles, 
this rally isn’t strictly about valuation. Taken as a multiple of expected 
earnings over the next two years, Micron is actually cheaper than the S&P 500 
as a whole (and much cheaper than the SOX):

This is a legacy of the long-standing perception that chips are a cyclical 
commodity and should be valued accordingly. But this cheapness assumes that the 
forward earnings estimates are reasonable. If we look at Bloomberg’s measure of 
rolling 12-month forward earnings estimates, we get an extraordinary picture:

Traditional chipmakers like Micron have some extremely deep-pocketed customers 
just where they want them, and it is only this year that brokers’ estimates 
have taken that into account. This is undeniably a period of explosive growth, 
which justifies putting a much higher price on Micron and the rest of the SOX. 
But in such periods, it gets much harder to gauge the future with any precision.

If these estimates are right, chipmakers will make fortunes for many. But 
there’s a very good chance that they aren’t.

Emerging Déjà Vu

After a brief sputter when the Iran war broke out, emerging markets appear to 
have found their footing — with a few exceptions among economies reliant on 
energy imports. But that resilience, it turns out, depends on a familiar force: 
Artificial intelligence quietly props up the cohort and masks any semblance of 
weakness. Indeed, chipmakers are doing even more heavy lifting for emerging 
indexes than they are for the S&P 500. This Oxford Economics chart shared by 
Clocktower’sEric Wallerstein <https://bloom.bg/4e7u3ly> is very revealing:

Essentially, strip EMs of their three biggest AI proxies and the group is 
essentially back to where it was immediately after Liberation Day last year. 
There is no telling how much further the AI trade has to run. Still, the 
reliance on this narrative underscores how heavily emerging markets have come 
to depend on technology. Semiconductor and chip-equipment stocks are 
understandably popular, and have far outperformed even the US with the aid of 
names like Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Co.:

Beyond those rosy headlines lies a familiar story. “Old habits die hard,” 
<https://bloom.bg/4e6Q9of> as a resurgence in political risk has prompted a 
rethink of the rally that predates the Iran war. The impact of higher oil 
prices has been cushioned by energy exporters such as Brazil, but the shock of 
the last three months has intensified inflationary pressures, which will only 
worsen as the conflict drags on without resolution.

Points of Return recently detailed investors’ <https://bloom.bg/4f60GRG> 
anxiety in Indonesia triggered by President Prabowo Subianto’s authoritarian 
policies. In Turkey,a court order to oust <https://bloom.bg/4tYXISU> the leader 
of the main opposition party has also prompted alarm. In Latin America, 
upcoming elections in Brazil, Colombia, and Peru all leave uncertainty hanging 
over market-friendly regimes, while Bolivia is embroiled inanti-government 
protests <https://bloom.bg/4f60H8c>. These risks are manifesting in Latin 
American bond markets, with Colombia hit particularly hard:

FIM Partners’ Francesc Balcells attributes the discontent in part to 
deteriorating macroeconomic conditions, particularly in oil importers grappling 
with surging prices. Frustration with incumbent governments grows harder to 
contain, fostering political disruption. Oxford Economics’ quarterly survey of 
EM economists, the first since the Iran conflict, found a severe deterioration 
in perceptions, with growth risks skewed to the downside while inflation and 
interest rates are now feared to stay higher for longer:

These threats are at their highest since November 2022, when the global 
economy was reeling from the post-pandemic inflation shock, aggressive central 
bank tightening, and the Russia-Ukraine war. Oxford Economics’ Joshua Fisher 
argues that risks have become a greater concern across a broad range of 
indicators, supporting the view that, in aggregate, concerns are more severe 
than previously feared:

We have raised our forecasts for food and fertilizer prices and expect food 
inflation pressures to emerge gradually. This represents a sharp turnaround 
since the war. But concerns are growing over the indirect impact, with domestic 
demand and monetary policy risks also increasing.

Where emerging markets go next depends largely on the impasse over the Strait 
of Hormuz. MSCI’s Thomas Verbraeken notes that the four largest constituents of 
MSCI Emerging Markets — China, South Korea, India, and Taiwan — are all among 
the economies most vulnerable to a prolonged closure of the Strait.

For now, the massive emerging market outflows at the war’s onset have eased as 
peace talks continue <https://bloom.bg/4f60HFe> — a strong indication of the 
market’s desire to return to normalcy. As Capital Economics’ most recent 
tracker shows, flows to bond markets turned positive, while outflows from 
equity markets eased:
Source: LSEG, Capital Economics 
While a long, drawn-out conflict or even an escalation carries risks for EMs, 
Capital Economics’ William Jackson suggests that they’re better positioned to 
avoid major outflows than in the past. And while the excitement around chips 
continues, so will the EM rally.

—Richard Abbey
