The US-Israel military campaign against Iran, now entering its second month, is the dominant macro variable shaping global energy markets, trade flows, and geopolitical alignments. The Strait of Hormuz remains largely closed to shipping, a constraint that is reverberating across oil-dependent economies in Asia, South Asia, and parts of Europe. Both Pakistan and Thailand, which rely heavily on Persian Gulf oil transiting the Strait, are experiencing direct fuel cost pressures. China and Pakistan issued a five-point joint peace proposal this week calling for the restoration of shipping through the Strait, a signal that Beijing's calculus is shifting from passive beneficiary to active mediator as the economic costs of prolonged conflict accumulate. President Trump has provided no firm timeline for the campaign's conclusion, sustaining the uncertainty premium embedded in energy prices.

The carbon markets are experiencing collateral damage from a separate but significant stress event. Koko Networks, a World Bank-backed clean cookstove company serving approximately 1.3 million Kenyan households, filed for bankruptcy in January amid disputed claims over credit issuance accuracy. The collapse has depressed prices on the Corsia aviation offset marketplace from approximately $15 to $12.85, tightened already-scarce supply of high-quality credits, and extended buyer decision-making timelines ahead of mandatory airline compliance beginning in 2027. The episode reinforces persistent credibility concerns about the avoided-emissions methodology underpinning much of the voluntary carbon market, and arrives at an awkward moment given the UN's newly operational country-to-country trading framework. Whether the Koko episode represents an idiosyncratic failure or a systemic indicator of methodological fragility across the sector remains genuinely unresolved.
