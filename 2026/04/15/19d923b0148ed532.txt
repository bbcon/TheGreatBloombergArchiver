Europe’s case for geothermal

By Alastair Marsh <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AQvVwWuhxcQ/alastair-marsh>

It’s an energy source that can deliver emissions-free power even if the sun 
doesn’t shine or the wind doesn’t blow, and can’t be easily blown up by 
adversaries.

On paper, at least, that’s what makes geothermal perfect for Europe, a 
continent determined to decrease its reliance on imported fossil fuels and 
build its own energy security — without sacrificing its climate goals.

That’s the argument that more than 60 companies and investors made earlier 
this month in an open letter to European leaders, urging them to “significantly 
strengthen 
<https://www.cleantechforeurope.com/policy/open-letter-europe-risks-missing-the-geothermal-revolution>
” their support for geothermal power. Meanwhile, some European politicians have 
also argued that the region must do more to become a leader in the sector.

Investors in European clean tech firms are also banging the drum. One of their 
key concerns is that Europe could lose its local geothermal champions to the 
US, wheredeal flow 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-03-19/fervo-energy-set-to-go-public-as-ai-demand-trump-propel-geothermal?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 is growing.

“Geothermal is a core competency in our race to energy independence,” said 
Nick de la Forge, general partner and co-founder of Planet A Ventures, a 
Berlin-based venture capital fund. “The current energy shock makes it clearer 
than ever that geothermal is a strategic imperative Europe cannot afford to 
ignore.”

De la Forge said one of Planet A’s portfolio companies 
<https://planet-a.com/startups/ga-drilling/>, which provides drilling 
technologies for geothermal developers, was founded in Slovakia but “had no 
choice but to relocate to the US” because “the market conditions to grow” 
pioneering technologies did not exist in Europe.
via Bloomberg
Geothermal is having a moment 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-19/fervo-plans-ipo-as-geothermal-avoids-trump-attacks?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 right now: Once confined to a few select locations with the right geology, new 
technologies that mimic oil and gas fracking techniques have brought down costs 
and expanded the potential for using the Earth’s heat for energy. Still other 
next-gen approaches could tap extra hot rocks to generate power.

New geothermal systems could be able to generate over 301 terawatt-hours 
<https://ember-energy.org/app/uploads/2026/02/Hot-stuff-geothermal-energy-in-Europe-1.pdf>
 of electricity in the European Union annually, equivalent to roughly 42% of EU 
coal- and gas-fired power generation, according to research from Ember.

The posterchild of modern geothermal is Fervo Energy, a US startup that’s 
thriving even under a US administration hostile to clean energy. The company is 
targeting an initial public offering this year.
Workers installing geothermal power production equipment at Fervo’s Utah 
facility.Photographer: Lindsay D’Addato/Bloomberg
Investor enthusiasm is growing for the sector. Financing for next-generation 
geothermal rose from about $22 million in 2018 to nearly $2.2 billion in 2025, 
according to an analysis from the International Energy Agency.

For Sebastian Heitmann, co-founder of Extantia Capital, these new drilling 
technologies have “unlocked the map,” making geothermal power available in more 
places at much more affordable prices. VCs like Extantia are “betting on 
geothermal as a scalable, profitable alternative to intermittent renewables 
like wind and solar,” he said.

The other benefit, Heitmann said, is that geothermal systems are distributed 
and largely underground, so they are “significantly harder for adversaries to 
attack.” That’s become a key consideration at a time when energy infrastructure 
have become key targets in the conflict in the Middle East.

Extantia, along with Planet A, were among signatories of the letter sent to 
political leaders including European Commission President Ursula von der Leyen 
calling for more geothermal support. They argued that the technology can help 
polluting industries cut emissions faster, make the continent’s electric grids 
more stable, and replace imported natural gas.

“We’re now in the second historic energy crisis in four years – and geothermal 
is one of the few energy sources that is domestic, reliable, and not exposed to 
global shocks like Hormuz,” said Ruben Davis, a senior policy officer at 
Cleantech for Europe, the lobby group that coordinated the letter.

Caterpillar’s e-tractor buy
Monarch Tractor vineyard test site in California.Photographer: Cayce Clifford
By Coco Liu <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AU-LNn94_HY/coco-liu> and Emma 
Court <https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUVJA-GbuIc/emma-court>

Caterpillar Inc. has acquired self-driving electric tractor startup Monarch 
Tractor, according to people familiar with the matter.

Monarch, which at its peak drew comparisons to Tesla within the agriculture 
industry, ran into problems while growing the business and laid off staff in 
recent months. The startup said in a LinkedIn post late last week that its 
technology had been bought by “a large global equipment manufacturer” but did 
not disclose the manufacturer’s name. The two people asked not to be identified 
because they weren’t authorized to speak publicly.

Caterpillar and Monarch didn’t respond to requests for comment.

“Building and scaling a new tractor platform in agriculture came with 
unforeseen challenges. We had to make difficult decisions,” like moving from 
manufacturing to licensing its technology for use in settings including 
tractors and construction equipment, Monarch wrote on LinkedIn. Construction 
equipment is a key part of Caterpillar’s roughly $369 billion business.

Monarch is the latest climate tech startup to struggle to commercialize its 
carbon-cutting solutions. In the past four weeks alone, green cement startup 
Sublime Systemslaid off two-thirds of its staff 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/massachusetts-green-cement-startup-slashes-staff-after-trump-cuts-support?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 and battery recycler Ascend Elementsfiled for bankruptcy 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-04-10/massachusetts-based-ascend-elements-files-for-bankrupty?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
.

Scott Bessent on water and ice
Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent at an International Institute of Finance 
event.Photographer: Matt McClain/Bloomberg
By Brian Kahn <https://bloomberg.com/authors/AWSJBMQx-iE/brian-kahn>

Treasury Secretary Scott Bessent acknowledged that the weather is growing more 
extreme, but falsely downplayed the causes and risks of climate change.

“The natural habitat of the Earth is actually water,” Bessent said during a 
discussion on Tuesday with Bjorn Lomborg, a Danish political science known for 
his climate skepticism. “Ice was probably — it’s a very long cycle — but ice 
was an unusual cycle. We are going through cycles, and I believe that it is 
very difficult to deconstruct the reasons around why anything changes.”

However, the causes of climate change are well-documented. That includes 
natural variations in the tilt and wobble of the Earth as well as its path 
around the Sun, which ensure the planet goes through periodic Ice Ages over 
tens of thousands of years. There’s even a term for it: Milankovitch cycles, 
which have been known about for more than a century.

The impact of burning fossil fuels and pumping planet-warming greenhouse gases 
into the atmosphere is another long-studied and well-understood phenomenon. So, 
too, are thethreats rising temperatures pose 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-17/us-spending-on-climate-damage-nears-1-trillion-per-year?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 to the economy.

Bessent said that rather than trying to understand why the climate is 
changing, it’s better to focus on improving resilience to extreme events. He 
alsocited Bill Gates’ memo 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-10-28/bill-gates-calls-out-doomsday-view-of-climate-change-in-memo?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 about the need to focus on humanitarian issues and rejecting a “doomsday” view 
of climate change published last year.

In the past, Bessent has criticized the International Monetary Fund 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-04-24/imf-chief-downplays-climate-work-after-bessent-criticism?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 and other countries for their efforts to cut emissions while lauding efforts 
to put more power on the grid. On Tuesday, he cheered on efforts to install 
nuclear power — which is emissions-free — in particular.

Under Bessent, the Treasury Department has curtailed its climate work 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-09-11/trump-administration-scraps-oversight-groups-on-climate-and-financial-risk?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
 and oversight. His rhetoric downplaying the threat of climate change also fits 
with how other Trump administration officials havetalked about the issue 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-17/decoding-climate-change-denial-under-trump-from-realism-to-clean-coal?cmpid=BBD041526_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260415>
.
