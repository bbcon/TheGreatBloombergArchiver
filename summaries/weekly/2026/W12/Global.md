• Brent crude rose above $118 per barrel on Thursday after Iran struck Qatar's Ras Laffan LNG complex — the world's largest — before pulling back to approximately $108–110 on Friday following signals of potential restraint from both Israel and Iran.
• The Strait of Hormuz remained effectively closed to commercial shipping throughout the week, with approximately 40,000 seafarers stranded on either side and vessel insurance premiums reaching roughly 5% of hull value.
• Gold fell approximately 7% on the week — its largest weekly decline since March 2020 and on pace for its worst monthly performance since 2008 — as global monetary policy repricing eroded the appeal of non-yielding assets.
• The Reserve Bank of Australia hiked rates, becoming the first of eighteen central banks to meet this week to move, signaling at least some inflation-targeting frameworks are treating the oil shock as requiring active tightening.
• Tungsten prices have more than doubled this year amid Chinese export controls and rising military demand, illustrating how the conflict is amplifying pre-existing commodity tensions.

**The Strait of Hormuz: Escalation and Tentative Signals**
The Iran war entered its most dangerous phase this week before showing tentative signs of restraint by Friday. Israel struck Iran's South Pars gas field — the first attack on Iranian energy production facilities — prompting Iran to inflict significant damage on Qatar's Ras Laffan Industrial City on Thursday, sparking fires and causing damage that Qatar indicated could keep two LNG trains offline for up to five years. Additional strikes hit a gas facility in Abu Dhabi, set ablaze two Kuwaiti oil refineries, and caused damage near a Saudi refinery. Tehran designated energy facilities in Qatar, Saudi Arabia, and the UAE as "legitimate targets." Brent surged above $118 on Thursday. By Friday, Prime Minister Netanyahu stated Israel would no longer target energy infrastructure and said the war would end faster than people expect; Iran's foreign minister signaled that recent restraint was deliberate. Whether this constitutes a durable de-escalation or tactical repositioning remains genuinely uncertain. Prediction markets put the probability of a ceasefire by May 31 below 50% as of early in the week.

**Energy Markets: A Fragmented Supply Landscape**
The price signals across different crude grades and delivery points illustrate the geographic severity of the disruption. WTI traded near $96 on Thursday, Brent at $115, and Middle Eastern grades above $150 — a fragmentation that reflects a shortage most acute for Asian importers dependent on Gulf barrels. Ras Laffan supplied roughly a fifth of global LNG before the war; a restart now expected to take months rather than weeks is pressuring gas prices globally, with downstream effects on fertilizer production, power generation, and semiconductor manufacturing costs. Saudi Arabia appears to have rerouted some exports to above half of normal volumes via contingency infrastructure, offering partial relief. Russia has benefited materially, accelerating crude loading to capture elevated prices while a US sanctions waiver window remains open, with revenues from its key export blend hitting a record for weekly income since the Ukraine invasion began. Trump waived the Jones Act for 60 days, allowing foreign-flagged vessels to transport commodities between US ports, and the administration has ruled out banning oil and gas exports.

**Central Banks and Macro Divergence**
The week's central bank calendar was historically dense, covering approximately two-thirds of the global economy across eighteen policy meetings. All major central banks held rates, but the signal embedded in forward guidance shifted meaningfully toward caution. The Reserve Bank of Australia moved first, hiking on Tuesday in a split decision — an indication that at least some policymakers are treating the energy shock as warranting active tightening rather than patient observation. The Fed held Wednesday, the ECB and Bank of Japan on Thursday, with all three communicating heightened vigilance. Goldman Sachs economists offered a partially reassuring view, arguing that broader supply-chain disruption comparable to 2021–22 is unlikely, as non-energy Gulf trade represents only 1% of global commerce. However, the energy shock alone is sufficient to complicate deliberations materially. Bloomberg Economics estimated March inflation across advanced economies is running approximately 0.4 percentage points above pre-conflict projections, and BofA's global fund manager survey found institutional investors executing the largest overweight rotation into commodities since the 2022 Ukraine invasion.

**Alliance Fractures and Geopolitical Reconfiguration**
Trump's appeals for allied military support produced little tangible commitment across the week. Australia ruled out deploying vessels, Japan's defense minister cited constitutional constraints, and Germany, France, and the UK all declined to participate militarily. Europe's largest naval powers stated that reopening the Strait during active hostilities would require a deeply complex multinational capability not currently feasible. China's state media were openly dismissive. The episode has exposed meaningful fissures in the Western alliance architecture. Simultaneously, the transactional character of US demands — overlapping with tariffs and large investment pledge requirements — appears to be accelerating the formation of alternative bilateral arrangements among US allies. The Japan-South Korea rapprochement is the clearest example: both finance ministers agreed to expand bilateral economic cooperation including a review of their $10 billion currency swap, with public favorability in each direction described as at or near record highs. The IPCC faces an institutional challenge following US withdrawal of scientific funding, and New York's proposed rollback of its 2030 climate targets illustrates the political difficulty of sustaining decarbonization commitments when energy affordability pressures are acute.
