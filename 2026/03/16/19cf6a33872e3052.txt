The Least-Bad Fix to Hormuz Crisis Is Still Nowhere in Sight
Traders are laser-focused on the number of ships passing through Hormuz. There 
are barely any.

Welcome to our guide to the commodities driving the global economy. Today, oil 
reporter Alex Longley looks at the prospects for a restart of ship traffic 
through the critical Strait of Hormuz as the war drags on.

Oil traders and shipowners are in crisis as the war in the Middle East keeps 
the vital Strait of Hormuz effectively closed.

Crude is above $100 a barrel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/latest-oil-market-news-and-analysis-for-march-16?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 for the third day running, and will likely stay elevated all the while traffic 
through the narrow waterway remains all but halted.

Since early this month, President Donald Trump has said the US will ensure the 
safe passage of oil 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-04/trump-pledges-safe-oil-transit-from-mideast-as-iran-war-widens?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 from the region, escorting tankers through Hormuz if necessary.

He’s now demanding other nations assist in this effort 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/trump-demands-help-from-other-countries-to-secure-hormuz?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
, calling on China, France, the UK and others to respond.

But there’s little sign of any action. And even when convoys do get going, 
much will depend on therisk tolerance 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/newsletters/2026-03-04/iran-war-hormuz-convoys-won-t-fix-vital-oil-and-gas-flows-overnight?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 of individual shipowners.

With each day that passes, the impact of the strait’s de facto closure on oil 
prices is getting bigger. Benchmark Brent crude is up 17% in six days and more 
than 40% since the conflict began.

That reflects the crucial role that Hormuz plays in the global market: About a 
fifth of the world’s barrels transit the chokepoint in normal times.
The Strait of Hormuz off Ras Al Khaimah in the United Arab Emirates.
Photographer: FADEL SENNA/AFP via Getty Images
Traders are keeping a close watch on the number of ships passing through. The 
answer is barely any — an oil tanker here and there, but nothing resembling a 
flow that could help tame prices.

Convoys would be key. Yet such a move doesn’t appear close because it remains 
simply too dangerous 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-16/iran-s-upper-hand-in-hormuz-is-pressuring-oil-buyers-and-trump?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
. Indeed, US Energy Secretary Chris Wright said just a few days ago that naval 
escorts“can’t happen now” 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-12/us-escorts-through-hormuz-may-start-by-month-end-wright-says?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 and would more likely start toward the end of the month.

That could mean another two weeks of near-zero flows through Hormuz to global 
markets.

There’s concern that if the route remains off limits for much longer, Brent 
could spike to “2022-style prices,” which may force a drop in demand, Morgan 
Stanley analysts wrote in a note.

Back then, crude neared $140 a barrel. We’re still a way off those heights, 
but until the promised convoys actually materialize, prices will likely 
continue to climb.

—Alex Longley, Bloomberg News

The war has caused an energy emergency in Asia, with billions of people 
dealing with gas outages and preparing for fuel rationing. And as ourBig Take

Gold traded in a narrow range near $5,000 an ounce 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-03-15/gold-steadies-as-oil-jumps-mideast-conflict-enters-third-week?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
 as the conflict in the Middle East entered a third week and investors weighed 
the inflationary impact of higher oil prices.

Fuel oil is known as the bottom of the barrel 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/opinion/articles/2026-03-15/iran-war-the-biggest-oil-risk-is-at-the-bottom-of-the-barrel?cmpid=BBBXT031626_ENERGY&utm_campaign=energy&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260316>
