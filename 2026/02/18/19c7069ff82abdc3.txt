US President Donald Trump looks like he will hold off attacking Iran for now.

Unless he’s calling Tehran’s bluff, US President Donald Trump looks like he 
will hold off attacking Iran, at least for the next two weeks.

The two countries both said yesterday’s nuclear talks in Geneva 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/iran-us-to-meet-for-second-round-of-nuclear-talks-in-geneva?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 were positive. The next step is for Iran to send the Americans detailed 
proposals to addresssticking points 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-17/us-iran-nuclear-talks-what-we-know-about-tehran-s-atomic-program?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
. There will then be a third round of negotiations to hash those out.

For all the positivity, the White House made clear that strikes are not off 
the table and it knows reaching a deal with the Islamic Republic — anadversary 
to the US 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-11/iran-edges-closer-to-a-revolution-that-would-reshape-the-world?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 for almost 50 years — will be anything but easy.

“The president has set some red lines that the Iranians are not yet willing to 
actually acknowledge and work through,” Trump’s vice president, JD Vance, told 
Fox News after the Swiss discussions ended.

Trump, who’s gearing up for tricky mid-term elections this year, faces plenty 
of risks if he chooses to bomb Iran.

Supreme leader Ayatollah Ali Khamenei has threatened a regional war, and he 
has the capability to target American bases in the Middle East, Israel and US 
allies such as Saudi Arabia. Oil traders,already nervous 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-29/brent-crude-hits-70-a-barrel-as-trump-ramps-up-iran-threats?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
, would push prices higher.

Yet Trump has amassed huge firepower himself in the region since last month’s 
protests 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-01-15/iran-protests-will-khamenei-s-regime-survive-and-what-lies-ahead?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 in Iran, which triggered the latest tensions. And he’s sending a second 
aircraft carrier to give himself even more options.

The US leader continues to ratchet up pressure on Tehran and days ago said that
regime change 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-01-14/iran-protests-how-inflation-and-a-currency-crash-fueled-unrest?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 “would be thebest thing 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-13/trump-says-regime-change-in-iran-best-thing-that-could-happen?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 that could happen.”

Last week, Trump hosted Benjamin Netanyahu in Washington, with the Israeli 
prime ministerclearly concerned 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-02-11/netanyahu-in-hasty-trip-to-warn-trump-against-narrow-iran-deal?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 about a deal he doubts Iran would stick to.

For now, Trump looks sincere in trying to give the talks a chance. But he’s 
wary of Iran dragging them out and he won’t give Khamenei much time to reach an 
agreement. — Paul Wallace 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/ASAuUIp54ZQ/paul-wallace>

And Finally

Bethelhem Eshetie gave up driving her taxi in Addis Ababa because the rising 
cost of gas and repairs meant “it was no longer worth it.” Six months later, 
she was back on the road in a new Chinese-made electric vehicle. After Ethiopia 
banned imports of combustion-engine vehicles in 2024 and slashed tariffs on 
electric counterparts,EV adoption 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2026-02-18/ethiopia-ev-boom-sparked-by-ice-car-import-ban?cmpid=BBD021826_politics&utm_campaign=bop&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260218>
 has grown from less than 1% to nearly 6% of vehicles on the road, according to 
official data. While the policy was driven less by climate ambitions than 
fiscal pressures from gasoline subsidies, the government aims to have 60 EV 
assembly plants by 2030.
