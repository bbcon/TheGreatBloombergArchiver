# China Macro: Credit Impulse, Stimulus, 15th FYP, and Commodity Demand
*June 5, 2026*

---

## Executive Summary

China's macro picture in 2026 presents a persistent gap between external strength and domestic weakness. The headline growth number (Q1 +5.0% YoY) flatters a composition in which retail sales, fixed investment, and services have deteriorated materially — with April data delivering a broad miss that no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated. The credit impulse is weak, property sector in managed contraction, and the 15th Five-Year Plan explicitly deprioritizes consumption in favour of industrial and export competitiveness. The commodity demand read-across is bifurcated: structurally declining for old-economy inputs (crude, coal, iron ore into construction) and structurally ascending for new-economy materials (lithium, copper into EVs and grid, polysilicon). The Hormuz shock has accelerated both the energy transition adoption curve and Chinese strategic hedging of its energy supply chain.

---

## Credit Impulse and TSF Composition

China's credit cycle is at a critical juncture. The PBOC has been accommodative in liquidity management — $86.4B injected via 14-day repos ahead of Lunar New Year, with Industrial Securities forecasting over $500B in holiday-period liquidity provision — but the transmission to the real economy has been weak. The structural constraint is the banking system's balance sheet quality: independent analysts estimate true NPL ratios at approximately 10% of total credit against an official 1.5%, implying roughly $3 trillion in under-classified bad debt. Regulatory forbearance is slowing loan growth and trapping capital in unproductive enterprises.

The 10-year CGB yield below 2% — now trading *below* JGB yields — is a powerful diagnostic signal:
1. The private sector lacks creditworthy borrowers willing to pay more for capital;
2. Deflationary expectations persist in the domestic economy even as export-sector profitability is healthy;
3. The monetary transmission mechanism is impaired — rate cuts alone cannot generate demand when balance sheet constraints bind.

January factory-gate deflation moderated more than expected, but Morgan Stanley's Robin Xing cautioned there is "no decisive reflation yet," with improvement leaning heavily on the global metals rally. PPI exited deflation in March (+0.5% YoY after -0.9% in February), partly attributable to the global energy surge. Capital Economics cautioned the turn may not persist.

Banks received $61.9B in net inflows on behalf of clients in April (sixth positive month in seven), a positive signal. However, the "national team" is cutting ETF holdings by roughly 90% in H1 2026, suggesting authorities are managing asset market froth rather than endorsing a credit-led demand recovery.

**Commodity read-through**: A weak credit impulse with impaired monetary transmission is the most direct headwind for copper, iron ore, and construction materials. The credit channel that historically drove infrastructure and property — and thus commodity demand — is not functioning as the primary growth lever. This is the single most important negative signal for the industrial metals complex from the China side.

## Property Sector: Managed Contraction, Not Reflation

The corpus does not report significant new property support measures in 2026. Fixed-asset investment contracted 1.6% YTD in April — the worst outturn in the dataset based on analyst reactions; no Bloomberg-surveyed economist had anticipated a negative print. Urban private-sector wage growth at sub-1% (against 8% pre-pandemic) creates both a demand constraint and a political legitimacy issue the government is managing through industrial and export policy rather than residential property stimulus.

The previous mechanism — households as the demand absorber of infrastructure-driven growth via residential property — has structurally exhausted itself. The current trajectory is property investment declining, contributing to weak FAI, creating headwinds for iron ore, rebar, cement, and the construction commodity complex broadly.

This is a structural rather than cyclical shift. The relevant question is not whether property rebounds in 2026 (it won't at a scale that matters for commodities) but how quickly the new-economy demand sources — EV charging infrastructure, grid expansion, data centers — fill the gap. The answer, based on current data, is: partially but not fully on a 2-3 year horizon.

## Stimulus Announcements and Credibility Gap

The Politburo has pledged repeatedly to "counter external shocks" and "enhance energy security." The 2026 NPC set a growth target of 4.5–5% — the first downward revision in three years, an honest acknowledgment of structural deterioration. The stimulus framework that Citi had previously anticipated — roughly one trillion yuan in fiscal support, a 20bp rate cut, and a 50bp RRR reduction — was revised to be characterized as a *ceiling rather than a floor*. This is a material shift: the market's implicit expectation that Beijing will deploy whatever stimulus is needed to hit growth targets has been systematically disappointed.

**Structural constraints on stimulus**:
- Xi's call for domestic demand to be "the main driver of growth" is rhetorical acknowledgment of what needs to happen; the IMF notes the implementation gap has been persistent since 2005.
- The 15th FYP's absence of numerical consumption targets confirms this aspiration is not being operationalized.
- Moody's upgraded China's credit outlook to stable — a signal that the fiscal posture remains orthodox enough to satisfy rating agencies, which simultaneously means insufficient for the scale of domestic demand support required.

**What stimulus is actually deployed**: PBOC liquidity management is doing the heavy lifting. Beijing released strategic oil reserves and granted crude import quotas during the Hormuz shock. The Politburo's post-April-data meeting signaled willingness to respond but did not produce specific measures. The August Beidaihe conclave — the traditional venue for deciding major policy pivots — may be the next catalyst for a materially larger package. Until then, the stimulus posture is best characterized as targeted and reactive rather than front-loaded and structural.

## 15th Five-Year Plan: Structural Shifts in Energy Intensity

The 15th FYP (2026–2030) represents a significant structural departure from prior planning frameworks in its commodity implications.

**No numerical consumption targets**: The plan explicitly prioritizes industrial competitiveness, AI/advanced manufacturing, and energy transition hardware over domestic consumption. This signals that the government's growth model continues to rely on investment and export rather than the consumption-led rebalancing that would be commodity-demand-positive for bulk materials.

**Energy intensity declining structurally**: China's CO2 emissions fell 0.3% in 2025 — the first non-pandemic annual decline — as renewables absorbed *all* incremental power demand. China added 543 GW of power capacity in 2025, the vast majority renewable. This is not a cyclical slowdown in energy intensity: it reflects a structural shift away from energy-intensive heavy industry (steel, cement, aluminum smelting) toward energy transition manufacturing (EVs, solar panels, batteries). Each unit of Chinese GDP growth now generates *less* demand for thermal coal and crude oil, and *more* demand for lithium, cobalt, polysilicon, and copper in a different form (grid and EV rather than construction).

**Strategic minerals as industrial policy axis**: The 15th FYP treats rare earth processing, battery materials, and semiconductor-related minerals as strategic assets. China dominates rare earth separation (underpinning approximately $1.2 trillion of US GDP-linked activity). Beijing's new supply chain coercion regulations — characterized by Rhodium Group as a shift from reactive to proactive economic statecraft — allow retaliation against foreign governments or firms assisting actions deemed to harm Chinese industrial security. This formalizes the commodity-as-geopolitical-weapon toolkit.

**AI as energy consumer**: ByteDance's approximately $70B capex plans and China's 543 GW capacity addition position it on a favorable power-cost curve for AI infrastructure — a genuine incremental tailwind for Chinese electricity generation (both coal for dispatchable base load and renewables). However, China's inability to procure ASML EUV machines (Huawei's LogicFolding architecture explicitly designed to circumvent this) limits the AI chip output that would fully leverage this infrastructure. Huawei targets 1.4nm-equivalent production by 2031, roughly three years behind TSMC's current roadmap.

## Hormuz Shock and China's Energy Supply Chain

The Hormuz closure exposed meaningful structural vulnerabilities in China's energy supply chain. Iran was a critical crude supplier; Saudi Arabia's shipments to Chinese customers were set to halve in May (to approximately 20 million barrels from roughly 40 million in April). With approximately two-fifths of China's oil imports historically transiting Hormuz, the supply disruption was acute.

Beijing's response:
- Released strategic petroleum reserves
- Granted additional crude import quotas
- Pivoted to US ethane imports (record 800,000 tons in April)
- Yuan-denominated crude transactions hit a single-day record of 1.22 trillion yuan through CIPS
- Chinese crude imports fell 20% YoY in April as state refiners drew on commercial inventories

The Hormuz shock is simultaneously accelerating China's energy transition: EV exports rose 112% YoY in April, solar panel shipments doubled month-on-month in March to a record, and battery exports climbed over a third. Chinese clean technology manufacturers are explicitly leveraging the shock as an export opportunity. The strategic logic — sustained high fossil fuel prices improve electrification economics across every geography — creates a powerful alignment between commercial and geopolitical interests for Chinese manufacturers.

## Key Risks to Watch

**Upside risk to Chinese commodity demand**: A large-scale Beidaihe stimulus decision targeting domestic consumption directly — direct transfers, property market floor policies, or a significant infrastructure spending round. The IMF consumption gap (40% of GDP since 2005) represents the largest identifiable source of unspent demand in the global economy. Any credible policy pivot toward unlocking it would be materially bullish for metals.

**Downside risk**: The true NPL picture ($3 trillion estimated under-classified bad debt) surfaces through a credit event or regulatory reset. A disorderly deleveraging scenario — analogous to a slower-moving version of 2015's shadow banking stress — would compress domestic activity and commodity demand substantially. The 10-year CGB below 2% is the market's quiet pricing of this scenario.

**Structural wildcard**: Huawei's LogicFolding architecture succeeding commercially. If China achieves chip self-sufficiency on an accelerated timeline, the AI buildout domestically expands substantially, driving incremental power demand, copper for data center wiring, and the broader digital infrastructure complex — shifting the composition but not the overall level of Chinese commodity demand.
