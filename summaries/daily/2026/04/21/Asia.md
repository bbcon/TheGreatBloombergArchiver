China's economic narrative has shifted meaningfully in recent weeks. GDP data surprised to the upside, property indicators showed tentative green shoots, and equity markets have recovered to near decade highs. Two influential academic papers published this month examine whether Beijing has escaped the "Japanification" comparison that has shadowed it since the property downturn began. Kenneth Rogoff and Yuanchen Yang, writing for the NBER, acknowledge that China's state-dominated financial system and interventionist policy apparatus have prevented a financial breakdown, but warn that without structural reforms to rebalance growth, today's managed adjustment could evolve into stagnation — particularly if export-oriented sectors like EVs and solar encounter external constraints. Stephen Jen and Joana Freire of Eurizon SLJ Capital are more sanguine, arguing China has "turned a corner" and that fierce domestic competition, combined with US-China technological rivalry, is sustaining productivity in ways that distinguish Beijing's trajectory from Tokyo's lost decades. The debate remains unresolved, though recent data lend some weight to the more constructive reading.

Asian technology exports are providing a separate source of optimism. South Korean chip exports surged 183% year-on-year in the first 20 days of April, driven by AI-related semiconductor demand, with overall exports rising 49%. Taiwan's export orders jumped 66% in March, the fastest pace in 16 years, reflecting continued strength in high-performance computing and cloud infrastructure. Victory Giant, a Chinese Nvidia supplier, surged as much as 60% in its Hong Kong debut after raising $2.6 billion. The South Korean Kospi is up 52% year-to-date, reaching a record, with Samsung and SK Hynix leading the advance. Japan's Topix Nonferrous Metals sub-index has gained over 400% since the Liberation Day tariff reversal a year ago, driven by data center component demand and accelerating corporate governance reform under Prime Minister Takaichi. Her cabinet also approved Japan's first overseas arms export framework since World War II, a significant strategic shift. The Bank of Japan is meanwhile leaning toward holding rates unchanged at its next meeting, citing uncertainties from the Iran conflict. Indonesia's equities declined after MSCI extended its market status review to June.
