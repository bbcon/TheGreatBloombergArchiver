The defining macro theme this week has been the recalibration of global monetary policy expectations in response to the energy shock produced by the US-Iran conflict. Bloomberg Economics' aggregate measure of advanced-economy interest rates now sits approximately 35 basis points higher at the end of 2026 compared with projections from three months ago — a meaningful upward shift reflecting widespread recognition that damage to Middle East energy infrastructure may prove durable. The Fed, ECB, and Bank of England are all navigating asymmetric pressures: growth headwinds on one side, energy-driven inflation on the other.

Oil markets remain central to the picture. WTI held near $99 per barrel Friday, on track for its steepest weekly loss in nine months as ceasefire optimism tempered the conflict premium, though Saudi Arabia's disclosure that Iranian attacks have impaired its own production capacity provided a partial floor. Strait of Hormuz traffic remains severely disrupted — only a handful of vessels transited on April 8-9 versus a pre-war norm of roughly 135 per day — and reopening the waterway is the critical near-term variable. Trump demanded Iran reopen the strait ahead of weekend talks in Islamabad, where Vice President JD Vance will lead the US delegation. Pakistan, which brokered the ceasefire, has gained significant diplomatic capital from hosting the talks, though it faces risks as a Saudi treaty ally should hostilities resume.

The geopolitical implications extend well beyond the Hormuz. China's role in persuading Iran to accept a ceasefire — credited by Iranian officials to a last-minute Beijing push — illustrates the growing influence Beijing derives from its deep financial footprint across the Gulf, where it has outcompeted the US as a regional financier. Xi's $270 billion in Middle East investments constrain how openly China can back Iran, creating a nuanced position: quietly facilitating de-escalation while avoiding overt alignment with either side. Separately, the US-China space competition took a concrete step forward with the successful Artemis II mission, which broke the distance record for human spaceflight and puts a crewed lunar landing as early as 2028 on the table — approximately two years ahead of China's current timeline. The IPCC's funding and data challenges following the US withdrawal represent a quieter but consequential long-term development, with China projected to surpass the US in publicly funded research within two years.
