US

Industrial Production

US factory output was little changed in May after an upwardly revised 0.7% advance in April, with total industrial production rising only 0.1%. The Federal Reserve data suggests supply chain disruptions linked to the Hormuz closure, combined with elevated input costs from tariffs, are beginning to restrain manufacturing activity — a meaningful deceleration after four consecutive months of gains.

Monetary Policy

Markets await Warsh's debut press conference Wednesday. Futures traders made no significant moves on rate expectations, with the Fed still priced for a hike by year-end. The more consequential question is structural: Warsh has signaled a preference for curtailing the dot plot and press conferences, reverting toward Greenspan-era opacity. Goldman Sachs research estimates that the post-2000 shift toward transparency reduced the 10-year term premium by roughly 5 basis points — gains that a communication rollback could partially reverse, introducing greater intraday volatility to rates markets.

China: Domestic Demand Deterioration

May's data confirmed a deepening bifurcation in China's economy. Retail sales contracted 0.6% year-on-year — the first negative reading since late 2022 — with auto purchases plunging 16%. Fixed-asset investment shrank 4.1% in the January-May period, the worst since 2020, with private capital expenditure falling 7.1% and manufacturing investment declining for the first time in six years. Against this, industrial production accelerated to 4.5% growth, driven by high-tech manufacturing expanding 15% in value-added terms. Some analysts estimate April GDP growth near 4%, below the government's 4.5%–5.0% full-year target, raising questions about whether Beijing will need to deploy meaningful demand-side stimulus.

Bank of Japan

The BOJ's 25-basis-point hike to 1.0% was accompanied by a commitment to hold bond purchases steady at approximately ¥2 trillion monthly from April 2027. A Bloomberg survey of BOJ watchers expects roughly two hikes per year; former BOJ chief economist Hideo Hayakawa sees the next move potentially as early as October. The Reserve Bank of Australia held at 4.35% — its first pause this year — warning inflation remains elevated while acknowledging tightening is beginning to weigh on activity. ---

G7 Diplomatic Friction

European governments expressed skepticism at the Evian summit that the Strait of Hormuz can operationally reopen by Friday, as Trump has asserted. G7 officials cited unresolved questions around de-mining missions, patrol commitments, and the absence of a publicly released MoU text. The divergence is more than procedural: European diplomatic sources indicated serious difficulty forging a common G7 position on Iran, complicating post-deal burden-sharing on security guarantees. French President Macron separately refused to withdraw France's digital services tax despite Trump's threat of retaliatory wine tariffs, setting up a bilateral confrontation at the summit.

Hormuz: Deal Mechanics and Market Reaction

The memorandum of understanding, brokered under Pakistani auspices, will reopen the Strait for an initial 60-day negotiating period covering Iran's nuclear program. Key terms — including the gradual release of blocked Iranian funds, a halt to regime-change rhetoric, and possible Iranian toll authority over the waterway after 60 days — represent a substantial diplomatic concession by Washington. Analysts at Macquarie assess re-escalation as unlikely given both parties' incentives ahead of US midterm elections. However, shipping operators including Maersk, Hapag-Lloyd, and Mitsui OSK Lines have all indicated they will wait for confirmed safety conditions before transiting. Bloomberg data shows zero container crossings since June 8; normalization of the approximately 21 daily transits recorded pre-war is expected to unfold over months, not days.

Structural Consequences

The conflict appears to have permanently altered the risk calculus for Persian Gulf oil trade. Asian refiners are expected to seek cargo discounts reflecting ongoing chokepoint risk; Japan, which sourced ~90% of crude from the Gulf pre-war, has already established a structural baseload of US oil imports. Saudi Arabia and the UAE are accelerating bypass pipeline capacity. Separately, the episode reinforces a broader deglobalization trend: China's rare-earth leverage and Iran's Hormuz control have each forced US policy reversals within 12 months, suggesting rising structural costs for global trade flows.
