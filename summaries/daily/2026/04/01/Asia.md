Asian equity markets surged Wednesday, with the Nikkei 225 rising approximately 5% and South Korean stocks posting their strongest session since 2001, driven by a rebound in Samsung and SK Hynix on revived semiconductor demand sentiment. The gains followed Tuesday's Wall Street rally and Trump's comments signaling a potential exit from the Iran conflict within weeks. Brent crude fell below $100 per barrel for the first time in a week on the news, offering some relief to the region's most oil-dependent economies, though the Strait of Hormuz remains largely closed to commercial shipping.

The divergence in economic resilience across Asia is sharpening. China's official manufacturing PMI returned to expansionary territory in March, and the country's vast strategic oil reserves and diversified energy sources appear to be providing meaningful insulation. However, a private Caixin survey showed factory activity slipping for export-oriented firms as input costs surged, and Alibaba released its new Qwen3.5-Omni multimodal model on a closed-source basis — a departure from its usual practice that may reflect a shift toward commercialization. South Korea's exports continued to expand at a strong pace, with record AI-linked semiconductor shipments providing a buffer against the external shock.

The picture deteriorates sharply elsewhere. India was forced to reverse a near-doubling of domestic jet fuel prices within hours of implementation, after State-owned Indian Oil initially raised prices to 207,341 rupees per kiloliter before revising them back to approximately 104,927 rupees. LPG shortages are rippling through India's food service sector, with restaurants from Delhi to Bangalore substituting kerosene or wood-fired stoves. The Philippines has declared a national emergency. Vietnam, Indonesia and the broader ASEAN manufacturing bloc saw PMI readings fall to their weakest since September, underscoring the asymmetric regional impact. The BOJ's quarterly Tankan survey showed large Japanese manufacturers remaining confident for a fourth consecutive quarter, though that data largely predates the conflict's escalation. US Trade Representative Greer said the Trump-Xi summit, delayed from its earlier date, remains on track for mid-May.
