**Equity Outperformance and the AI Rotation**
European equities extended what has become the defining cross-regional market theme of early 2026. The Stoxx Europe 600 is set to close February with an eighth consecutive monthly gain — its longest winning streak since 2013 — ending the month up 3.7%. The region's relative insulation from AI disruption risk and an underappreciated cyclical recovery appear to be drawing capital from US equity allocations. European companies in the Stoxx 600 announced €85.7 billion in share buybacks year-to-date through February, the highest ever for the January-February period according to Barclays. US borrowers simultaneously issued record volumes of euro-denominated "reverse Yankee" bonds, attracted by yields approximately 170 basis points below comparable US-denominated issuance — a tangible capital flow signal toward European debt markets.

**HSBC and Corporate Earnings**
HSBC reported full-year pretax profit of $29.9 billion on Wednesday, surpassing analyst expectations and sending shares up 5.4% in London trading. The result was underpinned by wealth management strength consistent with CEO Georges Elhedery's strategic pivot, and the bank's bonus pool rose 10% to $3.93 billion, the highest in at least a decade. Diageo fell 5.8% on Wednesday after cutting full-year organic net sales guidance for the second time this fiscal year, now projecting a decline of 2–3%, illustrating the compounding risk of simultaneous demand weakness in the US and China. Aston Martin announced plans to reduce its workforce by approximately 20%, citing Trump's tariffs. Johnson Matthey shares fell 17% on Monday after Honeywell reduced its offer price for the Catalyst Technologies business, and JD Sports rose 4.4% on a £200 million buyback announcement.

**Trade Policy and EU Industrial Strategy**
The European Parliament's trade chief Bernd Lange moved to freeze ratification of the EU-US trade agreement pending clarification of Washington's revised policy framework — a signal of the fragility of arrangements negotiated under the now-invalidated IEEPA tariffs. An EU assessment confirmed that Trump's new 10% global tariff, layered on existing levies, will push cumulative rates on certain EU exports above the 15% ceiling permitted under the EU-US agreement. Separately, the US warned the EU against advancing "Made in Europe" local-content rules, while Brussels finalized a draft industrial act with strict criteria favoring European firms in public procurement and requiring some foreign competitors to share technology and hire locally — a meaningful departure from the free-trade framework governing EU industrial policy for decades. Italy's industry minister escalated pressure on the EU's Emissions Trading System, calling it "ineffective and harmful," following an 8% carbon price decline after Chancellor Merz indicated openness to revisions.

**UK Politics and Fiscal Outlook**
Prime Minister Keir Starmer's Labour government faced a significant political setback when the Green Party won the Gorton and Denton by-election on Thursday with 40.7% of the vote, with Reform UK second at 28.7% and Labour trailing third at 25.4% — illustrating two-front pressure from both the progressive left and the nationalist right. Starmer is already registering as the most unpopular UK prime minister in modern times by some polling measures. On the fiscal side, Chancellor Rachel Reeves is expected to deliver a deliberately low-key economic update next week, with gilt issuance projected to be reduced — an outcome markets are likely to receive constructively. Separately, the collapse of London-based Market Financial Solutions created acute exposures for several Wall Street institutions, with Barclays carrying approximately £600 million, Atlas roughly £400 million, and Jefferies around £100 million.

**Germany and Iran Shock Transmission**
Germany's Ifo business confidence rose more than anticipated this week, and fourth-quarter GDP data confirmed a nascent recovery driven by domestic spending, with factory orders having jumped in December by the most in two years. Chancellor Merz's visit to Beijing midweek reinforced his administration's more balanced but watchful posture toward China, warning German business against deepening investments without government backstop. Saturday's US-Israel strikes on Iran introduce a new complication: the eurozone is a large net energy importer, and a sustained move toward $108 per barrel — Bloomberg Economics' Hormuz closure estimate — would exert renewed inflationary pressure on an economy the ECB has only recently guided toward its 2% target, complicating the easing path at a particularly delicate juncture.
