**Regional Markets Under Severe Pressure**
Asia-Pacific markets bore the heaviest losses of any region during the week, with the MSCI Asia-Pacific benchmark declining approximately 3% by Tuesday and individual markets suffering sharper dislocations. The most dramatic sequence occurred in South Korea: the Kospi fell 7.2% Tuesday as markets reopened following a long weekend — partly catch-up selling after a roughly 50% year-to-date rally driven by AI-exposed chipmakers Samsung and SK Hynix — then collapsed a further 12% Wednesday in the index's largest single-day decline in its history. President Lee Jae Myung, abroad in the Philippines, convened an emergency cabinet session and invoked the country's 100 trillion won market stabilization program, which helped the Kospi rebound 10% Thursday, illustrating the severity of the volatility. The political consequences for Lee appear significant: his identification with the rally has transformed the rout into a direct credibility test. Global funds sold a net $11 billion of emerging Asian equities excluding China in the week — the largest outflow since March 2022 — with Taiwan absorbing $7.9 billion, South Korea $1.6 billion, and India approximately $1.3 billion.

**China's National People's Congress**
China's annual legislative session dominated the region's policy agenda. Premier Li Qiang set the GDP growth target at 4.5% to 5% — the lowest since 1991 — in an explicit acknowledgment of structural headwinds, characterizing the transition to new growth drivers as "formidable" and citing imbalanced supply and demand. Officials made their strongest commitment yet to ending deflationary conditions in 2026. The accompanying 15th Five-Year Plan emphasized AI, brain-computer interfaces, and pharmaceutical innovation, signaling that Beijing's technology competition with Washington will intensify. Defense spending rose 7%, the slowest pace since 2022. China simultaneously instructed its largest oil refiners to suspend diesel and gasoline exports immediately, a precautionary measure reflecting the vulnerability of Gulf crude flows. Mainland Chinese investors sold a record HK$27.7 billion of Hong Kong shares in a single session Thursday — the largest net selling on record. Separately, US-China trade negotiators are expected to meet in Paris in mid-March, with Treasury Secretary Bessent, USTR Greer, and Vice Premier He Lifeng preparing the ground for a potential Trump-Xi summit.

**India and the Strait Incident**
India's position became diplomatically fraught mid-week after a US submarine sank the Iranian warship IRIS Dena off Sri Lanka — the first US submarine attack on a surface vessel since World War II — following the ship's participation in an Indian Navy-hosted multilateral fleet review. The Indian rupee fell to a record low against the dollar, and Indian equity benchmarks erased all gains in dollar terms since last April's Liberation Day turmoil. Prime Minister Modi called for "dialog and diplomacy" while facing pointed questions about sovereignty in India's own maritime neighborhood. The US Treasury's temporary waiver allowing Indian refiners to resume Russian oil purchases provided some relief, though it simultaneously illustrated India's acute energy exposure.

**Japan and Australia**
In Japan, the Toyota group reached a landmark settlement with Elliott Investment Management over the privatization of Toyota Industries, offering ¥20,600 per share — a 9.6% increase from the prior bid — in a deal valued at approximately ¥6.7 trillion ($42.8 billion). A Bank of Japan deputy governor declined to signal imminent rate increases, though a former official placed the odds of an April move at 50/50. Australia's Reserve Bank Governor Michele Bullock signaled the central bank's patient approach to inflation has limits, noting a rate hike remains possible — a reminder that the Iran-driven energy shock compounds pre-existing inflation management challenges across the region.
