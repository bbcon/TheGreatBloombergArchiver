Weekly Brief

May 25 – May 31, 2026

US

  • University of Michigan consumer sentiment hit its lowest reading since the survey's 1978 inception, a sharp disconnect from equity markets at all-time highs driven almost entirely by AI semiconductor names.
  • Fed Chair Kevin Warsh, sworn in Friday, faces markets now pricing rate hikes by early 2027 — a complete reversal from the cut expectations that prevailed in late February — with Thursday's PCE print (consensus near 4%) the week's pivotal data point.
  • S&P 500 closed May up 4.9%, on pace for a ninth consecutive weekly gain, with the SOX semiconductor index contributing roughly 369 of the index's 672-point year-to-date advance; Goldman Sachs raised its year-end target to 8,000 on Tuesday.
  • Dell surged 40% in premarket Thursday on an AI server outlook that far exceeded consensus; Micron reached $1 trillion in market cap in 48 trading days — the fastest doubling on record.
  • Ken Paxton defeated John Cornyn decisively in the Texas Republican Senate runoff after securing Trump's endorsement; a little-known hard-right candidate also unseated an oil-industry-backed incumbent on the Texas Railroad Commission, introducing regulatory uncertainty into the Permian Basin.

Equity Markets and the AI Momentum Trade

US equities extended their extraordinary run through the week, with the S&P 500 reaching successive record closes driven by a narrow but powerful semiconductor complex. Micron Technology surged 19.3% on Tuesday, joining a ten-member club of US companies valued above $1 trillion and reaching that threshold in 48 trading days — the fastest doubling on record — underpinned by a projected earnings increase exceeding 1,200% through fiscal 2027. The concentration of returns is striking: the SOX index contributed roughly 369 points to the S&P 500's 672-point year-to-date gain, against a more modest 79 points from the remaining Magnificent Six. MSCI momentum stocks are on track for their strongest two-month outperformance versus the broad market on record. Goldman Sachs lifted its year-end S&P 500 target to 8,000 on Tuesday, citing AI tailwinds and earnings momentum, with Ed Yardeni running the highest tracked estimate at 8,250. The AI infrastructure theme continued to produce uneven outcomes across the software stack: Snowflake surged approximately 36% Wednesday after a $6 billion AWS agreement and raised guidance, lifting MongoDB (+10%) and Datadog (+5.5%), while Salesforce fell roughly 3% on a disappointing revenue forecast — illustrating the tension between monetizing AI features and the risk that AI reduces the complexity of workflows enterprise vendors serve. Dell's Thursday premarket surge of 40% simultaneously lifted Hewlett Packard Enterprise (+20%) and Super Micro Computer (+12%). The week closed with household equity exposure now above even 2000 dot-com peak levels, a dynamic Bank of America's Michael Hartnett characterized as "bubbly," noting retail mania and compressed volatility.

Monetary Policy

Kevin Warsh's tenure as Fed Chair began with markets wasting little time pricing its implications. The 5s30s Treasury spread compressed to its tightest level in a year, reflecting a higher-for-longer repricing at the front end, as traders assigned high probability to the first rate hike arriving by March 2027. The inaugural Bessent-Warsh breakfast produced a carefully calibrated signal: the Treasury secretary framed his expectations around Warsh doing "the right thing for inflation and growth" — invoking the statutory dual mandate rather than political objectives — and did not request rate cuts. Minneapolis Fed President Kashkari, speaking in Seoul on Thursday, reinforced the cautious stance, warning that elevated energy prices and unanchored inflation expectations preclude declaring victory. Thursday's PCE print, with consensus near 4% driven primarily by energy costs, represented the week's pivotal data point; any significant upside surprise would likely accelerate the timetable for tightening. The tension between a potential cyclical oil-price disinflationary impulse — if the Hormuz deal holds — and persistently elevated real yields leaves the Fed's near-term path genuinely uncertain.

Consumer, Political, and Corporate Developments

The record equity prints stand in sharp contrast to a University of Michigan consumer sentiment reading that, by Bloomberg's account, is the weakest since the survey's inception in 1978. That divergence — financial asset inflation alongside deteriorating household confidence, partly reflecting elevated energy costs from the unresolved Strait of Hormuz situation — represents a tension the market has thus far chosen to set aside. In corporate news, Gap fell 15% after cutting its sales outlook, underscoring uneven consumer spending beyond the AI complex, while IBM gained 6% on a $10 billion quantum computing commitment. The Trump administration's effective freeze on onshore wind permitting is creating measurable economic consequences, with trade groups estimating $50 billion in stranded investment risk across approximately 150,000 jobs — a policy divergence that sits uncomfortably with the strategic case for domestic energy diversification at a moment of Middle East supply disruption. The Texas Railroad Commission primary result, in which energy majors including Occidental, ExxonMobil, Chevron, and ConocoPhillips failed to protect their preferred candidate, introduces genuine regulatory uncertainty into the US's most prolific producing basin. ---

Asia

  • Taiwan raised its 2026 GDP growth forecast to 9.6% from 7.7% on Thursday — a substantial beat relative to prior estimates — citing AI-driven semiconductor demand, as the island overtook India to become the world's fifth-largest stock market.
  • South Korea's Kospi surged 24% in May, its best monthly performance in recent memory, driven by Samsung and SK Hynix; SK Hynix surpassed $1 trillion in market capitalization on Tuesday, controlling 57% of the high-bandwidth memory market.
  • Huawei unveiled its "LogicFolding" chip architecture on Thursday, claiming 1.4nm-equivalent performance by 2031 without EUV lithography — sending SMIC and Hua Hong shares sharply higher and challenging the effectiveness of US export controls.
  • Beijing launched its most forceful crackdown on cross-border equity trading in recent years, costing Futu Holdings founder Leaf Li an estimated $1.7 billion in a single day; China also began restricting overseas travel for top AI professionals at firms including Alibaba and DeepSeek.
  • Hong Kong overtook Switzerland as the world's largest cross-border wealth hub, with $2.9 trillion in international assets under management, fueled by mainland Chinese inflows and a Hang Seng that rose 28% last year.

Semiconductors and the AI Investment Cycle

The dominant story across Asia this week was the extraordinary wealth and growth concentrated in semiconductor supply chains. Taiwan's GDP revision to 9.6% for 2026 — up from 7.7% — reflects an island economy that has become the physical backbone of the global AI infrastructure buildout. Taiwan's equity market overtook India in total market capitalization on Monday, driven primarily by TSMC's sustained rally. South Korea's Kospi posted a 24% monthly gain through Thursday, an extraordinary move for a major index, with the rally becoming self-reinforcing for the MSCI Emerging Markets index, which gained 7.8% in May to an all-time high. SK Hynix's crossing of the $1 trillion threshold on Tuesday — following Samsung's earlier milestone — reflects the company's 57% share of high-bandwidth memory, a component for which demand is expected to outstrip supply through at least 2027. The rally has become sufficiently concentrated that some funds are being forced to sell positions that have breached internal concentration limits, a technical dynamic that could introduce short-term volatility even as fundamentals remain supportive. Samsung's workforce separately accepted a wage agreement providing an average bonus of approximately $340,000 per chip worker, averting a strike that would have threatened near-term production.

Huawei's LogicFolding Disclosure

The most consequential technology development of the period was Huawei's public disclosure of its LogicFolding architecture at a chip conference in China on Thursday. Presented by semiconductor chief He Tingbo, the technology seeks to improve chip performance by shortening internal data-transmission distances rather than shrinking transistors — an approach designed explicitly to circumvent China's inability to procure ASML's EUV machines. The company targets 1.4nm-equivalent chip production by 2031, roughly three years behind TSMC's current roadmap. Bloomberg Intelligence notes that if commercially viable, this would help Chinese AI firms maintain competitiveness despite Nvidia export restrictions. Material engineering hurdles remain — heat dissipation, manufacturing complexity, and economic scale — but the market reaction was immediate. ByteDance's separate discussions around $70 billion in capital expenditure for 2026 signal an equally aggressive posture; Taiwan's tech firms are borrowing a record $14.5 billion to fund their own expansion. The disclosure reinforces an argument previously advanced by Nvidia's Jensen Huang: export controls may be accelerating rather than halting Chinese semiconductor self-sufficiency.

China Capital Controls and Geopolitics

Beijing's crackdown on illicit cross-border stock trading — described as the most forceful in recent years — sent an unambiguous signal about the authorities' intent to arrest capital outflows that reached an estimated $1 trillion in 2025. Separately, travel restrictions on leading AI professionals represent an escalation in Beijing's effort to prevent talent diffusion to Western firms. The yuan is on course for a sixth consecutive quarterly gain against the dollar, its longest winning streak since 2013, with the People's Bank appearing comfortable allowing appreciation as a signal of economic confidence. Chinese Foreign Minister Wang Yi visited Ottawa — the first such visit in a decade — reflecting a deliberate diplomatic realignment as Canada diversifies away from US dependency. In the South China Sea, China expelled a Dutch naval frigate near the Paracel Islands on Wednesday, and ship-tracking data revealed intensifying coast guard activity around the Spratly Islands, maintaining a persistent Indo-Pacific friction point even as geopolitical attention remains concentrated on the Gulf.

Japan, South Korea Policy, and Regional Divergence

Japan's net external asset position reached a record ¥561.8 trillion at end-2025, though the country slipped to third-largest creditor nation as China's position grew faster. Japan's two-year bond yield rose Friday after a lukewarm auction, extending pressure on JGB markets that has drawn renewed intervention warnings from Finance Minister Katayama. The Bank of Korea's incoming governor signaled a hawkish pivot on Wednesday, pointing to rate increases ahead — a meaningful shift for a central bank that spent much of 2024-25 in an easing posture, with energy-driven inflation pressures appearing to drive the recalibration. Singapore's Ministry of Trade reported Q1 GDP growth of 1.0% quarter-on-quarter, well ahead of the 0.2% consensus, but forward guidance struck a notably cautious tone, identifying Middle East energy disruption, US tariff escalation, and a possible AI capex correction as meaningful downside scenarios. The contrast between strong first-quarter momentum and deteriorating forward visibility has become a recurring theme across the region. ---

Europe

  • French and Spanish CPI accelerated Thursday to the fastest pace since 2024, above consensus, reinforcing the case for an ECB rate hike — even as France's economy simultaneously contracted in Q1, presenting the ECB with a direct stagflationary tension.
  • ECB Governing Council members including Isabel Schnabel called for a June rate hike, while Bund yields and inflation breakevens fell sharply Monday on Hormuz deal optimism before partially recovering on renewed US strikes; markets ended the week with three hikes priced for 2026.
  • A Romanian apartment building in Galați was struck by a Russian drone that entered NATO airspace on Thursday, the most serious such incident for the alliance's eastern flank since the Ukraine war began.
  • BP Chairman Albert Manifold disputed the company's account of his abrupt dismissal, deepening uncertainty around the UK oil major's strategic direction; a Bloomberg investigation found companies in at least nine European countries purchased fraudulent Chinese carbon credits.
  • The EU revived its Chips Act with a rebooted framework requiring an estimated €120 billion in public-private investment by 2035, a direct response to the semiconductor race being driven by US and Chinese capital.

ECB Policy: A Genuine Dilemma

The ECB's policy path crystallized into its most difficult configuration of the current cycle this week. Two Governing Council members, including Isabel Schnabel, called for a rate hike at the June meeting, and President Lagarde confirmed the bank will revise its inflation projections upward. The case for tightening was reinforced Thursday when CPI accelerated in France, Spain, and Italy to the fastest rates since 2024 — largely reflecting energy price pass-through from the Middle East conflict. Yet France's Q1 GDP contraction, reported the same day, presents the precise scenario that most complicates the ECB's task: inflation overshoot alongside weak growth. The conditionality in the Governing Council's language — a hike "unless a durable peace deal is reached" — introduces genuine two-way risk to the rate path. Bund yields and inflation expectations fell sharply Monday on thin holiday volumes as Hormuz deal optimism briefly dominated, before partially reversing as fresh US strikes on Iranian targets pushed energy prices back toward $97. Governing Council member Villeroy's observation that second-round inflation effects have not yet materialized sits in tension with Schnabel's more hawkish framing, and the ECB's semi-annual Financial Stability Review warned Wednesday that asset prices remain "stretched by historical standards," with geopolitical risks appearing underestimated by investors.

UK Political Economy

The UK's political situation continues to deteriorate in ways that carry medium-term economic significance. Commentary from Westminster suggests the Starmer administration is entering a period of effective policymaking paralysis as the leadership question remains unresolved, with Andy Burnham's potential candidacy drawing attention from fixed-income investors alert to any leftward fiscal shift during a leadership contest. The more immediate economic concern is consumer price pressure: over 80% of British food and drink manufacturers surveyed indicated they plan to raise prices, with energy cost pass-through from the Hormuz disruption the primary driver. A government-commissioned review warned of a "lost generation" of young unemployed workers, attributing the deterioration to surging employer national insurance contributions, worsening youth mental health, and AI-driven displacement of entry-level roles — structural drivers unlikely to self-correct through the cycle. Reform UK's position as Britain's most popular party by polling adds a longer-horizon policy risk: the party's deputy leader has outlined plans to dismantle net-zero commitments, which would create material discontinuity risk for UK renewable energy investments and carbon market instruments if translated into electoral power.

Carbon Markets and EU Industrial Policy

A Bloomberg investigation found that companies in at least nine European countries — including BP, Shell, TotalEnergies, EDF, ExxonMobil, and OMV — purchased carbon credits from Chinese projects in the EU's upstream emissions reduction scheme that appear to have been fraudulent or non-existent. German authorities have voided credits from 30 projects claiming 2.1 million tons of CO₂ savings. No criminal penalties are expected for the purchasing companies, as acquisitions were made in good faith, but the episode raises fundamental questions about the governance architecture of European carbon markets ahead of the EU's planned expansion of credit-based mechanisms toward its 2040 emissions target. The European Commission's rebooted Chips Act, targeting €120 billion in investment by 2035 in public-private partnership, reflects a strategic response to the semiconductor race — and specifically to Huawei's LogicFolding disclosure — that Brussels regards as a structural competitive threat requiring industrial policy intervention rather than market-driven adjustment alone. Oxford Economics published analysis quantifying a further upside risk to ECB inflation forecasts: severe climate-driven food supply disruptions could raise eurozone headline inflation by up to 0.6 percentage points annually, a supply-side risk that conventional monetary tools are poorly designed to address. ---

LatAm

  • The US announced it will designate Brazil's two largest criminal organizations — Primeiro Comando da Capital and Comando Vermelho — as terrorist entities, a move likely to reignite bilateral tensions with President Lula and carries indirect implications for Brazilian sovereign spreads and the real.
  • Industrial metals posted their best monthly performance since January, providing terms-of-trade improvement for Chile, Peru, and Colombia at a moment of tighter global financial conditions.
  • Brazil's President Lula is actively stimulating the economy ahead of elections, according to Bloomberg Economics, raising questions about fiscal sustainability and medium-term inflation dynamics.
  • Venezuela opposition leader María Corina Machado signaled willingness to negotiate a path to presidential elections, a potential opening backed by Washington; more than half of Venezuelans support full dollarization as inflation runs near 600%.

Brazil: Bilateral Friction and Fiscal Risk

The US designation of Brazil's two largest criminal organizations as terrorist entities is the most consequential LatAm development of the period. The move gives US law enforcement expanded tools to pursue international financial flows linked to PCC and CV, but carries significant diplomatic cost: Lula has consistently resisted framing Brazilian organized crime through a counterterrorism lens, and any perception of US interference in domestic security matters is politically toxic in Brasília. The relationship between Trump and Lula was already strained by trade policy and ideological divergence; this designation adds a new source of bilateral friction at a moment when Brazil is deepening commodity export relationships with China. The macro implications are indirect but real — political turbulence in Brazil historically widens sovereign spreads and weakens the real, though the magnitude of any market reaction depends on whether the designation produces measurable policy escalation from either side. The fiscal backdrop complicates the picture further: Bloomberg Economics characterizes Lula's pre-election stimulus as a deliberate effort to boost activity ahead of the vote. While the near-term demand impulse may support growth data, the strategy risks widening Brazil's already elevated fiscal deficit and adding to inflationary pressures at a point when the Banco Central do Brasil has been working to anchor expectations. The tension between electoral fiscal expansion and monetary credibility is a recurring dynamic in Brazilian political economy, and markets will likely price additional risk premium into Brazilian rates if the stimulus trajectory extends beyond what primary balance targets can accommodate.

Venezuela and Regional Political Economy

Machado's signal of willingness to negotiate on a path to presidential elections represents a meaningful shift in tone, suggesting Washington's pressure campaign may be producing marginal movement. The context is an economy in severe distress: inflation running near 600% has produced majority public support for full dollarization — a measure that would eliminate the inflation tax but remove the government's ability to monetize deficits, a constraint Venezuela's fiscal structure has historically been unable to absorb without substantial external support. The broader regional picture is shaped by the commodity cycle: industrial metals' best monthly performance since January provides genuine terms-of-trade relief for Chile, Peru, and Colombia, offering some offset to the tighter global financial conditions implied by higher US rates and the Hormuz-driven energy shock. China's commercial outreach in the Western Hemisphere is simultaneously broadening — Beijing's rapprochement with Canada, including reduced EV tariffs exchanged for lower agricultural levies, is a reminder that Latin American producers of agricultural commodities and resources face evolving competition dynamics in key export markets. Colombia's approaching presidential election, with a leftist candidate polling as frontrunner, adds to a cluster of EM political risk events that Oxford Economics' quarterly survey identified as contributing to the sharpest deterioration in emerging-market conditions since late 2022. ---

Global

  • A tentative 60-day US-Iran ceasefire extension was reached Thursday, pending Trump's approval; Brent crude fell approximately 19% in May — its steepest monthly decline since 2020 — though fresh US strikes on Iranian targets mid-week nearly reversed Monday's 7% single-session drop before the truce language stabilized markets.
  • Anthropic raised $65 billion at a $965 billion valuation Thursday, surpassing OpenAI; Apollo and Blackstone are assembling a $36 billion private credit facility to finance Google TPUs leased to Anthropic, effectively transforming advanced semiconductors into a financeable asset class.
  • Russia's GDP contracted in the most recent quarter for the first time in three years, with Bloomberg Economics cutting the 2026 growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3%; Russia launched a major assault on Ukraine mid-week involving a hypersonic Oreshnik ballistic missile and approximately 90 missiles and 600 drones.
  • Gold shed nearly 20% from its late-January peak and traded around $4,490 on Wednesday, with UBS trimming its year-end forecast to $5,500; the metal's inability to sustain a safe-haven bid despite ongoing geopolitical risk reflects rising real yields and unwind of 2025 speculative positioning.
  • The Pentagon's Indo-Pacific command submitted a 121-page report to Congress warning that the PLA is targeting Taiwan readiness by 2027 and requesting billions for asymmetric sea-denial and hypersonic capabilities.

Strait of Hormuz: Costly Stasis, Tentative Resolution

The trajectory of US-Iran negotiations remained the single most consequential variable for global markets across the week. The sequence illustrated the gap between headline optimism and operational reality: Monday's reports of a potential deal drove Brent crude down more than 7% in thin holiday trading; fresh US Central Command strikes on Iranian missile launch sites and mine-laying vessels Wednesday pushed prices back toward $97; and Thursday's tentative agreement on a 60-day ceasefire extension produced a further leg down that left Brent on course for its largest monthly decline since 2020. Trump has not yet formally approved the terms, and Iran's Foreign Ministry earlier in the week explicitly stated that "no one can claim that the signing of an agreement is imminent." Secretary Rubio acknowledged talks could take several more days. Republican hawks including Senator Graham have argued the emerging terms concede too much to Tehran, while Iran's Supreme Leader Khamenei declared that US military bases in the region will no longer be safe — language that complicates any clean resolution. The longer the stasis continues, the greater the risk that second-order economic effects — food and energy inflation, shipping cost pass-through — accumulate to a degree that becomes politically difficult to manage even without a market-based forcing event. China appears to be playing a quiet but consequential mediation role, with Pakistani Army Chief Munir shuttling between Beijing and Doha; the reported possibility of Iran's highly enriched uranium being transferred to China would represent an unusual expansion of Beijing's direct involvement in Middle Eastern security arrangements.

AI Capital Flows and the Infrastructure Supercycle

The scale of capital mobilizing around artificial intelligence infrastructure approached macro-relevant magnitudes this week. Anthropic's $65 billion fundraise at a $965 billion valuation — eclipsing OpenAI for the first time — was accompanied by a $36 billion private credit facility assembled by Apollo and Blackstone to finance AI chip procurement, effectively transforming advanced semiconductors into a financeable asset class with characteristics more akin to infrastructure than venture capital. ByteDance is in discussions around $70 billion in capital expenditure for 2026. Taiwan's tech firms are borrowing a record $14.5 billion. The Quad — US, Japan, India, Australia — committed up to $20 billion in support for critical-minerals supply chain development. These flows are compressing risk premia in private credit, concentrating equity gains in AI-adjacent equities globally, and creating feedback loops between chip demand, energy infrastructure requirements, and sovereign growth forecasts that are still being incorporated into official projections.

Russia: War Economy Under Strain

Russia's economy presents an unusual configuration: a wartime state running relatively orthodox macroeconomic policy. The Bank of Russia's benchmark rate at 14.5% implies real rates above 9% given sub-6% consumer price inflation — among the highest real rates globally. The Economy Ministry cut its 2026 growth forecast to 0.4% from 1.3%, with the non-oil private sector — roughly three-quarters of the economy — stalling under tight monetary conditions and a labor shortfall estimated at approximately 1.5 million workers. Russia's mid-week use of a hypersonic Oreshnik ballistic missile in a mass strike on Ukrainian cities represents a continued escalation in the quality of munitions deployed even as front lines remain relatively static. Foreign Minister Lavrov's call to Secretary Rubio advising evacuation of US personnel from Kyiv signals Moscow's intent to maintain pressure on Ukrainian decision-making infrastructure. Russia's consideration of restricting diesel and jet fuel exports — a potential response to Ukrainian drone strikes on refinery infrastructure — would add a further layer of complexity to already strained global energy markets.

Geopolitical Architecture in Realignment

Beyond the primary theater of the Iran conflict, several developments this week signal a global order in active reconfiguration. The Pentagon's Indo-Pacific command report frames Taiwan preparedness through the same maritime chokepoint lens sharpened by the Hormuz experience, requesting billions for asymmetric sea-denial capabilities with a 2027 urgency benchmark. China expelled a Dutch naval frigate near the Paracel Islands Wednesday while coast guard activity around the Spratlys intensified, maintaining persistent Indo-Pacific friction beneath the headlines. The China-Canada diplomatic rapprochement — Wang Yi's first Ottawa visit in a decade — reflects Beijing's deliberate effort to widen commercial relationships in the Western Hemisphere as the US-China strategic competition intensifies. Russia's GDP contraction and the Romanian drone incident on Thursday, which Bucharest called a "serious and irresponsible escalation," collectively underscore that the global geopolitical risk premium, while partly priced through energy markets, carries dimensions in equity and credit valuations that appear underweighted relative to the breadth of active stress points.
