One disaster after another

By Zahra Hirji 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVypnhGtqak/zahra-hirji?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
 andTodd Woody 
<https://origin.www.bloomberg.com/authors/AVl1IPrK6vs/todd-woody?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>

Across the US this week, different disasters — including Canadian wildfire 
smoke darkening the skies 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-15/new-york-washington-face-peak-heat-as-canadian-smoke-spreads?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
 in the Midwest and Northeast,extreme heat along the East Coast 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-13/new-york-city-us-northeast-will-see-temperatures-soar-this-week?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
 andcatastrophic flooding in Texas 
<http://google.com/url?q=https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-14/texas-braces-for-slow-moving-storms-one-year-after-deadly-floods?srnd%3Dhomepage-europe&sa=D&source=docs&ust=1784223477440765&usg=AOvVaw1OQDZaskuTXT-ENoq2a3PU>
 — are disrupting daily life and putting people’s health at risk.

When multiple weather catastrophes play out like this at the same time, or in 
quick succession, experts refer to them as compound events. As climate change 
makes compound events more likely, experts warn that grappling with them is 
further complicated by the Trump administration’scuts to disaster work 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-06-13/trump-s-fema-phaseout-what-cuts-mean-for-hurricane-season?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
.
The Guadalupe River in Center Point, Texas on July 16.Photographer: Brandon 
Bell/Getty Images
The American disaster complex has long been struggling to cope 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/features/2025-08-29/us-disaster-agency-fema-was-breaking-long-before-trump?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
 with the increasing number of costly crises, but recent cuts to the Federal 
Emergency Management Agency (FEMA) have made the situation worse, according to
Jeffrey Schlegelmilch 
<https://people.climate.columbia.edu/users/profile/jeffrey-schlegelmilch>, 
Columbia University’s faculty director of the National Center for Disaster 
Preparedness. “We are not as well prepared as we were a few years ago,” he said.

“It leaves us in a really difficult spot,” said Samantha Penta 
<https://www.albany.edu/cehc/faculty/samantha-penta>, an associate professor 
specializing in emergency preparedness at the University at Albany, noting that 
the scaleback of federal resources means “there’s a lot of pressure” on state 
and local governments to manage complex crises on their own.

John Abatzoglou, a climatologist at the University of California at Merced, 
said scientists expect compound calamities to increase as wildfires are ignited 
during more frequent heat waves. And a climate-chargedsuper El Niño 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-09/super-el-nino-will-give-us-a-glimpse-of-life-in-2035?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
 could bring more extreme rain, flooding and landslides across a swath of 
states.

“If you increase one type of extreme, the odds are that you’re going to get 
hit with another extreme that results in a larger impact,” he said.

When multiple disasters hit the same place simultaneously, local officials are 
tapping the same limited pool of resources and personnel to respond to both. 
And sometimes these responses may conflict, said Schlegelmilch.

One example of this is playing out right now 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2026-07-16/canadian-wildfire-smoke-stokes-health-threats-from-us-heatwave?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
 in New York and elsewhere, where smoky skies are hitting at the same time as 
high heat. When there’s high heat, officials may recommend kids and families 
take advantage of local splash pads or playgrounds with water, to help cool 
down. But that recommendation makes less sense when the outside air quality is 
dangerous.

Hazardous smoke during heat waves can also slow down emergency response, 
according to Michael Jerrett, a professor of environmental health sciences at 
the University of California at Los Angeles.

“There’s increased demand for emergency services and when the responders are 
on site, they often are dealing with more complicated cases and they’re staying 
longer,” he said.

Different problems can arise when repeat disasters hit the same place before 
it is fully recovered. This is what’s happening now in central Texas. Heavy 
rains last year resulted in flash flooding along the Guadalupe River,killing 
more than a hundred people 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/news/articles/2025-07-07/texas-flooding-death-toll-tops-100-as-camp-confirms-worst-fears?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>
. Despite still recovering from those severe floods, the region now faces new 
flooding.

“Every disaster starts out with a local response,” said Josh Morton, president 
of the International Association of Emergency Managers and an emergency 
management director in Saluda County, South Carolina. “Now once local resources 
are overwhelmed, that’s whenever we turn to the state.” If the state is 
overwhelmed, it may turn to other states and the federal level, he added.

So if there are overlapping disasters, or back-to-back ones happening so fast 
a place hasn’t recovered when the second event hits, local officials may get 
overwhelmed faster and look for outside help sooner.

Dangerous reality

By Emma Court 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AUVJA-GbuIc/emma-court?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>

An estimated 64 million Americans were suffering through high temperatures and 
also breathing air polluted by wildfire smoke on Thursday, a dangerous 
combination that’s straining the public’s ability to protect itself and is 
likely to become increasingly common.

The affected area stretches from Minneapolis to Chicago, across Ohio, much of 
Pennsylvania, the Washington, DC, area and up to New York City, according to 
Bob Oravec, a senior branch forecaster for the US Weather Prediction Center, 
which is part of the National Weather Service. A smaller population of roughly 
13 million people are in areas with both heat advisories and air-quality 
alerts, mostly in the Mid-Atlantic region, he said.
Smoke from wildfires in Canada shrouds the Manhattan skyline at sunrise in 
Jersey City on July 16.Photographer: Michael Nagle/Bloomberg
“Extreme heat is expected at this season, but what is new in the context of 
climate change is really to have simultaneously extreme heat and wildfire 
smoke,” saidTarik Benmarhnia <https://profiles.ucsd.edu/Tarik.Benmarhnia>, 
professor of epidemiology at the University of California at San Diego. “We 
need to get used to that because it’s not going to be exceptional anymore.”

As a warming planet makes extreme weather more intense and frequent, 
scientists predict such occurrences will increasingly happen at the same time 
or one after another, a phenomenon known ascompound 
<https://www.soa.org/resources/research-reports/2024/compound-weather-extreme-events/>
 events.

Compound climate events are often driven by the same underlying factors. For 
instance, the hot, dry conditions that fuel extreme heat can also dry out 
vegetation and spark wildfires, amplifying damage and creating new risks.

High temperatures alone are dangerous, as is inhaling polluted air. The harms 
of each individually can be magnified when people experience both together. For 
someone with a heart condition, whose heart is already working hard to regulate 
body temperature, breathing fine particles into the lungs can trigger 
additional health problems such as lung inflammation.

EU’s methane delay

By Ewa Krukowska 
<https://www.bloomberg.com/authors/AOg5H97xfGE/ewa-krukowska?cmpid=BBD071726_GREENDAILY&utm_campaign=greendaily&utm_medium=email&utm_source=newsletter&utm_term=260717&utm_content=6672>

The European Union’s executive plans to recommend that member states delay by 
three years penalties on energy importers that fail to comply with the bloc’s 
controversial methane emissions rules.

Over recent months, the European Commission has resisted pressure from the US, 
the oil and gas industry, and more than half of its member states to revise its 
methane rules, opting instead to issue non-binding guidance. It is set to 
recommend next week that penalties should not be applied between 2027 and 2029, 
except for “cases of large-scale fraudulent breaches,” according to a draft 
document seen by Bloomberg News on Friday.

Disagreements over the rules — which target emissions of the potent greenhouse 
gas within the EU and impose new requirements on fossil fuel imports — have 
intensified after the US, Qatar and other gas-producing nations warned the bloc 
that the regulation could jeopardize energy shipments.
